# The Quality Axiom

## When to Use
- When a client is spending disproportionately on product improvement while underinvesting in positioning.
- When the question is "why is the worse product winning?"
- When TQM, benchmarking, or product excellence is being offered as the primary marketing strategy.
- When a company with genuinely superior products can't understand their lagging market share.

## Fails When
- **Catastrophic quality failure** — Schlitz cheapened its beer in the early 1970s and saw market share initially *increase*. But when the word got out, the brand collapsed from 16 percent to under 1 percent. At the extreme, quality problems do matter — but notice the mechanism: it was the *publicity* about the product change, not the product change itself, that destroyed Schlitz. Perception, even in quality failures, is the decisive factor.
- **Pre-category nascent market** — When a category has not yet formed and no perceptions exist, the product is the only signal. Build the product well enough to survive initial scrutiny, then immediately build the perception.

## Core Concept

"To increase sales, you need to improve your product or service. Everybody knows the better product or service will win in the marketplace. This is the quality axiom, the basic axiom of business today." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* An axiom, by definition, is too obvious to question. Nobody debates a flat earth because nobody considers that the earth might be round. The quality axiom is exactly like that — so self-evident that it never gets examined. And like the flat earth, it is wrong.

"Is a Mercedes better 'quality' than a BMW? Is a Honda better 'quality' than a Toyota? Is a Coca-Cola better 'quality' than a Pepsi-Cola? Is a Nike better 'quality' than a Reebok? Sure, there are preferences, especially when it comes to style, taste, appearance, and other product attributes you can see, feel, or taste. But quality? Quality in the abstract is easy to define. Quality in the specific is a lot more difficult to pin down." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* When a customer walks into a store, looks at three television sets, and decides which has the best picture — nine times out of ten they cannot tell the difference. "Everybody talks about quality, but nine times out of ten the customer can't tell the difference."

The data, where it exists, confirms the disconnect. A major consumer-rating service tested sixteen small-car models and ranked them by quality. The number one quality car (Volkswagen Jetta) ranked twelfth in sales. Number two quality (Acura Integra) ranked ninth in sales. Number three quality (Volkswagen Golf) ranked sixteenth in sales. The number one car in sales (Ford Escort) ranked eleventh in quality. "If you take the cars in order of quality (1, 2, 3, etc.) and then list them in order of sales, here are the rankings: 12, 9, 16, 5, 7, 2, 3, 4, 15, 6, 1, 11, 8, 10, 13 and 15. Not much correlation there." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)*

The Coca-Cola experiment removes all remaining doubt. "The Coca-Cola Company conducted 200,000 taste tests that 'proved' that New Coke tastes better than Pepsi-Cola and Pepsi tastes better than their original formula, now called Coca-Cola Classic." *(22 Laws, Ch. 4)* Who is winning the marketing battle? "The drink that research has proven to taste the best, New Coke, is in third place. The one that research shows tastes the worst, Coca-Cola Classic, is in first place." Taste is in the mind as much as in the mouth. When consumers learned that Coca-Cola had changed its formula, they said to themselves, "It's not going to taste as good." So they tasted New Coke and said, "It doesn't taste as good." Expectation shaped perception. "The perception is the reality." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)*

The real driving force is the *perception* of quality, not quality itself. "Perception is reality. The real driving force in the business world is not quality but perception of quality." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* And perception of quality flows from four things that have little to do with manufacturing: the specialist effect (specialists are perceived to know more about their specialty), the leadership effect (the leading brand is perceived to be better because it leads), the price effect (higher-priced products are perceived as higher quality), and the name effect (the right name creates a quality perception before the product is even evaluated). "Perception is more important than reality." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)*

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the perception, not the product.** Before recommending product investment, run a perception audit. Ask customers what word or quality they associate with the brand. Ask non-customers why they buy the competitor. In almost every case, the gap is a perception gap, not a product gap. The product improvements that follow should be directed at closing the perception gap, not chasing abstract quality metrics.

2. **Build quality perception through focus.** "As luck would have it, when you focus a company, you improve its quality perception. When you unfocus a company, you do the opposite. You undermine its quality perception." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* The specialist effect is automatic: a company that does one thing is perceived as knowing more about that one thing than a company that does many things. IBM as a mainframe specialist was "inevitable." IBM as a generalist became just another computer company.

3. **Use leadership as a quality signal.** "Whether it's true or not, the consumer believes the better-quality product will win. Therefore the simplest, easiest, and most direct way to achieve a quality perception in the mind is to become the leader and then communicate your leadership." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* "What's the best photographic film? Kodak, the leading brand. What's the best imported beer? Heineken, the leading brand. What's the best ketchup? Heinz, the leading brand." Customers infer quality from market position, not from features.

4. **Set pricing as a quality proxy.** "If you want a high quality perception, you need a high price." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* "What's the best automobile in the world? Rolls-Royce. What's the most expensive automobile in the world? Rolls-Royce." The price communicates the quality before the customer experiences the product. A high-quality product at a low price creates cognitive dissonance — "that can't be right" — which the customer resolves by doubting the quality. Pricing is brand strategy.

5. **Distinguish internal and external quality work.** "There's nothing wrong with quality. There's no reason why a company should not try to build the highest quality into every product it sells. But building a quality product and building a quality perception are two different things." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* Quality in manufacturing prevents catastrophic failure. Quality in marketing is perception management. Both are necessary. Neither substitutes for the other. "Drive for a quality product in the plant. Putt for a quality perception in the mind." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)*

## Examples

**Situation:** Schlitz, in the early 1970s, substituted corn syrup and hop pellets for traditional ingredients and shortened the brewing cycle by 50 percent. Forbes asked at the time: "Does it pay to build quality into a product if most consumers don't notice?"
**Application:** Schlitz used the cost savings to fund marketing. Sales continued to *increase* after the product change, rising from 12 percent market share in 1970 to 16 percent in 1976.
**Result:** Then the publicity about the product change began reaching general beer drinkers. Schlitz collapsed from 16 percent to under 1 percent. "What happened to Schlitz also happened to New Coke." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* The collapse was driven by perception change, not by product change. The same beer that grew market share destroyed it — the only variable was what people believed.

**Situation:** Motorola was the gold standard of TQM — the first Baldrige Award winner, the "titan of TQM," the six sigma pioneer. Chief executives of the Business Roundtable ranked Motorola the country's top practitioner of total quality management.
**Application:** Motorola applied this quality discipline to enter the personal computer market (1985), workstation market (1990), and mainframe market (1992). World-class manufacturing quality, no focus, no position in the mind.
**Result:** "Yet all this emphasis on quality hasn't helped Motorola in the computer business. In 1985, the company launched a line of personal computers that went nowhere. In 1990, Motorola introduced a line of workstations that never got airborne. In 1992, they tried the mainframe business with similar results." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* Quality without focus does not work.

**Situation:** New Coke passed 200,000 blind taste tests over Pepsi and over Coca-Cola Classic. Objectively, by scientific measurement, it tasted better.
**Application:** Coca-Cola replaced the original formula with New Coke.
**Result:** Consumer revolt. New Coke ended in third place. Coca-Cola Classic — the "worse-tasting" formula — returned to first place. "People drink the label, not the contents, which is another way of saying that the perception is the reality." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use quality scores to predict sales success.
**Why:** "In reviewing quality ratings with sales over a host of different products, I find very little correlation between the two." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* Quality ratings measure what experts think. Sales results measure what uninformed consumers with existing perceptions choose. These are different populations making different decisions with different information. Volkswagen Jetta ranked first in quality, twelfth in sales.

**Don't:** Assume that "best product wins" is a neutral, defensible position.
**Why:** It is actually a claim about market mechanics that is demonstrably wrong. "Marketing is not a battle of products, it's a battle of perceptions." *(22 Laws, Ch. 4)* The company that believes the best product wins will consistently underinvest in positioning and overinvest in product features. The competitor that understands perception will outmaneuver them at every turn, winning with inferior products but superior positions. IBM didn't beat Remington Rand's UNIVAC because IBM built a better mainframe. IBM beat UNIVAC because IBM got into the mind first with a massive marketing effort.

**Don't:** Misunderstand what "quality" customers say they want.
**Why:** "Customers always claim to buy the leading brand 'because it's better.' Customers as well as companies believe the quality axiom that the better product will win. Therefore, if you have the leading brand, you must have the better product." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* Customer satisfaction surveys confirm quality beliefs, not quality realities. The leading brand always gets rated higher in quality — not because it is better, but because its leadership creates the perception of being better. Survey outputs reflect positioning, not product.
