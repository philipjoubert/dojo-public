# Divergence, Not Convergence

## When to Use
- Whenever a client claims the future belongs to whoever combines X and Y.
- When a board presentation argues for a major acquisition "to capture convergence."
- When evaluating whether to build a platform that spans two categories.
- When a technology company argues that digital integration is making all categories merge.

## Fails When
- **Category is truly brand new** — When a category has not yet formed, the law of division hasn't had time to operate. Division is a function of a market maturing, not of markets being born.
- **Short measurement horizon** — Convergence products do capture initial media attention and some early buyers. Measuring at launch shows "success." The divergence reality shows up over five-to-ten years.

## Core Concept

"Convergence is against the laws of nature." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* The single most persistent strategic error in business is the belief that technology is bringing categories together. It is not. Technology accelerates division. Every decade that passes, every new product category that matures, proves the same thing: categories divide, they do not converge.

"Like amoebas dividing in a petri dish, business can be viewed as an ever-dividing sea of categories. A category starts off as a single entity usually dominated by one company. IBM dominated the computer category, for example, with the mainframe. But over time, the category divides into two or more categories." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Mainframes, minicomputers, supercomputers, fault-tolerant computers, personal computers, workstations, laptops, notebooks, palmtops, file servers — and more to come. Beer used to be beer. Then the category divided: domestic and imported, premium and popular-priced, light, draft, dry, nonalcoholic, ice, red. Ford once dominated automobiles with the Model T. Then: luxury, moderately priced, inexpensive; full-size, intermediate, compact; imported, domestic; sports cars, sport-utility vehicles, RVs, minivans.

"Division is a fact of life, a driving force of business. Division is happening in every product category from computers to communications to consumer electronics to cable television." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Why do corporate executives believe the opposite? Because they want to. "Again, it's the enormous emphasis that chief executives place on growth. They want to believe in convergence because it promises to double their size. 'If my business is converging with somebody else's business, then I can put the two together and be twice as big.'" *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)*

Management fads have clothed the convergence idea in different garb by decade. In the 1960s, conglomeration: "a professional manager can manage anything." The conglomerates collapsed. In the 1970s, diversification: "every company needs a countercyclic business." Michael Porter studied thirty-three major U.S. firms from 1950 to 1986 and concluded that diversification had done more to destroy shareholder value than create it. In the 1980s, synergy: Time Warner combining magazines and movies, Sony buying Columbia Pictures, Coca-Cola buying Columbia. The results were dismal. In the 1990s, convergence: digital technologies were going to merge computing, communications, cable, consumer electronics, entertainment, and publishing into one mega-industry. "In physics, the law of entropy says that the degree of disorder in a closed system always increases. By contrast, a pattern of convergence would make things more orderly. In biology, the law of evolution holds that new species are created by the division of a single species. Convergence, on the other hand, would have you believe that species are constantly combining, yielding such curiosities as the catdog." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)*

The automobile is the clearest proof. "In more than one hundred years, has the automobile converged with any other vehicle? No, of course not, although many people have tried." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* The Hall Flying Car of 1945. The Convair ConvAirCar of 1946 — management confidently predicted minimum sales of 160,000 per year, two were built. The Amphicar of 1961 — "it drove like a boat and floated like a car." Combination washer/dryers exist but almost nobody buys them. Combination television/VCR sets exist; almost nobody buys them because the TV and the VCR almost never break down at the same time. "The driving force in business today is not convergence, it's division." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)*

## How to Apply

1. **Map the historical divisions in the category.** For any category your client is in, trace how many segments have been created from the original single category over the last twenty years. Then project forward. The trend in every mature market is more categories, not fewer. "Currently the American Kennel Club recognizes 141 breeds of dogs, with a new breed added to the list about once a year." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Categories split like biological species.

2. **Ask what division is happening underneath the client's feet.** "Leaders are almost never overthrown from the outside. Leaders are almost always toppled from within as the category divides beneath them." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* The cable industry was so busy trying to get into telephone service that it missed the division of cable itself into regular cable, wireless cable, and direct satellite service. The incumbent's threat is almost always a new segment forming within their own market.

3. **Challenge any "convergence" acquisition or strategy on history grounds.** AT&T bought NCR for $7.4 billion to "link people, organizations, and their information in a seamless, global computer network." NCR "bled losses ever since its acquisition. In five years Global Information Solutions had four CEOs." AT&T ultimately split into three separate companies — "an example of the driving force of division." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Ask: "Of all the companies that pursued convergence in your industry, how many succeeded?"

4. **Position for the emerging category before it fully separates.** If you understand that categories divide, you can see the new categories before your competitors do. "Each segment is a separate, distinct entity. Each segment has its own reason for existence. And each segment has its own leader, which is rarely the same as the leader of the original category." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Tylenol became the leader in acetaminophen. Advil became the leader in ibuprofen. Neither was the leader of the original pain-reliever category (that was Bayer aspirin). The divisions created new leaders from scratch.

5. **Resist the "one-stop shopping" trap.** "Another favorite convergence concept is 'one-stop shopping.' Many companies have dropped a bundle trying to make this one work. Saatchi & Saatchi, one of the world's largest advertising agencies, became enamored of the idea. So they bought a basket of design and consulting firms in an effort to handle all of their corporate clients' needs. Soon after, with losses mounting, Saatchi was forced to sell its nonadvertising businesses. The stock fell from $80 a share eight years ago to around $2 today." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Customers do not buy "all services." They buy specific services from specialists who have earned specific trust.

## Examples

**Situation:** AT&T pursued a "computers and communications" strategy for more than a decade, buying NCR for $7.4 billion to create seamless convergence.
**Application:** The merged entity (renamed "Global Information Solutions") tried to sell everything from personal computers to mainframes to massively parallel systems.
**Result:** "AT&T abandoned yesterday its once-grand vision of combining communications and computers into a single corporate empire. In the end, that vision proved to be a costly illusion." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* AT&T eventually split into three separate companies — the division law reasserting itself.

**Situation:** The computer industry was predicted to converge with television in the mid-1990s. Bill Gates described the "TV/PC" — "inside are chips that are even more powerful than today's PCs. But it also has all the benefits of a TV." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)*
**Application:** Multiple companies invested in set-top boxes, interactive television, cable-computer integration products.
**Result:** The computer and television categories continued to divide, not converge. Personal computers, then smartphones, then tablets — each a new category splitting from the original, none merging with television into a single device.

**Situation:** In 1961–1965, the Quandt Group marketed the Amphicar, a combination car and boat sold around the world with a serious marketing effort.
**Application:** The product combined automotive and marine capability in a single vehicle.
**Result:** "The Amphicar foundered for the same reason that almost all combination products go under. It combined the worst of both worlds. Owners said it drove like a boat and floated like a car." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Cite technological feasibility as a reason convergence will succeed.
**Why:** "What's technically feasible isn't always acceptable in the marketplace." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Convergence has been technically feasible in many categories for decades — combination washer-dryers, flying cars, fax-phones. The market rejects them not because they don't work but because human behavior is organized around specialized tools. People want a great phone and a great fax, not a mediocre combination of both.

**Don't:** Mistake media hype for market reality.
**Why:** Convergence is one of the most persistently hyped ideas in business journalism, precisely because it promises exciting future developments and appeals to large companies with PR budgets. "Why has convergence gained such acceptance in the absence of tangible evidence that it is happening?" Because large companies with large budgets fund the conferences, the press releases, and the coverage. "Some people call convergence 'the broadband-cyberspace-interactive-multimedia-full-service-network-500-channel-digital-information-superhighway revolution.' Whatever you call it, the reality is quite different." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* Track business outcomes, not headlines.

**Don't:** Confuse vertical integration with convergence.
**Why:** Owning content and distribution (Disney buying ABC, Matsushita buying MCA, Sony buying Columbia) is often presented as "convergence." The results are consistently poor. "Ask Matsushita. They thought they needed content to fill their Panasonic hardware, so they bought MCA... So they sold the movie business to Seagram. Ask Sony. They also thought they needed content to fill their hardware, so they bought Columbia Pictures. Five years and a string of box-office flops later, Sony was forced to take a whopping $2.7 billion write-down." *(Focus, The Driving Force of Division)* The integration of different categories does not make them one category.
