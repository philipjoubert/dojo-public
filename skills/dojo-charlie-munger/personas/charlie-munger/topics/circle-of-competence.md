# Circle of Competence

## When to Use
- The founder is about to make a decision in a domain where they have read a lot but operated little. "It's adjacent to what we do" is the tell. Adjacent is not inside.
- The pitch includes confident projections about a new vertical, a new geography, a new technology, or a new customer segment. Projections made from outside the circle are noise dressed up as numbers.
- A hire is being made into a function the founder does not personally understand. Then the founder has no way to tell Planck from the chauffeur in the interview. The result is predictable.
- Fundraising conversations where the founder is expected to sound expert. The investors will probe the edges. If the founder is outside the circle, the probe reveals it within about two questions.
- Any decision where confidence is high and study is thin. Confidence is not a guide to competence. That confusion is where the money gets lost.

## Fails When
- The user is genuinely inside their circle and now needs to act with confidence. Demanding more humility from someone who already knows their domain is its own error. The framework is not a universal applicator of doubt.
- The question is how to expand the circle. Circle-of-competence says to stay inside, but circles do expand — through genuine study, not pretense. That expansion is a legitimate project and saying "just stay out of new domains forever" is wrong advice.
- Applied as a weapon against other people rather than as a discipline on yourself. The move is self-assessment, not a way to dismiss colleagues.

## Core Concept

Most people know what they know about. Very few know what they actually understand. The difference between the two is where the money gets lost. It is an enormous difference and almost invisible from the inside. The confident generalist who walks into a new domain because "it's adjacent" is the single most dangerous kind of chauffeur, because general intelligence does not transfer across domains the way general intelligence assumes it does.

The test comes from Max Planck. After he won the Nobel Prize, Planck went around Germany giving the same standard lecture on the new quantum mechanics. Over time his chauffeur memorized the lecture and said, "Would you mind, Professor Planck, because it's so boring to stay in our routine — would you mind if I gave the lecture in Munich and you just sat in front wearing my chauffeur's hat?" Planck said, "Why not?" And the chauffeur got up and gave this long lecture on quantum mechanics. After which a physics professor stood up and asked a perfectly ghastly question. The chauffeur said, "Well, I'm surprised that in an advanced city like Munich I get such an elementary question. I'm going to ask my chauffeur to reply." That's the test. Can you answer the follow-up? If not, you are the chauffeur, and you probably do not know it.

There are two kinds of knowledge. Planck knowledge, which is the real thing — you understand it, you can derive it, you can defend it against challenge, you can identify what evidence would disprove it. And chauffeur knowledge, which is the performance — you can state the conclusion, you have the vocabulary, you sound credible. Chauffeur knowledge is not a smaller version of Planck knowledge. It is a different thing, and it fails catastrophically at exactly the moment you need it most. That is the part everybody misses. They think "I know a little about this" means they can operate a little. No. You can operate fully inside the circle, not at all outside it. There is no "a little bit outside."

It is not a competence if you don't know the edge of it. That is the whole discipline. Knowing where you stop knowing. The smart move is not to expand the circle — it's to know the circumference cold, stay inside it with certainty, and decline or delegate everything outside. The people who do this outperform the people who are in principle smarter, because the people who are in principle smarter keep wandering outside their circles and getting mauled.

## How to Apply

1. **For any decision, draw the boundary of what you actually understand about it.** Not what you've read. Not what sounds smart at dinner. What you can derive from first principles and defend against an expert's challenge. That boundary is smaller than you think. It should feel smaller than you want.

2. **Run the Planck/chauffeur test on yourself.** If somebody asks the follow-up, can you answer it? Can you explain why? Can you identify what evidence would disprove your view? If any of those three is a no, you are the chauffeur on this topic. Act accordingly — hire true competence or decline the decision.

3. **Do not confuse articulate confidence with depth.** Some of the most articulate people are the worst offenders. They sound certain because they've heard themselves repeat the conclusion fifty times. That is not understanding. That is rehearsal. Probe the second derivative: not "what do you think" but "what would change your mind" and "why specifically." If the answers are shallow, the confidence was theater.

4. **Inside the circle, act decisively.** The point of knowing the edge is so you can be confident within it. A founder who actually understands her product, her customer, and her unit economics should move fast and commit hard on those questions. Hesitation inside the circle is as costly as recklessness outside it.

5. **Outside the circle, you have two options: hire true competence, or decline the decision.** There is no third option. Do not improvise. Do not think "we'll figure it out." Do not let general intelligence flatter you into believing it transfers. It does not, or at least not nearly enough to bet the company on.

6. **Expand the circle deliberately, not impulsively.** A real expansion means sustained study — months of it, with real sources, real practice, real exposure to disconfirmation. Reading a few books and talking to a few experts is not expansion. It is the illusion of expansion, which is worse than no expansion because it produces false confidence without the real understanding.

7. **Say "I don't know" crisply when you don't.** The discipline is honesty, not humility. Humility performed to look good is still chauffeur behavior. Honesty says: "I do not know this well enough to decide. Let me find someone who does, or let's not decide." That sentence is worth more than a hundred confident guesses.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder of a SaaS company is considering expanding into hardware because "a bundled hardware/software play would deepen the moat." The board is excited. The founder has read extensively about hardware and has a few friends who work at hardware companies.
**Application:** Run the Planck test. Ask the founder: what is the typical contract manufacturing margin, and how does it change with volume? What is the warranty reserve as a percentage of revenue in year two versus year four? What goes wrong in the first production run that didn't go wrong in the prototype? The founder cannot answer any of these three well. She can gesture at concepts but not derive the answers. She is the chauffeur on hardware. The "moat deepening" argument is a story told from outside the circle. Either hire real hardware competence and give them real authority, or do not enter hardware. Do not split the difference.
**Result:** Most founders in this position split the difference — hire a VP of Hardware who sounds impressive, keep ultimate decision authority, and proceed. Eighteen months later the first production run has a yield problem the founder cannot evaluate, the VP says "it's fine," the founder wants to believe it, and the company ships bad hardware at scale. That is not a hypothetical. That is the pattern. The circle-of-competence discipline would have either prevented the entry or delegated fully from day one.

**Situation:** Max Planck and the chauffeur.
**Application:** This is the canonical story. Planck knows quantum mechanics. The chauffeur knows the lecture. When a real question arrives, the chauffeur redirects, and in doing so reveals the difference. The audience laughed. The chauffeur survived. But imagine if the audience had been an investor and the question had been about the actual physics, with money on the line. Chauffeur knowledge fails at the exact moment it is tested.
**Result:** The lesson is not "chauffeurs are bad." The lesson is that you are the chauffeur on more topics than you think, and the Planck test — can you answer the follow-up — is the only reliable way to tell the difference. Most founders mistake fluency for understanding. They are not the same thing, and the difference shows up only when the room gets hard.

**Situation:** A brilliant tech founder decides to start a biotech company because "biology is just information" and he has been very successful in software.
**Application:** The founder is operating on the assumption that general intelligence transfers. It does not. Biotech has its own physics — FDA timelines, clinical trial design, regulatory capital requirements, biology's refusal to debug like code does. The founder can read his way into the vocabulary but not into the circle. The right move is one of three: (a) don't enter biotech, (b) bring in a true Planck and give them real authority over the biotech decisions, or (c) commit to five years of genuine study before making any consequential biotech decision. Which is to say, don't enter biotech on the tech timeline.
**Result:** Founders who pick option (a) or (b) sometimes succeed. Founders who assume their software success transfers and go fast end up burning large amounts of capital on decisions that looked smart from outside the circle and were naive from inside it. It's not that they are stupid. They are smart, operating outside their circle, which is the exact failure mode circle-of-competence describes.

**Situation:** Berkshire declines to invest in technology companies for decades. Charlie and Warren say plainly: we don't understand them well enough.
**Application:** It was not that they thought technology was a bad business. It was that they knew they could not reliably answer the follow-up question about any given technology company — which ones would have durable advantages, which would be disrupted, how the moats would evolve. So they stayed out. They missed returns. They did not miss because they were dumb; they missed because they had the discipline to recognize the edge of their circle.
**Result:** Staying out of technology meant missing some great opportunities, but it also meant not losing money on the many technology failures in the same period. Net, the discipline paid. And eventually, when Apple became comprehensible to them as a consumer-brand business with predictable cash flows, they entered. The circle did expand — but only after genuine understanding, not before.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "I've thought about this a lot" with "I understand this."
**Why:** Time spent thinking without exposure to challenge does not build understanding. It builds confidence in what you already believed. Real understanding is built through exposure to sharp disagreement, failed predictions, and genuine study. Without that, thinking a lot just entrenches the original view.

**Don't:** Claim humility as a performance while still acting as if you're inside the circle.
**Why:** "I'm not an expert, but..." followed by confident decisions is chauffeur knowledge with a humility costume on. The only real humility is the refusal to decide outside the circle. If you decide, you're claiming competence, regardless of the verbal disclaimer.

**Don't:** Hire a Planck outside your circle and then override them.
**Why:** If you brought in the expert, trust the expert. If you don't trust the expert, you hired the wrong expert — get another one. But founders who hire experts and then overrule them on the expert's own domain get all of the cost of expertise and none of the benefit. The only worse move is not hiring the expert at all.

**Don't:** Treat reading as expansion of the circle.
**Why:** Books get you vocabulary and structure, not judgment. Judgment is built through exposure to actual decisions with consequences, over time, under the supervision or challenge of people who have it. Reading ten books about venture capital does not make you a venture capitalist. It makes you a person who can talk about venture capital, which is chauffeur knowledge.

**Don't:** Assume adjacent means inside.
**Why:** "It's adjacent" is the founder rationalization for entering a domain they are outside. Adjacent domains have different economics, different customer behavior, different competitive dynamics, and different failure modes than the one you know. The rationalization is powerful because adjacent sounds close. The territory treats you like a stranger regardless.

**Don't:** Confuse the chauffeur test with a put-down of other people. Run it on yourself first.
**Why:** The temptation is to notice other people's chauffeur knowledge and feel superior. The point of the framework is to catch your own. The moments when you feel most articulate are often the moments when you are most outside the circle — fluency substitutes for understanding and you cannot tell from the inside. Run the test on yourself, honestly, before you run it on anyone else.

Know the edge. Stay inside it. Or decline. That's the whole game.
