# Opportunity Cost

## When to Use
- Any allocation decision. Capital, time, headcount, founder attention. Especially founder attention — that is the scarcest resource in the building, and the one most often spent without comparison.
- You are about to say yes to something, and you have not named what you're saying no to. The yes is not real until the no is specific.
- A new market, a new product line, a new acquisition. "Is this good?" is the wrong question. "Is this better than doubling down on what already works?" is the right one.
- You are evaluating a hire. The question is not "can this person do the job?" It is "is this person the best we could get for this comp, in this time window?" Those are very different questions.
- Reviewing an existing project. The sunk cost is irrelevant. The forward question is: if we weren't already doing this, would we start it today with the resources it requires? If the answer is no, the opportunity cost of continuing is the thing you're not doing.

## Fails When
- **Comparing to nothing.** "Is this good" with no baseline. You can always find reasons a thing is good in isolation. In a world with choices, isolation is the wrong frame.
- **Comparing to an imaginary perfect alternative.** "We could theoretically do something even better" is not a real comparison. The comparison is to your actual next-best realistic alternative — the thing you would do if you didn't do this. Imaginary ideals are an alibi for never choosing.
- **Used to delay infinitely.** Opportunity cost analysis has a stopping rule. You compare to the three best alternatives you can realistically execute, pick the best, and move. Founders who keep adding hypothetical alternatives are not deciding; they are drifting while pretending to be rigorous.
- **Applied to trivial reversible decisions.** The opportunity cost of spending twenty minutes in a meeting is not worth a thirty-minute analysis. Reserve the discipline for the decisions where it matters.

## Core Concept

We sit around and look at companies and make comparisons. Essentially we're just looking at opportunity cost. That is the whole Berkshire method, in one sentence. Every decision Warren and I ever made, meaningful or trivial, was in the end a comparison to an alternative. The true cost of any action is not what it takes; it is what you give up by taking it. And in a world where you have choices — capital to deploy, hours to spend, people to hire — the only cost that matters is the alternative you did not take.

Most founders never make the comparison explicit. They evaluate opportunities in isolation, ask "is this good," answer yes, and proceed. That is not a decision. That is a drift dressed in language. The discipline is to ask, before any yes, what is the next-best use of this capital, this time, this attention? Name it. Specifically. Not a category — an actual alternative. Then, and only then, is the yes real. If the answer to "is this better than my next-best alternative" is no, you have just prevented a stupid decision. If it is yes, you have strengthened your conviction in the one you are making. Either way, the comparison is the whole value.

If you actually figure out how many decisions were made in the history of Berkshire Hathaway that were meaningful, it wasn't very many per year. The normal human allotment is not many great opportunities per lifetime. When one comes, you have to be able to see it clearly against the alternatives and commit hard. The founders who spread themselves across ten mediocre bets because each one was "good in isolation" are the ones who end up with nothing. The ones who compared ruthlessly and concentrated — they are the ones who got rich. That is not courage. It is arithmetic.

## How to Apply

1. **For any meaningful decision, name three realistic alternatives in writing.** Not "we could do other things" — name them. If you want to spend $5M building a new product line, name the three best alternative uses of $5M you have on the table. Hire ten senior engineers for the core product. Acquire the small competitor. Pay down venture debt. Whatever. Write them down specifically. If you cannot write three, you haven't considered the decision; you have fallen in love with the first option and called it strategy.

2. **Compare the decision to the realistic best, not to nothing.** The test is not "is this good." The test is "is this better than my best named alternative." This reframe alone kills half the bad decisions, because the decision that looked great in isolation turns out to be worse than doubling down on what already works.

3. **Price founder time at the right number, which is very high.** Every hour the founder spends on a project is an hour not spent on the three things only the founder can do — setting direction, recruiting A-players, and solving the hardest customer and product problems. If you're spending founder hours on work an ops person can do for $100/hour, the opportunity cost is not the $100. It is the direction-setting that didn't happen that week, the A-player who wasn't recruited, the customer problem that went unsolved. Price accordingly.

4. **Apply the fresh-start test to existing commitments.** For every project, every person, every investment, ask: if we were starting today, knowing what we know now, would we commit these resources to this? If no, the sunk cost is gone and the opportunity cost of continuing is what you're not doing with the freed resources. Stop, and redeploy. The sunk costs cannot be recovered. The opportunity cost of clinging to them can be.

5. **Concentrate when the comparison is clear.** If one opportunity is dramatically better than your next-best alternative, don't hedge by also doing the second-best thing. That is the spread-yourself-thin failure mode. Berkshire's greatest returns came from concentrated bets where the alternatives didn't come close. The discipline is to see the difference and act on it, not to diversify out of fear.

6. **Apply it to fundraising.** Every hour of founder time raising capital is an hour not building the product or recruiting the team. Raise in chunks that give you long stretches to operate. Don't optimize the round to the last penny if it costs another three months of founder attention. Sometimes the five-point-lower valuation that closes this week is worth the three months you reclaim — and sometimes it isn't. But you cannot judge without pricing the opportunity cost of your own time.

7. **Apply it to hiring.** The question is not "can this person do the job." It is "given this comp, is this the best person I can plausibly get in the time window I have?" If a better candidate is one month away, the opportunity cost of hiring the merely-okay one now is every month this role is filled by someone worse than the person you could have hired. Over a three-year tenure, that difference is enormous.

## Examples

**Situation:** Berkshire holds a large position in a wonderful business whose price has become expensive. A conventional manager would sell and redeploy. Munger and Buffett often don't.
**Application:** The opportunity cost analysis includes tax friction. Selling a great long-held position triggers a large capital gains tax. The redeployed capital must therefore earn significantly more, net of tax, than the existing holding earns, to make the trade worthwhile. Once you do the math honestly, most candidate redeployments don't clear the bar.
**Result:** We hold in certain of these positions when, normally, we wouldn't buy a new position. Practically everybody does that. The apparent inconsistency dissolves once you price the opportunity cost correctly. The worst thing you can do is sell a compounding position to buy something mediocre because "it's overvalued." The tax drag plus the mediocrity is a double loss.

**Situation:** A founder asks whether to pursue a new enterprise market adjacent to her core SMB business. The CFO's model shows a positive NPV.
**Application:** Positive NPV in isolation is the wrong test. The opportunity cost is the resources — engineering capacity, founder attention, sales capacity — that would otherwise go to deepening the SMB position where the company already has an edge. Name the three best alternatives for those same resources: build the feature that the top ten SMB customers are begging for; hire two senior engineers to reduce the 18-month feature backlog; acquire the small competitor who is picking off your SMB accounts. Now compare.
**Result:** Once the comparison is explicit, the enterprise expansion usually loses, because it requires the founder's scarcest resource (attention) and deploys it outside her circle of competence, while the alternatives compound an existing advantage. The positive NPV was real; the opportunity cost was bigger. Drop the enterprise play. Double down. That is the decision the numbers were trying to tell you, once you asked them the right question.

**Situation:** A CEO is considering a bolt-on acquisition. The banker has presented it as a clear value-add.
**Application:** Run the opportunity cost. The $50M purchase price could buy back stock at a discount to intrinsic value, hire 40 senior engineers, pay off the line of credit, or fund three years of aggressive R&D in the core product. Name those alternatives. Compare. Also audit the banker's incentive — he gets paid when the deal closes, not when it creates value three years later. His confidence should be weighted accordingly.
**Result:** Most bolt-on acquisitions fail the opportunity cost test once you actually run it. The acquirer's core business is almost always a better use of the capital, because that is the business you understand best, and the one where your edge is real. Integration risk is high. The banker's enthusiasm is informative only about the banker, not about the deal. Pass, in nine cases out of ten.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Evaluate opportunities in isolation.
**Why:** "Is this good" is a trap. Nearly everything looks good in isolation, because the brain manufactures the reasons. The useful question is always "better than what?" If you cannot name the what, you have not made a decision, you have made an impulse.

**Don't:** Compare to imaginary perfect alternatives.
**Why:** "We could theoretically do something even better" is an alibi for never choosing. The real comparison is to the actual next-best realistic alternative on your table — the one you would do if you didn't do this. Imaginary ideals make every real option look inadequate and ensure you do nothing.

**Don't:** Ignore the opportunity cost of founder attention.
**Why:** Capital is usually the lesser constraint. Founder attention is the scarcest input in the building, and the one most often spent without an explicit comparison. If you're doing work a $150K ops person could do, the cost is not the $150K you're saving. It is the strategic work you are not doing that week. Price accordingly and delegate.

**Don't:** Refuse to kill projects because of sunk cost.
**Why:** The sunk cost is gone. The opportunity cost of continuing is what you're not doing with the freed resources. The fresh-start test — if we weren't already doing this, would we start it today — will tell you. The sunk cost fallacy is one of the most common stupidities founders commit, and opportunity cost is the direct antidote. Write the list. Free the resources. Redeploy.

**Don't:** Use opportunity cost analysis to stall forever.
**Why:** There is a stopping rule. Name three realistic alternatives, compare, decide, move. The founder who adds a fourth and fifth and sixth theoretical alternative is not being rigorous; he is hiding from commitment. The comparison is a tool for action, not a substitute for it.

The real cost of every decision is the best alternative you did not take. Name it. Compare. Decide. That's the whole thing.
