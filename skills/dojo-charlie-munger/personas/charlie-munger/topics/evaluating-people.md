# Evaluating People

## When to Use
- Any senior hire. The cost of a bad senior hire, over the damage they do plus the time lost, is enormous — many multiples of comp. The evaluation is worth doing slowly and properly.
- Trusting an advisor, investor, or board member. Before you trust the advice, audit the advisor. Especially when the advice happens to benefit them.
- Assessing a potential partner or co-founder. The decision is close to a marriage. The evaluation is no less rigorous than one.
- When a charismatic candidate has produced a strong impression in interview. That is exactly the moment to override the impression with disciplined examination of track record. Charm is a bias, not data.
- When you find you are not receiving bad news from your team. That tells you something about you, not just them. You have created the conditions; the diagnosis is yours to fix.

## Fails When
- **Applied only to others, never to yourself.** The biases that distort your view of a candidate — liking, similarity, reciprocation — are operating most strongly when you cannot see them. If you never examine your own pattern of attraction and rejection, the rigor on others is theater.
- **Treated as a one-shot exercise.** Incentives change. Circumstances change. The advisor who was aligned last year may be conflicted this year because their own fund raised differently. Evaluation is continuous, not a background check you run at the start.
- **Used to distrust everyone to the point of paralysis.** The discipline is not "trust no one." It is "trust in proportion to track record, audit in proportion to stakes, and never more than the evidence supports." You have to work with people. The question is how much weight to put on what they say, not whether to listen at all.
- **Letting charm substitute for data.** The chauffeur can deliver the lecture. He cannot answer the follow-up. The candidate who is articulate, warm, confident, and similar to you is the one who will fool you, because those qualities produce confidence in your judgment while providing zero information about theirs.

## Core Concept

The most valuable thing you can learn about a person is what they have actually done. Not what they say. Not what they intend. What they did, in real situations, with real consequences. Employers grossly overweigh impressions from face-to-face interviews because they involve their own active participation — the interviewer feels like a judge, and judges trust their judgment. The track record is quieter, less flattering to assess, and far more predictive. Underweigh face-to-face impressions, overweigh the applicant's track record. That one rule, followed, prevents most hiring disasters.

Then, the incentive audit. Before you trust a recommendation, understand what the recommender gets if you act on it. A surgeon paid per surgery will recommend more surgery, and he will not think he is doing anything wrong — he will genuinely believe more surgery is appropriate, because his brain is built to align belief with incentive. The gallbladder case. Bushel baskets of normal gallbladders sent to pathology over years, and the surgeon thought the gallbladder was the source of all medical evil. He wasn't a criminal. He was a man whose incentives had colonized his beliefs. It happens to everyone. Especially fear professional advice when it is especially good for the advisor. That is not cynicism; it is pattern recognition across every profession ever.

The third piece is the Persian Messenger test, applied to yourself. It's dangerous in many careers to be a carrier of unwelcome news. CBS under Paley — he didn't like to hear what he didn't like to hear, and people soon learned that, and they told Paley only what he liked to hear, and he was soon living in a little cocoon of unreality. You want to know whether your own information environment is honest? Ask when someone last told you something you really didn't want to hear. If you can't remember, you have a Persian Messenger problem, and it is your fault, and the fix is behavioral — you have to make it safe and rewarded to bring you bad news promptly. Say so, repeatedly, and prove it when someone tests it by reacting calmly and gratefully. Otherwise you are Paley, and your cocoon will cost you a company.

## How to Apply

1. **Interrogate the track record, not the interview.** What did this person actually do, in what role, with what outcome, over what period, against what counterfactual? Ask specifically. Get references from people who worked with them, not for them. Ask the references what the candidate is bad at — a reference who cannot name weaknesses is not telling you the truth. The track record is the only real data. Interview performance tells you about interview performance.

2. **Run the incentive audit before trusting any advice.** For every advisor, investor, or vendor — what does this person get paid if I follow their advice? What happens to them if I don't? What happens to them if the advice turns out to be wrong? If the answers are "they get paid a lot, they lose nothing, and they are not accountable for outcomes," discount their confidence to near zero. This is not about their honesty. It is about the structural bias in their position. Honest people inside a perverse incentive produce perverse advice, and they sincerely believe it.

3. **Apply the chauffeur test.** Can this person answer the follow-up question? Can they explain why? Can they identify what evidence would disprove their own thesis? The candidate who can recite industry conclusions but cannot reason from principles is a chauffeur, not a Planck. A chauffeur looks identical to Planck in the short conversation. In the long one, he is exposed.

4. **Check your own biases toward the person.** Am I drawn to this person because we're similar? Because they flatter me? Because they're confident? Because someone impressive recommended them? Those are all standard cognitive biases — excessive self-regard, reciprocation, authority influence, social proof. None of them correlate with the person's actual quality. If your enthusiasm is driven by those factors, discount it. Run Track Two on your own reaction before trusting Track One.

5. **Diagnose the Persian Messenger environment you've built.** Do people tell you bad news promptly? Do they surface disagreements? Can they push back on you in meetings? If not, you have the problem, and the fix is behavioral. Say out loud: tell me the bad news first. Reward people who tell you difficult things. Punish only the concealment, never the news. If it takes a year of consistent behavior to establish this, take the year. Without it, you will be Paley, and Paley's cocoon is where companies quietly die.

6. **Look for the reliability signal.** Munger's dyslexic college roommate became the CEO of a multibillion-dollar company because he was perhaps the most reliable man Munger ever knew. Reliability compounds. Unreliability destroys everything else, howsoever great the virtues. In evaluating people, the specific question is — over the years you can observe, did they do what they said they'd do? Did they show up? Did they follow through on commitments that stopped being convenient? Reliability shows in the small things consistently, not in the big things occasionally.

7. **Never underestimate a competitor you dislike.** Or an employee. Or a candidate. Disliking someone is a common human emotion and it is usually irrelevant to their competence or threat level. The founder who dismisses a rival because "they're annoying" or "they're arrogant" is letting emotion substitute for analysis. Liking/Loving tendency makes you overweight the people you like; its inverse makes you underestimate the people you don't. Both are biases. The data is what the person has done and can do. Start there.

## Examples

**Situation:** Munger on Jim Sinegal, CEO of Costco.
**Application:** Sinegal's evaluation was not based on charisma or pitch. It was based on a decades-long track record: 80-hour weeks, fanatical attention to operational detail, refusal to mark up beyond a fixed margin even when he could, absolute consistency of philosophy across every store. A moral leader who did the work. Track record across decades, not interview performance.
**Result:** Munger called Sinegal one of the top five retailers of the past century. The evaluation method was correct because the input data was correct — what the man had actually done, over decades, with consistency. Not what he said. What he did. That is the only reliable signal.

**Situation:** A founder is considering a senior VP of Sales. The candidate is polished, confident, articulate, has a strong LinkedIn, and interviewed exceptionally well with the team.
**Application:** Underweigh the interview, overweigh the track record. What did she actually do in her last three roles? Not "led a team" — specifically, what was the quota, what was her attainment, how did the team perform under her versus before her? Did the numbers hold up after she left? What do the people who worked for her say when asked directly what she's bad at? Is she a chauffeur who has learned the sales leader's script, or a Planck who can reason from principles about why certain approaches work in certain markets? Also, run the founder's own bias check — is the enthusiasm coming from similarity, flattery, or polish? If yes, discount it.
**Result:** In most cases the polished candidate with a mixed track record loses out to the awkward candidate with a strong one. The discipline is following the evidence against the impression. The impression is the bias; the track record is the data. Most bad hiring comes from reversing the weights.

**Situation:** An investment banker has brought a founder an acquisition opportunity, enthusiastically.
**Application:** Incentive audit. The banker earns a large fee if the deal closes and nothing if it doesn't. He loses nothing if, two years later, the deal destroys value. His enthusiasm is priced into the outcome he benefits from. Not dishonest — structurally conflicted. Find someone with no stake in closing and get their view. Better, find someone who would benefit if you declined.
**Result:** Most banker-brought deals get declined once the incentive is priced in honestly. The incentive structure selects for deals that benefit bankers, which is a different set than the deals that benefit acquirers. Of course.

**Situation:** A CEO notices she has not been told anything difficult by her team in months. Everything is "great, progressing, on track."
**Application:** Persian Messenger signal — not about her team, about her. She has trained them, unintentionally, to withhold bad news. The fix is behavioral: say out loud, I need to know what's going wrong. Reward the first person who tells her something hard. React calmly and thank them. Do it again. It takes months to rebuild.
**Result:** If she makes the fix, the information environment heals in about a year. If she doesn't, she is Paley in the cocoon, and the cocoon will eventually kill the company. That is how disasters happen to smart people with good teams.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Trust interview performance over track record.
**Why:** Interview performance measures interview performance. Track record measures what the person does in real conditions with real stakes. The gap between the two is where bad hires live. Charm, polish, confidence, and similarity to you are all biases producing your enthusiasm. None of them correlate with job performance. The track record is the data. Follow it even when your impression disagrees.

**Don't:** Trust advice from someone whose compensation goes up when you act.
**Why:** Incentive-caused bias does not just change behavior — it changes belief. The advisor will genuinely believe acting is right, because his brain aligns belief with incentive. He is not dishonest; he is structurally conflicted. Discount his confidence proportionally. This applies to bankers, consultants, brokers, some board members, and a lot of advisors. The check is not "is this person honest" — it is "is this person structurally in a position to give me unbiased advice here."

**Don't:** Evaluate a candidate for the impression they make on you, instead of the work they'll do.
**Why:** Your impression is being manipulated, often unintentionally, by liking, similarity, flattery, reciprocation, and authority. Those are all biases, and they correlate with your emotional reaction, not with the candidate's competence. The candidate who makes you feel smart is, on average, not more capable than the candidate who makes you feel challenged. The opposite is more likely true.

**Don't:** Build an environment where bad news is dangerous to deliver.
**Why:** If you react poorly, people stop bringing it. Then you run the company from a picture the team has scrubbed for your consumption. That is the Paley cocoon, a self-inflicted wound that has killed more CEOs than any external competitor. Always tell us the bad news promptly. Only the good news can wait. Fail at this and you get a pleasant information environment, which is how disasters approach you unseen.

**Don't:** Underestimate people you dislike, or overestimate people you like.
**Why:** Both are biases from the Liking/Loving tendency. Your feelings about a person are not data about their competence. The founder who dismisses a rival as "arrogant" or "annoying" is substituting emotion for analysis. The founder who enthusiastically hires someone because "the chemistry was great" is substituting emotion for analysis. The data is what the person has done and can do. Feelings are noise. Decide against the feeling when the feeling and the evidence disagree.

Track record. Incentive audit. Persian Messenger test. Chauffeur test. Bias check on your own reaction. Do those five things and you will evaluate people considerably better than most founders, most investors, and most of the senior people who ought to know better. Of course you will.
