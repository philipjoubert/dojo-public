# Build Your Deterrence

## When to Use
- You're being attacked and debating whether to hit back
- You're a soft target and keep getting hit because people know nothing happens when they do
- You're early in a company's life and haven't yet demonstrated willingness to fight
- A cheap shot just happened and you're instinctively drafting a measured non-response

## Fails When
- You retaliate disproportionately to minor stuff — you look unstable, not strong
- You retaliate on claims you can't defend — every failed counter-attack weakens you
- You threaten and don't follow through — empty threats destroy deterrence faster than not threatening
- You pick fights with sympathetic targets — bad optics regardless of truth

## Core Concept

Make attacks expensive. That's the core insight. You can't prevent people from attacking you. But you can make the cost of attacking high enough that they think twice.

Deterrence isn't about winning every fight. It's about creating the conditions where most fights never happen. Countries avoid wars not by being weak and hoping enemies are merciful, but by being strong enough that attacking would be more costly than beneficial. The same logic applies to reputation. If attackers know you'll fight back — publicly, forcefully, personally — they calculate that cost before deciding whether to attack. Some will attack anyway. But many won't. They'll pick easier targets. This is why being visible matters, why showing up matters. Your willingness to fight back is your deterrent. But deterrents only work if they're credible, and credibility requires demonstration.

Palmer Luckey learned this the hard way. In 2016, attacked over political donations, he followed the Facebook strategy — don't engage, don't dignify it, stay quiet. His own summary: "A year of not trimming my hedges turned into a garden of weeds that could never be unpulled. Not in 10 years." A year of silence. A garden of weeds. Then ten years of pulling them out. That's the cost of not building deterrence. The optimal strategy isn't immediate retaliation for every offense — that's too aggressive, you burn resources and relationships. The optimal strategy is "tit for two tats." Cooperate by default. First offense, maybe let it go. Benefit of the doubt. But if they cross you twice, never let it go. You're not paranoid. You're not starting fights. But you have a clear red line, and everyone knows it.

Be spiky. Smooth surfaces are easy to attack. Spiky surfaces are painful to grab. Spiky means being visibly willing to fight back. When someone takes a cheap shot, they get a response they didn't enjoy — not because you're vindictive, but because you're establishing the cost of future attacks. Some fights are costs. Some fights are investments — they pay dividends in future peace. The fight where you visibly and thoroughly dismantled an attacker? Everyone who considered attacking you in the future watched that. They learned something. Building deterrence is the reputational equivalent of installing better locks.

## How to Apply

1. **Respond immediately when it matters.** The broken nose principle applies: reset it now or heal crooked. Every hour a false narrative circulates, it hardens. Speed matters more than perfection.

2. **Fight back publicly.** Private complaints are invisible. Public responses establish deterrence. Others are watching — future potential attackers, your employees, your allies. They all need to see it.

3. **Show up personally.** When attacked, don't hide behind corporate statements. Respond yourself. Put your name on it. Make clear that the attack hit a person, not a logo.

4. **Be specific.** Don't issue vague denials. Point out exactly what's wrong with the attack. Quote it. Correct it. Make the attacker defend their specifics.

5. **Make it memorable.** One forceful response teaches more lessons than ten polite corrections. You're not just winning this fight — you're setting the expected cost of future attacks.

6. **Maintain credibility.** Every threat you don't follow through on weakens deterrence. Better to threaten less and deliver more. If you say there will be consequences, there have to be consequences.

7. **Apply tit for two tats.** First cross, maybe let it go. Second cross, never let it go. The rule is knowable, consistent, and visible.

## Examples

**Situation:** Palmer Luckey, 2016. Attacked over political donations while at Facebook. Advisors said stay quiet.
**Application:** He took the advice. Said nothing. Let it fester.
**Result:** A year of silence turned into a decade of pulling weeds. He eventually built deterrence — built Anduril, became visibly willing to fight — but lost years he didn't need to lose. Anduril's reputation now is strong enough that casual attacks are rare. It took years of fighting to build that.

**Situation:** Substack under organized attack during the Notes launch. Soft response would have invited more pressure.
**Application:** Leadership responded publicly, personally, and forcefully. Named the principle at stake. Refused to issue the corporate pabulum the critics wanted. Pulled independent writers into the coalition.
**Result:** The pressure campaign exhausted itself against a spiky surface. Future activist campaigns pick easier targets.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Retaliate with disproportionate force against trivial criticism. Treating every attack like an existential threat amplifies the attack and makes the attacker look more important than they are.
**Why:** Match response to threat. Major attacks get major responses. Minor attacks get corrections. Over-response tells the world this attack really bothered you, which is its own reward for the attacker.

**Don't:** Announce a red line you can't or won't enforce. "We will not tolerate this" followed by tolerating it is worse than saying nothing at all.
**Why:** Deterrence is made of credibility. Every empty threat teaches future attackers that your red lines are theatrical.

**Don't:** Fight back from a position of anger. Emotional responses look defensive. They give the attacker satisfaction and shift the story from "look what they did" to "look how upset they are."
**Why:** Cold, precise, factual is the register for deterrence. Anger reads as weakness.

**Don't:** Build deterrence only when you're under attack. Starting in the middle of a fight is too late. Your reputation for fighting back is an asset that has to be built over years.
**Why:** Credibility requires demonstration. A deterrent that has never been tested isn't credible. Early, small, visible enforcement builds the reputation that prevents later, large fights.

**Don't:** Confuse deterrence with constant aggression. You're not trying to fight everyone. You're trying to make fighting you expensive enough that most fights never start.
**Why:** Tit for two tats, not tit for tat. Infinite forgiveness invites exploitation; infinite retaliation invites exhaustion. The middle path is the stable one.
