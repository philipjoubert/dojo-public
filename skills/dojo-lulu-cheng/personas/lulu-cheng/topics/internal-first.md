# Internal First

## When to Use
- You're about to make a major announcement (good news, bad news, any news)
- Crisis has hit and you're deciding who learns what, when
- Layoffs, leadership transition, or strategic pivot is coming
- News is going to leak anyway and you need to control the sequence

## Fails When
- You tell employees first via a committee-written email that has no conviction
- You inform employees but managers aren't equipped to answer the questions that follow
- You treat "internal first" as a paperwork step instead of actual communication
- You assume employees won't find out — they always find out

## Core Concept

Internal should always come first.

Even if you're not in a crisis. Even for everyday small announcements. Tell your employees before you tell the world. They deserve it. This isn't just a nice gesture. It's strategic necessity.

External crisis is inconvenient. A bad news cycle. Negative press. Public criticism. You can survive it. Internal crisis is existential. When you see companies fall apart and unravel, when CEOs get unseated, when organizations implode — it's almost always because of internal crisis, not external. The board turns on the CEO. Key employees walk. Trust collapses inside the organization. These are the death spirals. External attacks rarely kill companies. Internal decay kills companies all the time. This is why internal communication matters more than external. Get it wrong and your external crisis becomes an internal one. Get it right and your internal cohesion helps you weather any external storm.

Employees hear from you before they hear from anyone else. If you're making an announcement, employees should know before the press. If there's bad news, employees should know before Twitter. If there's a crisis, employees should know before the public statement. Why this matters: they signed up. Your employees made a choice to join. They're invested. They deserve the respect of hearing news from you, not from strangers. Information asymmetry is toxic — when employees learn company news from the press, they feel like outsiders, they start wondering what else they don't know, trust erodes. And they're your first line of defense: when employees are informed and aligned, they become ambassadors. When they're blindsided, they become critics — or worse, sources for journalists.

The leaks objection — "but if we tell employees first, won't it leak?" Maybe. Consider the alternative. You don't tell employees. The news comes out anyway (it always does). Now employees are surprised AND feel like leadership doesn't trust them. You've lost both ways. If you have a leak problem, you have a trust problem. Fixing leaks requires fixing trust, not information quarantine. External announcements leak too — journalists talk to competitors, read SEC filings, piece things together. The idea that you can perfectly control information flow is fantasy. Better to have informed, trusting employees who might occasionally leak than uninformed, resentful employees who definitely will.

## How to Apply

1. **Sequence: internal → external, every time.**
   - Step 1: Internal communication goes out (email from CEO, all-hands if big enough, manager talking points).
   - Step 2: Press embargo lifts / public announcement. Employees have context, are prepared for questions, know the company line.
   - Step 3: External follow-up. Employees engage as informed advocates.

2. **The gap between internal and external can be short.** Even an hour is enough. The point is sequence, not duration.

3. **Equip managers.** When big news breaks, employees turn to their direct manager first. "What does this mean for our team? Should I be worried? What's really going on?" If managers aren't equipped, the information vacuum fills with speculation. Give managers context, talking points, predictable-question answers, and clear guidance on what they can and can't say.

4. **Match format to stakes.** Big announcements: CEO email + all-hands + manager cascade. Routine updates: regular cadence (weekly email, monthly all-hands). Bad news: honest communication from the appropriate leader, direct and accountable, forward-looking.

5. **Be honest, not sugar-coated.** Employees can smell bullshit. If leadership consistently sugarcoats bad news, employees learn not to trust good news either. Every communication becomes suspect. Credibility compounds. Dishonesty compounds too, in the opposite direction.

6. **In crisis, over-communicate internally.** Employees will forgive imperfect information. They won't forgive being left in the dark. Share what's known, what isn't, what the company position is, what employees should say if asked, where to direct inquiries, when they'll get updates.

7. **Build listening in too.** Internal comms goes both ways. Q&A at all-hands (real Q&A, not softball). Anonymous surveys. Skip-level meetings. Open-door policies that are actually open.

## Examples

**Situation:** Company about to announce a strategic pivot that will affect team assignments. Team debates whether to do the press release first so the narrative is set, then tell employees.
**Application:** Wrong sequence. Internal first. Even if internal is 30 minutes before external. The sequence matters more than the duration.
**Result:** Employees hear from the CEO, not from TechCrunch. Managers can answer questions because they got the briefing. The company speaks with one voice externally because internal alignment exists.

**Situation:** Layoffs. The team plans a polished external statement timed with the internal email. Internal email is committee-written, vague, uses passive voice.
**Application:** Fail. The internal email is the most important communication of the entire event. It should be from the CEO, in first person, direct about what happened, clear about who is affected, specific about what comes next. Not "difficult decisions were made."
**Result:** How you communicate layoffs internally determines whether the team that remains stays or walks. The external statement matters less than the internal one.

**Situation:** A crisis breaks (product failure, executive misconduct, security incident). The instinct is to get the external statement right first.
**Application:** PATSLF: People first. Before anything else, your employees need to know what's going on and that leadership is handling it. Internal comms goes out, then external.
**Result:** Employees are prepared. They're not reading about their own company on Twitter. They become allies in handling the external storm, not another surprise leadership has to manage.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Delay internal communication because you're still perfecting the external statement. The external statement isn't ready until employees are informed.
**Why:** Employees finding out from the press is an internal crisis you'll be cleaning up for months. The polish on your external statement can't justify that cost.

**Don't:** Hide behind "we can't tell employees because it might leak." If you can't trust your employees with information, you have a trust problem, not a leak problem.
**Why:** Treating your team as a leak risk trains them to act like one. Trust has to flow somewhere, and if it doesn't flow to employees, it doesn't compound anywhere.

**Don't:** Write the internal communication in the same committee-approved voice as the press release. Employees can smell the pablum. Internal communication should feel like it came from a human.
**Why:** Internal first fails if "internal" means "same corporate memo, just sent to a different list." The voice matters as much as the sequence.

**Don't:** Equip employees with facts but not narrative. If they don't understand the master narrative, they can't represent the company accurately in their own conversations.
**Why:** Every customer interaction, every conference chat, every social post is a communication touchpoint. Unaligned employees create message chaos. Aligned employees amplify.

**Don't:** Confuse "internal first" with "internal only." Some news must also go external. The sequence is internal then external. Not internal instead of external.
**Why:** Some announcements need public distribution. The rule is order, not exclusivity. Skipping the external is a different failure mode.

**Don't:** Use internal channels to launder bad news. Employees notice when the internal version is sanitized compared to what they read elsewhere. Consistency between internal and external — adjusted for context — is load-bearing.
**Why:** If the internal version and the external version contradict, one of them is a lie. Your team will discover which one, and they'll act on that discovery.
