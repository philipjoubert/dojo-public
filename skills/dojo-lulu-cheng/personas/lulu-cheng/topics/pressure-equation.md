# The Pressure Equation

## When to Use
- You're under attack and the pressure feels unbearable because it's concentrated
- You're going on offense against a bad actor and want maximum impact
- You're tempted to complain broadly about "the media" or "regulators"
- You're deciding whether this fight should be about you or about a principle

## Fails When
- The coalition you claim to represent doesn't actually share your cause — you look like you're deflecting
- Your narrow target isn't actually responsible — the accusation doesn't stick
- You can't prove your specific claim — you go from underdog to liar
- You're attacking a sympathetic target — even true accusations look like bullying
- You're guilty — physics can't save you from bad facts

## Core Concept

P = F/A. Pressure equals force divided by area.

It's physics, but it's also strategy. When you're under attack, you can't change the force coming at you. Someone has decided to hit you with some amount of energy and attention. That's fixed. But you can change the surface area over which that force is distributed. And that changes the pressure you experience. The same principle works in reverse when you're on offense. You can't always increase your force. But you can concentrate it on a smaller target. This simple equation governs how public fights work. Understand it, and you have a framework for both defense and offense.

**Defense: Spread the surface area.** When you're attacked, your instinct is to defend yourself. This is natural but often wrong. Better: don't defend yourself. Expand the coalition being attacked. If someone attacks you personally, the whole weight of the attack lands on you alone. Small surface area. High pressure. But if you reframe the attack as hitting a larger group — a group that includes you — the same force distributes across more people. "You're not attacking me. You're attacking all of us." When Substack came under attack over content moderation, they didn't defend Substack as a company. They expanded: "You're not attacking Substack. You're attacking all independent writers asserting freedom of expression." Independent writers have audiences. Independent writers care about their platform. Independent writers showed up to defend Substack, not because they're Substack employees, but because they're defending their own interests. The force didn't change. The surface area expanded dramatically. The pressure became manageable.

**Offense: Narrow the surface area.** On offense, you want maximum pressure — maximum force on minimum surface area. This is why broad complaints fail. "The media is biased against us" hits every journalist. Enormous surface area. Minimal pressure on any individual. But "this specific reporter has a vendetta because their cousin runs a competitor" — that's narrow. Specific person, specific accusation. Tiny surface area. Intense pressure. Broad accusations sound like paranoia. "The media" isn't a person. "The establishment" isn't a person. When you complain about them, you sound like someone wearing a tinfoil hat. Specific accusations sound like credible claims. A named person, a specific action, documented evidence. That's something people can evaluate. That's something that creates accountability.

## How to Apply

**For defense:**

1. **Identify the principle at stake.** Not "what are they saying about me" but "what shared value does this attack threaten?"
2. **Identify the coalition that genuinely shares that value.** Independent writers? Crypto users? Defense contractors? Entrepreneurs in your category?
3. **Reframe the attack as hitting that coalition.** "You're not attacking me, you're attacking all of us."
4. **Activate the coalition.** They'll show up if the framing is honest. Manufactured coalitions collapse fast.
5. **Pass the test:** Would the coalition defend themselves even if you didn't ask? If yes, real coalition. If no, you're deflecting.

**For offense:**

1. **Narrow to a specific named person or organization.** Not "the media" — a specific reporter. Not "regulators" — a specific official at a specific agency.
2. **Document the specific transgression.** Quote them. Cite the date. Produce the evidence.
3. **Make it credible to outside observers.** Someone neutral should be able to read your accusation and verify it.
4. **Force them to defend their specifics.** Broad defenses won't work against narrow accusations.

## Examples

**Situation:** Substack launches Notes. Critics claim the platform is dangerous. Advertisers pressured to pull out. Coordinated campaign.
**Application:** Substack expanded surface area. "You're not attacking Substack. You're attacking all independent writers asserting freedom of expression." Writers on the platform, unaffiliated allies, free-expression organizations all showed up.
**Result:** The same force distributed over many surfaces. Pressure dropped. The campaign dissipated because it couldn't concentrate on a single target.

**Situation:** Coinbase fighting the SEC. They could have complained about "regulators" in the abstract. Too broad — every regulator could say "that's not us."
**Application:** Coinbase narrowed to specific actors, specific actions, specific failures. Named names. Documented specifics.
**Result:** The pressure concentrated on specific people who had to defend their specific conduct. The accusations stuck because they were credible and narrow. "Regulators are unfair" is paranoia. "This specific commissioner did this specific thing on this date" is accountability.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Claim a coalition that doesn't exist. If you frame an attack as hitting a larger group, but that group doesn't actually feel attacked, the reframe fails. You look like you're deflecting — trying to hide behind others.
**Why:** The test is: would the coalition members defend themselves even if you didn't ask? If no, you don't have a coalition. You have a prop.

**Don't:** Complain about "the media" or "the establishment" when on offense. You sound like a conspiracy theorist and the attack dissipates across too large a surface.
**Why:** Broad targets mean no one feels personally responsible. The pressure is zero. You also signal paranoia, which costs you credibility.

**Don't:** Narrow to a target you can't back up with evidence. A specific accusation that falls apart under scrutiny destroys you faster than vague grievance ever would.
**Why:** The equation tells you how to allocate pressure. Credibility determines whether you have any pressure to allocate. Narrowing to a false accusation just makes you a liar.

**Don't:** Pick a sympathetic target even if you're right. Bad optics regardless of truth.
**Why:** Audiences read the emotional frame before they read the facts. Attacking a whistleblower, a junior employee, or a beloved figure — even with airtight evidence — makes you the villain.

**Don't:** Mix the two modes. "You're attacking all of us, and also this one guy specifically" confuses the signal.
**Why:** Defense is spread. Offense is narrow. Pick the mode and commit. Blending dilutes both.
