# The Venn Diagram (Find the Overlap)

## When to Use
- You're crafting a message and it feels self-interested or generic
- Your audience is busy and you're competing with a thousand other things for their attention
- You want to find the "cultural erogenous zones" that make your audience lean in
- You're entering a new space where no one already cares about your category

## Fails When
- You start from your circle and hope people latch on — most won't
- You start from their circle with no path back to yours — you entertain, they move on
- You force overlap that doesn't exist — audiences sense contrived relevance
- You confuse overlap with pandering — chasing trending topics without genuine connection

## Core Concept

Draw two circles. One contains things on YOUR mind — what you want to say. The other contains things on THEIR mind — what your audience is already thinking about.

Don't tell the story in your circle. That's the story only you care about. It's hard to get people to care about your quarterly roadmap, your internal KPIs, or your product features. You've been living in your circle for months. Your audience hasn't. What feels interesting to you is noise to them.

Don't tell the story in their circle either. That's the story they already know. You get nothing from it. If you just talk about the current thing, the trending topic, the thing everyone is already discussing — you're repeating the discourse back at them. You add no signal and gain no ground.

Tell the story in the overlap. Where what you want to say intersects with what they already care about. This overlap is your gateway. It's the API into their mind. You start there — with something they already care about — and you walk them into your territory. The overlap is also where the "cultural erogenous zones" live — the specific topics, references, and frames that trigger a strong, specific response from your particular audience. Figure out what those zones are for the people you need, and enter through them.

Starting with what YOU want to say and hoping someone latches on is the common mistake. It rarely works. People are buried in content. They don't have bandwidth for your unsolicited message. Starting with what THEY care about, and connecting it to what you need them to know — that's the move.

## How to Apply

1. **Map their circle.** What is your audience actually thinking about right now? What's on their mind? What frustrations, aspirations, debates, jokes, and references do they engage with? This requires actually paying attention to them, not imagining them.

2. **Map your circle.** What do you need them to understand? What belief do you need to instill? What action do you need from them? Be specific — "we need talented engineers to apply" is better than "we need to be known."

3. **Find the honest overlap.** Where does a thing in their circle genuinely touch a thing in yours? The key word is *honest*. If you have to stretch to connect the two, audiences will feel the stretch.

4. **Enter through the overlap.** Lead with their concern. Build the bridge. Walk them into your territory. Don't open with "here's what we do" — open with "here's the thing you've been frustrated about, and here's an angle on it."

5. **Identify cultural erogenous zones.** For any given audience, there are specific topics that reliably trigger strong engagement. For crypto users, it might be regulatory overreach. For defense tech engineers, it might be procurement dysfunction. For independent writers, it's platform censorship. Find the zones that matter for your audience and speak from them.

6. **Test with the virality question.** Can you describe this in a way that someone hearing it would tell their family about it over dinner? Would they know how to describe it? Would they want to bring it up? If not, you failed the test. You reach however many people you reach with the first volley, then no legs.

## Examples

**Situation:** Barbie didn't ask people to talk about the movie. "Watch Barbie" lives entirely in Warner Bros' circle. Nobody cares.
**Application:** They found the overlap between what they wanted (awareness for the movie) and what the audience cared about (themselves — their identities, their humor, their aesthetic). They gave people vehicles to express themselves using Barbie imagery. "This Barbie is a lawyer." "This Barbie is an architect."
**Result:** People weren't promoting Barbie. They were expressing their own identities. The overlap — self-expression using Barbie as canvas — generated the distribution. Number one topic people like to talk about: themselves. Number last: products and services. Barbie weaponized that.

**Situation:** A B2B company wants to hire engineers and keeps publishing posts about its internal engineering culture, its tech stack, its hiring philosophy. The posts sit in the company's own circle.
**Application:** Move to the overlap. What do target engineers actually think about? Interesting problems. Smart colleagues. Frustration with BigCo bureaucracy. Concrete technical challenges. Write about the specific technical problem you solved last month, in a way that engineers would forward to other engineers.
**Result:** The post reaches engineers through engineers. The company gets recruiting attention without having to ask for it. Entered through the overlap.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Stay in your own circle and hope someone latches on. The circle of things on YOUR mind is the circle you live in. It's obvious and boring to everyone else.
**Why:** Audiences are buried in content. They have no bandwidth for unsolicited messages about your agenda. Your stuff only breaks through when it hooks into something they're already thinking about.

**Don't:** Stay in their circle with no path back to yours. Commenting on the trending topic without connecting to what you're building just makes you another voice in the discourse.
**Why:** You spend the attention budget with no conversion. You become known for takes instead of your actual work.

**Don't:** Manufacture fake overlap by chasing trending topics. Tying your product to every news cycle reads as opportunism.
**Why:** Audiences sense contrived relevance. Forced overlap is worse than no overlap — it actively costs you credibility.

**Don't:** Assume the overlap is the same for every audience. The cultural erogenous zones for engineers are not the same as for investors, customers, or journalists. Different audiences, different overlaps.
**Why:** One message for all audiences produces pablum. Different messages through the same founder voice, each entering through the right overlap, produces traction.

**Don't:** Treat "find the overlap" as a one-time exercise. The things on their mind change. The topics that hit last year might be dead now.
**Why:** The overlap is dynamic. You have to keep paying attention to your audience to know where the live zones are right now.
