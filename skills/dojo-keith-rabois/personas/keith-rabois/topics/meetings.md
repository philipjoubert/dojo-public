# Meetings

## When to Use
- Every meeting you schedule, you should be able to name which of the four types it is before the invite goes out. If you can't, cancel the meeting.
- Before any decision meeting: decide in advance who the decision-maker is and what the decision mode is — consensus, consultative, or directive. Do not leave that floating.
- When your weekly calendar has more than 15 hours of meetings and you still feel out of sync with the company — you probably have the wrong meetings, not too many.

## Fails When
- **You treat all meetings as equivalent.** The rules for a 1:1 are completely different from the rules for a decision meeting. Same rules for both means at least one is broken.
- **You over-invite.** Doubling the room halves the quality of each person's attention and triples the length of the meeting. Smaller rooms make faster decisions.
- **Pre-reads aren't written or aren't read.** The whole point of a decision meeting is to debate the tradeoffs, not to hear the same status update everyone could have read on their own.
- **You let 1:1s become status updates.** The 1:1 is for the direct report's benefit. If the manager is setting the agenda and running through their checklist, the meeting is inverted.

## Core Concept

There are four types of meetings. Each one has a different purpose and different rules. Confusion between types is the root of most meeting dysfunction.

**1:1s** are pioneered by Andy Grove. The canonical cadence is weekly, every two weeks at the relaxed end. I'd say don't go longer than two weeks. One week is ideal in most companies. The reason the cadence matters: it's why the old adage says you should only have five to seven direct reports. That number derives directly from the one-a-week 1:1 math — at more than seven, you can't fit weekly 1:1s and still get other things done.

The critical design choice: **the agenda is set by the direct report, not the manager.** The one-on-one is primarily for the benefit of the employee. They walk in with the three or four things they want to talk about. Ideally they send bullet points in advance by email so you have time to chew on it rather than winging your responses on the fly. The purpose of the 1:1 is performance guidance and — this is the crucial part — surfacing problems early. The earlier a problem is flagged, the more options exist to fix it. A problem caught in week one has ten options. The same problem caught in quarter two has two options, and they both cost money.

If someone is genuinely high on task-relevant maturity and has been shipping at that level for a long time, you might push the cadence to every other week, possibly once a month. I'd never go beyond once a month.

**Staff meetings** are all senior leaders in one room. The agenda covers topics that affect at least two people on the staff. Decide in advance how decisions will be made. Claire Hughes Johnson has a great talk on this — I'd recommend it. The mode can be democratic, consensus, or autocratic. Pick one and say so before the conversation starts, or you'll have twelve people thinking they're in different meetings.

**Decision meetings** are for major decisions that require real-time disagreement. Two rules here. First, **"Peers Plus One"**: invite the peers who'll execute, plus one level up — the person who holds ultimate responsibility and can break a tie. Without that plus-one, equal-authority peers deadlock. Second, **decide who the decision-maker is BEFORE the meeting starts.** Two modes. **Position-based:** the most senior person decides. **Knowledge-based:** the person closest to the work decides. Ideally decisions are made at the lowest competent level and only escalate when senior expertise is genuinely needed. Overruling junior people by position alone burns social capital. Done repeatedly without explanation, it drives away your best people.

**Operating reviews** are the metrics-driven cadence. Functional units present progress; peers from other groups give feedback. These act as mini-board meetings and should run at least quarterly for major teams. Weekly or biweekly metrics reviews for fast-moving surfaces. The format is: the team presents its one primary metric plus its paired counter-metric, its trajectory, what moved, what didn't, and the two or three experiments running next.

Across all four types, a few design principles. **Pre-reads replace status updates.** A pre-read — a Bezos-style memo or a deck sent 24 hours before — means the meeting itself is for debate, not for recitation. **The agenda lists decisions to be made, not topics to be discussed.** "Discuss the Q3 plan" is not an agenda item. "Approve Q3 hiring plan of X headcount" is. **Default-invite fewer people.** Doubling the room halves each person's attention. Smaller rooms produce faster and better decisions.

## How to Apply

1. **Classify every meeting before scheduling it.** 1:1, staff, decision, or operating review. Different rules, different prep, different outcome. If you can't name the type, cancel the meeting.

2. **For 1:1s, make the agenda the report's responsibility.** Tell them explicitly: this meeting is for you, you set the agenda, send bullet points the night before, bring the problems you haven't told anyone about yet. Keep reports to seven or fewer so weekly cadence is possible.

3. **For decision meetings, pre-declare the decision mode and the decider.** Send the invite with: "Decision to be made: X. Decider: Y. Mode: consultative." If you can't write that line, you're not ready for the meeting.

4. **Apply Peers Plus One.** Invite the peers who will execute plus one person one level up who can break ties and ensure follow-through. Not the whole exec team. Not the board. The operating group plus a decider.

5. **Require pre-reads for anything beyond a 1:1.** If there's nothing in writing before the meeting, the first 20 minutes of the meeting will be a status recap. That's theater. Send the memo 24 hours in advance. Meeting starts with "what do you disagree with," not "here's what we did."

6. **Write notes for every meeting with more than two people and send them to the company alias.** This scales transparency. People not in the meeting feel included. Decisions are documented. The side effect: it forces the meeting to actually make decisions, because something has to be written.

## Examples

**Situation:** You have 12 direct reports and can't fit weekly 1:1s.
**Application:** You have too many reports. The 5-to-7 rule exists precisely because weekly 1:1s are non-negotiable. Reorganize. Either promote someone to take half your reports, or delegate the ones on high task-relevant maturity to monthly cadence while you hold weekly cadence on the others — but only as a bridge while you fix the org shape.
**Result:** Problems get surfaced weekly, not quarterly. The team's top 3 issues don't compound into fire drills.

**Situation:** A decision meeting is scheduled with 14 people in the room. No one has been told who's deciding. Everyone brings a deck.
**Application:** Cancel. Reschedule with the four people who will actually execute plus the one person one level up who can break ties. Send pre-read 24 hours in advance. Declare decision mode and decider in the invite.
**Result:** Decision made in 30 minutes instead of 90. Follow-through happens because the decider is clear. The other nine people get the notes and stay informed without being in the room.

**Situation:** Your weekly product review feels like status-update theater. Everyone shows up, presents a slide, nothing is decided.
**Application:** This is an operating review in format, but it's being run like a staff meeting. Redesign. Require a written pre-read 24 hours in advance. The meeting agenda becomes "what do you disagree with in the pre-read" and "what decision do we need to make this week." Teams present exceptions only, not routine.
**Result:** The meeting shrinks from 90 minutes to 45. Decisions per meeting roughly triples. The team stops performing for each other and starts debating.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run 1:1s where the manager sets the agenda.
**Why:** The meeting is inverted. The manager uses it as a status check; the report walks out having not raised the problem they came with. You just wasted a week's worth of problem-catching.

**Don't:** Over-invite the decision meeting to "be inclusive."
**Why:** Inclusion is what the transparent notes alias is for. The decision meeting is for the four or five people who'll execute and the one who'll decide. More people means a longer, worse meeting, and the decision often doesn't survive contact with the room anyway.

**Don't:** Leave the decision-maker ambiguous.
**Why:** Ambiguous decisions don't stick. People relitigate the decision the next week. You end up making the same call three times. Declare the decider up front, declare the mode up front, move on.

**Don't:** Hold status meetings with 12+ people and no pre-read.
**Why:** That's a radio broadcast. No one is learning anything they couldn't have read in an update. You've converted 12 hours of person-time into the equivalent of a one-page memo. Send the memo instead. Meet when there's a decision.

**Don't:** Run operating reviews as vanity presentations.
**Why:** The operating review is a diagnostic, not a marketing exercise. If the team shows up with polished decks celebrating wins, they're not using the meeting to expose the exceptions and the misses. Require a section on what didn't work. Make the missing metrics visible.

**Don't:** Confuse "I asked for input" with "consensus mode."
**Why:** If you're the decider and you ran it as consultative, say so. If you're going to make the call regardless, don't pretend otherwise. People can smell fake consensus and it poisons trust more than directive decisions ever would.

**Don't:** Let meetings expand to fill the invited time.
**Why:** Decide the meeting ends when the decision is made. If the decision takes 12 minutes, end the meeting at minute 12. The 48 minutes you give back to the team are worth more than anything you'd have filled them with.
