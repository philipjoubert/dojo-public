# Culture as Cult

## When to Use
- You're building out the first 20 hires and want to set a cultural foundation that scales.
- You're deciding whether to take a WeWork desk, stay remote, or get your own office.
- You're debating whether to demand in-office, long hours, or explicit intensity norms.
- You're about to hire someone brilliant who doesn't share your core beliefs.
- A candidate is on the fence and you're wondering whether to harder-sell them.

## Fails When
- You try to build a cult around the founder's personality instead of a shared secret about the market. Personality cults don't scale; belief-based cults do.
- You import someone else's culture playbook — Netflix memo, Stripe doc, Amazon leadership principles — instead of deriving yours from the actual contrarian belief at the heart of your company.
- You use "cultural intensity" as cover for tolerating harassment, abuse, or bad behavior. Cult means shared belief, not abuse. Confusing the two kills companies.
- You try to cult-ify a mature, regulated, boring-but-necessary function. Compliance in a bank is not a cult. It's compliance. Don't force it.

## Core Concept

Every successful startup is a cult.

That's Peter's line from Zero to One and he's right. You have a belief about the world that most people think is wrong. That's your secret. The cult is built on the secret. You double down on the secret, you build around the secret, and you only want people who opt into the secret. That's it. That's the whole framework.

At Square the secret was that design was a strategic weapon in payments, an industry where design had never mattered. So we forced every hire — accountants, finance people, legal — to be design-driven. Good luck interviewing accountants and trying to find a design-driven one. We found one, a woman named Lauren, who turned out to be so design-driven she became our best PM and eventually CEO of her own company. We would have missed her if we hadn't applied the cult criteria to an accounting hire. The point: apply the cult criteria to every hire, including the accountant.

You anti-sell to non-believers and you hard-sell to believers. If someone is hesitant, you don't chase. You describe the intensity explicitly. You tell them 996 — 9 a.m. to 9 p.m., six days a week, in the office. You tell them the first-principles beliefs. You make it easy for the wrong people to opt out. Because the wrong person in a cult is more damaging than an empty seat. The right person hears 996 and lights up — Traba's head of finance proactively searched for "American company with 996 culture" before she joined. That's the magnet working. The magnet only works if you say it out loud.

You cannot build a cult in a WeWork. You cannot build a cult remote. Get your own office immediately. Small, crappy, whatever — but yours. Shared space with other startups dilutes the cult with people who don't share the belief. Remote breaks the osmosis that transmits the belief. Undiscovered talent is early-career and learns culture by watching; that does not happen on Zoom. Keep everyone on one floor as long as humanly possible. The moment you split floors, politics creep in. Glass conference rooms, not opaque ones — no secret meetings. Transparency is part of the cult architecture.

Missionaries vs. mercenaries. Mercenaries work for comp. Missionaries work for the belief. You cannot outbid Google for mercenaries, ever. You can outbid Google for missionaries, because Google doesn't have your secret. The entire recruiting advantage of a startup is that it's a cult with a real secret, and the right people will join for less money because they believe the secret. If you dilute the cult, you lose the one advantage you have.

The test: what do you believe about this market, this industry, this approach, that most smart people think is wrong? If you can't answer that in one sentence, you don't have a secret, which means you don't have a cult, which means you're a slightly worse version of the companies you're trying to beat. Find the secret. If you can't find one, don't start the company.

## How to Apply

1. **Find the secret.** One sentence. What do you believe about this market that most smart people think is wrong? If you can't articulate it, stop and find it. No secret, no cult, no advantage.
2. **Write the cult principles explicitly.** Three-to-five beliefs, stated as opinions, not platitudes. "Design is strategy" is a cult principle. "We value excellence" is meaningless.
3. **Apply the cult criteria to every hire.** Including the accountant. Including the office manager. If they can't opt into the core belief, they don't get hired, no matter how good they are at the job.
4. **Get your own office immediately.** Even if it's small and ugly. Not WeWork. Not shared space. Not your apartment past the first six months. You cannot cultivate a cult in someone else's space.
5. **Stay on one floor as long as humanly possible.** Politics enter through floor separation. Maximize the floor plate. Delay the split to the last possible moment.
6. **Use glass walls on conference rooms.** No secret meetings. Transparency is a structural feature of cults that work, not a nice-to-have.
7. **State the intensity norms explicitly.** 996, or whatever your equivalent is. Put it in the job description. Mention it in the first interview. Make it easy for non-believers to self-select out.
8. **Anti-sell the hesitant, hard-sell the believers.** If a candidate is on the fence about whether the mission matters, describe the downsides vividly. Don't chase. If a candidate lights up at the mission, close aggressively, use investors, close this week.
9. **Reinforce the cult through rituals, office design, language.** At PayPal we had internal shorthand, shared references, inside jokes about the secret. That's not cute — that's infrastructure. Rituals compound.
10. **Announce financings primarily to reinforce the cult with prospective hires and their families.** Candidates' spouses Google your company. Give them something to find that makes them nod.
11. **Kill non-believers early.** If someone is in the building and clearly doesn't share the belief, exit them quickly. Every day they stay, the cult dilutes.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is about to hire a head of finance. The candidate is competent, has great references, and would do the job well. But the founder suspects the candidate doesn't really buy the core contrarian belief that drives the company.
**Application:** Pass. This is exactly the Square-accountant test. At Square, the secret was design-driven everything. We forced design-fit on accountants even though it was agonizing to find one. You apply the cult criteria to every hire, especially the boring-seeming ones, because every non-believer in the building dilutes the belief. Keep searching for a believer.
**Result:** You eventually find a head of finance who opted into the cult. She makes finance-team decisions consistent with the rest of the company's beliefs. The competent-but-non-believing candidate would have quietly built a small kingdom of non-believers inside finance.

**Situation:** A Series A founder is debating whether to lock in a WeWork for the next year to save cash. The alternative is a small, crummy private office two blocks over for twice the cost.
**Application:** Private office. Every time. You cannot build a cult sharing space with three other startups who each have their own belief systems and their own intensity norms. Your team will see the other startups leave at 6 p.m. and internalize that as normal. Your office design, your walls, your rituals — none of it is possible in someone else's space. The cost delta is cheap compared to the culture damage.
**Result:** The team develops a distinct identity. The office reflects the cult. New hires feel it on their first visit.

**Situation:** A founder is recruiting a senior engineer. The engineer is on the fence — interested in the mission but hesitant about the hours and the intensity. Keeps asking about work-life balance and remote flexibility.
**Application:** Don't chase. Describe 996 explicitly, or whatever your actual norm is. Tell them the expectation is in-person, intense, missionary-level commitment. If they're the right person, they'll light up — like Traba's head of finance who was actively searching for an American 996 company. If they're not the right person, they'll opt out. Both outcomes are wins. The only losing outcome is softening the pitch to close the hesitant candidate and then having them in the building resenting the intensity.
**Result:** Engineer opts out. Three weeks later, a different engineer applies who was told about the culture by a friend and was specifically seeking the intensity. Closes in five days.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to build a cult without a secret.
**Why:** No secret, no cult. A "cult" with no contrarian belief is just a regular company with more yelling. You need an actual belief about the world that most smart people think is wrong. Find it first.

**Don't:** Work out of a WeWork past the first three months.
**Why:** You cannot cultivate a cult sharing space with other people who don't believe the same things. The cost savings are microscopic compared to the cultural damage. Get your own office even if it's small and ugly.

**Don't:** Build the culture around the founder's personality.
**Why:** Personality cults don't scale. They collapse when the founder is having a bad week. Belief cults scale, because the belief is about the market, not about the person. Cult-of-personality is a failure mode. Cult-of-secret is the real thing.

**Don't:** Soften the intensity pitch to close hesitant candidates.
**Why:** You'll close them and they'll resent the job. They'll leave in six months or, worse, they'll stay and slowly convert the norms toward their own preference. The cult dilutes from inside. Better to lose the hire than to lose the culture.

**Don't:** Apply the cult criteria only to "core" roles and relax it for ops, finance, and admin.
**Why:** The accountant matters. The office manager matters. Every non-believer in the building is a slow leak. Jack was adamant at Square: if the accountant isn't design-driven, we don't hire the accountant. This seems extreme. It's not. It's the whole game.

**Don't:** Hide the intensity norms until the candidate has already accepted.
**Why:** You'll hire someone who will quit in three months when they realize what the job actually is. Anti-sell upfront. Tell them 996 before they sign. Make it easy for non-believers to opt out before they're on payroll.

**Don't:** Confuse cult with abuse.
**Why:** Cult means shared belief and high intensity. It does not mean tolerating harassment, abuse, or bad behavior. People who confuse the two use "cultural intensity" to cover for toxicity and it destroys the real cult. Intensity without the belief is just cruelty.

**Don't:** Go remote for an early-stage company.
**Why:** Undiscovered talent is early-career and learns culture by osmosis. Osmosis does not work on Zoom. Remote-first for an early-stage company is remote-first for the culture to never form. You can add remote flexibility at 200 people. Not at 20.

**Don't:** Split the company across multiple floors before you're forced to.
**Why:** Politics enter through floor separation. The moment you're on two floors, information flow breaks and factions form. Maximize the floor plate. Compress the team. Delay the split until there's literally no other option.
