# Radical Transparency

## When to Use
- From day one of the company. It is vastly harder to retrofit transparency than to build it in.
- When you notice decisions at different levels of the company are not converging — that is an information-asymmetry problem, not a judgment problem.
- When gossip, politics, or "what's going on behind those closed doors" paranoia is showing up. Transparency is the direct antidote.
- When you are scaling past 30 people and people start feeling excluded from meetings they used to be in.

## Fails When
- **You share information you legally cannot share.** Public company insider-trading windows are real. Pending personnel decisions are real. Active M&A conversations are real. Draw those lines with lawyers, then push back on the lawyers for everything else. Peter did exactly that at PayPal.
- **You dump data without framing.** Transparency means everyone has the same context, not that everyone has the same inbox. A raw data dump with no interpretation is worse than useless — it generates confusion, not alignment.
- **You have toxic actors on the team.** If you have people who will leak to the press or weaponize information against colleagues, fix the personnel issue first. Transparency on top of a bad team amplifies the dysfunction.

## Core Concept

I believe in radical transparency. Most people talk about it. When push comes to shove, very few actually adhere to it. At Square we went further than almost anyone — and I still think we could have pushed the envelope more.

The logic is mechanical. If you want people to make good decisions without routing through you, they need the same context you have. Context is not a vibe. Context is every single employee having the same information that you have. If the people on your team don't have the same information, they will make different decisions than you would, and then you will be frustrated — and they will be frustrated that you are frustrated. The filtered-up report, the exec-only dashboard, the one-way all-hands — these are all ways of pretending to share information while actually gatekeeping it.

At Square we did this at the floor level. First: every single employee had access to every dashboard and every metric. Second: after every board meeting, I went on stage and presented the entire board deck slide by slide to the whole company. You can strip out the option-grant slide if you really must. Everything else — strategy, financials, board feedback — goes on stage. Third: for every meeting with more than two people, someone wrote notes and sent them to an alias that went to the entire company. As the company added employees, they never felt excluded from meetings they weren't in. Fourth: every conference room had glass walls. As soon as you have opaque walls, people wonder what's going on. If people can see who's in the meeting and who's meeting with whom, they don't worry nearly as much about what's being said. Fifth: we kept everyone on the same floor as long as possible. As soon as you split floors, politics start creeping in.

There is a second-order benefit that is arguably bigger than the decision-quality benefit. Radical transparency inculcates a true ownership mentality. People talk about "ownership mentality" as a slogan. If you actually want it, you have to act like the people you work with are owners, which means they get the owner's information. The same things the board gets, the employees get.

You can push this further than we did. Patrick Collison ran an email-transparency experiment at Stripe where internal email was visible across the company. Steve Jobs tried transparent compensation at NeXT — two bands, you were either junior-band or senior-band, and that was it. The Sports World is a live proof: every pro athlete's salary is public, and teams still collaborate and win championships. The argument that compensation must be hidden to preserve teamwork doesn't survive contact with reality in other domains where collaboration is existential.

## How to Apply

1. **Present the full board deck to the whole company after every board meeting.** Strip compensation if you must. Leave every other slide intact — strategy, financials, metric dashboards, risks, board feedback. Do this within days, not weeks. The closer to the board meeting, the higher the fidelity.

2. **Institute a meeting-notes alias.** Any meeting with more than two people generates notes. Those notes go to an alias that every employee is on. This scales communication without scaling meeting-room capacity. It is the cheapest transparency intervention there is.

3. **Design the physical space for transparency.** Glass conference rooms. One floor as long as possible. Open office with sight lines. Design is culture. What people see every day shapes how they behave. You cannot have a "no secrets" culture with a warren of closed doors.

4. **Put every metric that matters on a dashboard every employee can see.** Not an exec dashboard. The same dashboard. Build it in a way that is intuitive for customer support, not just for you. If customer support cannot navigate it, the whole company is not using it.

5. **Explicitly teach what not to leak.** Jack's opening line at Square: this level of openness is a privilege, and it depends on everyone keeping it confidential. If you leak, you are threatening your colleagues' work and their families' futures. In five years of this model at Square, we had essentially no employee leaks. The leaks that did happen traced back to investors, not employees.

6. **Fight the lawyers for everything non-essential.** When Peter wanted to keep sharing PayPal's spreadsheet post-IPO, the lawyers tried to shut it down for insider-trading reasons. Peter pushed back hard. We compromised: we kept all the metrics, stripped the revenue line (though of course you could calculate it). Default to transparency. Strip only what regulation genuinely requires.

## Examples

**Situation:** At Square, Starbucks came in on a Friday afternoon to negotiate the partnership. The Friday all-hands happened that evening. The Starbucks team was still in the conference room.
**Application:** Jack went on stage an hour after they walked out and told the entire company: we are negotiating this partnership with Starbucks, here are the terms we want, here's where we are. I remember thinking: oh shoot, this better work out, I have to sign this thing now.
**Result:** The deal closed. The company knew the big moves as they happened. No one found out about the Starbucks partnership from TechCrunch. Ownership mentality reinforces itself when you act like owners matter.

**Situation:** PayPal was going public. Lawyers wanted to stop the internal spreadsheet practice because of insider-trading exposure.
**Application:** Peter fought back. We kept the metrics, stripped the explicit revenue number (though anyone with basic math could reconstruct it). The company kept its information culture intact through the IPO transition.
**Result:** The company continued making aligned decisions at scale. The lawyers didn't like it, but inside-trading concerns were correctly scoped to one line, not used to shut down the whole practice.

**Situation:** You're a 40-person startup and the growing exec team has started having off-sites you're not invited to if you're below VP level.
**Application:** Write notes. Every off-site with more than two people generates notes that hit the company alias within 24 hours. Topics discussed. Decisions made. Follow-ups. The off-site itself may be closed. The output is public.
**Result:** The team below VP level does not invent conspiracy theories about what is happening above them. They see the output. They feel included in the direction without needing to be in the room.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Have "strategy" conversations in rooms with opaque walls without ever sharing the output.
**Why:** The team assumes the worst. Paranoia fills the vacuum. Your best people spend cycles speculating about their own future instead of building product. If they can see who is in the meeting, and they get the notes afterward, the paranoia collapses.

**Don't:** Build exec-only dashboards.
**Why:** You are signaling that there is a real dashboard and a fake dashboard. The real one has the real numbers. The fake one is what the employees get. People know. Morale and trust degrade. More importantly, the people closest to the work can't make good decisions because they don't have the inputs.

**Don't:** Do one-way all-hands where information flows from execs to the room and questions are filtered.
**Why:** That is a broadcast, not a meeting. The value of the all-hands is the bidirectional transmission. If questions are filtered or the Q&A is tokenized, you have created a theater of transparency, not transparency itself.

**Don't:** Strip the board deck down to a "nice" version for the all-hands.
**Why:** The team can smell a sanitized deck. The whole point of showing them the real deck is that they see how the board actually sees the business, including what the board is worried about. Sanitize too aggressively and you teach the team that they don't get real information.

**Don't:** Let one-on-one side conversations replace public ones.
**Why:** If decisions get made in Slack DMs between two people and never surface, you've built a shadow org chart where some people are "in" and most are "out." This is how politics starts. Move the decision-making conversation back into notes that hit the alias.

**Don't:** Hide bad news from the company.
**Why:** They already know. They have eyes. A company that loses a big deal, or misses a quarter, or has a key hire leave, and then gets a cheerful all-hands with no mention of it — that company is training itself to distrust its own leadership. Share the bad news, share what you're doing about it, move on.
