# Delegation: Task-Relevant Maturity × Rope Extension

## When to Use
- Every time you are about to tell a direct report how to do something. Ask first: how many times has this person done this exact task before? Not: how senior are they. How many times on THIS task.
- Every time you are about to overrule a direct report. Ask first: how high is my conviction, and how high is the consequence of them being wrong?
- Every time you are tempted to say "you're the boss, I'll just do it your way." That's the card you're burning social capital on.

## Fails When
- **You delegate on catastrophic decisions you know are wrong.** High conviction plus high consequence means you overrule. Rope-extension is not a theology. It's a tool for developing people on low-stakes calls.
- **You micromanage high-task-relevant-maturity people.** They will leave. A-players do not stay if you will not let them operate.
- **You treat "task-relevant maturity" as a proxy for seniority.** It's not. The twenty-year veteran running the first product launch of their career has low task-relevant maturity on that launch. The twenty-two-year-old who has shipped three launches at two startups has high task-relevant maturity on the fourth. Adjust accordingly.

## Core Concept

There are two frameworks stacked on top of each other here. You need both.

The first is **task-relevant maturity**, which I took from Andy Grove's High Output Management. Task-relevant maturity is a fancy phrase for a simple idea: how many times has this person done this exact task before? That is the variable that determines how much rope you give them. Not their title. Not their years of experience. Not how smart they are. Specifically, how mature are they on this task.

The radical implication is that any executive, any CEO, should not have one management style. Your management style should be dictated by your employee. With one particular person you may be very much a micromanager because they are quite low on this scale. With another person you may be delegating a lot because they are actually quite mature on this scale. A good sign in reference checks on an executive candidate is when half their former reports call them a micromanager and half call them hands-off. That is a feature, not a bug. That is adaptive management working correctly. I used to be befuddled when people would run reference checks on me and come back with that mosaic. I finally figured out I was actually doing my job correctly.

The diagnostic, when someone underperforms: could they do this if their life depended on it? If yes, it is a motivation problem, and motivation is on you — you have not communicated the why compellingly enough. If no, it is a capability problem, and that is on you too — you misread their task-relevant maturity and put them in a situation they were not ready for. Goethe: "Dream no small dreams for these have no power to move the hearts of men."

The second framework is the **rope-extension 2x2** — my first 2x2 ever, which I got from Peter. Sort every decision on two axes. Consequence of failure: low or high. Your conviction: low or high.

| | **Low Consequence** | **High Consequence** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Conviction** | Let them try their approach | Overrule if necessary |
| **Low Conviction** | Delegate fully | Gather more information first |

Where there's low consequence and you have low conviction, delegate fully and let them learn. Where the consequence is extremely dramatic and you have high conviction you're right, you cannot let a junior colleague make that mistake — you are ultimately responsible. Explain your thinking when you do overrule, because the why is how people develop. And track how often you play the "you're the boss" card, because every time you do, you burn social capital. Do it too often and your best people leave.

By extending rope when the downside is low, junior people gain task-relevant maturity faster. That is the whole point. You are not delegating to offload work. You are delegating to accelerate the growth of the person. Continuously expand each team member's scope until you find their breaking point. Everyone has one. Discover it, then pare back and coach them toward handling more.

## How to Apply

1. **Map every direct report on task-relevant maturity per area of responsibility.** Not per person — per person-times-task. One of your VPs may be world-class at hiring (high TRM) and brand new to fundraising (low TRM). You manage those two surfaces of their job completely differently.

2. **Before every significant decision you want to overrule, place it on the 2x2.** Write down the consequence and your conviction. If consequence is low, let them run — even if you disagree. Especially if you disagree. That's where the learning happens.

3. **Explain the why whenever you overrule.** At LinkedIn I had a talented colleague who would occasionally get annoyed if I didn't agree with his opinion. His ultimate card, when I hadn't persuaded him, was "okay, you're the boss." Every time he said that, I knew I was burning social capital. I was creating a thin line that would eventually backfire. Explain the reasoning. If your reasoning is weak, you probably shouldn't be overruling.

4. **When someone underperforms, run the life-depends-on-it test.** Sit them down. Ask yourself honestly: could they do this if their life depended on it? Yes means you haven't sold them the why. No means you misread where they are on the learning curve. Different diagnosis, different fix.

5. **Constantly expand scope.** Start every new person with something mundane and real — I once had an intern organize 9pm smoothies for the office, and my assistant and my office manager had both failed at this for months. The intern delivered them cold, on time, tasty, the first night. That's when I knew I could give him real stuff. Once someone handles a level of scope, expand it. Every day. Every week. Every month. Until they break. Then pare back and coach.

## Examples

**Situation:** At Square, Kyle — my first marketing hire, one of my favorite people in the world — wants to run an Inner Square program where Square merchants give out Squares to ten other potential merchants. My ten years of experience say this will not move the needle meaningfully.
**Application:** Rope-extension 2x2. Low consequence: the units don't cost much, the brand risk is near zero. My conviction is high, but the consequence is low. Do not overrule. Let Kyle run it. Let Kyle measure it. Let Kyle form his own opinion of what moves marketing metrics and what doesn't.
**Result:** The program did not meaningfully move the metric. Kyle learned what a real growth channel looks like versus what a feel-good program looks like. His judgment on the next ten proposals was dramatically better. I did not burn social capital playing the "I'm the boss" card. That investment paid back many times over.

**Situation:** A first-time product manager on your team wants to ship a pricing change that you believe will cannibalize a core revenue stream.
**Application:** High conviction, high consequence. Overrule. Then explain exactly why — walk them through the business equation, show them how this lever moves the other levers, show them how you'd test the hypothesis without putting revenue at risk. That is the editorial part. You are not just saying no. You are teaching them how to think about pricing decisions so the next one doesn't require overruling.
**Result:** You prevented a self-inflicted wound. The PM got a masterclass in the business model. Next time they come to you with a pricing proposal, it's better.

**Situation:** A senior engineer who has shipped four distributed systems elsewhere joins your team and is building the payments infrastructure. You have opinions about the API shape.
**Application:** Very high task-relevant maturity on shipping systems. You may have strong opinions about the API shape, but your conviction is weaker than his — he has done this more than you have. Gather more information by asking him to walk you through the tradeoffs. If after that conversation you still disagree, and the consequence is high, flag it. But default to his judgment on the actual design. Laissez-faire check-ins are sufficient.
**Result:** You don't demoralize a senior hire in his first month. He builds the thing the way he knows how. You stay close enough to catch a mistake but far enough not to impede velocity.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply the same management style to everyone.
**Why:** That is not management, that is a personal preference you are imposing on the whole team. With a low-TRM person it looks hands-off and they drown. With a high-TRM person it looks like micromanagement and they leave. Your style has to adapt to the employee, not to your comfort zone.

**Don't:** Use "rope extension" as cover for abdicating on high-stakes calls.
**Why:** The 2x2 has four quadrants for a reason. High consequence plus high conviction is where you show up and make the call. You are not extending rope — you are letting someone drive the company into a wall. That is not development. That is negligence.

**Don't:** Overrule without explaining the why.
**Why:** The why is the entire learning mechanism. If you just say "no, do it this way," you have not developed anyone. You have trained them to wait for instructions. The next decision will also route through you. You've just made yourself the bottleneck.

**Don't:** Tolerate "could they do this if their life depended on it? No — oh well" as a resting state.
**Why:** If the answer is no and you do nothing, you have accepted that this person cannot do the job. Either increase the hand-holding and coaching until they can, reduce the scope of their role until it matches their actual TRM, or replace them. "They'll figure it out eventually" without structured support is how you lose quarters.

**Don't:** Count how often you pull the "you're the boss" card as zero.
**Why:** Every time you pull it, you are spending social capital you won't get back easily. If you pull it weekly with the same person, you have the wrong person — or you are the wrong manager for them. Either way, it ends badly.

**Don't:** Confuse seniority with task-relevant maturity.
**Why:** Task-relevant maturity is task-specific. The 45-year-old CFO running their first fundraising round has low TRM on that fundraise. Manage them hands-on for that project. On the next fundraise they'll be laissez-faire. This is not a reflection on their worth as a person. It is a reflection on the specific task.
