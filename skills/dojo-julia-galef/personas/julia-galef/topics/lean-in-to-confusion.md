# Lean In To Confusion

## When to Use

- Your customer acquisition numbers don't match your model and you keep finding reasons each month.
- A co-founder or key hire behaves in a way that confuses you and your first move is to call them irrational.
- A competitor is doing something that makes no sense — and is winning.
- You're running an experiment and small things keep going not-quite-as-expected.
- Someone disagrees with you and your instinct is to dismiss them, but a quiet part of your brain says "wait…"
- You catch yourself saying *that's weird* and then continuing with what you were doing.

## Fails When

- **You're flooding signal with noise.** If you treat every anomaly as equally important, you'll paralyze. Some anomalies are real clues; some are just variance. The skill is noticing them — not halting on every one.
- **The decision is on a short clock.** You can't sit in confusion for a month when the ship leaves Friday. Note the anomaly, make the call, return to it.
- **The existing theory is genuinely correct.** Scouts don't abandon paradigms at the first conflicting data point. The move is to hold the paradigm while keeping a running tab of what it can't explain. Abandonment comes when the tab gets long.

## Core Concept

When reality violates your expectations, you have two options. The default one, the one most people take, is to explain the anomaly away — to shoehorn it into your existing worldview so you can get back to business. The other is to tug on it, the way you'd tug on a loose thread, and see what unravels. Scouts pick the second option. The phrase Isaac Asimov is reputed to have said: *The most exciting phrase to hear in science, the one that heralds new discoveries, is not "Eureka" but "That's funny…"*

The failure mode has a name. Decision researcher Gary Klein calls it a **de minimus error** — minimizing the inconsistency between observations and theory. Each conflicting data point, taken alone, is small enough to explain away. The doctor's initial diagnosis gets defended one symptom at a time. The general's "the enemy is on the run" story absorbs each contrary report individually. The MLM salesperson explains each month of losing money with "I'm not trying hard enough." If you could see all the anomalies stacked up at once, the paradigm would collapse. But you never do, because you dissolve them one at a time as they arrive. This is how people stay in wrong jobs, wrong marriages, wrong strategies, and wrong theories for years.

The technique researcher Marvin Cohen suggested is the spring metaphor: each time you explain away conflicting evidence, you're stretching a spring. A small stretch is fine — theories always have some slack. But keep stretching and eventually the spring snaps back. The skill is feeling the stretch and noting it. *This evidence stretches my theory by a little. This one stretches it by a lot.* You don't have to act on any single stretch. You just have to maintain the running tab. When the spring is visibly taut, that's your signal to consider alternate explanations — not to wait for the dramatic final data point that forces you.

The highest application of this is the paradigm shift, Thomas Kuhn's term. Paradigm shifts don't start with a dramatic reversal. They start with one person collecting anomalies — puzzles they can't quite explain under the dominant theory — and being willing to hold the uncertainty long enough for the alternative to crystallize. Darwin's peacock tail was one of those. Your weird KPI might be one too.

## How to Apply

1. **Treat anomalies as puzzle pieces to collect, not as problems to solve.** You don't have to know what they mean. You just have to write them down and not let them slide off the mental surface. A surprise journal — a running list of things that didn't go how you expected — is the cheapest version of this practice.
2. **When something confuses you, ask "why might this make sense to them?"** Not "are they rational?" Not "how could they be so wrong?" The right question is: *what am I missing that would make this the right call from their position?* This works for competitors, customers, employees, and strangers on the internet. 22% of inter-state conflicts between 1816 and 1974 were cases where the weaker side attacked the stronger. If that seems crazy, you're missing something — the information asymmetry, the stake asymmetry, the madman strategy, whatever. Go find it.
3. **Feel the stretch.** Before you accept an explanation for a conflicting data point, notice how hard you're working to make it fit. Phrases like *well, that must have been a fluke* or *they only invited me out of pity* or *my students are just mad I'm a tough grader* are stretch indicators. The explanation may still be right. Tally the stretches.
4. **When you have several stretched explanations, stack them.** Ask yourself: *is there any single alternative theory that would explain all of these without stretching?* This is the Donna-from-Rodan-and-Fields move. Individually, each anomaly was explainable; stacked up — *why is this so hard, why do the videos have no content, why can't anyone show me real numbers, how is this woman with a newborn really working full-time* — the alternative theory (this is exploiting me) became inescapable.
5. **Sit in confusion longer than feels comfortable.** The premature urge to resolve is the enemy. It's fine to say, *I don't know what's going on here, but something is. I'll keep watching.* Paradigm shifts happen on the scale of weeks, months, or years, not minutes. You operate under the existing paradigm while being aware it may be wrong — that's the hard skill.

## Examples

**Situation: Darwin and the peacock's tail.**
In 1860, one year after publishing *On the Origin of Species*, Darwin wrote: *The sight of a feather in a peacock's tail, whenever I gaze at it, makes me sick!* Natural selection said features that don't aid survival get weeded out. A five-foot gaudy tail that makes you slower is a contradiction. Darwin could have dismissed it as an odd case. He didn't. He followed a rule he called his "golden rule": whenever a published fact, a new observation, or thought came across him that was opposed to his general results, he made a memorandum of it at once — because he had learned that such facts and thoughts were far more apt to escape his memory than favorable ones. He tracked anomalies like these for decades. The peacock's tail, far from destroying his theory, is what forced him to discover sexual selection — which made his theory stronger. He did this with dozens of anomalies over twenty years of research. That's the model. Not one grand reversal, but a discipline of noticing and cataloguing the things that didn't fit, and trusting that they'd pay off eventually.

**Situation: Donna and the pyramid scheme.**
Donna was 22, working in a restaurant, and got recruited to sell skincare products for Rodan + Fields. She paid $1,000 for the start-up kit, worked full time, and couldn't recoup the cost. Her upline kept telling her the system worked and that if she was failing, it was because she wasn't trying hard enough. Donna had a series of confusions: *why isn't this making me feel independent? Why are these instructional videos empty? How are these other saleswomen with newborns really making money?* Each anomaly, alone, got explained away — *I'll understand when I level up*. She was making de minimus errors. What broke the paradigm wasn't new data about MLMs. She was scrolling Netflix and watched Leah Remini's Scientology documentary as a lark. The structure, the way the leaders spoke, the pressure — it all matched her last year. A new paradigm snapped into place: *this organization is exploiting me*. All her confusions suddenly fit. The anomalies had been telling her the truth for months. She just hadn't let them accumulate.

**Situation: The "this is awkward" conversation.**
Julia had a conversation with an acquaintance who commented *well, this is kind of awkward*, which she thought made things worse. She polled her Facebook friends and was shocked that two-thirds said it made things better. Her first move was classic explaining-away: *people who answered "better" don't really mean that*. She caught the stretch — it felt like a stretch. When she actually talked to someone who'd said "better," she realized they had a totally different model of conversational responsibility than she did. *Wait, you feel like it's your responsibility to make conversations go smoothly?* Her confusion was the clue. She'd been on the verge of writing off thirty-two people as confused about their own experience, which was the tell that her theory was wrong.

**Situation: The homeopathic hospital.**
In 1850s London, cholera was killing patients at a 46% mortality rate across the city's mainstream hospitals. The government commissioned a survey. One institution — the London Homeopathic Hospital — had an 18% mortality rate. Less than half. The council's response: exclude it from the survey, because homeopathy was obviously nonsense and the data would muddle the conclusions. Anomaly dismissed. It turned out the homeopathic doctors had stumbled, half by accident, on two real things: sterilizing blankets, and giving patients whey (essentially oral rehydration therapy, which didn't become standard until the 1960s). If the council had leaned into their confusion, rehydration therapy could have arrived a century earlier and millions of lives could have been saved. The council assumed the only two possibilities were *we're right* or *the homeopaths are right*. They missed the hidden option C.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Dismiss opposing behavior as "irrational."**
*Why:* "Irrational" is almost never an explanation. It's a label you put on something so you don't have to think about it. Top negotiators treat "the other side is being irrational" as a diagnostic — not about the other side, but about themselves. It means you're missing something. Chris Voss's version: *when we hear something that doesn't make sense, a crucial fork in the road is presented — push forward into what we can't process, or take the guaranteed-failure path of telling ourselves negotiating is useless.*

**Don't: Explain away anomalies one at a time.**
*Why:* This is the de minimus error. Each individual explanation can be defensible while the accumulated pattern is damning. Keep a running list. If you find yourself reaching for a fourth or fifth explanation in the same domain, stop and ask whether the pattern itself is trying to tell you something.

**Don't: Treat "people are unpredictable" as a get-out-of-jail-free card.**
*Why:* This is Tetlock's "Politics Is Hopelessly Cloudlike" defense, and it's Spock's move when the aliens don't behave logically — *most illogical*, shrug, next question. If someone is "unpredictable" to you, they're usually quite predictable to people with the right model. The shrug is how you stop improving.

**Don't: Demand an immediate answer to "what does this anomaly mean?"**
*Why:* Premature resolution is how anomalies get dissolved back into the existing paradigm. It's often better to say *I don't know what this means yet, I'm going to hang onto it* than to force a premature interpretation. Joshua Harris left himself "suspended in confusion" for years about his own book; that's not weakness, it's the skill.

**Don't: Act as if explaining a confusing data point means you've handled it.**
*Why:* An explanation that's plausible is not the same as one that's right. Ask of each explanation: *how hard did I work to make this fit?* If the answer is "pretty hard," mark the spring as stretched. The explanation may still be correct — but note the stretch, and watch for others.

**Don't: Confuse "tugging on the thread" with abandoning the sweater.**
*Why:* Leaning into confusion doesn't mean flipping positions. It means hanging onto the anomaly while continuing to operate. You can still run the company on the existing theory while simultaneously collecting evidence that the theory might be incomplete. Acting under a paradigm while being aware of its flaws is a specific and difficult skill — but it's the skill, not ambivalence.
