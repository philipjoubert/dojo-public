# How To Be Wrong

## When to Use

- You've taken a position publicly and new evidence has come in against it.
- You're about to say "I was wrong" and your gut is bracing for humiliation.
- Someone on your team keeps saying "I was almost right" instead of learning from a miss.
- You're treating your beliefs as fortresses to defend rather than maps to update.
- A colleague tells you you never admit mistakes — and you can prove they're wrong, and still they're onto something.
- You notice you're arguing against evidence instead of incorporating it.

## Fails When

- **Genuine negligence or dishonesty.** If you got something wrong because you were sloppy, willfully blind, or misrepresenting the facts, matter-of-fact updating skips a step the situation actually requires. Sometimes "I was wrong" does mean "I screwed up," and the honest move is to say so.
- **Legal or contractual stakes.** Saying "I update" in a deposition is a different act than saying it on a blog. Use the frame for your own thinking; choose your words for the setting.
- **Performance environments that punish updating.** If your boss or board treats any admission as weakness, you still have to update internally; just don't expect the cultural rewards this frame assumes. Change the environment or keep your updates quieter.

## Core Concept

Most people treat "being wrong" as a binary event — you were right, then you weren't, and the transition is a humiliation. The scout move is to treat belief as a dial, not a switch. You were 80 percent confident immigration helps the economy. A new study comes in against it. You move to 70 percent. Next month a better study comes in. You move back to 75. Nothing was humiliating. Nothing was surrender. The map got slightly more accurate.

The superforecasters Philip Tetlock studied — amateurs who beat CIA analysts by 30 percent — didn't win by being smarter or better-informed. They won by changing their minds more often and in smaller increments. The highest-scoring superforecaster, Tim Minto, revised his forecasts as many as forty or fifty times on a single question. Each revision was small. None of them felt like a crisis. That's the technique.

There are two reframes inside that technique. First, stop asking "Can I still believe I'm right?" and start asking "What does this new information change?" Second, replace the word *admit* with the word *update*. "I admit I was wrong" implies you screwed up and you're asking for forgiveness. "I've updated" means you learned something new and your model now reflects it. Those two sentences describe the same external event and completely different internal experiences. You want the second one. It's low-key, routine, and it doesn't trigger the defensive machinery that stops you from updating at all.

The deepest version of this: errors aren't bugs in the process, they're the output of the process working correctly. Your map was always going to start out wrong. Revising it is what you *do*; it's not a failure mode. A living document is supposed to get revised.

## How to Apply

1. **Default to small moves.** Before you decide whether to flip or hold, ask: how much should this specific piece of evidence shift my confidence? Two points? Ten? Rarely more than fifteen on any single data point. You're steering, not capsizing.
2. **Separate "being wrong" from "doing something wrong."** Ask: did I follow a reasonable process given what I knew then? If yes, this is an update, not a confession — you don't owe anyone contrition for learning. If I was sloppy, I own the sloppiness; that's the thing to apologize for, not the conclusion I reached.
3. **Swap the vocabulary.** Catch yourself saying "admit," "confess," "concede," "grant" — all the surrender words. Replace with "update," "revise," "shift." The language change bleeds into the felt experience within a few weeks.
4. **Extract the domain-general lesson.** Every wrongness is two lessons: the specific one (*X is true, not Y*) and the general one (*I'm too quick to assume people who disagree with me are simpletons*, or *I weight recent data too heavily*, or *I confused "if I were PM of Japan" with "what will the PM of Japan do"*). The general one is the one that compounds.
5. **Reach backward.** Think of a disagreement you had with someone in the past on which your view has since moved. Tell them. This builds the muscle and — more importantly — lowers the felt cost of the next update by making it a routine part of your operating rhythm.
6. **Budget for being wrong in advance.** Tell yourself, before you make a prediction or take a position: *I am going to be meaningfully wrong about something here. The only question is which thing.* When the wrongness arrives you've already paid its emotional cost; all you have to do is notice it.

## Examples

**Situation: The climate skeptic who switched.**
Jerry Taylor was one of the top professional climate skeptics in the US, working at the Cato Institute. In a televised debate, he used a standard talking point — that the 1988 Congressional testimony had overstated warming — and his opponent Joe Romm challenged him backstage to check it. Taylor did. Romm was right. Taylor went back to the skeptical scientist he'd been citing and asked what was going on; the scientist hemmed and hawed for twenty minutes. That didn't collapse Taylor's position. It nudged it. *Bit by bit*, as he put it, he got less confident. He started following up on every reference any fellow skeptic cited and was repeatedly disappointed. Years later, a conversation with a climate-activist investor named Bob Litterman shredded what remained, and Taylor crossed over. The point: he didn't flip in the debate. He updated a few points, then a few more, then a few more. The binary flip that looks dramatic from outside was actually a long sequence of small moves from inside.

**Situation: The forecaster who asked the wrong question.**
Superforecaster Bill Flack was predicting whether Japan's prime minister Shinzo Abe would visit the Yasukuni shrine — a diplomatic landmine. Flack said no; it made no sense for Abe to shoot himself in the foot. Abe visited anyway. Most forecasters, confronted with a miss like that, reach for one of Tetlock's seven belief-system defenses: *It was a one-off, politics is cloudlike, I was almost right.* Flack did something different. He asked: *what does this teach me?* His answer: *I wasn't answering "will Abe visit Yasukuni?" I was answering "would I visit Yasukuni if I were PM of Japan?"* That's a domain-general lesson — don't assume world leaders share your utility function — that makes every future forecast a little sharper. This is why superforecasters' accuracy improved about 25% per year while other forecasters' didn't improve at all. The miss was the power-up.

**Situation: The Humane League's commitment to being wrong.**
David Coman-Hidy runs the Humane League, one of the most effective animal-rights organizations in the US. Every new employee gets told the same thing on day one: *If we're not doing something totally different in five years than what we're doing now, then we failed.* The organization pivoted away from flashy lab-animal protests once they realized the impact wasn't there. They went all-in on Meatless Mondays in schools, poured four months of organizational effort into it, then saw the follow-up data, realized it didn't stick without infrastructure they couldn't provide, and announced: *Great job everyone, stop what you're doing, we're going back to what we were doing before.* That took billions of chickens from grinders to unharmed. Coman-Hidy's frame: "If you bring up these biases a lot — that we're always going to think we're right — it makes it an easier pill to swallow when something better comes along. You've inoculated yourself against the 'horror' of having been suboptimal for some period of time."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Make "admitting you were wrong" into a dramatic moment.**
*Why:* The moment mostly exists to let you perform humility. It also reinforces the assumption that being wrong is shameful in the first place, which is the thing making you bad at updating. Aim for matter-of-fact. *"Ah, you're right, scratch what I said earlier"* — cheerful, nonchalant, done. The less friction the process has, the more often you'll go through it.

**Don't: Treat a new piece of evidence as either 'decisive' or 'no effect.'**
*Why:* This is the binary trap dressed up as rigor. Almost no single piece of evidence should move you from 90% to 10% or from 90% to 90%. Most should move you a few points. If you've never moved five or ten points on something, your belief system is probably frozen and you haven't noticed.

**Don't: Use "I Was Almost Right."**
*Why:* This is Tetlock's #1 belief-system defense, and it's the tell that you're defending rather than learning. "Gore would have won if…" "My prediction would have been right except…" The exception-hunting isn't neutral. It's how you avoid extracting the lesson. When you catch yourself doing it, stop and ask the superforecaster question instead: *what does this teach me about how to make better forecasts?*

**Don't: Wait to be dragged across the line.**
*Why:* If you only update when the evidence is overwhelming and public, you'll always be late. You'll also have built a bigger ego-cost into the update, because the longer you hold a position the more defending you've done of it, and the more defending you've done the more it's tied to your identity. Update early, update small, update often.

**Don't: Apologize for learning.**
*Why:* "I'm sorry I was wrong about X" conflates two things — a model update and a moral failing. If the process was reasonable given what you knew, the update doesn't owe anyone an apology. Save the apologies for the things that actually warrant them: sloppiness, dishonesty, harm. "I've updated on X" is not an apology. It's a status report.

**Don't: Surrender the rest of the position when you lose a point.**
*Why:* New evidence should nudge confidence a few points, not require collapse. Admitting that one argument for your position was weak is not the same as admitting the position is wrong. Most people collapse because they've bundled all the supporting arguments together, so losing one feels like losing all of them. Unbundle. You can lose three points on argument A, gain one on argument B, net out at minus two, and still hold the overall position — just slightly more humbly.
