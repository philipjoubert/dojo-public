# The Five Safeguards

## When to Use
- The user has identified a default that keeps firing and now needs the protective layer that interrupts it.
- A high-stakes decision is approaching and the user knows they'll be in HALT or under social pressure when it lands.
- A behavior has resisted willpower-based fixes and the user is ready to redesign the environment.
- A team is making the same kind of mistake at the same point in their process — a checklist or guardrail problem.
- A founder needs to protect their own future from their own present-moment self.

## Fails When
- **The problem isn't a default — it's a knowledge gap.** Safeguards protect you from yourself. They don't compensate for not knowing the right answer in the first place. A checklist for a domain you don't understand will execute the wrong steps consistently.
- **The user installs so many safeguards that they become brittle.** A system with twenty rules has no rules. The user starts bypassing them all and ends up worse off than before. Pick the few that interrupt the most expensive defaults.
- **The safeguard becomes a substitute for thinking.** A checklist isn't a brain. The pause is supposed to create space for reason, not to *replace* reason.

## Core Concept

Strengths handle what you can reprogram. Safeguards handle what you can't — biological weaknesses and acquired weaknesses that willpower won't fix. Shane's framing: "Safeguards are tools for protecting ourselves from ourselves — from weaknesses that we don't have the strength to overcome."

The deep claim underneath all five safeguards is that environment beats willpower. Always. On a long enough timeline, willpower loses every battle. The person who looks disciplined is almost never the person with extraordinary resolve — they're the person who has constructed an environment in which the right behavior is the path of least resistance. Shane's parents couldn't quit smoking until they changed their social environment. His own habits — no meetings before lunch, gym every day, certain decisions deferred until certain conditions are met — aren't acts of will. They're rules and structures.

Five safeguards. Each one is a different way of installing protection ahead of the moment of vulnerability.

### 1. Prevention (HALT)

The most direct safeguard: avoid making the decision in the first place. Don't decide under unfavorable conditions.

The HALT check, borrowed from AA and adapted: before any significant decision, ask — am I **H**ungry, **A**ngry, **L**onely, **T**ired? Shane extends "lonely" to include lonely, stressed, rushed, in an unfamiliar environment.

If the answer is yes to any: defer if you can. Shane: "If the answer is yes to any of these questions, avoid making the decision if you can. Wait for a more opportune time. Otherwise, your defaults will take over."

Most decisions can wait an hour. The ones that can't are usually structured by someone else's urgency — which is itself often a social-default trigger. The first move when "I need an answer right now" lands in your inbox is to ask whether the urgency is real or manufactured.

### 2. Automatic Rules for Success

Replace decisions with rules. Pre-decide once, let the rule execute every time after.

Why it works: it removes the in-the-moment willpower cost, and people respect rules in ways they don't respect decisions. Shane: "People typically don't argue with your personal rules. They just accept them as features of who you are. People question decisions, but they respect rules."

Kahneman's rule, which Shane cites as a favorite: never say yes to a request on the phone. "I'll have to get back to you after I think about it." Kills the social-default yes-in-the-moment pattern. The rule is doing the work that self-control would otherwise have to do.

Shane's own rules: no meetings before lunch (protects the biggest opportunity block of the day). Gym every day (easier than going some days, because there's no decision to make). The generation prompt he offers: "If a film crew were documenting whether I'd be successful, what would I want them to see me doing or not doing?" That's the rule.

### 3. Friction (environment design)

Behavior follows the path of least resistance. So make the bad path harder and the good path easier.

Remove junk food from the house — now you have to leave to get it. Put your phone in another room when you write — now you have to walk to be distracted. Schedule the workout for the morning — now you can't talk yourself out of it as the day's energy drains.

Shane's peer-pressure variant: "I'll buy you all lunch if you catch me with my email open before 11 a.m." Friction can be financial, social, physical, temporal — pick whatever the lever is for the specific behavior.

The deeper line: "It's easy to underestimate the role ease plays in decision-making." We tend to attribute behavior to motivation when most of it is friction.

### 4. Guardrails and Checklists

Force a pause. Force the return to basics: what am I actually trying to accomplish, and what's needed to do it well?

The pilot's preflight checklist. The surgeon's preoperative checklist. Your own "before I send this email," "before I fire this person," "before I sign this contract" list.

The point isn't the items on the list — they vary by domain. The point is the *forced pause*. The checklist is a mechanical way of inserting the space between stimulus and response. It defeats the social-default rush to action and the emotion-default urge to act now. It also catches the small omissions that become large failures — the unbuckled fuel hose, the unsigned co-signer line, the missing CC on the announcement.

### 5. Shifting Perspective

Each of us sees from one vantage point. The reference-frame safeguard is the deliberate move into someone else's.

Shane's two-question protocol:
1. "Here's how I think you see it — what did I miss?"
2. "What else did I miss?"

This leverages a near-universal instinct — people love to correct others — and turns it into structured information. Far more useful than asking "what do you think?" which produces social-default platitudes.

Deeper frame: physics has frames of reference, and so does every human situation. The view from your seat on the train is different from the view from the platform. Blind spots live between the frames. The safeguard is the discipline of moving between them before deciding.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify which default is producing the recurring loss.** Safeguards are targeted. The HALT check protects against emotion-default decisions. Rules protect against social-default yeses. Friction protects against inertia and emotion. Checklists protect against omission and rush. Perspective-shifting protects against ego-default tunnel vision. Pick the safeguard that matches the failure mode.

2. **Pre-install the safeguard before you need it.** A safeguard installed in the moment is a safeguard that won't fire. Build the rule, the checklist, the friction, or the deferral protocol when you're calm. Then trust it.

3. **For HALT, build the deferral language ahead of time.** "I'll need until tomorrow morning to give you a real answer" is a sentence you should be able to say without thinking. Practice it. Most regret-decisions get made because the deferral move was harder than the wrong answer.

4. **Make the rule single-line and externally visible.** "I don't take meetings before 11 a.m." beats "I try to protect mornings." The single-line, externally-visible version sets others' expectations and removes the in-the-moment negotiation.

5. **Engineer friction asymmetrically.** Don't try to fight the trigger directly — shift the path. If you keep checking email at night, leave the laptop in the car. If you keep saying yes to coffee chats, route all introductions through a single weekly slot. The friction does the work, not your resolve.

6. **Build checklists for the moves that fail catastrophically when one step is missed.** A pre-firing checklist. A pre-launch checklist. A pre-major-purchase checklist. The items don't have to be elaborate — they just have to force the pause and surface the omissions you're prone to.

7. **For perspective-shifting, default to the two-question protocol.** Especially when you're already inside an argument or a negotiation. Asking "what did I miss?" produces information. Asking "what do you think?" produces social default.

8. **Audit the safeguards quarterly.** A safeguard that no longer fires is dead weight. A safeguard you've started bypassing is worse — it teaches you to ignore your own systems. Either reinstall the discipline or delete it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder routinely sends regrettable late-night emails after a long day. He's tried to "be more disciplined" three times and failed each time.
**Application:** Friction safeguard. Move the email client off the phone. Set the desktop client to require a manual sync. Combine with a rule: "no business email sent after 8 p.m." The willpower attempts failed because they put the burden on the moment of vulnerability. The friction puts the burden on the calm pre-installation.
**Result:** The pattern stops within a week. Not because his self-control improved — because the path got longer than the urge.

**Situation:** A CEO keeps saying yes to investor coffee meetings during fundraising and then resenting the time burn.
**Application:** Kahneman's rule, applied verbatim. "I never commit to meetings on a call. Send me a note and I'll get back within 48 hours." Pre-decide the rule. Practice the sentence. The investor will accept it as a feature of who he is, not as a personal slight.
**Result:** Half the meetings stop being requested at all once they require a follow-up email. The remaining half are the ones that mattered.

**Situation:** A product team keeps shipping features that break customer onboarding because someone forgets to update the welcome flow each time.
**Application:** Checklist safeguard. A two-line pre-ship check: (a) does this change touch onboarding? (b) if yes, has the welcome flow been verified? Required signoff from the PM. The forced pause catches the omission that motivation can't.
**Result:** The class of bug disappears. The team stops apologizing for the same mistake every quarter.

**Situation:** A board chair is about to make a hiring decision under significant time pressure. The candidate is on a competing offer with a 24-hour deadline.
**Application:** Prevention safeguard. The pressure is itself the trigger. The right move is to slow it down — extend the deadline, pay for the candidate's time, or pass. The HALT state of urgency under social pressure is exactly when the worst hiring decisions get made.
**Result:** Either the candidate accepts an extension (in which case the urgency wasn't real) or walks (in which case you avoided a hire that would have started with an unsustainable time pressure). Both outcomes beat a hire made under HALT.

**Situation:** A leader is convinced their go-to-market plan is right and is about to greenlight it. The team has been quietly skeptical but hasn't pushed back.
**Application:** Perspective-shifting safeguard. The two-question protocol, run with each direct report individually: "Here's how I think you see this plan — what did I miss?" Followed by: "What else did I miss?" Done in private, not in a group meeting where the social default will suppress signal.
**Result:** Three of five reports surface specific risks the leader hadn't considered. The plan ships with two adjustments and one deferred experiment. The leader hasn't lost authority — they've gained accuracy.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to install a safeguard while you're already in the failure mode it's meant to prevent.
**Why:** A HALT check applied while you're HALT will rationalize itself away. Install the rule when you're calm. Trust it when you're not.

**Don't:** Use a safeguard you can secretly bypass.
**Why:** A rule you negotiate with isn't a rule. The whole point is removing the in-the-moment decision. If "I don't do meetings before 11" is renegotiated every Monday, the rule is fictional and you've added negotiation overhead on top of the original problem.

**Don't:** Ladder up too many checklists.
**Why:** A checklist with thirty items will be ignored. A checklist with three will be followed. Pick the few items where omission is catastrophic and let the rest be judgment.

**Don't:** Use perspective-shifting as a way to delay deciding.
**Why:** Asking "what did I miss?" of fifteen people is analysis paralysis dressed up as humility. Two or three high-signal sources is the right dose. After that, you're using the safeguard as an avoidance mechanism.

**Don't:** Treat rules and friction as substitutes for self-knowledge.
**Why:** The right rule is targeted at a real default vulnerability. Generic rules — "wake up at 5," "no carbs," "inbox zero by Friday" — are aspirational productivity copy, not safeguards. The safeguard is built around the *specific* failure mode you've diagnosed in yourself.

**Don't:** Apply HALT only to the obvious states.
**Why:** Hungry, angry, lonely, tired are the headline states. Rushed, stressed, in an unfamiliar environment, just out of an emotional conversation — these also degrade decision quality. Treat the extended list as the actual checklist.

**Don't:** Skip the safeguards because they look unimpressive.
**Why:** "Don't decide when you're hungry" sounds trivial. It isn't. The trivial-sounding safeguards are usually the highest-leverage because they prevent the most expensive defaults. The exotic-sounding ones are usually substitutes for actually doing the work.
