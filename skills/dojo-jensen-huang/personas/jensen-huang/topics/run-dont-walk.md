# Run, Don't Walk

## When to Use
- The industry around you is moving on a shorter cycle than your company is moving on, and you are losing ground without realizing it.
- You have just had a quarter that blew the doors off, and your executive team is sending signals that they'd like a little breathing room now.
- A technology signal has appeared — a paper, a customer email, a partner conversation — and the signal is weak, but the implications are enormous if it's real.
- You are running a company that is no longer small, and you are worried about the founder-era urgency getting diluted by headcount.
- You are giving career advice to someone graduating into a period of rapid change and they are asking whether they should take the summer off before starting.

## Fails When
- **The pace is theatre.** If everyone is running for the sake of looking like they're running, you are producing heat, not light. Run-don't-walk only works when the pace is in service of the mission, not the optics.
- **You are running in the wrong direction.** Speed amplifies direction. If your strategy is wrong, running makes it wronger, faster. Run after you've reasoned from first principles about where the food is.
- **The team has been sprinting for so long they can't see straight.** Fatigue breaks judgement. There is a version of this where the pace becomes self-defeating. The leader has to know the difference between the urgency that sharpens and the urgency that dulls.
- **You mistake 'running' for 'doing everything.'** The opposite of walking is not adding more tasks. It is moving on the most important thing without hesitation. Running toward the wrong priority is worse than walking toward the right one.

## Core Concept

If you're hunting for food, run, don't walk. Either you will be food, or you will be finding food. And often you can't tell which. Either way, run.

I told the graduating class at NTU in 2023 that this is the disposition they should take into their careers, and I meant it literally. The world around them is going to keep moving. If they stand still, something catches up. If they move, they at least have a chance of getting ahead of it. This isn't a mantra about productivity. It's about the internal register of a builder. You have to feel, at a low constant hum, that time is the adversary. Not today's deadline, not this quarter's earnings — time itself. Every day the industry moves, and you are either moving with it or behind it.

At Nvidia we say, and I say this at every monthly meeting, "our company is thirty days from going out of business." In 1997 it was literally true — we were running on vapors when the RIVA 128 shipped. Today we are not thirty days from going out of business. Everyone knows it isn't literal anymore. It's still true as a disposition. There is a path that leads to our destruction thirty days from now. I can describe it: we miss a product transition, we fumble a customer, a partner moves against us, a new architecture emerges that we didn't see coming. Those things have happened to companies bigger than Nvidia, and they happen on a year-rhythm. The mental posture of being thirty days away is what keeps you paying attention to the weak signals when everyone else is celebrating the quarter.

This is not the same as the toxic-productivity culture that Silicon Valley sometimes glamorizes. I am not telling you to grind eighteen hours a day so you can post about it. I am telling you that the internal orientation of a builder is different from the internal orientation of someone who is comfortable. The comfortable person has an off-switch. The builder's off-switch is broken, not because they forgot to install one, but because the mission won't let them. The Japanese gardener I met at the Silver Temple had been tending his moss garden with bamboo tweezers for twenty-five years. I asked him how he was going to finish. He said, "I have plenty of time." That is the paradox. Run, don't walk — and also, I have plenty of time. You have plenty of time because you started early and you never stopped. The two statements are the same statement.

Nvidia at three trillion dollars runs on the same clock it ran on at three hundred million. The product cadence is the same or faster. The e-staff meetings are the same. The Top 5 emails still come in at ten o'clock on Sunday night. Scale does not mean slack. Scale means a bigger machine operating at the same tempo. If you let the tempo slow down when you get big, you are handing the next decade to whoever is still hungry.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick one thing and move on it today.** The biggest cost of not-running is not the hours you don't work, it's the decision you don't make. When you see a signal, act on it. Hire the person, start the project, call the customer, draft the pitch. The opposite of running is not thinking — the opposite of running is deferring.
2. **Track your product cadence, not your hours.** The question isn't "how hard are people working?" The question is "how often are we shipping?" At Nvidia we moved graphics cards from a two-year cycle to a six-month cycle in the 1990s, and we moved AI chips from two-year to one-year in the 2020s. Cadence is the only honest measurement of pace.
3. **Maintain the 30-days-to-live ritual even when you don't need it.** At every monthly meeting, say out loud what would have to go wrong for this company to be in trouble a year from now. Name the scenarios. The ritual prevents complacency. It also trains the organization to spot the weak signals before they become strong ones.
4. **When you win, celebrate for one evening. Then back to work.** We just blew the doors off the quarter and I stood up in a review and said "I look in the mirror every morning and say, you suck." That's not self-loathing. That's a refusal to let yesterday's win become today's coasting. Enjoy the win. Name it. Then go to the next thing.
5. **Teach the disposition, don't just demand it.** If you want run-don't-walk culture at 30,000 employees, you have to model it. You can't hire a COO to run it on your behalf. The CEO's own pace is the ceiling on the company's pace.

## Examples

**Situation:** NTU commencement, 2023. Jensen is addressing graduating students in Taiwan, many of whom will go into the chip industry where Nvidia's partners live. He needs to leave them with a single thesis about how to operate in the AI era that is just opening.
**Application:** He tells them: "Whatever it is, run after it like we did. Run, don't walk. Remember, either you are running for food, or you are running from being food. And often times you can't tell which. Either way, run." He does not qualify it. He does not say "but also take care of yourself." He gives them the disposition raw, on purpose, because the speech is for the internal register of a builder, not for the comfort of the audience.
**Result:** The line becomes the most-quoted moment of the speech and gets re-invoked in commencement addresses around the world. It lands because Jensen is the living proof: he has run every day for thirty-one years without stopping, and the company behind him is the receipt.

**Situation:** 2012. AlexNet is trained on two GeForce GTX 580 gaming cards and wins ImageNet. The result is a research curiosity. Most of the industry reads it, nods, and moves on. The people who would have been justified in saying "let's wait for more evidence" are everyone.
**Application:** Jensen doesn't wait. He reorients the entire company on deep learning. He invests billions into the unknown — thousands of engineers for a decade, working on a technology whose commercial market is, at the moment, zero. He runs at the weak signal because the weak signal, if it's real, is the biggest signal he has ever seen. If he had walked — if he had said "let's see how this develops and then decide" — the Hopper architecture is not ready when ChatGPT launches in late 2022, and Nvidia is not the engine of the AI revolution. The run, not the wait, is what builds the moat.
**Result:** By 2023 Nvidia's data center business has become the fastest revenue ramp in hardware history. The company's market capitalization moves by more than Intel's total value in a single day. The run started eleven years earlier with a research paper most people ignored.

**Situation:** An e-staff member at Nvidia, after a blockbuster quarter, schedules a "strategic planning off-site" to lay out a five-year roadmap.
**Application:** Jensen declines to participate. He tells the team: "Strategy is not words. Strategy is action. We don't do a periodic planning system. The reason for that is because the world is a living, breathing thing. We just plan continuously. There's no five-year plan." The off-site is converted into a working session about the current quarter's product risks. The team runs on the live problem rather than performing strategy.
**Result:** Nvidia keeps shipping on a cadence that its competitors can't match. The absence of a formal five-year plan is the reason the pivots into CUDA, into deep learning, into robotics, into AI factories were possible. Plans would have locked the company out of the runs it needed to make.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "running" with "being busy."
**Why:** The busy CEO is a bad CEO. Busy means reactive — your calendar is someone else's priorities. Running means you've decided what the food is and you're moving toward it without hesitation. If your week is full of status meetings and reviews, you are walking in circles, not running.

**Don't:** Use "run, don't walk" to justify skipping the reasoning.
**Why:** I reasoned for six months about whether CUDA was the right bet before we committed. The run part came after the first-principles thinking. If you run before you've reasoned, you end up sprinting off a cliff. Run-don't-walk is the execution disposition, not a substitute for the strategic disposition. Reason hard, decide, then run.

**Don't:** Let the pace be something you do and not something your team does.
**Why:** A CEO who runs while the organization walks is building an hourglass — everything has to go through them, and the moment they stop, the company stops. The pace has to be contagious. The Top 5 emails, the whiteboard meetings, the 30-days ritual — those are the mechanisms by which pace transfers from the CEO to the org. Without them, your speed caps at whatever you personally can do.

**Don't:** Keep running when the team has genuinely run out of fuel.
**Why:** People aren't engines. The run-don't-walk disposition is about keeping the internal register sharp, not about grinding people into dust. When you see a team that has stopped producing good judgement — they're missing things they used to catch, their decisions are getting sloppier — that's not a sign to push harder. That's a sign the pace is broken. Rest the team, reset the priorities, and restart.
