# The Taiwan Relationship

## When to Use
- You're making a manufacturing decision and wondering whether to vertically integrate or partner.
- You need a strategic relationship with someone much larger than you who doesn't yet care about you.
- You're thinking about your own identity as an immigrant or outsider founder and whether to lean into it or minimize it.
- You're trying to understand why Nvidia speaks one language at GTC in San Jose and another language at Computex in Taipei. The two registers are not cosmetic. They reflect two real postures the company holds simultaneously.
- You're building a partner ecosystem and want to understand what it takes to be the one who makes the partners succeed, not just the one who extracts from them.

## Fails When
- **You treat the immigrant identity as a brand rather than a posture.** Being an immigrant is not a marketing story. It's a continuous reminder that the ground you stand on is not inevitable and that you have to earn your place. If you perform it, you lose the benefit of it.
- **You copy the TSMC-partnership model without doing the physics.** We partner with TSMC because we cannot out-invest them on manufacturing. The foundry model is a recognition of specialization and comparative advantage. If your competitive situation is different, the right answer might be different. Don't copy the surface.
- **You try to be "global" at the expense of any particular place.** Nvidia has a specific relationship to Taiwan that is not interchangeable with its relationship to the United States. Both are real. Both require a real voice. Generic multinational doesn't work.

## Core Concept

I was born in Tainan, Taiwan, on February 17, 1963. My parents were not wealthy. My father worked, and the family moved with his work, which took us to Thailand for an extended period. My mother — who didn't speak English well herself — taught my brother Jeff and me English by opening the dictionary every day to ten random words and asking us to memorize the spellings and the meanings. That's how I learned the language that would become the language of my career.

When I was nine and my brother was ten, our parents sent us to the United States — to the Oneida Baptist Institute in eastern Kentucky, because my uncle had heard of it and thought it was a good boarding school. It was actually a reform academy in a town of three hundred people. My roommate was a teenager covered in knife scars who couldn't read. I taught him to read. He taught me to do push-ups, and I've kept doing push-ups every day for the rest of my life. A couple of years later my father got work in the United States and we rejoined my parents in Beaverton, Oregon. A few years after that I started waiting tables at a Denny's in Portland, which is how I ate my way through the American menu.

Here's the line I want you to hold onto. Witt asked me years later whether I felt like an insider now that I was running Nvidia. I told him: you're always an immigrant. I'm always Chinese. That wasn't a complaint or a boast. It's a description. The disposition never fully switches. You are always watching from a slight distance, always a little more aware that the place you're standing in was not given to you, always ready to work harder than the people who assume it was. I have carried that into every decision I've made as a CEO. It shapes the tempo at which I work, the preparedness with which I show up, and the humility with which I ask for help when I need it. Immigrants ask for help. It's one of our cultural strengths.

The Taiwan relationship inside Nvidia has two load-bearing components. The first is TSMC. When we started the company in 1993, we couldn't get TSMC to care about us. TSMC was the best-regarded chip manufacturer in the world. Don Valentine at Sequoia had recommended that we partner with them. I cold-called them. I left voicemails. Nothing. By 1996 I realized I needed to do something more personal, so I sat down and wrote a letter to Morris Chang, TSMC's CEO. I put it in the mail. I didn't expect to hear back. One afternoon the office was noisy — my engineers were gaming, as they did — and the phone rang. According to one engineer who was there, I said, everybody shut up, I've got Morris Chang on the phone. Morris was calling me back. We set up a visit. He came to Sunnyvale. I walked him through our plans for the chip, the next chip, and the one after that. He started jotting notes in a small black book. He kept coming back. He even came during his honeymoon in 1998 to make sure we had the capacity we needed.

TSMC became our main supplier in 1998 and has been our main partner for every generation of chip we've ever made since. Morris once said the biggest joy he got out of this job was to see his customers grow, make money, and succeed, and that was especially true for a fast-growing customer like Nvidia. I believe him. That relationship is one of the most important in the semiconductor industry, and it's one of the most important in my life.

The reason the partnership works, from a strategic standpoint, is first principles. Manufacturing chips at the leading edge of physics requires tens of billions of dollars of capex per fab, decades of process learning, and a workforce culture built around extraordinary manufacturing discipline. TSMC does that better than anyone in the world. Nvidia cannot out-invest TSMC on manufacturing, and we have not tried. Our comparative advantage is in architecture, software, systems, ecosystem. Theirs is in manufacturing. The partnership is built on that recognition. If we tried to own the fab, we'd be mediocre at both. By specializing, we're each excellent. This is the case for the foundry model generally — and it's why, when people ask me if we'll build our own fab, my answer is always no, TSMC is better at this than we could ever be, and I want TSMC doing what TSMC is best at. That's not outsourcing in the weak sense. That's partnership in the strong sense.

The second load-bearing component is the ecosystem — the rest of Taiwan. Foxconn, Wiwynn, Quanta, Inventec, Pegatron, MediaTek, ASUS, MSI, Gigabyte, Acer, and dozens more. When I speak at Computex, I am speaking in a different register than I do at GTC. At GTC I'm the head of a company announcing products. At Computex I'm a partner in an ecosystem that collectively produces the world's AI infrastructure. Taiwan is the home of our treasured partners, I said in 2024. This is in fact where everything Nvidia does begins. Our partners and ourselves take it to the world. That line wasn't rhetoric. It's a description of the supply chain. Without the Taiwanese ecosystem there is no Nvidia product. Without Nvidia there is no market pull for the Taiwanese ecosystem in AI. We're mutually constituted. The Computex register is sharper on sovereign AI — the idea that every nation needs its own AI infrastructure, its own models, its own data centers — because in Taipei I'm speaking to the companies that will make that possible, and to a government that understands at a visceral level what sovereignty means.

## How to Apply

Not a process. A posture and some tactics.

**Write the personal letter.** When I couldn't get TSMC to return my calls in 1995, I sent Morris Chang a personal letter. That worked when nothing else had. The letter is an underused tool in the age of email. If the person you need to reach is too senior to read your email, a physical letter, handwritten or at least hand-signed, still cuts through. The message should be specific: here's what I need, here's what I can offer, here's why a conversation would be worth your time. Ask for a meeting, not a decision.

**When you make a strategic partner relationship, invest in their success, not in extracting from them.** Morris Chang took the call because he has a philosophy of growing his customers. I've tried to hold to the same principle with our partners. The asymmetry — Nvidia needing TSMC more than TSMC needed Nvidia in 1996 — has reversed over the decades, but the relationship is unchanged because it was built on mutual growth, not on leverage.

**Design your company's structure around where you can actually be the best.** Don't fight for capability you can buy from a partner who is better at it. We don't run our own fabs because we'd be mediocre fab operators, and mediocre fab operators in this industry lose. We're good at chip architecture, GPU software, systems design, developer ecosystem. That's where we invest. Comparative advantage is a real concept, not a classroom abstraction. Use it.

**Speak to the ecosystem you're in, in its register.** At Computex in Taipei I speak Mandarin when I want to. I talk about our partners the way a member of the ecosystem talks, not the way a visitor talks. I name individual companies. I bow to founders. I show up at small events as well as the keynotes. The register is not decoration. It's the demonstration that I understand where I am. If you're trying to build a partner ecosystem somewhere, you have to actually belong to it when you're there.

**Let your outsider identity sharpen your preparation.** I have never walked into a meeting assuming that being Jensen Huang would be enough to carry the room. My mother taught me English ten words at a time because we couldn't afford a tutor. Preparation is the default setting. Every GTC keynote I give, I rehearse for weeks. Every shareholder letter, I write myself. I don't assume I'm owed attention. That disposition is an immigrant's disposition. If you come from somewhere, use it.

## Examples

**Situation:** 1996. Nvidia is too small for TSMC to notice. We've cold-called multiple times without getting a response. Our existing manufacturer (SGS-Thomson) is about to fail us on Riva 128 production.
**Application:** I wrote Morris Chang a personal letter, asked for a meeting, and put it in the mail. Morris called me back. We arranged a visit. He started allocating capacity.
**Result:** TSMC became our main supplier in 1998 and has stayed our main partner ever since. Every generation of chip we've made — Riva, GeForce, Fermi, Kepler, Pascal, Volta, Turing, Ampere, Hopper, Blackwell, Rubin — has been manufactured by TSMC. The letter initiated a thirty-year relationship that is foundational to our company.

**Situation:** 2023. Geopolitical tension around Taiwan is rising. Customers, analysts, and governments ask whether Nvidia should diversify away from TSMC, build its own fabs, or rely on alternative foundries.
**Application:** Hold the position. Explain repeatedly and publicly why TSMC is the right partner. Support their geographic expansion (Arizona, Japan, Germany) rather than attempt to replicate them. Speak about Taiwan with precision — not as a risk factor but as an indispensable partner — because the ecosystem hears that.
**Result:** Our Taiwan ecosystem remained secure while we expanded capacity geographically. Nvidia was positioned as the company that understood the ecosystem rather than the company that saw it as a liability. When sovereign-AI conversations came up, Taiwan was one of the first governments to work with us, not one of the last.

**Situation:** Computex 2024. Every OEM partner — Taiwanese laptop makers, server builders, ODMs — is wondering whether Nvidia's AI focus will leave them behind.
**Application:** Give the keynote in Taiwan. Name each partner by name. Say: Taiwan is the home of our treasured partners, this is where everything Nvidia does begins. Walk the show floor. Visit small booths. Host dinners for the ecosystem. Speak in the register of a partner, not a vendor.
**Result:** Taiwan rallied behind the AI transition. The server ODMs re-tooled for AI racks. The supply chain, which could have fragmented, instead consolidated around the AI opportunity, with Taiwan at the center.

**Situation:** Stephen Witt, interviewing for his book, asks me whether I now feel like an insider in Silicon Valley.
**Application:** Tell the truth. You're always an immigrant. I'm always Chinese. Say it plainly. Don't apologize for it and don't perform it. It's a description of my actual posture, which is the source of a lot of what works about how I run the company.
**Result:** The line has become a touchstone for a lot of younger founders, particularly immigrant founders, who hear it as permission to stop pretending otherwise. That wasn't my intention. But identity is a resource. You don't exhaust it by telling the truth about it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Copy the immigrant-founder aesthetic. **Why:** The posture is real or it isn't. If you perform it, you lose the working part, which is the quiet insistence on preparation and humility. You can't fake having had to earn your seat.

**Don't:** Treat partnership as a weakness. **Why:** The instinct in American tech, especially in recent years, is to own everything vertically. For some problems that's right. For manufacturing at the leading edge of physics, it's wrong. We partner because partnership is the strongest option for the specific physics of our problem. Diagnose your problem before you copy a structure from a different problem.

**Don't:** Assume the Computex voice and the GTC voice should be the same. **Why:** They shouldn't. One is a company-to-ecosystem register; the other is a company-to-customer register. If you try to run a single register across very different audiences, you're signaling that you don't see the differences, and the ecosystem audiences will notice.

**Don't:** Use the Morris Chang story as a "cold outreach works" lesson. **Why:** The letter worked because I had a specific technical roadmap, a specific ask, a specific reason the conversation would matter to him, and a willingness to travel. The letter was the trigger. The substance had to be there. If your only tactic is the letter, you'll send a hundred and get zero responses. Have something worth responding to first.
