# Learn in Public

## When to Use
- You're the CEO or a senior leader and you want the organization to learn how you think, not just what you decided.
- You want to model intellectual honesty from the top — being wrong publicly is the demonstration that being wrong is survivable.
- You want to break a PR-approved culture where every executive statement has been sanitized into a press release.
- You want a staff meeting that operates like a teach-in, not a theater piece.

## Fails When
- The leader performs humility but never actually updates. "Learning in public" is a posture, not a show. If the view at the end of the meeting is identical to the view at the start, you didn't learn anything; you rehearsed.
- The surrounding culture hasn't internalized intellectual honesty yet. In a political environment, a leader who admits uncertainty in public gets weaponized. Build the culture first. Then go public.
- Public embarrassment slides into cruelty. The point is that the company learns from a mistake — not that a person feels small. If leaders can't tell the difference, the team will stop taking the risk of saying anything at all.
- The leader is the only one doing it. If you learn in public but everyone else still presents polished conclusions, you've made a one-way vulnerability that doesn't spread. The whole senior team has to be willing.

## Core Concept

As CEO, I make decisions in front of the whole company. Intellectual honesty. Tell the truth, acknowledge failure, be willing to move forward and learn from past mistakes. That's not a banner on a wall at Nvidia. That's how we actually run the staff meeting. When I'm working a problem and I don't know the answer, I say I don't know the answer, and then we work it on the whiteboard together. When I made a call a month ago that turned out to be wrong, I say so — in the room, with the same people who heard the original call — and we figure out what we're going to do differently. This is not performance humility. This is the operating posture.

The reason this matters more than most executive practices is that it sets the tempo for everybody else. If the CEO will admit in public that they were wrong, then the VP will admit in public that they were wrong, then the director will, then the engineer will. Intellectual honesty flows downhill from whoever is willing to demonstrate it first. If the CEO never does, nobody else will either. What you get instead is a company where every meeting is a performance of certainty, every status update is a press release, and the real problems live in private side-channels where they can't get fixed. That's the upside-down V organization. I don't want that.

This works only when paired with two other things. One: a flat organization and a town-hall staff meeting where everyone hears everything at the same time. You can't "learn in public" if there's no public. Two: a CEO who can actually update. Not perform updating — actually hear a contradiction, work it in real time, and walk out of the room with a different position than the one they walked in with. If you can't do that, you shouldn't be running the staff meeting like a teach-in. You should be running it like a broadcast, and everyone will know which one is which within about two weeks.

Intelligence is about to be a commodity. What's left is courage, intellectual honesty, the absence of ego, the ability to be vulnerable in public so that you can create. As inventors and creators know, they are humiliated, laughed at often — because what they do isn't always perfect. You need the humility and the vulnerability and yet the courage to put yourself out there and be laughed at. I can tolerate that. I can tolerate a lot of discomfort. I'm the senior-most person. That's part of the deal. If I can't handle being wrong in front of the staff, I'm not equipped to run a flat organization, and the whole architecture falls apart.

There's a companion piece that people miss. When someone on the team is wrong — a PIC, a VP, a general manager — I don't take them aside. I don't say "great job" in the meeting and then privately correct them afterward. I don't take people aside. We're not optimizing for not embarrassing somebody. We're optimizing for the company learning from our mistakes. If a leader can't handle the slight embarrassment, they can come talk to me. But it's never happened. That's the bargain. Everybody gets to learn from every mistake, including mine — and no one gets a private protection layer that isolates them from the culture.

## How to Apply
1. **Run the staff meeting like a teach-in, not a broadcast.** You're not there to deliver a prepared message. You're there to work on real problems with real people in the room. Your own reasoning is on the whiteboard, visible, revisable.
2. **When you don't know, say so.** "I don't know" is a leadership move, not a confession of weakness. It invites the smartest person in the room to contribute. It models that knowing-not-knowing is the starting point, not the ending point.
3. **When you were wrong a month ago, say so — in the same room, with the same audience.** This is where trust is built. Watch how the team's behavior shifts in the weeks after. Suddenly they're saying "I got that one wrong" in their own meetings. That's the culture spreading.
4. **Fix the mistake on the whiteboard, then move.** Don't dwell. The lesson is the update itself, not the flogging. Draw the new picture, commit to the new action, move on. Intellectual honesty is about updating, not about marinating.
5. **Don't pull people aside for private correction.** If someone missed a goal or mis-ran a project, discuss it in the meeting where the work happens. Not to humiliate — to educate. Ten people learn the lesson instead of one. The person being corrected also learns that the feedback channel is real and not subject to politics.
6. **Call out over-polished presentations.** When somebody shows up with a slide deck so polished that the reasoning has disappeared into the design, bring them back to the board. "Walk me through how you got here." Polish is a hiding place. Strip it off.

## Examples

**Situation:** Nvidia's stock price drops sharply. Everyone is watching. The CEO has to do a company meeting and explain what happened — except, as Jensen puts it, you're not entirely sure why, you're not entirely sure how long, and you're not entirely sure how bad.

**Application:** Jensen shows up to the company meeting anyway. He stands in front of precisely the people he cares most about, and he tells them what he knows, what he doesn't know, and what he's watching. He doesn't hide behind a rehearsed PR line. He reasons through it live — the parts he can see, the parts he can't, the parts he's worried about. It's uncomfortable. It's the job.

**Result:** Not a single person on his leadership team leaves during the downturn. The team has seen the CEO do the hard thing in public, under pressure, without a script. That's what they needed to see in order to know the company is led by a person who can handle it — not a person who hides behind a communications team.

**Situation:** A six-hour e-staff meeting. A general manager has just presented a plan that Jensen thinks is wrong. In most companies this would become a quiet conversation after the meeting.

**Application:** Jensen launches into it in front of everyone. "Mike, you need to get Tegra done. You've got to tape Tegra out. Guys, this is an example of how not to run a business." One former employee describes it as the most embarrassing, humiliating thing they've ever seen. Jensen's rationale: offering private feedback behind closed doors would slow him and the company down by requiring the scheduling of separate meetings, and it would also deprive junior employees of a learning opportunity. "We're not optimizing for not embarrassing somebody. We're optimizing for the company learning from our mistakes."

**Result:** Every other leader in the room has just learned something — not just about the Tegra plan, but about what "intellectual honesty" means when it collides with the real world. They will run their own meetings differently. When the GM who got called out emails later, it's with a smiley face — not because the moment was pleasant, but because the culture's premise is well understood: the embarrassment was for the company, not against the person.

**Situation:** A new college grad is in a robotics meeting with Jensen, three VPs, and two e-staff members. Something complicated is being debated.

**Application:** Jensen reasons through it out loud, on the whiteboard. He shows his work — including the steps where he's unsure, the trade-offs he's weighing, the places he's changing his mind. The new college grad is watching Jensen think. By the time the decision is made, the grad has learned not just what was decided but how Jensen gets to decisions like this one.

**Result:** A year later that new college grad, now running a small piece of a project, runs a meeting the same way. The culture has been transmitted. This is how the staff meeting as a teach-in scales: every room becomes a classroom where the senior person is willing to be the student for parts of the material they don't fully know.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Perform humility without actually updating.
**Why:** People can tell within a meeting or two whether "I was wrong" is a rhetorical move or a real update. If the view never changes, the "learning" is theater and everybody starts quietly ignoring it. The point is to be genuinely corrigible. If you're not corrigible, don't pretend.

**Don't:** Learn in public while everyone else around you presents polished certainty.
**Why:** If you're the only one doing it, you've made yourself the lone exception and the norm is still performance culture. The job is to make intellectual honesty contagious, which requires your senior team to do the same thing. Start naming the norm explicitly and correcting the people who hide behind polish.

**Don't:** Confuse being hard on an idea with being cruel to the person.
**Why:** The culture requires this distinction to be visible. We are hard on the idea because we are optimizing for the company learning. We are not cruel to the person because that kills the next person's willingness to bring a hard problem into the room. If leaders can't tell the difference, they'll break the culture. The test: did the person leave the room more likely or less likely to say something hard the next time?

**Don't:** Rehearse the "learn in public" moment.
**Why:** A rehearsed admission is not an admission. It is a press release in the grammar of vulnerability. The whole posture depends on the reasoning being live. If you caught yourself drafting how you were going to "admit uncertainty" before the meeting, back up — either you actually aren't uncertain (in which case say the thing you actually think), or you are (in which case you don't need a draft, you just need to say it).

**Don't:** Protect certain executives from public feedback.
**Why:** The moment the rule becomes "public accountability, except for Joe," the culture unravels. Either everyone is subject to being corrected in front of the room, or no one is, and the "no one" path has dead ends everywhere — hoarded information, hidden problems, private side-channels. The CEO has to take the hit first and the hit most often. That is the tax of the most senior person.

**Don't:** Run this in a culture that isn't ready.
**Why:** In a political environment where careers are made or broken on optics, "learning in public" becomes a trap. A rival will weaponize the CEO's admission. A subordinate will mimic it and get punished. Build the intellectual-honesty culture, the flat org, the town-hall staff meeting, the whiteboard norm first. Then the public posture works. Order matters.
