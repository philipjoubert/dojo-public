# The Production Function of Progress

## When to Use
- You are building a research organization, an internal R&D group, a grant program, or any institution whose output is supposed to be discovery rather than iteration on a known product.
- You are trying to figure out why an extremely well-resourced team is generating far less than the sum of its parts.
- You are making an argument internally for faster decisions, more pluralism in funding, or higher tolerance for risk, and you need a frame that is more rigorous than "let's move faster."
- You are thinking about the cultural inputs — openness, egalitarianism, risk tolerance — that determine whether a team actually produces novel work.

## Fails When
- **The real problem is execution, not inputs.** If the team simply is not shipping, redesigning the funding structure is a distraction. First check whether the basic mechanics of doing the work are healthy.
- **Applied as a totalizing theory.** Not every slowdown is a production-function problem. Sometimes it is one bad manager, one bad hire, or one bad dependency. Pluralism and speed will not fix a local pathology.
- **Used to justify infinite experimentation without accountability.** Risk tolerance is not the same as no evaluation. If every project is "exploratory," nothing is.
- **Applied to domains where measurement really is tractable.** Manufacturing and operations are legible. The production-function framing is most useful exactly where legibility is hard; do not import it into contexts where it is easy.

## Core Concept

Progress is not a natural phenomenon. That is one of the most consequential things I have come to believe, and it is at odds with the ambient optimism in technology circles. Progress does not emerge inevitably from human activity, any more than electricity flows inevitably from copper wire. It has inputs — cultural openness to heterodox ideas, classical liberal values that let anyone's ideas be taken seriously regardless of background, healthy scientific processes, functional entrepreneurship, reasonable distribution of gains. Those inputs can strengthen or decay. Understanding progress as having inputs means we can deliberately improve them, and we have to, because the current trajectory is not particularly encouraging.

There is a paper by Nick Bloom and colleagues, "Are Ideas Getting Harder to Find?", that shows something alarming: the returns to R&D are not merely declining, they are declining steeply. We need dramatically more researchers to sustain the same rate of discovery. The number of people required to keep doubling crop yields, or to maintain Moore's Law, or to produce pharmaceutical breakthroughs, has been rising for decades. When I have asked senior people at major technology companies what fraction of their potential productivity they believe they are capturing, they tend to say around 20%. Eighty percent of the value is being left on the table. The number might be wrong in the specifics, but the people closest to the work believe the waste is enormous. Something in the production function is broken.

Part of what is broken is what I think of as the legibility problem. We do not have a meta-understanding of research and innovation as domains. We do not know what makes certain labs extraordinarily productive while labs with similar resources produce much less. We do not know how to identify the most promising research directions in advance. We do not have good ways to measure what matters, and what gets measured gets managed. In domains where measurement is easy, optimization is possible. But the most important aspects of research — problem selection, taste, the ability to recognize a live lead — resist measurement. Pre-product-market-fit metrics in companies have the same property: the data can mislead because you do not yet know what success looks like. The correct posture is high-throughput qualitative feedback. With twenty users, look at everything they do. With twenty research projects, study them deeply rather than trying to extract premature metrics. The question of problem selection may be one of the most important questions in all of research, and we have essentially no methodology for it.

The structural problem is funding. The current US system, dominated by NSF and NIH, has calcified in ways that impede the research it was meant to enable. Grant applications take months to prepare and months more to review. A researcher with an idea in January submits in March, hears back in September, and may not receive funds until the following year. The opportunity may have passed entirely. Pre-World War II science funding was far more variegated — private foundations, industry, universities, state governments, each with different priorities. The Vannevar Bush "Endless Frontier" vision, whatever its merits in 1945, has evolved into something that manufactures homogeneity. We should have at least ten meaningfully different major funders: some fast, some high-variance, some focused on young researchers, some on interdisciplinary work. The current near-monopoly on major funding decisions creates intellectual monoculture. Everyone optimizes for the same review criteria, pursues the same safe directions, avoids anything too unusual for reviewers. Fast Grants, during COVID, committed to decisions within 48 hours. We did not solve evaluation; we accepted more risk in exchange for speed. And what we found was that the bottleneck really was speed. The talent existed. The resources existed. What was missing was responsiveness.

The hopeful implication of viewing progress as manufactured rather than automatic is that we have agency over it. If progress were automatic, its current slowdown would be mysterious or natural. If it is manufactured, the slowdown means we are manufacturing it poorly — and we can do better. The manufacturing view converts despair into a research program.

## How to Apply

1. **List the actual inputs to the work you care about.** Funding source, decision speed, risk tolerance, cultural openness, mentorship structure, talent pipeline, measurement regime. Treat these as adjustable, not fixed.
2. **Attack speed first.** Delays in research funding are not neutral — they are destructive. Every month a promising researcher waits is a month of potential discovery lost. Every year a grant sits in review is a year the person who had the idea may have moved on. Collapse decision cycles before you try anything else.
3. **Deliberately create pluralism in evaluation.** One funder, one review panel, or one person with veto power over direction will produce monoculture. Create multiple evaluators with meaningfully different philosophies. Accept that they will disagree. The disagreement is the point.
4. **Use qualitative depth before product-market fit.** Twenty users, studied deeply, will tell you more than metrics. Twenty projects, studied deeply, will tell you more than KPIs. Resist the urge to impose legibility prematurely.
5. **Protect the cultural inputs.** Openness to heterodox ideas, classical liberal willingness to take anyone's ideas seriously regardless of background, comfort with disagreement, tolerance for failure. These are not atmospherics. They determine whether the output is actually novel.
6. **Preserve tacit knowledge transfer.** Mentorship — proximity, apprenticeship, spending time together — transmits how to think, not just what to know. You cannot replace it with process. You can only stop undermining it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A well-funded internal research lab has hired excellent people but produces very little actual discovery. The leadership's reflex is to add more process — quarterly reviews, OKRs, milestone gates.
**Application:** The instinct is wrong. Process is already the bottleneck. The inputs to fix are speed of decision-making, diversity of evaluators, and risk tolerance. Decide within days, not quarters. Let a small group of senior researchers fund work on judgment rather than on slide decks. Measure the portfolio qualitatively at long intervals rather than quantitatively at short ones.
**Result:** Decision cycles compress from months to days. Researchers stop writing pitch decks and start doing work. The portfolio looks messier on a dashboard and produces more actual output.

**Situation:** A country is trying to reform its research funding system and the default proposal is a single large new agency modeled on NSF.
**Application:** The structural problem with the existing system is monoculture, not insufficient scale. Another large agency with similar criteria compounds the problem. Fund ten different bodies with meaningfully different philosophies — some fast, some focused on young researchers, some high-variance, some interdisciplinary — and let them compete. The diversity is the feature.
**Result:** Researchers gain access to multiple serious paths for different kinds of work. Risk-taking becomes possible because there is somewhere to go that does not punish it.

**Situation:** A team is debating whether to keep running small-number user research or switch to large-scale A/B testing. They are pre-product-market-fit.
**Application:** Pre-product-market-fit metrics are actually unhelpful because the data can mislead — you do not yet know what success looks like. Double down on high-throughput qualitative feedback. With twenty users, look at everything they do. The legibility will come later, once the product actually has a shape.
**Result:** The team stops chasing statistical significance it cannot achieve on small N, and starts noticing the qualitative signals that actually distinguish early product-market fit from noise.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the slow pace of innovation as natural or inevitable.
**Why:** If it is natural, nothing is your job. If it is manufactured, the slowdown is a choice, and specific interventions can change it. The manufacturing view implies agency; the natural view implies passivity. Passivity is always wrong here, because the trends are not in your favor.

**Don't:** Build a single canonical review process for everything.
**Why:** A single review process produces a single kind of research. Review criteria leak into research direction. If you want variance in output, you need variance in evaluation. The most productive eras of science had many competing funders with many competing tastes; the least productive eras are the ones that standardize.

**Don't:** Confuse speed with sloppiness.
**Why:** Fast Grants was fast because it accepted more variance on individual decisions, not because it cared less about quality. The goal is responsiveness — matching the pace of decisions to the pace of opportunity. Researchers with good ideas should not be waiting a year. That has nothing to do with lowering the bar on the underlying work.

**Don't:** Let credentialism suppress the people who would actually move the frontier.
**Why:** Progress tends to come from the margins — from graduate students, outsiders, people who are not fully socialized into existing paradigms. Cultures that defer too heavily to credentials and status suppress exactly the challenges that would move them forward. Classical liberal values — that anyone's ideas can be taken seriously regardless of background — are not a political preference, they are a production-function input.
