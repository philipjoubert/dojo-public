# Multiple Mental Models

## When to Use
- You are facing a decision that genuinely crosses disciplinary boundaries. Compensation structures, market-entry calls, organizational design, pricing strategy, hiring executives, research portfolio allocation. Any of these will punish a single-lens thinker.
- You have noticed that a specific executive on the team keeps missing the same kind of problem. Probably a mental model gap.
- You are building a personal learning agenda and wondering what to read.
- You are deciding whether to hire from within your industry or outside of it.

## Fails When
- Breadth becomes a substitute for depth. The goal is not to become a dilettante who knows a little about everything and a lot about nothing. Deep expertise in at least one domain remains essential.
- You borrow a framework without understanding its limits. A tool imported from Hollywood or chemistry or finance may or may not map to your problem. The person who applies every framework universally will do worse than the person who applies one framework carefully.
- You confuse collecting models with using them. Being able to name a concept is not the same as being able to recognize when it is relevant and when it is not.

## Core Concept

The most consequential decisions in business are rarely solved by any single discipline. A compensation structure requires understanding finance, psychology, and organizational behavior simultaneously. A product decision demands fluency in engineering constraints, user psychology, and competitive dynamics. A strategic choice about entering a new market involves economics, regulatory analysis, and cultural factors no single framework can encompass. The person who can only see problems through one lens will consistently arrive at suboptimal solutions, not because they lack intelligence, but because they lack the conceptual vocabulary to even perceive certain dimensions of the problem.

Charlie Munger has the famous speech about multiple mental models, arguing that you should crib the best mental models from different industries and disciplines. This is exactly right, and probably underrated even among people who nod along when they hear it. Most people acquire one professional framework, through education or early career, and then apply it universally. Finance people see everything as cash flows and discount rates. Engineers see everything as systems and optimization problems. Sales people see everything as relationships and pipelines. Marketing people see everything as positioning and messaging. Each perspective captures something true. Cash flows do matter. Systems do have constraints. But each perspective also has blind spots, things it cannot see because they are not part of its vocabulary. The person who only knows finance cannot perceive product quality issues that will eventually destroy the cash flows. The person who only knows engineering cannot perceive organizational dynamics that will prevent the optimal system from being built.

There is a well-known formulation: "To a man with a hammer, everything looks like a nail." The situation is worse than this suggests. It is not just that you try to hammer things that should not be hammered. It is that you literally cannot see the screws and bolts, the things that need different tools entirely. The hammer is not just your preferred solution; it is the only solution you can conceive of, because your mental vocabulary lacks the words to describe alternatives. I have seen this repeatedly with both founders and large company executives. The engineer-founder tries to solve management problems with better systems rather than recognizing some problems are fundamentally about people. The sales-background executive tries to solve product problems with better relationships rather than recognizing some problems are fundamentally about what you are building. Each is intelligent and well-intentioned, but each is blind in predictable ways.

The solution is not to become a dilettante who knows a little about everything and a lot about nothing. Deep expertise in at least one domain remains essential. The aim is to consciously cultivate mental models from disciplines outside your primary expertise, so you can at least perceive problems your native framework would render invisible. You do not need to be an expert in finance to recognize that a cash flow question is relevant. You do not need to be an expert in organizational behavior to recognize that a people dynamics question is relevant. You just need enough vocabulary in those domains to see the questions, even if you then need genuine experts to answer them.

A warning about Silicon Valley specifically. It has developed its own dominant mental models that can blind people just as effectively as expertise in any single discipline. There is tremendous emphasis on disruption and the idea that incumbents are always vulnerable. Sometimes true, treated as more universally true than evidence supports. There is emphasis on TAM analysis that I think is often misleading. People get into TAM analyses, business school types always talking about where the profit pools are, which is a really unambitious way to look at the world. It assumes markets are fixed and you are just carving out a share, rather than that you might create entirely new markets. The most valuable companies are often ones that would have looked terrible in a TAM analysis when they started. The zero billion dollar markets, the ones that do not yet exist in anyone's spreadsheet, are frequently where the most counterfactual GDP ends up being created. The consumer product instincts that pervade Silicon Valley often get inappropriately applied to B2B. The consumer model assumes you have users by the wheelbarrow; people talk about MAUs as their way of referring to users, but as the old television show had it, "I'm not a number, I'm a free man." In B2B, each customer is a relationship that matters. The 1% who have problems might be the 1% most likely to become your largest customers. Treating them as statistical noise rather than individuals to be served is a category error. Silicon Valley has figured out many things other contexts have not. But it is a monoculture in certain respects, and monocultures create blind spots.

## How to Apply

1. **Build a minimum viable model set.** Anyone in a leadership position should have working vocabulary across several domains. From finance: NPV, IRR, hurdle rates, time value of money, option value under uncertainty. From engineering: systems thinking, constraint analysis, feedback loops, how local optimizations create global problems, technical debt as a general concept. From product thinking: jobs-to-be-done, activation energy, switching costs, network effects. From sales: qualification frameworks, pipeline stages, the difference between champions and decision-makers, how procurement can derail what looked like a done deal. From psychology: incentive structures, loss aversion, social proof, commitment bias. "Working vocabulary" means you recognize when a model is relevant, even if you cannot fully apply it yourself. That is usually enough, because you can then ask someone who can.

2. **Study other industries explicitly.** Most problems have been faced before by someone, usually in a different industry or context. Software shares important structural features with Hollywood: both involve uncertain creative processes where talent is scarce, both require large capital outlays with uncertain returns, both produce products with high fixed costs and low marginal costs. Hollywood has been navigating these dynamics for a century. Professional services firms, law firms and consulting and accountancy, have thought much more carefully about training and development than most technology companies. They take training really seriously in a way Silicon Valley typically does not. The chemical industry in the early twentieth century was doing uncertain R&D investments, figuring out how to allocate capital to research projects with highly unpredictable outcomes, decades before the venture capital industry existed. DuPont worked out the dynamics of basic research leading to unexpected applications, which we now associate with the Valley. Go read about them.

3. **Study businesses, including failures, as a practice.** At Stripe, we spend a lot of time studying other companies, to the point where I sometimes joke we would not win a business trivia competition but we would have a respectable showing. Apple and Amazon are both enormously successful technology companies, but you cannot imagine two companies that work more differently. Apple has a functional organizational model, one person responsible for all of design, one for all of engineering, with heavy coordination at the top. Amazon has a GM model, each business unit relatively autonomous with its own P&L. Both models work. Both have serious failure modes. Understanding why each company chose its model, and what trade-offs that choice implies, is knowledge that turns out enormously useful when you face analogous decisions. Failures are equally instructive. Wang grew to billions in annualized revenue and then disappeared so completely that most people have not heard of it. Osborne Computer had similar dynamics. These were not stupid people. They were smart people making decisions that seemed reasonable at the time but failed to account for shifts in the competitive landscape. Understanding what they missed is inoculation against missing similar things yourself.

4. **Recognize the Silicon Valley monoculture and deliberately counter it.** If everyone in your information diet is from the same five podcasts and the same set of VCs, you are operating with shared blind spots. The antidote is deliberate exposure to other ways of thinking, other industries, other historical periods. The world's number one chess player, Magnus Carlsen, has also studied the most chess history. Not a coincidence. The correlation between historical knowledge and current excellence is probably causal.

5. **When facing a problem, run the lens exercise.** Deliberately ask yourself what the problem would look like through different frames. If this is a strategy question, what does it look like through finance, through psychology, through engineering? What would a Hollywood producer see that you are missing? What would a chemistry lab director notice? Not every lens will be relevant, but the exercise surfaces dimensions your primary framework renders invisible.

6. **Import mental models through people as well as reading.** Surrounding yourself with people who have different expertise is a way of borrowing their models. Hiring executives from different backgrounds imports ways of thinking you lack. Building a company where intellectual diversity is valued creates an environment where multiple perspectives actually get surfaced. You cannot personally acquire deep expertise in everything. You can assemble a leadership team whose combined mental vocabulary is far broader than your own.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder with a deep engineering background is trying to figure out why their senior engineering team is grinding on a reorg. Every proposal they make is technically coherent; none of them seem to land with the people affected. The founder's instinct is to push harder on the design of the new structure, to get the system right.

**Application:** Running the lens exercise. Through an engineering lens: is the structure correct? Yes. Through a psychology lens: who has loss-averse concerns about status or autonomy in the new structure? Through a sales lens: who is the champion among the senior engineers who will carry the proposal inside the team, and do they actually have buy-in? Through a finance lens: what is the option value of staying with the current structure versus switching, and does the team understand why the switch is worth the transition cost? The reorg is a sale, not a spec. The founder has been optimizing the spec. The missing work is the sale.

**Result:** The founder spends two weeks one-on-one with the six most affected engineers, starts by listening for concerns rather than explaining the structure, identifies two champions who shape the proposal itself. The reorg lands. What was blocking them was not engineering quality; it was a set of problems their native framework rendered invisible.

**Situation:** A Silicon Valley-shaped team is building a B2B product and treating customer support as a cost center, measured by MAU ratios and ticket close time. A small percentage of merchants keep hitting edge cases and writing long complaints. The team is rationally ignoring them as statistical noise.

**Application:** The consumer product instinct is causing the blindness. Through a B2B sales lens, each customer is a relationship. The 1% who are sophisticated enough to hit edge cases might be the 1% most likely to grow the fastest and become the largest accounts. Through a product lens, edge cases are often symptoms of underlying design flaws that will eventually surface at the mean. Reframe support from cost center to intelligence signal. The 1% are telling you something about the product.

**Result:** The team repositions support as a forward deployed product research function. Bugs that would have compounded get caught. Two of the complaining merchants become the company's largest customers in the next 18 months. The consumer product monoculture almost cost the company its best future accounts.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Collect frameworks without developing depth in any of them.
**Why:** A person who can name twenty models but cannot apply any of them carefully will lose to a person with one deeply mastered model. The point of multiple mental models is not to show off breadth; it is to recognize which lens is relevant to this problem, then apply it well, often with the help of actual experts.

**Don't:** Import a framework without considering whether it maps.
**Why:** Hollywood practices do not translate directly to software, nor should professional services firm tournament structures drop into a startup without modification. The value of cross-industry comparison is analogical, not literal. Use other industries to see your problem more clearly, not to prescribe a solution from them.

**Don't:** Treat Silicon Valley frameworks as universally correct.
**Why:** Silicon Valley has figured out many things other contexts have not. It is also a monoculture in certain respects. Disruption is overemphasized. TAM analysis is a really unambitious way to look at the world. Consumer product thinking misleads when applied to B2B. The path to founding a company quickly is valorized over spending years mastering a domain, yet many of the most consequential contributions have come from people like Herb Boyer at Genentech, who spent decades in basic research before commercializing their expertise. The monoculture creates predictable blind spots. Name them, then counter them.

**Don't:** Mistake the breadth of mental models for a substitute for really really really caring about the specific problem in front of you.
**Why:** Multiple mental models help you see problems more clearly. They do not do the work of solving the problem. The work of solving it still requires the depth, the detail, the pace, the attention. Breadth without depth is dilettantism. Depth without breadth is tunnel vision. You need both.
