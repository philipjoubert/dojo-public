# Punchline First (Information Hierarchy)

## When to Use
- The user is sending an async message (Slack, email, memo, doc) where the recipient is busy, distracted, and skimming.
- The user is asking for approval, sign-off, or a decision.
- The user is giving a status update where the manager wants to know what's happening, what's at risk, and what to do next.

## Fails When
- The medium is a personal note, a long-form essay, or a story where the slow build is the point.
- The conversation requires emotional warmth or relationship-building first (a delicate piece of feedback to a peer, a sensitive personal request).

## Core Concept

Assume your reader will skim, not read. **Most people are busy doing something else when your message arrives. They're feeling dull, distracted, or cranky. They're scrolling through their phone. They have 15 browser tabs open and running late. Mostly, their lives were fine before you arrived (and will be fine after you leave).**

You can't assume undivided attention. This shapes the CONTENT of what you should include — not just the form. Skimmable writing increases the chances your ideas are actually heard.

The principle: **Put your recommendation, action item, or question at the top. Context below.** This lets folks read as much or as little of the context as they need. It's especially useful for remote teams, where most communication happens in writing.

🚫 Action items, backstory, bottlenecks, and context all jumbled together.
✅ Action items or question at the top, context separately below.

Don't be coy when you can be direct. Don't speak in stream of consciousness. Assume your manager is task-switching or checking your Slack message between meetings — make it super easy for them to catch up on context and dive into what you want to discuss.

🚫 "This happened, then this other thing, this important thing that's completely buried, and then this other thing."
✅ "Here's the minimum viable backstory, so we can spend time on the juicy stuff."

A related point: **simplify first, not last.** One common approach is to write a lot, then trim later. But there's no point writing 500 words only to cut 90% of it. If you start knowing you need to be at 50 words, you draft a different note from the start — one that gets your point across within that constraint. Simplifying first surfaces options that are categorically better. By setting a different target, you play a different game altogether.

When Wes had to write a hard email about a misunderstanding — needing to be respectful, empathetic, and firm — she asked herself, "What if this note could only be three sentences?" The result was 80% shorter than what it would have been, and BETTER. The goal wasn't to explain everything. The goal was to get the recipient to agree.

## How to Apply

1. **Lead with the punchline.** What's the recommendation, ask, or decision you need? Put that in the first sentence.
2. **Then specify what you need.** Approval? FYI? A reply by Friday? State it explicitly. Don't make people guess what they should do with your message.
3. **Then give the minimum context.** A sentence or two on why. Where you left off. What changed.
4. **Then layer in supporting detail BELOW.** Rationale, alternatives considered, risks, links. The skimmer can stop at the top; the careful reader can keep going.
5. **End on a positive note.** Your reader's eyes see the first and last words more than what's in the middle.
6. **Read it before you send.** Ask yourself: "Is this easy to read? If they only read the top, would they get the point?"

## Examples

**Situation:** A marketer needs their CMO to approve a budget reallocation. The first draft is six paragraphs of context with the ask buried at the end.

**Application:** Rewrite as:

> Need your sign-off to move $40K from paid social to email by Friday for the Q2 launch.
>
> Why: paid social CPCs are up 60% YoY and email open rates are climbing. Detailed analysis below.
>
> If no objection by Thursday EOD, I'll move forward.

**Result:** The CMO sees the ask, the deadline, and the default behavior in three seconds. They can read the analysis if they want, or just reply "approved."

---

**Situation:** A team member sends Slack: "The new ad is updated in our Google Doc. It's published in FB as well, but not running."

**Application:** What's wrong: the recipient doesn't know if (a) something is broken and they should worry, or (b) it's an FYI. Rewrite:

> FYI — new ad is updated and published on FB. Not running yet because we're holding for the Tuesday launch date. No action needed from you. Will share results next Wednesday.

**Result:** Recipient knows in five seconds whether they need to do anything. They don't.

---

**Situation:** Customer service call. The user could waste their breath explaining backstory before they even know if they're talking to the right person.

**Application:** "I'm having trouble logging in. Who would be the right person to talk to?" Three to five sentences max.

**Result:** You save your breath and your sanity. Don't make things more complicated than they have to be.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Bury the ask under context "to be polite."
**Why:** Politeness is doing your reader a favor. Making them dig for the actual point is the opposite — it's making them do extra work to figure out what you want. The polite move is to be direct AND warm.

**Don't:** Dump links, screenshots, and details with no framing.
**Why:** "Here's the doc" with no context forces the recipient to context-switch into your problem from scratch. Always say where you left off, what you need, and the next step. Otherwise it's lazy thinking — and the fact that they have to ask what you need from them slows everyone down.

**Don't:** Hide an FYI in a wall of text.
**Why:** If it's an FYI, say "FYI" at the top. People stop reading after they hit the first wall. If your "no action needed" message looks identical to your "needs approval today" message, you've trained your reader to ignore both.

**Don't:** Confuse "punchline first" with "no context at all."
**Why:** The recommendation goes first, but the context goes BELOW. For high-stakes decisions — spending money, customer-facing changes, legally binding stuff — err on the side of more context, including the criteria you used, options you considered, risks, and assumptions. The hierarchy doesn't mean stripping detail; it means ordering it for the skimmer.

**Don't:** Ask "what do you think?" when you have a recommendation.
**Why:** That makes your manager do the work of generating an answer from scratch. Instead: "I recommend X. Here's the rationale, data, and risks. If you agree, I'll start by doing [first step]." Now they can edit instead of generate.

**Don't:** Trim a 500-word note to 50 words at the end. Start at 50.
**Why:** When you simplify last, you carry the assumptions of the long version into the short one. When you simplify first, you write a different note entirely — usually a better one. Set the constraint up front.
