# Spiky Point of View (SPOV)

## When to Use
- The user is producing content (essays, talks, pitch decks, marketing copy, internal strategy memos) and the work reads like everyone else's.
- The user wants to be hired, followed, or remembered for something specific — not just be "in the conversation."
- The user is going into a meeting, podcast, or panel and wants to leave a mark instead of nodding along.

## Fails When
- The take is contrarian for sport — there's no rationale underneath. That's intellectually lazy, and it's irritating to be around. Don't be that person.
- The user has no credibility on the topic. A spiky POV needs to be on a topic where you're credible and that's relevant to your audience. Otherwise it's a random hot take.
- The user wants 100% consensus before saying it out loud. That day will never come.

## Core Concept

We live in a noisy world. Whichever industry you're in, there are thousands of other people like you trying to get noticed. Unless you distinguish yourself, you'll never get a chance to show how different you actually are.

A **spiky point of view** is a perspective others can disagree with. It's a belief you feel strongly about and are willing to advocate for. It's your thesis about topics in your realm of expertise. It's almost impossible to imitate — it's the culmination of your experience, skills, personality, instincts, and intuition.

The five elements:

1. **It can be debated.** If everyone agrees with it, it's too middle of the road.
2. **It isn't controversial for the sake of it.** No pot-stirring. There's nothing more irritating than a contrarian who just wants attention.
3. **It teaches your audience something relevant they don't already know.** Don't summarize information. Don't just ask questions. Offer a frame that makes people see their problem in a new way. The reaction you want: "Hmm I hadn't thought about it that way but this is so true. This is making me rethink a lot of things."
4. **It's rooted in evidence — but doesn't have to be a proven fact.** Defensible. Not absolute objective truth.
5. **It requires conviction.** You're trying to convince someone because you genuinely believe they'll be better for it. Stance of advocacy, bias toward action.

A spiky POV doesn't have to be a hot take. It can be the **frame you use to describe an idea in the first place**. When Wes says "there are 2 ways to build community: be an expert OR be a beginner" — that's a spiky frame. Someone could disagree and say "the two ways are be an expert OR be a curator." Both are valid SPOVs. The frame itself is the spike.

The fear is real: if you share it, people could disagree. You could be wrong. Your POV won't cover an edge case. This is why most people choose the safer path of saying what everyone around them already agrees with.

Don't.

## How to Apply

1. **Draft 10+ candidate SPOVs.** Many will be tame on the first pass. That's fine. The act of writing them sharpens what you actually believe.
2. **Test each one against the five elements.** Can someone disagree? Is it rooted in evidence? Is it actually relevant to your audience? Do you feel conviction, or are you fishing?
3. **Keep refining the generic ones.** Your initial drafts will have rounded edges because you're used to making things palatable. Push for specificity. "Teach as you learn" is too high-level. "If you don't have it all figured out — this is a feature, not a bug. There are 2 ways to build community: be an expert, or be a beginner" is sharp.
4. **Notice what makes you a little nervous to publish.** That's usually a good sign. Resist the urge to add preambles, caveats, disclaimers, counterpoints. The point of a SPOV is to start a conversation, not to be airtight.
5. **Do not copy other people's SPOVs.** When you parrot someone else, your energy and conviction won't match. Customers can feel that.
6. **Let the market react.** Post on LinkedIn or Twitter. Mention it on a customer call. Watch eyes light up — or not. If you get replies (agreement OR disagreement), it's working. Silence is the bad signal.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is building a course platform and asks Wes how to stand out among 50 other instructors teaching marketing.

**Application:** Wes pushes them past the safe answer ("I have 10 years of experience"). She prompts: "What's a generally accepted best practice that everyone seems to do or believe is good, that you think is not useful or doesn't work based on your experience?" The instructor lands on: "Start with why has done more harm than good. It's given a generation of professionals permission to indulge in navel-gazing. No one cares about your why — they only care about how you can help them." That's a real SPOV. It's defensible, it teaches a frame, and it's spiky enough that some Simon Sinek fans will push back hard.

**Result:** The instructor's positioning becomes a magnet. The right students self-select in. The wrong ones go elsewhere. Conversation starts.

---

**Situation:** A marketer is preparing a 30-minute conference talk titled "How we grew our newsletter to 50K subscribers." The outline reads like every other growth talk.

**Application:** Reframe the entire talk around a SPOV: "Cheap leads are actually expensive." Then walk through how they ignored conventional advice (cheap CPCs, free lead magnets, low-friction signups) and instead optimized for total cost of acquiring AND servicing the customer. Lead with the spiky frame; the case study illustrates it.

**Result:** The talk has a spine. People remember the line, not the chart.

---

**Situation:** A founder is writing their first newsletter and keeps producing posts that summarize what other people have said about a topic.

**Application:** Wes's prompt: "What do you wish more people understood? This should be something that makes you a little angry or frustrated. Conviction and emotion matter — if it's something you're ambivalent about, it's not worth your time to convince others." The founder realizes they're frustrated by how prototyping is treated as gospel. They write: "Prototyping is unnecessary in 90% of cases. Take 30 minutes to scenario plan. You'll find your logic gaps faster than a 5-day sprint will." That's the post.

**Result:** The post gets restacked. Comments split — some agree, some push back. Both groups are now in conversation with the founder's brand.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Caveat your SPOV to death the moment someone might disagree.
**Why:** The whole point is to spark dialogue. If you preempt every objection with a disclaimer, you've sanded off the spike. You end up back at middle-of-the-road. People can disagree — that's the feature, not the bug.

**Don't:** Pick a SPOV that's outside your area of credibility just because it sounds edgy.
**Why:** A hot take from a non-expert is just noise. Your SPOV needs to live where your experience and your audience's needs overlap. Otherwise people read it as posturing, and you lose trust on the topics where you DO have authority.

**Don't:** Copy someone else's SPOV verbatim because it sounds good.
**Why:** Your conviction won't match. Customers can sense it. The energy will be off, and the SPOV that worked for the original person will fall flat coming from you. Spend the time to find the version of the idea that's actually yours — even if it's adjacent or builds on someone else's frame.

**Don't:** Demand 100% consensus before publishing.
**Why:** That day will never come. If you wait until you can prove your SPOV is universally true, you've waited yourself out of having a SPOV at all. Ship at "strong opinion, loosely held." Update when better evidence shows up.

**Don't:** Mistake a complaint for a SPOV.
**Why:** "I hate when companies do X" is a complaint. "Companies that do X are optimizing for short-term metrics at the cost of long-term brand equity, and here's why that math is wrong" is a SPOV. The difference is the frame and the rationale.
