# Increase Desire, Don't Just Decrease Friction

## When to Use
- User has spent months making the button bigger, the signup faster, the copy shorter — and the line still isn't going up.
- User keeps reaching for discounts, bundles, and "price goes up next month" promotions.
- User's funnel is technically excellent and growth has plateaued anyway.
- User is asking "how do we improve conversion?" when the better question is "do people even want this?"

## Fails When
- **Real friction is the actual blocker.** — If the signup form is broken, the page takes 30 seconds to load, or pricing is genuinely confusing, fix that first. You can't increase desire past a wall.
- **The user has zero distribution.** — Desire-building requires an audience that can be moved. If nobody is seeing the product, no amount of messaging change will help. Increase reach first.
- **The product genuinely has no story.** — Some commodity products (a generic API, a B2B middleware) really are bought on price and friction. Forcing a desire narrative onto them produces cringe.

## Core Concept

Most marketers, salespeople, UX designers, and product leaders default to one move: reduce friction. Make the button bigger. Use brighter colors. Shorter copy. Above the fold. CTA everywhere. Reminder emails. Discounts, bundles, promotions, reverse promotions ("the price goes up next month!").

The underlying assumption is: *if I make this easier or cheaper for you, you'll eventually want it.* This logic is fundamentally flawed.

🚫 "If I just remove one more friction point, they'll convert."
✅ "Why don't they want this enough to push through friction in the first place?"

People jump through hoops all the time for things they want. They will pay more, do more work, try harder. There are also tons of free apps you don't want — free costs nothing, and you're still not itching to download them. So friction isn't the limiter. Desire is.

To drive growth, you can't just decrease friction. You have to increase desire. There will be a ceiling on what you get from removing friction. You can only discount so much before the product is free, or your business is unprofitable. When 90% of your marketing strategy is friction reduction, it's a race to the bottom.

There's a deeper reason this matters: cognitive dissonance. Your brain likes being consistent with itself. When a customer waits in line, jumps through hoops, fills out the application — they tell themselves "I would only work hard for things I really want, so I must really want this thing." Value gets created out of thin air. The reverse also happens: "I had to sign up because I wanted the gift card, not the product. They had to bribe me to join. I must not like this product very much." Heavy discounting and aggressive friction reduction can make customers tell themselves the wrong story.

Look at the line outside Momofuku Milk Bar. Same people who say "I'm too busy, I don't have enough time" wait in line for ice cream. People wait for new iPhones. Harvard makes you take the SAT, write essays, get two recommendations — and the dean isn't saying "let's remove all requirements because it's friction." They know their value, and they're betting you will too. If you get in, you say "take my money."

You can't spam your way into people's hearts. Find ways to earn their attention instead.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit where your team's last 90 days of effort went.** What percentage was friction reduction (CRO, button copy, page speed) versus desire creation (story, positioning, proof, narrative)? If it's 80/20 toward friction, you've found the leak.
2. **Ask: what story does the customer get to tell themselves when they use your product?** Not what features the product has — what story. How does it help them be the person they always imagined being? That story is the desire engine.
3. **Find the irrational hoops your best customers already jumped through.** Did they wait? Pay more? Switch from a competitor? Configure something complicated? Those hoops are evidence of desire — and signal what to amplify, not eliminate.
4. **Stop the discount reflex.** Before you reach for a 20% off promo, ask: "Will this make customers tell themselves the right story or the wrong one about us?" If it teaches them you needed to bribe them, the discount is destroying brand equity faster than it's building revenue.
5. **Pick ONE desire lever this quarter.** Not five. One. A new positioning line. A flagship case study. A point-of-view essay. A founder narrative. Something that tells a sharper story to the inner ring.
6. **Measure differently.** Desire shows up in retention, referrals, willingness to pay, and word-of-mouth — not in next-week conversion rate. If you measure desire work with friction-work metrics, you'll always be disappointed.

## Examples

**Situation:** SaaS founder spent six weeks A/B testing the signup flow — three CTA colors, two copy variants, a one-click Google login. Conversion moved 0.4%. They want to run another test.

**Application:** "You've squeezed the friction lemon. There will be a point where you've made the button as big as it can be. The next 0.4% is going to cost you another six weeks. Instead, ask: what's the strongest reason someone in your bullseye should want this enough to push through any signup flow? If you don't have a confident answer, no button color will save you. Spend the next six weeks rewriting the homepage hero, shipping one flagship customer story, and tightening the positioning. Then re-measure."

**Result:** Conversion eventually moves more from sharper desire than from any further friction work. And the gains compound — desire affects retention and referral, not just signup.

**Situation:** Consumer brand keeps running 20% off promos every six weeks to hit the quarterly number.

**Application:** "Cognitive dissonance is real. Every promo is teaching your customers a story about you: 'they had to discount to get me, so this product must not be that valuable, and I should wait for the next sale.' You're training the market to ignore your full price. Pull the discount lever. Spend that margin on a story that earns full-price desire — a campaign, a partnership, a piece of content that makes someone want the brand." Walking away from short-term sales is hard. "Most people want long-term brand equity, but aren't willing to live with worse click-throughs or lower revenues."

**Result:** Short-term revenue dips. Within two to three quarters, full-price sell-through climbs and CAC drops because pull is doing work paid acquisition was doing before.

**Situation:** Course creator obsesses over making the application easy — short form, no essay, instant approval.

**Application:** Counterintuitive: make the application harder. The altMBA didn't beg people to join. Application required real reflection. Harvard makes you jump through a lot of hoops. The dean doesn't say "let's remove all requirements because it's friction for you." Friction in the right place creates cognitive dissonance that increases perceived value. "I worked hard to get in, so this must be worth it."

**Result:** Higher-quality applicants, higher completion rates, higher word-of-mouth.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reflexively send another reminder email to people who didn't convert.
**Why:** You've sent so many reminder emails that people start to unsubscribe or report you as spam. The fifth reminder doesn't increase desire — it tells the recipient you're desperate. That story works against you.

**Don't:** Confuse a frictionless product with a desirable one.
**Why:** Slick design, fast loading, one-click signup — none of that matters if the customer doesn't want to create an account. There are tons of free apps you still don't download. Frictionless and unwanted is a real combination.

**Don't:** Use bribes (gift cards, free trials with auto-charge, signup bonuses) as your primary growth lever.
**Why:** Bribes invert the desire story. The customer thinks "I had to sign up because I wanted the gift card, not because I wanted the product. They had to bribe me to join." You're paying to teach customers your product isn't valuable on its own.

**Don't:** Treat "increase desire" as a vague aspiration with no owner.
**Why:** Friction reduction has a checklist — that's why teams default to it. Desire creation is messier: it depends on your business, your levers, your relationship with customers, your story. Without an owner, it never happens. Assign desire work to a specific person with a specific deliverable.

**Don't:** Try to increase desire and decrease friction in the same week with the same person.
**Why:** They are different muscles. Friction work is convergent and tactical. Desire work is divergent and strategic. Asking one marketer to do both means one of them gets done badly. Sequence them.
