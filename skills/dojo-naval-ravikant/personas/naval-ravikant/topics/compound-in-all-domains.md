# Compound Interest in All Domains of Life

## When to Use
- The user is looking at a major life choice — marriage, having kids, moving cities, choosing or dropping a close friend group — and framing it purely as an isolated decision.
- The user has quietly left a string of relationships, cities, or jobs and is wondering why nothing in their life feels solid.
- The user is optimizing their career heavily and ignoring the rest of the portfolio.
- The user is evaluating whether someone is "long-term" material — spouse, co-founder, close friend — and wants a concrete test.
- The user is tempted to treat a close relationship as transactional or short-term because the other person "hasn't delivered lately."

## Fails When
- The relationship is actually damaging and long-term framing is being used to rationalize staying. The long-term-games logic is powerful; it can also trap people. If someone is being exploited, the right move is to leave, not to compound further with someone who isn't a long-term player.
- The city, community, or industry is actually dead, and the user needs permission to restart rather than a reason not to. Not every environment is worth staying in.
- The user is a chronic self-sacrificer whose problem is that they stay too long, not that they leave too often.

## Core Concept

The existing `long-term-games.md` topic covers this from the business side — iterated games, trust networks, reputation, why burning bridges is costly. This file is about the same principle applied to the rest of life.

**Compound interest applies to everything, not just money.** It applies to relationships. It applies to your body. It applies to your reading. It applies to your reputation. It applies to your mental state. The math of compounding doesn't care what asset you're compounding — small edges held consistently over long horizons annihilate large edges grabbed once.

The three big ones in life are wealth, health, and happiness. We pursue them in that order; their importance is reversed. All three compound. The one most people do the worst on is the one they've been lectured about least, because the cultural conversation about compounding is almost entirely about money.

Most people's lives look like this: career compounded ruthlessly, body neglected until year fifteen, friendships restarted three times, marriage bet on late and quickly, reading replaced by news, mental practice never started. They optimized one slot and left the rest flat.

The winners over a lifetime — the people who are genuinely healthy, happy, wealthy, and connected in their sixties and seventies — didn't just compound money. They compounded in parallel across every domain that mattered to them. They kept showing up in the same friendships, the same body, the same reading diet, the same daily practice, the same marriage. The flywheel got heavy and then got magical.

## Relationships Compound

Getting married — really married, not married-with-a-foot-out-the-door — is a compound-interest play on one person. The trust accumulates. The communication becomes more fluent than anything you could build with a new partner in the first five years. The joint history gives you a shared map that new relationships don't have. The risks you can take together grow as the trust grows.

Serial short relationships don't compound. Every restart is the counter going back to zero. The first six months of every new relationship is the low-return period — you're learning each other, the communication is clunky, the trust is unearned. Most of the value shows up later. If you keep restarting, you never get there.

Same for friendships. Getting and keeping a small number of close friends for decades is worth more than a wide network of acquaintances rebuilt every five years. Your oldest friends know you at a resolution that nobody new can reach inside ten years of work. They're also the ones who will still be there when you're sick, old, divorced, broke, or unfamous — the states where your optional acquaintances evaporate.

If you look at the people who seem to have rich social lives in their sixties and seventies, the common thread is: they invested hard in a small number of relationships and stayed. They didn't optimize their friend roster. They showed up for the same fifteen people for fifty years.

## Health Compounds

Health is the cleanest example of compounding that people consistently get wrong.

The fitter you are, the easier it is to stay fit. The more deteriorated, the harder it is to climb back. This is basically algebraic. A body in decent shape at thirty becomes a body in decent shape at fifty with almost no heroics — small daily inputs, sustained. A body that was neglected for twenty years and is being frantically rehabilitated at fifty-five is a near-impossible project, not because it's physically impossible, but because the habits of neglect are now twenty years deep and the habits of maintenance never formed.

My number one priority in life — above happiness, above family, above work — is my own health. Physical first, mental second, spiritual third. Then my family's health, then their wellbeing, then the rest of the world. This sequence sounds selfish and it is the opposite of selfish. My health is the foundation on which I'm able to be present for the people who matter. If it collapses, nothing I was trying to do for anyone else still functions.

The daily morning workout is the unskippable input. I do not start my day until it's done. I don't care what the day requires. The workout happens first. It's been the single habit with the highest positive impact on my life, measured across every domain I care about.

You don't need to be doing anything exotic. Walk, lift, stretch, swim, bike, whatever you'll actually do every day. The specific protocol matters far less than the consistency. The best workout for you is the one you'll do every day.

Jerzy Gregorek's line: **easy choices, hard life; hard choices, easy life.** The short-term sacrifices — the workout, the skipped dessert, the early bedtime — buy you the long life where your body still works. The easy choices today — skip the workout, eat the junk, stay up — buy a long period of diminishing capacity later. The short-term pain is the long-term gain. Your brain will always overvalue the near-term comfort. Override it.

## Reading and Knowledge Compound

A separate topic file covers the specific practice of reading. The compound-interest angle is this: knowledge in a domain compounds non-linearly. Year one of reading in a field is brutal because you have no scaffolding and every sentence is hard. Year ten is effortless because you have the latticework to hang any new idea on; half of what's in the new book is already in your head in some form and you're just noting what's new.

This is why older people who read seriously through their career seem magically wise. They aren't smarter. They have thirty years of scaffolding. You can't buy that. You can only begin it.

One hour a day of reading in math, science, and philosophy will put you in the top echelon of human thinkers within seven years. Seven years is not nothing, but seven years is also nothing. Seven years is going to pass whether you read during it or not.

The person reading a minute a day — which is most people — ends year seven roughly where they started. The person reading an hour a day is a different person. The gap is the compounding.

## Reputation Compounds

Reputation is the slowest-compounding asset and the highest-leverage one.

Every act of integrity, every delivered promise, every time you did what you said you'd do even when no one would have known if you hadn't — it all goes into the flywheel. You don't see it moving for a long time. Then, somewhere around year five to fifteen depending on the industry, it inflects. Work starts finding you instead of you finding it. People pitch you. Deals get offered to you that nobody pitches to your less-reputable peers because the reputable person is the one the counterparty actually wants to deal with.

Warren Buffett gets offered deals no one else gets offered because of his reputation. That's not a parable. That's the actual mechanism — decades of compounding reputation translating into unique deal flow. His opportunity set at eighty is wider than it was at fifty because the compounding kept going.

Reputation is binary on integrity. You can be wrong in public, fail in public, pivot in public — all survivable. You cannot be dishonest. One lie caught, one theft, one deliberate misrepresentation — the counter resets. You won't rebuild in a comparable timeframe.

Treat integrity as a rule, not a weighing. Not "is this worth lying about." Just: no.

## The "Can I See Myself With This Person for Life" Test

Here's the practical test I use and recommend: **if you can't see yourself working with this person for life, don't work with them for a day.**

This sounds extreme. It's not. Here's why: every relationship you engage with is either compounding or not. Short-term relationships don't compound — you do one transaction, extract what's available, move on. You've just joined a game with a capped return. The more short-term slots in your life, the less room for the ones that actually compound.

When you meet someone and your instinct says, *this is someone I'd still be in business with in twenty years* — invest. Money, time, trust, favors. When you meet someone and your instinct says, *I can get one transaction out of this person* — don't. The second category is a trap. They'll feel transactional to you because they are, and they'll treat you the same way because that's the game being played.

The test generalizes beyond business. If you can't see yourself married to this person in twenty years, don't get into the relationship. If you can't see yourself close to this friend in twenty years, don't tell them your secrets. If you can't see yourself still living in this city in twenty years, don't buy a house. The prep work for long-term people is not casting wider; it's getting more selective about who gets the deep seat.

## Signals That Someone Is a Long-Term Player

Concrete markers. Use these the way you'd use a due-diligence checklist.

- **They've stayed in things for a long time.** Same industry, same city, same close friends, same marriage. Continuity is the raw material. Someone who's restarted everything every four years has a pattern, and the pattern is the pattern.

- **Their reputation precedes them specifically.** Not "everyone says good things" — everyone says generic good things about everyone in polite society. Specifically: people cite specific episodes. "I did three deals with X over ten years and he rounded every close call in my favor." "I've been her friend since college and she's been the same person the whole time."

- **They behave well when no one's watching.** You can only infer this indirectly: how they treat waiters, how they treat people who can't help them, how they handle tiny transactional moments, how they talk about their exes and former partners.

- **They've passed at least one short-term temptation you know about.** Everyone who plays long-term gets offered a cheat at some point with a real payoff. Ask about those moments — not directly, but listen for them in stories.

- **They'd rather lose than win badly.** They'll walk away from a deal rather than strain a relationship. They'll eat a cost rather than push it onto a partner.

## Signals That Someone Is Not

- **They change partners, cities, or scenes frequently** — not once, which is normal, but as a pattern.
- **They're vindictive toward enemies.** Disproportionate punishment of someone who crossed them tells you how they'll treat you when you, inevitably, disappoint them.
- **They treat service workers badly.** The quickest integrity test available. Run.
- **They talk a lot about being trustworthy.** Usually a cover. Real integrity is demonstrated silently.
- **Every interaction is an extraction.** You notice they keep score in small ways, that favors get called in, that nothing is freely given. That's the game they're in, even if the time horizon looks long.

## Restarting Is a Tax

This is the part most people underweigh. **Every restart zeros the counter.**

Switch industries: the specific knowledge you built transfers at maybe 20%. The relationships were warm only in the old industry. The reputation was illegible in the new context. You're close to starting from zero, even if you feel wiser.

Change cities: the local network you built has to regrow. The coffee-shop routines, the friend groups, the doctor you trusted, the professional favors-owed — all gone. You'll rebuild in five to seven years if you're deliberate.

Change marriages: every compounding trust benefit reset.

Change core friends: the backlog of shared context lost.

Sometimes restart is right. Some industries die. Some relationships end. Some cities become uninhabitable for you. The question isn't whether restart is ever correct — it obviously is. The question is: **how often are you restarting, and is the instinct to restart avoidance dressed up as change?**

If your pattern across a decade is: three cities, two industries, five partners, ten close friends — you're paying a restart tax most of your life. You may think you're gaining freshness. You're actually just preventing yourself from ever seeing what compounding feels like. The people who seem magically ahead in their fifties have been showing up in the same game with the same people for most of their life.

## Protect the Inner Circle

The small number of people who have earned your trust over decades — the ones you can say anything to, the ones whose integrity you've verified across hundreds of interactions — are your most valuable asset outside of your own health.

Protect them. Help them. Promote them. When they call, answer. When they ask for something, give it. When their reputation is on the line, defend it. This is not charity. It is the most self-interested possible use of your time, because their success is the flywheel that generates most of your future opportunity. And the reciprocity runs both ways — which means when you need help, genuine high-leverage help, they are the ones who will actually move for you.

The outer rings of acquaintances are politeness and networking. The inner circle is the thing. A handful of people. Invest accordingly.

## Single-Player Games

Most of the compounding that matters in life is in single-player games. Training yourself to be happy, fit, calm, kind, curious — no external validation, no scoreboard, no comparison. The work is internal.

Humans are bad at single-player games. We're evolved for tribal status games — multiplayer, zero-sum, competitive. Our attention naturally flows to the scoreboards. That's why yoga and meditation are so hard to sustain: there's no extrinsic return. Purely single-player. No one will notice if you keep going or if you stop.

But the single-player compounds are the ones that actually improve your life. The multiplayer games — status, social rank, industry awards — are zero-sum, and to win you have to make someone lose, and that's how you become an angry person. The single-player games — fitness, calmness, curiosity, integrity, internal peace — are positive-sum with yourself. Every day's input adds to tomorrow's starting point.

The closer your life gets to being mostly single-player games, the more compounding you capture, and the less noise from the multiplayer games decides your mood.

## How to Apply

1. **Inventory your compounding accounts.** Wealth, health, reputation, close relationships, knowledge domain, mental practice. Which ones have a counter that's been running for more than five years? Which ones have been reset in the last three?

2. **Identify the counters that need to stop resetting.** Usually one or two. Commit to not restarting them for at least ten years, absent a ruin-scenario reason.

3. **Apply the lifelong test to anyone new.** New partner, new close friend, new hire, new business partner: can I see myself in relationship with this person in twenty years? If no, don't give them the deep seat, no matter how tempting the short-term payoff.

4. **Protect the inner circle deliberately.** Five to fifteen people. Know who they are. Invest time in them even when you're busy. Especially when you're busy.

5. **Treat your daily workout as the non-negotiable input.** Before work, before calls, before email. Peace of body is the foundation everything else sits on.

6. **Spend an hour a day on long-form reading in a foundational domain.** Not news. Not feed. A book, slow. This is the knowledge compound.

7. **Keep one big desire at a time; drop the rest.** The more active desires, the less compounding in any one of them. Fewer, deeper.

8. **Track restart frequency and audit it.** How often are you starting over? Is it for reasons, or is it a pattern?

## Examples

**Situation:** A 33-year-old tech exec is considering whether to stay in her current close friend group — people she's known since college who are now less ambitious than her — or deliberately pivot to a higher-status, more successful social circle who could help her career.

**Application:** This is a category error. She's conflating business network (which is a career asset and should be high-status, high-specific-knowledge-density) with close friendships (which is a life asset and compounds on decades of shared history, not on status). The college friends are almost certainly her actual life asset — people who knew her before the resume was anything, who will still care about her if the career blows up, whose trust has been verified through a thousand small interactions. The new circle is professional network, which is a different portfolio. Build the new professional network. Keep the friends. Don't let status games move you to drop decades of compounded trust for people who would evaporate the moment your career did.

**Result:** She keeps the friends, builds the professional network on top. Ten years later the college friends are the ones at her hospital bed when she has a serious health scare; the high-status network sends thoughtful emails. She understands in that moment what she almost traded.

---

**Situation:** A founder is considering a fourth city move in ten years for a new startup opportunity. His wife is increasingly quiet about it.

**Application:** Three prior moves means three prior compounding accounts that got zeroed — local network, local friendships, local trust, local family integration, local doctor, local school for kids, local everything. His wife going quiet is the signal. She's paid the restart tax three times and is running out of willingness. The opportunity is real, but the question isn't whether the opportunity is real — it's whether the compounded cost of another restart is worth the upside. For a genuinely transformational bet, maybe. For a Series A with decent but not extraordinary expected value, almost certainly not. The pattern is starting to be the pattern. Every move he tells himself is the last one. The most valuable thing he could probably do right now is stop moving, let the compounding resume, and build the next thing from where he is.

**Result:** He turns down the role, reinvests in his current city and community, builds the next company from where he is. The company takes six months longer to get going but has lower burn and a much happier household. His kids stay in the same school.

---

**Situation:** A reader asks: how do I know if someone is a long-term person?

**Application:** Three tests, increasingly strict.

*Test 1 — Continuity.* Have they stayed in things for a long time? Same city, same partner, same industry, same close friends? Not rigidly — nobody should be frozen — but continuous. If everything about their life is less than three years old, the pattern is the pattern.

*Test 2 — Short-term passes.* Ask about moments where they could have cheated and didn't. Not directly; listen for them in stories. If the stories never include a moment where they turned down a shortcut, they may not have hit that moment yet — or they may have taken the shortcut and rewritten the story.

*Test 3 — The wholesale swap.* Would you want to be this person? Not their career, their body, their money — them, fully, for 24/7 for the rest of your life. If yes, or if yes with small modifications, they're probably net positive to compound with. If no, you're romanticizing a piece of them and will be disappointed by the rest.

**Result:** The reader applies the tests to a potential business partner. Partner passes tests 1 and 3, fails test 2 — can't produce a single story about turning down a shortcut. Reader invests cautiously, with a smaller initial commitment than planned. Six months later partner shows short-term behavior in a small transaction. Reader exits cleanly without having bet the house. Months of work saved because the diligence was run.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Optimize your career compound while neglecting every other compound.
**Why:** You will arrive at fifty with a great resume, no friends, a broken body, and a marriage of convenience. The career was the easy one. The other compounds are the ones that make the career worth having.

**Don't:** Treat close friendships transactionally.
**Why:** Counting favors, measuring effort, comparing who reached out last — these are symptoms of running a short-term game inside what was supposed to be a long-term relationship. The friendship will collapse without you noticing because you'll have already eroded the trust that makes it work.

**Don't:** Confuse "long-term person" with "convenient person."
**Why:** Some people are pleasant and nearby and low-friction, and therefore you spend a lot of time with them. That's not the same as them being long-term. Low-friction acquaintances are fine. Don't upgrade them to deep-seat slots just because they're around.

**Don't:** Use long-term framing to rationalize staying in a relationship that's genuinely harming you.
**Why:** The logic is powerful and can trap people. Long-term games require long-term *people* — and if the other party isn't a long-term player, they're a short-term player inside what looks like a long-term container. The honest move is to exit cleanly and restart the compound with someone who actually belongs in the iterated game.

**Don't:** Let the inner circle erode silently.
**Why:** The close relationships degrade when unattended, the way a muscle atrophies when unused. Ten missed birthdays in a row, ten unreturned calls, and the trust you spent twenty years building is quietly gone. The cost of maintenance is small. The cost of rebuild is almost infinite — there is no rebuild at that depth, only a new compound starting from zero.

**Don't:** Neglect your body because you're too busy compounding your wealth.
**Why:** You are racing to be rich in a body that won't work by the time you get there. The year-one investment in daily exercise is trivial; the year-twenty return is being able to actually enjoy the life the money bought. Skip the exercise and you've wasted the wealth.

**Don't:** Restart in response to short-term frustration.
**Why:** Compounding goes through dips. Every relationship has years that are harder than others. Every career has plateau periods. The instinct to restart hits hardest when you're in a dip. That's usually the worst possible moment to act on it — the compound is about to inflect and you're about to exit right before it pays. Wait, check whether the dip is structural or situational, and only restart if it's truly structural.
