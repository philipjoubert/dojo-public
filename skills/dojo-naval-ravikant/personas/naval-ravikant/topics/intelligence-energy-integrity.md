# Intelligence, Energy, Integrity

## When to Use
- The user is hiring for any seat at any level — but especially for early-stage companies where each hire is disproportionately consequential.
- The user is choosing a co-founder.
- The user is evaluating an investor or board member.
- The user has a reservation about a candidate and is trying to rationalize around it.

## Fails When
- The user is already committed to the person and the question is how to make it work, not whether to hire. At that point the checklist is less useful than working on the relationship.
- The role has a fourth dominant requirement (specific domain expertise, regulatory qualification, language) that can't be traded off against these three. The checklist is necessary but not sufficient.

## Core Concept

When picking people to work with — hire, partner, invest — there are three non-negotiable qualities:

1. **Intelligence**
2. **Energy**
3. **Integrity**

You need all three. Missing any one is fatal.

**Intelligence** is whether they're smart at the specific thing you need. Not generic IQ — specific ability to reason, pattern-match, and judge in the domain. Someone who's brilliant at math and terrible at sales is not "smart" for a sales role. Smart is always smart-at-something.

**Energy** is whether they're intrinsically motivated for this specific work. Not whether they're a high-energy person in general. Whether they're lit up by *this*. A world-class mathematician trying to run a restaurant will look low-energy, because the energy isn't there for this domain. A natural-born operator put in the wrong role will look lazy, because the motivation won't fire. Energy is not a general trait. It's a pairing between the person and the work.

**Integrity** is whether their actions match their words. Not whether they say they have integrity — that's the opposite signal, often a con-man tell — but whether you can observe the integrity in what they do, especially when no one's watching. Do they keep their word when it costs them? Do they tell hard truths? Do they behave consistently across audiences?

All three. No compromises. The arithmetic of pairing failures:

- **Intelligence without energy** — wasted potential. They'll see the right answer but never get there. Lots of brilliant, low-output people fit here. They look like an asset on paper and produce nothing.
- **Intelligence without integrity** — a smart, working crook. The worst combination. They'll use the intelligence to steal, cheat, or extract, and they'll be good at covering it. You will not detect it until they've damaged you.
- **Energy without intelligence** — hard work in the wrong direction. They'll break things faster than they fix them. Exhausting to manage because the output is voluminous and wrong.
- **Energy without integrity** — a dangerous hustler. Fast, convincing, and eventually catastrophic.
- **Integrity without intelligence** — honest but unable to help. Good person, bad hire for the specific role.
- **Integrity without energy** — good intentions, no execution. Nice human, nothing gets done.

The point is that no single quality is enough, and no two are enough. You need all three in the specific pairing you're hiring for.

## Start With Integrity

If you have to pick an order, start with integrity. Then intelligence. Then energy.

**Why integrity first.** If someone has intelligence and energy but no integrity, you have a smart, hardworking crook. They will eventually cheat you. Intelligence and energy become weapons in the hands of someone who won't constrain themselves. You can lose more in a year with this hire than you made in five.

**Why intelligence second.** Once you know someone won't cheat you, you need them to actually solve the problem you're hiring them for. Without intelligence, they'll try honestly and fail.

**Why energy third.** Energy is the easiest to verify quickly (you can see it in a conversation) but also the easiest to misread (people can perform energy for short periods). Let it be the last filter, after integrity and intelligence have been satisfied.

## How to Test for Integrity

Integrity is the hardest to evaluate because people lie about it. The more someone talks about being honest and trustworthy, the more suspicious you should be — that's the con man signal. Real integrity is demonstrated. It's boring. Nobody narrates it.

Better signals:

**Watch how they treat service workers.** If someone treats a waiter or a taxi driver badly, they'll eventually treat you badly. The quickest test available. The way someone behaves toward people who can't affect their career is a pure signal of who they are.

**Watch how they handle enemies.** If they're vindictive toward people who wronged them — disproportionate, personal, lasting grudges — you could become an enemy someday. Expect the same treatment. Vindictive people are always vindictive. You're never an exception.

**Check their self-esteem.** People with high self-esteem tend to be easy to work with. They don't need external validation. They don't backstab. They sleep fine at night. Self-esteem is the reputation you have with yourself — and good people have built it by actually being good, over time, including when nobody was watching.

I had a friend once whose company I invested in. The company failed, and he could have wiped out all the investors. Instead he kept putting more and more of his personal money in. Through three different pivots he put personal money in until the company finally succeeded, and in the process he never wiped out the investors. When I thanked him, he got offended. He said, "I didn't do it for you. I didn't do it for my investors. I did it for me. It's my own self-esteem. It's what I care about. That's how I live my life." That's the kind of person you want to work with. That's integrity. Not the talk. The structural inability to live with himself if he took the easy way out.

**Check back-channel references.** Not the ones they gave you. The ones they didn't. Find someone who worked with them five years ago and ask specifically what they were like when things went wrong. Integrity shows up under stress, not in the easy moments.

**Watch what they do when no one's looking.** This is the hardest to check in advance, and it's why some judgment errors on integrity only become visible after you've been burned. But the earlier signals — how they treat waiters, how they treat enemies, their self-esteem — give you most of the signal before the test.

## How to Test for Energy

Energy is domain-specific, so the test is: do they light up when talking about *this*?

Not about business in general. Not about themselves. About the specific work you want them to do. Watch what happens when the conversation moves from abstract career talk to the concrete domain. If the energy goes up, you have energy for this role. If the energy stays flat or drops, you don't, no matter how impressive the resume.

I don't believe you can talk someone into having energy for something. When I was younger, I tried. I had this idea that you could sell someone into doing something, sustain their motivation for them. You can't. You can inspire them initially, but that only works if you're a king trying to get troops to charge into battle — and even then, they have to figure out the fight themselves once the initial charge is over. Sustaining motivation for the long term requires it to come from inside them. If it doesn't, you become the crutch, and the moment you let go, they stop.

So the test is essentially: would this person do this if I weren't paying them? Not literally — they need to eat. But can I imagine this person in this role on a Saturday afternoon with no boss watching? If yes, the energy is real. If no, you're going to be the crutch.

## How to Test for Intelligence

Intelligence is the easiest of the three to test, because you can give someone a problem and see what they do with it.

Specifically: give them a problem from the domain of the role. Not a puzzle, not a brainteaser, not a generic IQ check. A real problem from the real work. Watch how they reason through it. Do they ask the right clarifying questions? Do they consider second-order effects? Do they notice where the data is thin? Do they arrive at a decent answer in a reasonable amount of time?

Smart for the role isn't smart-in-general. A brilliant physicist would be the wrong hire to run your sales team no matter how high their IQ. Intelligence for this job is the pattern recognition and judgment specifically required to do this job well.

## Signals Are What They Do

Integrity is revealed through signals — what they do despite what they say. The non-verbal stuff. The default behavior. The behavior when nobody's watching.

It's much like status signaling. If you openly bid for status, that's a low-status move. If you openly talk about being high-integrity, you're probably not high-integrity. Con men talk the most about being trustworthy. People with actual integrity rarely describe themselves that way — they just behave consistently, and others eventually notice.

Pay attention to small actions that reveal default behavior: do they follow through on a small commitment, like a minor scheduling promise? Do they credit others for ideas? Do they admit when they don't know something? Do they bail out of the easy lie, even when the easy lie would have worked?

These are tells. The person who passes on the small lie under no pressure is the person who'll pass on the big lie under huge pressure. The reverse is also true.

## The Trio in Early-Stage Hiring

In an early-stage company, each hire is disproportionately consequential. You're not adding to a team of fifty — you're shaping the DNA of a team of five. Every person changes the culture by 20%.

This is where the checklist matters most. A single hire who scores 95% on intelligence and energy but 60% on integrity can destroy a five-person team in ways it'll take you a year to even detect. And the damage will be invisible until you've lost the best people to the toxic presence.

For early hires, overweight integrity. You can afford to hire someone who's 90% as smart as the ideal but rock-solid on integrity. You cannot afford to hire someone who's 120% as smart as the ideal but 70% on integrity. The math doesn't work.

This is why the "right cultural fit" framing — when done honestly — is usually just the integrity check by another name. You're not filtering for personality. You're filtering for the default behaviors that will compound across years.

## How to Apply

1. **Check all three before hiring.** Not two. Not "we'll work on integrity later." Three. Walk away from misses on any of them.

2. **Start with integrity.** If integrity fails, don't evaluate the other two — there's no deal to save. Close the interview politely.

3. **Use domain-specific tests.** Give a problem from the actual work. Watch them reason. Watch how the energy shows up. Watch whether they tell the truth when they don't know the answer.

4. **Back-channel references.** The ones you didn't get from them. The earlier the stage of company, the more this matters. Ask specifically how they behaved under stress.

5. **Weigh small signals.** How they treat your assistant. How they handle a schedule change. How they describe their last manager. These small moments are more predictive than the prepared answers to rehearsed questions.

6. **For co-founders, test with real work.** Don't just have dinner and vibe-check. Build something together on a small scale first. Integrity, intelligence, and energy all reveal themselves in the actual doing of the work, fast. Two weeks of building together is worth six months of conversations.

7. **For investors, run the same checklist.** A cap-table partner is a hire who can't be fired. Apply the three-part test at least as rigorously as you would for a full-time hire.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is torn between two senior engineering candidates. One is brilliantly credentialed, high-energy, and has been through two successful exits — but two of his previous reports mentioned, off the record, that he takes credit for others' work. The other is nearly as technical, clearly integrity-driven, but slightly less pedigreed.

**Application:** Don't hire the first candidate. The off-the-record integrity signal is the kind of thing that only shows up in back-channel references, and when it does, it's real. He'll be an asset until the first high-pressure moment, and then he'll damage the team to protect himself. The second candidate is the correct hire — the integrity compounds, the marginal technical gap is small and learnable, and the team culture stays intact.

**Result:** Integrity beats credentials at the margin. Always. Hire the second candidate. Call the first candidate's previous references one more time to confirm before declining.

---

**Situation:** A founder is interviewing a potential co-founder — a friend of a friend, technically excellent, intellectually impressive, hungry for the opportunity. The founder keeps getting a vague "off" feeling she can't articulate.

**Application:** The "off" feeling is data. It's pattern-matching on small signals she's receiving below conscious processing — maybe the way he talks about his last boss, the slight exaggeration of past outcomes, the way he praises her when he wants something and cools off when he doesn't. Her conscious mind hasn't connected the dots, but her unconscious mind is flagging an integrity issue. The instinct is usually right, especially on co-founder decisions where the cost of being wrong is catastrophic.

**Result:** Don't proceed. Invent a good reason to slow the process (the real one, if she can articulate it by then; a face-saving one if not). Use the extra time to test with real work, not more interviews. If the co-founder feeling persists, don't marry him. Divorce at the co-founder level is devastating.

---

**Situation:** A venture investor is deciding between two firms to join as a partner. Firm A has higher-profile deals and a prestigious brand. Firm B has a slightly less visible portfolio but every partner who left in the last decade speaks well of the firm.

**Application:** The signal from Firm B is integrity at the firm level — partners who left still vouch for it, which is rare. Firm A's prestige masks whatever the internal behavior is. Over a career, joining Firm B is almost certainly better. You'll be treated fairly if things go badly, you'll be promoted honestly if you're earning it, and the reputation you accumulate inside that firm will compound into real opportunities later. Firm A may give you a flashier Rolodex and a worse five years.

**Result:** Firm B. Prestigious branding doesn't tell you how the firm treats its own people. Back-channel references tell you that, and they're the only reliable source.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Compromise on integrity because the skills are exceptional.
**Why:** The skills will be weaponized. The short-term benefit of the exceptional skills is always less than the long-term cost of the integrity violation that will follow. Every time. The math is punishing and it's the math.

**Don't:** Read "high-energy" as a general trait.
**Why:** Energy is domain-specific. A person with tons of energy for their hobby might have zero energy for your business problem. Watch what lights them up specifically.

**Don't:** Trust talk about integrity.
**Why:** Real integrity shows up in action. Self-descriptions of integrity are almost worthless and sometimes anti-signal. If they're telling you how honest they are, ask yourself why they felt the need to say it.

**Don't:** Hire someone because they'd be hard to replace in the short term.
**Why:** Replacing someone later who failed one of the three tests is much more expensive than replacing them now. The decision to keep a misaligned hire compounds. Team dynamics degrade. Good people leave. You end up with a team you wouldn't have assembled on purpose.

**Don't:** Rely on interviews alone.
**Why:** People perform in interviews. They describe themselves in flattering terms. The real test is actual work together, over time, under some pressure. Do it in stages if you can — trial project, contract engagement, trial period before full commitment. Especially for co-founders and senior hires.

**Don't:** Assume you can coach someone into integrity.
**Why:** You can coach skills. You can sometimes coach energy, if the role shifts closer to what they actually love. You cannot coach integrity. It's either there or it isn't, and it doesn't develop on your timeline. The correct response to an integrity gap is not to fix the person. It's to not hire them.
