# Specific Knowledge

## When to Use
- The user is asking what to learn, what to specialize in, what to major in, what to pivot into.
- The user is in a role that can be explicitly trained for and is watching it become more competitive every year.
- The user is wondering why smart peers are outearning them despite equal effort.
- The user is trying to design a career deliberately from the outside in instead of observing what they're already doing.

## Fails When
- The user wants a list of hot industries. This framework is about *you*, not markets. If you follow the heat, you're doing the opposite of building specific knowledge.
- The user is asking for credentials. Credentials are the opposite of specific knowledge. If a class exists for it, somebody else can be trained for it too.
- The user is under 18 and still genuinely figuring out what they like. They need exposure, not a framework. Let them dig.

## Core Concept

Specific knowledge is knowledge you cannot be trained for.

If society can train you for it, society can train someone else for it. And then you're replaceable. Then you're just another credentialed professional competing with every other credentialed professional on price and hours.

Specific knowledge is found, not taught. Observed, not instructed. When it is transmitted, it's through apprenticeships — person to person, not through textbooks. It tends to be highly technical or creative. It lives at the bleeding edge, where frameworks haven't solidified into curricula yet.

Here's the test: if you can take a class on it, it's not specific knowledge.

The most important tell: **specific knowledge will feel like play to you but will look like work to others.** That's the whole diagnostic. What do you do for hours that bores your friends? What do you obsess over that exhausts other people? What would you do even if no one paid you? That's where your edge is, whether you've noticed it or not.

If you're not 100% into it, someone else who is 100% into it will outperform you. And they won't outperform you by a little. They'll outperform you by a lot, because we're operating in domains where leverage and compound interest apply. Someone 90% right with 1,000x leverage beats someone 80% right by hundreds of times. You cannot fake passion at that level. The person who has it wins.

## Specific Knowledge Is Discovered, Not Designed

If you're past twenty or twenty-two, you almost don't get to pick. You get to look at what you've already been building.

Look back. What have you actually spent your time on — not what you wanted to spend it on, not what looked impressive on a resume, but what you couldn't help doing? That's your raw material. Everything you build from here on sits on top of it.

I wanted to be a scientist. Specifically an astrophysicist. My whole value system was built around scientists. But when I looked back at what I was actually good at and what I actually did with my time, it was different — making money, tinkering with technology, absorbing data, breaking things down, selling people on ideas, explaining. Those felt like play to me. They looked like work to other people. That was my specific knowledge. My mom saw it before I did. I was fifteen, telling a friend I wanted to be an astrophysicist, and from the kitchen she said, "No, you're going to go into business." I was offended. She was right.

This is why observation beats ambition in finding specific knowledge. Your conscious mind is trying to impress people. Your actual behavior is telling you the truth. Pay attention to the behavior.

## The Combination Is Where the Value Lives

Your specific knowledge almost never lives in a single dimension. It sits at the intersection of skills.

Scott Adams built his over decades: hypnosis when he was young, cartooning, Periscope and video early, persuasion theory, economics. Each skill alone was learnable. The combination was unique. No one's going to take it away from him.

The statistical logic is simple. If there are ten thousand valuable skills in the world, the number-one slot in each is taken — and unless you happen to be one of the ten thousand most obsessed people in the world at something, you won't get the top slot in any single skill. But when you combine skill #3,728 with top-notch sales ability and really good writing and a deep understanding of one specific industry, the intersection expands through combinatorics into millions of possible slots, and most of them are empty.

So don't compete on a single dimension where you'll lose. Stack three or four things you're naturally top-25% at, and the intersection is yours.

The key word is *naturally*. Don't assemble the stack deliberately. Follow your own obsessions, then notice the pattern. The stack you build because the market told you to build it is fragile. The stack you build because you couldn't help building it is a moat.

## Pursuing Genuine Curiosity

Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion — not whatever is hot right now.

The person who went to business school in 1995 because consulting was hot, then left to do dot-com in 1999 because dot-com was hot, then real estate in 2005 because real estate was hot, then crypto in 2017, then AI in 2024 — that person never developed specific knowledge. They were always chasing. Never digging.

The person who obsessed over one thing, who couldn't help themselves, who kept going deeper even when it was unfashionable — that's who built specific knowledge.

There's a reason for this beyond the romantic one. Pursuing what's hot puts you in a crowded market against everyone who's also pursuing what's hot. Pursuing what you're genuinely obsessed with puts you on a frontier where very few people are digging, because most of them aren't interested enough to tolerate the weirdness. You have to be a little eccentric to be out on the frontier by yourself. That eccentricity isn't a bug. It's the whole point.

## Learn to Sell, Learn to Build

Two specific knowledge super-categories in business: building and selling.

**Builders** make the product. Engineering, design, manufacturing, operations. In tech this is the CTO, the engineer, the designer. In other industries it's whoever makes the thing work.

**Sellers** get it to people. Marketing, sales, recruiting, fundraising, inspiring, PR. Communicating the value in a way that moves people to action.

Most people do one. Very few do both. The people who can do both start billion-dollar companies. Steve Jobs and Wozniak. Gates and Allen. Elon understands both domains well enough that no one can snow him on either. Bill Gates said: "I'd rather teach an engineer marketing than a marketer engineering." He's right — building requires focused technical time that a late-career transition rarely affords, while selling can be picked up later.

If you only get to pick one, pick building first. Then layer selling on top. This is the path with the fewest regrets.

## Apprenticeship, Not Curriculum

When specific knowledge is transmitted, it's through apprenticeships. Warren Buffett went to Benjamin Graham after school. Offered to work for free. Graham said "actually, you're overpriced" — and he was right; an apprenticeship with Graham was worth paying to get. That's how value investing passed on. Not in classrooms.

The best careers are apprenticeship careers and self-taught careers. Those are the ones society still hasn't figured out how to automate. The moment it figures out how to automate a career — the moment it can be taught to anyone — your edge disappears into the labor pool.

So: if you have a chance to apprentice with someone genuinely great at something that matters to you, take it. Pay them if you have to. The apprenticeship will teach you what no classroom can — the subtle pattern recognition, the judgment calls under ambiguity, the moves that only make sense once you've been in the seat. That is specific knowledge being transmitted in its purest form.

## The Market Finds You

When you have true specific knowledge, the market comes to find you.

If you're the best deep-sea diver in the world, and someone finds a sunken treasure ship, they have to come to you. You didn't need to find the ship. You needed to be so specifically excellent that whoever finds the ship has no choice but to call.

That's the goal. Become so specifically excellent that opportunities seek you out, rather than you seeking opportunities.

This is why specific knowledge beats networking, personal branding, and career planning. A great network is a byproduct of specific excellence, not a substitute for it. Build the knowledge. The market catches up.

## How to Apply

1. **Look back, not forward.** Write down the last five years of what you actually did — including the things you did for fun, the obsessions, the side projects. Look for the pattern. Your specific knowledge is probably already there; you just haven't seen it.

2. **Run the play/work test.** For each candidate skill, ask: does this feel like play to me but look like work to others? If yes, double down. If no, it isn't yours.

3. **Find the apprenticeship.** Identify one person genuinely world-class at a specific knowledge you care about. Figure out how to get close to them. Work for free if you have to. You will learn more in a year next to them than in three years of classes.

4. **Stack the intersection.** Don't try to be #1 at one thing. Try to be top 25% at three or four things that combine into something only you can do. The combinations compound.

5. **Ignore the hot industry.** Whatever everyone is telling you is the next big thing is already too crowded, and the returns are being competed away. Dig deeper into what's interesting to you, especially if no one else is excited about it. That's where the frontier is.

6. **Pick a profession where your inputs are disconnected from your outputs.** Software. Media. Investing. IP-heavy businesses. Avoid professions where the best practitioner can only be 2–3x the average — lumberjacking, most services, most hourly work. Those ceilings are built in.

7. **Give it a decade.** Specific knowledge compounds across iterations. You don't have it at year two. You might have it at year ten. Stay in the game long enough that compounding actually runs.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 28-year-old quant at a bank is considering three paths: staying, moving to another hedge fund for 50% more pay, or quitting to start a crypto market-making shop.

**Application:** Look at what he actually does with his nights and weekends. If he's reading crypto protocols recreationally, arguing about market microstructure in Discord, running his own prop models for fun — his specific knowledge is already being built there, not at the bank. The bank pays him a salary; the crypto shop would compound his specific knowledge and give him accountability and leverage. Another hedge fund is the worst of the three: more money, same replaceable role, no compounding of his actual edge.

**Result:** Quit. The pay cut in year one is irrelevant against ten years of compounding specific knowledge in a domain he's obsessed with. The universe where he's wealthy at 45 almost certainly has him building in crypto from his late 20s, not arbitraging another 50K in bonus at a second bank.

---

**Situation:** A founder with five years running a small B2B SaaS is considering a second company. She's good at ops, decent at product, not a natural seller.

**Application:** Her specific knowledge is probably not "founder" in the abstract. It's some specific combination: mid-market SaaS ops, a particular vertical she sold into, a pattern of product decisions she intuits. The second company should sit on that specific stack, not start from scratch in a new vertical because the market "feels hot." And she should partner with a strong seller — not because sales is beneath her, but because Gates was right: the builder-seller pair is the most common winning structure. Her sales gap isn't a character flaw. It's a co-founder slot.

**Result:** Pick a company adjacent to the specific knowledge she's already accumulated. Find a seller co-founder. Don't rebuild the specific knowledge stack from zero because the current vertical "feels boring" — boring to her is the opposite of boring to the market; her boredom is domain expertise talking.

---

**Situation:** A 19-year-old asks whether to drop out of college to start a company.

**Application:** At 19, the specific knowledge probably isn't built yet. The argument for college isn't the credential — it's cheap time to apprentice with people smarter than you, read widely, and figure out what you're obsessed with. The argument for dropping out is that if you've *already* found your obsession and there's an urgent market, credentials slow you down. The deciding factor is not bravery. It's whether the specific knowledge has actually shown up yet. Most 19-year-olds haven't discovered it and drop out into generic hustle, which is indistinguishable from a salaried job at Starbucks except for the story they tell about it.

**Result:** Only drop out if there's a specific thing you can't stop building. Otherwise stay, use the time to build the specific knowledge, and drop out when the obsession is undeniable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Design your specific knowledge from the outside.
**Why:** If you ask "what's valuable?" and work backward to the skill, you will pick something that thousands of other people also picked using the same calculation. Your specific knowledge has to be something you couldn't help building, because the compounding only works when you're 100% into it, and you can't fake 100%.

**Don't:** Assume credentialed knowledge is specific knowledge.
**Why:** An MBA is not specific knowledge. Neither is a JD. Neither is a medical degree by itself. They are credentials that admit you to a competitive labor pool. They can be *complements* to specific knowledge — a psychology degree in the hands of a natural salesperson is leverage — but they are not the knowledge themselves.

**Don't:** Mistake "I've read about this" for "I know this."
**Why:** Reading about negotiation is not negotiation. Reading about startups is not starting a startup. Specific knowledge is built through iterations — you tried, you failed, you adjusted, you noticed something subtle that only someone who ran the experiment could notice. Reading is preparation for iteration, not a substitute.

**Don't:** Follow advice that tells you to pick a passion.
**Why:** "Follow your passion" is a vague injunction. The more accurate version is: observe what you actually do, double down on the part that feels like play, and combine it with skills that multiply it. It's excavation, not choosing.

**Don't:** Commit to the specific knowledge you think you want to have.
**Why:** I wanted to be an astrophysicist. My actual specific knowledge was in making money and explaining complex things. If I'd committed to the astrophysicist story, I'd have wasted decades underperforming in a field where my real edge didn't apply. Your self-image is not your specific knowledge. Your behavior is.

**Don't:** Wait for the specific knowledge to feel "big enough" before monetizing it.
**Why:** You will underestimate it because it feels easy to you — that's the whole point, it feels like play. Other people find it hard. The market will pay for it sooner than you think. Ship something based on it and see what happens.
