# Finding Conversations

## When to Use
- You've defined your customer segment and you need to actually get in front of them.
- You have no relevant network in this industry — you're starting cold.
- Your meeting-request emails are getting ignored and you don't know why.
- You're at the stage where warm intros are the bottleneck and you need to get the snowball rolling.

## Fails When
- **You're fishing without a target.** If you don't yet know who your customer is (sub-segment, not just industry), no outreach tactic will save you. Sort out `choosing-customers-segmentation.md` first.
- **You spam.** The point of cold outreach is to *stop* doing cold outreach. If you blast 500 generic emails, you'll burn the few warm doors that might have opened. Quality of framing matters more than volume.

## Core Concept

If you're scratching your own itch, you already know your customers. Go talk to them. The Mom Test still works on people you know — just ask about their life rather than your idea.

If you don't, the question becomes: *where do these conversations come from?* There are two basic moves and one tactical art:

- **Going to them** — cold calls, cold emails, serendipity at events, finding excuses to chat in their natural habitat. Hard, high-friction, but you can always do it.
- **Bringing them to you** — organising meetups, speaking and teaching, blogging, clever moves like "host a knowledge exchange." Higher leverage, takes longer, but flips the power dynamic.
- **Warm intros** — the goal. Conversations are infinitely easier when someone you both know vouches for you. The first cold conversations exist mostly to convert into warm ones.

The aim of cold outreach isn't to scale to a thousand cold meetings — it's to get the snowball rolling, then ride warm intros forever. You hustle the first one or two from wherever you can, and if you treat people's time well and are genuinely trying to solve their problem, those cold contacts start introducing you to others. End every conversation with *"Who else should I talk to?"* and the leads compound.

The other thing nobody does, but they should: **organise a meetup.** Want to figure out the problems HR professionals have? Organise *"HR professionals happy hour."* People will assume you're credible just because you happen to be the person who sent the invite. You'll have an easy time chatting to them about their problems. As a bonus, it instantly bootstraps your industry credibility. It's the first thing I'd do if I got moved to a new industry or geography. It's literally the most unfair trick I know for rapid customer learning, and almost nobody does it.

For meetings you do have to formally request, the framing is what gets the email read. Vague asks ("can we grab a coffee?") get auto-deleted. Specific, framed asks get answered. The format I use is **Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask** — mnemonic *Very Few Wizards Properly Ask*.

*Rule of thumb: Kevin Bacon's 7 degrees of separation applies to customer conversations. You can find anyone you need if you ask for it a couple times.*

## How to Apply

1. **Start with who you know.** Stand on a chair in your coworking space and yell *"Does anyone here know anyone who works at McKinsey? I'll buy you a beer."* (I've literally done this.) Send a what's-app to your group chats. Email the people who've mentored you, invested in you, or supported you in any past venture. The world is small. Most people you need are 2 hops away.

2. **Cash in stale favours.** Remember everyone who said *"Sounds great, keep me in the loop and let me know how I can help"*? Now's the time. Reply to that ancient email and tell them you're ready for an intro to that specific person they know. They'll often have forgotten the offer, but who cares — sometimes they'll come through. Use sparingly; can burn bridges if overdone.

3. **Use the Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask format for cold and warm requests.**
   - **Vision** — half-sentence on the problem you're solving or the world you're trying to make. Don't pitch the idea.
   - **Framing** — what stage you're at, and (if true) that you have nothing to sell.
   - **Weakness** — show vulnerability about the specific thing you don't understand. Reassures them you won't waste their time.
   - **Pedestal** — name why *they specifically* can help. Not "you're so great" — *"you've spent 20 years on the front lines of this industry, you're uniquely positioned to..."*
   - **Ask** — make the actual request. Specific time slot, specific topic.

4. **Make the cold email about *them*, not your idea.** The reason cold emails get deleted is they read as *"give me your time so I can pitch you."* The framing flips it: *"You can help me with this specific thing I don't understand, in a way nobody else can."* People love feeling uniquely useful. Lean into the pedestal honestly — if you can't think of a real reason this person specifically can help, you're probably emailing the wrong person.

5. **Get clever to bring them to you.**
   - **Organise a meetup.** Single most underused tactic. Send the invite emails, introduce the speaker, you're now The Person Who Hosts This Industry's Thing.
   - **Speak and teach.** Conferences, workshops, free office hours, blog posts. Useful for refining your message AND for getting a room of self-qualifying potential customers.
   - **Blog.** Even with no audience, having a blog at your domain when you cold-email gives you credibility — people google you and see you've thought hard about the space.
   - **Host a "knowledge exchange."** I once heard about a founder who wanted to sell to top universities. He organised a semi-monthly call between department heads of three top schools to share best practices on his topic — open to other universities to dial in and listen. By organising the call, he absorbed the credibility of the top three and got direct phone access to a roomful of qualified leads.

6. **End every conversation with the same ask.** *"Who else should I talk to?"* Not at the close, exactly — earlier than that, with at least 5 minutes left so they can think and check their phone. If they have suggestions, ask for the intro right now: *"Could you send a quick email connecting us today or tomorrow?"* If they won't intro, treat that as quiet rejection — either you screwed up the meeting (too pitchy, too clingy, too formal), or they don't really care about the problem.

7. **Use the advisory flip.** Don't go into discovery looking for customers — that creates a needy vibe. Go in looking for *advisors.* Helpful, knowledgeable people who are excited about your space. Same questions, completely different mindset. You're now evaluating *them* (are they someone whose insight is worth my time?), which paradoxically puts you in control. Some of these advisors will become customers. Some will become formal advisors. All of them will produce more useful information than the same conversation framed as a sales meeting.

8. **Skip the phone if you can; show up.** Calls and Skype damage the delicate power dynamic of these conversations. People squeeze calls between meetings, wondering how fast they can hang up. They feel weird about "just chatting." In person, you can grab a coffee, shoot the breeze, keep it casual. People become friends in person; they don't on Zoom. (For when you have to do remote, see `remote-mom-test.md`.) You should at least *start* in person — too easy to default to surveys and calls as an excuse to skip the awkwardness rather than as a considered trade-off.

## Examples

**Situation:** A team of recent graduates needs to talk to McKinsey-style consultants. They have zero network there.

**Application:** I literally stood on a chair in our coworking space and yelled, *"Excuse me — does anyone here know anyone who works at McKinsey? Can we talk for a second? I'll buy you a beer — you could really help us out."*

**Result:** They bought 3 beers, had 3 quick chats, and walked away with a calendar full of warm intros. Cost: a tenner of beer and 30 minutes of being mildly embarrassing. Most cold-outreach problems are solved by remembering you're allowed to ask.

---

**Situation:** A founder cold-emailing for a meeting about their desk-rental startup, no network, no product yet.

**Application:** Use Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask:

> Hey Pete,
>
> I'm trying to make desk & office rental less of a pain for new businesses (*vision*). We're just starting out and don't have anything to sell, but I want to make sure we're building something that actually helps (*framing*).
>
> I've only ever come at it from the tenant's side, and I'm having a hard time understanding how it works from the landlord's perspective (*weakness*). You've been renting out desks for a while and could really help me cut through the fog (*pedestal*).
>
> Do you have time in the next couple of weeks to meet up for a chat? (*ask*)

**Result:** This gets read. The recipient knows you don't have a product to pitch them, knows you have a specific thing you need help with, and knows their time will be respected. Response rates on this format are dramatically higher than *"can we grab a coffee?"* The structure is doing the work — the order can shuffle, lines can collapse, but all 5 elements need to be there.

---

**Situation:** A founder is trying to talk to the owners of independent cafes about a new product, has been cold-walking-in for two weeks, getting turned away.

**Application:** Use a real-feeling reason to be there. Don't ask for the owner directly. Flag down a server: *"Excuse me — can I speak to the owner? Don't worry, it's nothing bad. This coffee is amazing and I wanted to ask him about the story behind the beans."*

**Result:** "I want to ask about your beans" works where "I'm a founder building a tool" doesn't. The cafe industry runs on people who are passionate about their beans — they love being asked. The owner you couldn't reach in two weeks of cold-walking suddenly takes the meeting. The technique generalises: if it's a topic you and they both genuinely care about, find an excuse to talk about it. Your idea never has to enter the conversation, and you'll both enjoy the chat.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Send *"can I get your opinion on what we're doing"* as the meeting ask.
**Why:** This signals neediness and that you want compliments or approval — which means even if they say yes, they'll come prepared to be supportive, which means you'll get bad data. Worse, most won't say yes because the ask sounds like a time-sink. Frame around what they uniquely know, not your need for validation.

**Don't:** Send *"do you have time for a quick coffee/chat/meeting?"* with no agenda.
**Why:** No expectations are set, which signals "I'll waste your time." Vague asks get auto-deleted. The fix is to give the topic and the framing — *"I'm trying to figure out [specific thing], you have unusual expertise here, can I steal 20 minutes?"* — even if you'd rather keep it open-ended.

**Don't:** Lead the cold email with the idea.
**Why:** *"I'm building [product] and would love your feedback"* triggers shields and tells them this is going to be a pitch. Lead with the *vision* (the problem space, not the product) and then the *weakness* (the thing you can't figure out alone). The product can come up later, in person.

**Don't:** Cold-call your way to a thousand meetings.
**Why:** The point is to stop having cold conversations. If you reach out to 100 people and 98 hang up, you don't have a 2% conversion problem — you have 2 conversations to nurture into intros. After the first 5 cold meetings, your time is better spent extracting *who else should I talk to* from each one.

**Don't:** Pitch the meetup organiser thing as marketing.
**Why:** If you organise *"HR professionals happy hour"* and pitch your product from the front, attendees will remember the night as a sales gauntlet and never come back. The meetup works because it's actually a meetup — people get value, you get conversations on the side. Stay genuine. Your product never needs to come up; the credibility and conversations are the leverage.

**Don't:** Skip the *"who else should I talk to?"* question because it feels pushy.
**Why:** It's not pushy when the meeting went well — it's the natural close. If they don't want to intro you, that's information (probably you came across as too pitchy or formal, or they don't really care about the problem). If they do want to, every intro saves you the friction of starting cold next time. Skipping this question is leaving the snowball at the top of the hill.
