# The Remote Mom Test

## When to Use
- Your customers and you can't easily meet in person — distributed team, customer base in another city/country, lockdowns, travel-cost constraints.
- You've tried running Mom Test interviews on Zoom and you're getting wooden, sales-meeting-flavoured answers instead of real talk.
- You need the response rate on your interview-request emails to actually be above 0%.
- You're shifting an in-person customer development practice to fully remote and trying to figure out what to keep, what to drop, and what to invent.

## Fails When
- **In-person is genuinely available.** For early discovery, in-person beats remote on every dimension that matters — body language, casual margin space, rapport building, emotional read. If you can do in-person, do it. Remote is the fallback, not the upgrade.
- **You expect remote conversations to give you 100% of what in-person gives you.** They won't. Goal: get from 0% (no learning at all because you're stuck at home) to 60–70% (good enough to make decisions, with more conversations to make up for some signal loss). Don't try to make remote perfect; make it useful.

## Core Concept

The whole reason in-person customer development works so well is the *space at the margins* — the five minutes walking from the lobby to the meeting room, waiting for the barista, walking out together at the end. That's where shields come down and the real chat happens. *"Hey, how do you guys deal with X?"* asked at the coffee bar gets a different answer than the same question asked across a boardroom table. Online meetings don't have that margin — they have an agenda, a calendar slot, and a participant list. People joined to "have a meeting." They're watching the clock. The default mode is sales pitch / demo, not chat.

So you don't get the casual conversations for free anymore. You have to engineer them. There are five moves that make remote customer development work:

1. **Lean harder on friendly first contacts.** The Mom Test still works on people who like you — they won't lie about facts of their life as long as you ask Mom Test questions. Network out from there. People who owe favours, accelerator mentors, advisors-of-advisors, "if there's anything I can do" promises — call them in. Friendlies are forgiving of imperfect remote conversations because they actually want to help.

2. **Manufacture casual space at the start of calls.** Set up the Zoom for 1pm. The "meeting" you'll have is the demo or pitch. But before you start, just chat: *"Thanks for taking this — quick question while we wait, I was wondering, how do you guys deal with [problem]?"* This works even better with a fake-or-real second person: *"Sorry, just waiting for my colleague to join, should be a minute or two — in the meantime, I've been wondering..."* You've created a no-agenda window in which they can talk freely. (Window: this trick stops working once everyone knows it. Use it now.)

3. **Use industry experts, hard.** Industry experts (journalists, retired insiders, academics, investors, consultants who used to serve the space) are easy to pin down for remote calls — they don't have a horse in the race, they're flattered by the ask, they're rich in industry facts. Use them for *how the industry works* (budgets, processes, structure). Don't use them for *what customers think* — for that, talk to customers. But for the layer where remote works fine (extracting facts from a willing expert), this is a clean win.

4. **Run the customer safari.** This is Amy Hoy's technique and it's superb for remote: instead of asking customers questions, *go where customers already are and watch them.* Your competitors' support forums. Reddit threads about the problem. Q&A sites (Quora, Stack Overflow). Discord servers, Slack groups, niche Twitter. Customers are already voicing concerns, asking questions, complaining about workarounds — for free, in their natural voice, without anyone interviewing them. You get unfiltered emotional language and real-life context. Doesn't replace interviews but is a brilliant complement.

5. **Frame every request explicitly.** Vague asks (*"can we have a quick call?"*) get auto-deleted online. Pre-COVID, vague was sometimes fine because in-person social glue carried the relationship. Online you need: vision (what you're working on), framing (you have nothing to sell), weakness (what specifically you can't figure out), pedestal (why *they specifically* can help), ask (specific time slot, specific topic). The mnemonic is *Very Few Wizards Properly Ask*. (See `finding-conversations.md` for the format in detail.)

A few other things shift remote:

- **You'll have a higher dud rate.** Some calls will be stilted no matter what — busy people, grumpy people, people who joined for the pitch and don't want to chat. That's fine. Wrap up early. Don't try to force learning out of a closed conversation.
- **You can volume-up.** No commute, no travel time. You can do 3 calls in a morning that would have been a full day in person. Some of the casual-margin signal loss can be made up with more conversations.
- **You still need to take notes.** Tempting to just hit screen-record and move on. Don't — your team won't watch hours of unedited recordings. Capture key quotes plus emotion in real time, even when remote. Symbols + verbatim is still the format.
- **The discovery → commitment transition is trickier.** Hard to ask for a real commitment at the end of a discovery-framed call. Instead, follow up after via email: *"Loved chatting — based on what you said, I think the thing we're building could really help. No pressure, but I'd love to demo it for you next week if you're interested."* The willingness to schedule the follow-up *is* the commitment, and it's easier to extract async.

The biggest lesson: **the principle hasn't changed, but the tactics have.** You're still avoiding biasing questions, still anchoring fluff, still pushing for commitment. You just have to manufacture the casual space that came for free in person, accept a higher failure rate, and lean on a wider toolkit (experts, safaris, friendlies) to make up the gap.

*Rule of thumb: Online is less forgiving — be a bit more precise, a bit more structured, and accept a higher dud rate.*

## How to Apply

1. **Start with the friendliest contacts.** Mentors, advisors, peers who owe favours, founders in your accelerator, anyone who's said *"let me know how I can help."* These calls are forgiving — even a clumsy remote interview produces real signal because the friendly is genuinely trying to help. Use them to find your rhythm before going colder.

2. **Engineer 5 minutes of casual space at the start of every call.**
   - Set up the call with a stated purpose (demo, pitch, advice on the product). The state purpose lowers the prospect's guard about what the call *is*.
   - Open with *"thanks for taking this — quick question while we get started: how do you guys handle [the thing]?"*
   - Or: *"Just waiting for my colleague — should be a minute. While we wait, I've been trying to understand how [thing] actually works in practice — could you walk me through how it does at your shop?"*
   - The casual chat happens in this manufactured window. After 3–5 minutes, transition to the stated purpose if you still need to.

3. **Lean on industry experts for industry facts.** Journalists who cover the space, investors who back companies in the space, retired senior people, academics, consultants. Easy to schedule, no defensive posture, no horse in the race. Get the *how does this work* / *what's the budget process* / *what kills companies in this space* layer from them. Don't ask them what customers want — they don't know better than the customer does.

4. **Run a customer safari weekly.** Block 30–60 min. Read your competitors' support forums, the relevant subreddits, Q&A threads, niche Slack/Discord groups. Look for: emotional intensity, specific workarounds people describe, repeated complaints, questions that nobody's answering well. Capture 5–10 verbatim quotes per session. This becomes your unfiltered emotional-language corpus.

5. **Frame every cold/warm request explicitly.** Use Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask. Specifically *call out* that it's not a pitch:
   > Hey [Name], I'm working on [vision]. I don't have anything to sell yet — really just trying to understand [specific area I can't figure out alone]. You've spent [X years doing Y] and could help me cut through the fog faster than anyone else I could ask. Could I steal 20 minutes [specific week]?

6. **Anchor the value to *them specifically*.** Generic compliments (*"you're so successful"*) feel cynical. Specific anchors (*"you've been a surgeon for 20 years and know more than anyone about how equipment decisions actually happen"*) make them feel uniquely valuable — and they are. People love feeling uniquely useful, and authentic pedestal gets dramatically better response rates.

7. **Take notes throughout. Don't rely on recording.** Capture verbatim quotes + emotion symbols (the same shorthand from `running-the-process.md`). Recordings sit unwatched in folders. Notes get reviewed and shared with the team.

8. **Wrap up early on dud calls.** If you've tried the casual opening and the prospect is in shields-up mode, just go through the planned demo / pitch and end the call. Forcing learning from a closed conversation produces bad data and burns the relationship. Better to bail and try again with a friendlier contact.

9. **Convert discovery to commitment via async follow-up.** Right after a discovery-framed call: *"Loved chatting. Based on what you said about [specific pain point], I think what we're building might really help. No pressure — but if you'd like, I could show you a demo next week. Otherwise, no worries, you've helped me a ton already."* The reply-rate to this is your commitment signal: high engagement = real prospect; ignored = polite chat that won't convert.

10. **Volume-up to compensate.** No commute. Schedule 3–5 calls in one block. The marginal cost of a fifth call is much lower remote than in-person. Use volume to recover some of the per-call signal loss.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is exploring a hospital-equipment-purchasing tool. No in-person hospital meetings possible.

**Application:**
- Friendlies first: 2 calls with retired hospital admins from her LinkedIn network ("if there's anything I can help with").
- Industry experts: 3 calls with a journalist covering medical procurement, a former hospital CFO, and a consultant who sells into the space — "for industry facts about budget cycles and approval flows."
- Customer safari: she joins a hospital-administrators forum and reads 6 months of threads, captures verbatim complaints about current procurement tools.
- Explicit framing on cold calls: *"I'm not trying to sell you anything — I'm trying to understand how equipment decisions actually happen at hospitals like yours, and you have unique insight."*
- Manufactures casual space: opens calls with *"While we get started — could you walk me through how the last big purchase decision actually played out?"*

**Result:** She doesn't get the same depth as a string of in-person hospital visits, but she gets enough convergent signal across friendlies + experts + safari + structured customer calls to make decisions. The combined toolkit gets her to ~70% of in-person quality at much lower cost per learning.

**Situation:** A founder cold-emails 30 potential customers asking *"can we have a quick call?"* — 1 reply (apologetic no).

**Application:** Rewrite using Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask. Anchor the pedestal to the recipient specifically:
> Hey [Name], I saw your post about [specific thing] — really resonated. I'm working on a tool to help [vision], and we don't have anything to sell yet — I'm just trying to understand how [specific question] actually plays out at companies like yours. You've been at the front lines of this for [N years] and could help me cut through the fog faster than anyone else I could ask. Could I steal 20 minutes next week?

**Result:** Rewrite produces 4 yeses out of 30. Same list, totally different response rate. The framing was doing the work; the original ask was failing because it gave the recipient no reason to say yes other than generosity.

**Situation:** A founder runs a discovery call. Goes well. Wants to ask for commitment but it would feel pushy in the same call.

**Application:** Don't ask in-call. Let the conversation end naturally with a thank-you. Send a follow-up email two hours later: *"Really enjoyed chatting. Based on what you said about [specific pain], I think what we're building might be a fit for you. No pressure — but if you'd like, I could show you a demo next week. Otherwise, no worries; you've already helped me a ton."*

**Result:** The prospect either says yes (real signal — they're committing time, which is currency) or doesn't reply (polite ignore — they were friendly but not a buyer). Either way, the founder learns more than they would have by awkwardly trying to switch modes mid-call. Async follow-up is the remote-version of the in-person *"thanks for the chat — when can I show you what we're building?"*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Schedule a remote *"open chat with no agenda."*
**Why:** Vague asks get auto-deleted online. The vacuum that an in-person bump-into can fill (small talk → topic → real chat) doesn't exist on Zoom. You have to give them a reason and a frame, even if your real intent is open-ended discovery.

**Don't:** Hit "screen record" instead of taking notes.
**Why:** Recordings nobody watches are worse than nothing — you skip the active note-taking that processes the conversation while it's fresh, and you replace it with an artefact that feels safe but is unusable. Take notes during the call. Use the recording (if at all) as a backup for the one verbatim quote you need to nail later.

**Don't:** Try to force a discovery → commitment transition mid-call.
**Why:** The framing whiplash is jarring online — *"so I was just here to learn... but actually, would you like to buy?"* lands worse on Zoom than in person, where social cushioning helps. Use async follow-up. Lower-pressure, higher-quality signal.

**Don't:** Expect the same conversational quality as in-person.
**Why:** Remote calls are inherently more agenda-driven, more guarded, less candid. Founders who expect parity get frustrated. The realistic target is: enough learning to make decisions, supplemented by experts + safari + friendlies. Calibrate expectations and the practice becomes sustainable.

**Don't:** Use phone instead of video if you can avoid it.
**Why:** Phone strips one more channel of signal — body language, facial expression. Voice-only calls are also harder to escape; people can't read social cues that the conversation is wrapping. Video keeps as much in-person signal as remote allows. Default to video; use phone as a last resort.

**Don't:** Burn relationships by trying to make every call into a learning *and* sales call.
**Why:** If you set the call up as *"just here to learn, no pressure,"* and then halfway through start pitching hard, you've broken trust. The recipient won't take a follow-up. Keep the framings clean. Use the follow-up email to softly transition into sales mode if you want to convert; the in-call experience should match the framing you set.
