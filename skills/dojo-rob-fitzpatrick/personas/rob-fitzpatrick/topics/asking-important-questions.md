# Asking Important Questions

## When to Use
- Before any batch of customer conversations, when you're deciding what to actually learn.
- When you've run a bunch of interviews and feel like you "know more about the customer" but can't point to anything that's changed in your plan.
- When you notice yourself shrinking from a particular question because the answer would be uncomfortable.
- When you're tempted to start the conversation by zooming in on the specific problem you've decided to solve, before you've checked it actually matters.

## Fails When
- **You ask the right questions of the wrong people.** The most painful version: brilliant, terrifying questions about budget and ownership, asked of the user instead of the buyer in an enterprise B2B context. The questions are correct; the audience is wrong.
- **The risk is in your product, not in the market.** "Would you like more money?" is a perfectly important question for a farmer using a fertility tracker. "Yes, obviously" is the answer. None of that resolves the product risk, which is whether you can actually build the tracker. No conversation will save you here — you have to start building.

## Core Concept

Once you've internalised the Mom Test and started avoiding biasing questions, there's a sneaky failure mode: you ask cleanly-phrased *trivial* questions. "How old are you" is unbiasing. It's also pointless. Asking unbiased questions isn't the goal — the goal is asking unbiased questions about *things that could change your business*.

Here's the test: **if you imagine getting any answer at all to this question — best case, worst case, weird case — does anything in your plan change?** If nothing changes, the question doesn't matter. Cut it. Replace it with one whose answer would force you to do something different.

A useful complementary test: **you should be terrified of at least one question you're asking in every conversation.** If none of the questions scare you — if you're already comfortable with all the possible answers — you're not de-risking, you're going through the motions. The scary questions are the ones whose answers might tank the business. Those are exactly the questions you most need to ask. One of my companies had legal ambiguities around content ownership. I never asked customers' lawyers about it, even though I was nervous about getting "found out." That avoidance cost us at least half a million bucks. The question I shrank from was the question that mattered.

The third trap is **premature zoom** — getting into the details of a problem before you've checked the problem matters. If you ask someone "what's your biggest problem with going to the gym?" they'll give you a confident-sounding answer ("commute time") whether or not they actually care about going to the gym. Now you're optimising commute-friendly workouts for someone who hasn't done a push-up in five years. The fix is to start broad — *"What are your big goals and focuses right now?"* / *"What are the three big things you're trying to fix or improve?"* — and only zoom in once they've told you (unprompted) that the area you care about is on their list.

The fourth trap is **looking past the elephant in the room.** Successful startups fail at multiple points — customer demand AND ability to pay AND ability to build AND ability to reach. It's tempting to obsess over the most interesting failure point and ignore the others. The teachers-need-this-tool example: maybe they do. But maybe their schools have no budget. Validating the need without validating the ability to pay is the kind of thing that lets you spend three months "validating" something that was never going to work.

*Rule of thumb: You should be terrified of at least one of the questions you're asking in every conversation.*

## How to Apply

1. **Run two thought experiments before any batch of conversations.** First: *"If this company fails, what would have killed it?"* Force yourself to write down the top 3 ways. Second: *"What would have to be true for this to be a huge success?"* Both come from gut, not from rigorous strategy work — gut is enough. The intersection is where the important questions live.

2. **Pre-plan a list of 3 important questions for each type of person you'll talk to.** Different segments get different lists. Investors, users, buyers, partners — each get their own three. Don't try to memorise the list during the meeting; write it on a card. Keep it small enough you can actually retain it.

3. **Apply the "answer changes plan" test to every question.** Look at each question and ask: if I get a totally unexpected answer, does it change what I do tomorrow? If not, cut it. The remaining questions are the important ones.

4. **Make sure at least one of those questions scares you.** If none of them do, you're avoiding the elephant. Common avoided categories: budget ("are you the buyer / can you actually pay?"), ownership ("does this need legal sign-off / IP review?"), the use-it test ("when would you actually use this in your week?"), and the disqualifier ("is this even a top-3 priority for you right now?"). One of these is probably the question you're shrinking from.

5. **Start broad before zooming.** If you don't already have rock-solid evidence that the problem is a top priority for this segment, *don't lead with the problem.* Open with: *"What are the big goals and focuses for you right now?"* / *"What are the 3 big things you're trying to fix or improve?"* / *"What's keeping you up at night?"* If your problem area shows up unprompted, great — zoom in. If it doesn't, the problem may be real but it's not a priority for this person, and you've just saved yourself an hour of misleading detail.

6. **Distinguish must-have from nice-to-have with cost-of-the-current-state questions.** *"How are you dealing with this now?"* is the gold question — it gives you both a workaround signal and a price anchor. If they're paying £100/month for a duct-tape solution, you're in the £100 ballpark. If they're paying an agency £10k/month, you're in a different ballpark entirely. Other useful must-have probes: *"How seriously do you take this?"* / *"How much time and money are you already spending here?"* / *"What are you already doing to fix it?"* / *"Have you tried to find a solution?"*

7. **Love bad news.** The worst thing you can do is ignore the bad news and search for tiny grains of validation to celebrate. You want the truth, not a gold star. If the conversation is telling you the idea is wrong, the conversation has just saved you months. "Meh" is more reliable than "wow" — you can't build a business on a lukewarm response, and a lukewarm response is the loudest signal you can get for free. Take it, thank them, and go re-plan.

8. **Separate product risk from market risk before you decide which questions to ask.** Some businesses are pure market risk (will they pay? do enough of them have the problem?) — conversations are powerful here. Some are pure product risk (can we build this fun? can we hit the page-view threshold?) — conversations help around the edges (budget, willingness to switch) but can't validate the core. Know which yours is. If product risk dominates, accept that you'll have to start building earlier and with less certainty.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder building fitness apps starts a conversation with: *"How often do you go to the gym?"* → "Not really ever." → *"What would you say your biggest problem with the gym is?"*

**Application:** That's premature zoom. The follow-up assumes the gym is a problem worth caring about, even though the person just said they don't go. The right question is *"Why not?"* — and if the answer is some version of "it's just not a priority for me," the conversation is over. They're not a customer. Move on.

**Result:** Without the fix, you collect a phantom answer ("commute time, I guess") and treat it as validated, then build a workout-at-home app for someone who is never going to use it. With the fix, you spent two minutes confirming non-customer status and you're free to find someone who actually cares.

---

**Situation:** A founder is building a tool for poor schools to help overloaded teachers.

**Application:** The teacher conversations are coming back great — they're absolutely overloaded, they'd love the tool, they're emotional about how hard their job is. The founder spends weeks designing the perfect feature set. The question they aren't asking is: *"Where would the budget come from?"* Because the answer is: poor schools don't have one. The teachers can't sign a check.

**Result:** The need was real. The buyer didn't exist. Three months wasted because the founder asked great questions of the wrong stakeholder, and shrank from the question they should have asked someone with a procurement title.

---

**Situation:** A founder is talking to a public speaker about a marketplace that connects speakers directly to event organisers, cutting out agents.

**Application:** The speaker says: *"Man, that's awesome. If you could get me more gigs — or better paid ones — I'd happily drop my agent and pay you 20% of the boost. I know a bunch of other people who'd love to as well."*

This *seems* like a great answer — emotional, specific, includes a price commitment. But what did he actually say? *"If you can get me more gigs, I'll pay a cut."* Well, obviously. Who doesn't want free money? The risk is now entirely on the founder's product (can you grow a healthy supply of gigs?) and not on the customer (will they pay?). The customer has shifted the burden without the founder noticing.

**Result:** This is the moment to recognise that the important question isn't "would you pay" — that's settled. The important question is whether the founder can actually deliver supply. That's a product/market question, and it requires different conversations (with event organisers) and probably an MVP test. Mistaking the customer's willing-to-pay for validation of the *whole* business is exactly how startups burn cash building marketplaces with no supply side.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead the conversation by stating the problem you've decided to solve.
**Why:** This locks the prospect into the problem frame whether or not they actually have the problem. They'll generate plausible-sounding pain that doesn't reflect their real life. Start broad with goals/priorities. Let them surface the problem area themselves. If they don't, you have your answer.

**Don't:** Ask "would you pay X for a product that did Y?"
**Why:** Adding a number doesn't make the question better. The number makes it feel rigorous, which is exactly the trap. Future hypothetical questions are wildly over-optimistic. Ask about what they're paying *now* — current behaviour gives you a price anchor that's actually true.

**Don't:** Skip the scary question because the conversation has been going so well.
**Why:** A meeting going well is, paradoxically, a warning sign that you've avoided the question that would have ended it. The point of customer conversations isn't to feel good. The point is to learn whether to keep building. The pleasant meeting where you didn't ask the budget question is the meeting that costs you six months.

**Don't:** Ask important questions in a context where the prospect can't tell the truth.
**Why:** A formal sales meeting is the worst environment for learning. Their shields are up because they expect everything they say to be used in negotiation. Ask the same question over coffee on the way to the meeting room and you'll get the truth. Ask it across the boardroom table and you'll get the polished version. Match the question to the context.

**Don't:** Treat your "list of 3" as a survey to march through linearly.
**Why:** The list is the safety net — what you must walk out with. The conversation is the surface area in which you find it. If you robotically tick through the list, you'll miss the side comments, the surprise emotion, the workaround they mentioned in passing — which is usually where the real answer hides. Memorise the three things you need, then have a real conversation, and double back to what you didn't get if needed.
