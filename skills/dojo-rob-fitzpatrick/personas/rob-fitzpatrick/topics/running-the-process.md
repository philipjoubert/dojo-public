# Running the Process

## When to Use
- Before any batch of customer conversations — to do the prep that makes the conversations worth having.
- During a meeting — to keep notes that you'll actually use later.
- After a meeting — to review with the team and update beliefs before they fade.
- When customer learnings are getting siloed in one person's head and the rest of the team is building on stale assumptions.
- When the team is fighting because "the customer said so" is being used as an unfalsifiable trump card.

## Fails When
- **You're a solo founder.** Most of the team-coordination steps don't directly apply, but prep/review/notes still do — they keep you honest with yourself, even without a co-founder to argue with.
- **The process becomes ceremony.** If you're spending more time prepping for and reviewing meetings than actually having them, you've over-rotated. The lightweight version of this process is the goal — anything heavier is procrastination wearing rigour clothes.

## Core Concept

Even if you ask great questions of the right people, you can still get bad results if the process around the conversations is broken. Customer learning is fragile. It needs scaffolding before, during, and after the meeting, or the value leaks away.

The most common breakdown is the **learning bottleneck.** One person — usually the "business" co-founder — goes to all the meetings, takes (or doesn't take) notes, and then tells the rest of the team *"what I learned."* This is functionally equivalent to telling them *"what you'll do."* It creates a de-facto dictator with *"the customer said so"* as the trump card. The tech team builds based on a filtered, second-hand summary that they can't verify. Tensions rise. In my case, my CTO eventually quit, saying *"we're never going to succeed if you keep changing what we're doing."* The stuff I'd learned was true. But I'd communicated it badly, and that mattered more.

The fix is three-part: **prep, review, and good notes.** Each part is light, but skipping any of them turns the conversations into expensive theatre.

**Prepping** ensures you walk in knowing your three big questions (the ones whose answers might tank or transform the business) and what advancement looks like. It's done with the team — both business and product side. It also includes desk research: if you can answer a question with Google, do it before the meeting. You want conversations spent on what you can't get any other way.

**Notes** are the audit trail. Without them, the conversation evaporates. With them, the team gets first-hand exposure to customer language and you have something to point at when "we should do X" turns into "but the customer said Y." Take verbatim quotes where you can. Use a small symbol vocabulary so signals are easy to scan later. Don't rely on memory; in a week you'll remember the *vibe* of the meeting, which is the least useful part.

**Reviewing** is what makes the rest of the team better, and what makes *you* honest about what you actually heard. Talk through the key quotes with your team. Update the three big questions. Decide what changes. Talk about meta — which questions worked, which ones didn't, what we should do differently next time. This stuff is more craft than science. You only get better by reviewing.

The whole rhythm is fast. Prep is an hour, not a week. Conversations are 10–30 minutes each. Review is 20 minutes per batch. The point of the process is to make the business move *faster*, not slower. If your customer development is taking months and producing minimal action, the process is wrong.

*Rule of thumb: If you don't know what you're trying to learn, you shouldn't bother having the conversation.*

## How to Apply

1. **Prep with the team. Decide your three big questions.** Whole founding team in the room — business AND product. Ask: *"If this company fails, what would have killed it?"* and *"What would have to be true for this to be a huge success?"* The answers point at the questions you should be asking. Write down 3 things to learn from this batch of conversations. They'll evolve, that's fine — pick the murkiest 3 right now.

2. **Pre-write your beliefs.** Spend 30–60 minutes writing down your best guesses about what the person you're about to talk to cares about, what their workflow looks like, what they're paying for current solutions. You'll be wrong about most of it — that's the point. The skeleton makes the conversation easier to navigate; the wrongness is where the learning happens.

3. **Do the desk research first.** If a question can be answered by Google, LinkedIn, or a public 10-K, answer it that way. Don't waste customer time on what's already known. Five minutes of due diligence on the company before any B2B meeting saves you from looking like an idiot.

4. **Decide your ideal advancement.** What's the next step you'll push for at the end? Write it down. If you walk in without one, the meeting will drift no matter how well the questions go.

5. **Take notes that are sortable, sharable, and persistent.**
   - **Verbatim quotes** wherever possible, in quote marks. You'll use them later — in marketing copy, in fundraising decks, in resolving team arguments.
   - **Symbol vocabulary** for signals. Mine: ☇ (pain), ⨅ (goal), ☐ (obstacle), ⤴ (workaround), ＄ (money signal), ☑ (feature request), ♀ (named person/company), ☆ (follow-up), :) :( :| (emotion). Yours will differ. The point is fast tagging.
   - **One quote/learning per index card** is my preferred medium — sortable, mixable, lays out on a table during review. Spreadsheets and Evernote also work. A primary notebook does *not* work — you'll never find anything in it later.
   - **If notes-during would blow your cover** (the casual chat at the meetup), retreat afterwards and write them down immediately. *"Immediately"* — five minutes later, not on the drive home. Detail evaporates fast.

6. **Send two people to most meetings.** One leads, one takes notes. The note-taker also catches the lead asking bad questions or missing signals — and just jumps in to fix them. Three people is overwhelming for the prospect; two is the sweet spot. Solo is fine once you're good, but harder to recover from off-track moments.

7. **Make sure tech and product team members go to *some* meetings.** Not all — they need to build. But the gap between "the business guy told us what the customers said" and "I sat in the room and watched her get angry about her current tool" is enormous. Direct exposure shifts what people prioritise. Aim for at least 1 in 5 meetings to include a tech or product team member.

8. **Review immediately after every batch.**
   - Lay the cards (or the doc) on the table with the team.
   - Read the key quotes aloud — exact words.
   - Talk through what surprised you.
   - Update beliefs, plans, the three big questions.
   - Talk about the meta-level: *which questions worked, which didn't, what should we do differently?*
   The review is the part most people skip. Don't. It's where the learning gets converted into action.

9. **Don't outsource the conversations.** You can hire someone to set up meetings, send emails, organise logistics. But the founders need to be in the room, listening. When a hired gun comes back with bad news ("nobody cares about this problem"), it's hard to internalise. When a lukewarm signal needs nuance, a hire will gloss it. Until you have a proven sales process and a working business model, the founders go to the meetings.

10. **Hack the process when you can.** A personal trainer in one of my workshops figured out he should serve police precincts (no commute time, less price-sensitive). We started geeking out about validation, segmentation, interview questions. He held up his phone, looked at us like we were idiots: *"Why wouldn't I just call them?"* Came back twenty minutes later with a trial session scheduled. The process is a tool. Sometimes you can pick up the phone and skip the whole thing.

## Examples

**Situation:** A team is about to interview a senior person at a hospital procurement department about a new blood-testing tool.

**Application:** Prep — they decide their three big questions: (1) how does equipment purchasing actually flow internally (who decides, who signs)? (2) what's the budget cycle and where does discretionary spend sit? (3) what's killed similar product introductions in the past? They do desk research on the hospital, the senior person's LinkedIn, and skim a public industry report on procurement timelines. They write down their advancement target: an intro to whoever owns the relevant budget line.

**During:** Two-person team. Lead asks, second takes notes. Verbatim quotes captured. Symbols on signals. Strong emotional moment when the prospect describes a vendor that overpromised — note marked with :( and ☇.

**After:** Review next morning. Read the quotes back. Realise their second big question (budget cycle) wasn't fully answered — schedule a 10-minute follow-up call to fill the gap. Update beliefs: procurement is slower than they expected and committee-driven. Update advancement target for the next meeting accordingly.

**Result:** A clean process turns one meeting into directional intelligence the whole team trusts. Without prep, they'd have rambled. Without notes, they'd have lost the procurement-cycle nuance. Without review, the tech team would still be building toward an unrealistic 30-day sales cycle.

---

**Situation:** A founder is the only one going to customer meetings. The CTO is increasingly skeptical about pivots.

**Application:** Force the CTO to attend the next 3 meetings as the second person (note-taker). Make the prep session a joint one — both write down the questions and the advancement target together. After each meeting, do a 20-minute review with the whole team where the CTO reads the verbatim quotes back.

**Result:** The "the customer said so" trump card stops working — because the customer is now in the CTO's head too, not just the founder's. Decisions become joint. Pivots become defensible because they're grounded in shared exposure to actual customer language. The original CTO-vs-founder tension was almost always a learning-bottleneck problem dressed up as a strategy disagreement.

---

**Situation:** A founder takes notes in their primary notebook — same one used for to-dos, ideas, and meetings.

**Application:** Several months in, they have hundreds of pages of mixed content and no way to find specific customer notes. Move to index cards. One quote per card. Date and name on each. Stack the cards in a small box. After every batch of conversations, the cards lay out on the table for review — sortable by symbol (all ☇ pain cards together), by date, by person, by segment.

**Result:** The same notes that were unfindable in the notebook become a working artefact. *"Show me all the cards where someone mentioned budget"* becomes a 30-second action. The cards beat audio recordings (which nobody re-listens to) and notebooks (which nobody re-reads).

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Send the "business" co-founder alone to every meeting and have them brief the team after.
**Why:** Creates the learning bottleneck. The team builds on a filtered, paraphrased summary they can't verify. Disagreements become political rather than evidential. The fix is shared exposure: send pairs, do team reviews with verbatim quotes, get the tech team into the room periodically.

**Don't:** Take audio recordings as a substitute for notes.
**Why:** I tried it. The team won't listen to dozens of hours of unedited audio. Recordings sit in a folder forever, doing nothing. Notes are short, scannable, and reviewable in 10 minutes per batch. Audio is also weird in casual contexts — it blows the cover and changes the conversation. Use audio as a backup if at all, not as the primary record.

**Don't:** Write "we should do X" without writing the verbatim quote that supports it.
**Why:** Without the quote, you're working from your *interpretation* of what the customer said, which by next week will have drifted. With the quote, anyone on the team can re-read it and form their own view. Quotes are the audit trail; interpretations are politics.

**Don't:** Spend a week prepping for one meeting.
**Why:** Prep is meant to be an hour. Anything more is procrastination. The point of customer development is to learn, build, and ship — not to write the perfect interview guide. If you're spending days on prep, you're using the process to avoid the awkwardness of actually talking to people.

**Don't:** Skip the review because "the meeting is fresh in my head."
**Why:** It's fresh in *yours*. It's not fresh in your team's. The whole point of review is to transfer the learning out of your head and into theirs. Skipping it means you remain the bottleneck regardless of how well you ran the meeting. Twenty minutes of review per batch is the price of avoiding the dictator pattern.

**Don't:** Treat the customer's exact quote as gospel without thinking.
**Why:** Quotes are *evidence*, not commands. The customer's job is to share their problem; your job is to design the solution. Confusing the two is what produces feature-creep and product-by-committee. The discipline is: capture the quote verbatim, then in review, ask *what does this actually tell us about the underlying problem and motivation?* — and design from there.
