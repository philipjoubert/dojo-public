# The Excellence Reflex

## How it relates to its source

The phrase **excellence reflex** was coined by Michael Romano, longtime executive chef at Union Square Cafe. Credit Romano for naming it. Danny absorbed it as the central hiring question for the work-ethic skill — the framework around it (how to hire for it, why it can't be trained, how it shows up in everyday cues) is Danny's adaptation.

## When to Use
- Hiring anyone whose work involves noticing and fixing — which, in any people-facing or quality-driven business, is everyone.
- Diagnosing why standards drop the moment a leader leaves the room.
- Reviewing performance: separating who actively pushes for better from who tolerates the status quo.
- Designing interview prompts and trial shifts to *see* the reflex (or its absence) before you commit to a hire.

## Fails When
- **You try to install it via training.** The reflex is rooted in instinct and upbringing. Training can sharpen it, awareness can deepen it, practice can hone it — but if it isn't there at the start, no training programme will create it. (It is the most common training-spend mistake leaders make.)
- **The role has no slack for the reflex to operate.** A line worker on a fully scripted process with no ability to deviate cannot exercise the reflex. The reflex needs surface area to act on.
- **You confuse it with perfectionism.** The excellence reflex fixes what's wrong; perfectionism prevents anything from being finished. They are not the same.

## Core Concept

People duck when something is hurled at them. That's a reflex — instantaneous, automatic, no thought. The **excellence reflex** is the same kind of response, except the trigger is *noticing something that isn't right or could be better*, and the reflex is *moving to fix it before being asked*.

> "People duck as a natural reflex when something is hurled at them. Similarly, the excellence reflex is a natural reaction to fix something that isn't right, or to improve something that could be better. The excellence reflex is rooted in instinct and upbringing, and then constantly honed through awareness, caring, and practice. The overarching concern to do the right thing well is something we can't train for. Either it's there or it isn't. So we need to train how to hire for it."

This is the work-ethic emotional skill, made operational. It shows up in tiny cues: the server who lifts a wineglass to check it for smudges before service; the cook who tastes the sauce again before plating; the host who notices a coat slipping off a chair across the dining room and walks over to right it; the team member who quietly fixes the typo on the menu board without being asked, because it bothered them.

The reason the reflex matters more than the individual fixes: in a service business, *thousands* of small things go off-centre per day. No leader can personally catch them all. The only way the system stays at standard is if every team member is independently catching and fixing as a reflex. Without it, the standard collapses to whatever the least-observant person in the room tolerates.

The reflex's origin is upbringing. Most people who have it learned it before they walked into the interview — usually from a parent, a coach, a grandmother, an old boss. The grandmother who said *"the sauce is never going to be any better than the worst ingredients you put into it, and the ingredients won't be any better than how well you treat them after you buy them"* was teaching the excellence reflex about food. Some people internalise that lesson early; some never do. Hiring is mostly a matter of recognising which is which.

## How to Apply

1. **Design interview prompts that surface the reflex.** Ask: *"Tell me about a time you noticed something at work that nobody else had noticed and you fixed it."* The vivid, unhesitating answer with specific detail is the reflex. The vague *"I always try to do quality work"* answer is its absence. Listen for the verb: *I noticed... I fixed... I went back...* — not *we tried... the team focused on...*

2. **Use working trials to see the reflex live.** A trail (paid working trial — see `51-percent-solution.md`) gives you 4–6 hours to watch the candidate in the actual environment. Does the candidate, unprompted, straighten a chair? Notice a smudge? Pick up a dropped napkin? Or do they walk past? The reflex shows up within a few hours — you just have to be watching.

3. **Ask references for examples, not adjectives.** *"Can you give me a concrete example of [name] noticing and fixing something on their own initiative?"* If the reference can produce a specific story easily, the reflex is real. If they hedge or generalise, it isn't.

4. **Remove the artificial constraints that suppress the reflex.** Even a person with the reflex won't exercise it if the system says *do not deviate*. Give the team explicit permission and authority to fix — within riverbanks (see `constant-gentle-pressure.md`). The reflex needs slack.

5. **Honour the reflex publicly when you see it.** When a teammate exercises the reflex, acknowledge it specifically — to them and (selectively) to the broader team. *"Catch them in the act of doing things right"* (a Ken Blanchard line Danny adopted). This reinforces both the reflex in the individual and the cultural expectation that the reflex is what the company runs on.

6. **Stop tolerating its absence.** The most expensive thing you can do is keep on payroll a person without the reflex who is otherwise technically competent. They produce the *whelmer* problem (see `whelmers.md`) — quietly transmitting that the standard is whatever can be ignored. Either coach the reflex (sharpen what's there) or part ways.

7. **Test for it in promotion decisions, not just hiring.** A strong performer at level 1 who has technical chops but lacks the reflex will sink at level 2, where independent judgment becomes the job. Gate promotions on demonstrated reflex, not just demonstrated execution.

## Examples

**Situation: Pre-service in the dining room. A waiter, alone, picks up a wineglass, lifts it to the light, checks for smudges, polishes it, sets it down. No manager is watching. No guest is in the room.**

> "This is not because I'm an unreformed smudge freak, but because someone is showing care for a small detail — smaller even than what the average guest may notice."

**Application:** This is the reflex unprompted, with no audience and no immediate reward. The waiter notices what would not have been noticed and fixes what would not have been fixed. Multiplied across every prep moment, the entire room reaches a standard the leader could never personally enforce.

**Result:** The dining room runs at standard not because of supervision but because of dispersed, independent care. This is the only way standards hold at scale.

---

**Situation: Danny's grandmother teaching the tomato sauce recipe.**

> "She said, I'll give you the recipe, but I'll tell you one thing right now. It's never gonna be any better than the worst ingredients you put into it. And number two, those ingredients themselves won't be any better than how well you treat them after you buy them. You better get the best tomatoes, which in New York is like a two-month season. And even if you get the best tomatoes, you better not throw them in the back corner of the walk-in refrigerator and bruise them so that they lose their sugar."

**Application:** The grandmother is teaching the excellence reflex applied to ingredients. Notice the worst input, fix it; notice how you handle it, fix that too. The same principle then transfers — Danny's adaptation — to people: *"You can have the best recipe in the world for how you hire people, but you better pick the best tomatoes. You better treat them the right way if you want the best sauce."*

**Result:** The reflex is recursive — it applies to the inputs of the inputs. Once you have it, you apply it to the supply chain, the hiring funnel, the training programme, the room temperature. It scales horizontally because it's a *disposition*, not a checklist.

---

**Situation: A trail shift candidate at a working trial. Two hours in, a stack of menus on the host stand has tipped over. The candidate walks past it twice. Then walks past it again.**

**Application:** This is the absence of the reflex showing up. No formal test was administered; the candidate simply didn't notice (or noticed and didn't act). A candidate with the reflex would have righted the menus on the first pass, probably without thinking about it.

**Result:** Don't hire. Not because the menus are critical — they aren't. Because what didn't happen at the menus will keep not happening at the wineglass, the chair, the silverware, the guest's coat, the spilled water, the half-burned-out lightbulb. The reflex either fires or it doesn't.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to "build" the reflex through a training programme.
*Why:* You can sharpen it. You cannot install it. Training spend on installing the reflex in people who don't have it is the most consistently wasted training budget in service businesses. Hire for it; train for skill on top of it.

**Don't:** Treat the reflex as nitpicking when a teammate exercises it.
*Why:* When someone says *"the menus are tipped, can we fix the host stand layout?"* and the response is *"that doesn't matter, focus on the bigger stuff,"* you've trained the reflex out of them. Honour the catch. The bigger stuff is downstream of the small stuff.

**Don't:** Mistake busyness for the reflex.
*Why:* A person who is constantly doing things — and constantly doing the *wrong* things, or the same things the system already covers — is not exercising the reflex. The reflex is *noticing what is off-centre and fixing it* — selective, observant, additive. Busy is just busy.

**Don't:** Use the reflex as the only standard for promotion.
*Why:* People with strong reflex but weak technical or emotional skills in other dimensions can become tyrants of the small fix. The reflex is one of five emotional skills (see `51-percent-solution.md`). Promote on the bundle, not on the single trait.

**Don't:** Punish the reflex when it surfaces an inconvenient truth.
*Why:* The reflex sometimes catches things that *are* wrong — a procedure, a policy, a manager. The team learns very quickly whether you actually want what's-wrong-fixed or just what's-wrong-out-of-sight. If you punish surfacing, the reflex stops firing. Then standards collapse. Then you're running the room alone again.
