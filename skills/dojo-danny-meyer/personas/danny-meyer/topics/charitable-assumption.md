# Charitable Assumption

## When to Use
- Setting the company's *default disposition* — the starting stance from which every interaction begins.
- Hiring or coaching managers — disposition is one of the nine traits Danny screens for explicitly.
- Designing customer-dispute resolution, supplier-dispute resolution, internal-conflict resolution.
- Reviewing the team's posture in difficult moments: are we starting from generosity or starting from suspicion?
- Codifying USHG's Six Behaviors — *"Bring a charitable assumption and err on the side of generosity"* is one of the six.

## Fails When
- **There is a clear pattern of dishonesty.** Charitable assumption is a *starting* stance, not an indefinite one. After the third or fourth instance of someone proving the assumption wrong, it's no longer charitable to keep assuming — it's negligent.
- **The user uses it to avoid the hard conversation.** *"I'm sure they have a good reason"* can be a euphemism for *I don't want to address the problem*. The framework is about *starting* generously, not avoiding responsibility for surfacing concerns.
- **Used in a context of clear safety or integrity violation.** The first instance of theft, harassment, or violence is not the place to start with charitable assumption. Safety first; charitable assumption is for ambiguous, low-stakes situations.

## Core Concept

Charitable assumption is the default disposition — the *starting* belief that the other person's stumble came from a good place. The opposite default is the suspicious one: assume they're trying to take advantage, assume they're lying, assume their mistake was malicious or careless. Both dispositions become self-fulfilling.

> "Enlightened hospitality is a philosophy that works best with optimistic, hopeful, open-minded people at the helm. It tends not to work when the leaders are skeptics who think they already have all the answers... A charitable mind-set assumes the best in other people. Mind-sets tend to become self-fulfilling prophecies. When you assume that people's stumbles are honest mistakes that come from a good place, you get farther with them during their victories. When you assume the worst of people, you get the worst from people."

The framework operates at three levels.

**With guests/customers.** The third-time-late guest probably *did* have a foul-up on the subway. The complaint about the steak being undercooked probably *is* sincere. The angry email probably *did* come from a real bad experience, not a scheme to scam a free meal. *"It's hard to justify being ungracious to anyone who wants to spend money at your restaurant. A charitable assumption might be, 'You must have had a tough time getting here. We're delighted that you made it!'"* The default earns goodwill across thousands of interactions; the cost of the occasional bad-faith customer who exploits the disposition is small compared to the cumulative loss from a suspicious default that frustrates the 99% of honest ones.

**With teammates.** The colleague whose work is late probably had a real reason. The teammate whose tone in the email was sharp probably wasn't attacking you — they were probably tired, or pressured, or had bad context. Start there. Often the charitable interpretation is correct. When it isn't, you can recalibrate without having damaged the relationship.

**With suppliers, partners, the press.** The supplier who missed delivery probably had a real problem — call before assuming malice. The journalist who got something wrong in the article probably misheard or got bad source material — write a polite correction, don't go to war. The default of generosity scales every relationship that lasts longer than a single transaction.

The economic logic: in any business with repeat interactions and word-of-mouth dynamics, the math runs over years and reputations. The charitable default produces compound goodwill — *"there's practically no downside to a hospitable, charitable assumption. Experience has shown me that 90 percent of the time the bottle of wine whose cork broke is going to be good. If the guest rejects it and I still believe it's good, I can always sell the wine by the glass at the bar. And if the bottle's not good, it's far better for me to have been gracious about the transaction in the first place."*

The disposition is also a hiring criterion. When Danny screens managers for the nine traits (see `constant-gentle-pressure.md`), charitable assumption is one of them. Skeptical managers — even effective ones — don't fit the company because their default rolls downstream and creates a culture of suspicion that breaks the rest of the system.

## How to Apply

1. **Make the charitable assumption the explicit default.** Write it down. Train it. Repeat it in meetings. Most teams operate on an implicit suspicious default and need to be moved deliberately to the charitable one. The implicit version doesn't work.

2. **Frame the first instance as a question, not an accusation.** First-time-late: *"Hope the subway wasn't too rough — let's get you started."* First missed delivery: *"Hey, is everything okay on your end? Just wanted to check in."* The opening sentence sets the trajectory of the relationship.

3. **Treat the second instance as a moment for honest dialogue.** The second time the same person is late, the same supplier misses, the same teammate's tone is sharp — gently surface the pattern. *"I've noticed twice now — is something going on?"* Charitable assumption doesn't preclude the conversation; it changes the tone of it.

4. **Recalibrate after a clear pattern.** *"The third time we'll realize that our colleague may just have a poor work ethic."* By the third instance, the data has changed. Charitable assumption was the starting posture; it now updates. This is not abandoning the framework — it is the framework working as designed.

5. **Make the disposition visible to customers.** When something goes wrong with the guest's order, the bottle, the timing — start with charitable interpretation aloud. *"Sounds like there's been a delay — let me see what happened."* Not *"Are you sure you ordered medium-rare?"* The opening words determine whether the rest of the interaction is hospitality or interrogation.

6. **Coach managers to model the disposition.** Managers' defaults set the team's defaults. A skeptical manager creates a skeptical team — even if the company values say otherwise. Hire and promote managers whose disposition is genuinely generous. (When you hire someone whose disposition isn't, you get the team's disposition you didn't ask for.)

7. **Apply it inward too.** When you stumble, give yourself the charitable assumption. The disposition is recursive — leaders who assume the worst about themselves usually project it onto everyone else. Self-charitable is part of the practice.

## Examples

**Situation: The waiter accidentally breaks the cork of a guest's wine bottle.**

> "If you come to one of our restaurants and the waiter accidentally breaks the cork on your bottle of wine, everyone is uncomfortable. The waiter is embarrassed, and you might be wondering if that means the wine itself is bad. At this point I would want the waiter to be candid: 'I'm sorry I broke the cork. I assume the wine is still fine but if for any reason it's not, please let me know and we'll gladly replace the bottle for you.'"

**Application:** The charitable assumption runs in two directions. Toward the wine (it's probably fine — 90% of the time, it is) and toward the guest (if they say it's off, they're not scamming us — replace the bottle). The disposition removes the awkward dance and produces a clean, generous outcome.

**Result:** Today dollars or tomorrow dollars — both available. The guest gets a stress-free moment to be right (if the wine is off) and feels respected. The bottle, if good, gets sold by the glass at the bar. No one loses.

---

**Situation: A guest is twenty minutes late for a reservation. The host's first reaction is to be irritated — the table is being held, the kitchen is going to back up.**

**Application:** Charitable assumption gets the first sentence. *"You must have had a tough time getting here. We're delighted that you made it!"* Not *"You're twenty minutes late — we may not be able to seat you."* The opening sentence determines whether this guest's evening (and their next twenty visits) starts in resentment or in welcome.

**Result:** The guest, who actually *was* delayed by something genuine, becomes a regular. The competing restaurant down the street, where the same guest was scolded for being late, never sees them again.

---

**Situation: A colleague's email lands sharp. You read it and feel attacked.**

**Application:** Charitable assumption first — they were probably tired, pressured, had context you don't have. Reply with the same warmth you'd reply with if you didn't feel attacked. If the next exchange is also sharp, then ask in person — *"Hey, the tone of your last email surprised me — is everything okay?"* The disposition gives you the chance to *not escalate* a misread that wasn't actually an attack.

**Result:** Most of the time the charitable read was correct. The colleague was fine, the tone was a misfire, the relationship is intact. Without the disposition, the same exchange could have produced a months-long cold war over nothing.

---

**Situation: Tim Ferriss asks Danny about the disposition itself.**

> "Can't we please have a charitable assumption about one another? I think I'd put that on there. I feel like when we go into a conversation with somebody or read about somebody, and we assume the worst as the starting point, it doesn't usually end up very well. On the other hand, when you assume the best intentions, you just never know. It's very possible that you hadn't communicated, understood what someone really meant. And I feel like going into any relationship or experience with a charitable assumption is such a helpful thing."

**Application:** This is the disposition stated as a life principle, not just a management one. The framework scales beyond the restaurant — into politics, friendship, family, public discourse. The default of generosity is the operational form of hope; not naïveté, but a deliberate posture that keeps the door open.

**Result:** A culture (in the restaurant, the company, the relationships) where most people feel trusted, behave more trustworthily as a result, and produce the kind of repeated interactions that make compounding loyalty possible.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use charitable assumption to avoid surfacing real problems.
*Why:* The disposition is a starting stance, not a permanent one. *"I'm sure they had a good reason"* repeated indefinitely becomes negligence — both to the team and to the person, who deserves the honest conversation. Charitable opening; honest follow-up.

**Don't:** Apply it to integrity violations.
*Why:* The first instance of theft, harassment, deception, or safety violation is not the place to start with *"I'm sure there's a good reason."* Those situations require investigation and clarity, not charity. The framework is for ambiguous, low-stakes interactions, not for character violations.

**Don't:** Confuse it with naïveté.
*Why:* Charitable assumption is *deliberate*, not *innocent*. You know the person might be taking advantage; you choose to start generously anyway because the math runs in your favour over the long run. The cynic mistakes the disposition for not knowing better. The disposition is a strategy, not a blind spot.

**Don't:** Stop updating after the third instance.
*Why:* When the data changes, the disposition updates. Continuing to assume good faith after repeated bad-faith behaviour is its own failure — and a failure of leadership to the rest of the team, who watches and calibrates.

**Don't:** Make it a slogan instead of an operating practice.
*Why:* "We bring a charitable assumption" on the values poster doesn't matter unless the manager who hears the third late arrival actually opens the conversation with *"Hope you're okay"* instead of *"This is the third time this month."* The disposition lives in the opening sentence of difficult moments, not in the values document.
