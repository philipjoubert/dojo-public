# Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice

## How it relates to its source

The brain-physiology framing here — *amygdala vs. pre-frontal cortex* — comes from Robert Sapolsky's *Behave* and Jonathan Haidt's *The Righteous Mind* (the claim that human thought starts with an emotion and the rational brain assembles a story to justify it). Mochary cites both directly. His distinctive contribution is the operationalization: the prediction-and-bet practice, the Fear Journal, the *I perceive you to be in fear/anger* outsourcing protocol, and the rule of *shift first or do the opposite*. Credit Sapolsky and Haidt for the science. The protocol is Mochary's.

## When to Use
- Any moment a founder is about to act on a decision and the underlying energy is fear or anger.
- Any moment a founder is *delaying* an action — a piece of feedback, a layoff, a price hike, a hard conversation — and the stated reason sounds reasonable but the actual driver is dread.
- Coaching another leader (a direct report, a co-founder) when you can see fear gripping them and they cannot.
- As a reasoning-move check before answering any tactical question. Before solving the problem the user describes, ask: is this person in fear or anger right now? If yes, the *story* they're telling about the situation is almost certainly distorted, and a tactical answer downstream of a distorted story will land badly.

## Fails When
- **The user is in real physical danger.** Fear evolved to keep you alive when there was a sabertooth tiger in the cave. If there is an actual tiger, listen to fear. The protocol is for the modern case where the dangers are to ego, status, reputation, optionality — not body.
- **You diagnose fear when there isn't any.** A founder asking a calm tactical question doesn't need to be told they're in fear. Calling fear when it isn't there is condescending and erodes trust in the framework.
- **The user uses the protocol to delay action they've already decided on.** "Shift out of fear first" can become its own avoidance ritual. The whole point is to *act* — either after shifting or, if there's urgency, immediately and opposite to what fear is recommending. A protocol that produces more delay has been misused.

## Core Concept

There are five basic emotions — excitement, joy, fear, anger, sadness — and every other emotion is some shade of these. Two of them — fear and anger — emanate from the amygdala, the reptile brain, and they always give bad advice in modern life.

Fear is future-looking: *we don't know what will happen, and we predict it will not be good.* Anger is present-tense: *what is happening right now is not good.* Both evolved to protect the body when the threat was a wild animal or an unfamiliar berry. In that world, the brain's job was to assemble a quick story — *that cave is dangerous, that fruit will kill me* — and the story kept you alive. Today the dangers are to your ego, your status, your perceived reputation. The amygdala is still calibrated for tigers. So when it tells you *"if I give her this feedback she'll quit and write a hit piece in the New York Times,"* what you're hearing is a tiger story applied to a feedback conversation. The story is almost never true. But because Haidt is right that thought starts with emotion and confirmation bias hardens the story, you cannot reason your way out of it from inside the moment.

> "Fear and anger both emanate from the amygdala and they grip our brain and they start giving our brain really exaggerated and frankly bad advice. Particularly fear. Fear says don't do anything. Fear is trying to prevent us from acting because what fear is associated with is physical danger." (Afore keynote)

The opportunity cost is enormous. The board member you don't push back on stays on the board. The exec you don't give kind, direct feedback to keeps underperforming. The price you don't raise, you don't raise. Across a year of decisions made by a fear-gripped brain, the company's trajectory bends meaningfully downward — and you can't see it because every individual delay felt rational at the time.

The exit is not to suppress the emotion. Fear and anger are healthy signals — *something here is new or risky; pay attention.* That signal is intelligent. The story the brain assembles to justify the signal is the part that's wrong. The work is to receive the signal, not act on the story.

## How to Apply

1. **Outsource the detection.** You will not catch yourself in fear or anger in the moment. You can spend years meditating and the marginal improvement in self-detection is small. Instead, ask the people you live and work with — spouse, co-founder, exec team, chief of staff — to tell you when they perceive it. Give them the exact phrasing: *"I perceive you to be in fear"* or *"I perceive you to be in anger."* Not *"You're in fear"* (triggers defensiveness). Not *"Are you in fear?"* (triggers anger). The construction *"I perceive..."* is observational, gentle, and works.

2. **When you hear the signal, stop.** Take no action. The default protocol is *shift first, decide second.* If a teammate has just told you *"I perceive you to be in anger,"* the move is not to argue the point — the move is to pause whatever you were about to send, say, or do.

3. **If you genuinely cannot wait, do the opposite of what fear or anger is recommending.** This case is rarer than founders think — almost nothing in a CEO's life requires action in the next sixty seconds. But when it's real:
   - In anger: your brain is telling you to crush the person who wronged you. Instead, get curious about why they did what they did. (You can still end the relationship later. First understand.)
   - In fear: your brain is telling you not to do the thing — not to give the feedback, not to raise the price, not to push back. Instead, do the thing. Kindly. The unwanted outcome is almost never as dire as the brain is predicting.

4. **Shift physically.** If you're sitting, stand up. If you're inside, go outside. If you're still, sprint as hard as you can for two minutes. Anger lives in the body — physical state changes break it. For deeper anger: go into another room or your car, grab a pillow, yell into it as loud as you can for thirty seconds. The release is real and the pillow muffles it.

5. **Run the prediction explicitly.** Fear comes dressed as a prediction: *"If I do X, Y will happen. Then Z."* Run it all the way to the end. Write down each step. Then read it back. The catastrophe usually evaporates the moment it's on paper, because the brain refuses to let you stop at "they'll be upset" — it has to keep going, and three steps in the prediction usually becomes obviously implausible.

6. **Make the bet.** Find someone you trust who is *not* in your fear about this situation — accountability partner, coach, spouse, co-founder. Tell them your prediction. Ask theirs. If you disagree, bet on it. Then take the action and see who wins.

   > "I have made these bets hundreds of times with people that I perceive to be in fear. I have yet to lose." (Afore keynote)

   The brain that's not in fear sees the situation accurately. The brain that's in fear is reading the modern situation through a tiger filter. Almost every time, the in-fear prediction is wrong — and not just slightly wrong, dramatically wrong. The board member doesn't sue. The exec doesn't quit. The customer doesn't churn. The press doesn't write the story. Most of the time, in fact, the action you were afraid to take produces a *better* outcome than the status quo you were protecting.

7. **Keep a Fear Journal.** This is Regina Gerbeaux's contribution to the protocol. Whenever you feel fear, or someone tells you they perceive you in fear, write down the prediction your brain is making. Date it. A week or a month or a year later, go back and read your predictions. See how many came true. See how many of the ones that did come true were actually net-negative when you got there. Over time the journal trains you to disbelieve your own catastrophe stories — not abstractly, but with your own data.

8. **Push through the fear-to-joy valley.** Once you start consistently disbelieving fear, you'll notice you've been running your business on fear of failure as the primary motivator. When that engine quits, the business dips for a while because you're between motivators. Push through. Joy is on the other side, and joy is a better engine — you stay motivated for decades, you feel peaceful and satisfied, and the business performs better long-term. (See `conscious-leadership.md` for the full sequence — discover joy at work first via the Energy Audit, *then* lose fear.)

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder has a senior exec underperforming. The exec is the only woman of color on the leadership team. The founder is in fear that giving direct performance feedback will be perceived as racist, end in a lawsuit or a hostile press story, and damage the company permanently. The decision has been delayed for three months.

**Application:** This is a textbook fear story. Run the bet. Founder predicts: *"If I give her this feedback, she'll be furious, take it to the press or a lawyer, and we'll be on the front page."* The non-in-fear prediction: *"She doesn't even know she's underperforming because nobody is giving her feedback. If you give it kindly, she'll most likely thank you, ask what to do, and try to perform."* Bet on it. Then deliver the feedback — kindly, specifically, with a concrete picture of what good performance looks like. Make her feel heard (see `heard.md`) before, during, and after.

**Result:** The likely outcome is the non-in-fear prediction. The exec is grateful for clarity, asks specific questions about what to change, and either improves or eventually self-selects out of the role with the relationship intact. The catastrophe scenario doesn't happen. The founder logs it in the Fear Journal as one more piece of evidence that fear's stories don't materialize.

---

**Situation:** Founder calls in a panic the morning of a planned termination meeting. *"Matt, I feel like I'm going to die. I can't do this. I have to delay."* This is the first executive they've ever had to let go.

**Application:** This is anger and fear stacked together — a body-level reaction. The protocol: get someone else to sit with the founder while they do the meeting. Don't try to talk them out of the panic. The only way out is through. The pattern recognition is from childhood — *disappointing an adult is dangerous when you are five.* It is not dangerous when you are forty. Going *into* the action is what proves the prediction wrong. Avoiding it deepens the panic. Drugs and alcohol mask it temporarily and then it breaks the body.

**Result:** Two hours later, founder texts: *"That was amazing. The anxiety is completely gone."* Because they did the thing, the catastrophe didn't materialize, the person they let go cried but did not die, and the body learned — at the cellular level, not the conceptual level — that fear was lying. The next termination is meaningfully easier. Each cycle requires less push.

---

**Situation:** A founder's spouse says, calmly, at dinner: *"I perceive you to be in anger."* The founder was about to fire off a Slack message to a board member who'd publicly questioned a strategic decision in a board meeting earlier that day.

**Application:** Stop. Don't send. The phrasing *"I perceive..."* lands cleanly and the founder does not get more angry — they get aware. Now apply step 4: stand up, go outside, walk for ten minutes. Then run the prediction at step 5: *"If I send this message, the board member will... and then... and then..."* The catastrophic spiral the founder was building (board member resigns, signals to other directors, fund stops returning calls) deflates the moment it's on paper. The actual move turns out to be a one-on-one phone call the next morning, framed as curiosity — *"I want to understand what you saw in the meeting"* — instead of an angry Slack.

**Result:** The relationship is intact. The board member's actual concern (which was reasonable) gets surfaced and addressed. A Slack message that would have damaged the relationship for a year never goes out. The cost of the spouse's fifteen-second intervention: zero. The cost of the message: catastrophic and avoidable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to reason your way out of fear from inside the moment.
*Why:* Haidt's point is that the rational brain serves the emotional brain, not the other way around. While you are in fear, your "reasoning" is just confirmation-biased story construction. The fix is shift-or-bet, not argue. Reasoning your way out is what the in-fear brain *thinks* it's doing while it actually digs in deeper.

**Don't:** Suppress or push the emotion away.
*Why:* The signal is intelligent — something here is new, risky, or wrong. Suppressing the signal does not eliminate the fear, it just buries the awareness while the body keeps running the response. The move is to *feel* the emotion (Sabrina Wang's 90-second observation technique works — body scan, name the sensation, breathe through it) and *not act on the story.* Feeling and not-acting are different from suppressing.

**Don't:** Use *"I perceive you to be in fear"* as a weapon in an argument.
*Why:* The phrasing is a gift. It works because it's observational and offered with care. The moment it becomes a way to dismiss someone's argument — *"you're just in fear, so what you're saying doesn't count"* — it stops being a tool for shift and becomes a manipulation. People feel it instantly and the protocol stops working with them forever. Use it for good only.

**Don't:** Tell a person they're in fear before establishing the relationship for it.
*Why:* The phrasing only lands cleanly if the person has already agreed to be told. Mochary explicitly asks his wife, kids, and team to use it. Springing it on someone who hasn't opted in produces defensiveness, not awareness — they will hear it as an accusation rather than an offer.

**Don't:** Use the bet practice to humiliate the person in fear.
*Why:* The point of the bet is data, not victory. *"I told you so"* erodes the relationship and trains the person to hide their fear from you next time, which removes the entire detection mechanism. When you win the bet, the move is to log it gently — *"the prediction didn't come true, let's note that"* — not to celebrate. Over time the person learns to disbelieve their own fear stories on their own.

**Don't:** Try to lose fear before you've discovered joy at work.
*Why:* Fear is a powerful motivator. If you remove it before you've found a joy-based motivator (your Zone of Genius, work you genuinely love, energy-giving activities), you fall into a motivation valley with nothing pulling you forward. You can get stuck there. The order matters: discover joy first via the Energy Audit, *then* do the conscious leadership work to lose fear. Reverse it and you'll quit your company on a beach somewhere.

---

If running the full protocol — outsource detection, shift, predict, bet, journal — feels like too much for the situation in front of you, start with one move: the next time you feel the body-level signal of fear about an action, write down the prediction your brain is making, take the action anyway, and check the prediction a week later. That single loop, run twenty times, will do most of the work.
