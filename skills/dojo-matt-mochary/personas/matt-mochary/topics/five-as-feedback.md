# The Five A's of Receiving Feedback

## When to Use
- A direct report, co-founder, board member, or customer is about to give you (or has just given you) feedback you may not like.
- You are building a feedback culture and the receiving side keeps breaking — people give once, see how you respond, never give again.
- You have noticed your team has stopped bringing you problems. They have decided giving you feedback is an asymmetric bet in the negative direction. Run the protocol to repair that.
- You are reading negative feedback from an exit interview, an anonymous survey, a customer email, or a board memo. Same five steps; same discipline.

## Fails When
- **You skip Acknowledge.** Acknowledge is the load-bearing step — it is the thing that proves you actually heard them. Skipping it and jumping to "thank you" reads as dismissive. Skipping to "let me explain why you're wrong" reads as defensive. Either way the feedback channel closes.
- **You "accept" feedback you have no intention of acting on.** The Five A's include Accept-or-not as a deliberate step because not all feedback is correct. Performing acceptance and then ignoring the feedback teaches the team that the protocol is theatre. Reject cleanly with a reason, or accept and act. Never fake-accept.
- **You run the protocol without first making it safe to give the feedback at all.** If your team has not yet seen you receive negative feedback well, they will not give it spontaneously. The Ask step is non-trivial — most leaders skip it and wonder why no one ever has anything critical to say.
- **You use the protocol on someone you don't actually intend to treat well.** This is the deeper warning. The Five A's are manipulative — they create trust and connection. Use them on a person whose feedback you have no real interest in and you will burn the trust they generate. The discipline is to deserve the trust the tool generates.

## Core Concept

If you want to receive accurate, honest feedback — and you must, because your team is in the trenches every day and they know things about your company that you do not — you have to actively pull the feedback from each person. Asking once doesn't work. "Give me feedback anytime" doesn't work. Anonymous surveys after meetings don't work. Your team will not voluntarily walk into a room and tell the most powerful person in their professional life that they are getting something wrong.

The reason is simple: it is an asymmetric bet, and they lose the asymmetric direction. If you take the feedback well, your behaviour improves and they get a +1. If you take it badly — defensive, vindictive, retaliatory — they get a -100. Most rational humans will not place that wager. So the work, the *first* time you receive negative feedback from someone, is to prove you will take it well. The Five A's are the protocol that does that proving.

> "If you want to receive accurate, honest feedback and improve and keep your team communicating, you must make an effort to seek it. Do so using the five A's: Ask for it. Acknowledge it. Appreciate it. Accept it (or not). Act on it."
> — *Feedback: Receiving: the 5 A's*

The five steps are not optional and not interchangeable. **Ask** is the unlock — without it the team won't bring the hard stuff. **Acknowledge** is the proof that you heard — without it everything else lands as performance. **Appreciate** is the tax you pay for the gift you just received — no excuses, no arguments, just thank you. **Accept (or not)** is the integrity of the protocol — you are allowed to disagree, but you must declare your stance. **Act** is the proof of life — feedback received and never acted on tells your team the loop is closed for show. Run all five every time. Skip a step and the protocol breaks.

> "Acknowledging is the single most powerful tool that I know of to create trust and connection. People need to feel heard. And they can only know that you heard them if you say back their words (or, even better, their thoughts) to them. Oddly, this step is actually more impactful than acting on their feedback."
> — *Feedback: Receiving: the 5 A's*

## How to Apply

**1. Ask for it.**

Make sure your team understands explicitly that giving you negative feedback will not be punished but cherished. Saying it once is not enough. You have to actively pull. The technique that works:

> *"Please don't say it, just think it. What is the thought you have that you are afraid to tell me because you think it will hurt my feelings? Again, please don't say it; just think about it. Do you have it in your brain chamber? OK, now please say it."*

(This is Lachy Groom's method, which Mochary uses constantly.) Carve out the space — last five to ten minutes of every meeting, written into a shared doc in real time, archived so you can refer back forever. Do not send a post-meeting survey. Surveys collect almost nothing useful because no one trusts that anyone will read them or act on them.

If a report has *never* given you negative feedback, tell them: *"Until you share truly negative feedback with me, I will think you are simply withholding, and I won't be able to fully trust you."* This is a real, fair statement. A report who never tells you what is actually wrong is not your ally — they are managing you.

**2. Acknowledge it.**

Confirm that you heard the feedback correctly: *"I think I heard you say…"* Summarise back, in their voice, as if you were them talking to you. Then: *"Is that right?"* If yes, go deeper: *"There's more. Please tell me more."* Repeat until they say "that's everything."

If you want to go to the advanced level — and you should — describe the *emotion* they are experiencing and the *thought* that is actually in their head, not just the polite version they said out loud. Feel the emotion yourself. Imagine you were them. Go big. Use swear words. They have toned the thought down because they fear you will get angry; if you tone it down further, they do not feel heard. If you amp it up, they will say "no, not that extreme" and feel completely heard.

> What was said: *"I wish that you wouldn't act in anger."*
> What is likely in their head: *"Goddammit, why do you always have to be such an f-ing bully? Let people talk and at least have the courtesy to not rage at the ones who are telling you what you need to hear while the others cower in silence!"*

Reflect the actual thought. They will exhale. They will say "yeah, that's basically it." That moment is the unlock. To confirm: *"Do you feel heard?"* If they say yes, move on. If they hesitate, you missed something — go again.

**3. Appreciate it.**

Say *"thank you"* for the precious gift your colleague just gave you. Not "thank you, but…" Not "thank you, I appreciate it, however…" Just thank you. Don't make excuses. Don't argue back. Don't explain. The Appreciate step is where most leaders blow up the protocol — they appreciate-then-defend, and the "but" eats the thank you.

The reason this matters: giving you feedback cost the person something. Possibly a lot. They sat with the thought, drafted it, decided to risk it, walked into the room, and said it. The minimum response is gratitude. Anything less and the lesson they carry forward is "feedback is more expensive than I thought." Next time, they will not give it.

**4. Accept it (or not).**

Declare whether you accept the feedback. You do not have to accept it. If you had to accept all feedback it would become tyranny of the feedback-giver, and the protocol would collapse under the weight of every conflicting opinion you ever received.

If you do *not* accept it: explain why. Bring them into your world. Make them see why this feedback doesn't work for you. *"I hear you, and I'm choosing not to act on this because [reason]. Here's what's true in my world that makes this trade-off the right one."* This is honest and survivable — most people will accept "I disagree, here's why" cleanly. What they will not accept is silent rejection dressed up as listening.

If you do accept it, move to step 5.

**5. Act on it.**

Co-create an action item with the feedback-giver. Put a due date on it. Do it. Then tell them you did it. Then publish the feedback and the action widely to the organisation. This is the move that completes the loop and proves to the rest of the team that feedback gets read, accepted, and acted on — that the channel is real.

The size of the action doesn't matter. A small, specific, dated action — *"I'll stop interrupting you in 1-1s; you'll have full ten-minute blocks; if I slip, call me on it"* — is worth more than a vague *"I'll work on my listening."* Specific actions get done. Vague intentions don't.

## Examples

**Situation:** A new VP gives you feedback in a 1-1: *"You don't always include me in strategy discussions even though decisions affect my team."*

**Application:**
1. **Ask:** You opened the space — last ten minutes of the 1-1 — *"What feedback are you afraid to give me because you think it will hurt my feelings?"* She wrote it in the shared doc.
2. **Acknowledge:** *"I think I heard you say that I'm running strategy as a closed group, and the thought you're having is something like 'Matt invited me onto this exec team and then immediately treated me like an outsider — what was the point of hiring me?' Is that close?"* She: *"Yeah, that's exactly it."* You: *"Is there more?"* She goes deeper. You keep going until she says "no, that's everything." You: *"Do you feel heard?"* Yes.
3. **Appreciate:** *"Thank you. That took guts to say in your second month. I'm grateful."* (No "but.")
4. **Accept:** *"I accept it. You're right, and the gap is real."*
5. **Act:** *"Concrete: starting next week, you're in the Monday strategy block, full attendance, and I'll bring you into the strategy doc as a co-editor. Two weeks from now we'll review whether it's working from your end. Sound good?"* Yes.

**Result:** She brings tougher feedback the next 1-1, and the next, and the channel stays open. Six months later she tells a peer who is interviewing: "this is the first place I've worked where I can actually say what I think."

---

**Situation:** A board member gives you feedback you don't agree with: *"I think you should fire your CTO."*

**Application:**
1. **Acknowledge:** *"I hear you saying you think the CTO is a problem, and the thought going through your head is something like 'engineering velocity is half what it should be, the architecture decisions are wrong, and Matt is being loyal at the expense of the company.' Close?"* He: *"Yeah, that's it."* Deeper. *"Is there more?"* Etc.
2. **Appreciate:** *"Thank you for being direct. That's exactly the kind of board feedback I need."*
3. **Accept (or not):** *"I'm not going to act on this. Here's why: I agree velocity is too low, but I don't think the CTO is the cause — I think the cause is unclear strategy from me, and we're working on that. I'm asking you to give me ninety days. If velocity hasn't improved by then, I'll revisit."* Bring him into your world. Show the data you're seeing.

**Result:** He doesn't love the answer, but he respects the protocol. He gives you the ninety days. Velocity does improve. He brings you the next piece of hard feedback at the next board meeting because the channel still works.

---

**Situation:** Anonymous engagement survey results land. Someone wrote: *"I don't trust Matt. He listens, but nothing changes."*

**Application:** You can't run Acknowledge with an anonymous person. But you can run the rest of the protocol publicly. At the next all-hands: *"Someone wrote in the survey 'I don't trust Matt — he listens, but nothing changes.' Thank you for writing that. It's the most useful thing in the survey because it tells me the feedback loop is broken from the receiver's side. Here are three pieces of feedback I've received in the last quarter, what I accepted, what I rejected and why, and what I actually changed. From now on, every quarter, I'll publish this list."* Then publish.

**Result:** The next survey has more honest feedback, not less. The team sees the loop close visibly.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Acknowledge by paraphrasing the polite surface version of what they said.
*Why:* They toned it down because they were afraid of you. If you tone it down further, you have not heard them — you have heard a sanitised version, and they will know it. Reflect the *actual* thought, the unfiltered one, the one with the swear words. They will say "no, not that extreme" and feel heard for the first time in their professional life.

**Don't:** "Thank you, but…"
*Why:* The "but" eats the thank you. The Appreciate step has to land alone. Pause. Breathe. Move to the Accept-or-not step as a separate beat. Combining them tells the giver that the appreciation was performance.

**Don't:** Accept feedback you have no intention of acting on.
*Why:* Your team is watching. They learn very quickly whether your "I accept" is real. If feedback gets "accepted" and never produces an action, the team concludes the whole protocol is theatre and stops giving real feedback. Reject cleanly with a reason — that they will respect.

**Don't:** Skip the publish step.
*Why:* Acting on feedback in private benefits the giver. Publishing the feedback and the action benefits the *next* feedback-giver — they see the loop close visibly, which is what gives them the courage to bring the next hard truth. Without publish, the protocol works once and then dies.

**Don't:** Run the protocol on someone whose feedback you don't actually want.
*Why:* The Five A's create real trust. If you use them on a person you intend to ignore — or on someone whose feedback you've already decided is wrong — you are using a trust-creating tool to extract goodwill you don't deserve. The trust burns. The team senses it. The discipline is: deserve the trust the tool generates. If you cannot deserve it on this particular feedback, don't run the protocol — give the person an honest "I'm not going to take this in" and explain why.

The Five A's run thirty minutes the first time and five minutes once they're in your muscle. Once your team has seen you take three pieces of negative feedback well, they will give you the rest for the rest of your tenure. The single hardest part is the first time — the moment when someone says something that makes you flush, and your brain is screaming "defend yourself," and you take a breath and say "I think I heard you say…" instead. Do that once. The rest unlocks.
