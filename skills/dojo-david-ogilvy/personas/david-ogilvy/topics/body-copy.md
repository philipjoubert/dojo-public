# Body Copy

## When to Use
- You have a headline that made a specific promise and the body copy must now prove it, fact by fact.
- The product is considered — a car, a camera, a computer, a piece of machinery, a financial service, a trip. The reader is willing to work if you give him something to work on.
- The ad is mail-order or direct-response: every word will be measured against coupon returns or orders, and the split-run results are about to overrule every opinion in the room.
- The client has been told that "nobody reads long copy" and you need to show him split-run results that say otherwise.

## Fails When
- **There is nothing to say.** Chewing gum, underwear, and beer rarely justify 600 words. Do not pad. Long copy compounds facts; it does not stretch thin ones.
- **The copy is an essay.** A copywriter admiring his own prose produces copy that nobody reads. Pretend you are answering one person at a dinner table, not lecturing a stadium.
- **The copy closes on adjectives instead of facts.** Generalities, superlatives, and brag-and-boast convince nobody. Specifics convince.
- **The voice is borrowed from literature.** Aldous Huxley, who was once a copywriter himself, put it: "any trace of literariness in an advertisement is fatal to its success." — quoted in *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

## Core Concept

"The more facts you tell, the more you sell." — Dr. Charles Edwards, quoted in *Ogilvy on Advertising* and *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

The entire craft of body copy is in that sentence. An advertisement's chance for success increases as the number of pertinent merchandise facts in it increases. Direct-response advertisers — the copywriters whose sales are measured to the penny, who split-run one version against another and count the coupons — know this better than anyone. In their split-run tests, long copy invariably outsells short copy. — *Confessions* General advertisers, who are insulated from the sales curve by brand managers and committees, have spent decades ignoring this finding. That is their mistake, not the finding's.

"The consumer isn't a moron; she is your wife. You insult her intelligence if you assume that a mere slogan and a few vapid adjectives will persuade her to buy anything. She wants all the information you can give her." — *Confessions* That is the reader you are writing to. A grown woman deciding whether to spend her family's money on your product. She is not a moving demographic. She is not a creative target. She is a particular person and she is paying attention because she is thinking of buying. Give her the facts, not your style.

How long should your copy be? It depends on the product. If you are advertising chewing gum, there isn't much to tell, so make your copy short. If, on the other hand, you are advertising a product which has a great many different qualities to recommend it, write long copy: the more you tell, the more you sell. — *Confessions* In my first Rolls-Royce advertisement I used 719 words, piling one fascinating fact on another. In the next one I used 1,400 words. — *Confessions* A Louis Engel advertisement for Merrill Lynch ran to 6,450 words in *The New York Times* and pulled 10,000 responses without a coupon. Claude Hopkins wrote five pages of solid text for Schlitz beer; Schlitz moved from fifth in sales to first in a few months. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The voice is not an essay voice. It is a conversation. "Do not, however, address your readers as though they were gathered together in a stadium. When people read your copy, they are alone. Pretend you are writing each of them a letter on behalf of your client. One human being to another, second person singular." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Queen Victoria complained that Gladstone talked to her as if he were addressing a public meeting; she preferred Disraeli, who talked to her like a human being. Follow Disraeli's example. Or, as I told my writers at the agency: pretend you are talking to the woman on your right at a dinner party. She has asked you, "I am thinking of buying a new car. Which would you recommend?" Write your copy as if you were answering that question. — *Confessions*

Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Short words. When copywriters argue with me about some esoteric word they want to use, I tell them, "Get on a bus. Go to Iowa. Stay on a farm for a week and talk to the farmer. Come back to New York by train and talk to your fellow passengers in the day-coach. If you still want to use the word, go ahead." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* I once used the word *obsolete* in a headline for Dove, only to discover that the majority of housewives did not know what it meant. I had to change it to "old-fashioned." I once used *ineffable* in Hathaway copy, only to discover that I didn't know what it meant myself. Now I keep a dictionary beside my telephone.

What sells: facts, testimonials, specifics, helpful information. Testimonials from fellow consumers beat the puffery of anonymous copywriters; testimonials from experts — ex-burglars testifying they couldn't crack a Chubb safe — are even stronger. Helpful advice ("How to remove stains") pulls 75 per cent more readers than copy about the product alone. — *Confessions* What doesn't sell: superlatives, generalizations, analogies ("just as plants require moisture, so too your skin"), brag-and-boast ("Our product is the best in the world"), fine writing, and unique literary style. Claude Hopkins said it plain: "fine writing is a distinct disadvantage. So is unique literary style. They take attention away from the subject." — quoted in *Confessions*

And never tell lies. "If you tell lies about your product you will be found out — either by the government, which will prosecute you, or by the consumer, who will punish you by not buying your product a second time." — *Confessions* "Never write an advertisement which you wouldn't want your own family to read. You wouldn't tell lies to your own wife. Don't tell them to mine." — *Confessions*

## How to Apply

1. **Open with a grabber.** The first paragraph decides whether the rest of the copy gets read. If you start with a mushy statement of the obvious — "Going on vacation is a pleasure to which everyone looks forward" — you have lost them. A Harvard professor used to open his lectures with "Cesare Borgia murdered his brother-in-law for the love of his sister, who was the mistress of their father — the Pope." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Copy that.

2. **Write to one person.** Second person singular. The reader is not a crowd. The reader is a woman at a dinner party who has asked you a specific question. Answer it.

3. **Pile on the facts.** Specifics beat generalities every time. Sixty miles an hour. 100,000 door-slammings. 123-item checklist. 607 words. The goal is not a word count but the compounded weight of particulars. Each fact should be one the reader could not have guessed.

4. **Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs.** Rudolph Flesch's *Art of Plain Talk* is still the manual. If a word would make a farmer in Iowa ask what it means, change it. If you don't know what a word means yourself — yes, it happens — look it up.

5. **Avoid analogies.** Gallup has demonstrated that two-stage arguments are widely misunderstood. If you write "just as plants require moisture, so too does your skin," the reader doesn't complete the equation. Be direct.

6. **Work in testimonials where you can.** Fellow consumers are more credible than copywriters. Experts are more credible still. If a celebrity testimonial is being considered, weigh it carefully: readers remember the celebrity and forget the product, and they assume the celebrity was bought, which is usually the case. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

7. **Include the price if you can.** People turn the page when the price is missing. When Ellerton Jette retired as head of Hathaway and became a picture dealer, he violated tradition by showing the prices of the paintings in his advertisements; it worked. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Mandatory for considered purchases.

8. **End with what you want the reader to do.** A coupon, a phone number, a store. In direct-response, close with the offer, the address, and the phone. The average business publication is read by three readers besides the subscriber; if the first cuts out the coupon, the others need the second address. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

9. **Sign the ad.** All copy, in my view, should be signed by the agency. It is seldom done in the United States; short-sighted. When agencies sign their ads, they produce better ones. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

10. **Do not write an ad you would not want your family to read.** This is not sentiment. This is the most reliable hedge against dishonesty and bad taste on the desk of a tired copywriter at five o'clock.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958). Body copy: 719 words.
**Application:** Seventeen numbered facts below the headline. Each fact is quiet, specific, and verifiable: every Rolls-Royce engine is run for seven hours at full throttle before installation; the finished car is inspected for 98 separate faults by a single engineer on a test track; the picnic tables, veneered in Circassian walnut, swing out from under the dash; the Rolls-Royce is guaranteed for three years. No adjectives. No "gracious living." One fascinating fact piled on another. "When I advertised Rolls-Royce, I gave the facts — no hot air, no adjectives, no gracious living." — Ogilvy (quoted in *Ogilvy on Advertising*)
**Result:** Sales rose 50 per cent year-over-year. The second ad in the campaign used 1,400 words. Demonstrates that long copy works in luxury categories — especially in luxury categories, where the reader is willing to earn the purchase with time and attention.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience…" (1957).
**Application:** Conversational body copy — a woman on the telephone from her bath, talking to her lover in the voice a real woman uses. The word *darling* was chosen because a psychologist tested hundreds of words for emotional reaction and it ranked highest. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The copy below makes the specific ingredient claim: DOVE IS ONE-QUARTER CLEANSING CREAM — IT CREAMS YOUR SKIN WHILE YOU WASH. The claim is a fact about the bar's formula, not an adjective.
**Result:** Dove became the number-one cleansing brand in the world. Every Dove advertisement since 1955 has repeated that promise. Demonstrates that "conversational voice" and "fact-dense" are not opposites: the voice is the delivery mechanism for the single hard claim the ad is built around.

**Ad / Situation:** Merrill Lynch, "What Everybody Ought to Know About This Stock and Bond Business" (1948). 6,540 words in *The New York Times*.
**Application:** Louis Engel's page. The longest single-page ad ever run. No coupon. The offer of a booklet was buried near the end.
**Result:** 10,000 replies. Cited in *Ogilvy on Advertising* as example number one in the "nine examples of long copy that succeeded" list. The clearest possible evidence that "nobody reads long copy" is a myth maintained by people who have never tested.

**Ad / Situation:** Shell, Platformate newspaper campaign (1960s). 800 words per ad.
**Application:** A series of ads explaining Shell's fuel additive (Platformate) with demonstration-and-number body copy. Not short, not pretty, not flourished — just explanations piled on demonstrations.
**Result:** 26 per cent of male readers read more than half of each ad, and Shell's share of market reversed a seven-year decline. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Demonstrates that long copy works even in categories — petrol — where nobody would have predicted the reader would stay on the page.

**Ad / Situation:** Zippo, "The amazing story of a Zippo that worked after being taken from the belly of a fish" (1961).
**Application:** Story-form body copy. The headline is the story; the copy is the details. A lighter is found inside a fish's belly; it still works. The guarantee ("If it ever fails to work, we'll fix it free") is delivered not as fine print but as a vivid instance.
**Result:** Every morning, old and battered Zippos arrived at the factory in the mail; they were returned the same day, repaired, at no charge. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Demonstrates that a guarantee in story form is more powerful than a guarantee in legal form, and that a single specific incident out-sells any amount of general claim.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Default to short copy because "nobody reads long copy."
**Why:** Direct-response split-runs have proved for fifty years that long copy outsells short when the product has facts to give. The short-copy default is a superstition of general advertisers who never have to meet a sales curve. If your product has nothing to say, write short. If it has things to say, write long. The length question is a consequence of the product, not a rule about the medium.

**Don't:** Pad the copy to hit a word count.
**Why:** Long copy sells because it compounds pertinent merchandise facts. Pad it with adjectives and atmosphere and you have not written long copy; you have written short copy spread over more paper. The reader can tell the difference in the first paragraph.

**Don't:** Use brag-and-boast superlatives ("the best in the world," "incomparable quality," "unmatched in its class").
**Why:** Gallup called this Brag and Boast. It convinces nobody. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Every other brand in every other category is saying the same thing at the same volume. Replace the superlative with a specific number, and the sentence starts to sell.

**Don't:** Write analogies.
**Why:** "Just as plants require moisture, so too your skin" — Gallup proved the reader does not complete the equation. Two-stage arguments are misunderstood. Be direct about the product.

**Don't:** Borrow literary style (belles-lettres, "The Penalty of Leadership," "Somewhere West of Laramie").
**Why:** The business community of forty years ago was impressed by such purple prose; I have always thought it absurd. "Fine writing is a distinct disadvantage. So is unique literary style. They take attention away from the subject." — *Confessions* The reader came to buy a thing, not to admire a sentence.

**Don't:** Leave out the price on a considered purchase.
**Why:** People turn the page. You can walk past a necklace in a jeweler's window because you are too shy to ask; the same reader walks past the ad. When Jette began showing prices in his art gallery ads, he sold more art. Make the ad easy to act on.

**Don't:** Tell lies, even small ones.
**Why:** "You wouldn't tell lies to your own wife. Don't tell them to mine." — *Confessions* The government will prosecute you or the consumer will punish you by not buying a second time. Both are terminal. Short-term gain, long-term grave.
