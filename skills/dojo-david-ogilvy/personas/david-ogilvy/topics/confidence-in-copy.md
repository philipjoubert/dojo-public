# Confidence in Copy — Facts, Not Adjectives

## When to Use
- Drafting or editing body copy for a considered purchase where the reader wants evidence, not enthusiasm.
- Reviewing a page that leans on superlatives — "best", "premium", "world-class", "industry-leading" — and wondering why the copy feels inert.
- Choosing between a specific number you can prove and a round claim you cannot. ("60 miles an hour" versus "high speed"; "$13,550" versus "expensive".)
- Writing the first ad for a luxury or expensive product where readers will not accept "just trust us" and will accept forty specific facts.

## Fails When
- **The product has no facts.** Chewing gum, cigarettes, mass-market beer — there is often nothing to say, and forcing specificity produces padding. Copy the product warrants, not the copy the rule demands.
- **The rule is applied as a veto on voice.** The Dove "darling" headline was chosen because a psychologist tested hundreds of words and *darling* scored highest for emotional reaction. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Evidence can select a warm word as readily as a factual one. The rule is against empty adjectives, not against feeling.
- **The writer is using "facts" to produce dullness.** Facts are not the goal; persuasion is. A copy block of seventeen verified specifics that nobody reads has not sold anything. Facts must be pertinent, arresting, and merchandisable.
- **The category regulates what can be claimed.** Pharmaceutical, financial, and safety categories have legal limits on specific claims. Respect them; do not fabricate a number to satisfy the rule.

## Core Concept

"When I advertised Rolls-Royce, I gave the facts — no hot air, no adjectives, no gracious living. In my first Rolls-Royce advertisement I used 719 words, piling one fascinating fact on another." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The craft is simple and unfashionable. A specific fact outsells a general adjective in every split-run that has ever been run. "Very few advertisements contain enough factual information to sell the product. There is a ludicrous tradition among copywriters that consumers aren't interested in facts. Nothing could be farther from the truth." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The reader is not a moron; she is your wife, and she wants all the information you can give her. She has been lied to by a thousand brag-and-boast ads and she has learned to discount every superlative on sight. When Gallup studied copy he gave a name to the style that convinces nobody: "Brag and Boast." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* "Our product is the best in the world" is Brag and Boast. "At sixty miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" is not. The second claim is observable; the first is just a copywriter's opinion.

The mechanics of confidence are three. First, the specific number in preference to the round claim. Sixty miles an hour, not "high speed". 719 words, not "long". $13,550, not "expensive". 607 words of factual copy, 100,000 door-slammings, 98 separate faults checked by a single engineer, seven hours of full-throttle running. When research told me the average shopper thought Sears Roebuck made a 37 per cent profit, I headlined an ad *Sears makes a profit of 5 per cent* — the specific number was more persuasive than saying "less than you might suppose." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Specificity is the mechanical signal that the writer has done the work and is willing to be checked.

Second, the observable consequence in preference to the bare claim. Rolls-Royce does not say "the quietest car in the world"; it says the loudest noise at 60 mph is the electric clock. The reader hears the silence before reading the next line. Hathaway does not say "a shirt for men of distinction"; it shows a man with an eye patch and the reader asks what's going on here. Zippo does not say "we stand behind our lighters"; it tells the story of a lighter found in the belly of a fish that still worked. Every morning old and battered Zippos arrived at the factory in the mail; they were returned the same day, repaired, at no charge. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A guarantee in story form is stronger than a guarantee in legal form.

Third, the voice of one human talking to another, not the voice of a corporation addressing a stadium. "Pretend you are writing each of them a letter on behalf of your client. One human being to another, second person singular." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Queen Victoria preferred Disraeli to Gladstone: Gladstone talked to her as if addressing a public meeting. Disraeli talked to her like a human being. Disraeli wins. And write the way people talk — short words, short sentences, short paragraphs. "Get on a bus. Go to Iowa. Stay on a farm for a week and talk to the farmer. Come back to New York by train and talk to your fellow passengers in the day-coach. If you still want to use the word, go ahead." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* I once used *obsolete* in a Dove headline; a majority of housewives did not know what it meant. I once used *ineffable* in Hathaway copy; I did not know what it meant myself. I keep a dictionary beside my telephone.

And — non-negotiable — never tell lies. "You wouldn't tell lies to your own wife. Don't tell them to mine." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The consumer punishes a lie by not buying a second time; the government punishes it by prosecution. Both are terminal. The facts-not-adjectives rule is partly a style discipline; it is mostly an honesty discipline. You cannot write brag-and-boast for a product that does not deserve it without, in effect, telling lies.

## How to Apply

1. **Replace every superlative with a number.** "The best in its class" becomes "tested faster than the other four leading brands in a third-party lab". If you cannot produce the number, the claim is brag-and-boast and should be cut.

2. **Name the observable consequence, not the abstract quality.** Do not say "smooth"; say "the cup rests on the dash without spilling at 60 miles an hour". Do not say "durable"; say "the door has been slammed 100,000 times in testing". Let the reader infer the adjective from the fact.

3. **Cite the source when you can.** "A Harvard professor used to open his lectures with…" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Attributed specifics read as evidence; unattributed claims read as hype.

4. **Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs.** Rudolph Flesch's *Art of Plain Talk* is still the manual. If a word would make a farmer in Iowa stop, change it. If the word is *ineffable* and you are not sure what it means, look it up.

5. **Write second person singular.** One reader, one letter. "The consumer is not a crowd." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Pretend you are talking to the woman on your right at a dinner party who has asked you whether this product is any good.

6. **Be direct. Avoid analogies.** Gallup demonstrated that two-stage arguments are widely misunderstood. "Just as plants require moisture, so too your skin" — the reader does not complete the equation. Be direct about the product.

7. **Pile facts until the word count runs out.** "The more facts you tell, the more you sell." — Dr. Charles Edwards, quoted in *Ogilvy on Advertising* Long copy outsells short copy in direct-response split-runs for fifty years; not because long is good, but because facts compound.

8. **Never tell a lie.** "If you tell lies about your product you will be found out — either by the government, which will prosecute you, or by the consumer, who will punish you by not buying a second time." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The dinner-table test is the backstop: would you be proud to read this to your own family?

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958).
**Application:** The archetype. The headline is not "the quietest car in the world" — that is brag-and-boast. The headline is a specific observable consequence at a specific speed. Body copy below — 719 words, seventeen numbered facts: every engine run seven hours at full throttle, 98 separate faults inspected, picnic tables veneered in Circassian walnut, guaranteed for three years. No adjectives. No "gracious living." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Sales rose 50 per cent year-on-year. Demonstrates that confidence in copy comes from specificity, not assertion. The reader does not need to be told the car is excellent; the facts tell her.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "$13,550" (1958).
**Application:** The price as the headline. No adjective, no qualifier. Put the number on the page. For a luxury audience the price is both a qualifying signal and a curiosity hook. Long copy follows, piling specifics underneath. — *example-index*
**Result:** Demonstrates that the specific number is persuasive even when the number is a price. Most advertisers bury the price; Rolls-Royce opened with it. The reader who is the wrong buyer moves on; the right buyer reads on.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "Teazles, entasis and other tremendous trifles make the Rolls-Royce a bargain at $15,655" (1961).
**Application:** "Doors are tested for 100,000 slammings." — *example-index* The tell: obscure technical vocabulary (teazles are brushes used to nap fabric; entasis is the architectural swelling of a column) signals that the writer has done the research and respects the reader enough not to dumb it down. Specificity as proof of care.
**Result:** Demonstrates that confidence in copy extends to the writer's willingness to use the exact technical word when the exact word is true.

**Ad / Situation:** Schweppes, the Commander Whitehead campaign (1953 onward).
**Application:** The real client, Commander Edward Whitehead, photographed as the symbol of his own product. The reader asks *who is this man?* and the answer is, the man who brings you Schweppes. Body copy specifies the curatorial facts — British-made, quinine water, a brand with a hundred years of pedigree. A confident voice because the voice is a real person with a real beard and a real company behind him.
**Result:** Eighteen-year campaign on a peppercorn budget. Schweppes established as the tonic of discrimination. Demonstrates that "facts, not adjectives" includes facts about the person speaking — a real named commander beats any anonymous copywriter's superlative.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience… I'm head over heels in Dove" (1957).
**Application:** The apparent exception that proves the rule. The headline is warm, not factual. The body below carries the hard claim: *DOVE IS ONE-QUARTER CLEANSING CREAM — IT CREAMS YOUR SKIN WHILE YOU WASH.* The word *darling* was chosen because a psychologist flashed hundreds of words on a screen with an electric gadget measuring emotional reaction, and *darling* came out top. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Dove grew into the world's number-one cleansing brand. Demonstrates that the rule is against empty adjectives, not against emotion. An emotional word chosen by test is still a fact-driven decision.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write "the best in the world", "premium quality", "unmatched in its class", "world-class", "industry-leading".
**Why:** Gallup named this Brag and Boast. It convinces nobody. Every other brand in every other category is saying the same thing at the same volume. Replace the superlative with a specific number, and the sentence starts to sell. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Use analogies.
**Why:** Gallup has shown two-stage arguments are widely misunderstood. "Just as plants require moisture, so too your skin" — the reader does not complete the equation. Be direct about the product. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Use pretentious jargon.
**Why:** "Our business is infested with idiots who try to impress by using pretentious jargon — *reconceptualize, demassification, attitudinally, judgmentally, suboptimize.* They are the hallmarks of a pretentious ass, and the consumer does not speak that language." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Write in the corporate "we".
**Why:** "We believe in service. We are committed to excellence. We strive to delight our customers." This is stadium-talk. Switch to second person singular and name a specific thing you do. The reader is alone when she reads the ad; write to her, not to the board.

**Don't:** Close with the superlative hoping the facts above will carry it.
**Why:** The superlative in the closing line undoes the trust the specifics earned. End on the action — the coupon, the address, the phone — not on the adjective the client's brand team put there to feel good.

**Don't:** Invent a number because the rule says to use numbers.
**Why:** "If you tell lies about your product you will be found out." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The specific-number rule is useless without the research that produces the number. Make the homework produce the specifics, not the deadline produce the invention.

**Don't:** Write a word you cannot define.
**Why:** I once used *ineffable* in Hathaway copy and had to confess to a reporter that I did not know what it meant. I now keep a dictionary beside my telephone. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* If you are not sure of the word, the reader certainly is not.
