# Research — Homework Before Headlines

## When to Use
- At the very beginning of every campaign, before a word of copy is written.
- Whenever the writer is tempted to rely on "instinct" in place of evidence.
- When a client or a creative director proposes to kill a campaign that tests are still saying sells.
- When choosing between two competing strategies — do not pick, test.

## Fails When
- **Research is used as a lamp post, not a lantern.** "We all have a tendency to use research as a drunkard uses a lamppost — for support, not for illumination." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* If the findings are already in your head and you are only looking for a number to cite, you are not doing research.
- **The sample is so small it tells you nothing.** Pre-tests on twelve housewives in a conference room are a ritual, not a reading. Save the money for a real split-run.
- **The right question was never asked.** A beautiful methodology around the wrong question produces confident wrongness.
- **The research is hoarded.** "It might be a good idea to declare a five-year moratorium on new research projects while we analyze the huge volume of discoveries that is gathering dust on the shelves." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* Research that never reaches the copywriter on the firing line is waste.

## Core Concept

"We prefer the discipline of knowledge to the anarchy of ignorance. We pursue knowledge the way a pig pursues truffles." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

I started my career with Dr. Gallup at Princeton. As far as I know, I am the only creative hot-shot who started out in research, and I have looked at the creative function through the objective eyes of a researcher ever since. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Research is not the enemy of creativity. It is the raw material. It is the stack of facts and consumer voices out of which the big idea assembles itself, usually at two in the morning in the bath. Without the stack there is no idea; there is only the slippery surface of irrelevant brilliance, which my brother Francis named and which every copywriter who skips the homework eventually goes skidding across.

The first homework is on the product itself. "You don't stand a tinker's chance of producing successful advertising unless you start by doing your homework. I have always found this extremely tedious, but there is no substitute for it." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Study it. Taste it. Take it apart. Find out how it is made, who buys it, how often, at what price, under what circumstances, and against what competitors. Find out what the competitors are saying and how long they have been saying it. Then and only then go to the consumer. Find out how she thinks about your kind of product, what language she uses when she talks about it, what attributes matter to her, and — this is the point of the whole exercise — what promise would be most likely to make her buy your brand.

Then test. "The most important word in the vocabulary of advertising is TEST. Test your premise. Test your media. Test your headlines and your illustrations. Test the size of your advertisements. Test your frequency. Test your level of expenditure. Test your commercials. Never stop testing, and your advertising will never stop improving." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Every promise you are considering can be put in front of consumers before it goes on a billboard. Every headline can be split-run in a newspaper before it is set in a magazine. Every commercial can be measured. The copywriter who refuses to test is asking his client to bet millions of dollars on his intuition, and when I asked an indifferent copywriter which books on advertising he had read, he told me he preferred to rely on his own intuition. I asked him whether, if his gall-bladder had to come out this evening, he would choose a surgeon who had read some books on anatomy, or one who relied on his intuition. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The Magic Lantern was the name I gave to the internal teaching show at the agency — the slides and rules that every copywriter, art director and television producer was herded into a conference room to see on their first day. "The rules I postulate do not represent my personal opinions; they are the quintessence of what I have learned from research." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Research becomes wisdom only when the agency puts it back into its own bloodstream. Otherwise every campaign begins at zero and every writer makes the same mistakes his predecessors have already disproved.

## How to Apply

1. **Study the product for as long as it takes.** When I got Rolls-Royce I spent three weeks reading about the car, and out of that reading came a single sentence in a motor magazine that at sixty miles an hour the loudest noise in the new car came from the electric clock. That sentence became the headline, and underneath it I piled 607 words of factual copy. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* For Mercedes I sent a team to Stuttgart for three weeks of interviews with engineers; the resulting long, factual ads took U.S. sales from 10,000 cars a year to 40,000. For Good Luck margarine, which I had assumed was made from coal, ten days of reading taught me otherwise, and the factual ad that emerged worked.

2. **Study the competition.** Look at every advertisement that has run for competing products over the past twenty years. — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy (the Calt letter)* This tells you what has been said, what has been tested, and what territory is still open.

3. **Go to the consumer.** Informal conversations with half a dozen housewives can sometimes help a copywriter more than formal surveys in which he does not participate. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The point is to hear the consumer's own words, her own worries, her own reasons. She is not a moron; she is your wife.

4. **Test the promise before you test the copy.** At Ogilvy, Benson & Mather we used five research techniques to find out which promise is the most powerful. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Run pairs of ads in the same position in the same issue of a newspaper with an offer of a sample buried in the copy. The promise that pulls more orders wins. For Dove, "Creams Your Skin While You Wash" pulled 63 per cent more orders than the next best promise, and it has been the fulcrum of every Dove advertisement ever since. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

5. **Split-run the headline.** Write twenty alternative headlines and never select the final one without the opinions of other people in the agency. Where the stakes justify it, put them on a split-run and let the coupons decide. — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy (the Calt letter)*

6. **Put winning ads on the air, and keep them there until the research says they have worn out.** Scores of good advertisements have been discarded before they lost their potency, largely because their sponsors got sick of seeing them. You are advertising to a moving parade, not a standing army. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Measure the selling effectiveness of the campaign at regular intervals and go on running it until the research — not a bored client — says stop.

7. **Codify what research teaches and teach it.** Keep a Magic Lantern. Write the findings down. Accumulate the factors the way Gallup accumulated readership scores, and Rudolph accumulated the attention factors, and the mail-order people accumulate what pulls orders. "For 35 years I have continued on the course charted by Gallup, collecting factors the way other men collect pictures and postage stamps." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958).
**Application:** Three weeks of reading everything about the car that had been published in the English motor press. The electric-clock line was not invented by me — it was a single sentence in a technical write-up that I noticed because I had done the homework. Underneath the headline went 719 words of facts, piled one on another: no adjectives, no "gracious living," only the specifics I had gathered. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Rolls-Royce sales rose 50% the year the ad broke. The campaign remains the most quoted example in the corpus of what research — slow, tedious, unglamorous reading — can unlock at the very moment of writing.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience…" (1957).
**Application:** Two research moves, stacked. First, the product's own formula. The client wanted to position Dove as a neutral detergent; consumer interviews killed that angle. A review of the ingredient list turned up that Dove contained one-quarter cleansing cream, and the research lab confirmed it creamed the skin while you washed. That fact became the promise, tested against others, and it pulled 63 per cent more orders than its nearest rival. Second, the execution. A psychologist flashed hundreds of words on a screen and used an electric gadget to measure emotional reactions. High marks went to *darling*. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* So *darling* went into the headline.
**Result:** Dove became the number-one cleansing brand in the world. Both of the big choices — the promise and the headline word — were decided by research, not meeting-room preference.

**Ad / Situation:** Shell, the Platformate campaign (late 1950s).
**Application:** "Same thing with Shell gasoline. A briefing from the client revealed something which came as a surprise to me; that gasoline has several ingredients, including Platformate, which increases mileage. The resulting campaign helped to reverse a seven-year decline in Shell's share-of-market." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Seven-year share decline reversed. One fact, unearthed in a client briefing because somebody troubled to ask, carried the whole campaign.

**Ad / Situation:** "The Guinness Guide to Oysters" (1950).
**Application:** The first ad I wrote as head of my own agency. The idea was to borrow interest for Guinness from the fascinating foods you drink it with. I immersed myself in a Yale biologist's book on shellfish and built the ad around nine varieties of oyster, each with a fascinating caption.
**Result:** Readers wrote in for framable copies. The format spawned a series on game birds and cheeses. The big idea came from research on the adjacent subject; no research, no Guide.

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway, "The man in the Hathaway shirt" (1951).
**Application:** Harold Rudolph, in *Attention and Interest Factors in Advertising* (1947), had shown that photographs with an element of "story appeal" draw attention far above average. This led me to put an eye-patch on the model in my advertisements for Hathaway shirts. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* I tested eighteen story-appeal devices before choosing the patch.
**Result:** The campaign ran for two decades. The lesson is that the single most iconic image in the Ogilvy corpus traces directly to a research finding published four years earlier.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write the ad before you have read the research.
**Why:** You will produce something that sounds brilliant and says nothing. "A blind pig can sometimes find truffles, but it helps to know that they are found in oak forests." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Accept "we already know our consumer" as a reason to skip interviews.
**Why:** What the client knows is what she said at the last focus group about the last campaign. You need her language about this product, in her own words, today. The phrase that becomes the headline will be a phrase the copywriter has heard, not invented.

**Don't:** Pre-test ads on tiny samples and then never test anything in-market.
**Why:** You are pretending to be scientific where it is cheap and taking pure guesses where the money is. The important test is the split-run in a real magazine with real readers.

**Don't:** Kill a campaign because the agency is bored of it.
**Why:** "You aren't advertising to a standing army; you are advertising to a moving parade." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The consumer is not bored. Only the client and the account team are, and neither of them is the buyer.

**Don't:** Let the researcher become a permanent bottleneck.
**Why:** "They are so infernally *slow*, and their reports are so long." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* A Research Director should be able to use research fast and cheaply. Honest, quick, imperfect research that reaches the writer beats a perfect report that arrives after the ad has run.

**Don't:** Mistake pre-testing a commercial for understanding the product.
**Why:** Pre-tests can tell you whether your ad is communicating; they cannot tell you whether you chose the right thing to say. Positioning and promise are chosen before the copy is written, and they are chosen by research on the product, the category, and the consumer — not on the ad.

**Don't:** Let creative people refuse to read the research.
**Why:** "Creative types also bug me. They are terrified of research — terrified that it will reveal that their genius is not infallible." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* A copywriter who refuses to learn the plus and minus factors is asking his client to pay for his ignorance. You cannot run a surgery on intuition; you cannot run an agency on it either.
