# TV commercials — start selling in the first frame

## When to Use
- You are briefing or judging a :30, :60, or :120 that has to move the sales curve.
- The advertiser is tempted by an "attention-getting" open that has nothing to do with the product.
- An agency reel is being built from commercials that won awards but produced no sales.
- A client wants a jingle or a celebrity before the demonstration has been written.

## Fails When
- The job is a pure brand film with no selling objective (Ogilvy had nothing to say about that genre).
- The product cannot be shown on camera at all (rare, but it happens in financial and some B2B categories — fall back to the radio principles).
- Local regulations forbid the demonstration you would otherwise do (name-your-competitor commercials in Germany; product-in-commercial rules for some categories).
- The brand has already built a successful mnemonic over many years and the right move is to protect it, not to open with a harder sell.

## Core Concept

The purpose of a commercial is not to entertain the viewer, but to sell her. Every frame is real estate you have already paid for. You have got the prospect's attention at the beginning — you do not need to *grab* it with a musical overture or a helicopter shot of the sea. "In television advertising you have exactly fifty-eight seconds to make your sale, and your client is paying $500 a second. Don't mess about with irrelevant lead-ins. Start selling in your first frame, and never stop selling until the last." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*.

What sells on television has been measured, over and over, by the same short list of techniques. They are above average in changing brand preference: *humor* (when the joke is about the product, and only if the writer can actually be funny); *slice of life*; *testimonials from real users* filmed candidly; *demonstrations*; *problem-solution*; *talking heads*; *characters* used consistently over years (Titus Moody for Pepperidge Farm, Cora for Maxwell House, Commander Whitehead for Schweppes); *reason why*; *news*; and *emotion* — provided you can actually deliver it, and provided there is a rational excuse tucked inside. Three techniques are below average and deserve skepticism: *celebrity testimonials* (viewers remember the celebrity and forget the product — "the vampire effect"); *cartoons* aimed at grown-ups; and *musical vignettes* with parades of fleeting impressions. "Viewers have a way of remembering the celebrity while forgetting the product. I did not know this when I paid Eleanor Roosevelt $35,000 to make a commercial for margarine… Not one of my proudest memories." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.

Length is a weapon. In measured tests, :60 and :120 commercials pull better than :30s, and :30s better than :15s — more facts, more sell, more time to demonstrate. Direct-response advertisers, who know to the penny whether a commercial pays out, "have almost never seen a commercial shorter than two minutes produce profitable sales." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. General-TV conventional wisdom — buy :30s in prime time, change the commercial every season — is precisely the opposite of what sells. Show the product in the first ten seconds. Name the brand often enough that you irritate the agency. End on the package. Pick one mnemonic and run it for decades. The Pepperidge Farm horse-drawn wagon has been driving up that country lane for more than a quarter of a century; the Shell car drives through the paper barrier; Dove creams the skin while you wash. None of these were rotated out because somebody got bored. "A shocking percentage of viewers remember your commercial, but forget the name of your product. All too often they attribute your commercial to a competing brand." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.

The other enemy is expense masquerading as craft. Television programs cost about four dollars a second to produce, but commercials cost two thousand dollars a second. That money is nearly always being spent in the wrong places — overseas locations, original music, expensive celebrities, animation layered onto live action, scenery the art director wants and the viewer will never notice. "I have no research to prove it, but I suspect that there is a negative correlation between the money spent on producing commercials and their power to sell products." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The easiest way to cut the bill is to cut actors from the storyboard. The easiest way to cut the bill *without* cutting the sell is to open with the fire.

## How to Apply

1. Write the strategy down in one sentence before you write anything visual. One or two selling points, not a hodgepodge. "Commercials should never be created in committee. Compromise has no place in advertising. Whatever you do, go the whole hog." — *Confessions*.
2. Show the product in the first ten seconds. Identify the brand early. If it is a new product, teach people its name on television — twenty repetitions in 340 seconds has not been known to irritate anyone.
3. Open with the fire. If you are advertising a fire-extinguisher, show a fire, not a sunset. "People screen out a lot of commercials because they open with something *dull. You* know that great things are about to happen, but the viewer doesn't. She will *never* know; she has gone to the bathroom." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
4. Pick one of the techniques that has been proved to move preference: demonstration, problem-solution, candid testimonial, character, talking head, news, slice of life, reason-why, emotion-with-a-rational-excuse, humor-about-the-product. Reject the three below-average forms unless you have specific reason not to.
5. Make the product the hero. Use close-ups. Show the package. Show food in motion. Show the end-result of using the product. "It is fatal to warn her that she is about to hear 'a friendly word from our sponsor.'" — *Confessions*.
6. Run your commercial with the sound off. If it does not sell without sound, it is useless. Words explain what the pictures are showing; pictures do not illustrate what the words are saying.
7. If there is a super, make the words on screen exactly the same as the words spoken. Any divergence confuses the viewer.
8. Set a reasonable word rate — Ogilvy's number was ninety words a minute. A torrent of words is logorrhea, not selling.
9. Repeat the selling promise at least twice. End by showing the package.
10. Pre-test for *change in brand preference*, not recall. "Some kinds of television commercials which get high recall scores get low scores on changing brand preference, and there appears to be no correlation between recall and purchasing." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
11. If the commercial is direct-response, write at least two minutes. Set up a problem, demonstrate the solution, promise several benefits, give a money-back guarantee, include the price, and ask for the order explicitly and urgently. Buy the time slots the orders come from (late evening, weekends, January through March), not the slots the cost-per-thousand tables recommend.
12. Once you have a commercial that pulls, do not change it because the creative department is sick of it. Change it only when tracking studies show the promise has worn out.

## Examples

**Situation / Ad:** Pepperidge Farm — Titus Moody driving the horse-drawn wagon up the country lane.
**Application:** A single character-and-wagon mnemonic dreamed up at two in the morning to give the commercial "a touch of singularity, a visual burr that will stick in the viewer's mind." Titus Moody extolled the quality of Pepperidge Farm bread for twenty-six years — the same mnemonic, tuned but never abandoned. "Today, 27 years later, that horse-drawn van is still driving up that lane in Pepperidge commercials." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
**Result:** Demonstrates that a strong character, run long enough, becomes the brand. The discipline is *not* reinventing the spot every year because the agency is bored of it.

**Situation / Ad:** Ogilvy & Mather Paris — Super Glue-3 demonstration.
**Application:** To demonstrate the strength of the glue, the Paris office applied it to the soles of the announcer's shoes and hung him upside down from the ceiling. He delivered the sales pitch from that position.
**Result:** The product became brand leader. Also won the Cannes Grand Prix — a rare case where the award and the cash register agreed, because the stunt *was* the demonstration. The lesson is not to do stunts; the lesson is that demonstrations do not have to be dull, and that the sell and the entertainment can be the same shot.

**Situation / Ad:** Shell — the candid-camera testimonial with Mrs. Longo at the forecourt.
**Application:** Announcer plants an actor dressed as a Shell dealer who pretends to fault the product. A real Shell customer, Mrs. Longo, defends Super Shell unprompted — with far more conviction than a scripted endorsement. "When you pick loyal users to testify, avoid those who would give such polished performances that viewers would think they were professional actors. The more amateurish the performance, the more credible." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
**Result:** Demonstrates the candid-testimonial technique: the loyal user under attack is the most persuasive witness television can produce.

**Situation / Ad:** Maxwell House — the sound of coffee percolating.
**Application:** A commercial built around a single sound effect — coffee percolating — around which the copy was arranged. "A commercial for Maxwell House was constructed around the sound of coffee percolating. It worked well enough to run for five years." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Music as background does no measurable harm and no measurable good; *sound effects* relevant to the product do.
**Result:** Demonstrates that sound is a tool for selling, not for mood. The test is whether the sound is functional — the percolator, the sizzle in the pan, the crunch of the cornflake.

**Situation / Ad:** Fabergé / Eleanor Roosevelt for margarine / the wall of celebrity spokesmen.
**Application:** Fabergé paid Farrah Fawcett an estimated two million dollars over three years; Bob Hope, Gregory Peck, Candice Bergen, and Dean Martin charged about a million each. Ogilvy himself paid Eleanor Roosevelt $35,000 for a single margarine spot.
**Result:** "Viewers have a way of remembering the celebrity while forgetting the product." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The vampire effect is not a folk belief; it is a measured finding from factor analyses commissioned at regular intervals from Mapes & Ross. Do not hire the vampire unless the tracking study says so.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with scenery, a musical overture, or an "attention-getter" that has nothing to do with the product.
**Why:** You already have the prospect's attention at the beginning. The overture is where she leaves the room. The first frame is where you start selling, not where you warm up.

**Don't:** Write a jingle and call it a commercial.
**Why:** Jingles are below average in changing brand preference. If you went into a Sears store and asked the salesman to show you a refrigerator, how would you react if he started singing at you? Never air a jingle without first testing whether people who haven't read the script can decipher the words; most can't.

**Don't:** Hire a celebrity who is more famous than the product.
**Why:** Vampire effect. The viewer remembers Farrah Fawcett and forgets Fabergé. If you must use a known face, it should be a *character* who belongs to the product (Titus Moody, Commander Whitehead, John Houseman for Smith Barney), not a star being rented by the month.

**Don't:** Rely on voice-over when you could have the actor on camera.
**Why:** Research shows it is harder to hold an audience with voice-over. In identical commercials tested head-to-head, the voice-on-camera version sold more of the product.

**Don't:** Change the commercial every season because the creative department is bored of it.
**Why:** Wear-out is a measurement, not a feeling. Tracking studies tell you when attitudes stop improving. Until they do, the commercial that pulls keeps pulling. Shell ran the mileage theme for five years until tracking flattened, then switched to user testimonials — and the upward trend resumed.

**Don't:** Spend production money on Bali, original music, expensive animation, and overdressed extras.
**Why:** Every actor you cut saves between $350 and $10,000. Ogilvy's partner Al Eicoff was once asked by a client to remake a $15,000 commercial for $100,000. Sales went down.

**Don't:** Use a :15 "teaser" spot to set up a later :30. And don't run a :30 when a :60 would let you demonstrate.
**Why:** The direct-response fraternity, who measure every dollar, almost never see a commercial shorter than two minutes produce profitable sales. More facts, more sell. The conventional wisdom that favors :30s in prime time is the wisdom of people who do not count orders.

**Don't:** Test for recall when what you care about is sales.
**Why:** There is no correlation between recall and purchasing. Factor-analyze for change in brand preference; the Mapes & Ross method is what actually tracks the cash register.

**Don't:** Let the commercial be designed for the award reel first and the cash register second.
**Why:** "Of 81 television classics picked by the Clio festival in previous years, 36 of the agencies involved had either lost the account or gone out of business." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Cannes Lions are not sales. If a commercial cannot survive the question "did it move brand preference?" it is a showreel piece, not a commercial.

**Don't:** Open on celebrity eating scenery while a voice-over insists the product is delicious.
**Why:** Three below-average techniques stacked on top of each other. Open with the demonstration, show the food in motion, let the sound effect — the sizzle, the crunch, the pour — do the selling the words would fumble.

**Don't:** Miscomprehend yourself into obscurity.
**Why:** Professor Jacoby's Purdue study found that all 25 typical television commercials tested were miscomprehended by between 19 and 40 per cent of viewers. "If you want to avoid your television commercials being misunderstood, you had better make them *crystal clear.*" — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Clarity is not a stylistic preference. It is the baseline.
