# Secrets — the Contrarian Question

## When to Use
- Running the contrarian question: "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
- Evaluating whether a company has genuine insight or just a plausible-sounding pitch.
- Coaching a founder through articulating their specific thesis.
- Assessing whether a market is open for a new entrant or already saturated with consensus.
- Diagnosing why a pitch feels generic.

## Fails When
- A founder has a clearly articulated secret and is asking about execution — different topic.
- The question is about a commodity business where the "secret" is operational rather than strategic — fine, but the secrets framing doesn't add much.
- A founder has used the contrarian question as a trick to produce an edgy-sounding answer without substance. Watch for this failure mode.

## Core Concept

"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

This is not a parlor game. It is the most important question in business. Every correct answer is a secret. Every great company is built on one.

A secret is something true and important that most people don't believe or haven't noticed. It sits in a precise location: harder to find than a convention, easier to find than a mystery. Most people act as though this middle category is empty — as though everything worth discovering has already been discovered. They are wrong, and their error is your opportunity.

### The Three Categories of Knowledge

All knowledge falls into one of three categories.

**Conventions** are easy and widely known. The earth orbits the sun. Supply and demand govern prices. Code runs on servers. Conventions are true but confer no advantage — everyone knows them. They are the table stakes of any field.

**Mysteries** are fundamentally unknowable, at least with current tools. Is the universe infinite? What does consciousness consist of? These questions may have answers, but pursuing them will not give you an edge in business or in life. They are beyond the reach of any individual.

**Secrets** occupy the space between. They are hard but discoverable. They require effort, contrarian thinking, and courage. They are what remains when you clear away what everyone already knows and what no one can know. And they are the source of every great business ever built.

The problem is that most people have collapsed the middle category. They act as if the world contains only conventions and mysteries — as if the search space of undiscovered truths has been fully explored. This is the intellectual equivalent of declaring the frontier closed. Four social trends push in this direction: incrementalism, which trains us to advance in small prescribed steps; risk aversion, which makes the social cost of being lonely-and-wrong unbearable; complacency among elites who have it good enough that the urgency to discover has faded; and conventionality, which rewards giving the "right" answer on tests rather than discovering true ones. Together, these forces produce a civilization that believes it has nothing left to find.

### Two Types of Secrets

Not all secrets are alike. There are secrets about nature and secrets about people.

**Secrets about nature** concern what the physical world allows that we have not yet realized. What new materials are possible? What biological mechanisms remain unexploited? What can computation do that we have not tried? These are discovered by studying the world directly — through science, engineering, experimentation.

**Secrets about people** concern what humans want but will not admit, what they believe but have not articulated, what they hide from themselves. These are often more accessible than secrets about nature, because you do not need a laboratory. You need observation and honesty. You need to ask: What are people not allowed to talk about? What is forbidden or taboo? What do people think but won't say out loud?

The best businesses are usually built on secrets about people.

Airbnb was built on a secret about people: that ordinary homeowners would rent their living spaces to strangers, and that strangers would accept this. Before Airbnb proved it, most people found the idea absurd. But the secret was hiding in plain sight — people had been hosting guests for thousands of years. The only thing that changed was the medium.

Uber was similar: people would get into cars driven by strangers summoned through a phone. The taxi industry had operated for a century on the assumption that regulation and medallions were necessary to make this work. Uber's secret was that they weren't.

PayPal was built on the secret that email could replace checks for small transactions. The digital-cash experts had spent a decade publishing papers about why online payments couldn't work without perfect cryptographic security. We compromised on security in favor of convenience and built the largest payment network on the internet. The experts who had studied the problem longest were the least able to solve it, because they couldn't accept imperfect solutions.

In each case, the secret was not about exotic technology. It was about something people wanted but hadn't been given permission to want.

### What Bad Answers Look Like

Most people's answers to the contrarian question are not actually contrarian. They are conventional beliefs in a thin disguise:

- "Our education system is broken." Everyone already agrees.
- "There is no God." Courageous in some rooms; not an actionable business insight.
- "America is exceptional." Widely believed.
- "Failure is overrated." Now a convention in startup culture.

A good answer takes the form: "Most people believe X, but the truth is the opposite of X." The truth must be specific. It must be uncomfortable — something you can feel yourself hesitating to say. It must have an implication for action. And you have to be able to defend it with evidence, not just assert it. "Monopoly is the condition of every successful business" is a good answer because it contradicts consensus (everyone is taught that competition is good), has evidence (airline-industry returns versus Google), and has an implication (build monopolies, not competitive businesses).

The quality of a founder's answer tells you whether they have done the work. A founder with no contrarian answer has no secret. A founder with no secret has no basis for a monopoly. The chain is direct.

### Where to Look

The best place to look for secrets is where no one else is looking. This sounds tautological but has sharp practical implications.

Fields heavily scrutinized by smart people are unlikely to yield new secrets. Fields ignored, dismissed, or considered unfashionable are rich with undiscovered truths. The cleantech bubble produced a hundred companies with no secrets because every smart person in Silicon Valley was staring at the same space. Facebook was hiding in a Harvard dorm room because venture capitalists weren't in college.

Ask: what fields matter but haven't been standardized and institutionalized? Nutrition, for example, matters for everyone, but you can't major in it at Harvard. Most top scientists go into other fields. Most of the big studies were done 30 or 40 years ago and are seriously flawed. We know more about the physics of faraway stars than we know about human nutrition. Exactly the kind of field that could yield secrets.

Ask: what are people not allowed to say? The monopoly secret is an example — monopolists downplay their monopoly to avoid regulation; competitive firms exaggerate their uniqueness to avoid looking like commodities. What are founders not allowed to admit? What are investors pretending not to see? The taboo is often where the truth lives.

### The HP Cautionary Tale

What happens when a company stops searching for secrets? Hewlett-Packard provides the answer. By 1990, HP was worth $9 billion. Through a decade of invention — DeskJet color printer, OmniBook superportable laptop, OfficeJet all-in-one printer — HP was worth $135 billion by mid-2000. Then starting in late 1999, around a new branding campaign to "invent," HP stopped inventing. It launched HP Services, a consulting shop. It merged with Compaq. Its board split into factions: Tom Perkins, an engineer, wanted to continue searching for new technology. Patricia Dunn, a banker, wanted to focus on governance and watching the stock price. The banker's faction won. With the engineering vision gone, the company had nothing to do except manage what already existed. When there are no secrets driving a company forward, politics rushes in to fill the vacuum. HP's board became consumed by gossip about who leaked information to the press, who was allied with whom, who should be fired. Market cap fell from $135B to $23B. The company didn't collapse because of competition. It collapsed because it stopped believing secrets were worth finding.

This is not a unique story. It is the default trajectory of any organization that declares the frontier closed. When you stop searching for secrets, you stop creating value. When you stop creating value, you start fighting over the value that already exists — mimetic rivalry applied to corporate strategy.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask the contrarian question, and wait.** When interviewing a founder or evaluating a company, ask the question directly. Sit with the silence. Good answers take time to form; bad ones arrive instantly because they are recycled convention. A founder who gives you an edgy-sounding one-liner immediately is almost certainly reciting something they have said before — test for original thought by following up.

2. **Test the answer for four criteria.** A good answer is *specific* (names a concrete truth, not a vague orientation), *uncomfortable* (you notice resistance to saying it in public), *arguable with evidence* (not just an assertion), and *has implications* (points to a concrete action). "The search market is not actually full" would be a bad answer in 1998; "link analysis ranks pages better than any other method, but the major search engines have not built for it" would be a good one — specific, arguable, and implying an action.

3. **Distinguish conventions from mysteries from secrets.** If an answer is widely agreed-upon, it's a convention. If it's fundamentally unknowable, it's a mystery. The middle zone — hard but discoverable — is where the real answers live. A lot of "contrarian" answers are really about mysteries ("we don't know the true nature of consciousness"), which makes them intellectually interesting but commercially useless.

4. **Prefer secrets about people over secrets about nature.** Natural secrets are accessible only to specialists and take years to verify. Human secrets are often hiding in plain sight — things people want but won't admit, things obvious in retrospect. Airbnb, Uber, and PayPal were all secrets-about-people companies. Ask: what do my customers (or target customers) quietly want that nobody is giving them?

5. **Look where no smart people are looking.** If every top-tier VC is in a space, the secrets there are already being exploited or bid up. If no one is in a space, either the space is empty — or everyone else is missing something. Unfashionable fields (nutrition, applied mycology, construction management, criminal rehabilitation) are often undervalued because the social signaling is poor, which is exactly why the secrets are still there.

6. **Watch for the taboo.** The thing the market is not supposed to say is often where the truth is. Ask yourself what claim about your industry would get you in trouble at a cocktail party. If that claim is also *true* and actionable, you may have found something.

7. **Share the secret with a small group — the company itself.** Once you find a secret, do not broadcast it and do not hide it. Share it with whoever needs to help you build. A great company is a conspiracy to change the world; the people who join it become fellow conspirators. The wrong moves: telling everyone (in which case imitators catch up quickly) and telling no one (in which case you can't build with a team).

## Examples

**Situation:** A first-time founder tells you his contrarian truth is "most education doesn't work and students would learn more from real-world projects." He wants to build a startup that replaces lecture-based instruction with project-based alternatives.

**Application:** This is a convention in disguise. Everyone in education-reform circles already believes this. It has been the pitch of every ed-tech company for fifteen years, and almost none have built durable businesses around it because the consensus is too broad and the competitive density is too high. The useful follow-up: "what do you believe about learning that almost everyone else in ed-tech disagrees with?" If he can produce a specific, defensible answer — perhaps about the structure of credentialing, or about what actually transfers from one context to another, or about how people acquire skill at a specific vertical like medicine or coding — there may be a secret. If not, the company is built on a convention and will fight inside the existing ed-tech competitive density.

**Result:** Forcing the follow-up usually surfaces either a real thesis or the honest realization that the founder has a passion but not a secret yet. Both are useful outcomes.

---

**Situation:** A founder has a clear contrarian thesis: "The taboo in B2B SaaS is that customer success teams are almost always net-negative — they retain unhappy customers who would otherwise churn toward products that actually work. If we let the bad-fit customers go, the company looks worse in the short term but builds a much stronger base. We intend to design our entire post-sale motion around honest disqualification rather than retention theater."

**Application:** This is a real secret — specific, uncomfortable, arguable, and with clear implications. It is a secret about people (what B2B buyers want versus what they are told they need) rather than a secret about nature. The test is whether the founder can build a company whose structure embodies the thesis — compensation, hiring, metrics — without regressing to conventional practice under commercial pressure. The second test is whether the thesis produces a measurable difference in outcomes. The third test is whether the founder has the courage to stay with the thesis when the board starts pushing for "standard SaaS benchmarks" that the thesis explicitly rejects.

**Result:** A thesis like this is worth funding if the founder has the discipline to keep it intact. Many such theses collapse not because they are wrong but because the founder lacks the courage to implement them under pressure.

---

**Situation:** An investor describes the crypto industry's slowdown and asks where to look for the next cycle of opportunities. They expect you to name a buzzword.

**Application:** The secrets framing inverts the question. Do not ask "what's the next hot sector." That is the mimetic question and it guarantees a crowded trade. Ask "what's true and important about technology right now that almost no one is building against?" The sectors where smart money is not looking — construction, regulated heavy industry, nutrition, applied biology, aging, even deeply unfashionable categories like farming — are where the undiscovered secrets live. Most investors will refuse to take these categories seriously because the social signaling is bad and the deal flow is poor, which is exactly why the returns, when they come, will be concentrated.

**Result:** The honest answer is "I don't know, but I know where to look — the places nobody wants to go." That is rarely the answer investors want, but it is the answer consistent with how the best returns have actually been made.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the contrarian question as an aesthetic challenge.
**Why:** Some founders treat the question as a cue to produce the most provocative-sounding statement they can. That misses the point — the question is about independent thought, not about being edgy. "Capitalism is evil" said to a venture capitalist is provocative but useless; "the customer-success function in B2B SaaS is structurally misaligned with customer interests" is useful.

**Don't:** Confuse "nobody else is doing this" with "there is a secret here."
**Why:** Sometimes nobody else is doing something because the thing is impossible, or because the market does not exist, or because the returns are terrible. The absence of competitors is a necessary but not sufficient condition. You also need a real reason why the thing is now possible and why you are the person to do it.

**Don't:** Broadcast your secret on social media because it sounds good.
**Why:** A great company is a conspiracy to change the world — the secret is shared with whoever needs to help you build, and no one else. Founders who tweet their contrarian theses before the company can execute on them give the idea away and often accelerate copycat competition. Silent execution beats loud thesis-statement.

**Don't:** Declare victory when you find a single contrarian belief.
**Why:** One secret is not enough. You need to keep looking. HP stopped looking around 1999 and spent the next decade dying. The discipline is to maintain the practice of searching — to keep asking what your company could do that no one else sees — even after you have built something on the first secret. The moment you stop looking, you start managing instead of creating.

**Don't:** Confuse being lonely-and-right with being lonely-and-wrong.
**Why:** Just because your view is unpopular does not make it correct. Contrarianism-as-identity is as mimetic as conformity-as-identity — you are still taking your cues from the crowd, just with the sign flipped. The discipline is to think about the object, test the thesis against evidence, and accept the conclusion regardless of whether it ends up with or against consensus.
