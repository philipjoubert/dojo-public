# Definite Optimism

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether a company or founder has a real multi-year plan or just a process.
- A founder describes their approach entirely in terms of methodology (lean, agile, AB testing) without a substantive vision.
- Someone is making a career decision by diversifying across options because they cannot commit.
- The pitch invokes "we'll figure it out as we go" as if that were a strategy.
- Reviewing a business plan that has detailed quarterly OKRs but no concrete ten-year picture.

## Fails When
- The domain is so early (pure research, moonshot biology) that honest planning beyond a few years is genuinely impossible.
- The founder has a definite plan and the useful critique is about whether the plan is correct, not whether there is one.
- The question is about when to remain flexible within an overall strategic envelope — flexibility inside a definite thesis is fine; it's flexibility as a substitute for a thesis that fails.

## Core Concept

How you think about the future determines everything you do in the present. Two axes organize the possibilities: whether you think the future will be better or worse (optimism versus pessimism), and whether you think you can plan for it or not (definite versus indefinite). These produce four quadrants, and each produces a different way of living.

**Definite Optimism — United States, 1950s and 1960s.** The future will be better, and we know how to make it better. We will build it. Engineers dominate. The country builds the Interstate Highway System, the Apollo program, the Transcontinental Railroad. Robert Moses reshapes New York. John Reber — a California schoolteacher with no credentials — proposed re-engineering the entire San Francisco Bay; newspaper editorial boards endorsed it, Congress held hearings, the Army Corps of Engineers built a 1.5-acre model to test it. The Reber Plan was probably insane, but the fact that it was taken seriously tells you what kind of era that was. People had specific dreams about the future and marshaled resources to realize them. The dominant mathematics was calculus — the mathematics of mastery over change.

**Indefinite Optimism — United States, 1982 through roughly the present.** The future will be better, but nobody knows how. Nobody has a plan. The dominant figures are financiers and lawyers — people who specialize in process, not substance. The dominant mathematics shifts from calculus to statistics — from mastering change to describing randomness. Instead of building specific things, people build portfolios. Instead of having visions, they have options. This is the quadrant the United States has been stuck in for four decades, and it is the central dysfunction of American economic life.

**Definite Pessimism — China today.** The future will not be as good as the present, at least not for a long time. But we know exactly what to do about it. China copies the developed world with meticulous precision. Its citizens save 30% of their income. The plan is specific: build a somewhat poorer version of what the West already has. The word "developed world" implicitly means "the part of the world where nothing new is going to happen." China is determined to reach that endpoint.

**Indefinite Pessimism — Europe and Japan.** The future will be worse, and nobody knows what to do. The response is insurance, bureaucracy, and hedging. The ECB prints nothing but improvisation; it might as well stamp "Kick the can down the road" on the euro. Education becomes not preparation for building but insurance against falling through the cracks.

Of these four, only definite optimism produces a future worth having. The other three are forms of drift, decline, or managed retreat.

### The Pathology of Indefinite Optimism

The United States has been stuck in the indefinite-optimism quadrant since about 1982. This is not a minor intellectual problem — it is the central dysfunction of American life. The most damning evidence is real interest rates trending toward zero and below. When interest rates go negative, the market is saying that people with capital have so few ideas about what to do with money that they will pay someone else to hold it for them. This is not a sign of a dynamic economy; it is a sign that nobody knows what to build.

Follow the money, and you find a closed loop: founders sell companies to acquirers, who pay with investor money, which came from wealth managers, which came from endowments diversifying across asset classes because nobody has conviction about any single investment. The returns flow back to the endowments, which allocate them again. At no point in this cycle does anyone build anything new. Money circulates; fees are skimmed at each intermediary; nothing gets built.

The United States savings rate, including government borrowing, has been around negative six percent. Americans are so optimistic about the future that they do not even save. They borrow against a future they assume will be better without having any specific plan for making it better.

Finance has become the dominant discipline because finance is process without substance. A financier does not need to know what the future looks like. He needs to know how to diversify, how to hedge, how to optimize risk-adjusted returns. These are sophisticated skills, but they are content-free. Portfolio theory is the mathematical formalization of intellectual bankruptcy: spread your money across many things because you have no idea which one will work.

### The Four Dot-Com Lessons, and Why They Are Wrong

After the dot-com crash, Silicon Valley drew four lessons:

1. Make incremental advances.
2. Stay lean and flexible.
3. Improve on the competition.
4. Focus on product, not sales.

These became dogma. They were taught in business schools, repeated in blog posts, encoded into an entire generation's operating system. The opposite principles are more correct:

1. It is better to risk boldness than triviality.
2. A bad plan is better than no plan.
3. Competitive markets destroy profits.
4. Sales matters just as much as product.

The dot-com lessons are not entirely wrong — there are contexts where leanness and incrementalism make sense. But taken as gospel they produce the exact pathology described above: indefinite companies run by founders who have no vision, no plan, and no conviction.

The lean-startup methodology is the most elaborate version of indefinite thinking in startup clothing. "Don't plan, experiment. Don't commit to a vision, iterate. Don't think you know the answer, test hypotheses." It sounds humble and scientific. In practice it produces companies that stand for nothing and build toward nothing. The search space of all businesses is much bigger than the search space of great businesses. Random iteration explores the vast landscape of mediocrity and settles on a local maximum — a slightly better part of an infinitely large slum.

Progress without planning is what we call evolution. Darwinian evolution works on a billion-year time scale. It is enormously powerful over geological time. But no individual business can afford to evolve. A business has ten years, maybe twenty. In that time frame, intelligent design works best.

### Definite Optimism as the Ideal

What does definite optimism look like in practice? It looks like Steve Jobs. The greatest thing Jobs designed was not the iPhone or the Mac — it was his business. Apple imagined and executed definite multi-year plans to create new products and distribute them effectively. Jobs had a product roadmap extending years into the future. He did not ship minimum viable products. He did not AB-test his way to the iPod. He had a specific vision of what each product should be, and he executed against that vision with relentless precision.

Forget "minimum viable products." The purpose of a plan is not to be validated by customer feedback. The purpose of a plan is to create something customers do not yet know they want. The most successful products are not discovered through iteration — they are designed through conviction.

The most characteristic thing of companies with a plan is that they do not sell. Mark Zuckerberg turned down Yahoo's billion-dollar offer in 2006 because he had specific products he wanted to build that Yahoo could not see or value. A company with no definite idea of the future cannot properly evaluate a company that has one.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask the 10-year question.** "Where will this company be in ten years, in specific terms?" If the answer is vague — "bigger," "expanded into adjacent markets," "the dominant platform" — there is no plan, only hope. A real plan has specific products, specific markets, a specific order in which they will be dominated, and a specific reason why this company is the one to do it. If the founder hedges, they are an indefinite optimist dressed up as a CEO.

2. **Refuse the process substitution.** When the answer to "what is your strategy" is "we iterate," "we're lean," "we pivot when the data says to" — the answer is non-responsive. Those are methodologies, not strategies. Push back until you get a substantive answer about what the company is trying to build, for whom, and why.

3. **Test for the definite-optimist founder archetype.** Zuckerberg turning down $1B because he had specific plans Yahoo could not value. Jobs with the multi-year product roadmaps. Musk with the reusable rockets leading to Mars. The pattern: the founder can describe the long-term vision in specific enough terms that an acquirer offering "the market price" cannot price what the founder actually values. If a founder would sell for a reasonable number, the number is probably too high because they have no plan worth more than the money.

4. **Apply definite optimism to your own career.** The power law tells you one career move will matter more than all others. Keeping options open is not a strategy; it is the absence of a strategy. Ask: what specifically am I trying to build? If the answer is "I don't know, I'm keeping options open," you are defaulting to the indefinite-pessimist move: insurance against an unknown future. That is an expensive move. The correct practice is to form a specific thesis about what matters and act on it — even knowing the thesis might be wrong. A wrong definite plan is still more productive than no plan at all.

5. **Concentrate, don't diversify.** Diversification is the financial expression of indefinite thinking. At Founders Fund, we focus on five to seven companies per fund, each of which we think could become a multibillion-dollar business based on its unique fundamentals. Portfolio theory spreads bets because the investor does not know which will work. Definite optimism forces you to choose — to name the specific company or career move or market that will define the outcome, and commit.

6. **Calibrate between extremes.** Extreme optimism says: the future will happen automatically, so I do not need to work. Extreme pessimism says: nothing I do will matter, so I do not need to work. Both converge on inaction. The productive position is the middle: the future is contingent on specific people making specific decisions. Hard but doable.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series A founder describes her approach as "we're lean, we AB-test our way to product-market fit, and we'll pivot when the data tells us to." She is eighteen months in, has tested four distinct product directions, and cannot currently articulate what her company is trying to be.

**Application:** The "lean" frame is doing the work of a plan here, and it is not a plan. Four pivots in eighteen months is not iteration — it is the search space of all businesses, explored randomly. The founder needs to do the harder work of forming a thesis: what is this company, specifically, trying to build? For whom? Based on what insight nobody else has? That is the definite-optimism move. It may produce a wrong thesis, which will need revision, but even a wrong thesis concentrates the search. Four more pivots with no thesis will produce a local maximum on a flat landscape — a slightly better part of an infinitely large slum.

**Result:** Founders who make this shift usually stop looking for "what resonates with the market" and start asking "what am I trying to build and why." The second question is harder but the only one that produces a company worth being in.

---

**Situation:** A 29-year-old engineer who has been coasting in a stable-but-unexciting corporate role is considering three moves: an MBA, a bootstrapped side project, or a junior VC role. His reasoning is "all three keep options open."

**Application:** "Keeps options open" is the indefinite-optimist tell. It is the application of portfolio theory to a life. The power law says one move will dwarf the others; keeping them all half-open guarantees none of them produces a power-law outcome. The definite question is: what specifically am I trying to build with the next five years of my life? If the answer is "a technology company in X space," the bootstrapped side project is likely the correct move, even with the highest risk, because it is the move that compounds toward the goal. If the answer is "I want to become an investor in early-stage technology," the junior VC role is correct. If the answer is "I don't actually have a thesis yet, and I need a year to form one," that is also a definite answer — the MBA may be a rational "think-time" choice, as long as he understands that is what he is buying.

**Result:** Most of the paralysis around career moves disappears once the person is forced to articulate a specific goal. The paralysis comes from refusing to pick the goal, because picking feels like losing the other options. The power law says losing options is exactly the point.

---

**Situation:** A VC partner argues the firm should move to a "wider but smaller checks" model, from 8 concentrated bets per fund to 40, on the logic that "we can't pick winners so we should spread risk."

**Application:** The argument is a formal endorsement of indefinite optimism at the firm level. The problem with the "we can't pick winners" premise is that in a power-law world, spreading forty small checks guarantees you will hit some of the returns, but also guarantees your fund's performance will converge toward the median VC outcome, which is slightly worse than the S&P. Concentration does not eliminate risk; it forces the firm to do the work of forming high-conviction views about specific companies. That work is harder. It is also the only work that produces the upper-tail returns the fund is theoretically in business to produce. The firm is choosing process (diversification) over substance (picking), which is the indefinite-optimism pathology exactly.

**Result:** Most firms that adopt "wider but smaller" as a strategy are making a quiet admission that they do not know how to pick and are optimizing for fee-generation rather than returns. The honest move is either to rebuild the picking discipline or to charge lower fees reflective of an index product.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat "flexibility" as a strategic virtue.
**Why:** Flexibility inside a definite thesis is fine; flexibility as a substitute for a thesis is fatal. A company that can "pivot into anything" is a company that stands for nothing. Flexibility in tactics, yes. Flexibility in strategic commitment, no.

**Don't:** Equate "process discipline" with "strategic rigor."
**Why:** Running an AB test is not strategy. Running a weekly sprint is not strategy. Having an OKR process is not strategy. Process is how you execute a strategy; process without a strategy is motion without direction. The test: if someone asked "what is your strategy," could you answer without describing any process? If not, you have process, not strategy.

**Don't:** Plan with only quarterly horizons.
**Why:** Quarterly planning is fine operationally but disastrous strategically. The value of a technology company comes from cash flows ten or twenty years in the future. Every quarterly decision should be evaluable against that ten-year horizon. If the company's longest-horizon plan is the next quarter's OKRs, the company will make optimal short-term trades that compound into long-term irrelevance.

**Don't:** Rationalize indefiniteness as "epistemic humility."
**Why:** Saying "we can't predict the future" sounds wise but is often intellectual cover for refusing to form a view. The humility is false: you *have* to make decisions under uncertainty, and refusing to commit is itself a decision (usually the worst one). A definite optimist is not claiming certainty; they are claiming a specific bet worth making under uncertainty. That is different from either arrogance or cowardice.

**Don't:** Confuse "optionality" with "opportunity."
**Why:** Keeping options open preserves the choice to decide later. But the decisions that matter have power-law payoffs, and the payoffs go to those who commit specifically and early. Optionality has a real cost — the cost of never concentrating. Ivy-League graduates are often the most miscalibrated here, because the educational system rewards optionality-accumulation up through graduation and then punishes it afterward in markets that pay only for committed specific bets.
