# Memoir and the Self

## When to Use

- A founder is writing the company's origin story for the website, a blog post, a fundraising deck, or a public launch.
- A founder is writing an anniversary essay — 5 years, 10 years, IPO, exit.
- A founder is writing a personal essay about why they started the company, why they left a previous one, or why they are leaving this one.
- A founder is doing a talk or keynote and the format is "my journey."
- A founder is writing about a difficult past — the previous startup that failed, a co-founder breakup, a wrongful-termination lawsuit, a hard year.
- A founder is processing a current event — shutdown, acquisition, ouster — and considering writing about it.
- A founder wants to write a book or long-form essay about their experience.

## Fails When

- **The wound is too fresh.** Writing about a failure or betrayal six weeks after it happened almost always produces material that should not be public. Wait. The same material a year out reads as wisdom; six weeks out it reads as venting.
- **The piece is settling scores.** "Let me tell you what really happened with my co-founder" is almost never a good reason to write. The reader registers the score-settling and discounts everything in the piece, including the parts that are true.
- **The founder mistakes "memoir voice" for self-indulgence.** Memoir is not the licence to ramble about yourself. It is a craft form with its own rules — selection, compression, voice — and ignoring them produces work no one wants to read.
- **The "memoir" is being used to dodge accountability.** A founder who shuts down a company and immediately writes a 5,000-word post about what they "learned" is sometimes processing in public when private processing is what's owed. Notice when the writing is for you and being charged to the reader.

## Core Concept

Founders writing about their own past — the company's origin, the previous failure, the difficult year, the moment everything changed — almost always make the same set of mistakes. They tell too much. They use the writing as therapy. They settle scores. They preach. They produce 4,000 words about a journey that should have been 800. They confuse "what happened" with "what is worth saying about what happened."

Memoir, done well, is not autobiography and not therapy. It is a craft form — closely related to journalism — with strict disciplines of selection, compression, and voice. *The strongest memoirs are those that preserve the unity of a remembered time and place.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder writing the origin story is, technically, doing memoir. The disciplines apply.

The first discipline is selection. *Don't rummage around in your past — or your family's past — to find episodes that you think are "important" enough to be worthy of including in your memoir. Look for small self-contained incidents that are still vivid in your memory. If you still remember them it's because they contain a universal truth that your readers will recognize from their own life. Think small.*

*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*

This is the move founders most resist. They want the founding story to include everything that happened. The frustration with the previous job that primed them; the cofounder conversation in the Toyota Camry; the first customer; the seed round; the pivot; the layoff; the recovery. None of these can be cut, the founder thinks, because all of them are "important." But importance is not the criterion. Vividness is. *The small stories that still stick in your memory have a resonance of their own. Trust them.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder's actual founding story is probably a single afternoon, a single conversation, a single specific moment when something tipped. That is the story. The rest is context, and most of it can be cut.

There is a deeper principle at work. *Remember: Your biggest stories will often have less to do with their subject than with their significance — not what you did in a certain situation, but how that situation affected you and shaped the person you became.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder's pivot from product A to product B is technically interesting but emotionally generic. The conversation with the early employee who said "I trusted you and I'm not sure I should have" is technically minor and emotionally definitive. The second is the memoir. The first is the press release.

The second discipline is voice. *Above all, there's the matter of voice. Not being a writer, my father never worried about finding his "style." He just wrote the way he talked, and now, when I read his sentences, I hear his personality and his humor, his idioms and his usages. Remember this when you write your own family history. Don't try to be a "writer." Be yourself and your readers will follow you anywhere. Try to commit an act of writing and your readers will jump overboard to get away. Your product is you. The crucial transaction in memoir and personal history is the transaction between you and your remembered experiences and emotions.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) For founders this is the warning against the tone they most often reach for in personal writing — the elevated, slightly Aaron-Sorkin register, full of cadenced sentences about destiny and risk. That register reads as performance. The plain register, where the founder tells the story the way they would tell it to a friend over dinner, reads as truth.

The third discipline is the relationship to other people who appear in the story. Founders often write about their past with insufficient consideration of the co-founders, employees, investors, and partners whose names are in the narrative. *What about the privacy of the people I write about? Should I leave out things that might offend or hurt my relatives? Don't worry about that problem in advance. Your first job is to get your story down as you remember it — now. Don't look over your shoulder to see what relatives are perched there. Say what you want to say, freely and honestly, and finish the job. Then take up the privacy issue.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) For founders the equivalent: write the honest first draft. Then make decisions about what is fair to publish. *If you have in mind a broader audience, you may want to show your relatives the pages in which they are mentioned. That's a basic courtesy; nobody wants to be surprised in print.*

The fourth discipline — and this is where most founder memoir fails — is intention. *We can write to affirm and to celebrate, or we can write to debunk and to destroy; the choice is ours. Destruction has long been a journalistic mode, rewarding the snoop and the hatchet man. Nobody can make us write what we don't want to write. We get to keep intention. Writing is related to character. If your values are sound, your writing will be sound. It all begins with intention.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Tyranny of the Final Product"*) The founder writing about a co-founder breakup or an investor who behaved badly has a choice. The piece can be a settling of scores or it can be an act of understanding. *Don't use your memoir to air old grievances and to settle old scores; get rid of that anger somewhere else. The memoirs that we do remember are the ones that were written with love and forgiveness. Although the childhoods they describe were painful, the writers are as hard on their younger selves as they are on their elders. We are not victims, they want us to know.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder essay that lands is the one in which the founder is at least as hard on themselves as on anyone else.

A specific point about therapy. The act of writing is therapeutic — it produces understanding, it processes grief, it helps a founder come to terms with what happened. But the therapy is for the writer, not the reader. *It can also be an act of healing for you. If you make an honest transaction with your own humanity and with the humanity of the people who crossed your life, no matter how much pain they caused you or you caused them, readers will connect with your journey.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder who publishes the still-bleeding draft is asking the reader to bear the cost of their healing. The founder who writes the draft, processes through it, and then publishes a much later version that has the benefit of distance gives the reader something usable.

There is one more move that is specific to founder writing: the post-failure essay. After a shutdown, an acquihire, an ouster, a public scandal, founders feel compelled to write the long post that explains what happened and what they learned. Most of these posts are bad. They are written too soon, they are mostly about the founder, and they substitute "what I learned" for what actually happened. The good ones are short, are written about something that happened to the company rather than about the writer's feelings, and are honest about responsibility. The form is closer to journalism than to confession.

Finally, on organisation: most founder memoir pieces are too long because the founder cannot decide what to leave out. The mechanical solution: *Go to your desk on Monday morning and write about some event that's still vivid in your memory. It doesn't have to be long — three pages, five pages — but it should have a beginning and an end. Put that episode in a folder and get on with your life. Tuesday's episode doesn't have to be related to Monday's episode. Take whatever memory comes calling.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) The founder who writes vignettes — small self-contained pieces — and then, after a month, decides which to combine into a single essay produces work that has the property of life. The founder who tries to write the whole anniversary essay in one Sunday afternoon produces a forced march.

## How to Apply

1. **Wait.** The wound that is six weeks old is not yet writeable. The wound that is six months old often is. Do not publish about a current loss until you can read the draft a week later and not flinch. If you can't, it isn't done.

2. **Pick one specific moment, not the whole arc.** The 10-year anniversary essay should not try to cover ten years. It should be about one Tuesday afternoon, one conversation, one specific small moment that captures more than its size. Founders consistently miss this — they assume bigger frame = bigger piece. The opposite is usually true.

3. **Write small vignettes first; combine later.** *Take whatever memory comes calling.* Write a series of 500-1,000-word episodes — the conversation in the car, the customer who saved you, the day everything almost broke, the hire who became a co-founder. Don't try to organise them. Write twelve. Spread them on the floor. The shape of the longer piece will appear.

4. **Be at least as hard on yourself as on anyone else.** This is the test. If the piece makes the founder look smart and the co-founder look bad, throw it out. The reader's trust is built by the founder's willingness to indict their own past judgment, mistakes, blind spots. Without this, the piece is propaganda.

5. **Refuse the heroic register.** "I had to choose between everything I had built and the principles I held dear" is the dialect of bad memoir. Real memoir voice is plain: "I was tired, and I made a decision I'd been avoiding for two months." The flat sentence is much more powerful than the Sorkinised one.

6. **Show people. Don't summarise them.** The co-founder, the investor, the early employee — when they appear, they should appear in their own words, in a specific moment, doing a specific thing. The summarised version of a person is a cardboard cutout. The reader doesn't believe them.

7. **Get permission for things that involve other people.** Show drafts to the people named. Not all of them — sometimes that's not feasible — but the ones who get most directly hit. Their objections are often correct. Sometimes they are wrong, and you publish anyway, but you do it knowing the cost. Don't be ambushed.

8. **Know your intention before you write.** Are you writing to understand? To celebrate? To indict? To process? To inform? To recruit? Be honest with yourself. The piece written to indict and labelled "exploration" is dishonest in its frame, and readers feel it. Choose the intention; commit to it.

9. **End where the work ends.** Founder memoir essays consistently overrun their natural ending. The piece is over when the specific incident has been told, the small lesson has been registered, and the reader has the image they will carry. Anything after that is the founder's reluctance to stop talking. Stop talking.

10. **Trust the small story to do the big work.** *I never felt that my memoir had to include all the important things that ever happened to me. Many of the chapters in my memoir are about small episodes that were not objectively "important" but that were important to me. Because they were important to me they also struck an emotional chord with readers, touching a universal truth that was important to them.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing Family History and Memoir"*) Apply directly: write about the toy. Write about the boxcar in North Africa. Write about the pokeweed. Don't write about "the journey."

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is writing the company's origin story for a new website. The current draft starts: "It all began in 2018 when, as a frustrated former [previous job], I realised that the way [industry] was structured fundamentally limited what teams could accomplish. After months of research and conversations with dozens of operators, my co-founder and I decided to build [Company]..."

**Application:** This is a press release pretending to be a memoir. Replace with a specific moment. "I was in a 2018 budget review at my previous job when my CFO told me, in front of fifteen other people, that the project I had spent six months on couldn't be greenlit because no one could verify the savings projections. He was right. The data lived in seven different systems and nobody had the energy to reconcile them. I left the meeting and started writing what would become this company on my phone in the parking lot." The reader now has an image, a specific person (the CFO), a specific moment, and a specific motivation. The press release version had none.

**Result:** The website's "About" page is shareable. Candidates cite it in their applications. Investors ask about the moment in the parking lot. The originating frustration is now legible because it is anchored to a specific incident rather than a generic complaint about the industry.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a 5-year anniversary essay. The current draft tries to summarise the company's history through five sections: founding, early traction, the pivot, scaling, and the current moment.

**Application:** Throw out the structural plan. Pick one moment from each year that the founder still remembers vividly. Year 1: the first customer's specific complaint. Year 2: the all-hands where you announced the pivot and one engineer asked the question that crystallised it. Year 3: the night before the Series A close. Year 4: the layoff. Year 5: the customer call last week that closed the loop on something started in year 1. Write the essay as five small vignettes, in order. Resist the urge to add a "summary" section.

**Result:** The essay reads as a piece of writing rather than a corporate retrospective. It is shared widely. Employees recognise themselves in the vignettes (most are present in at least one). Customers recognise the type of company they are part of. Length: 1,800 words instead of 5,000. The compression is the value.

**Situation:** A founder is writing about a co-founder breakup that happened 18 months ago. The current draft is 4,200 words and contains seven specific things the co-founder did that hurt the company.

**Application:** Cut to one moment that captures the breakup, told fairly. Let the co-founder be a specific person, not a villain. Let the founder's own mistakes be visible — the conversations they avoided, the warning signs they missed, the harshness in their own behaviour. *We are not victims, they want us to know.* The piece is half its current length and twice as honest.

**Result:** The piece is read with respect rather than pity. The co-founder, when they read it, can disagree with the framing without feeling betrayed. Other founders who have been through similar breakups recognise the dynamic and reach out. The four-paragraph version that was a hit-piece would have made the founder feel better for a week and damaged their reputation in the industry permanently.

**Situation:** A founder shuts the company down after seven years and wants to publish a "what I learned" essay within a week.

**Application:** Don't publish in a week. Wait a month, ideally three. Use the intervening time to write privately — to process, to talk to former employees, to make peace with what happened. The published essay should not be the first draft of grief. *I was struck by how often my students' gropings led to a sudden revelation of the proper path. The student had been given time to find it.* When the essay is finally written, it should be specific (one moment, one customer, one decision), structurally unornamented, and at least as hard on the founder as on circumstances.

**Result:** The essay, published three months out, is read by the people the founder wanted to reach — investors, future employers, future co-founders. It reads as a person who has thought, not a person who is venting. The week-one version would have been re-read in two years with embarrassment; the three-month version becomes the canonical account.

**Situation:** A founder is doing a keynote at an industry conference. The format is "my founder journey." They have 40 slides.

**Application:** Replace the slides with three specific stories. The week the company nearly died. The customer call that reframed the strategy. The hire who became indispensable. Each story is 6-8 minutes long, specific, with named people. The forty-slide version was a corporate timeline; the three-story version is a talk.

**Result:** The talk is the most-shared one of the conference. People come up to the founder afterwards quoting specific moments. The forty-slide version would have been forgotten by the next afternoon.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write the heroic origin story.
**Why:** The "I saw the problem and decided to dedicate my life to solving it" register is the dialect of bad founder writing. Real founding stories are more accidental, more specific, and more humbling. Reach for the small specific moment, not the destiny narrative. Readers know the difference.

**Don't:** Use the memoir to settle a score.
**Why:** The reader can always tell. The piece may be technically accurate; the energy is wrong. *Don't use your memoir to air old grievances and to settle old scores; get rid of that anger somewhere else.* The grievance can be in your journal. It should not be in the published essay.

**Don't:** Substitute "what I learned" for "what happened."
**Why:** Most founder essays end every section with a one-paragraph "lesson learned." The lessons are usually generic ("I learned that culture matters"; "I learned that hiring is everything"). Cut them. Let the story do its work. The reader can extract their own lessons; if the story is specific enough, theirs will be more interesting than yours.

**Don't:** Try to be definitive.
**Why:** The 10-year essay does not need to be the definitive account of the company. It needs to be one founder's specific reflection on a specific time. The temptation to comprehensive coverage is the same temptation that wrecks unity in any nonfiction. *Nobody can write a book or an article "about" something.*

**Don't:** Publish before testing on someone you trust.
**Why:** Founder memoir is a high-stakes form because what is published is permanent and personal. Show drafts to one or two trusted readers — ideally one who knows the events and one who doesn't — before publishing. The first will catch what is unfair; the second will catch what is incomprehensible without context.

**Don't:** Inflate the prose to match the gravity of the subject.
**Why:** The founder writing about a serious topic — failure, loss, betrayal, illness — sometimes reaches for elevated language because the subject "deserves it." The opposite is true. Plain language, in the gravest moments, hits harder. The eulogy that says "she was funny in a way that surprised people" is more powerful than the eulogy that says "her mirth illuminated all who encountered her."

**Don't:** Confuse the publication with the work.
**Why:** Some founder writing should be done and never published. The journal entry, the unpublished essay, the long letter to a former co-founder that you never send — these do real work for the writer. Not everything you write needs an audience. Some of the most important writing a founder does should remain in a folder marked "did not publish."

**Don't:** Write the "founder's curse / founder's burden" essay.
**Why:** The genre — "no one understands how hard it is to be a founder" — is well-mined and ages badly. If the impulse to write it strikes, write it, save it, and don't publish. The act of writing will yield value; the act of publishing will produce eye-rolls in the people whose respect you actually want.
