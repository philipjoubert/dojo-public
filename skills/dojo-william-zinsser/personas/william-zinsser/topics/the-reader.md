# The Reader

## When to Use

- A founder is writing a 12-page board memo for a board that has been clear they want three pages.
- A founder is writing a fundraising email and asks "how do I write this so it resonates with all kinds of investors?"
- A founder is being told repeatedly that their writing is unclear and is blaming the audience.
- A founder is writing a launch post and trying to address customers, prospects, employees, investors, and competitors all at once.
- A founder assumes investors / employees / customers will sit with a document for an hour. They will not.
- A founder is producing context-heavy preambles to ensure no reader is left behind, and the actual content has been buried.
- A founder is writing for "anyone in the industry" — a definitively unhelpful target.
- A founder has not visualised the specific person opening the email at a specific time on a specific device.

## Fails When

- **The document is a long-form essay where the reader is committing time on purpose.** A 5,000-word industry essay can earn its length. A board update cannot.
- **The user mistakes "imagine one reader" for "dumb the writing down."** Respect for the reader is not condescension to them.
- **The user uses "the reader can't follow this" as an excuse to over-simplify a complex argument.** Some arguments are genuinely complex; flattening them is its own failure.
- **The reader is genuinely captive.** Sometimes the audience must read the document — a 10-K, a board package, a regulatory disclosure. They will read it slowly because they have to. Don't over-optimise for skim.

## Core Concept

Founders consistently misjudge their readers. They imagine investors who will read the deck closely; investors are scanning on a phone in an Uber. They imagine employees who will read the all-hands email carefully; employees are reading it between meetings while half-paying-attention. They imagine customers who will work to understand a complex pricing change; customers will read the first paragraph and decide whether to be angry. They imagine board members who will absorb a 14-page memo overnight; the board member opens it on Sunday morning, gives it ten minutes, and forms an impression that lasts for the entire meeting.

*The reader is someone with an attention span of about 30 seconds — a person assailed by many forces competing for attention. The man or woman snoozing in a chair with a magazine or a book is a person who was being given too much unnecessary trouble by the writer.*

*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*

The reader's situation has only gotten worse since Zinsser wrote that sentence. The competing forces are now Slack and email and the phone in their hand. The reader is half-distracted at all times. Every sentence is fighting against the urge to switch back to the inbox. The writer who does not internalise this — who imagines a reader committed to giving them sustained attention — produces writing that the actual reader cannot use.

*It won't do to say that the reader is too dumb or too lazy to keep pace with the train of thought. If the reader is lost, it's usually because the writer hasn't been careful enough.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*) For a founder, this is the move that prevents the easy excuse. When the board says "we didn't follow your strategy memo," the temptation is to think "they didn't read it carefully." Sometimes that is true. Almost always it is also true that the memo could have been organised, opened, and compressed in a way that did not require the reader's careful work. The reader does not owe the writer effort. The writer owes the reader clarity.

The first move is to imagine a specific reader, not a generic audience. *Don't try to visualize the great mass audience. There is no such audience — every reader is a different person. Don't try to guess what sort of thing editors want to publish or what you think the country is in a mood to read. Editors and readers don't know what they want to read until they read it.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Audience"*) For a founder this means: when writing the all-hands email, picture one specific employee. When writing the investor letter, picture one specific investor. When writing the customer apology, picture one specific customer. The piece written to a specific person is almost always more useful to a group than the piece written to a group.

This sounds like solipsism but is the opposite. *You are writing for yourself. If it amuses you in the act of writing, put it in. (It can always be taken out, but only you can put it in.) You are writing primarily to please yourself, and if you go about it with enjoyment you will also entertain the readers who are worth writing for. If you lose the dullards back in the dust, you don't want them anyway.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Audience"*) The audience-of-everyone is the audience of no one. The piece that tries to serve the bullish investor and the bearish one and the contrarian one ends up serving none. The piece that has a specific reader in mind has a specific tone, a specific level of context, a specific implicit relationship — and other readers, encountering it, recognise it as a real piece of writing rather than a one-size-fits-none communication.

The second move is craft, not attitude. *I'm talking about two different issues. One is craft, the other is attitude. The first is a question of mastering a precise skill. The second is a question of how you use that skill to express your personality. In terms of craft, there's no excuse for losing readers through sloppy workmanship. If they doze off in the middle of your article because you have been careless about a technical detail, the fault is yours. But on the larger issue of whether the reader likes you, or likes what you are saying or how you are saying it, or agrees with it, or feels an affinity for your sense of humor or your vision of life, don't give him a moment's worry. You are who you are, he is who he is, and either you'll get along or you won't.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Audience"*) For founders this is liberating. You owe the reader a piece they can follow without unnecessary effort. You do not owe them agreement, approval, or the suppression of your specific positions. The work of clarity is the writer's; the work of agreement is the reader's.

The third move is to give the reader credit. Founder writing often errs in two opposite directions. The corporate-style document assumes the reader is an idiot and explains everything five times. The technically-aspirational document assumes the reader is an expert and skips the context that anyone outside the writer's head needs. Both are failures of imagination about the actual reader. The right move is in the middle: assume the reader is intelligent, busy, and motivated, but not an expert in your specific topic. Explain what they need to know. Don't explain what they already know. Don't dwell.

The fourth move is to consider the reader's body. Where will they read this? On their laptop in a meeting? On their phone in line at Starbucks? On a printed copy in a board meeting? Each setting changes what the document needs to do. The board memo that will be printed and read in a meeting can have more density than the all-hands email that will be skimmed on a phone walking to lunch. The investor letter that will be read on a Saturday morning with a coffee can be longer than the recruiting email that will be opened between two interviews. Founders rarely think about the physics of reading; they should.

The fifth move applies to the most common founder error: the over-explained document. Founders, anxious about losing the reader, pile on context. The board update has three paragraphs of "where we are as a company" before any specific information. The all-hands email has a "framing" section before the news. The customer email has a "I want to start by thanking you for your continued partnership" before the actual point. Each of these is the writer protecting themselves against the imagined accusation of being too abrupt. The actual reader, far from appreciating the warm-up, has already moved on. *Readers want to know — very soon — what's in it for them.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 11*)

There is one final discipline. The reader, properly respected, will return for more. The reader, exhausted by an over-long document or insulted by an over-simplified one or confused by an under-edited one, will not. Founder writing is not one document; it is a series across many years. Each board update sets the expectation for the next one. Each all-hands email teaches the team how to read your communications. The reader is not just the person on the other end of this email — they are the same person, three months from now, deciding whether to read your next email at all. The writer who burns reader attention pays for it forever.

## How to Apply

1. **Name a specific reader before you write.** Not "the board." Not "investors." A specific person. The board member you most want to convince. The investor whose firm you most want to lead the round. The employee whose retention you most worry about. Write to that person. Edit later if needed for wider distribution.

2. **Imagine the reader's situation.** Where are they when they open this? On a phone, on a laptop, in a meeting, in bed Sunday night? How much time do they have? Three minutes? Twenty? Match the document's length and density to the situation, not to your sense of how important the topic is.

3. **Front-load the news.** Whatever the reader most needs to know, put it first. The reader's attention is a gradient, sharpest at sentence one and dropping each paragraph. Don't bury the news in paragraph four hoping they get there. *Readers want to know — very soon — what's in it for them.*

4. **Cut the context paragraphs.** Most founder drafts have one or two paragraphs of "framing" before the actual content. The framing is for the writer, not the reader. The reader, having opened the document, is already framed. Get to the content.

5. **Don't apologise for the length.** The "I know this is long, but bear with me" preamble does not earn the reader's patience; it makes them resent the length more. If the document is the right length, no apology is needed; if it isn't, no apology will save it.

6. **Test by skim.** Read your own draft as if you were the busy reader receiving it for the first time. Do you get the news from the first paragraph? Do the section headings carry the argument if they're the only thing read? Does the document survive being skimmed? Founder documents almost always fail this test on the first try.

7. **Pick one reader, not five constituencies.** A piece that tries to serve customers, employees, investors, and the press at once serves none. If you genuinely have multiple audiences, write multiple documents. The press release for the launch is not the same document as the customer email is not the same document as the investor update.

8. **Respect the reader's time as a real cost.** A 14-page memo for a board that wants three is not "thoroughness"; it is the founder offloading the work of compression onto the reader. Compression is the writer's job. Do it.

9. **Don't lose readers through sloppy craft.** A sentence that can be read two ways. A pronoun whose antecedent is unclear. A transition that doesn't follow. These are craft failures. *If they doze off in the middle of your article because you have been careless about a technical detail, the fault is yours.*

10. **Assume intelligence; don't assume expertise.** The reader is smart but doesn't necessarily know your specific domain. Don't condescend. Don't assume. The right level of context is "what an intelligent person not steeped in your industry would need to follow this," and almost no founder calibrates correctly on the first try.

11. **Build reader trust across documents, not within one.** The reader who has trusted your last six communications will give the seventh more attention than they would have given the first. Don't blow the trust by ever sending a document that wastes their time. The compounding works in both directions.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is writing the quarterly board update. The current draft is 14 pages. The board has been polite but the founder suspects no one is reading them carefully.

**Application:** Find the actual reader. Most boards read the update on Sunday night for fifteen minutes. The 14-page document was always going to be skimmed. Cut to four pages. Lead with: the headline number, the variance to plan, the one thing that worked, the one thing that didn't, the one decision the founder needs from the board. Move everything else to appendices the board can read if curious.

**Result:** Board members read the four-page version closely. The Monday meeting is more substantive because the board arrives oriented. The 14-page version had been treated as a ritual; the four-page version is treated as a working document. The founder has not lost information; they have moved it to the right place.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the all-hands email about a major strategic shift. The draft is 1,800 words and includes context on the market, the competitive landscape, the financials, and the founder's reasoning, before stating the actual change.

**Application:** Imagine a specific employee — say, an engineer on the platform team — opening this on a phone between meetings. They have ninety seconds. They want to know: what is changing, when, and what does it mean for their job. Cut to 600 words. Open with the change. Add a paragraph on the reasoning. Add a paragraph on what this means for different parts of the company. End with the next steps. Move all the market analysis into a follow-up Notion doc that interested employees can read.

**Result:** The team registers the change immediately. The engineer who would have given up on the 1,800-word version reads the 600-word version twice. The follow-up questions are substantive. The market analysis, in a separate doc, gets read by the people who actually want it; the rest are not forced to skim it.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a launch announcement that will go to existing customers, prospects, the press, and internal employees, all at once. The draft tries to address all of them.

**Application:** Write four documents. The customer email speaks specifically to existing customers ("here's what's new and what stays the same for you"). The prospect-focused blog post speaks to people who don't yet use the product ("here's the problem this solves"). The press release speaks to journalists ("here's the news, the executives to quote, the spec sheet"). The internal email speaks to employees ("here's what we shipped and what we're proud of"). Each document is shorter than the original consolidated draft. Each lands harder.

**Result:** Customer churn does not spike (the customer email reassured them about continuity). New signups increase (the blog post spoke to their actual situation). Press coverage is more accurate (the release was self-explanatory). Employees feel proud (the internal email celebrated rather than informed). The all-purpose draft would have done all of these jobs poorly.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a fundraising memo. They are trying to write it so that "any kind of investor" will be receptive.

**Application:** "Any kind of investor" is not a real audience. Identify the specific funds and partners you most want as the lead. Write the memo to them — to a specific named partner whose previous investments you have studied, whose thesis you understand, whose questions you can predict. The memo will have a sharper position because it will have a specific reader. Other investors who pick it up will encounter a memo that knows what it is.

**Result:** The targeted memo gets passed up to the partner. The partnership meeting starts with informed questions. The "any kind of investor" memo would have been forgettable; the targeted version generates conviction. The fund leads the round.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a long-form blog post about a contested industry issue. They are nervous that the post will alienate readers who disagree, so they are writing in a hedged voice that takes no clear position.

**Application:** The hedged version pleases nobody. Identify the specific reader you most want to convince — the operator who is on the fence, not the partisan on either side. Write to them. Take a position. The reader who already agreed with you will find the piece sharpening; the reader who already disagreed will be more thoughtful in their disagreement; the on-the-fence reader, the one you actually wanted, will be moved.

**Result:** The post gets shared and quoted. The disagreement it generates is useful disagreement. The hedged version would have been read by no one; the clear version is read by everyone in the relevant sphere within a week. Most importantly, the founder learns what they actually think because the act of writing forced them to commit.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write to the imagined hostile reader.
**Why:** Many founder drafts are quietly defending themselves against an imagined critic. The defense is felt by the actual reader as evasion or hedging. Identify the imagined hostile reader. Then take them out of the room. Write for the reader you actually want.

**Don't:** Apologise for the document's length, complexity, or seriousness.
**Why:** "I know this is a lot" / "Sorry for the long email" / "Bear with me" — these are the writer asking permission to take time the reader has already given them. The apology makes the reader more frustrated, not less. Either the document earns its length or it doesn't.

**Don't:** Over-explain because you're worried someone might miss something.
**Why:** The "let me make sure everyone is on the same page before I get into this" preamble is the writer's anxiety, not the reader's need. Most readers will follow without the preamble. The few who won't are not your audience for this document.

**Don't:** Mistake "respect the reader" for "dumb it down."
**Why:** The reader is intelligent. Respect for them includes giving them ideas they have to actually think about. The condescending version of "respect" — short sentences, simple words, repeated context — is its own form of disrespect.

**Don't:** Lose the reader through small craft errors.
**Why:** A pronoun without a clear antecedent. A transition that doesn't follow. A sentence that can be read two ways. Each is a small breakdown that costs reader trust. The writer who eliminates these is not being pedantic; they are honouring the reader's time.

**Don't:** Send the same document to multiple audiences as a shortcut.
**Why:** The all-purpose document is a false economy. The minutes saved in not customising are paid back tenfold in reduced effectiveness. If the document matters, write the version each audience actually needs. If it doesn't matter enough to write multiple versions, it probably doesn't matter enough to send.

**Don't:** Underestimate the device.
**Why:** A document optimised for desktop reading is brutal on mobile. A document with too many bullet points is hard to follow on a phone. A document with elaborate formatting may render badly in email clients. Test how it will be read in the actual reading conditions.

**Don't:** Confuse "I made it short" with "I respected the reader."
**Why:** A short document that omits crucial information is not respectful — it is lazy. The discipline is to give the reader exactly what they need, in the right order, with the right context, in the smallest space that does the job. Short is sometimes a virtue and sometimes an evasion. Calibrate.

**Don't:** Treat reader patience as renewable.
**Why:** Readers do not give you fresh attention with each new document. They give you attention based on what they have learned to expect from your writing. If you have wasted their time before, they read your next document with reduced attention. The compounding is real and goes both ways. Treat each piece as a deposit or a withdrawal from a finite reader-trust account.
