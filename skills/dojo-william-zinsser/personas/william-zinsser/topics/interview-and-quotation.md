# Interview and Quotation

## When to Use

- A founder is doing customer discovery and needs to capture what is actually said, not what they expected to hear.
- A founder has stacks of customer-interview transcripts and the resulting strategy memo distils them all into "users want simplicity."
- A founder is writing a case study and has paraphrased the customer instead of letting them speak.
- A founder is interviewing executive candidates and needs to render what they said in writing for the board.
- A founder is writing a fundraising memo and has access to specific customer voices but is not using them.
- A founder is preparing to interview an advisor, mentor, or industry figure and wants the conversation to yield reusable material.
- A founder is publishing a blog post about an industry shift and has spoken to ten operators — the quotes are sitting in the founder's head, not on the page.

## Fails When

- **The conversation was private and quoting would breach trust.** Some material — co-founder disputes, board confidences, sensitive customer disclosures — must not be quoted, even if the words are perfect. Voice without trust is poison.
- **The user fills the piece with too many quotes.** A case study where every other paragraph is a verbatim quote is a transcript, not a piece of writing. Quotes are seasoning, not substance.
- **The quote is awkward enough to embarrass the speaker.** The discipline is to honour the speaker's voice, not to expose every disfluency. Unedited transcripts are usually unfair.
- **The piece is genuinely the writer's analysis.** Some pieces (a strategy memo, an opinion piece, an investor letter) are about what the writer thinks. Quotes can support; they should not dominate.

## Core Concept

Founders, by the nature of their job, are professional interviewers. They run customer discovery, prospect interviews, candidate interviews, advisor calls, peer-CEO conversations, board meetings, and reference checks. They generate enormous amounts of valuable spoken material. Almost none of it makes its way into their writing in usable form. The conversations happen, the founder takes a few notes or records the call, and then the material is paraphrased into "what we learned was..." prose — which is several orders of magnitude weaker than the actual words.

*Get people talking. Learn to ask questions that will elicit answers about what is most interesting or vivid in their lives. His own words will always be better than your words, even if you are the most elegant stylist in the land. They carry the inflection of his speaking voice and the idiosyncrasies of how he puts a sentence together. They contain the regionalisms of his conversation and the lingo of his trade. They convey his enthusiasms.*

*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*

The first discipline is to do the interview properly. Most founders interview badly. They ask leading questions, they talk too much, they jump in with their own theories before the subject has finished, they paraphrase back instead of letting the silence work. *Many beginning interviewers are inhibited by the fear that they are imposing on other people and have no right to invade their privacy. This fear is almost wholly unfounded. The so-called man in the street is delighted that somebody wants to interview him. Most men and women lead lives, if not of quiet desperation, at least of desperate quietness, and they jump at a chance to talk about their work to an outsider who seems eager to listen.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) The customer who has been wrestling with their workflow for two years is not annoyed that you want forty-five minutes of their time. They are flattered. The discipline is to ask, prepare, and listen.

The mechanics matter. *Never go into an interview without doing whatever homework you can. If you are interviewing a town official, know his or her voting record. If it's an actress, know what plays or movies she has been in. You will be resented if you inquire about facts you could have learned in advance. Make a list of likely questions — it will save you the vast embarrassment of going dry in mid-interview.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) For a founder, this means: read the customer's blog before the call, know their company's recent moves, have your three most important questions written down. Show up with a list. Allow the conversation to depart from the list when it wants to.

A specific founder discipline: ask the dumb question. Founders, especially in technical or specialised industries, often refuse to ask basic questions because they don't want to look ignorant. *Trust your common sense to figure out what you need to know, and don't be afraid to ask a dumb question. If the expert thinks you're dumb, that's his problem.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) The customer who has been doing compliance for fifteen years actually likes explaining the basics — they have explained them often enough to be precise about them, and the explanation often surfaces the assumption underneath the assumption that the founder needed.

Then comes the second answer. *Your test should be: is the expert's first answer sufficient? Usually it's not.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) The first answer is what the customer says by reflex; the second is what they say after thinking. The founder's job is to push past the first answer. "Why?" "What else?" "Tell me more about that." "What does that look like in practice?" Most useful customer-interview material is the second or third answer to a question that was not the founder's planned question.

The third discipline is the note-taking itself. Zinsser is unfashionably skeptical of tape recorders for most interviews, and his reasons are practical: *I do my interviewing by hand, with a sharp No. 1 pencil. I like the transaction with another person. I like the fact that that person can see me working — doing a job, not just sitting there letting a machine do it for me. Educated people who you think have been talking into your tape recorder with linear precision turn out to have been stumbling so aimlessly over the sands of language that they haven't completed a single decent sentence. The ear makes allowances for missing grammar, syntax and transitions that the eye wouldn't tolerate in print. The seemingly simple use of a tape recorder isn't simple; infinite stitchery is required.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) For a modern founder, the tradeoff is different — recording is so easy that it is foolish not to — but the underlying observation holds. Recordings produce more raw material than you will use. Notes force you to filter in real time, which is itself part of the work. The best practice is to record *and* take notes; use the notes for the writing and the recording for verification of specific quotes.

The fourth discipline is the quote itself. Not every word a customer says is a quote. The job is to find the sentences that are sentences — that have a shape, a rhythm, a specific verb, a real claim. Most of what people say is fluff. Some of it is gold. *You'll find that he said much that's not interesting, or not pertinent, or that's repetitive. Single out the sentences that are most important or colorful. You'll be tempted to use all the words that are in your notes because you performed the laborious chore of getting them all down. But that's a self-indulgence — no excuse for putting the reader to the same effort. Your job is to distill the essence.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) The case study with eight quotes is usually worse than the case study with two. The two are doing real work; the eight are diluting each other.

The ethics: *What about your obligation to the person you interviewed? To what extent can you cut or juggle his words? This question vexes every writer returning from a first interview — and it should. But the answer isn't hard if you keep in mind two standards: brevity and fair play. Your ethical duty to the person being interviewed is to present his position accurately. Or you might misrepresent him by quoting him out of context, or by choosing only some flashy remark without adding the serious afterthought. You are dealing with a person's honor and reputation — and also with your own.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) For a founder, the customer who said "I love your product but I'm worried about your roadmap" cannot ethically be quoted as "I love your product." The first half on its own is a misrepresentation, even if the words are exact.

There is a permitted compression. *Therefore if you find on page 5 of your notes a comment that perfectly amplifies a point on page 2 — a point made earlier in the interview — you will do everyone a favor if you link the two thoughts, letting the second sentence follow and illustrate the first. This may violate the truth of how the interview actually progressed, but you will be true to the intent of what was said. Play with the quotes by all means — selecting, rejecting, thinning, transposing their order, saving a good one for the end. Just make sure the play is fair. Don't change any words or let the cutting of a sentence distort the proper context of what remains.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) For a founder doing a case study, this means: it is fine to combine the customer's three best sentences into a single paragraph that didn't exist in the original conversation, as long as each sentence is verbatim and the combined meaning is faithful to what they actually said. It is not fine to elide a qualification that changes the meaning.

A specific tactical rule: when you use a quote, start the sentence with it. *BAD: Mr. Smith said that he liked to "go downtown once a week and have lunch with some of my old friends." GOOD: "I usually like to go downtown once a week," Mr. Smith said, "and have lunch with some of my old friends." The second sentence has vitality, the first one is dead. Nothing is deader than to start a sentence with a "Mr. Smith said" construction — it's where many readers stop reading.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) The founder's case study that opens "Linda Brooks said that her team was experiencing significant friction" reads as report. The case study that opens with "'My team was wasting four days a quarter doing something the software should be doing,' Linda Brooks said" reads as a person speaking.

The deepest move, when you have the time and the relationship, is the Joseph Mitchell move: stay long enough that the conversation produces material you would not have gotten from a single interview. Mitchell's customer-interview equivalent — a New York oysterman whose words filled a New Yorker piece — was talked to over many afternoons. The founder doing customer-discovery interviews in 30-minute chunks is doing the speed-dating version. The founder who keeps in touch with a handful of customers over years, and lets the conversations roam, accumulates the kind of material that produces strategic insight rather than tactical observation.

## How to Apply

1. **Prepare a question list. Then be willing to abandon it.** Have your three most important questions written before the call. Use them as a backstop. If the conversation departs into more interesting territory, follow it. The list exists so you don't go dry, not so you can keep the conversation on rails.

2. **Push past the first answer.** When the customer says "we wanted something simpler," the work has not started. "Simpler than what?" "What did simple look like in practice?" "Tell me about the moment you decided the old approach wasn't working." The second and third answers are the gold.

3. **Ask dumb questions deliberately.** "What does that mean exactly?" "Why is that hard?" "Why doesn't [obvious alternative] work?" The customer is unlikely to think you are stupid; they are likely to give you a more concrete answer than they would have without the prompt.

4. **Record and take notes.** Modern tools make recording trivial. Do it. Also take notes. Notes force real-time filtering and capture what struck you as interesting in the moment, which is information the recording will not preserve. The recording is your verification layer.

5. **Transcribe the recording yourself, or read the auto-transcript carefully.** This is where most founders break the chain. They have the recording but never review it. Block 30 minutes after each important interview to skim the transcript and extract the three to five sentences that were genuinely usable.

6. **Save quotes in a quote file.** Not in the document where you might use them later — in a single ongoing file of customer quotes, organised by speaker, with date and context. Over time this becomes a reusable corpus that powers blog posts, decks, board updates, and case studies.

7. **Use verbatim, not paraphrase.** *His own words will always be better than your words.* If the customer said "I haven't had to fight my team to use it," that is the line. Do not paraphrase to "users adopted it organically." The founder's instinct to "polish" customer language almost always weakens it.

8. **Get permission before publishing.** Most customers will agree to be quoted, by name, especially in B2B. Ask explicitly: "I'd like to quote you in our investor memo / blog post / case study, by name and title — would that be OK?" Asking is free. The named quote is several times more powerful than the anonymous one.

9. **Compress fairly. Combine cleanly.** It is allowed and often necessary to combine two sentences that were not adjacent in the original. It is not allowed to change the words. It is not allowed to cut a qualifier that changes the meaning. Default to fairness; when in doubt, send the customer the final excerpt and ask "is this how you'd say it?"

10. **Start the sentence with the quote.** "'X,' she said" is alive. "She said that X" is dead. The reader's eye lands on the quoted words first; the attribution comes after. This is a small mechanical rule that improves almost every quoted passage.

11. **Bracket your own confidence in the material.** When the quote feels too good to be true ("this is the best product I've ever bought"), pause. Real customer language is usually more specific and less universal than that. The too-good quote is often the customer's polite generalisation; the lukewarm-sounding-but-specific quote ("we stopped having Tuesday status meetings because we don't need them anymore") is the gold.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has done thirty customer interviews to support a Series B fundraising memo. The current memo summarises: "Customers consistently report dramatic improvements in productivity and significant satisfaction with the product."

**Application:** Replace with three or four verbatim quotes that do specific work. "'I cancelled our Confluence subscription the day we hit two weeks on your platform,' said Dan Cho, head of engineering at [Company]." "'My team's gone from a 6am all-hands every Monday to a written update on Sundays. The all-hands was just to make sure everyone knew what was going on. They know now.' — Sara Tahir, COO at [Company]." Each quote is doing different work: cancelling a competitor; replacing a meeting with documentation; specific behavioral change.

**Result:** Investors who would have skimmed the original abstraction now have three vivid customer voices in their head. They reference them in their internal memos. The conversation in the partner meeting is no longer "the founders say customers love them" (the default for every fundraise) — it is about Sara's team and what changed about how they work.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a customer case study. The current draft has been written by marketing about an abstract entity called "the customer." It contains zero verbatim quotes and one anonymised testimonial in the closing.

**Application:** Throw it out. Get on a call with the actual customer — preferably the operator who lives the product, not the executive sponsor. Spend 45 minutes. Ask: "Walk me through how your team worked the week before you bought us." "Walk me through how it works now." "What's the most surprising thing about how your team uses the product?" Get permission to use names and exact words. Build the case study around three or four direct quotes from the operator, framed by minimal narrative.

**Result:** The case study is sharable. Prospects read it and recognise their own situation in the operator's words. The marketing version was indistinguishable from twenty other case studies; the rewritten version is the company's. It gets cited in sales calls for the next two years.

**Situation:** A founder has interviewed five candidates for a VP Engineering role and needs to write a memo to the board recommending one. They start writing in summary mode: "Candidate A demonstrated strong technical depth. Candidate B brought exceptional people leadership..."

**Application:** Replace summaries with quotes. "When I asked her how she'd approach the platform rebuild, [Candidate A] said: 'I wouldn't. I'd freeze new features for six weeks, ship a clean migration in week seven, and unfreeze. Anything else costs more in support fires than it saves in velocity.' I have not heard a candidate this clear about where the actual cost is." Now the board is reading the candidate's own thinking, not the founder's gloss on it.

**Result:** The board's discussion moves to the substance of the candidate's positions, not the founder's framing of them. One board member spots a concerning implication of Candidate A's quote ("does this mean she'd push back on growth-driven feature requests?") that the founder hadn't surfaced. Better hire, better-aligned expectations.

**Situation:** A founder is publishing a blog post about a shift in their industry. They have spoken to a dozen operators, each of whom said something interesting. The current draft cites none of them by name; it makes the founder's own argument with vague reference to "the operators we've spoken with."

**Application:** Pick the three most useful, most willing-to-be-named operators. Ask permission. Anchor the essay around their words. The piece is now a small piece of journalism about an industry shift, not a manifesto. The founder's own argument can still come through — but it is now arguing alongside named, specific people, which is more credible than arguing alongside the founder's own anonymous synthesis.

**Result:** The essay is read by a much wider audience because it now has voices from several operators in it; each named operator shares it with their own network; the founder's argument carries the credibility of being made in the company of others rather than as a solo position.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Talk more than the customer in customer interviews.
**Why:** Most founders talk roughly half the time in customer-discovery calls. The right number is closer to a quarter. The customer is the source of the data. The founder's job is to extract; talking is the opposite of extracting. If you find yourself rehearsing your pitch on a discovery call, you are wasting both people's time.

**Don't:** Use a customer quote out of context to support a claim it didn't actually make.
**Why:** "I love your product" cherry-picked from "I love your product but I won't renew unless you fix support" is the kind of misrepresentation that destroys trust the moment the full context is discovered. Customer quotes are a trust mechanism. Misuse them once and you can never use them credibly again.

**Don't:** Polish the language of the quote.
**Why:** The slightly inelegant phrase, the specific verb, the minor grammatical irregularity — these are the marks of authenticity. Marketing's instinct to clean up customer language strips the marks of authenticity. The polished version reads as fabricated; the rough version reads as real.

**Don't:** Stuff the document with quotes to make it look researched.
**Why:** A piece with twelve quotes in eight hundred words is a transcript with editorial voice-over. The reader cannot follow it. Two or three load-bearing quotes do more work than a dozen decorative ones.

**Don't:** Skip the homework.
**Why:** The founder who walks into a customer interview without having read the customer's last quarter or the candidate's last role is performing rudeness. The customer notices. They will give you the standard pre-prepared answers, not the real ones, because they correctly assess that you have not earned the harder material.

**Don't:** Use anonymous quotes when named ones are available.
**Why:** "Said one customer" is a tell. Either the customer didn't agree to be named (in which case ask again, and if they still decline, consider whether the quote is worth using anyway) or the founder didn't ask. The anonymous quote is read with skepticism; the named quote is read with belief. The friction of asking is much less than the credibility cost of not asking.

**Don't:** Confuse "lots of interviews" with "good interviews."
**Why:** Founders often pride themselves on having done thirty customer interviews when in fact none of the thirty produced a usable sentence. Better to do six interviews that yielded fifteen usable quotes than thirty that yielded none. Quality is interview-craft, not volume.

**Don't:** Fail to record while on the move.
**Why:** Useful customer material happens at conferences, hallway conversations, kickoff dinners, and casual coffee. Most of it is lost because the founder thought they would remember and didn't. Voice memo as you walk to the car. The lost-because-not-recorded conversations are the dominant source of waste in founder customer-research time.
