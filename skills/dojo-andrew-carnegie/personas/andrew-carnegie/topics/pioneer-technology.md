# Pioneer Technology

## When to Use

- A new process has been demonstrated — in another country, in a laboratory, in an adjacent industry — and its economics, if reproduced at scale, would be transformative.
- The incumbents in your industry are dismissing the new process as impractical, too expensive to adopt, or unproven. Their dismissal is structurally self-interested: they have sunk capital in the old way.
- You have the capital, the operating discipline, and the time horizon to absorb the cost of being the first to adopt at scale — knowing that the first adopter bears the learning-curve expense that later adopters will not.
- The underlying physics, chemistry, or information structure of the new process is sound. This is not a matter of taste; it is a matter of whether the thing can actually be made to work.

## Fails When

- **The technology does not yet work anywhere.** There is a difference between "unproven at commercial scale" and "has not yet been made to function at all." The first is an opportunity for the pioneer; the second is speculation.
- **You lack the operating depth to absorb the risk.** Being first at scale is expensive. If the adoption fails, you have sunk a disproportionate amount of capital. Only firms with meaningful financial reserves and operating discipline should pioneer.
- **The innovation is marketing.** Many "new technologies" are rebranded versions of the old method with a modest improvement at the margin. Study the physics before committing. If the improvement is real, it will show up in raw-material efficiency, energy consumption, or throughput. If it does not, it is not real.
- **The existing technology is still improving faster.** Sometimes the old method is not standing still. Open-hearth steel eventually overtook Bessemer in many applications because the open-hearth process continued to improve faster than Bessemer's incremental gains. The pioneer of Bessemer who did not also watch the open-hearth got caught. Study the improvement rate, not only the current state.

## Core Concept

I crossed the Atlantic in 1872 to see Henry Bessemer's process working in Sheffield. What I saw there settled a decision I had been considering for three years. The Bessemer converter — an enormous, pear-shaped vessel into which molten pig iron was poured, through which air was forced, and from which, in under twenty minutes, a ton of steel emerged — was producing steel at a cost and a quality no puddling furnace could match. The older Pittsburgh manufacturers had seen the same demonstration and pronounced it extravagant. I saw it and organized the Edgar Thomson Steel Company in January 1873.

The Edgar Thomson site was chosen with care — rail access to three major lines, river frontage for coal and ore, named for Mr. Edgar Thomson of the Pennsylvania Railroad to encourage that firm's orders. The works opened in 1875. In its first month of operation we earned $11,000 of profit, and as I noted at the time, "so perfect was our system of accounts that we knew the exact amount of the profit" — which most of our competitors, still on the lump-accounting system, could not have told you of their own operations.

By the time the older Pittsburgh firms began their own Bessemer conversions, we had three years of operating experience, a cost structure they could not match, and the Pennsylvania Railroad's rail orders. We had not merely beaten them to the technology; we had beaten them to the *learning curve* around the technology. Our competitors, when they finally came, were buying the Bessemer converters at retail — the equipment, the know-how, the trained crews — after we had paid wholesale for all of them at the pioneer's rates.

The same pattern repeated with the chemistry laboratory (1870), which I have discussed elsewhere. The same pattern with the Siemens open-hearth gas furnace, which cut heating waste by nearly half and which we adopted years before the Pittsburgh industry took it seriously. The same pattern with direct relationships to the railroad presidents — Thomson of the Pennsylvania, Scott of the same line, Garrett of the B&O — relationships we cultivated personally while our competitors sold through agents.

The principle is this. The cost of pioneering is real — you pay for the first crews, the first machines, the first mistakes, while others watch and learn. The *benefit* of pioneering is that by the time the others arrive, you are already two or three years ahead on the operating experience, the cost structure, and the customer relationships. The gap does not close; if anything it widens, because your further improvements come from a base the latecomers do not yet have.

"I believe the true road to preeminent success in any line is to make yourself master in that line." The master is not the man who waits for the technology to be proven. The master is the man who understood, three years before his competitors, why it would work — and built the plant.

## How to Apply

1. **Study the underlying physics or information structure.** Before investing in a new process, understand why it works. Is the improvement a matter of chemistry, thermodynamics, mechanics, computation? The answer must be specific enough that you could draw it on paper. "It's a better method" is not an answer; "it substitutes air-blown oxygen for external fuel, which converts the pig-iron carbon into heat, allowing the charge to self-heat and reducing fuel cost by a factor of four" is an answer.

2. **Go where it is already working.** Cross the ocean if you must. I went to Sheffield to see Bessemer; I went to Britain repeatedly to see the manufacturing methods I would later bring to Pittsburgh. Firsthand observation of a working plant beats any number of technical papers. You are looking for what the papers do not describe — the operating temperament, the crew's adjustments, the failure modes.

3. **Decide based on the ratio of promised economics to current cost.** If the new process, at mature scale, would operate at half the cost of the old, and the old process is your primary competitor — you should pioneer. If the saving is ten per cent, the payoff is marginal and the risk may not be worth it. The pioneers' prize is paid in magnitudes, not percentages.

4. **Adopt at scale, not experimentally.** The half-hearted adoption is the worst of all worlds — you have paid the pioneer's learning cost without the pioneer's benefit. If you are going to adopt, commit. The Edgar Thomson Works was designed from the ground up around the Bessemer process; no compromises to accommodate the older methods, no convertible equipment, no hedging.

5. **Protect the technology while the incumbents are still sleeping.** Patents, where available. Supplier exclusivity, where possible. Personal relationships with the customers who will use the output — these are more durable than any patent. The technological lead gives you a window of two to five years; use it to build the customer relationships and the cost discipline that will survive after the technology has become standard.

6. **Do not pioneer and also diversify.** The pioneer bets the firm on the new technology. That commitment is incompatible with scattering capital across other ventures. If you are going to pioneer in steel, you must pioneer *in steel* — not in steel and oil and railroads and telegraph. The concentration doctrine (see `concentration.md`) and the pioneer doctrine are two sides of the same coin.

## Examples

**Bessemer adoption at Edgar Thomson (1872–75).** Described above. The three-year lead time, converted into a permanent cost advantage, converted into a dominant market position. Note the details: I had seen other American firms attempt the Bessemer conversion and struggle — Freedom Iron Works, Pennsylvania Steel at Harrisburg — and I waited until the process was fully established in England before committing. Pioneering is not recklessness; it is early-but-informed commitment.

**The chemistry laboratory (1870).** Before any American iron firm employed a scientifically-trained chemist, we did. The cost was a single annual salary. The return was the discovery that our competitors' ore specifications were wrong by ten to twenty per cent — meaning we could buy ore they were rejecting, reject ore they were overpaying for, and run our furnaces on inputs they had never properly analyzed. The competitive advantage lasted nearly a decade because the competitors did not understand why our costs were lower. They thought we had better ore; in fact we had better chemistry.

**The Siemens gas furnace.** Adopted over "extravagant" protests from older Pittsburgh manufacturers. The furnace cut heating waste by nearly half — meaning we were producing steel with half the fuel cost of our competitors. In years when the margin was thin, the savings from the furnace constituted most of our profit. "Yet it was many years before we were followed in this new departure; and in some of those years the margin of profit was so small that the most of it was made up from the savings derived from the adoption of the improved furnaces." The sentence summarizes the pioneer's dividend.

**The Dodds patents (pre-Bessemer).** Before the Bessemer process was commercially established, we obtained control of the Dodds patents for carbonizing rail heads and conducted experiments at Pittsburgh for the Pennsylvania Railroad. Had Bessemer failed — as some serious engineers believed it might — we would have had the Dodds process under our control. Pioneering is not always about backing a single horse; it is sometimes about having the next best option already in hand.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Waiting for the technology to be proven.** The industry standard for "proven" is three to five years after the pioneer has captured the economics. By the time the conservative adopter moves, the pioneer has paid the learning cost, built the customer relationships, and set the cost structure. The conservative adopter is no longer catching up; he is entering a mature market behind the incumbents.

- **Adopting and diversifying simultaneously.** Pioneering requires focused capital and focused attention. A firm that pioneers in steel while also investing in oil and railroads will do neither adequately. The pioneer commits.

- **Confusing operational improvement with technology adoption.** Many firms believe they are pioneering when they are merely installing the latest version of the same method. Real pioneering changes the underlying economics — by a factor, not a percentage.

- **Paying for pioneering while under public market pressure.** The public company that pioneers a technology pays for it under quarterly-earnings scrutiny from investors who do not understand why this year's profit is lower. The private partnership pays for it under partners' scrutiny, who (if chosen well) understand what the investment is buying. This is one of several reasons Carnegie Steel was never listed publicly.

- **Announcing the pioneer investment before it is operating.** The technological edge is durable only as long as the competitors do not know what you have learned. Operate quietly. The results will announce themselves when your cost per ton has fallen below what the competitors can explain.
