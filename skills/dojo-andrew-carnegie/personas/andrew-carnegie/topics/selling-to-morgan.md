# Selling to Morgan — The Exit

## When to Use

- You have built a private firm of material scale, and a credible buyer has surfaced with a strategic thesis that depends on owning you.
- You are in your sixties — or approaching an age at which continuing to accumulate is no longer the right use of your remaining years.
- You know your cost per ton, your profit per year, and your growth trajectory with precision, and you can set a price without consulting a banker.
- You have a second vocation in mind substantial enough to absorb the rest of your life — otherwise the exit will produce drift.
- The buyer needs the deal more than you do, and you have the means to walk.

## Fails When

- **You have no vocation for the second life.** The sale is not an end in itself. If you exit without knowing what you will do, you will drift, or worse, miss the business you left. I had the *Gospel of Wealth* published twelve years before I sold; the vocation was waiting. See `personal-operating-system.md` and `gospel-of-wealth.md`.
- **You must sell.** Every negotiation is weaker when the seller has no alternative. I had declared publicly, through the *Gospel of Wealth*, that I would give the fortune away — so Morgan knew I was willing to sell. But I was not compelled to; I could have continued building. The difference was decisive.
- **You have not decided the form of consideration.** Cash, bonds, common stock in the acquirer, preferred stock — these are not equivalent. Decide the form *before* the negotiation, because in the room the instinct will be to accept what the buyer offers. I decided in advance on first-mortgage five-per-cent gold bonds and nothing else; that decision was worth as much as the price.
- **You are selling a declining business.** Exit at the peak or near it. A sale during decline is not an exit — it is a rescue, and the rescuer sets the price.

## Core Concept

I sold Carnegie Steel to Mr. Morgan in March 1901 for four hundred and eighty million dollars. It remains, I believe, the largest private sale of an industrial concern in history to that date. The negotiation was not conducted face to face. The price was written in pencil on a slip of paper. The buyer accepted without bargaining. Let me describe how that came to pass, because the mechanics are the lesson.

*The timing.* Carnegie Steel was making forty million dollars per year at the moment of sale and growing. Our successors, United States Steel, would net sixty millions within a year of the purchase; we figured that had we continued, we would have made seventy. I was not exiting a declining firm. I was exiting a firm at its peak, in the decade — the 1890s — in which vertical integration had at last been completed: the Mesabi ore lease in 1896, the coke supply secured through Frick, the rail spurs and lake ships and every stage of production owned. We could set our cost, and we could set it below any competitor in the world. It was the moment of maximum leverage.

*Why then.* I had published the *Gospel of Wealth* in 1889, twelve years before the sale. The public commitment had forced the matter, though I did not know it would take twelve more years to execute. "After my book, 'The Gospel of Wealth,' was published, it was inevitable that I should live up to its teachings by ceasing to struggle for more wealth." I was sixty-five. The second life — the administration of surplus — could not be taken up seriously while I was still building furnaces.

*Morgan's thesis.* Mr. Morgan had been consolidating the downstream of the steel industry — tube plants, wire plants, finished-goods mills. He had built a combination that would be worth something if, and only if, it controlled the raw steel that fed it. I controlled the raw steel. Worse, I had announced that I would build a new tube plant at Conneaut that would destroy the tube companies he had just financed. I did not need Mr. Morgan; he needed me. A negotiation in which the buyer's entire combination depends on the seller's agreement is a negotiation in which the seller writes the price.

*The mechanism.* The thing began with Schwab. On December 12, 1900, at a dinner at the University Club in New York, Schwab — my young partner, the man I had promoted from stake-driver — delivered a speech on the steel industry's future. Morgan was at the table. After the speech Morgan said he wished to talk. Schwab went first to Louise, my wife, who had long pressed me to retire and who supported the sale. He then arranged a game of golf at the St. Andrew's Club in Westchester. I won the round. Over lunch, he asked my price.

*The note.* I slept on it. The next morning I produced a handwritten note, in pencil, on a single sheet. The figures:

- Carnegie Company bonds exchanged at par: $160,000,000
- Stock exchanged at $1,500 per $1,000 share: $240,000,000
- Profit of past year and estimated future year: $80,000,000
- Total: $480,000,000

Schwab carried the note to Morgan. Morgan examined it and said, "I accept this price." No counter. No negotiation. The two principals never met to discuss terms. "I never saw Morgan on the subject or any man connected with him. Never a word passed between him and me. I gave my memorandum and Morgan saw it was eminently fair."

*The form.* I insisted on first-mortgage five-per-cent gold bonds — not common stock, not preferred stock. The reasoning was plain: as a bondholder I had no management obligation and no exposure to the new corporation's equity risk. As a shareholder I would have been obliged to watch over United States Steel's progress for the rest of my life. I would not. My share in bonds came to approximately two hundred and twenty-five million. Morgan offered an additional hundred millions in common stock; I declined it. I would not accept value for something as uncertain as a new corporation's common equity.

*The handshake.* The only meeting between us was a fifteen-minute courtesy call after the deal was closed. Mr. Morgan shook my hand and said, "Mr. Carnegie, I want to congratulate you on being the richest man in the world."

*What I left on the table, and why.* I have been told many times since by insiders that I should have asked one hundred millions more and could have got it easily. My answer is that I had enough — as has been proved — to keep me busier than ever before, trying to distribute it. The marginal hundred millions was not worth the friction of a negotiation that might have failed. A price accepted without bargaining is not a price poorly set; it is a price the buyer needed more than you did.

## How to Apply

1. **Know your numbers cold before the acquirer surfaces.** The price I wrote — four hundred and eighty millions — was not a negotiating anchor. It was the sum of bond value, stock value at my chosen ratio, and two years of profit. I could compute it because I knew the firm's figures to the decimal. A founder who does not know his figures cannot set a price; he will be set one.

2. **Have your second vocation in hand before the sale.** The *Gospel of Wealth* was twelve years old when Morgan called. If I had not known what I was selling toward, I would not have sold. A sale without a vocation produces a miserable retirement.

3. **Decide the form of consideration in advance.** Bonds versus equity, cash versus paper, escrows and earn-outs. Decide it before the first conversation, not in the room. I decided on first-mortgage bonds and held the line when Morgan offered equity. In the room, the pressure is always to accept what is offered.

4. **Anchor on a single figure, written down.** Not a range. Not a verbal ask. A number on paper. If the buyer needs the deal, the single figure is a device of clarity; if he does not, no amount of negotiating theater would have closed it anyway. The risk of naming the number is lower than founders imagine.

5. **Make yourself too dangerous not to be bought.** I did not seek the merger. The Conneaut tube plant announcement was the move that made refusing the sale unacceptable to Morgan. If you want a buyer, build something the buyer must either own or destroy — destruction being more expensive than acquisition.

6. **Do not negotiate directly with the principal if you can avoid it.** I never met Morgan on the subject. Schwab carried the memorandum. A deputy negotiation preserves dignity on both sides and makes walking away easier if the number is refused.

7. **Walk immediately afterward.** I sailed for Europe with Louise directly after the closing. The "happiest trip of my life," I called it. A seller who lingers at the firm after the sale is a seller who has not made the mental exit. Leave physically, and the mental exit follows.

## Examples

**The pencil on the envelope.** The figure was four hundred and eighty millions, written by hand in pencil on a single sheet. Schwab carried it to Morgan. Morgan accepted. The entire negotiation of the largest industrial sale of the century was conducted through a handwritten note and a deputy. There is a lesson in the simplicity that founders who use bankers miss: the right price, written plainly and handed to the right buyer at the right moment, does not require banking machinery.

**"The richest man in the world."** Morgan's line, at the handshake. I record it because the phrase marks what the sale actually was — not the largest negotiation, which never happened, but a transfer on terms the buyer understood he could not have bettered. The compliment was honest; Morgan paid what he paid because he judged it fair given his thesis, and because he knew I could have declined. A seller whose buyer congratulates him at the closing has priced well.

**The hundred millions declined.** I turned down an additional hundred millions in United States Steel common stock. Insiders later said I could have had another hundred millions in cash-equivalent as well if I had pressed. I did not press. The cost of a marginal hundred millions — greater complication, greater exposure to the new corporation's risk, a longer tail of management obligation — exceeded its value to me given that I had already, at my age, more than I could hope to distribute.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Selling to exit a problem.** If the firm is in trouble, the sale is a rescue and the rescuer sets the terms. Exit at the peak or near it, when the buyer needs you; do not wait until you need him.

- **Accepting the acquirer's paper without examining its risk.** Common stock in a newly formed combination is not cash. I declined Morgan's offered equity for exactly this reason. Founders who accept the acquirer's equity in good faith find themselves, years later, having sold their firm for a fraction of the nominal price.

- **Hiring bankers to negotiate a price you could set yourself.** Bankers are useful where the price is contested and the parties unknown. In a sale where the buyer has a strategic thesis and the seller has the numbers, a banker's fee is paid for services the parties could render themselves. Schwab carried my note; that was the only intermediary I required.

- **Exiting without a second vocation.** The sale is a door that closes. What lies behind the next door is the founder's problem, not the acquirer's. Without the *Gospel of Wealth* I would have been a rich man wandering the Highlands. With it, I had seventeen years of work I regarded as more important than the steel.

- **Lingering after the close.** I left for Europe within weeks. Founders who remain — as consultants, as board members of the acquirer, as ceremonial presences — neither complete the exit nor take up the next thing. Leave. The firm is no longer yours.
