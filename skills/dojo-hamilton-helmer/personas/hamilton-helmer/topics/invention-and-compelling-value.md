# Invention and Compelling Value

## When to Use
- You are at the origination or takeoff stage and the question is how Power can be created at all.
- You are diagnosing whether explosive growth reflects a durable strategic position or will be arbitraged away as growth slows.
- You are evaluating whether a product has achieved a "gotta have" response from customers — the threshold that converts invention into market expansion.
- You are deciding how to sequence the pursuit of product-market fit against the construction of a Barrier.

## Fails When
- **The strategy is "me too."** Imitation cannot reach Power. Each Power type requires an asymmetry between the Power holder and competitors, and by definition imitation eliminates asymmetry.
- **The product is merely "nice to have."** Without compelling value, there is nothing for a Barrier to protect. The business dies of irrelevance before Power can be established.
- **The team defers strategy until after PMF.** Product architecture choices made during origination lock in whether various Power types are even possible. By the time the product is beloved, the Power window may have closed.
- **A rigid 10x rule is applied universally.** Andy Grove's standard is probably right for semiconductors. In other domains, far smaller improvements constitute compelling value. A 2x improvement in battery energy density would make you a billionaire.
- **Attractive financials during high growth are read as proof of Power.** Compelling value produces explosive growth, which produces attractive financials, which can be a false positive. The test for Power applies only when growth slows and competitive arbitrage asserts itself.

## Core Concept

The first cause of all Power is invention. Power arrives only on the heels of invention — whether invention of product, process, brand, or business model. "Me Too" won't do. You cannot reach Power through imitation. This is an empirical observation drawn from several decades of strategy work, and it admits of no exceptions I have encountered.

The reason is structural. Each of the 7 Powers requires some form of asymmetry between the Power holder and competitors. Scale Economies require a cost structure that smaller players cannot replicate at their volume. Network Economies require an installed base that competitors cannot match. Counter-Positioning requires a business model that incumbents will not adopt. Switching Costs require customer embedment that competitors have not achieved. Branding requires decades of association-building that cannot be accelerated. Cornered Resources require preferential access that others lack. Process Power requires organizational evolution that defies imitation. In every case, the asymmetry originates in something new — something invented, not copied.

But invention alone is insufficient. The invention must produce what I call **compelling value** — a product sufficiently superior to evoke a "gotta have" response from customers, not merely "nice to have," but a feeling that not having the product is unacceptable. Compelling value is the threshold that converts invention into explosive market growth. Without compelling value, the market does not expand, the business does not scale, and the takeoff-stage Powers (Scale Economies, Network Economies, Switching Costs) cannot be established because the conditions that make their establishment possible — rapid growth, winner-take-most dynamics, the defining of customer routines — never materialize.

Compelling value arrives through three distinct paths, each with different uncertainties. The **capabilities-led** path translates an internal capability into a product for a market that does not yet exist — Adobe Acrobat launched in 1993 to $2 million in first-year revenue, then became indispensable five years later when the Internet created an urgent, previously unarticulated need for document sharing. The primary uncertainty is whether customers will want it. The **customer-led** path addresses a widely recognized unmet need for which no one has yet found a solution — Corning's development of fiber optics pursued pure silica as the transmission medium while competitors improved existing glass incrementally. The primary uncertainty is technical. The **competitor-led** path produces something dramatically better than an incumbent's product — Sony's PlayStation exploited real-time 3D graphics to leapfrog Nintendo and Sega, requiring Norio Ohga's $50 million chip guarantee. The primary uncertainty is whether the new features are differentially attractive enough, and whether incumbents will be delayed in responding.

The **practical consequence** is a conjunction. You must invent AND you must build Power. These are related but distinct accomplishments — what I have elsewhere called the dual task. Invention is the first cause. Without compelling value, no route to Power exists because there is no explosive growth within which a Barrier can be constructed. But compelling value alone does not create Power. Many companies have produced products that customers found irresistible, achieved explosive growth, and then discovered that competitive arbitrage caught up the moment growth slowed. Attractive financials during high growth can be a false positive. Cromemco — once the largest S-100 bus computer manufacturer, growing at triple digits, cash positive — had compelling value but no Power. When the shakeout came, compelling value without Power proved worthless. The business dies of commoditization if it has compelling value without a Barrier, or of irrelevance if it has a Barrier without compelling value. Both are required. Neither is sufficient.

## How to Apply

Treat invention and Power construction as simultaneous questions, not sequential ones. The two questions to hold in mind throughout origination and takeoff are: "Is this invention creating compelling value that will drive market growth?" and "Is this invention creating the conditions for one of the 7 Powers?" If the answer to the first is yes but the second is no, your invention will make customers happy but not make your business durably valuable.

1. **Diagnose the path to compelling value.** Capabilities-led, customer-led, or competitor-led? Each path carries different uncertainties and demands different tactics. Capabilities-led requires patience — five or more years before the market clarifies — and avoidance of high-testosterone commitments before the need has emerged. Customer-led requires leveraging distinctive capabilities to solve a known problem in a way competitors cannot. Competitor-led demands massive upfront commitments because the time constants are short and the incumbents will respond.

2. **Test for "gotta have" rather than applying a rigid multiple.** Ignore the universal 10x rule. Andy Grove's standard is appropriate for semiconductors, where incremental improvements are rapidly matched. In other domains, far smaller improvements constitute compelling value. A 50% increase in photovoltaic efficiency would transform the energy industry. A 2x improvement in battery energy density would make you a billionaire. The test is whether customers experience a "gotta have" response, and that threshold varies enormously across contexts.

3. **Distinguish strategic invention from operational re-invention.** Most invention in business is operational excellence — the constant improvement of products, processes, and capabilities that every well-run company pursues. This is valuable and necessary, but it is subject to competitive arbitrage. Only rarely does invention create the conditions for one of the 7 Powers. The strategist's task is to recognize which inventions have that potential and act on them before the window closes.

4. **Check whether the product choices open Power routes.** Product architecture decisions made during origination often lock in whether various Power types are even possible. Netflix's move to originals and exclusives was not merely a content decision — it converted content from a variable cost into a fixed cost, creating the conditions for Scale Economies. Had Netflix merely launched a streaming service with the same licensed content model as every competitor, they would have had a commodity business with no route to Power. Ask at every major product decision: what Power type does this architecture enable, and what Power types does it foreclose?

5. **Embrace Knightian uncertainty rather than demanding false precision.** Invention occurs in conditions of profound uncertainty. If you can estimate your market size with great confidence, you are probably too late. Frank Knight's distinction between risk (known probability distribution) and uncertainty (distribution unknown) applies with full force. The strategist who has deeply internalized the 7 Powers framework develops what Pasteur called the prepared mind — a cognitive frame for recognizing Power opportunities as they emerge amid uncertainty, rather than waiting for analytical certainty that will never arrive.

## Examples

**Situation:** Adobe launched Acrobat in 1993. The technology was elegant, the team superb, the company had deep expertise in software and digital graphics. First-year revenue: $2 million. A flop by any measure.
**Application:** This was a capabilities-led path. Adobe had the capability; no customer was asking for it. The correct tactics were to avoid high-testosterone commitments, stay in the game, and be prepared to morph the product as emerging requirements clarified. Five years later, the explosive growth of the Internet created an urgent need for documents that looked the same on every screen, and Acrobat — initially a solution in search of a problem — became the solution to a problem that had not existed at launch.
**Result:** Compelling value was ultimately achieved, not through better marketing but through external conditions that transformed an unarticulated need into an urgent want. The strategic lesson: in a capabilities-led path, customer expressions of wants can be highly misleading, because customers themselves do not yet understand the need your capability addresses. Market surveys fail for genuinely new products because the respondents do not know what you are talking about.

**Situation:** Netflix, with its existing DVD business, contemplated launching streaming. The technologically obvious path was to license content from the same studios that supplied everyone else.
**Application:** The invention that mattered strategically was not streaming itself — that was technologically inevitable — but the business model restructuring: the move to originals and exclusives. This converted content from a variable cost into a fixed cost, creating the conditions for Scale Economies that would be foreclosed by the commodity licensing model. Simultaneously, exclusive prestige content like *House of Cards* attracted millions of subscribers who would not have joined for a library of licensed reruns. The invention delivered a one-two value punch: it opened the door for Power (the Scale Economies afforded by fixed-cost content) and it propelled market size (the market expansion driven by compelling exclusive content). Both terms of the Fundamental Equation — Power and Market Scale — were addressed by the same strategic invention.
**Result:** A structural advantage that persists because a commodity-licensing competitor cannot match Netflix's fixed-cost content amortization at their volume. Without this invention, Netflix's streaming would have offered no structural advantage whatsoever.

**Situation:** A startup achieves explosive triple-digit growth, becomes cash positive, and its founders conclude that the business is durably valuable.
**Application:** Attractive financials during high growth are not evidence of Power. Compelling value is necessary for explosive growth, but compelling value alone does not create Power. The test comes when growth slows and competitive arbitrage asserts itself. Cromemco was once the largest S-100 bus computer manufacturer, growing at triple digits, cash positive — and had compelling value but no Power. When the shakeout came, compelling value without Power proved worthless. The diagnostic question is not "are we growing?" but "when growth slows, what Barrier will protect our differential returns from competitive arbitrage?" If the honest answer is "we haven't constructed one yet," the strategic priority is to do so within the window during which such construction remains possible.
**Result:** The founders' conclusion is premature. The correct strategic question is whether product choices made during this growth phase are opening or foreclosing Power routes — and whether the remaining origination and takeoff window is long enough to establish a Barrier before growth slows.

**Situation:** A founder is planning to enter an established market with a product broadly similar to existing competitors, arguing that superior execution will produce a superior outcome.
**Application:** "Me too" cannot reach Power. Each Power type requires an asymmetry, and imitation eliminates asymmetry by definition. The imitator may capture some of the market's growth, but will not establish the structural advantage that produces persistent significant differential returns. This does not mean competitors cannot learn from one another. It means the path to Power requires something genuinely new — a novel product, a different business model, a heterodox approach — that creates the asymmetry on which Power depends. The distinction between first mover and inventor is relevant: MySpace came before Facebook, but the fact of being first did not diminish the creative act of everything Zuckerberg and his team invented. What matters is not being first, but inventing something that creates the conditions for Power.
**Result:** The founder's plan, if unchanged, is a commodity outcome. The strategic question is what can be invented — product, business model, go-to-market — that creates asymmetry versus the incumbents. Without that invention, operational excellence will produce a well-run business with no durable advantage.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse constant operational re-invention with strategic invention.
**Why:** Every well-run company constantly improves its products, processes, and capabilities. This is operational excellence, and it is essential. But it is not the invention that creates Power. The improvements are imitable, the advantages temporary. A company that mistakes its operational improvement cycle for strategic invention will be surprised when competitive arbitrage erodes what it believed to be a durable advantage.

**Don't:** Apply the 10x rule universally.
**Why:** Andy Grove's standard is appropriate for semiconductors, where incremental improvements are rapidly matched. In other domains, far smaller improvements can constitute compelling value. A rigid 10x threshold causes companies to dismiss inventions that would, in their specific domain, create enormous value. The test is whether customers experience a "gotta have" response, and that threshold varies by context.

**Don't:** Wait for compelling value before thinking about Power.
**Why:** This is a particularly insidious error. Product choices made during origination lock in a great deal. They set the stage for whether the various Power types are even possible. A company that defers strategic thinking until after achieving product-market fit may discover that its product architecture, business model, or customer acquisition approach has foreclosed the routes to Power that were available during origination. The two questions — "Does our invention create compelling value?" and "Does our invention create the conditions for Power?" — must be pursued simultaneously.

**Don't:** Believe that a "gotta have" product guarantees strategic success.
**Why:** Compelling value is necessary for explosive market growth, which in turn is necessary for establishing the takeoff-stage Powers. But compelling value alone does not create Power. Many companies have produced products that customers found irresistible, achieved explosive growth, and then discovered that competitive arbitrage caught up the moment growth slowed. Attractive financials during high growth can be a false positive. The Cromemco pattern recurs in every era.

**Don't:** Demand analytical certainty before acting on an invention opportunity.
**Why:** If you can estimate your market size with great confidence, you are probably too late. The moment when strategic thinking is most needed — when Power could be created through invention — is precisely the moment when the noise-to-signal ratio is highest and the evidence for any particular course of action is weakest. The resolution is not better planning but the prepared mind: deep internalization of the 7 Powers framework that enables recognition of Power opportunities as they emerge under uncertainty.

**Don't:** Treat invention and Power as sequential goals.
**Why:** They are a conjunction, not a sequence. Both must be pursued simultaneously. The strategist's lens must stay simultaneously on compelling value (without which the market does not expand) and on the Power architecture (without which compelling value is eventually arbitraged). Missing either produces a predictable failure mode — commoditization if there is no Power, irrelevance if there is no compelling value.

Strategic planning is essentially an oxymoron. Strategy is something you craft over time — an emergent, deliberative, creative effort that evolves as you respond to markets, customers, and competitive dynamics. In the formative period as invention takes shape, the strategist must stay constantly vigilant for Power openings. Benefits are common. Barriers are not. The sole objective of strategy is to find the rare conjunction of both — an invention that produces compelling value and simultaneously opens a route to one of the 7 Powers, pursued during the narrow window when both are still achievable.
