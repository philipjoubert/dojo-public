# Design Is Not How It Looks — It's How It Works

## When to Use
- A designer just handed you a design and someone on the team said, "yeah, but can we make it look nicer?" — they're missing the point.
- You're deciding where the design function sits in the org.
- You're evaluating a product that technically works but doesn't *feel* right and you can't explain why.
- You're tempted to outsource design to an agency because "we'll hire real designers later."
- You're rethinking a business model, an expense base, or an org chart and you think of those as "business problems" — they're design problems.

## Fails When
- You don't have taste and can't judge the work. The standard collapses to "does it look like the competitors? Good, ship."
- You confuse "design runs everything" with "the pretty people run everything." That's not what this is about. Design is about how a thing *works*.
- You let designers catch things at the end, after engineering and marketing have already shaped the product. At that point you've already lost.

## Core Concept

Design is a funny word. Some people think design means how it looks. In most people's vocabularies, design means veneer — interior decorating, the fabric of the curtains and the sofa.

Nothing could be further from the meaning of design. Design is the fundamental soul of a man-made creation that ends up expressing itself in successive outer layers of the product or service.

When we designed the iMac, the design decision wasn't what color the plastic was. It was: one plug, one cable to the internet. That's it. Eliminate the cable chaos. Eliminate the setup. The design was about what the user experiences, what the user has to do, what the user has to understand. The color of the plastic was the last layer.

To design something really well, you have to *get* it. You have to really grok what it's all about. It takes a passionate commitment to thoroughly understand something, chew it up — not just quickly swallow it. Most people don't take the time to do that.

Here's what most companies miss. Designers can design more than pixels. A designer can design a business model, because a business model is a system of parts that have to fit together and the fit can be elegant or ugly. A designer can design an org chart, a manufacturing process, a supply chain, a company's story. The way you organize a company is a design problem. The way you price a product is a design problem. The way a customer flows through your purchase is a design problem.

And here's the other thing: simplifying is not removing. Simplifying is distilling something to its essence. To distill something to its essence, you have to deeply understand it. It's physics. It's first principles. Subtraction without understanding gives you a worse product that looks cleaner. Distillation is the work. Cutting is the shortcut.

## How to Apply

1. **Put design in the boardroom — literally.** The head of design should report to the CEO, not to the head of product. If design reports to product, design becomes a service organization that catches things at the end. If design reports to the CEO, design shapes the product from inception.

2. **Make the CEO care about the details.** The purpose of a company is to make a product. The person who cares most about the product should be the CEO. If the CEO doesn't review the work and doesn't have the taste to judge it, design-as-how-it-works collapses to design-as-decoration.

3. **Start with the experience, not the surface.** Before anyone draws a pixel, ask: what is this supposed to feel like to use? One plug, one cable. Then work backwards through every technical constraint until you can make that experience real.

4. **Peel the onion past the first answer.** The first solutions are complex. Live with the problem longer. The simple, elegant solution is what's left after you've fully understood it.

5. **Use design on business problems.** Your expense base is a design problem. Your pricing is a design problem. Your hiring process is a design problem. A company with fewer parts is a better company — and you get there through design thinking, not spreadsheet thinking.

6. **Kill design-as-service-org.** Design should not be a service organization unless that is explicitly the intention of the CEO — and if it is, the CEO should reconsider. Catching things at the end is a losing game. Put design in the room from the very beginning with engineering and product marketing.

7. **Refuse "make it look nicer" as a brief.** If the only feedback on a design is aesthetic, the reviewer hasn't engaged with the work. Send it back with real questions about how it works, not how it looks.

## Examples

**Situation:** The iMac design has a beautiful, no-visible-screw, single-piece shell. The engineers come back with 38 reasons it can't be built.
**Application:** "We're doing this." "Why?" "Because I'm the CEO and I think it can be done." The engineers begrudgingly do it.
**Result:** The iMac ships with the design. It's a big hit. The aesthetic isn't the point — the point is the one-plug-to-the-internet simplicity, the lack of setup friction, the way the machine integrates into a home. Design as how it works, pushed through by authority when the engineers defaulted to impossible.

**Situation:** A SaaS team wants to simplify their onboarding. Instinct: delete steps. Delete fields. Ship a shorter flow.
**Application:** Wrong move. Before you delete, deeply understand what each step is doing — what job it serves, who it serves. Only then decide what to strip. Distillation, not subtraction. What comes out is not a shorter flow; it's a coherent flow. Fewer parts, but the remaining parts carry the weight.
**Result:** Customers activate faster not because there are fewer steps, but because the steps that remain are exactly the ones that matter. That's what distillation does. Subtraction would have just produced a broken shorter flow.

**Situation:** A company has an expense base that's grown. The CFO is about to cut 10% across the board.
**Application:** That's not a design. That's a lawnmower. The question isn't "how do I cut 10%?" — it's "what is the right shape for this company's expenses to have?" Redesign the company to have fewer parts. Fewer projects, fewer people, fewer processes. Design the company itself. You can save more money that way than any CFO can cut across the board, because you've redesigned what the company *is* — not just how much it spends.
**Result:** The company doesn't just have lower expenses. It has a coherent expense base that matches the company's design. Every dollar is spent on something load-bearing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Have design report to product.
**Why:** Design becomes a service function that polishes what product already decided. The only thing design can do in that setup is catch things at the end — and catching things is a losing game. Design at the CEO level shapes the work from inception.

**Don't:** Outsource design to an agency.
**Why:** An agency doesn't wrap its arms around your company. They can't. They don't live in the details. You cannot ship a company's soul via purchase order.

**Don't:** Let "make it look better" be the feedback cycle.
**Why:** Design feedback should be about how the thing works — what the user experiences, what they have to do, what they have to understand. Aesthetic feedback in isolation is either taste-signaling or procrastination.

**Don't:** Confuse simplification with deletion.
**Why:** Deletion without understanding produces a worse product that looks cleaner. The team is proud of "shipping a simpler version" when what they actually shipped was a broken version. Distillation is the work.

**Don't:** Treat design as something engineers and PMs can do on the side.
**Why:** They can't. The training is different. The lens is different. The taste required takes years to develop. Put a real designer in the room — and make sure they report to the CEO.
