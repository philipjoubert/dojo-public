# The Product Line 2x2 — Four Great Things, Kill the Rest

## When to Use
- You've just taken over a company (or a division, or a product) and you can't tell your mother which thing to buy.
- The portfolio has accumulated variants over years — each reasonable when added, collectively a disaster.
- You have A-players but they're spread so thin that every project has a B-team.
- You're in a strategic review and the roadmap has thirty things on it and all of them look "important."
- Your own sales force can't articulate which product is for which customer, so they pitch every prospect the same deck.

## Fails When
- Your business genuinely needs many products for many segments. The 2x2 is a focus tool, not a universal template. Don't force it onto AWS or a consumer-packaged-goods portfolio or a bank's lending book.
- People draw the grid but don't actually kill what's outside it. Then the grid becomes a wall chart next to the same seventeen active projects. Focus requires the cut, not just the diagram.
- The cut becomes performative — slashing to look decisive, with no theory of what the survivors have to be. A 2x2 with four mediocre products in it is worse than seventeen mediocre products and at least some optionality.

## Core Concept

When I came back to Apple in 1997, I found a zillion and one products. It was amazing.

I started asking people questions. Why would I recommend a 3400 to somebody over a 4400? When should somebody jump up to a 6500 but not a 7300? What's the difference between the 4400 and the 7220? After three weeks I could not figure it out. And if I couldn't figure it out — I'd been in the business for twenty years — then how the hell were our customers supposed to figure it out?

They couldn't. That's why they weren't buying.

So I drew a simple chart on the whiteboard. Two columns, two rows.

Consumer. Professional.

Desktop. Portable.

Four products. That was it.

We reviewed the roadmap of new products and axed more than seventy percent of them, keeping the thirty percent that were gems. If we could make four great platforms — that was all we needed. We could put our A-team on every single one of them, instead of having B or C teams on any. We could turn them much faster. Marketing got simpler. The supply chain got simpler. The sales force finally knew what they were selling. And customers finally knew what they were buying.

The four products that came out of those four boxes over the next eighteen months were the iMac (consumer desktop), the Power Mac G3 (pro desktop), the iBook (consumer portable), and the PowerBook G3 (pro portable). A company that lost a billion dollars the year I came back was profitable within a year, and went on to become the most valuable company in the world. Not because we invented something new. Because we subtracted.

### What the Grid Actually Is

The 2x2 is not a branding exercise. It's not a planning artifact. It is a forcing function for a real strategic decision: *we will put our best people on exactly these things and nothing else*. The axes aren't magic. For Apple in 1997 they were Consumer/Pro × Desktop/Portable because that was the natural shape of the personal computer market at the moment. For a different business the axes will be different — or the grid will be a 3x1, or a single cell, or not a grid at all. The point is not the geometry. The point is clarity: one product per cell, A-team per product, everything else dead.

You know you have a good grid when you can hand it to a new sales hire and they can immediately tell a prospect which product is for them. If the grid requires a paragraph of explanation, you haven't simplified. You've just rearranged.

### Why Seventy Percent

We cut about seventy percent. That wasn't a target we picked in advance. It was what came out when we asked, honestly, product by product: is this a gem? The thirty percent that survived weren't the thirty percent that were "performing" — they were the ones where we could still credibly say "this could be the best thing in its category in the world." Everything else — good ideas that weren't great ideas, things that made sense individually but not for the company, products a team loved and had worked on for two years — got killed.

Focus requires sacrifice. You have to tell people who have been pouring their lives into a product that their product is dead. If you can't do that, you can't lead a turnaround. The cost of not cutting is that you protect one engineer's feelings at the price of the company.

### The A-Team Cascade

The reason the cut matters isn't money. You don't save much by killing a product that was going to fail anyway. The reason is that every project absorbs one or two A-players, and your A-players are the scarcest resource you have. Four projects × A-team beats seventeen projects × B-team every single time. The benefit of subtraction is the *concentration of talent* on what remains. This is why "just hire more" is the wrong answer. You can't buy fifteen A-teams. You can barely staff four. Cut to what you can actually do insanely well.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask the recommendation question.** Can you, the CEO, tell a friend which product to buy and why? If you can't articulate it in under a minute, your customers can't either. That is the problem.

2. **Draw the grid — but let the axes emerge.** Don't force Consumer/Pro × Desktop/Portable onto your business. Ask: what are the two most important dimensions along which my customers differ? Those are your axes. For a SaaS company it might be SMB/Enterprise × Self-serve/Sales-assisted. For a marketplace it might be Buyer/Seller × Domestic/Cross-border. Let the business tell you.

3. **One product per cell.** If a cell has three products today, you're choosing which one survives. Not merging them — choosing. Merging is usually a way to avoid the hard choice.

4. **Axe seventy percent of the roadmap.** Not the bad thirty percent — the merely good seventy percent. Keep the gems. If everything looks like a gem, you haven't been honest yet.

5. **Name what you killed, publicly and internally.** Be proud of it. "Here are the fifteen things we are no longer doing, so that the four remaining things can be great." This is the posture of focus. Hiding the cut is the posture of cowardice.

6. **Put the A-team on each survivor.** This is the payoff and the whole point. If you cut and don't concentrate talent, you've just done layoffs. Move your best people to the four that matter.

7. **Defend the grid in month three, month six, month twelve.** Complexity creeps back in. Salespeople will ask for a variant to close a deal. A customer will ask for a fifth product. A new exec will arrive with a "strategic adjacency." The grid is not a one-time event — it is a posture you hold.

## Examples

**Situation:** September 1997. Apple has about forty different Macintosh SKUs with numbers like 3400, 4400, 5500, 6500, 7200, 7220, 7300, 8500, 9500, plus clones licensed to third parties. The CEO himself can't explain the difference between them. The company lost a billion dollars the prior year and is months from insolvency.
**Application:** Draw the grid. Consumer/Pro × Desktop/Portable. Four cells. Axe the rest — including the Newton, the Pippin, the licensing program for the Mac OS (which was cannibalizing Apple's own hardware). Over 70% of the roadmap dies. Move the A-team onto the four survivors.
**Result:** The iMac ships in 1998 and becomes the best-selling computer of its generation. PowerBook and iBook follow. The company returns to profitability within a year and launches a decade of growth that ends with Apple as the most valuable company on earth. The iPod and iPhone — neither in the 2x2 — get added later, but only after the four cells were insanely great.

**Situation:** A Series C SaaS company has 18 modules in the platform, a dedicated PM per module, and no module that's world-class. Every prospect demo feels like a tour of an attic. Customers love two or three modules; the rest are low-usage or abandoned.
**Application:** Draw the grid. It turns out the natural shape is SMB/Enterprise × Workflow/Analytics. Pick one module per cell — the one most loved by that customer type. Kill (or deprecate to maintenance mode) the other fourteen modules. Consolidate the PMs and engineers onto the four survivors. Tell the sales team: we sell four things now.
**Result:** Prospect demos compress from 60 minutes to 15. NPS climbs because the four surviving modules finally get the investment they deserved. The company loses a handful of customers who loved a deprecated module — and wins far more because the pitch is finally clear. Revenue per employee doubles over two years.

**Situation:** A founder has built a successful consumer product with three major feature areas. The team wants to add three more. The roadmap for next year has all six expanding in parallel.
**Application:** Ask: of these six, which three could be world-class if the A-team gave them undivided attention for a year? Draw the grid. Put the survivors in the cells. Kill the rest. Tell the team we'll revisit in twelve months — maybe one of the dead ones comes back next year. But not this year. This year, four great things.
**Result:** Velocity on the survivors 3x what it was when spread across six. One of the killed features would have been a flop; the team didn't know it yet. Another dead one turns out to have been important — and gets added back in year two, built better, because the team has more capacity and clearer thinking.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Keep a product because a team has worked on it for two years.
**Why:** That's sunk cost, dressed up as respect for the team. Real respect is moving great people off a dying product onto one that can be insanely great. The team's work isn't wasted — the team isn't wasted. The product is wasted if it was never going to be a gem.

**Don't:** "Consolidate" three products into one mega-product.
**Why:** Merging is usually the cowardly version of the cut. You end up with a Frankenstein that has the sales-team confusion of three products with the mediocrity of B-team shipping. Better to kill two and make one of them the best in the world.

**Don't:** Leave the grid on a whiteboard and keep shipping the dead products at half speed.
**Why:** The grid is meaningless without the cut. A portfolio of four survivors plus a long tail of half-staffed legacy products is just a portfolio of nineteen products with a worse diagram. Either the cut is real or the exercise was theatre.

**Don't:** Pick axes that flatter existing products.
**Why:** If the axes are chosen so that everything you ship falls into a neat cell, you've drawn the grid around the answer you wanted. Pick axes that describe your *customer*, not your *portfolio*. Then see which of your products earn a cell.

**Don't:** Add a fifth cell to "be flexible."
**Why:** The discipline is in the two-by-two. The moment you make it a 2x3 or a 3x3, you're already letting complexity back in. If you genuinely need more cells, say so — but most of the time "we need a fifth cell" is code for "I don't want to kill this product."

**Don't:** Let the salespeople or finance people redraw the grid to add SKUs.
**Why:** Salespeople want more products because more products means more things to sell. Finance people want more products because more products means more optionality. Neither of them has to own the A-team's time. The CEO does. Protect the grid.
