# Product People Must Run Product Companies

## When to Use
- A board is picking a new CEO and the shortlist is heavy on sales, finance, and operations backgrounds.
- A once-great product company is in slow decline and nobody can quite explain it.
- You're a product person being asked to focus on "vision" while someone else runs the company.
- The CEO has stopped reviewing the product because "that's what the CPO is for."
- Process is expanding faster than product improvement.

## Fails When
- The business genuinely isn't product-led. Some categories win on distribution, sales, or capital. If the actual moat is sales, then sales people should probably run it.
- "Product person" gets defined as whoever the loudest founder wants. The framework collapses if it becomes political.
- A company has a product-person-in-title who long ago stopped caring about the work. Titles don't save companies; behavior does.

## Core Concept

Some very good product people invent some very good products, and the company achieves a monopoly. After that, the product people aren't the ones that drive the company forward anymore. It's the marketing guys or the ones who expand the business into Latin America or whatever. A different group of people starts to move up. And who usually ends up running the show? The sales guy.

The sales guy doesn't understand product. The sales guy understands complexity — more products mean more things to sell. The sales guy doesn't understand focus — focus means fewer products, which means fewer things to sell. And the product people get driven out of the decision-making forums, and the companies forget what it means to make great products. The product sensibility and the product genius that brought them to their position gets rotted out by people running the company who have no conception of a good product versus a bad product.

They have no conception of the craftsmanship required to take a good idea and turn it into a good product. And they really have no feeling in their hearts, usually, about wanting to help the customers.

That's what happened at Xerox. They had invented the future of computing at Xerox PARC — the graphical user interface, the mouse, object-oriented programming, networked computers. They had everything. The people at Xerox PARC used to call the executives "tonerheads" — copier executives who had no idea what they were looking at. And so Xerox grabbed defeat from the greatest victory in the computer industry. They could have been IBM. They could have been Microsoft. But the tonerheads couldn't understand what the product people had built.

That's what happens when sales people run product companies.

### The Sculley Disease

After I left Apple, John Sculley got a very serious disease. I've seen other people get it too. It's the disease of thinking that a really great idea is 90% of the work. That if you just tell all these other people "here's this great idea," they can go off and make it happen.

The problem is there's a tremendous amount of craftsmanship between a great idea and a great product. As you evolve that great idea, it changes and grows. It never comes out like it starts, because you learn a lot more as you get into the subtleties. You find there are tremendous trade-offs you have to make. There are certain things you can't make electrons do, certain things you can't make plastic do, or glass do, or factories do. Designing a product is keeping 5,000 things in your brain and fitting them together, continuing to push to fit them together in new and different ways.

John came from PepsiCo. At Pepsi, you at most change your product once every ten years. To them, a new product was a new size bottle. If you were a product person at PepsiCo, you couldn't change the course of that company very much. Who influenced PepsiCo's success? The sales and marketing people. That's who got promoted. That's who ran the company. For PepsiCo, maybe that was okay. But that's not how technology companies work. You can't sell your way to innovation. You can't market your way to breakthroughs. The product has to be great. And if the product people aren't in charge, the product won't be great.

### Process Over Content

Companies get confused. When they start getting bigger, they try to replicate their initial success. A lot of them think there's some magic in the process — that the success came from how things were done rather than what was done. So they start to institutionalize process across the company. Before very long, people get confused and think the process is the content.

That's the downfall of IBM. IBM has the best process people in the world. They just forgot about the content. And that's what happened at Apple too during the Sculley years. We had a lot of people who were great at management process. They just didn't have a clue about content. And in my career, the best people are the ones who really understand the content. They're a pain in the butt to manage because they push back and argue. You put up with it because they're so great at the content.

Process is important. You need good processes to run a large company. But process is in service of content. The moment process becomes more important than content — the moment the meeting about the product is more important than the product itself — you're in trouble.

## How to Apply

1. **The CEO must be the Chief Product Officer.** The purpose of a company is to make a product. Why would the person who knows the most about the product not be running the company? If you're the CEO and you can't articulate what makes your product great versus mediocre in this category, you're already in decline.

2. **Review the work personally, on a cadence.** Steve reviewed everything. Weekly, monthly, quarterly, depending on the area. If you as CEO don't see the work before it ships, you don't run a product company — you run a process company that happens to ship products.

3. **Protect the product people from the process people.** As the company scales, process people multiply faster than product people. Unless the CEO actively stands between them, the process people will gradually win every meeting, and the product people will leave.

4. **Refuse sales-led product decisions.** Sales always wants more products, more variants, more features to sell. Focus means fewer. The product function decides what gets built; sales decides how it gets sold. Confuse the two and you get the PCjr.

5. **Hire successors who live in the work.** When picking the next generation of leaders, bias toward the people who ship the product, not the people who manage the people who ship the product. Managers who can't do the work they manage are cavalry generals who can't ride a horse.

6. **Audit for the tonerhead.** Periodically ask: can the senior executives in this company actually explain what makes our product better than the alternatives? Not in slogans — in specifics. If they can't, they're running the company on process instinct, and the decline has already started.

## Examples

**Situation:** Xerox invents the graphical user interface, the mouse, object-oriented programming, and Ethernet at PARC in the 1970s. They could own personal computing.
**Application:** They don't. The copier executives (the "tonerheads") can't see what they have. The product people at PARC are ignored. Apple visits, sees the GUI, and ten minutes later knows all computers will eventually work this way. Xerox had the innovation; it just had the wrong people running the company.
**Result:** Xerox stays in copiers. Apple ships the Mac. Microsoft copies the Mac. An entire industry grows around ideas Xerox could have owned. The company that invented the future of computing becomes a footnote in it — because the tonerheads ran the company.

**Situation:** Apple in 1985–1997, under Sculley and his successors. Great ideas flow through PARC-like research groups. Nothing ships that matters. R&D spend is enormous. Output is incremental. The product evaporates.
**Application:** Why? Sculley came from Pepsi. He thought the idea was 90% of the work. He didn't understand the craftsmanship between a great idea and a great product. Product people got driven out of the decision-making forums. Process people inherited the company.
**Result:** Apple, the most innovative computer company in the world, was three months from bankruptcy in 1997. The fix wasn't strategy. It wasn't new financing. It was putting a product person back in the CEO chair.

**Situation:** A high-growth SaaS company has a founding CEO who's a product person. The company scales to 500 people. The board is pushing the founder to "focus on vision" and hire a COO to run operations. The COO will naturally rise toward CEO over time.
**Application:** Stop. The board is pattern-matching to Pepsi, where the sales/ops hire would be right. This is a product-led software company. The COO hire shifts the center of gravity away from product every year they're in the seat. Either find an operator who reports to the founder and is clearly not the succession candidate — or hire someone who is themselves a product person, and keep the decision rights about the work with a product person.
**Result:** Either the founder stays in the details and the product keeps getting better (founder mode, the Brian Chesky playbook), or the product slowly becomes average and the company declines from the inside out over five to ten years.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire a CEO with a strong sales or finance resume and no product taste for a product company.
**Why:** They will optimize for what they can see — revenue, process, distribution — and the product will decay under them because they can't judge it. Craftsmanship doesn't care about your quarterly target.

**Don't:** Let the CEO skip product review.
**Why:** "The CPO is handling it" is how the CEO stops knowing what's shipping. Once the CEO is a stranger to the work, every product decision becomes a budget or political decision, and quality exits.

**Don't:** Institutionalize process as a substitute for judgment.
**Why:** Process people will always sound reasonable. Their reasonableness is the problem. The best people understand the content and push back on the process. If they're losing those arguments, content is losing.

**Don't:** Measure the CEO on ability to run meetings.
**Why:** You want someone who can *ship the product*. Meeting fluency is a bonus, not a qualification. Some of the best product CEOs are terrible at conventional management theatre, and it doesn't matter.

**Don't:** Promote sales to product leadership.
**Why:** Sales is a discipline. Product is a discipline. They are not interchangeable. A great VP of Sales running product will build products that are easy to sell. Products that are easy to sell are rarely great.
