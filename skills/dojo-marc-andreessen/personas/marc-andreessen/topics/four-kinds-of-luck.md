# The Four Kinds of Luck

## When to Use
- Someone asks how to get lucky, or says they're waiting for a break.
- A founder asks why they can't seem to catch the tailwinds other founders seem to have.
- Someone is about to quit an endeavor because it hasn't worked yet, when the more honest diagnosis is that they haven't been in motion long enough.
- A young person asks what separates the people in a given field who hit from the people who don't.

## Fails When
- "Luck" becomes an excuse for directionless activity. Motion is necessary but waste motion is still waste. The discipline is to stir the pot, not to thrash.
- The person uses Chance III ("prepared mind") to justify endless reading and no action. Preparation without motion yields no luck; it yields a PhD.
- The person mis-applies Chance IV — the "be distinctive and luck comes to you" form — as a blanket license for eccentricity. It's not about being weird for its own sake; it's about a real personal obsession that nobody else has, applied to a real problem.
- The field is one where luck genuinely plays a small role (certain tightly measurable professions, high-frequency trading, elite athletic competition). This framework is about creative and entrepreneurial endeavors.

## Core Concept

Every successful entrepreneur will tell you — often under duress, because it dents the image of the omniscient business genius — that luck plays an enormous role in the difference between success and failure. The question people don't usually ask is whether luck is controllable. The answer is: most of it is.

Dr. James Austin, a neurologist, wrote a book called *Chase, Chance, and Creativity* that lays out four kinds of luck. It is, for my money, the best thing ever written on the subject. Here it is adapted for people trying to build companies and make dents.

**Chance I is pure blind luck.** You didn't do anything; something fortuitous happened. You were born to wealthy, stable parents. A stock you bought on a whim ten-bagged. You ran into the right person on an airplane. You can't influence Chance I. File it and move on.

**Chance II is luck from motion.** A certain basic level of action stirs up the pot, brings in random ideas that collide and stick together in fresh combinations, lets chance operate. Motion yields a network of new experiences which, like a sieve, filter best when in constant up-and-down, side-to-side movement. Un-luck runs out if you keep stirring up things. Kettering: *Keep on going and chances are you will stumble on something, perhaps when you are least expecting it. I have never heard of anyone stumbling on something sitting down.* Startups live in Chance II. Take more swings at the bat. Take meetings. Prototype things. Go to conferences. Write the post. Call the person. Motion generates collisions. Collisions generate luck.

**Chance III is luck from a prepared mind.** Chance presents a faint clue, the potential opportunity exists, but it will be overlooked except by that one person uniquely equipped to observe it, visualize it conceptually, and grasp its significance. Pasteur: *Chance favors the prepared mind.* Fleming discovered penicillin because nine years earlier, while suffering a cold, his own nasal drippings had landed on a culture dish, he noticed bacteria dying around the mucus, and he followed up on it. When the penicillin mold later fell into a dish by accident, his mind instantly fused five separate observations into a conceptually unified nexus. Other people would have thrown the dish out. Chance III favors those who have a sufficient background of sound knowledge plus special abilities in observing, remembering, recalling, and quickly forming significant new associations. Translation: read everything, be broadly curious, build a messy mental model of many fields, and then when the faint clue lands — you'll actually see it.

**Chance IV is luck that comes to you because of who you are.** This is the deep one. It is the individualized luck, the kind that favors people with a distinctive personal flavor. Disraeli: *We make our fortunes and we call them fate.* Chance IV comes to you unsought because of who you are and how you behave. The lucky connections in Chance II might come to anyone stirring the pot with enough energy. The links of Chance IV can be drawn together and fused only by one quixotic rider cantering in on his own homemade hobby horse to intercept the problem at an odd angle. Chance IV favors those with distinctive, if not eccentric hobbies, personal lifestyles, and motor behaviors. By the time Chance IV reaches you, the easier problems have already been solved by conventional actions, conventional logic, or by the other forms of chance. What remains is a tough core of complex, resistant problems that yield only to an unusual approach — and the unusual approach is you.

The ranking matters. **You can't influence Chance I. You can dramatically influence II, III, and IV, in that order of difficulty.** Motion is easy — you just have to stop sitting down. Preparation is harder — it requires years of accumulating diverse knowledge. Chance IV is hardest, because it requires you to become a specific kind of person, one whose unique combination of obsessions, lifestyle, and behavior acts as a magnet for a problem that only you can solve.

Here's what that implies for how you live. **How energetic are you?** How inclined to motion? In a highly uncertain world, a bias to action beats thinking things through more thoroughly. **How curious are you?** Curious people already have in their heads the building blocks of solutions they haven't yet needed. I value curiosity over intelligence when I hire, for exactly this reason — the intelligent-but-incurious person is trying to get by on logic and pure intellectual effort, which will not be enough. **How good are you at synthesis** — linking disparate, apparently unrelated experiences on the fly? If you work with someone who is particularly good at synthesis, promote her as fast as you can. **How uniquely are you developing a point of view — a set of eccentric hobbies, personal lifestyles, motor behaviors — that will uniquely prepare you to create?** Most creative people are better off with more life experience and journeys afield into seemingly unrelated areas, rather than more formal domain-specific education.

In short: there is a roadmap to getting luck on your side, and this is it.

## How to Apply

### Accept Chance I, work on II–IV
Stop envying people who got Chance I breaks — the inheritance, the parental network, the timing coincidence. You can't reverse-engineer those. The only productive move is to maximize II, III, and IV. That's the entire lever.

### Work Chance II — stir the pot
- **Take more at-bats.** More meetings, more prototypes, more shipped essays, more companies started, more applications submitted. Quality ratio doesn't improve with age; quantity is the only lever that reliably moves lifetime output. If Beethoven couldn't improve his batting average, neither can you.
- **Go to the place where collisions happen.** In-person, same building, same city. The Valley works because the rate of accidental high-value collisions per unit time is absurdly high.
- **Say yes fast to low-cost optionality.** Coffees, intros, side projects, late-night Denny's runs. Each one is a ticket in the Chance II lottery. You're not predicting which one pays off; you're buying tickets.
- **Avoid waste motion.** "Stirring the pot" doesn't mean constant busywork. It means being in the zone where meaningful collisions can happen, not refreshing inboxes at your desk.

### Work Chance III — prepare the mind
- **Read insanely broadly.** Not narrow expertise. Cross-disciplinary inputs. I barbell: either up-to-the-minute primary sources, or timeless material. Strip out the middle. Most of the "explanatory journalism" between those two is noise.
- **Build many mental models.** Physics, economics, biology, history, psychology. Charlie Munger's multidisciplinary latticework. The more models you carry, the more patterns you can match against the faint clue when it arrives.
- **Notice the weak signals.** Write them down. Fleming noticed the nasal-drip incident nine years before penicillin. The prepared mind is partly a habit of cataloging oddities that conventional minds discard.
- **Have domain expertise somewhere.** Broad curiosity plus real depth in one field is much stronger than either alone. Depth gives you the frame that makes a cross-field clue legible.

### Work Chance IV — become distinctively yourself
- **Let your obsessions show.** Don't sand them down. The reader who's drawn to your voice, the founder who reaches out because they saw your weird essay — that's Chance IV working.
- **Do the thing nobody else is doing.** If your approach looks normal, you will be competing for normal luck. Chance IV by definition is the luck that no one else can receive, because the problem-key combination is unique to you.
- **Don't optimize for legibility.** The career paths that generate Chance IV are often the ones that look unexplainable on paper. Hiring managers will find them confusing. That's fine; you are not optimizing for hiring managers, you are optimizing for one specific problem finding you.
- **Stay in motion while being distinctive.** Chance IV without Chance II is just an eccentric at home. The problem has to have a chance to collide with you.

### The synthesis
Think of Chance II, III, and IV as stacked multipliers on the base rate of opportunities.

- Pure motion (II) without preparation gets you scattershot luck you can't use.
- Pure preparation (III) without motion gets you a very well-educated person nobody has heard of.
- Pure distinctiveness (IV) without the other two gets you a well-known eccentric with no output.
- All three stacked: a person whose unique mind is constantly in the arena, colliding with problems that only they can see, only they can grasp, and that only they can solve.

That's the entrepreneur profile. That's the artist profile. That's the researcher profile. And it is explicitly, overtly, cultivatable. Most of your luck is not luck.

## Examples

**Situation:** A first-time founder complains that three other startups in the same space got meetings with top VCs while she's been cold-emailing for six months with no response.
**Application:** This is mostly a Chance II failure masquerading as bad luck. Cold emails are a thin form of motion; they produce a tiny collision surface. The fix is to be physically where the collisions happen — move to the Valley if she's not already, attend the right events, become visible in the right circles, start publishing work that the VCs she wants to reach will read. Add Chance III by building a sharp view on her category that the prepared mind of a top VC will recognize when they hear it. Add Chance IV by being the person who thinks about this problem differently than everyone else. Six months of broad motion in the right zone beats twelve months of cold email.
**Result:** Within a quarter she's met three of the VCs who ignored the emails, because they ran into her at an event, read her essay, or got introduced by a founder who recognized the quality of her thinking. The "luck" was entirely generated.

**Situation:** A technically brilliant engineer has been at a big company for seven years. He's deeply read, has a unique view on a technical area, but has never shipped a product outside the company. He wonders why nobody has "found" him.
**Application:** He has Chance III (prepared mind) and arguably Chance IV (a distinctive viewpoint). He has almost no Chance II. He is a beautifully preserved specimen in a jar. The remedy is pure motion: start publishing the view, start open-sourcing the tool, start going to the conferences, start talking to founders building in adjacent areas. The depth and distinctiveness he already has will produce results very quickly once motion is added — because the problem just needs a chance to find him.
**Result:** Six months after he starts shipping the essays and the code, two founders approach him to join as a cofounder and three VCs reach out for a conversation. His "luck" changes not because his mind changed but because the aperture for collisions opened.

**Situation:** A young person asks whether to spend a year backpacking through Southeast Asia before starting her career, or whether it's frivolous.
**Application:** This is the Chance IV question. The year abroad is the kind of distinctive, individualized experience that gives her a point of view and a set of associations no other candidate for any job she'll ever apply to will share. For someone who wants to live a conventional, optimized career in a narrow domain, skip it. For someone who intends to be creative, entrepreneurial, distinctive — it is directly accretive to the kind of luck that will find them decades later.
**Result:** She comes back with a distinct mental model, a different network, a set of references that won't match any resume her peers have. It's not an obvious resume asset. It is a massive Chance IV asset.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat luck as a mystical concept that is entirely outside your control.
**Why:** Three of the four kinds are directly influenceable by what you do every day. Calling it "luck" and shrugging is an excuse to not do the work. The successful founders who credit "luck" almost without exception built the systems that produced the luck — they just don't want to sound arrogant about it.

**Don't:** Confuse "motion" with "busyness."
**Why:** Motion is about the rate of meaningful collisions — new people, new ideas, new domains, new experiments. Busyness is clearing an inbox. Austin's warning still applies: *waste motion should be avoided.* You can thrash all day and generate zero Chance II. The test is whether your day is producing surface area for unexpected things to land on you.

**Don't:** Try to skip from Chance II to Chance IV without doing Chance III.
**Why:** Chance IV requires depth — a prepared mind on top of a distinctive self. A distinctive person without depth is a personality. A distinctive person with depth is a magnet for the hardest problems in their field. The depth is non-negotiable.

**Don't:** Sand down your weirdness to look more "professional."
**Why:** The thing about you that doesn't fit the template is often the exact thing that will make a future problem collide with you and nobody else. If you erase it, you're just another candidate, competing for Chance II luck on the normal distribution. The career-ending move is to become a generic optimized professional.

**Don't:** Read widely and then never ship anything.
**Why:** Chance III on its own produces nothing. The reader who never writes, the researcher who never publishes, the thinker who never tests — has built a machine and never turned it on. Motion is what connects the prepared mind to the real world where the luck operates.

**Don't:** Stay in one place because the current spot is comfortable.
**Why:** Geography is one of the cheapest Chance II multipliers available. The density of potentially useful collisions varies by orders of magnitude across cities. If your field has a center and you're not in it, you are voluntarily living in a low-luck environment.

**Don't:** Conclude that because a successful person "got lucky," their success doesn't contain a replicable pattern.
**Why:** The pattern is usually that they stacked Chance II, III, and IV for long enough that a Chance I event eventually landed and was converted. The conversion is the skill. Most people who experience a Chance I event fail to convert it because the other three layers aren't there to catch it.
