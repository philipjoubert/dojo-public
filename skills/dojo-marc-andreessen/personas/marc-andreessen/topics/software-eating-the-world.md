# Software Is Eating the World

## When to Use
- A founder is trying to pick an industry to attack and needs a lens for which industries are ripe for a software-native rewrite.
- An operator inside a "traditional" industry — automotive, banking, retail, logistics, insurance, healthcare, real estate, education, defense — is asking whether their company can survive a decade more without becoming a software company.
- A venture investor is pitching a deal in a vertical that doesn't look like tech and needs the civilizational argument for why this is, in fact, tech.
- A regulator or journalist is arguing that tech is a "sector" rather than an expanding frontier. The right response is: software is the control layer for *everything*. There is no "tech sector" vs. "non-tech sector" anymore — every sector is on a clock.
- Someone is trying to understand why the incumbents, who have all the advantages — capital, brand, distribution, regulatory relationships — still keep losing. The answer is structural and knowable.

## Fails When
- The category is genuinely bottlenecked on atoms, not bits, and software can only shave the margin. Heavy industrials with deep capital moats, long asset lives, and legally-mandated physical compliance are slower to fall — not immune, but slower. The thesis is directional, not a literal time-stamp.
- The user reduces it to "everything tech wins." Software eats industries through specific mechanisms; if you can't name the mechanism, you're cargo-culting.
- Applied to a specific startup as a success guarantee. "We're doing software for X" does not equal "we will win." You still need product-market fit, distribution, capital, and the right team. The thesis tells you the *game* is on; it does not tell you which *player* wins.
- Used to dismiss incumbents entirely. Some incumbents do adapt. The race framing — startup distribution vs. incumbent innovation — leaves open the possibility that the incumbent gets there first. Rare, but real.

## Core Concept

In 2011 I wrote an essay in the Wall Street Journal titled *Why Software Is Eating the World*. The thesis was simple: we are in the middle of a dramatic and broad technological and economic shift in which software companies are poised to take over large swaths of the economy. More than a decade later, this has turned out to be correct — and I think we are still only partway through the story. The AI wave is the next turn of the same wheel.

Here is the underlying mechanism, in three stages. This is the structure.

**Stage 1: The product is transformed from non-software to software.** Music compact discs become MP3s and then streams. An alarm clock goes from a physical device on your bedside table to an app on your phone. A camera goes from a body-plus-lens-plus-film to firmware inside a phone. Mail goes from paper to email. A taxi dispatcher goes from a radio-plus-paper to a matching algorithm. A car goes from bent metal and glass, to software wrapped in bent metal and glass. The *what it is* changes. This is the first move, and it is the one people usually notice first.

**Stage 2: The producer is transformed from a manufacturing or media or financial services or retail company to (entirely or mainly) a software company.** Their core capability becomes creating and running software. This is a very different discipline and culture from what they used to do. A retailer is now a logistics network with software on top. A bank is now a balance sheet wrapped in software. A car company is now a software platform with wheels. A hotel chain is now a booking engine. A taxi company is now a two-sided marketplace with drivers and riders as the two sides. The *what the company is* changes. This is harder and people notice it more slowly.

**Stage 3: As software redefines the product, and assuming a competitive market not protected by a monopoly position or regulatory capture, the nature of competition in the industry changes until the best software wins — which means the best software company wins.** The best software company may be an incumbent or a startup. It is *whoever makes the best software*. This is the stage that kills incumbents. Not "someone digitizes part of our process." The end state: the rules of the game in your industry are now the rules of software, and the winners are whoever is best at software.

My partner Alex Rampell frames the endgame perfectly: *"Competition between an incumbent and a software-driven startup is a race, where the startup is trying to get distribution before the incumbent gets innovation."* The incumbent starts with giant advantages — existing customer base, brand, distribution, regulatory relationships, capital. The software startup also starts with a giant advantage — a culture built to create software from the start, with no need to adapt an older culture designed to bend metal, shuffle paper, or answer phones. That is the whole race.

And as time passes, I am increasingly skeptical that most incumbents can adapt. The culture shift is just too hard. Great software people tend not to want to work at an incumbent where the culture is not optimized to them, where they are not in charge. It is proving easier in many cases to just start a new company than try to retrofit an incumbent. A good test: count the percent of top 100 executives and managers with computer science degrees. For a typical tech startup, the answer might be 50–70%. For a typical incumbent, the answer is more like 5–7%. This is a *huge* gap in software knowledge and skill, and you see it play out across industries every day.

The legacy car CEOs used to come to me and say: "Oh, you Californians, it's nice and cute that you're doing all this stuff with software, but you don't understand the car industry is about the real world. It's about atoms and it's about steel and it's about glass and rubber and it's about cars that have to last for 200,000 miles and function in the snow. You guys test your electric self-driving cars in the California weather, wait till you have a car on the road in Detroit." That argument is the universal argument of every incumbent in every industry that is about to lose. Publishing said it about the web. Hotels said it about Airbnb. Taxis said it about Uber. Banks said it about fintech. Defense primes are saying it right now about software-first defense companies. It is always the same sentence: *you don't understand our industry, it's about real things.*

The sentence is both true and irrelevant. Yes, there are atoms. Yes, regulation is real. Yes, 200,000 miles in Detroit snow is a real requirement. And the software-first player will build to those requirements, because that is table stakes. But the *structure* of competition, the *velocity*, the *feedback loops*, the *cost curves*, the *margin economics* — those are now governed by software. The incumbents are bringing bent metal to a software fight.

Why do the incumbents lose so reliably? Three structural reasons.

First, **the culture is wrong.** A company designed to optimize a manufacturing line or a sales force or a branch network is not a company that can ship software on two-week cycles with the culture of iteration and failure-tolerance that requires. Slapping "Digital" on a VP's title does not make them a CTO. Hiring one McKinsey-sourced "Chief Digital Officer" does not rewire a 50,000-person org. Culture is the operating system; you cannot install a new app on top of the wrong OS and expect it to run.

Second, **the incentives are wrong.** Incumbents have to defend margin, which means they have to defend pricing. Software entrants can, and do, destroy pricing because they are optimizing for market share over margin. The incumbent's CFO cannot, politically, approve dropping the price by 80%. The startup's CFO doesn't exist yet. The startup wins the early adopters and then moves up market while the incumbent is still deciding whether to respond.

Third, **the talent flows the other way.** The best software people in the world want to work in an environment where software is the point, where software people are in charge, where decisions are made on software timelines. The best software people do not want to spend their careers inside a 50-year-old company explaining to the board why the "digital transformation" is taking another year. The talent flows to the startups. Over a decade, this is decisive.

The operational implication for *you* is simple.

**If you are in a "non-tech" industry: software is coming for you.** It will eat your industry on a clock you can roughly predict by looking at how much of your product has already become software, how many of your executives have CS degrees, and how hard your regulatory moat actually is. If you are a CEO of an incumbent, your only viable move is to become a software company. Not to "do a digital transformation." To actually become a software company — meaning your top 100 execs are 50–70% CS, your product roadmap is owned by engineering, your capital allocation favors software teams over the legacy divisions, and you accept margin compression as the cost of survival. Most incumbents will not do this. That is why most incumbents lose.

**If you are building software: go hunt an industry that hasn't been eaten yet.** Look at the map. Which industries are in Stage 1 (product partly software)? Which are in Stage 2 (producer becoming a software company)? Which are still pre-Stage 1, where the product hasn't even become software yet? Those pre-Stage 1 industries are where the most upside is — they are the next Uber, the next Airbnb, the next Stripe, the next SpaceX. The rule: the further behind the industry, the larger the prize, and the harder the execution. Heavy industrials, infrastructure, biotech, defense, government, construction, agriculture — these are still in the foothills of the thesis. Go there.

AI is not a different thesis. AI is the next turn of the same wheel. AI is software that writes software, makes decisions, runs the control loops. AI expands the set of things that can be expressed as software — meaning the number of industries on the map that are *about* to enter Stage 1 just exploded. Every customer-facing interaction is now eligible. Every back-office process is now eligible. Every skilled-labor judgment is now eligible. Software was eating the world before AI. With AI, the pace accelerates, and the footprint expands. If you found the 2011 thesis persuasive, you should find the 2025 thesis overwhelming.

## How to Apply

1. **Drop any industry into the three-stage model and locate it.** Is the product still mostly physical/paper/analog (pre-Stage 1)? Is the product now mostly software but the producers are still old (Stage 1)? Are the producers now software companies but the competitive dynamic still looks like the old industry (Stage 2)? Has the "best software wins" dynamic fully taken over (Stage 3)? Based on where it is, you know roughly what comes next.

2. **Count the CS degrees.** The percentage of the top 100 executives with CS or adjacent engineering degrees is the single cleanest leading indicator of whether an incumbent will make it. Below 10%: they will not survive. 10–30%: coin flip, depends on the CEO. 30%+: they're at least in the game. This is a napkin metric and it is ruthlessly predictive.

3. **Run the Rampell race for any incumbent/challenger pair.** The startup is racing to get distribution. The incumbent is racing to get innovation. Whoever completes their race first wins. Ask: how close is the startup to the incumbent's distribution? How close is the incumbent to the startup's innovation? The gap that is closing fastest is usually distribution — distribution compounds once PMF hits. Innovation at a big company rarely compounds. Bet accordingly.

4. **When an incumbent protests "but our industry is about real things," translate the sentence.** They are saying: "The things that protected us in the past — physical footprint, regulatory scar tissue, long-cycle capital, relationships — are still protective." Ask which of those four are *actually* software-proof. Usually at most one. The rest erode on a decade timeline. Map the erosion.

5. **For founders: prioritize industries where the incumbents still have the argument memorized.** The louder the incumbent CEO says "you don't understand our industry," the more confident you should be that you are looking at a Stage 1–Stage 2 industry in year 1–3 of the transition. The incumbent's confidence is itself evidence. When they're genuinely scared and humble, it's often already too late to be the winner — the incumbent has started to adapt. The sweet spot is incumbents who still think it's cute.

6. **Ask the regulatory-capture question hard.** The thesis assumes "a competitive market not protected by a monopoly position or regulatory capture." Some industries have genuine regulatory moats — banking, defense, healthcare, utilities — where the incumbents can bend the state to block software entrants. That doesn't mean software doesn't win eventually; it means the timeline stretches, and the winners are usually a combination of (a) startups that attack an unregulated sliver and expand, or (b) startups that build a better regulatory strategy than the incumbents. Know which game you're playing.

7. **Recognize AI as Stage 0 for a wave of new industries.** AI expands the set of things expressible as software. Any industry where the key activity is human judgment — legal, medical, financial, creative, design, diagnostics, planning — is now eligible for Stage 1 in the next few years. If you are a founder and you are looking for a blue ocean, look at the judgment-bound industries. If you are an incumbent in one of those industries, you have less time than you think.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder asks whether to build a software-first insurance company or stick with their current B2B SaaS idea.
**Application:** "Insurance is a late Stage 1 industry. The product is already mostly software — policies, claims, actuarial models, distribution — but the producers are still old companies with old cultures. Top 100 exec CS-degree percentage at most insurers is under 5%. That's an enormous delta. The race is on. Some of the incumbents have hired token CTOs, but none of them have genuinely become software companies. The prize is massive — insurance is one of the largest industries on earth. The execution is hard because of regulatory capture in specific lines. Pick a line with the thinnest regulatory moat — renters, pet, specialty — and attack there. Once you have distribution, expand. If you already have PMF in your SaaS idea, finish that. If you don't, insurance is the higher-prize hunt."
**Result:** Founder stops hunting generic SaaS and targets a specific insurance line with clear theory of distribution.

**Situation:** A defense prime CEO is asking whether the new software-first defense companies (Anduril, Palantir, Shield AI) are a real threat or a phase.
**Application:** "They are the threat. The Pentagon is realizing that every modern weapon is a software platform with ordnance attached. Your company ships hardware on 20-year procurement cycles. They ship software on 6-month release cycles. Your top 100 execs are maybe 3% CS. Theirs are 60%. You have distribution, brand, decades of Pentagon relationships. They are racing for that. You have all the innovation — on paper. But count your shipping software engineers. Count theirs. Now count what percentage of your capital budget goes to them vs. yours. The race is running. You do not lose tomorrow. You lose in ten years, and the decision that decides it is whether *you* become a software company now, or try to use regulation and lobbying to freeze the market. Lobbying only buys years — and those years, the talent is fleeing to the other side. Pick."
**Result:** The CEO either doubles down on becoming a software company or commits to regulatory warfare. Both are rational; at least the choice is clarified.

**Situation:** A journalist is arguing that "tech has peaked" because mature sectors like ride-sharing and streaming are consolidating.
**Application:** "Tech has not peaked. *A specific generation* of tech has matured. But software is not a sector — it is a substrate that is eating every other sector. Ride-sharing maturing is not the end of the thesis; it's evidence for it. The wheel turns. Now AI is Stage 0 for a dozen more industries simultaneously — law, medicine, finance, creative, design, logistics, defense, education. The map of what is software is expanding, not shrinking. You are looking at the wrong end of the telescope. The right question is not 'has tech peaked.' The right question is 'which industry is next, and how soon.' I'll give you a list."
**Result:** The journalist either takes the frame or doesn't, but the frame is now in their head and it will come back the next time they're writing about an "adjacent" sector.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply the thesis as an investment shortcut.
**Why:** "Software is eating industry X" is a claim about the game, not a claim about any specific player. Many software startups attack eaten industries and still fail — because they have the wrong team, wrong PMF, wrong distribution, wrong capital structure. The civilizational thesis is correct on a ten-year horizon. The specific company you're about to invest in is a much harder question. Don't let the macro overwhelm the micro.

**Don't:** Ignore the cultural impedance of the incumbent.
**Why:** Some incumbents actually do make the transition. Microsoft did. Disney mostly has. Visa did. The rare incumbents that make it do so because a CEO with actual conviction spent a decade rewiring the culture — usually firing the old guard, buying a software company, putting engineers on the board, and accepting five years of margin pain. If you're advising an incumbent, the question is not "should you transform" but "does your CEO have the guts to fire his friends." If not, the trajectory is decline.

**Don't:** Assume the software entrant always wins.
**Why:** The Rampell race is a race. Sometimes the incumbent gets there first. Sometimes the regulatory moat holds for decades. Sometimes the startup fails at distribution. The thesis describes the dominant vector; it does not describe every outcome. If you are a founder, respect the race. If you are an incumbent, respect that you *can* win it, but only if you actually run.

**Don't:** Use "software eating the world" to dismiss physical-world craftsmanship.
**Why:** The thesis is not that atoms don't matter. Cars still have to drive 200,000 miles. Hospitals still have to treat patients. Reactors still have to be built. The thesis is that the *control layer* becomes software. The atoms are still there, harder and more important than ever — but the competitive structure is governed by who is best at software. Don't lose the atoms in the argument; the best software company also has to do the atoms.

**Don't:** Forget that AI is a continuation, not a contradiction.
**Why:** Some people argue "AI is the end of software" or "AI kills SaaS." That's a category error. AI *is* software. It is software that makes more things expressible as software. The 2011 thesis was about software eating industries where the product could be digitized. The AI thesis is about software eating industries where the product could be *judged* — legal reasoning, medical diagnosis, creative work, planning. Same wheel, wider footprint, faster turn. Don't let "AI is different" be an excuse to stop running the same playbook.

**Don't:** Preach the thesis to incumbents who are too far gone.
**Why:** Some incumbents are dead and don't know it yet. Going to them and saying "you need to become a software company" with five years left on the runway is not going to produce a software company — it's going to produce a panicked acquisition spree that wastes the remaining cash. If you're advising and the patient is already terminal, the honest advice is: maximize dividends, harvest the brand, and accept the decline with dignity. Not every business that will be eaten is worth saving. The thesis is descriptive, not a miracle cure.
