# Third-Party Decisions — Who Pays the Price for Being Wrong

## When to Use
- Hiring a consultant, strategy firm, or external advisor who will propose changes.
- Evaluating a policy, process, or rule imposed by someone who does not operate under it — corporate on the field, HQ on the branch, compliance on the line, the board on the business.
- Any situation where the decision-maker and the bearer of consequences are different people.
- Designing approval, review, or gatekeeping structures. Deciding who needs to sign off on what.
- Any moment a confident recommendation is being given by someone who will not be there in eighteen months to face the result.

## Fails When
- A genuinely disinterested check is what the situation needs — an auditor who is *supposed* to have no skin, precisely so they will ask the uncomfortable question. The discipline is to know when insulation is the design and when it is the bug.
- The outsider has more relevant information than the operator. Rare, but it happens: the friend visiting the family business sees the dysfunction the family cannot see. Weight the outsider as a fresh perspective, not a verdict.
- The "third party" turns out to have skin. An investor with board seat and capital at risk is not a third party — their career and fund track it. A co-founder not currently operating is not a third party — their equity does.

## Core Concept

The question to ask of any decision is not "is it the right decision?" It is who is making the decision, and what happens to that person if they are wrong.

Every decision has three parties: the person who makes it, the person who bears the consequences, and — increasingly in modern organizations — a third party observing from outside who imposes preferences on the first two. When the decision-maker and the bearer of consequences are the same person, mistakes get corrected because the cost lands where the power lives. When they are different, systematic error is the expected outcome, because the feedback never reaches the person who could change course.

A consultant recommends a restructuring. If the restructuring works, the consultant adds it to their deck. If it fails, "implementation challenges are cited." The consultant's fee is unchanged. Their reputation in the consulting market is unchanged, because they are in that market, not yours. The people who bear the consequences — the people who were restructured, the people who inherited the resulting dysfunction, the customers who felt the degradation in service — have no way to reach the consultant, and the consultant has no way to feel them.

This is not a characterological criticism of consultants. It is a structural observation. The structure insulates the decision-maker from the consequence, and the structure is what you must diagnose.

The same logic applies, in varying degrees, to: corporate executives making rules for field operations they have never run; board members with no money at risk voting on strategy; committee members signing off on hires they will never work with; policy authors writing rules for employees they will never meet; industry analysts pronouncing on companies they do not own; central functions imposing process on line businesses they do not operate.

In each case, the confidence of the decision-maker is uncalibrated by consequence. Confidence without consequence is free. It will be supplied generously.

## The Three Questions

Before accepting any significant decision — or before making one yourself about someone else's operation — ask three questions in order. They take thirty seconds. They will save you years.

**Who is making this decision?** Name the specific person or group. Not "the company," not "the committee," not "policy." A specific human being, or a small group of them, is choosing. Identify them.

**Who will bear the consequences if it is wrong?** Again, specifically. Not "the organization," not "the team." Which individuals will lose money, lose sleep, lose customers, lose jobs, lose a year of their lives undoing the decision?

**Are these the same people?** If yes, the decision has the right structure — the cost lands where the power lives, and if the decision is wrong, feedback will reach the person who can change course. If no, the decision is structurally insulated, and you should expect systematic error regardless of how smart or well-intentioned the decision-maker is.

Most bad decisions in large organizations, and a surprising number in small ones, fail the third question.

## Why Insulation Produces Systematic Error

It is not that third parties are stupid or malicious. Most are neither. The problem is that they are solving a different problem than the one in front of you.

A consultant's job is to produce a recommendation that sounds good in a boardroom. That is what they are paid for. It is what the market selected them for. If their recommendations also work, that is a bonus, but it is not what their income depends on — their income depends on getting hired for the *next* engagement, which depends on how the current one looked at the time.

A corporate rule-maker's job is to produce a rule that survives review by peers and legal. That is what they are paid for. The rule's effect on the field operator — who must now fill out another form, or wait three days for approval, or route around the rule because the rule makes the work impossible — is not part of the rule-maker's performance review.

A board member's job is to satisfy fellow board members and preserve their own position. That is what they are paid (in equity and fees) for. Whether a specific recommendation works eighteen months later, when the quarterly cycle has moved on, is not something the board member directly experiences.

In each case, the person is doing their job. The job just isn't the job you thought it was.

This is why so many organizations produce absurd outcomes with intelligent people at every desk. The intelligence is being deployed against an incentive structure that isn't pointed at the outcome you care about.

## The Elite Substitution Move

A particular form of third-party decision-making has become so common it deserves its own name. Call it elite substitution: the move by which someone with credentials, confidence, and articulation substitutes their judgment for the judgment of people with direct knowledge and direct stakes.

Experts on forestry at the turn of the twentieth century confidently predicted a timber famine within decades. Their forestry was sound. Their economics was not — they did not understand that prices rising would encourage conservation and substitution. The famine did not come. The confident prediction did. It had to, because the prediction cost the experts nothing to make and paid them attention in return.

Experts on city planning today design transit systems, zoning codes, and density rules for neighborhoods they will not live in. The residents — who have direct knowledge of how the streets actually work, where the foot traffic goes, what the neighborhood actually needs — are told their views are parochial, NIMBY, insufficiently informed by "best practices." The planners' careers are unaffected if the plans fail. The residents live in the result.

In your company, the same move happens daily. A product executive with an MBA and no operating history in your category overrides the lead who has run the product for three years. A strategy team from outside the business proposes a pivot, supported by analysis the operating team finds thin. A policy team at headquarters writes a rule that makes sense on paper and is impossible in the field.

The third party has articulation. The operator has knowledge. When articulation wins the meeting, the decision goes to the articulate party and the consequence goes to the operator. A month later the operator is called into another meeting to explain why results are disappointing.

## The Decision-Structure Audit

When you inherit or design a process, audit its decision structure by running this exercise for each major decision point.

1. **Name the decision.** "Who gets hired into this role." "Whether to extend credit to this customer." "What features ship this quarter."

2. **Name the decider.** A specific title or person. If it's a committee, name each member.

3. **Name the bearer.** Who lives with the outcome? Sometimes several people — the hiring manager, the team, the customer, whoever manages the work.

4. **Score the overlap.** How much does the decider's fortune move with the outcome? Fully (same person), partially (some accountability but delayed or diluted), or not at all (the decider moves on)?

5. **Name the feedback path.** How does the decider find out whether the decision was right or wrong? Timeline, channel, specificity.

Decisions with full overlap and fast feedback will be roughly right over time; even when individual calls are wrong, the system corrects itself. Decisions with no overlap and no feedback will drift into systematic error. The drift is invisible for a while — people adapt around the bad decisions, work extra hours to absorb the friction, and the decider congratulates themselves on how well the rule works. Then the talented operators leave, and suddenly the drift becomes visible all at once.

The remedy is not always to move the decision; sometimes that isn't possible. The remedy is always to close the feedback loop: to ensure that the decider, whoever they are, hears directly and specifically from the bearer when the decision was wrong, in a way they cannot dismiss or filter.

## "But They're the Experts"

The standard defense of a third-party decision is that the third party has expertise. The operator, by implication, does not.

Two things to note.

First, expertise does not transfer across domains. The consultant with a decade at McKinsey has expertise in running a consulting engagement. That is not the same as expertise in running your company. The retired general has expertise in military command. That is not expertise in running a sales team, however much the metaphors overlap. The academic has expertise in publishing papers about organizations. That is not expertise in running one. When expertise is invoked, ask: expertise in what, precisely, tested how, with what track record?

Second, even genuine expertise does not substitute for the specific, local, mundane knowledge the operator has. The operator knows that this customer always calls on Fridays, that the account manager is pregnant, that the integration broke last time with this configuration, that the sales rep is junior and will over-promise if left unsupervised. None of this appears in the consultant's report, because it cannot — it is too specific, too dispersed, too embedded in ongoing relationships. But it is what the decision actually depends on, and the operator has it.

Expert knowledge and operator knowledge are different kinds of knowledge. Both matter. When you substitute one for the other, you lose the one you substituted away.

## When the Third Party Is Actually Useful

Third parties are not always bad, and the discipline here is not to ignore them. It is to know what they are good for and how to use them.

A third party with no skin is useful when you specifically want someone whose judgment is not polluted by the local politics. An auditor is designed to be a third party — that is the feature, not the bug. A new hire is a third party for their first few months, and their fresh eyes can catch what long-timers have stopped seeing. An outside advisor who genuinely has no axe to grind can sometimes say the uncomfortable thing the insiders cannot.

The discipline is to treat what they say as a perspective, not a verdict. Weight it appropriately. Test it against what operators know. Never let it override direct operating judgment without strong supporting reason. And — most importantly — never structure the decision so that the third party's recommendation becomes binding, because then you have given control to someone who will not bear the consequence, and you have structurally insulated the decision.

A useful third party advises. A harmful third party decides.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the three questions on every significant decision.** Who is deciding? Who will bear the consequences if it is wrong? Are these the same people? Thirty seconds of honest answers surfaces whether the decision has the right structure or is structurally insulated.
2. **Audit the decider-bearer overlap.** For each recurring decision in your operation (hiring, expenditure, policy, strategy), name the specific decider, name the specific bearer, and score the overlap: full, partial, or none. No-overlap decisions are where systematic error accumulates; prioritize fixing those.
3. **Close the feedback loop when you cannot move the decision.** If you cannot put the decision in the operator's hands (sometimes you can't), at minimum ensure the decider hears directly and specifically from the bearer when the decision was wrong. Make the channel short, the cadence regular, and the content unfilterable.
4. **Reframe third-party recommendations as hypotheses, not verdicts.** Have the operator who will bear the outcome restate the recommendation in their own words and propose what would make them change their mind. Where operator and third party agree, trust the overlap. Where they disagree, default to the operator unless there is strong supporting reason otherwise.
5. **Get falsification criteria in writing.** Before committing to a third party's plan, ask: "If this is wrong, what will we see in six months, and what will you do about it?" Their answer tells you whether they are offering a hypothesis you can test or a position you cannot walk back.
6. **Slow meetings down so articulation doesn't win by default.** Have the operator speak last, without interruption. Make the outside party name the evidence that would change their view. This equalizes articulation and knowledge in the room.
7. **Never make a third-party sign-off binding unless they have skin.** If someone's signature is required but their outcome does not depend on the decision, either give them accountability or drop the sign-off. Signatures without skin create the appearance of oversight while manufacturing latency.
8. **Use third parties for what they are good for.** Fresh perspective, cross-portfolio pattern recognition, audit-style checks, uncomfortable questions long-timers have stopped asking. Not: final verdicts on your specific situation. Know the distinction and route accordingly.

## Examples

**Situation:** A consulting firm has been hired at significant expense to recommend a new org structure. They have interviewed the senior team and produced a 40-slide deck proposing a restructure.
**Application:** Who decides whether to implement? Officially the CEO, but the consultants' reputation is on the line and they will push hard. Who bears the consequence? The CEO (a little — she wears the outcome), the team being restructured (a lot — their jobs, reporting lines, and daily work change), and customers (indirectly, through the disruption). The consultants bear nothing; in eighteen months, if the restructure failed, they will be working with someone else's company. This is a classic third-party decision: high confidence from an insulated party, high cost landing on uninsulated parties.
**Result:** Either renegotiate the engagement so a meaningful portion of the fee depends on measurable outcomes twelve months out, or take the deck as one input and run the decision through the people who will bear it. Have the team leads whose orgs are being redesigned write down, in their own words, what they would actually change and why. Compare. Where the team leads and the consultants agree, that is probably signal. Where they disagree, default to the operators.

**Situation:** Corporate compliance has rolled out a new approval process. Every expenditure over $5,000 now requires three signatures, including one from legal. Field teams report it is taking three weeks to get decisions that used to take a day.
**Application:** Who designed the process? Compliance, under pressure from an incident six months ago. Who bears the consequence? Field teams, every single day, now operating with three-week latency. Are these the same people? No. Does compliance hear about the latency? Not directly — they hear when someone escalates, which is rare because escalating is itself costly. Does compliance bear a cost when field teams work around the process (and they will)? No — if anything, compliance benefits, because workarounds become violations they can cite as evidence that more enforcement is needed. The structure is self-reinforcing in the wrong direction.
**Result:** Do not try to persuade compliance the rule is too heavy; they have no incentive to hear it. Instead, close the loop. Measure the latency the rule introduces, quarterly, and report it to the same executive who reviews compliance performance. When the latency has a number and an owner who sees it, the rule becomes negotiable. Without the number, it doesn't.

**Situation:** The board has asked for a pivot. Two board members — both partners at funds, neither ever a CEO — have presented a detailed thesis. The CEO's instinct is to resist, but the board is unified.
**Application:** Who is making the decision? The board, with strong pressure on the CEO. Who will bear the consequence? The CEO directly, the team significantly, the customers through the disruption. Do the board members bear a consequence? Partially — their fund's performance depends on the outcome over a 5-10 year window, diluted across many portfolio companies. Individually, neither partner's career tracks tightly to this specific company; if the pivot fails, they will attribute it to execution. If it works, they will attribute it to their thesis.
**Result:** Do not refuse the board's input — they have legitimate stake and fresh perspective. But surface the structure explicitly. Ask each board member: "If this pivot is wrong, what happens to you?" The honest answer is not much. Then ask what would have to be true for them to be willing to walk back the thesis in six months. Getting the falsification criterion in writing, in advance, converts their input from a verdict into a hypothesis — one the operators can now test and, if necessary, refute with data. Without that, their articulation wins automatically and the consequence lands on the operators.

**Situation:** An industry "best practice" recommends a particular org design (separate CRO, separate CPO, separate COO). It is endorsed by industry analysts, cited in peer decks, and your head of talent advocates for it.
**Application:** Who developed the best practice? Industry analysts, consulting firms, and the universe of VP-search recruiters. What happens to them if the best practice is wrong for your company? Nothing — they are not in your company. The practice is selected for being easy to describe and easy to recommend, not for working across the specific context variations that actually determine fit. Your head of talent is closer to accountable, but their career also depends on seeming to know what the industry is doing, which biases them toward orthodoxy.
**Result:** Treat the best practice as one hypothesis. Test it against your specific context: who specifically would you hire, what specifically would they own, what specifically would change? If the answers are vague ("they would be responsible for revenue"), the practice is orthodoxy, not evidence. If the answers are specific and the specifics still point to the structure, go. But run the specifics first, and do not let the practice override them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat consulting output as a decision.
**Why:** The consultant is a third party. Their deck is, at best, a structured input. It becomes a decision only when an operator with skin accepts it as their own. Accepting someone else's recommendation without that step transfers decision-making authority to a party who will not bear the consequence. The result will be systematic over-confidence in the recommendation and systematic under-weighting of the operator's own signal.

**Don't:** Let articulation win meetings.
**Why:** Third parties are typically better presenters than operators, because presentation is part of their trade and operation is not. A polished 40-slide deck from an outside team will beat a rambling but correct memo from an operating lead, in the vote of a room full of generalists. The vote will go to the articulation and the consequence will go to the operator. Slow the meeting down. Make the operator speak last, without interruption, and ask the outside team what evidence would make them change their view.

**Don't:** Design approval structures around signing parties who aren't accountable.
**Why:** If the person whose signature you require has no consequence when they are wrong, their signature is entertainment. It creates the appearance of oversight without the substance, and it creates latency and friction without the correction. Either tie their outcome to the decision (through accountability, compensation, or role) or drop the sign-off.

**Don't:** Confuse disinterest with expertise.
**Why:** The outside advisor's independence is sometimes mistaken for superior judgment. "They have no horse in the race" is treated as "they can see clearly." Often the opposite is true: because they have no horse in the race, they have no incentive to be right, only to be plausible. Independence is a feature when you need an audit-style check; it is a bug when you need a verdict, because verdicts are only reliable from people who will live with them.

**Don't:** Assume third parties are stupid or malicious. They rarely are.
**Why:** The critical error is to personalize the problem. The consultant is not a fraud; they are solving the problem they are paid to solve. The rule-maker is not trying to sabotage the field; they are optimizing for a different variable than the field cares about. The board member is not ignorant; they are reasoning from the context they have, which does not include what you see daily. Structure, not character, is the issue. Fix the structure.

**Don't:** Use third-party skepticism to refuse all outside input.
**Why:** Some outsiders are invaluable — the first customer who points out the obvious flaw in your product, the new hire who notices the cultural rot long-timers have stopped seeing, the investor who has watched ten companies make this mistake. The discipline is not to reject third parties but to know which kind of input they can reliably provide (fresh perspective, pattern recognition across their portfolio, uncomfortable questions) and which kind they cannot (specific operating judgment about the situation in front of you). Use them for the first. Never let them monopolize the second.

The question to ask of any decision-making structure is not who is the smartest, or even who is the most senior. It is: where does the cost land, and does it land with the person who made the call? When the answer is yes, the structure will self-correct over time. When the answer is no, the structure will drift into systematic error, and no amount of intelligence at the front will prevent it. Fix the structure or expect the drift.
