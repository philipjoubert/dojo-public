# Visualization, Goggins Style

## When to Use
- The week before a hard event — a race, an interview, a board meeting, a fight, a launch.
- When you've already done the training and you're trying to engineer the *response* to the failure modes you can predict.
- When you've been told to visualize the trophy ceremony and you can feel that it isn't loading anything real into your nervous system.
- After a previous failure, when you're rebuilding for the next attempt and the failure modes are now data, not surprises.

## Fails When
- **You haven't put the work in.** Visualization will never compensate for work undone. You cannot visualize lies. If your training is empty and you sit there imagining the finish line, you're just daydreaming with extra steps. The rehearsal only works because the body knows it can do the thing — the visualization is the operating manual on top of the engine, not a substitute for the engine.
- **You only rehearse the win.** That's the dirtiest trick the motivational industry pulls. They show you the trophy. They never show you mile 80, the cramping calf, the bloody pee, the loose pull-up bar, the recruiter laughing in your face. When the bad thing happens — and it *will* happen — you have no rehearsal to fall back on. You panic. You spazz out in the one-second decision. You quit.
- **You catastrophize without rehearsing the response.** This is not the point. The point isn't to scare yourself into not showing up. The point is to walk through every way the thing breaks and decide *now*, in the calm, what your move is when it breaks then, in the fire.
- **You skip the research step.** This isn't meditation. Goggins drives the course. Studies the maps. Annotates the descents. Sits at the kitchen table reading and re-reading the file like he's prepping for the ASVAB. Visualization without research is fantasy.

## Core Concept

Most people use the clean mirror — the one that flatters. Most people visualize the trophy. The trophy ceremony version of visualization is a cope. It's the same self-help fantasy as motivation, dressed up. It feels good for forty-five minutes and then the suffering shows up and the visualization is gone because nothing in it prepared you for what suffering looks like.

The dirty mirror version is different. It says: *you're going to do this hard thing. Highlight every part of it you do not like. Sit with the parts you're afraid of. Spend time visualizing each and every obstacle you can see coming.* When I show up for a foot race now, I drive the entire course first, visualizing success but also potential challenges, which helps me control my thought process. When I prepped for Badwater, I drove the course, pulled over, closed my eyes, and visualized the eighteen-mile climb to Towne Pass. Then the bone-crushing nine-mile descent that comes after — I knew running downhill messes you up bad and this time I'd be doing it on asphalt. I closed my eyes and tried to *feel* the pain in my quads, my calves, my knees, my shins. I knew my quads would bear the brunt, so I made a note to add muscle. My thighs would need to be plated in steel. *That's* visualization. Not a daydream. A pre-game scout report on every place the race is going to try to break me, and a written-down response for each one.

The reason this works is that nobody quits an ultra race or Hell Week in a split second. People make the decision to quit hours before they ring the bell. The body's signals — the cramp, the dizziness, the doubt — are predictable. Most of them announce themselves before the failure point. If you've rehearsed each one, when it shows up at mile 70 your mind doesn't spazz. It says *oh, this is the one with the bloody pee. I have a move for this.* You take care of the dehydration, you eat the real food, you walk one mile, you walk another, the goal you set in the calm is already standing there waiting for you. Without rehearsal, the same moment fries your circuits. Your mind can't process 130 hours of suffering in one second, so it quits. *Once you quit, you take that warm shower. And guess what happens? You have regret.*

Pair this with the cold rule that visualization sits on top of the work, never instead of it. Self-talk without the work is a lie. Visualization without the work is a daydream. I tell myself in the worst moments of an event that I'm the hardest man God ever created, but the only reason that self-talk holds when the body is breaking is because behind it is three hours a day of training, the running shoes laced up on day 75 when nothing felt like it, the seventy-minute pull-up sessions every morning. The visualization is calling on the deposit. There's no deposit, there's nothing to call.

## How to Apply

1. **Do the research first.** Drive the course. Study the floor plan of the building. Read the deal memo three times. Know your opponent's last six fights. Visualization without research is making up a fake event in your head and rehearsing for that.

2. **Visualize the win in the right register — once.** Paint the picture of what success looks and feels like. Use it as a fuel source, the reason you're doing this. Then put it down. The win is not where the work is.

3. **List every failure mode you can see coming.** This is the dirty-mirror part. Highlight what you do not like about it. Spend real time on each one. *At mile 24, 25, 35, 45, this is going to happen. At hour six, the pull-up bar will start to flex. The carbohydrate drink isn't going to give me the power I need. The recruiter is going to look at me like a 300-pound joke. The investor is going to ask me the one question I don't have a great answer for.* Don't flinch from the list. Make it long.

4. **Engineer the response to each failure, in advance, in writing.** This is the part most people skip. *If the bar flexes, here is the alternate grip. If the carb drink fails, I order a cheeseburger. If the cramp hits at mile 70, I take care of dehydration first, eat real food second, walk one mile third, decide about the next mile after that. If the investor asks the question, I have these three sentences.* The whole point is to have already made the decision in the calm, so the in-fire version of you doesn't have to.

5. **Run the rehearsal at the level of the body.** Goggins doesn't just think about the descent — he tries to *feel* the pain in his quads. He doesn't just think about Hell Week — he flashes onto the chafe, the cold deep in his bones, the rolling in the sand all night. The nervous system needs to recognize the situation when it shows up. A purely intellectual rehearsal won't load.

6. **Bank it in the Cookie Jar after the event, win or lose.** Every time a failure mode shows up and you execute the rehearsed response, that's a reference point. *I've been here before. I have the move.* The cookie jar fills with these. Next event, the rehearsal compounds.

7. **Run the After Action Report on the rehearsal too.** If a failure mode hit you that you didn't see coming, that's a tell — your visualization didn't go deep enough. Note the gap. The next prep cycle gets thicker.

## Examples

**Situation:** Badwater 135 prep, 2006. Goggins has run two hundred-mile races. Now he's facing 135 miles through Death Valley with an additional 35 miles he's never run before. He's afraid of the heat.

> *(From* Can't Hurt Me *Ch. 7, lines 1932–1972.)* "I marked off the best spots to set up my cooling stations. I noted wherever the shoulder was wide, and where stopping would have to be avoided. [...] After running the desert gauntlet I'd earn some relief from the heat and pay for it with altitude. The next stage of the race was an eighteen-mile climb to Towne Pass at 4,800 feet. The sun would be setting by then and after driving that section, I pulled over, closed my eyes, and visualized it all. Research is one part of preparation; visualization is another. Following that Towne Pass climb, I would face a bone-crushing, nine-mile descent. [...] One thing I learned from the Hurt 100 is that running downhill messes you up bad, and this time I'd be doing it on asphalt. I closed my eyes, opened my mind, and tried to feel the pain in my quads and calves, knees and shins. I knew my quads would bear the brunt of that descent, so I made a note to add muscle. My thighs would need to be plated in steel. [...] This is an exercise in recognition and visualization. You must recognize what you are about to do, highlight what you do not like about it, and spend time visualizing each and every obstacle you can. I was afraid of the heat, so in the run-up to Badwater, I imagined new and more medieval self-torture rituals disguised as training sessions."

**Application:** Drive the course. Note the cooling stations, the wide shoulders, the stopping zones. Visualize each section in the body, not just the head. Make the pain part of the rehearsal. Then re-engineer training to plate the quads in steel. The visualization is producing the training plan, not replacing it.

**Result:** Goggins toes up to the Badwater line having moved his governor to 80 percent. He doubled his ceiling in six months. He still gets smashed in places — that's not the point. The point is when the smash comes, he has rehearsed it; he keeps moving instead of quitting. He finishes Badwater that year.

**Situation:** First pull-up world record attempt, NBC studios, 2013. Goggins is going for 4,021 pull-ups in 24 hours on the *Today Show* set, wearing gloves, in front of millions of people.

> *(From* Can't Hurt Me *Ch. 9, lines 2658–2674. After-action passage on the failure.)* "From the very first pull-up I noticed that the bar had a lot more give than I was used to. [...] At first, I blocked that out. Had to. A looser bar just meant a stronger effort and gave me another opportunity to be uncommon. Throughout the day people passed by on the street below, waved, and cheered. [...] After more than six long hours, and with 2,000 pull-ups in the bank, I took my first ten-minute break. [...] I didn't vocalize my negativity, and I tried to reset my mind for the second half push, but the truth was my whole plan had gone down the toilet. My carbohydrate drink wasn't giving me the power I needed, and I didn't have a Plan B, so I ordered and downed a cheeseburger. [...] In the military, after every real-world mission or field exercise, we fill out After Action Reports (AARs), which serve as live autopsies. [...] First of all, I should never have gone on that show. [...] I didn't respect the record enough going in. I thought I could have broken it on a rusty bar bolted to the back of a pickup truck with loose shocks, so even though I tested the bar twice before game day, it never bothered me enough to make a change, and my lack of focus and attention to detail cost me a shot at immortality."

**Application:** This is what a *failure of visualization* looks like, in his own words. The bar's give was a failure mode he could have surfaced — and partly did, when he tested it twice. But he hadn't visualized it deeply enough to engineer the response. No Plan B for the carbohydrate drink. No protocol for the bubbly looky-loos. No rehearsal for muscle failure (which he'd never hit in his life). The visualization was thin. The dirty mirror wasn't dirty enough. He stops at 2,500 pull-ups, in front of millions.

**Result:** Failure becomes data. Goggins runs the AAR, lists the gaps, attempts the record again, fails again, attempts a third time, and breaks it. Every gap from attempt one becomes a rehearsed response in attempts two and three. The record is the proof that the dirty-mirror version of visualization works *over multiple cycles*. Failure is the gift no one wants — but only if you take it apart on the kitchen table the next morning.

**Situation:** San Diego One Day, mile 70 of 100. Goggins, no ultra training, sitting in a folding chair, blood and pee running down his leg, has 30 miles to go. He has not visualized this.

> *(From* Can't Hurt Me *Ch. 6, ~lines 1968–1972, and audiobook line 1582.)* "Before I ever asked myself, 'Why?' I'd need my Cookie Jar on recall to convince me that despite what my body was saying, I was immune to suffering. Because nobody quits an ultra race or Hell Week in a split second. People make the decision to quit hours before they ring that bell, so I needed to be present enough to recognize when my body and mind were starting to fail in order to short circuit the impulse to look for a way out long before I tumbled into that fatal funnel. Ignoring pain or blocking out the truth like I did at the San Diego One Day would not work this time, and if you are on the hunt for your 100 percent you should catalog your weaknesses and vulnerabilities. Don't ignore them. Be prepared for them, because in any endurance event, in any high-stress environment, your weaknesses will surface like bad karma, build in volume, and overwhelm you. Unless you get ahead of them first."

**Application:** This passage is the doctrine being *invented in real time* off the back of a failure of visualization. Goggins did San Diego One Day with three days' notice, no training, no rehearsal. The body fell apart at mile 70. He scraped through by accident. The next time — Badwater — he doesn't go in blind. He rehearses every failure. The contrast is the lesson: *you got away with no rehearsal once. You won't twice.*

**Result:** The Cookie Jar concept is born inside this story (mile 81), but the visualization doctrine is born off the back of it the next year. Goggins makes the rule — *catalog your weaknesses and vulnerabilities; be prepared for them; get ahead of them* — and never runs another race without driving the course first.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Visualize the trophy and stop there.
*Why:* The trophy is the thing on the other side of the suffering. The suffering is what most people quit during. If you've only rehearsed the trophy, your nervous system has nothing for mile 70. The motivation industry sells the trophy version because it's the easy one to sell. Don't buy.

**Don't:** Use visualization to scare yourself into not showing up.
*Why:* The list of failure modes is supposed to make you *more* prepared, not less willing. If your visualization session ends with you in bed under a blanket, you missed the move. Each failure mode gets a *response.* The response is the whole point. The fear without the response is just fear.

**Don't:** Visualize without putting in the work.
*Why:* Self-talk doesn't mean anything. You can tell yourself you're the hardest person ever lived. Self-talk without the work is just lies. Visualization is self-talk's older brother — same rule. The reason the rehearsal loads is the body has *been there* in training and the visualization is calling on the file. No file, no load. No load, no help when the body breaks.

**Don't:** Make it abstract.
*Why:* Goggins doesn't think *I will run downhill.* He drives the descent, sets out the cooling stations, puts the pain in his quads, calves, knees, shins. He prep-files Badwater like the ASVAB. The whole game is specificity. *At mile 24 this is going to happen. At hour six the bar starts to flex. At minute thirty of the keynote my voice will go.* If you can't get specific, you don't know your event well enough. Go back to the research step.

**Don't:** Treat the rehearsal as one-and-done.
*Why:* Goggins did Badwater prep at the kitchen table, *read and re-read* the file. He visualized each mile *the best he could.* It's repetition. Once is a daydream. Fifty times is a rehearsal. The reason it loads is the same reason calluses build — reps. The hand and the mind both harden the same way: by getting used to the surface again and again.
