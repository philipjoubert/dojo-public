# Triple Down on Your Weaknesses, Not Your Strengths

## When to Use
- You are at the bottom or near it, and you're trying to figure out where to spend your reps.
- You catch yourself preferring the workouts you're good at, the calls you're comfortable on, the parts of the job you already know how to do — and avoiding the rest.
- You've been told the popular advice — *triple down on your strengths* — and something in you knows that's a permission slip to stay average.
- You're trying to make the phrase *I'm not good at that* stop being an excuse you accept from yourself.

## Fails When
- **The user is already an Olympic-level specialist and needs to optimize their one strength to win at the highest level.** That's a different question. This doctrine is for the climb out, not the gold medal lap. Most people aren't actually in that gold-medal scenario, but if you genuinely are — narrow specialist at the absolute frontier — the math is different. For everyone else, the doctrine holds.
- **The user reads it as *attack every weakness simultaneously.*** That's how you spread thin and quit. The doctrine is sequential. Pick the weakness that's blocking the next thing you want. Attack *that* weakness. Then move to the next. The Accountability Mirror lists everything; the workout schedule attacks one at a time.
- **The user uses it to publicly self-flagellate.** This is private work. Goggins's Post-its are on his bathroom mirror, not on social media. The minute the weakness becomes a performance, the work goes hollow.

## Core Concept

There's a saying out there: *triple down on your strengths.* Tripling down on your strengths does one thing to you. It makes you very comfortable. It makes you very strong at what you're good at. And the reason most people walk around making excuses is because that's the world they live in — they live in a world where if they're good at running, all they do is run; if they're good at reading, all they do is read. So when life puts something in front of them that they're *not* good at, the whole strengths-based mentality forces them to say *I'm not good at it, so I'm not going to do it.* That's how you build a soft mind. That's how mediocrity becomes a standard.

You triple down on your weaknesses, not your strengths. That's how you grow. That's how you stop making excuses. Life doesn't give a damn what you're good at. Life puts the thing you suck at right in your face. So work on what you can't do. Be obsessed with what scares you. The thing you're avoiding *is* the door. The reason you're avoiding it is precisely the reason you have to walk through it.

When I was twenty-four, my weaknesses were everything. I was 300 pounds. I couldn't run. I was terrified of water and couldn't swim a stroke. I was dyslexic — couldn't read or write at grade level. I had a stutter so bad I walked off the stage of a Christmas play in second grade. My GPA was a joke. I was a cockroach exterminator at Ecolab making a thousand dollars a month, eating chocolate donuts at four in the morning. If I'd taken the popular advice and tripled down on my strengths, I'd have spent the next forty years getting better at killing cockroaches. There were no strengths to triple down on. Every direction I looked there was weakness, and the weakness was the only honest place to start. So I attacked them one by one. The weight first, because I had ninety days to lose 106 pounds to get into the Navy. Then the running. Then the swimming. Then the reading. Then the writing. Decades later I run ultras, I've written two books, I've addressed Congress, I've held a Guinness pull-up record. None of that came from leaning into a strength. All of it came from picking up the thing I was bad at and refusing to put it down until I wasn't bad at it anymore.

This connects to the Accountability Mirror. The Post-it notes I stick to the glass aren't a list of strengths — they're a list of weaknesses, named publicly to myself. The mirror is for naming what you suck at out loud. *I'm fat. I read like a third grader. I'm a coward. I cheated my way through school.* The list is the weakness audit. The training schedule the next morning is the weakness attack. They're the same operation in two parts.

It also connects to the 40% Rule. The 40% you're sitting at right now is the side of you that quits at the *weakness*, not the strength. Your governor is loudest exactly where you're worst. The cold-water swim. The page you can't read. The conversation you can't have. The reason most people stop at 40 percent is they only run into the governor when they hit a weakness — and they call it talent, or genetics, or *I'm just not built for this,* and they walk away. The man who walks toward the weakness instead of away from it is the man who finds out where the 60 percent lives.

## How to Apply

1. **Inventory the weaknesses publicly to yourself.** Stand in front of the mirror. Say them out loud. Write them on Post-its and stick them to the glass. *I can't write a paragraph. I'm afraid of cold-calling. I'm out of shape. I avoid math. I freeze in groups bigger than three.* Don't dance. Don't soften. The list is the diagnostic — soft list, soft training plan.

2. **Pick the weakness that's blocking the next thing you want.** Don't attack everything at once; that's a quitter's plan disguised as ambition. If your next move requires running and you can't run, running is the weakness. If your next move requires reading and you can't read, reading is the weakness. The weakness selects itself once you name what's next.

3. **Attack that weakness like it's the only thing on your schedule.** Three hours a day. Every day. No off days at the start. The reason most people fail at this is they treat the weakness as a side project. The weakness is the *main* project. The strengths can coast for ninety days while you fix the leak.

4. **Use the discomfort as the verification signal.** If the workout feels good, you're back in your strengths. *The discomfort zone is the only zone where growth happens.* When the rep is uncomfortable in a way you don't recognize, that's the rep. That's the one. You're at the edge of the weakness.

5. **Get loud with the failure on the way.** When you fail at the rep — and you will, because it's a weakness — name it raw. *I quit on myself today. I had grilled chicken and rice on the schedule and I got fries and a milkshake again.* The failure on the weakness is honest information. The training plan adjusts. The mirror knows.

6. **Keep going past the point where you can do it badly.** Don't stop at competent. Most people, the second they're not embarrassing on the weakness anymore, declare victory and go home. That's not the move. The move is keep grinding until the weakness becomes a *strength* — until the thing you used to suck at is the thing your team can rely on. That's where Goggins becomes a writer, where Goggins becomes an ultra-runner, where Goggins becomes a public speaker. Each one started as the worst thing on the list.

7. **Then move to the next weakness.** The Post-it comes off. The next one is on deck. The list is long because you started low. That's not a problem; that's a runway. The cookie jar fills with each weakness-turned-strength. Years later you turn around and the kid at 24 — fat, can't read, can't swim, can't talk — is unrecognizable.

## Examples

**Situation:** Goggins, 24 years old, three months before he ships out for SEAL training. 297 pounds. Can't run more than half a mile. Terrified of water — can't swim a length of a pool. Reads at the level of a much younger kid. The Air Force Academy rejected him. Seven Navy recruiters laughed him off the phone. He gets one *yes* from a recruiter who tells him he has to lose 106 pounds and pass the ASVAB just to get a shot.

> *(From* Can't Hurt Me *Ch. 3 — Accountability Mirror genesis. Compressed across the bathroom-mirror scene at lines ~600–630 and the next-morning training scene that follows.)* If you have worked for thirty years doing the same stupid job you've hated because you were afraid to quit and take a risk, you've been living like a coward. Period, point blank. Tell yourself the truth! [...] You are giving up instead of getting hard! Tell the truth about the real reasons for your limitations and you will turn that negativity, which is real, into jet fuel. Those odds stacked against you will become a runway!

**Application:** Goggins doesn't lean into a strength — there isn't one. He inventories the weaknesses. The weight first because the Navy weight cutoff is the gate. He attacks 106 pounds in three months. Then the swim — *just like me, he taught himself, and after three attempts he finally passed the SEAL swim test* (he says this about Freak Brown later, but it's literally the same path Goggins took with himself). Then the running. Then the ASVAB, twice. Each weakness is the door to the next gate. He doesn't move on to the next until the current weakness is no longer disqualifying.

**Result:** Three months in, he's down 106 pounds and sitting in a Navy training pipeline. Years later, he's the only African-American Navy SEAL in 80 years to graduate BUD/S, Army Ranger School, and Air Force Tactical Air Controller School. None of that comes from a strength he leaned into at 24. All of it comes from him picking up his weaknesses one at a time and refusing to put them down.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to attack every weakness at once.
*Why:* The Post-it list is everything. The training schedule is one. Attack the weakness blocking the next thing you want. Get loud at the mirror about all of them; spend your reps on one. The guy who tries to fix his weight, his reading, his swim, his stutter, and his cold-calling in the same week ends the week the same as he started it. Sequence is the discipline.

**Don't:** Lean into a strength to *avoid* a weakness and call it strategy.
*Why:* This is the popular advice dressed up. *I'm just leaning into my strengths.* Fine. What weakness are you hiding from? Most of the time the answer is the weakness blocking the next promotion, the next relationship, the next level of the business. The strengths-based move feels like efficiency and is actually avoidance with better PR.

**Don't:** Use the doctrine as public self-flagellation.
*Why:* The Accountability Mirror is in your bathroom. The Post-its are between you and the glass. The work is private. The minute you turn the weakness audit into a social-media performance — *look how honest I am about my flaws* — the audit is broken, because now you're optimizing for the audience, and the audience can be lied to. The mirror can't.

**Don't:** Stop the second the weakness becomes barely-passable.
*Why:* Most people quit the weakness work the moment they're no longer embarrassing. That's the worst place to stop. Now it's neither a strength nor a clear weakness — it's a soft middle that breaks the first time it's tested. Keep going past competent. Keep going until the thing you sucked at is one of the things your team can rely on. Then take the Post-it down.

**Don't:** Confuse this with self-hate.
*Why:* This isn't *I am worthless*. This is *I have weaknesses I'm going to attack one by one.* The whole point is the weakness is changeable. The whole point is the kid at 24 who can't read becomes the man who writes two books. If your weakness audit ends in despair instead of a training plan, you ran the wrong protocol. Get back in front of the mirror, get loud about the *next* attackable weakness, and lace the shoes up tomorrow.
