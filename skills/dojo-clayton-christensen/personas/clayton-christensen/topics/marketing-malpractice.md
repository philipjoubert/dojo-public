# Marketing Malpractice

## When to Use
- Your innovation pipeline is producing a high rate of flops despite large investments in research, design, and advertising.
- New-product success feels like luck — some work, some don't, and nobody can predict which.
- The marketing organization is organized around demographic segments, product categories, or price points rather than around jobs.
- Executives are spending heavily on advertising to "build the brand" of products that don't have a clear purpose.
- You keep hearing "we have great data on our customers" paired with "we're not sure why product X flopped."

## Fails When
- **You use the diagnosis as an insult rather than a redirect.** The tools of correlation-based marketing are not evil, and the people using them are not stupid. They are using the wrong tools for a job the tools can't do. Shaming them doesn't change the work.
- **The product is genuinely in a commodity fight on cost.** Marketing malpractice is about missing the job. If your actual problem is that your competitor makes the same product for 40% less, diagnosing malpractice is the wrong tool — you need a cost-structure conversation.
- **You apply the diagnosis to a product that is failing for reasons other than misreading the customer.** Distribution failures, regulatory issues, timing mismatches with capital markets — not every failure is a marketing failure.

## Core Concept

Here is the diagnosis, stated bluntly. Thirty thousand new consumer products hit store shelves each year. Ninety percent of them fail. That number has not moved in decades. It has not moved despite companies having access to more customer data than at any point in human history, more sophisticated analytical tools than ever before, more carefully structured innovation processes — stage-gates, rapid iteration, checks and balances — and more talented people running them. From the outside it looks like a scientific process. From the inside it's still hit-or-miss. What's gone wrong?

The fundamental problem is this: the masses and masses of data that companies accumulate are not organized in a way that enables them to reliably predict which ideas will succeed. The data is organized along the lines of "this customer looks like that one," "this product has similar performance attributes as that one," "these people behaved the same way in the past." None of that data tells you *why* customers make the choices they do. It tells you correlations. It does not tell you causation. And marketing decisions made on correlations are, over time, marketing decisions made by coincidence. Some work out. Most don't. And the activity gives the illusion of progress without actually causing it. As Yogi Berra said, "We're lost, but we're making good time."

That is the malpractice. Three symptoms follow from it. One: markets are segmented by the wrong axes. Companies slice customers by demographics ("small, medium, and large enterprises" or "age, gender, lifestyle brackets") and then design products for the "average" customer in each segment. Actual human beings don't behave like statistically average customers. A forty-year-old commuter and a twenty-five-year-old commuter may have exactly the same job in the morning — but your segment model treats them as different targets and you design two mediocre products instead of one excellent one. Two: products are developed with feature lists that try to please everyone a little. The milk-shake case study is the canonical example: McDonald's asked its demographic-matched panelists whether the milkshake should be thicker, more chocolaty, cheaper, chunkier. The panelists answered clearly. The consequent improvements had no impact on sales, because the improvements were irrelevant to the actual job the morning commuters were hiring the milkshake to do. Three: brands are managed as general endorsers rather than as purpose brands. Instead of each product having a clear name that signals which job it does, companies build general corporate brands and hope each product gets hired for every job. The result — visible in the car industry with its Taurus and Cavalier and Prizm and Sonata — is substantially undifferentiated products with average subbrands holding less than 1% share.

The deepest version of this failure is Kodak. Kodak spent eight billion dollars, starting in the early 1990s, getting good at digital imaging. They hired researchers from Bell Labs. They saw the disruption coming. They invested. And they still failed. Why? Because they had the job wrong. They thought the job people hired a camera to do was "preserve memories for posterity." But ninety-eight percent of the images ever taken have only been looked at once. The prints came back from the drugstore, got flipped through once, got put in a drawer, and were never looked at again. If "preserve memories" had been the real job, people would have gone back to the drawer. They didn't. What gradually emerged — inadvertently, through Kodak's own double-print promotion — was a very different job: "I want to communicate with people who are not here and now, and I can do it better with an image than with words." Mom, look at this photo. Little by little that job coalesced. When flash memory shrunk into a smartphone, the job got served — by a device Kodak had not built. Kodak was not disrupted by digital imaging. Kodak was disrupted by its own misreading of the job. All $8 billion went against the wrong target. That is marketing malpractice in its most expensive form.

The cure is a reorganization of first principles. Customers do not buy products — they *hire* them to do a job. The job has a functional dimension, an emotional dimension, and a social dimension. The fundamental unit of marketing analysis is not the customer and not the product: it is the job. Once you organize around that, markets segment themselves by job rather than by demographics. The real competitive set becomes visible (the milkshake competes with bananas, not just with Burger King). Markets turn out to be much larger than product-category math suggests. Brands become purpose brands, linked tightly to jobs. Advertising stops trying to stuff meaning into empty words and starts clarifying jobs that good products already do. Innovation becomes predictable — because the job exists whether or not you build for it, and if you build something that does the job better than anything else, you can predict the customer will buy it. You can leave luck to your competitors.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose by listening to how people describe their work.** If the marketing team describes the market by product category (milkshakes, quarter-inch drills, toothpaste), by demographic segment (millennials, HENRYs, empty nesters), or by price point (mass, mid, premium), that's the correlation lens. If they can't describe the market by the job a customer is trying to get done, the malpractice is present. This is usually the first hour's worth of a diagnostic conversation.

2. **Pull a failed product out of the last 24 months and re-analyze.** Ask: what job did we think this product was being hired to do? Was that actually a job? Or did we confuse a demographic correlation for a job? (Quicken Financial Planner captured 90% of retail sales in its category and still flopped — because "construct a retirement plan" turned out not to be a job most people were actually trying to do. The fact that the design team struggled to fill a focus group with "planners" was the tell. Making it easier and cheaper for customers to do things they aren't trying to do rarely leads to success.) This exercise, done honestly on a few failed products, is the cheapest possible diagnostic.

3. **Audit the competitive-set definition.** Ask: when we report market share, what denominator do we use? If the denominator is the sum of sales in our product category (milkshakes sold), we are probably using too small a market. The job-defined market is almost always larger. The milkshake market turned out to be seven times larger than the product-category market, because bananas and bagels and donuts and boredom are in the denominator. Forcing this rewrite of market definition is a big lever.

4. **Audit the brand architecture against jobs.** Walk through each product's brand. For each one, ask: when a customer has a job to do, does this brand pop into their head? If the answer is no — if the brand is just a corporate endorsement, or a made-up name with no referent — the brand is doing no work. Consider whether to convert it to a purpose brand tied to a specific job, or to consolidate it under an endorser brand with sub-purpose-brands for each job.

5. **Reorganize the innovation process around jobs, not segments.** Instead of "we need a product for the millennial segment," the brief becomes "we need a product for the job of arriving-at-a-dinner-party-with-something-you-can-say-you-made-yourself." The brief for the design team is the job, with all three dimensions spelled out. The success criterion is whether the product gets hired when the job shows up.

6. **Rewrite the role of advertising.** Advertising cannot build brands from nothing. It can clarify a job that a good product already does — as Unilever's Soupy Snax became Soupy Snax-4:00 with ads showing lethargic workers perking up, at which point customers said "that's what happens to me at 4:00!" and sales soared. Advertising is a clarifier of existing jobs, not a manufacturer of meaning.

## Examples

**Situation:** A fast-food chain wants to grow milkshake sales. They run demographic-profile studies, invite quintessential milkshake customers into focus groups, ask what would make the milkshake better. Customers give clear answers. The chain improves the shake on those dimensions. Sales don't move.

**Application:** The malpractice is visible in the research itself. The segmentation is by product category ("milkshake customers") and demographic. The question asked is "how would you improve the milkshake" — a feature-list question. The panelists are asked as if they are average representatives of a segment, and their answers are averaged, which produces a one-size-fits-nobody answer. Once the team switches to the job lens, the research becomes: observe in the restaurant, interview in circumstance, ask what they hired when they didn't hire a milkshake. The job (long-boring-commute plus stave-off-10am-hunger plus stay-engaged-with-life plus one-free-hand) is a completely different brief than the feature list.

**Result:** Sales of milkshakes increase 7x in the parts of America where the chain implements the job-based redesign. Not because the company got smarter about who the customer is. Because it finally asked the right question: what progress is this person trying to make?

**Situation:** Kodak, a wildly successful company, sees digital imaging coming in the early 1990s. They invest $8 billion over the next decade. They hire researchers from Bell Labs. They assume they are being diligent about the disruption. They are disrupted anyway, and a generation of business commentators says "the idiots should have invested in digital."

**Application:** This reading is wrong and that matters. Kodak did invest in digital. What they got wrong was the job. They assumed the job being done by a film camera was "preserve memories for posterity" — which sounded right to everyone, because the whole industry had assumed it for eighty years. But 98% of the images ever taken had only ever been looked at once. The prints went from the drugstore to an envelope to a box to a drawer and never came out. There was no preserve-memories job driving that behavior. The actual job — the one that gradually coalesced, inadvertently, through Kodak's own double-print promotion — was communication with people who aren't here and now. "Mom, I love you but I'm busy today — look at this photograph." When flash memory shrunk into a phone, that job was finally served properly, not by a better camera but by a networked device. Kodak had engineered against the wrong target for a decade.

**Result:** No amount of additional investment in imaging quality, lens sharpness, or megapixel count would have saved Kodak, because the job the customer cared about was not about image quality at all. It was about getting the image to someone else, fast, with no friction. Marketing malpractice at the job level is not just expensive. It is unrecoverable when the scale of investment is that large.

**Situation:** A newspaper like the New York Times, which was built around the job of "publish anything that's fit to print," gradually adds more product lines to its paper because it has a relationship with the reader. Personal ads. Real estate listings. Job listings. Automotive classifieds. Each addition is a reasonable-sounding extension of "our customers want more things from us."

**Application:** Each of those additions is a different job. "Sell a piece of used furniture" is one job. "Find a new place to live" is another. "Kill time on a 45-minute commute" is another. The New York Times was organizing around the customer relationship when it should have been organizing around jobs. Each job became a vulnerability. A company focused on one single job — Craigslist for classifieds, Metro for the 25-minute commute read, Zillow for real-estate browsing — could nail that one job better than the Times ever could. Newspapers that tried to bundle every job into one product lost each job one by one.

**Result:** Most newspapers are "just falling off the cliff" not because readers stopped wanting information but because single-job specialists took every job one at a time. The malpractice was organizing around the customer relationship instead of around jobs. The lesson is that the original success was always about a specific job (publish what's fit to print), and once the organization drifts from the job, the job becomes contestable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Declare a correlation to be a cause.
**Why:** "People who buy our product tend to be 25-34 and urban" is a correlation. Acting on it as if it were a cause — redesigning the product for urban 28-year-olds — will overshoot them on dimensions that don't matter to their job, and will miss the 45-year-olds who have exactly the same job. The malpractice begins the moment the correlation is treated as the brief.

**Don't:** Ask customers to rank feature improvements and act on the averages.
**Why:** When McDonald's asked panelists whether the shake should be thicker, more chocolaty, cheaper, or chunkier, it got clear answers. It built the improvements. Sales didn't move. The averaged answers described no actual person's job. Different jobs were being served by the same product, and averaging them produced a product optimized for nobody. Feature-ranking surveys are a legitimate tool in mature categories, but they cannot substitute for understanding the job — and if they become the primary tool, malpractice is present.

**Don't:** Let "brand" become a noun you spend money on rather than a thing a product earns.
**Why:** Managers who believe brand advertising is the mechanism for creating brand equity "generally succeed in stuffing it full of vagueness." The ad agencies and media companies win that game. The company funding it does not. Great brands — Crest in its prime, Starbucks, Kleenex, eBay, Kodak in its heyday, FedEx, Google — were purpose brands that got built because the product did a specific job and customers talked about it. Advertising clarified. Advertising did not create.

**Don't:** Sum your category's sales and call it your market.
**Why:** If milkshakes compete with bananas and bagels, the market is the job, not the category. Reporting market share of milkshakes tells you your share of milkshakes, which is the wrong denominator. The right denominator — the share of the job — is almost always larger, and it's where new entrants will attack you from.

**Don't:** Trust that the job you assumed at founding is still the job at scale.
**Why:** Kodak assumed "preserve memories" was the job. Successful companies at founding are almost always organized around a real job. The logic of growth then takes over — "we have customers, they have other jobs, let's offer products for those jobs too" — and the focus drifts. Every few years, re-ask: what job is this product actually being hired to do, right now, in the circumstance in which the customer uses it? If the answer has shifted and you haven't shifted with it, a specialist is about to eat you.

**Don't:** Use "jobs to be done" as a vocabulary upgrade without changing the work.
**Why:** A team that says "we're job-focused" but still organizes its brief around demographics, still sums sales by product category, still asks panels to rate feature improvements, and still builds general corporate brands — has upgraded the vocabulary without changing the malpractice. The test is not what the team says. The test is what the work looks like.
