# Innovation Killers (Three Financial Tools That Misfire)

## When to Use
- A capital committee keeps rejecting a disruptive investment on DCF/NPV grounds, and the person pitching it senses something is wrong but cannot articulate what.
- The finance team has built a spreadsheet comparing a new-capability investment against "leveraging our existing assets," and the existing-asset option keeps winning on marginal cost.
- The CEO or the board is announcing another buyback program at a moment when competitors are investing, and you need to explain why this is precisely backwards in a disruptive market.
- A stage-gate innovation process is systematically green-lighting incremental projects and killing every bet that could actually matter in five years. You need to locate the specific financial misanalysis driving this pattern.
- An incumbent is watching an attacker take its market and cannot understand why its analyses keep concluding that leveraging existing assets is the right response.

## Fails When
- The project actually is bad. Not every rejected project was killed by faulty finance. Some projects deserve to die. Use this framework to locate misapplications — not as a blanket defense of every underdog pitch.
- The user is trying to abandon financial discipline altogether. The tools here — DCF, NPV, marginal-cost analysis, EPS focus — are not bad tools. Applied correctly they are indispensable. The argument is not anti-finance. It is anti-misapplication.
- The firm genuinely cannot afford the investment. In capital-constrained firms, these tools may be correctly signaling risk. The framework is most relevant in firms that have capital and are nonetheless refusing to invest — which is most large firms today.

## Core Concept

For years we've been puzzling about why so many smart, hardworking managers in well-run companies find it impossible to innovate successfully. The investigation keeps turning up the same culprits — paying too much attention to the most profitable customers, building products that don't do the job the customer needs done, and so on. But there is another accomplice in the conspiracy against innovation, and it is hiding inside the finance function. Three financial-analysis tools, applied to decisions they were not designed for, produce a systematic bias against innovation. These are not bad tools. They are misapplied tools. That distinction matters, because the fix is not to throw out finance — it is to see where the tools misfire.

**The first tool is discounted cash flow applied against the wrong base case.** DCF is logically impeccable. But in practice, analysts commit two errors that destroy its usefulness for innovation decisions. The first error is assuming that the base case — the scenario you compare the innovation's cash flows against — is the present health of the company continuing indefinitely. It almost never is. In reality, competitors' sustaining and disruptive investments over time produce price pressure, margin compression, technology changes, share losses, and a declining stock price. The most likely stream of cash in the do-nothing scenario is not a continuation of the status quo. It is a nonlinear decline in performance. Philip Bobbitt called this Parmenides' Fallacy, after the ancient Greek logician who claimed to have proved that conditions in the real world must necessarily be unchanging. Analysts who compare a proposed investment to a stable baseline are trapped by Parmenides' Fallacy. It's tempting but wrong to assess an investment by asking whether it will make us better off than we are now. It is wrong because, if things are deteriorating on their own, we might be worse off than we are now after the investment — and still better off than we would have been without it. The right question, the one good economists are taught to answer when asked "How are you?" is: relative to what? That is the crucial question. Answering it means comparing the innovation's cash flows against a realistic scenario of competitive erosion, not against a fictional unchanging present.

The second DCF error is overweighting terminal value. Analysts project three to five years of specific numbers and then punt by calculating a terminal value for everything thereafter. In practice, terminal value often accounts for more than half of a project's total NPV — which is exactly the period where the do-nothing decline begins to bite most sharply, and where the single-discounted-number method does not permit scenario testing. The place where the comparison matters most is the place where the method is most opaque.

**The second tool is fixed-and-sunk-cost analysis — the marginal-cost argument for disruption.** When evaluating a new investment, managers are taught to consider only the marginal cash outlays required, subtract those from the marginal cash flowing in, and compare the result to alternatives. This is correct — as long as the capabilities that succeeded yesterday will succeed tomorrow. When new capabilities are required for future success, marginal-cost analysis biases managers toward leveraging obsolete assets. The late-1980s steel industry story is the canonical illustration. By 1988 Nucor and the other minimills had driven the integrated mills out of rebar, bar, rod, angle iron, and structural steel. Nucor was building a minimill at Crawfordsville, Indiana, to attack the last remaining tier — sheet steel. The investment was $260 million. At $350 per ton of output and a cash cost of $270, the IRR cleared 20%, well above Nucor's cost of capital. So Nucor built it.

US Steel faced the same decision and ran the numbers the way they had been taught. USX's existing mills had 30% excess capacity. The marginal cash cost of an extra ton of steel from that excess capacity was under $50. So the marginal cash flow of leveraging the old technology was about $300 per ton ($350 revenue minus $50 marginal cost). The greenfield minimill's average cash flow per ton was $80. On marginal-cost grounds, it was obvious: leverage the old. Except — and here is the trap — Nucor, the attacker, had no existing assets to compare against. To Nucor, the full cost was the marginal cost. The attacker and the incumbent did not choose from the same menu. The incumbent had two options (marginal vs. full) and chose the cheap-looking one. The attacker had one option (full) and built the new capability. Twenty years later Nucor makes more steel than any American integrated producer, and every integrated mill but one has gone bankrupt. The argument that investments should be evaluated on marginal cost is always correct — but when creating new capabilities is the question, the relevant marginal cost is actually the full cost of creating the new.

The same logic explains why executives in established companies bemoan how expensive it is to build new brands or new distribution channels, and choose instead to leverage existing ones. Entrants simply create new ones. The incumbent isn't outspent; it is trapped in a menu choice the entrant never faces.

**The third tool is EPS focus.** The governance theory behind it is the principal-agent problem: align managers' interests with shareholders' by tying compensation to share price. In practice this compresses attention onto whatever can move EPS in the next quarter. The most reliable lever is buybacks — reduce the share count and EPS rises arithmetically, regardless of whether anything in the underlying business improved. Over 90% of public-company shares in the United States are held by mutual funds, pension funds, and hedge funds, with average holding periods under ten months. The "principals" being aligned to are not long-term owners; they are intermediaries with their own short-horizon metrics. What you have is an agent-agent problem, in which the long-term health of the enterprise — the thing innovation investments pay off — has no party at the table. EPS dominance is how firms end up with record buyback programs in the same years they underinvest in the bets that will decide whether the firm matters in ten years.

## How to Apply

1. **Start every DCF with an explicit, hostile do-nothing case.** Force the analyst to model the competitive decline that occurs if the investment is not made. What do competitors do? Which customer segments erode first? When does pricing power break? The do-nothing scenario is almost never a flat line. If you can't write the decline model, you have not done the analysis. You have done half of it.

2. **Apply Parmenides' test to every number.** Is this value a comparison to something that can actually happen in the world, or to a fictional unchanging present? If it's the latter, the number is wrong in a specific direction (it undervalues the investment). Every value derived from that comparison is biased.

3. **Interrogate terminal value.** What fraction of total project NPV sits in terminal value? If more than half, you are betting the recommendation on the period you can model least well — and where the do-nothing decline will be sharpest. Either model year 5–15 explicitly, or discount your confidence proportionally.

4. **Never use marginal cost to compare a new-capability investment to leveraging existing assets.** The comparison is structurally dishonest. Existing-asset leverage looks cheaper only because the sunk cost is not charged against it; the attacker faces no such asymmetry. For any decision that involves new capabilities, compare full-cost-of-new to full-cost-of-new — as if you were the attacker, with nothing to leverage. If the new capability still doesn't clear the hurdle, fine. But start there.

5. **Ask: "What is on the attacker's menu?"** If your menu has two options (marginal leverage vs. full-cost new build) and the attacker's menu has one (full-cost new build), and your menu is producing the "don't build" answer while the attacker's menu is producing the "build" answer — your menu is the bias. Strip the marginal option off the menu and re-decide.

6. **Value strategies, not projects.** Mauboussin's prescription is the antidote to Parmenides-trapped project-level DCF. Evaluate the ensemble of investments required to maintain long-term position, not each candidate against a fictional steady state. Messier, harder to spreadsheet, closer to true.

7. **Name the EPS-versus-investment tradeoff explicitly in boardrooms.** When a firm announces a buyback while an attacker is gaining ground, somebody should say out loud: we are returning capital to share owners whose average holding period is under one year, while the competitor is investing in a ten-year position. That tradeoff may still be right — but it must not be invisible. Most of the time it is invisible, and that is how it wins by default.

8. **Use discovery-driven planning for innovations, not stage-gate NPV.** Stage-gate processes reward projects with credible-looking numbers, and project teams who know what the numbers need to look like can tweak any eight-variable model over the hurdle in nanoseconds. Discovery-driven planning inverts the frame: here are the minimally acceptable numbers; list the assumptions that must prove true for them to materialize; rank-order them by riskiness; test the deal-killers first at low cost. The output is not a green-lit project; it is a plan to learn whether the project should be green-lit.

## Examples

**Situation:** A consumer-goods incumbent runs NPV on an investment in a new direct-to-consumer business. Against a base case of stable existing-channel revenue, the NPV is marginally positive. Against a base case in which competitors' DTC investments erode channel revenue 4% a year, the NPV is strongly positive.
**Application:** Parmenides' Fallacy. The analyst initially compared the investment to a fictional unchanging present. A realistic do-nothing scenario includes the competitive DTC erosion already visible in the market.
**Result:** With the corrected base case, the board approves the investment. Without the correction, the firm would have rejected it, watched its channel decline for three more years, and then attempted the same investment from a position of weakness, at a higher cost, against entrenched DTC competitors.

**Situation:** US Steel, 1988. Nucor is building the Crawfordsville minimill to attack sheet steel. USX's engineers analyze whether to build a comparable greenfield plant. Existing mills have 30% excess capacity. Marginal cash cost from excess capacity is under $50/ton; marginal revenue is $350/ton. Marginal-cost analysis says leverage the old. Greenfield NPV says build the new.
**Application:** The marginal-cost argument for disruption. USX and Nucor are choosing from different menus. Nucor has one option: build, at full cost. USX has two options: build at full cost, or leverage existing assets at marginal cost. The marginal option always wins on marginal grounds, because the sunk cost has already been paid and isn't re-charged.
**Result:** USX leverages the old. Nucor builds the new. Nucor's costs fall with production experience; its quality rises; it moves upmarket. USX doubles down on depreciated legacy assets that become competitively obsolete. USX goes bankrupt. Every other American integrated steel firm follows. The incumbent's strategy was mathematically correct on its own terms and disastrous in practice.

**Situation:** A large software incumbent announces a $5B buyback program. A smaller competitor is investing $1B a year in a new product category that, on current trajectory, will take 30% of the incumbent's revenue base in seven years.
**Application:** EPS focus override. The buyback will measurably move EPS next quarter and next year. The $5B invested in matching the competitor's category bet will not move EPS for five years and will depress earnings in the interim. The buyback wins the internal argument because the metric it moves is the metric that is measured.
**Result:** EPS rises for two years; the stock rises with it; executive compensation vests. In years four through seven the competitor's category matures and takes the promised 30%. The firm's ratios collapse because revenue is collapsing. At that point a buyback cannot save it. The seeds of the collapse were planted in the years the buybacks were celebrated.

**Situation:** A stage-gate innovation process has eight assumptions underpinning each project's NPV model. Every disruptive project gets delayed or killed at the gate; every incremental project sails through.
**Application:** Two pathologies at once. Parmenides-trapped NPV understates the disruptive projects' returns. And project teams know exactly what the gate requires — tweaking two or three of eight assumptions by 2–3% each moves any model over any hurdle. The incremental projects clear because they need less manipulation; the disruptive ones die because their real payoff sits in the opaque terminal-value period.
**Result:** Switch the disruptive projects off stage-gate and onto discovery-driven planning. The projects that survive are different from the ones that would have survived stage-gate — and the ones that matter in five years come disproportionately from this list.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use DCF to compare a disruptive investment to "doing nothing."
**Why:** Doing nothing is not the baseline. The baseline is your eroding competitive position as competitors invest in the disruption. In most real cases, not investing produces a nonlinear decline in performance, not a flat line. Comparing against the flat line — Parmenides' Fallacy — understates the disruptive investment's real return by exactly the amount of the decline you are refusing to model. The discipline is to explicitly model the do-nothing decline and compare against that. If you cannot or will not model the decline, your DCF is not neutral; it is quietly tilted against investment by a large and unknown factor.

**Don't:** Evaluate new-capability investments on marginal-cost grounds against leveraging existing assets.
**Why:** The comparison is structurally dishonest because the attacker never faces the same choice. Marginal-cost analysis makes existing-asset leverage look cheaper only because the sunk cost isn't re-charged. The attacker pays full cost for everything and invests anyway, because for the attacker there is no marginal alternative. The incumbent ends up trapped in an escalating cycle of commitment to capabilities built for yesterday's market. Answer the question the way the attacker does: compare full-cost-of-new to full-cost-of-new, with no marginal option on the menu.

**Don't:** Let EPS dominate the decision about whether to invest in the business or return cash to shareholders.
**Why:** EPS is a useful indicator, not a sufficient one. The principal-agent doctrine that gave EPS its primacy assumed long-term shareholders aligned with long-term firm health. In reality, the average holding period of the "principals" is under a year. Running the firm for EPS is running it for intermediaries, not for the actual long-term owner. A firm that buys back aggressively while an attacker is investing looks wonderful on EPS for two or three years and is structurally hollowed out by year seven. The cost of EPS primacy is mostly invisible until the collapse makes it visible.

**Don't:** Blame bad managers when the tools are misfiring.
**Why:** The managers running these analyses are thoughtful, hardworking, and doing exactly what they were trained to do. The tools themselves are fine; the application to innovation decisions is where the bias enters. Attacking people — or the finance function — produces defensiveness and no change. The moves that work: make the do-nothing decline explicit, strip the marginal option off the menu for new-capability decisions, and separate the EPS-versus-investment tradeoff so it has to be chosen deliberately. Keep the disagreement with the misapplication, not with the person holding the tool.
