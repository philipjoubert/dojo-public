# The Capitalist's Dilemma

## When to Use
- You are sitting in a capital allocation meeting where smart people keep rejecting the long-payoff bet in favor of the short-payoff one, and you can feel something is wrong but can't quite name it.
- A company generates strong cash, reports strong ROIC, and is clearly going to matter less in ten years than it does today. The numbers look right and the business is quietly dying.
- A board is importing targets from consultants or activists — "we will deliver X% RONA" or "every project must clear Y% IRR." The targets are about to do damage.
- A CEO genuinely wants to invest for long-term growth and cannot figure out why the machinery of her own firm keeps refusing to let her.
- You need to explain to a policymaker or founder why abundant capital has produced so little new investment — the paradox at the heart of post-2000 developed-world economics.

## Fails When
- The company has a real capital constraint. In a genuinely capital-scarce setting — a bootstrapped startup, a firm on the edge of a covenant breach — ratio discipline is not the disease, it is survival. The Capitalist's Dilemma is a large-cap, mature-economy disease.
- The rejected project is actually a bad idea. Managers sometimes kill long-payoff bets because they are long-payoff and bad. Ratios didn't do it; judgment did. Don't use this framework to rescue every project somebody fell in love with.
- The user wants a simple scapegoat. This is not "finance bad, vision good." It is "these particular tools, in this particular environment, produce a particular bias." That distinction matters and keeps the argument honest.

## Core Concept

For years we've been puzzling over a strange contradiction. Capital is the most abundant input in the developed world. The cost of capital is near zero; in some cases — Square raised a round of equity at a negative cost of capital; the investors literally paid Square to take their money — it is below zero. The head of the Federal Reserve spends his days, as Christensen liked to put it, standing on the beach with a fire hose trying to fill the ocean with more capital. And yet, despite this flood, smart hardworking managers in well-run companies systematically underinvest in the one type of innovation — market-creating — that could absorb that capital and create new jobs. Why?

The answer is not that the managers are stupid, or greedy, or captured by Wall Street. Most of them are thoughtful and long-sighted people. The answer is that the tools they have been trained to use were designed for a different world. They were designed for a world in which capital was scarce and costly. In that world — roughly 1940s through 1970s, when the modern finance curriculum took shape — you needed disciplined ways to deploy capital only where it would generate the highest return. You needed to measure the productivity of each dollar carefully. The theologians of what Christensen, half-teasing, called the "seminary of new finance" — the business school professors and the partners in private equity firms who teach the doctrines — gave managers the tools for that job. Ratios. Return on net assets. Return on capital employed. Internal rate of return. Economic value added. Every one of them a ratio.

Ratios are seductive, and they are dangerous, because a ratio has two ways to move. The numerator can go up. Or the denominator can go down. And both paths register as "improvement" on the metric. So if you are being measured on return on net assets, sure, you can make more profit and put it in the numerator. Or you can outsource a business and get the assets off your balance sheet to shrink the denominator. Either way the number goes up. Either way you get promoted. And critically, one of those paths requires building new businesses that create jobs and absorb capital. The other path requires the opposite. Guess which one is faster and less risky.

Look at internal rate of return. It too is a ratio — profit over time. You can get IRR up by growing the profits, or you can get it up by shrinking the time horizon, investing only in things that pay off in one or two years. A market-creating innovation pays off in five to ten years. In IRR terms, that payoff gets discounted almost to nothing. An efficiency innovation pays off in one or two years and throws off capital — so it boosts both numerator and denominator of your ratios. The tool is not neutral. In an era of abundant capital, it systematically routes investment away from the one type of innovation the economy most needs, and toward the type that produces stagnation.

That is the Capitalist's Dilemma. Managers doing exactly what their training prescribes, using tools that are logically impeccable, produce an outcome that is collectively catastrophic. The individuals are not malfunctioning. The system is. And the system is an inheritance from a capital-scarce era applied to a capital-abundant one. Capital is no longer the scarce resource. What is scarce is people who know how to create new markets — and we are measuring and managing as if the reverse were true.

The dilemma has a historical arc. The American economy ran the perpetual motion machine — efficiency freeing capital, capital funding market-creation, market-creation creating jobs — for roughly a century. By the 1980s, business schools had trained a critical mass of the seminary and its doctrines spread into corporate capital-allocation processes. Japan imported the same doctrines in the 1980s. Since 1990, Japan has produced one major market-creating innovation (the Nintendo Wii). One. The United States has seen its recession recoveries get steadily longer — six months in each of the first six postwar recessions, then fifteen months after 1991, thirty-nine after 2001, sixty-seven and counting after 2008. The Capitalist's Dilemma is the microeconomic cause of a macroeconomic phenomenon. It is why an economy with the cheapest capital in history produces the slowest recoveries.

## How to Apply

1. **Find the ratio metrics driving allocation decisions in your firm, and write them down.** RONA, ROCE, ROIC, IRR, EVA, EPS growth. These are the metrics that win or lose the internal argument. If you don't know which ratio is in force, ask: "When a project gets rejected in this committee, what number is cited?" That is your governing ratio.

2. **For each ratio, list both pathways — numerator up, denominator down.** This is the test of whether the ratio can be gamed into anti-investment. RONA can be improved by more profit (investment-friendly) or by fewer assets (investment-hostile). IRR can be improved by more profit (neutral) or by shorter time horizons (investment-hostile). Once you see the two pathways, the question is: which one is easier, faster, less controversial? Almost always the denominator path is. That tells you which direction the organization will drift under pressure.

3. **Ask what era the tool was designed for.** Most of the ratios were refined and canonized when the prime rate was 6–18% and capital was genuinely expensive to obtain. In that era the ratios aligned managers with scarce capital. Today capital is not scarce. A ratio optimized for capital scarcity, applied to a capital-abundant world, is exactly backwards. It treats the abundant resource as if it were scarce and the actual scarce resources — market-creating skill, long-horizon patience, talent formation — as if they were abundant.

4. **Diagnose who the real principal is.** The seminary teaches you to align agents (managers) with principals (shareholders). But over 90% of the equity in public U.S. companies is held by mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds — intermediaries whose average holding period is under ten months. The "principal" your metrics are aligning you to is not a long-term owner; it is another agent optimizing its own short window. The principal-agent problem is misnamed. It is an agent-agent problem, and the ultimate principal — the retiree whose pension is invested, the worker whose job depends on long-term firm health — has no seat at the table where these metrics are chosen.

5. **Name the seminary's doctrine out loud in the room.** You don't need to attack finance or CFOs. You need to say, clearly: "The ratio we are optimizing was designed for a world of scarce capital. Capital is no longer scarce. This metric is pointing us away from the investments our economy most needs. What would we measure instead?" It sounds like a simple question. It is not. It opens the only conversation that can get the firm unstuck.

6. **Replace ratio targets with absolute-dollar or absolute-growth targets when you can.** An ROCE target of 20% rejects a $1B investment at 15% returns — exactly the kind of market-creating investment the firm should make. An absolute-growth target of "+$150M in operating profit" accepts it. Absolute measures don't have a denominator to shrink. They can't be gamed by asset-shedding. They are blunter, less elegant, and much harder to fake.

7. **Watch for the giveaway symptom: buybacks.** When a firm has abundant capital, rejects most of its market-creating bets on ratio grounds, and returns the resulting cash to shareholders via buybacks — that is the Capitalist's Dilemma in its natural habitat. The capital had nowhere to go, under the governing metrics, and so it flowed backward out of the firm. The firm's stock price rises; its future shrinks.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Fortune 500 industrial company's capital committee rejects a proposal to build a new market-creating business line with an estimated ten-year payback. The stated reason: "This is below our 15% IRR hurdle rate."
**Application:** Run the Capitalist's Dilemma lens. The IRR hurdle rate was set when the firm's cost of capital was substantially higher than it is today. The project's actual NPV at the firm's true cost of capital is strongly positive. Meanwhile, the project that does clear the hurdle — a two-year efficiency automation — will free up capital that the committee will not then redeploy into the rejected market-creating bet. Net effect: the firm keeps the capital, doesn't build the new business, and in five years cedes the emerging market to a new entrant who had no hurdle rate because he had no existing business to compare against.
**Result:** The incumbent's ratios stay strong for three to five years. Then the market-creating competitor reaches scale, attacks from below (the classic disruptive path), and the incumbent's ratios collapse because revenue is collapsing. By the time the ratios register the problem, it is too late to respond.

**Situation:** A board is considering importing a RONA-based executive compensation scheme from a benchmark peer set.
**Application:** The Capitalist's Dilemma diagnosis: RONA is a ratio with two pathways. Under pressure, executives will shrink the denominator — outsource, sell assets, lease instead of buy, defer capital investment. In the short run, the metric improves. In the long run, the productive capacity of the firm declines. You will have paid executives to hollow out the firm.
**Result:** Either choose absolute-dollar targets (operating profit growth, absolute revenue growth in new segments) or, if you must use a ratio, pair it with an absolute-investment floor — "RONA of X% and at least $Y in new market-creating investments." The floor blocks the denominator-shrinking pathway. Without it, the metric is a license to harvest.

**Situation:** A Silicon Valley founder is mystified that Square was able to raise an equity round at a negative cost of capital — the investors effectively paid Square to take their money.
**Application:** This is the full-voltage Capitalist's Dilemma. Capital is so abundant, and productive investment opportunities so few under prevailing metrics, that the marginal dollar has a negative return. Investors are willing to lose a little just to be in the one company that might actually find something to do with the capital. The Fed is standing on the beach with a fire hose full bore, and the ocean keeps absorbing every drop. Teaching finance as if capital were scarce in this environment is not just outdated — it is actively misdescribing reality.
**Result:** The founder learns the real implication: in a world of negative cost of capital, financial discipline is worth essentially nothing and strategic discipline is worth almost everything. What is scarce is not money. What is scarce is the rare ability to build a new market. The firm that figures this out first wins a decade.

**Situation:** A student asks why Japan's extraordinary 1950s–80s market-creating engine — Toyota democratizing the car, Honda the motorcycle, Sony the television, Canon the copier — simply stopped around 1990.
**Application:** The answer is almost precisely the Capitalist's Dilemma. In the 1980s Japan's firms began hiring graduates trained in ratio-based finance — the same seminary's American graduates a decade earlier. When those graduates ran the capital allocation committees, they rejected market-creating bets on the same grounds their American peers had. Since 1990, Japan has produced one major market-creating innovation (the Nintendo Wii). Capital accumulated. Jobs did not. The economy flatlined. Thirty years of monetary policy experiments have failed to fix it because the problem is microeconomic, in the chairs where the ratios are calculated.
**Result:** The student understands that the diagnosis is portable. Britain and the United States are running the same arc, a few decades behind. The intervention has to be at the level of what is measured in the capital-allocation meeting, not at the level of the central bank.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Optimize RONA, ROCE, or IRR when capital is abundant.
**Why:** These metrics were designed for an era when capital was scarce and costly. In a capital-abundant era, they systematically starve market-creating investment — the only investment that creates net new jobs and absorbs the capital productively. You are using a scarcity tool in an abundance environment. The more rigorously you apply it, the faster you hollow out the firm. The individual metric is not wrong. The application is wrong, and the mismatch is severe enough that the "right answer" per the metric is reliably the wrong answer per the business.

**Don't:** Attack finance or CFOs personally to make this point.
**Why:** The seminary's students are doing exactly what they were trained to do, and their training was excellent for a world that no longer exists. Attacking people for using the tools they were trained in produces defensiveness, not change. What moves the conversation is naming the era the tool was designed for, showing the two-pathway structure of the ratio, and inviting a better measurement. Keep the disagreement with the tool, not with the person.

**Don't:** Reject ratio-based thinking entirely.
**Why:** In capital-scarce settings — startups, small businesses, firms in distress — ratio discipline is genuinely useful and sometimes life-saving. The Capitalist's Dilemma is specific to large, mature, capital-rich firms in capital-rich economies. If you generalize "ratios bad" to every setting, you will damage the companies that actually do need capital discipline. The rule is context-dependent. Diagnose the context first.

**Don't:** Stop at diagnosis.
**Why:** Naming the dilemma in a boardroom feels satisfying and accomplishes nothing if it is not followed by concrete changes to what is measured and rewarded. The practical follow-through is: (1) replace or floor the ratio targets, (2) protect a defined budget for market-creating investments from the ratio-optimization process entirely, (3) measure those investments on learning milestones and assumption-validation rather than on financial ratios in their first five years. Without those three, the diagnosis is a speech. With them, it is a strategy.
