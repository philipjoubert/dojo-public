# Overshoot Detection

## When to Use
- You are looking for industries ripe for low-end disruptive attack. Overshoot is the prerequisite — a low-end disruption cannot take root in a market where customers are still undershot, because there is no overserved segment waiting to trade quality for price.
- A founder is trying to decide where to launch and needs to know which markets have customers currently paying for performance they no longer value.
- An incumbent's product management team is trying to detect whether their own trajectory has passed the point where their best customers will pay more for improvement. That is the moment where the next dollar of R&D produces diminishing returns and the next dollar of cost reduction or convenience starts to matter more.
- An investor is diligencing a low-end disruptor's claim about its target market and needs to verify that the claimed overshoot exists.
- You are reading a trade press item about a startup offering a "good enough for much less" product and trying to tell whether it is a serious threat or a curiosity.

## Fails When
- The market has undershot customers — people still unable to get what they want from existing products, paying premiums for improvements, cobbling together workarounds. In undershot markets, the opportunity is up-market sustaining innovation, not low-end disruption. Reading the overshoot diagnostic when the market is undershot will produce the wrong answer and the wrong strategy.
- The apparent overshoot is actually a sustaining displacement — customers are shifting away from Product A, but because a better Product B has appeared along the same performance axis at a comparable or lower price. That is not overshoot; that is competition within a value network. Don't classify every loss of pricing power as overshoot.
- You watch only the median customer. Overshoot starts at the bottom tier and creeps upward (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). Tier-by-tier analysis is required. A market can be overshot at the bottom while its demanding customers at the top are still undershot.
- You project the technology's historical slope forward without asking whether the technology still has room to improve. Sometimes trajectory slows for reasons other than overshoot — physical limits, regulatory limits — and the product-lifecycle phase you are in is not what it appears.

## Core Concept

Companies innovate faster than customers' lives change. That is the bedrock finding (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). Customers' jobs to be done remain remarkably consistent over decades, but products always improve. Thus products eventually become too good. Overshooting is the driver of commoditization — the moment when companies can no longer profitably differentiate along the performance dimensions they have historically competed on, because those dimensions have raced past what customers can absorb (Innovator's Dilemma, Principle #5). When this happens, the basis of competition shifts. The criteria by which customers choose one product over another change — typically in a predictable order: from *functionality* (does it do the job at all?) to *reliability* (does it do the job consistently?) to *convenience* (how easy is it to use, customize, access?) to *price* (Innovator's Dilemma ch. 9; Seeing What's Next ch. 1). In early markets customers pay premiums for functionality and will keep paying as long as the existing product is not good enough. After functionality is good enough, they pay for reliability. After reliability is good enough, they pay for convenience. After convenience is good enough, the only thing left to pay for is price, and that is the commoditization phase. Overshoot is what drags a product through this sequence.

Detecting overshoot is, in practice, listening for a particular kind of silence — the silence of customers who have stopped paying more for what used to matter. Overshot customers pay decreasing premiums for improvements they used to value (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). In economic parlance, they derive diminishing marginal benefits from product enhancements. The symptoms are specific and recognizable. Companies add extra features that never get used. People begin to complain about things they ignored in the past — "this product is too complicated"; "it costs too much"; "I don't need any of the new stuff." The most telling sign is that price-based competition begins to break out in a market that used to reward premium pricing for premium features. Another sign is that specialist companies start to flourish where previously only integrated providers could win — a signal that the hard problem has shifted from integrating many pieces together (which requires integration) to delivering convenience and customization (which often requires modularity at specific interfaces). When the basis of competition shifts from functionality and reliability toward convenience and price, modular architectures tend to displace interdependent ones, because the modular design is better suited to the new basis of competition ("Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", HBR 1995; Seeing What's Next ch. 1).

Overshoot is a precondition for low-end disruption, but it is not the entire disruption. A low-end disruptor still has to arrive with a business model capable of earning attractive profits at the discount prices overshot customers are willing to pay. If overshoot exists but no one shows up with the right cost structure, the market sits in commoditization and margins erode across everyone, but no structural displacement occurs. The steel minimills built the right business model for a market whose rebar customers had been overshot by integrated-mill quality (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Wal-Mart and Kmart built the right business model for branded hard goods that had been overshot by full-service department store service. Discount brokers built the right business model for trading services that had been overshot by full-service Merrill Lynch advice. In each case the two preconditions combined — overshoot on one side, disruptive business model on the other — to produce a classical low-end attack. Overshoot detection alone tells you that the market is ready. Whether anyone attacks it is a separate question.

One more subtlety, and an important one. Overshoot does not propagate evenly across a market. It starts at the bottom tier and creeps upward (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). Not all customers become overshot at once. The least demanding customers — who needed the least from the product to begin with — are overshot first. They defect to a low-end alternative. As the incumbent's trajectory continues, the next tier up becomes overshot; they defect too. This is the migration mechanism that pulls a low-end disruptor from rebar into angle iron, from branded hard goods into apparel, from bottom-tier customers into the next tier. It also means overshoot analysis must be done tier by tier. A market that looks healthy at the top may be rotting quietly from the bottom, and the rot proceeds upward until it reaches the top. Watching the median customer will tell you nothing useful about this.

## How to Apply

1. **Segment the market by tier and examine each tier separately.** Pick a meaningful way to tier customers — by price sensitivity, by use intensity, by job-to-be-done complexity. For each tier, ask: are they still paying premiums for performance improvements on the historical basis of competition? If the lowest tier says no, that tier is overshot. Work upward. The top tier may still be undershot even when the bottom is overshot. The disruption windows are at the overshot tiers.

2. **Run the "what do customers complain about now?" check.** Early in a market, customers complain about functionality — the thing doesn't do what I need. Later they complain about reliability — it crashes, it breaks. Later still about convenience — it's too hard to use, too hard to buy, too hard to customize. Finally about price. The content of the complaints tells you which phase the market is in. When the dominant complaint is "it costs too much" or "it has too many features I don't need," you are in or approaching overshoot.

3. **Track the slope of pricing power on improvements.** If the incumbent introduces a next-generation product with 20 percent more performance and can charge 20 percent more for it, the market is still undershot on that dimension. If the next generation adds 20 percent more performance and the market only pays 5 percent more — or nothing, or moves down to the prior generation at a discount — pricing power has broken. Customers' willingness to pay for the historical basis of competition has died. That is overshoot, observed directly in the revenue line.

4. **Watch for specialist entrants gaining ground on integrated players.** When a market has been overshot, modular architectures tend to displace integrated ones, because the hard problem has moved from integrating everything to delivering convenience at specific interfaces (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). Specialist companies that do one piece well — and can plug into standardized interfaces — begin to flourish. When you see specialists stealing share from integrated giants, somewhere nearby customers are overshot. This is a structural signal that complements the direct pricing-power signal.

5. **Examine whether rules and standards have emerged.** When an industry is undershot and solving hard problems, it prizes deep expertise and proprietary integration. As it solves those problems and overshoots, rules emerge: cause-and-effect patterns become explicit; standards codify. With standards, less-expert providers can produce good-enough products. The spread of certifications, recipe-like operating procedures, or interface standards where once there was craft knowledge is a strong signal that overshoot has arrived (Seeing What's Next ch. 1).

6. **Do not confuse slowing growth for overshoot.** A market can slow for demographic reasons, regulatory changes, or macroeconomic forces unrelated to customer satiation. Overshoot is specifically about customers deriving diminishing marginal benefits from the next unit of *performance along the historical basis of competition*. If customers are still hungry for performance but the market is slow for other reasons, overshoot logic does not apply.

## Examples

**Situation:** In the late 1970s, Seagate leads the 5.25-inch hard-disk-drive market. Its customers — PC makers like IBM — are pushing for 40-MB and 60-MB drives for their next-generation models. Seagate's engineers build prototype 3.5-inch drives that hold 10 MB. The customers reject them. Seagate shelves the 3.5-inch project.
**Application:** ("Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", HBR 1995.) Was the PC market overshot on storage capacity? Not at the high end — IBM's mainstream customers were undershot. They still wanted more capacity and were willing to pay for it. Seagate's signal, interpreted through the lens of its best customers, was correct: invest in higher-capacity 5.25-inch drives, not in underpowered 3.5-inch drives. But there was a *different* market, emerging separately — portable computers — where small size mattered more than raw capacity. That market had different customers, different uses, different overshoot conditions. Compaq bought its 3.5-inch drives from Conner Peripherals, which sold into the portable market. The 3.5-inch drive's capacity then grew at 50 percent per year while PC-market demand grew at 30 percent per year. The trajectories crossed. By the late 1980s, the 3.5-inch was good enough for the PC market, and it pulled customers over.
**Result:** The lesson for overshoot detection: it is possible for the mainstream market to be undershot while a disruptive foothold is already viable in a new context of use. Seagate was not wrong about its mainstream customers. It was wrong to rely only on those customers as its signal, because the next wave of overshoot-enabled disruption was forming elsewhere, on a trajectory that would eventually cross its market.

**Situation:** By 1983, people were using PCs for word processing. Typists often had to stop their fingers to let the Intel 286 chip catch up. Chip performance was not yet good enough.
**Application:** (Innovator's Solution ch. 2.) Today's processors offer much more speed than mainstream customers can use — although there are still unsatisfied customers in the most demanding tiers who need even faster chips. Between the two moments, something shifted. In the early 1980s, the market was undershot on processor speed; performance commanded premium pricing, and Intel could sell each new-generation chip for more than its predecessor. By the mid-2000s, the median customer had passed the overshoot threshold — a 3 GHz chip and a 4 GHz chip produced indistinguishable user experiences for word processing, spreadsheets, email. Intel's pricing power on raw clock speed broke. The basis of competition shifted from clock speed to energy efficiency, portability, and cost — attributes formerly dismissed as niche.
**Result:** This overshoot created the opening for ARM-based mobile chips, which had lower raw performance but far better energy efficiency and cost profile. Mobile was a new-market foothold enabled by the overshoot of the desktop processor market. The classical symptom — declining willingness to pay for improvements on the historical basis of competition — was present throughout, detectable by anyone watching which features the market was and was not rewarding.

**Situation:** Full-service department stores in the 1950s serve a market in which customers pay 40 percent gross margins for expert floor salespeople, a wide selection, and a unified shopping experience. By the 1960s, customers are buying national-brand hard goods — paint, kitchen utensils, toys, sporting goods — that are familiar enough that the salesperson adds no value.
**Application:** (Innovator's Solution ch. 2; Seeing What's Next ch. 1.) Overshoot test: in the branded-hard-goods tier, were customers still paying premiums for the full-service bundle? No. The product sold itself. The service was paid for but no longer valued. Apparel and cosmetics buyers — higher-tier customers with more complex jobs to be done — were still undershot; they still benefited from expert advice and wanted the full-service experience. This is the classical pattern: overshoot starts at the bottom tier (branded hard goods) and has not yet reached the top (apparel, cosmetics). The discount retailers' opportunity is at the overshot tier, not at the full-service tier.
**Result:** Wal-Mart and Kmart took the overshot segment. Department stores fled up-market into higher-margin cosmetics and apparel at 50 percent gross margins. Over decades, overshoot crept upward, and discount retail expanded its scope into apparel (eventually) and beyond. The tier-by-tier diagnostic explained both where the initial attack would land and where it would move next.

**Situation:** In the early 2000s, long-distance telephony prices fall sharply. AT&T's historical premium pricing for long-distance service evaporates. The basis of competition appears to be shifting rapidly to price.
**Application:** Overshoot? Yes — long-distance telephony, which had charged premiums for decades, had become good enough on the historical dimensions (voice clarity, connection reliability, geographic coverage). Customers stopped paying for further improvements along those axes. MCI had already exploited the overshoot at the enterprise tier via low-end disruption (Execunet service, Seeing What's Next ch. 1). The overshoot creeping upward into residential long distance, combined with regulatory unbundling and VoIP technology, collapsed the long-distance premium.
**Result:** AT&T's long-distance business, once one of the most profitable in America, became a commodity within a decade. The signal was visible earlier — declining pricing power, emerging specialists (MCI, Sprint), shifting customer complaints from "I can't hear you" to "why am I paying so much" — and anyone tracking the overshoot metrics could have predicted the commoditization ahead of the accounting catching up to it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Read overshoot from median customer behavior.
**Why:** Overshoot starts at the bottom tier and creeps upward (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). The median customer can be perfectly content with current performance — or even undershot — while the bottom tier has been overshot for years. If you watch only the middle, you will see a healthy market long after low-end disruption has already gotten its foothold. Tier-by-tier analysis is the only reliable method. A useful quick check: break customers into at least three tiers — high, middle, low — and ask the pricing-power question separately for each.

**Don't:** Conflate "customers switching away" with overshoot.
**Why:** Customers can switch away for many reasons. A better competitor along the same axis at the same price is a sustaining displacement, not overshoot. Regulatory changes that restrict product use are not overshoot. Fashion shifts that move attention to a different category are not overshoot. Overshoot specifically requires that customers have stopped rewarding improvements *on the historical basis of competition* — they still value the product; they just no longer pay more for the next increment of the thing that used to define premium quality. Before calling it overshoot, rule out the alternatives.

**Don't:** Assume overshoot extends indefinitely.
**Why:** Just because the bottom tier is overshot today does not mean the top tier will be overshot next year. The trajectory can slow. Enabling technologies can stagnate. New performance dimensions can become important that reset undershoot conditions — when mobile broke out, it reintroduced undershoot along battery life, size, and connectivity, even though desktop CPUs had been overshot for years. Treat overshoot as a condition of a specific market along a specific basis of competition, not as a universal trend.

**Don't:** Detect overshoot and then do nothing.
**Why:** Overshoot is a precondition for low-end disruption, not the disruption itself. A market can sit in overshoot indefinitely — margins compressing, pricing power gone — without any entrant successfully disrupting, because the disruptor still has to build a business model that earns attractive profits at the discount prices the overshot customers are willing to pay. Many overshot markets simply commoditize, and everyone loses money together. If you identify overshoot as an investor or entrant, the next question is: does anyone have the cost structure to exploit it? If no one does, the opportunity is real but unclaimed — that is the entrepreneur's signal, not a guarantee of someone else's existing success.

**Don't:** Project the incumbent's past trajectory forward without checking whether the enabling technology still has room to progress.
**Why:** Overshoot means the market has passed what customers can use, given the *current* performance trajectory. But trajectories sometimes slow for non-overshoot reasons — Moore's Law slowing, for instance, or steel mill efficiency improvements running into physical limits. If the trajectory has slowed, the gap between what the product delivers and what customers can use may not continue to grow; the overshoot may be bounded. Check the technology's physical and cost roadmap, not just the curve of past improvement.

**Don't:** Listen to lead customers when trying to detect overshoot.
**Why:** Lead customers — the most demanding, often the most profitable — are reliably accurate about sustaining innovations and reliably inaccurate about disruptive ones ("Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", HBR 1995). They are at the top tier, often still undershot, and they will tell you the market wants more performance. That is true for *them*. It is not true for the bottom-tier customers who have gone quiet about new features and loud about price. Overshoot detection requires listening to the customers the incumbent does not usually hear from — the overshot ones, who have stopped making demands because they have stopped valuing what the incumbent is offering to sell them.
