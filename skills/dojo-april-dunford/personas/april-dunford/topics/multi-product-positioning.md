# Multi-Product Positioning

## When to Use
- You have (or are about to have) more than one product and need to decide how company positioning, product positioning, and product-family positioning relate to each other.
- You're adding a second product and unsure whether it needs its own positioning, inherits the existing positioning, or changes the company's positioning entirely.
- You have one product sold to multiple market segments and are debating whether each segment needs different positioning.
- You have multiple personas involved in one B2B purchase (champion, economic buyer, end users, IT) and you're trying to figure out whether to tailor positioning for each.

## Fails When
- **You're still a single-product company.** Don't pre-build company-vs-product positioning infrastructure for a company that still has one product. Single product = single positioning = single brand. Fewer things for customers to remember is always better early on.
- **You create separate positioning for each target segment when the differentiated value is actually the same across segments.** That's not positioning, that's just tailoring content. Treat it as such.
- **You try to fix a product-strategy problem with positioning.** If two segments genuinely need completely different positioning — different competitors, different differentiated value, different roadmaps — you don't have a positioning problem, you have a "you probably need to fork the product" problem.

## Core Concept

Most positioning discussion assumes a single product sold to a single segment. The real world gets messier. You end up with questions like: do we need company positioning separate from product positioning? If we have two products, do we position them separately or together as a "platform"? If we sell to banks and insurance companies, do we need two positions? If the purchase involves a champion plus an IT person plus an economic buyer, do we write three pitches?

Here's the decision tree.

**Single-product company: don't overthink it.** One product, one position, one brand. Slack, pre-acquisition, was just Slack — company and product were the same thing. Customers have enough trouble holding one brand in their heads. Don't make them hold two. If you're planning to become the Dyson Labs of your market, great, figure out the multi-brand architecture when you actually have the second product. Until then, single.

Related aside on naming: product names don't have to be creative brands. At IBM the database division had the brand DB2, and each product had a descriptive name like "DB2 Information Integrator." Boring? Yes. Clear? Also yes. Startups tend to over-invest in creative product names; customers don't care. Descriptive is usually better than clever for anything that's not the main brand.

**Multi-product: three shapes to pick from.**

1. **Lead product + follow-on products.** You always sell one product first, and other products come later as upgrades or cross-sells to the same account. Salesforce did this: Sales Cloud was the lead, and Marketing Cloud positioned itself as "the best marketing platform for teams that already have Salesforce CRM." Much easier to position Marketing Cloud as "best for Salesforce customers" than as "best marketing platform in the world." You get to assume context from the lead product.

2. **Product family / platform.** Multiple products sold to the same type of company, where you want buyers to know about all of them and understand the combined value. You can position them together as a family or a platform. (Side note on "platform": historically the word meant something with tightly integrated functionality that could be extended. Today people use it for any collection of products regardless of integration. Customers don't reliably distinguish "platform" from "product family" anymore. Don't agonize over the word; pick whichever conveys how integrated the products actually are.)

3. **Split company positioning.** Two or more products sold to genuinely different types of companies. Usually these should be positioned separately — different competitors, different differentiated value, different audiences. The open question is whether there's an umbrella company story that helps, or whether the products should basically operate as separate brands. Sometimes expertise in one market helps build trust in the adjacent one (a streaming-video company serving both big media and embedded developers). Sometimes the segments are unrelated and it's cleaner to run two independent stories — a holding-company architecture, even if it's not formally incorporated that way.

**Multiple market segments, same product: usually one positioning.** Common mistake: assuming different segments need different positioning. Usually they don't. Positioning is centered on your differentiated value. If your differentiated value is the same across banks and insurance, positioning is the same across banks and insurance — even if competitors are slightly different in each. You tailor *content* (campaigns, case studies, website imagery, vertical-specific terminology), not positioning.

The red flag: if you genuinely need completely different positioning for each segment — different competitors, different capabilities, different differentiated value — that's not a positioning problem. That's a product-strategy problem. Those segments will eventually demand different roadmaps, and you'll end up forking the product or disappointing half your customers. Solve the product-strategy problem; don't paper over it with positioning.

**Multiple personas in a B2B purchase: position for the champion.** A B2B deal typically involves 5-8 stakeholders — economic buyer, champion, end users, IT, legal, procurement. Don't build different positioning for each. The champion is the person doing the research, building the shortlist, and driving the purchase recommendation. They're the person across from you in the first call. If you don't win the champion, you never get to engage the others. Position for the champion. Worry about the rest as objection handling once the champion is on board. The champion's concerns are about whether the product can solve the problem; the other stakeholders' concerns are about risk and fit. Different conversations, sequenced.

## How to Apply

1. **If single-product: stop here.** One positioning. One brand. Move on.

2. **If multi-product: classify the shape.** Is the second product a follow-on sold after the first, or a parallel product, or aimed at a completely different customer? The shape tells you the positioning architecture.

3. **For follow-on products: positioning assumes the lead product.** Don't position the second product in isolation — position it as the best option for customers who already have the first. You get to inherit trust and context from the lead.

4. **For product families: write one umbrella position and individual product positions that cascade from it.** The company position expresses the combined value; each product position is about its specific slot in the family. Keep them consistent — product positions should be recognizable as members of the family.

5. **For segment variation: keep positioning constant, tailor content.** Same core positioning for banks and insurance companies. Different case studies, campaigns, trade show booths, and vertical-specific vocabulary. If you find yourself writing fundamentally different differentiated value for each segment, stop — you have a product-strategy problem.

6. **For persona variation: write for the champion, handle the rest as objections.** The IT person's security questions, the CFO's ROI questions, the end user's usability questions — these are objection-handling material, not separate positioning. Arm the champion with answers; don't build parallel pitches.

7. **Keep it as simple as you can get away with.** Every additional positioning artifact is another thing to keep consistent, another thing to update when the market shifts, and another thing for customers to be confused by. Push back on "let's do separate positioning for every segment/persona/product line" unless the evidence genuinely demands it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A single-product startup wants to brand "the company" separately from "the product" because they're planning future products.
**Application:** Don't. You don't have future products yet. Having two brands — one the customer encounters and one the customer doesn't — doubles the complexity without any upside. Be a single brand. When (if!) you add the second product, reopen the question.
**Result:** The founder saves the budget and mental overhead, customers have one name to remember, and the architecture question gets deferred to when it's actually needed.

**Situation:** A company adds a second product aimed at a different buyer persona within the same customer.
**Application:** Position the second product assuming the customer already has the first. The positioning is "best [category] for customers already using our lead product" — much easier to defend than "best [category] in the world."
**Result:** The sales team can pitch the second product in customer accounts with context instead of from scratch. Cross-sell rates improve.

**Situation:** A company sells one product to banks and insurance companies and thinks they need two positions.
**Application:** Run the five components for each segment. Compare. Almost always, the differentiated value is the same even if the competitors are slightly different. Conclude that positioning is shared, content is tailored. Build one core position, two sets of vertical content (case studies, campaigns, terminology).
**Result:** One sales deck with swappable case study slides, not two independent decks. Consistent narrative. Less marketing overhead. Happier sales team because the pitch is familiar everywhere.

**Situation:** A team wants to write separate pitches for the CEO, the CTO, and the end-user personas.
**Application:** No. Write one pitch for the champion — the person who owns the shortlist and drives the decision. The other stakeholders' concerns are objection-handling content. Train the champion to handle those objections. The pitch stays singular.
**Result:** Clearer sales execution. No parallel pitches to maintain. The champion becomes empowered to evangelize internally, which is how B2B deals actually close.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build company-vs-product positioning architecture as a single-product company.
**Why:** It's a solution to a problem you don't have yet. It confuses customers and doubles the content load. Figure it out when you have the second product.

**Don't:** Use "platform" as a synonym for "product family" unless there's real integration.
**Why:** The word has lost most of its meaning but customers still have vague expectations from it. If your products aren't integrated in a meaningful way, calling it a platform sets you up for a credibility gap. Use "family" or "suite" or the plain product list instead.

**Don't:** Treat segment variation as a positioning problem.
**Why:** If the differentiated value is the same across segments, the positioning is the same. Segment-specific content is not segment-specific positioning. Keep them separate.

**Don't:** Write separate positioning for every persona in the buying group.
**Why:** You can't win a B2B deal by pitching separately to each stakeholder. You win by getting the champion to champion. Position for the champion; handle the rest as objections the champion can carry into internal conversations.

**Don't:** Ignore the signal when segments genuinely need different positioning.
**Why:** If after honest analysis two segments really do need different competitors, different capabilities, and different differentiated value — that's a product-strategy problem. Your roadmap will eventually fork. Better to see that early and make a conscious decision (dedicate two products, dedicate two companies, or drop one segment) than to paper over it with two positioning documents and hope the rest of the business falls in line.
