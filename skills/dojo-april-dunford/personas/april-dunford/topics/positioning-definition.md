# Positioning: The Definition

## When to Use
- Before any messaging, website copy, sales deck, or campaign is written.
- When someone on the team says "we just need a better tagline" — that's a positioning problem wearing a tagline costume.
- When new prospects can't figure out what you sell but existing customers love you.
- When sales cycles are long, close rates are low, and you're losing to competitors who look worse on paper.
- When you're under price pressure and everyone thinks you're interchangeable with the alternatives.

## Fails When
- **The product is truly undifferentiated.** Positioning is not a band-aid for a crummy product. If you deliver nothing that customers can't get from the alternatives, no amount of repositioning will save you. Go build something different first.
- **You're pre-launch with no customers.** You can write a *positioning thesis* — a best guess — but real positioning requires enough happy customers that you can see a pattern in who loves you and why. Until then, keep it loose.
- **You try to make positioning "own" the overall business strategy.** Positioning is an input to strategy, not a replacement for it. If your strategy is broken, tighter positioning won't fix the business.

## Core Concept

Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about. Read that sentence slowly, because it's doing a lot of work. Three pieces, and all three have to be true.

**"Deliberately defining"** — not accidentally inheriting. Most companies don't position their products at all; they end up with a default position based on what they *thought* they were building when they started. The problem is, products evolve. Markets shift. What you set out to build is rarely what you end up with, and the market category you originally aimed at may no longer be the one that makes your strengths obvious. If you don't consciously choose your positioning, the market chooses for you — badly.

**"How you are the best at something"** — not everything. Not even several things. One thing. Positioning is a choice about what you win on, and a choice about what you don't. Michael Porter had this right: "Strategy is about making choices, trade-offs; it's about deliberately choosing to be different." If you're trying to be the best at "fast, easy, and reliable," you're not positioning — you're reciting the same platitude every one of your competitors also recites. Every competitor says they're fast, easy, and reliable. That is a table-stake, not a differentiator.

**"That a defined market cares a lot about"** — not a market that *could* care, not a market that *might* care, not "business buyers broadly." A specific segment of buyers who look at your unique value and lose their minds. Your positioning isn't worth anything if the people who care about it are three people in Kansas. And it's not worth anything either if you claim to serve "everyone" — because if everyone's your customer, nobody is. You have to find the group where your specific differentiated value is the thing that tips them over, and aim everything at them.

Think of positioning as context-setting for your product. Joshua Bell, one of the greatest violinists alive, sells out concert halls at $300 a ticket. The *Washington Post* put him on a corner outside a DC subway station playing the same Stradivarius and he made $32 in 45 minutes. Seven people stopped. His product — the music — didn't change. The context did. In the concert hall, he's a genius. On the corner, he's "just a guy trying to make a buck." A world-class product, badly positioned, can fail. Your job is to make sure customers encounter your product inside the frame where its value is obvious.

Positioning is the foundation. Every sales tactic, every marketing campaign, every piece of content uses it as an input. If positioning is wrong, everything downstream is working against a headwind. If it's right, the wind is at your back and it feels like you're cheating.

## How to Apply

1. **Acknowledge you probably haven't actually done positioning yet.** Most teams have a tagline, a pitch deck, and a homepage — none of which is positioning. Positioning is the underlying decisions those artifacts reflect. If you can't articulate your five components (alternatives, unique attributes, value, target customer, category) in a sentence each, you don't have positioning yet. You have marketing copy.

2. **Stop writing a positioning *statement* and start doing positioning *work*.** The "For [X] who [Y], our product is a [Z] that [W]" template is garbage. I had to fill one of these out at IBM for a database and it came out as "For people who want a non-mainframe database, our non-mainframe database is a non-mainframe database which provides a non-mainframe database." The template assumes you already know the answers. Positioning is about *figuring out* the answers.

3. **Start from your happiest customers, not your product.** The customers who buy fast, pay full price, renew, and tell their friends — those people are the key to your positioning. What do they have in common? What did they almost buy instead? Why did they pick you? Your own head is the worst place to do positioning; your best customers' heads are the best place.

4. **Treat positioning as a company-wide decision, not a marketing project.** Positioning impacts product roadmap, pricing, target-account lists, hiring, the investor pitch, and the sales script. The CEO or business unit leader has to own it. If marketing is off doing positioning alone in a room, it won't stick.

5. **Check it with the sales team.** If your "positioning" doesn't change what a rep says in a first sales call, it isn't positioning. It's a document. The question to ask at the end of any positioning exercise is: can the head of sales rewrite the first-call deck from this? If the answer is no, you're not done.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup founder tells you they "need better positioning" because their homepage converts badly.
**Application:** Before touching the homepage, you ask: who are your five happiest customers, what did they almost buy instead of you, what's the one thing you do that none of those alternatives do, and what value does that unlock for them? Nine times out of ten, they can't answer those questions cleanly. The homepage isn't the problem. The underlying positioning is undefined, which is why the homepage is vague.
**Result:** You redirect the project. No homepage rewrite until the five components are on a page. Then the homepage writes itself.

**Situation:** A database startup is getting kicked out of every sales meeting because prospects already have a database and don't want another one.
**Application:** The "default" position — database — isn't doing them any favors. Their differentiated value is fast analytical queries on huge datasets, which is not what people hire a database for. A customer offhand mentions "you're more like a data warehouse." They reposition from "database" to "data warehouse" and suddenly prospects stop arguing and start listening.
**Result:** Same product. Different context. Deals start closing. This is mine, by the way — it's the story that convinced me positioning was a real discipline, not a vibes exercise.

**Situation:** A team has run a "positioning workshop" with marketing, produced a one-pager, filed it, and nothing changed.
**Application:** The workshop produced a document but didn't change any behavior. No product decision, no pricing decision, no sales pitch rewrite, no content shift. The positioning is meaningless because it doesn't flow into anything.
**Result:** Either the team redoes the work with the CEO in the room and with a commitment to rewrite the sales deck and homepage at the end, or they accept that nothing will happen. You can't have positioning in a drawer.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat positioning as a tagline contest. "We're the Uber of X!" is not positioning. Nobody on your customer's procurement team can justify a purchase using a tagline.
**Why:** Positioning drives the *substance* of your pitch — what you're the best at, for whom, and against what alternatives. A tagline is at best a compressed reminder of the positioning. You cannot substitute the reminder for the substance.

**Don't:** Let the marketing team own positioning alone.
**Why:** Marketing doesn't control the product, the pricing, the sales motion, or the roadmap. If positioning lives only in marketing, every other function will execute on an inconsistent story. The business leader has to own it, or it's dead on arrival.

**Don't:** Assume your positioning today is the positioning forever.
**Why:** Products evolve. Markets shift. Competitors emerge and die. Buyer preferences move. Arm & Hammer was baking soda; now it's a deodorizer. Wattpad was a writing platform; now it's an entertainment company. Your positioning has to evolve with your business. Check it twice a year minimum, and whenever something big changes — acquisition, funding round, new exec, bad quarter, surprise competitor.

**Don't:** Position the product the way you'd position the company to investors.
**Why:** Investors are buying your future. Customers are buying a solution to a problem they have right now. When an investor hears "disruption," they hear opportunity. When a customer hears "disruption," they hear "this is going to replace the thing I already paid for and I'll have to learn something new." Use your vision with investors. Use your current differentiated value with customers. Confusing the two is a classic way to ruin both pitches.

**Don't:** Let customers tell you what market category you're in.
**Why:** Customers are not positioning experts. They'll put you in whatever market is closest to something they already know. That market is almost never the one that makes your strengths obvious. Listen to customers about their problems and their alternatives, not about where you should be positioned. That's your job.
