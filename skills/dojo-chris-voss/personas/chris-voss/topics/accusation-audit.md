# The Accusation Audit

## When to Use
- You are walking into a conversation where the other side is already loaded up with negative things to think or say about you, and you want to defuse them before they go off.
- You missed a deadline, broke a commitment, or delivered bad work, and you have to face the person who was counting on you.
- You are about to make a big ask (raise, scope change, favor, renegotiation) that an outside observer would call greedy, unrealistic, or tone-deaf.
- You are delivering news that will land badly, and you want the conversation to be about resolution rather than blame.

## Fails When
- You name one soft accusation and skip the real one. The real one is still in their head, still loaded, and now they think you're either clueless or slippery.
- You deliver it with a smirk or a knowing tone. Sarcasm signals you don't actually believe the accusation is valid, and that blows up trust faster than staying silent would have.
- You try to weaponize it. Naming accusations the other side wasn't thinking to plant doubt about yourself is manipulation and people feel it.
- You do it halfway through the meeting, after things have already gone sideways. By then the accusations are already live in their head and you look like you're scrambling.

## Core Concept
The Accusation Audit is listing every terrible thing your counterpart might think or say about you, and then saying it first. Before they can call you greedy, unreasonable, unfair, or careless, you beat them to it. You bring those accusations into the open, label them, and take the sting out.

Lawyers call this taking the sting out. In opening statements, a good defense attorney doesn't hide from the bad facts. They walk the jury through everything their client is accused of, right up front. By the time the prosecution raises any of it, it's old news. The shock value is gone. The Accusation Audit works the exact same way. When you say, "You're probably going to think I'm being greedy by asking this," you've just defused greedy. Even if they were thinking it, they can't say it now without sounding petty. And most of the time, they'll actually argue against the accusation. "No, no, I don't think you're being greedy at all." That's when you know it's working.

Here's the thing most people get wrong. They walk into difficult conversations hoping the negatives won't come up. They figure if they don't mention the elephant in the room, maybe the other person won't either. That's magical thinking. The elephant is there. Everybody sees it. Pretending otherwise just makes you look clueless or deceptive. Name the elephant. Call it out. You demonstrate self-awareness, you build trust, and you take control of the narrative.

## How to Apply
1. Before the conversation, sit down and list every negative accusation your counterpart might be making in their head. Don't be reasonable about it. Go worst-case. What's the most uncharitable read of your ask? What would they vent to a friend about after this call?
2. Turn each accusation into a label. Same format as always: "You're probably going to think," "You might feel like," "This might seem like," "I know this could come across as." State them as possibilities, not facts. You're not confirming you are greedy, you're acknowledging someone could see it that way.
3. Deliver them at the start, before the substance. Use the script pattern: "You're going to think X. You're going to think Y. You're probably worried about Z." Stack the labels. Don't hedge. Name the big ones, not just the baby ones.
4. Bridge to your point with something like "I've thought about all of that, and I'm still hoping we can find a way to make this work." You haven't agreed that you are greedy. You've acknowledged the frame and invited them past it.
5. Then shut up. Let the accusations land. Often the other side will actually argue against them. That's the move working.

## Examples

**Situation:** Anna runs a prime contractor that has to cut a subcontractor's pay on a government job to stay in budget. The sub believes this work was promised to them from day one. Walking in cold, Anna gets eaten alive.
**Application:** Anna opens with, "You're going to think we're a big, bad prime contractor when we're done. You're going to feel like this work was promised to you from the beginning, and that we're going back on our word. You're probably worried about how this affects your team and your revenue for the year. I understand all of that." Then she gets to the ask.
**Result:** The sub doesn't unload. The defensive speech they'd been rehearsing is already neutralized. Conversation moves to what's actually negotiable. Saved a million dollars on the contract and kept the relationship intact.

**Situation:** You missed a critical deadline. You have to call the client. Every instinct says minimize it, explain the circumstances, offer a quick fix.
**Application:** "Before I explain what happened, I want to acknowledge something. You're probably thinking we dropped the ball. You're probably thinking we don't take your project seriously. You're probably worried about whether you made the right choice working with us. Those are all reasonable things to be thinking right now."
**Result:** You've taken away their ammunition. There is nothing left to accuse you of that you haven't already acknowledged. The conversation shifts from blame to what happens next, which is the conversation you actually needed to have.

**Situation:** Asking your boss for a raise significantly above the standard increase, in a year when the company is tightening.
**Application:** "I want to talk to you about compensation, and you might think this is tone-deaf given everything the company is dealing with. You're probably going to think I'm not seeing the bigger picture. You might feel like my timing is bad. I've thought about all of that. I still wanted to have this conversation because there are some things about my role worth discussing."
**Result:** You come across as self-aware, not entitled. The easy dismissal ("How can they ask for this right now?") is gone. You've earned the conversation.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Name only the soft accusations while skipping the real one. "I know this is short notice" when they're also thinking you're underprepared, disrespectful of their time, and dumping your mess on them.
**Why:** A partial Accusation Audit is worse than none. You've signaled that you are self-aware enough to see the easy criticisms but not self-aware enough to see the real ones. That reads as either clueless or slick, and both erode trust.

**Don't:** Deliver it with sarcasm or a smirk. "Oh, I'm SURE you're going to think I'm being SO greedy here."
**Why:** Sarcasm tells the other side you don't actually believe the accusation is valid. It signals the opposite of what you want, which is genuine acknowledgment. They hear contempt instead of humility, and you've now confirmed the very thing you were trying to defuse.

**Don't:** Use it to plant accusations they weren't thinking. "You might think this deal is too good to be true."
**Why:** The goal is to clear the air of actual concerns, not to seed new ones. Manufactured accusations feel like manipulation, because they are, and people pick up on it instantly. Trust drops. You'd have been better off saying nothing.

**Don't:** Wait until things get tense and then try to smuggle it in. "Look, you're probably thinking I haven't considered your side here."
**Why:** At that point the accusation is already live. You're not defusing it, you're confirming what they've been stewing on for the last ten minutes. The Accusation Audit only works at the start, before the other side has locked in on any single grievance. That's the whole point of taking the sting out early.
