# The Three Negotiator Types

## When to Use
- Before any significant negotiation. Diagnose type first so you can calibrate every technique you deploy.
- When your standard approach is bouncing off. The issue is probably style mismatch, not content.
- When silence happens and you don't know what it means. Same silence means three different things across the three types.
- When you're about to concede, push, or press — check the type first, because the same move lands differently on each.

## Fails When
- You lock in the type after a single data point. Types are working hypotheses, not permanent labels. People show different sides under different conditions.
- You use typing as a shortcut to stop listening. The point of typing is to listen more carefully, not less.
- You assume the type is their problem, not your adaptation challenge. If the negotiation is failing, adapt to them. They're not going to become you.
- You confuse Accommodator warmth with weakness. An Accommodator who feels the friendship is over turns cold and deadly fast.

## Core Concept

Three negotiator types. Three different "normals." And the math that should keep you humble: with three types in the world, there's a 66% chance your counterpart is a different type than you. That's Voss's "I Am Normal" trap. You unconsciously project your own style onto the counterpart, assume they think like you, and get frustrated when they don't respond the way you expected.

The Golden Rule needs editing. Don't treat others the way you want to be treated. Treat them the way they need to be treated.

### Analyst (Flight Response)

Methodical. Not in a hurry. Prides themselves on minimizing mistakes. Prefers working alone or in small, controlled groups. Rarely deviates from stated goals once committed. Comes across as cold and distant even when they care. Warren Buffett. Elon Musk.

To an Analyst, conflict is inefficient. They're not trying to beat you; they're trying to not lose to their own ignorance. They value preparation over improvisation, precision over approximation, thinking over talking.

**What silence means: they're thinking.** Don't fill it. Don't interrupt. Don't read it as disagreement. If you talk over their thinking, you'll get an incomplete answer and annoy them.

### Accommodator (Friend Response)

Sociable. Peace-seeking. Optimistic. Values rapport above all else. Asks about your weekend and remembers your kid's name. Wants to remain friends even if the deal falls apart. Richard Branson.

Accommodators are hope-focused but poor at implementation. They over-promise because saying no feels confrontational. They agree to things they can't actually deliver. And here's the twist: when an Accommodator feels the friendship is gone, they become deadly creatures. That warmth flips cold in an instant. Don't mistake friendliness for weakness.

**What silence means: anger.** They've disengaged emotionally. If an Accommodator stops talking, the rapport is broken. Repair it before you try to move forward.

### Assertive (Fight Response)

Direct. Candid. Time is money. Loves winning above all else. Short emails. Interrupts. Gets to the point. Doesn't worry much about future interactions. Can fight with you at noon and play golf at three. Heals from emotional wounds like Wolverine. Donald Trump.

The critical thing about Assertives: they cannot listen until they feel heard. Every sentence out of their mouth is a bullet they need to fire before they can holster and listen. Fight them for airtime and you lose. Let them talk, mirror them, label what you hear, and work toward "that's right" (which with an Assertive often comes out as "that's it exactly"). Only after that can you present your position.

**What silence means: they've run out of things to say.** Rare. And it's your cue to fill the space, because they see every silence as a chance to reload.

### The core diagnostic move: words, music, dance

Listen for how they talk, not just what they say. Words are the content. Music is the tone. Dance is the rhythm and pace. An Analyst's dance is slow and steady. An Assertive's is fast and interruptive. An Accommodator's is warm and meandering. All three can agree to use the same word "yes," but the music and dance around it tells you what the yes actually means.

## How to Apply

1. **Pre-diagnose before you open your mouth.** Their emails, their LinkedIn, how they structured the meeting, who they brought, what time they scheduled it. All of it is signal. An Assertive books 30-minute slots. An Analyst sends an agenda. An Accommodator asks how you've been.

2. **Confirm in the first few minutes.** Are they making small talk (Accommodator), asking detailed questions (Analyst), or getting to the point (Assertive)? Are they interrupting you or letting you talk? Are they warm, neutral, or direct?

3. **Identify your own type and watch for its shadow side.** Analysts need to remember relationships matter. Accommodators need to remember a "yes" means nothing without follow-through. Assertives need to remember listening isn't losing.

4. **Calibrate your techniques by type.**
   - **Mirrors.** With Analysts, use them to draw out thinking, then pause long. With Accommodators, keep the mirror warm. With Assertives, mirror to keep them talking until they feel heard.
   - **Labels.** With Analysts, label their concern about accuracy and mistakes. With Accommodators, label their relationship concerns. With Assertives, label their drive for results.
   - **Calibrated questions.** With Analysts, let them demonstrate expertise. With Accommodators, ground them in implementation details. With Assertives, ask questions after they've talked, not before.
   - **That's right.** With Analysts, summarize the data and logic. With Accommodators, summarize the relationship and shared goals. With Assertives, summarize their position and priorities.

5. **Match pace and rhythm.** Don't match aggression, but do match tempo. An Analyst feels rushed if you talk at Assertive speed. An Assertive feels like you're wasting their time if you speak at Analyst pace.

6. **Don't match type — adapt to it.** You're not pretending to be them. You're adjusting how you deploy your own techniques so they land. Your content stays. Your delivery adapts.

## Examples

**Analyst in the wild.** A procurement lead sends a 600-word email with three numbered questions and a link to a spreadsheet. She hasn't responded to your last email in four days. Don't chase. Don't call to "check in." Reply with precise data, flag one risk she hasn't thought of (she'll appreciate being warned early), and give her space to process. When you next meet, if she goes quiet after a question, stay quiet too. She's thinking, not stonewalling.

**Accommodator in the wild.** A partner at a firm agrees to everything in the meeting. Timeline, scope, pricing, delivery. You walk out feeling great. Three weeks later, nothing's moved. The Rule of Three would have caught this: get them to agree three different ways, including a calibrated question about implementation. "How will we make sure this actually gets done on your side?" The quick, vague answer is your red flag. The slow, specific answer is your commitment.

**Assertive in the wild.** The CEO opens with: "Here's what we need. Tell me if you can do it." Don't interrupt. Don't counter-pitch. Mirror the last three words: "You need it?" He keeps talking. Label: "It sounds like speed is what really matters here." He keeps going. Summarize: "So what I'm hearing is you need this shipped by Q2, you need it to integrate with X, and you've been burned before on timeline slippage — is that right?" You want "that's right" or "that's it exactly." Only now, after he feels heard, will he actually listen to what you're going to propose.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

- **Projecting your silence onto theirs.** If you're an Assertive and the Analyst goes quiet, you'll read it as disengagement and start talking to fill the void. You just interrupted their thinking. If you're an Analyst and the Accommodator goes quiet, you'll read it as thinking. It's not. They're hurt. Silence means different things. Know which.

- **Treating an Accommodator's "yes" as a commitment.** Their yes is often a peace offering, not a plan. Use the Rule of Three. Follow up with an implementation question. If the answer is vague, the commitment is vague.

- **Fighting an Assertive for airtime.** You can't win this fight. They'll keep talking until they feel heard. Let them. Mirror, label, get to "that's right." Then — and only then — take your turn.
