# Black Swans and Leverage

## When to Use
- The negotiation is stuck and nothing is moving. Something you don't know is blocking progress.
- Their behavior doesn't make sense. They're turning down a good offer, pushing oddly hard, or sending mixed signals. Their behavior makes sense to them. You're missing the information that would make it make sense to you.
- You feel outgunned. A single hidden fact can flip the power balance.
- Mirrors, labels, and calibrated questions aren't getting traction. You're probably working off false assumptions.
- You want leverage that doesn't poison the relationship.

## Fails When
- You treat every deal like it has a secret twist. Sometimes the deal is exactly what it looks like. Hunt, but don't force the find.
- You find one Black Swan and stop. The hypothesis is three per side. Finding one should raise your curiosity, not lower it.
- You weaponize what you learn. Black Swan discovery is meant to build a better deal, not to exploit the counterpart. Use it as a threat and you leave behind resentment that kills the next deal.
- You use negative leverage when normative leverage was on the table. Threats work short-term and cost long-term.
- You neglect the stated needs while hunting the hidden ones. Do both.

## Core Concept

Black Swans are the unknown unknowns. The pieces of information you don't even know to ask about, that would change everything if you uncovered them. Not the counterpart's budget (that's a known unknown). The fact that the decision-maker was burned by a consultant two years ago and got humiliated in front of his board. That's a Black Swan.

The hypothesis, based on thousands of negotiations: every deal has at least three Black Swans per side. The negotiator who finds them first wins.

Black Swans only matter because they change your leverage. There are three kinds of leverage, and knowing which one you're holding tells you how to use it.

**Positive leverage** is the ability to give or withhold something they want. Money, expertise, access, a relationship. Simple. But often not enough. If they need $100K and you have $50K, positive leverage doesn't save you.

**Negative leverage** is the ability to cause pain, embarrassment, or loss. It works, but it leaves what Voss calls toxic residue. Every implicit threat poisons future interactions. In hostage work, the FBI had negative leverage (storming the building). They rarely used it because people died. In business: just because you can hurt them doesn't mean you should.

**Normative leverage** is the elegant one, and it comes directly from Black Swan discovery. You use their own stated values, standards, or identity against them. Nobody wants to look like a hypocrite. When a "partner-first" company is treating you like a vendor, you have normative leverage. When someone built their reputation on fair dealing and is trying to squeeze you, you have normative leverage. Their stated values conflict with their current behavior. Hold that up.

The counterpart's "religion" is the worldview that makes their apparently crazy behavior logical to them. Find the religion, and you find the normative leverage. And you stop being surprised.

## How to Apply

1. **Assume Black Swans exist.** Walk in believing there are at least three things you don't know that would change everything. This keeps you in curiosity mode instead of assumption mode. Most negotiators think they have the picture after fifteen minutes. They don't.

2. **Get face time.** Black Swans reveal themselves in tone, hesitation, and facial reactions that email kills. Negotiate in person where possible. The 7-38-55 rule (7% words, 38% tone, 55% body) is why.

3. **Listen for what doesn't make sense.** When their behavior seems irrational, ask yourself: "What would I have to know for this to be rational?" People always act on some logic. You just don't see theirs yet.

4. **Use labels, not questions.** Direct questions make people defensive. Labels open doors. "It seems like there's something else going on here." "It sounds like this isn't really about the money." "It looks like there's a reason you're hesitant." Labels invite disclosure without demanding it.

5. **Ask calibrated questions designed to expose the hidden.** A few that do heavy lifting:
   - "What are we up against here?"
   - "What happens if you do nothing?"
   - "What does doing nothing cost you?"
   - "How does this resonate with what your company prides itself on?"
   - "What does success look like for the people not in this room?"

6. **Pay attention to the unguarded moments.** Beginnings and endings of meetings. Hallway conversations. Dinners. The bathroom walk. Guards drop when the formal negotiation pauses. Voss says he's found more Black Swans at restaurants than at tables.

7. **Once you find one, keep hunting.** Finding one Black Swan is a signal there are more. Don't declare victory and stop exploring.

## Examples

**The tobacco farmer on the National Mall.** 2003. Dwight Watson drove a tractor onto the National Mall and threatened to blow it up over tobacco subsidies. Negotiators spent two days getting nowhere. His demands seemed disproportionate to any rational outcome.

The Black Swan: Watson was a military veteran. His religion was the military honor code. He'd given his word to his father, also a vet, that he'd fight for the family farm. Once negotiators saw the religion, they worked within it. They reminded him that soldiers don't give up after just two days. They suggested the "dawn of the third day" was the honorable time for a soldier to make his final call, fight or retreat with dignity. He surrendered peacefully. Normative leverage replaced what would otherwise have been negative leverage (storming the tractor).

**The budget that didn't exist.** A consultant kept hearing "we have to stay within budget" from a prospect who wouldn't name a number. Multiple proposals at different price points, none working. Finally, instead of another proposal, the consultant used a label: "It sounds like there's something about this budget process that's creating complications. What are we up against here?"

The Black Swan surfaced. There was no set budget. The real issue was that the decision-maker had been burned by a previous consultant who blew past the agreed scope, embarrassing him in front of his board. His "budget" language was fear of looking foolish again. Once the consultant understood this, the offer shifted to a capped engagement with milestone checkpoints. They weren't selling consulting. They were selling peace of mind. The deal closed at a higher rate than originally proposed, because it addressed the actual fear.

**Normative leverage in everyday business.** A vendor you've used for years announces a price increase. Instead of pushing back on price, you pull up their website where they market themselves as "partnership-first." You say: "Given how much you've talked about partnership over the years, help me understand how an 8% increase fits that philosophy when we're mid-contract." You haven't threatened. You haven't argued. You've held their stated values up to the behavior. Normative leverage.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

- **"I found the Black Swan, now I'll squeeze them with it."** Wrong instinct. The point of Black Swan discovery is to build a better deal that accounts for the real need you just uncovered. Squeeze them and you get one win and burn the relationship. Use the insight to create options they couldn't see before, and you get a deal plus goodwill.

- **"They must be lying, they must be hiding something."** Black Swan hunting isn't paranoia about deception. It's humility about your own blind spots. The hidden information is usually something the counterpart doesn't think to mention, not something they're actively hiding. Approach with curiosity, not suspicion.

- **"Direct questions will surface it faster."** Direct questions trigger defensiveness and put people in answer-mode instead of reflection-mode. "It seems like there's more going on here" pulls more than "What aren't you telling me?" every single time.
