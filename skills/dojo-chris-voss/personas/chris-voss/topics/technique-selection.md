# Technique Selection

## When to Use
- In the moment, when you need to pull the right tool out of the bag and you have seconds to decide.
- When you've been trained on every technique but freeze when the conversation moves fast.
- When you notice yourself reaching for the same technique (usually mirroring or labeling) regardless of situation, and it's not landing.
- When coaching someone through a live negotiation and they need a decision tree, not a lecture.

## Fails When
- You select a technique before you've diagnosed the situation. The order is diagnose, then select. A mirror deployed on an Analyst who's mid-thought interrupts their processing. A calibrated question to a fatigued counterpart runs into a wall. Wrong technique, right situation, same bad outcome.
- You run through the techniques in sequence instead of picking one. "Let me mirror, then label, then ask a calibrated question, then get to that's right, then…" You look like a checklist robot. Pick one move, deploy it, see what happens, then pick the next.
- You treat the technique as the point. The point is reading the counterpart and giving them what they need in that moment. The technique is a vehicle.

## Core Concept

Voss spent 24 years watching negotiators succeed and fail. The pattern: technique matters less than technique *selection*. The best negotiators he worked with weren't the ones who knew the most moves. They were the ones who could read the room and pull the right tool in a split second.

Before you open your mouth, ask one question: **What is the emotional state of my counterpart right now?**

Everything flows from this. The human brain processes emotion before logic. System 1 (fast, emotional, reactive) runs the show until System 2 (slow, logical) gets permission to engage. You don't get to the logical conversation until you've addressed the emotional one.

So read them first. Then select.

## How to Apply

The decision tree, from the top:

### If they're emotional or upset → Labels + Tactical Empathy

Raised voice. Closed body language. Venting. Sharp tone. Stop everything else. You cannot reason with someone whose amygdala is firing. Logic bounces off. Name the emotion out loud. "It seems like you're frustrated with how this has been handled." "It sounds like this hasn't been a good experience." Labeling activates the prefrontal cortex and begins to calm the amygdala. When you name an emotion, you tame it (Matthew Lieberman's neuroscience, UCLA).

Address emotion before logic. Always. Try to solve the problem while they're upset and you'll fail twice: once on the problem and once on the relationship.

### If you need information → Labels (not questions)

Counterintuitive. Questions feel confrontational. They put people on the defensive. They guard their answers. Labels invite elaboration without demanding it. "It sounds like there's something else going on here." "It looks like this isn't just about the price." People fill in the gaps. They explain themselves. They reveal constraints, fears, and real motivations that a direct question would never pull.

In Voss's evolution: early career he asked questions constantly. As he got better, he found labels pulled more information with less resistance.

### If you need to say no → Calibrated "How" questions (the four-step escalation)

1. "How am I supposed to do that?"
2. "Your offer is very generous. I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me."
3. "I'm sorry, but I'm afraid I just can't do that."
4. "I'm sorry, no."

Start at step one. Most negotiations never get past it. "How am I supposed to do that?" is the greatest calibrated question in negotiation. It forces the counterpart to confront the unreasonableness of their own request without you having to call it unreasonable. Makes your problem their problem. Gets them bidding against themselves.

You can repeat step one multiple times. Every time they come back with a demand, hit them with another "How am I supposed to do that?" Watch them solve the problem for you.

### If they're ghosting you → "Have you given up on...?"

Emails ignored. Calls unreturned. The deal is stalling and you don't know why. One message: "Have you given up on [the project / us working together / finding a solution]?" Near 100% response rate. Responses come fast, usually three to five minutes. The phrase triggers loss aversion. Nobody wants to be the one who quit.

Critical: this is a one-shot reset. Use it once per relationship. Twice and you've cried wolf. After they respond, change your approach. The ghosting happened for a reason. Figure out whether you were pushing too hard (back off), too passive (be more direct), or missed something (ask what's going on).

### If they're fatigued → No-oriented questions

Late in a negotiation, cognitive resources deplete. Calibrated questions require mental energy they don't have. But people can always say no. "No" requires almost no cognitive load. So flip the framing. "Is it a ridiculous idea to close this today?" "Would it be crazy to revisit the timeline?" "Are you against moving on this?" A no to a negatively-framed question is a yes to what you actually want.

### If they're Assertive → Mirrors + Labels + get "that's right"

They can't listen until they feel heard. Let them talk first. Mirror the last few words. Label what you hear. Summarize until you get "that's right" (or "that's it exactly"). Only then present your position. Fighting for airtime with an Assertive is a losing game.

### If they're an Analyst → Data + Time + Labels

Bring data. Be prepared. No ad-libbing. Warn them of issues early; they hate surprises. Give them time to respond, even days. Labels help them articulate what they're thinking. Apologies have little value to them. They want competence, not emotion.

### If they're an Accommodator → Relationship + Implementation questions

Sociable and friendly first. Listen to their ideas. Then pressure-test commitments with calibrated questions about implementation. "How will we make sure this actually happens?" "What could get in the way?" Their yes is often counterfeit. Your job is to verify without damaging the relationship. Use the Rule of Three to test critical commitments.

### If you're haggling over price → Ackerman Model

Set your target. First offer at 65%. Raises to 85%, 95%, 100%. Use empathy and "How am I supposed to do that?" between offers. Precise non-round numbers ($37,893, not $38,000). On the final offer, throw in a non-monetary item to show you've reached the bottom of your pockets.

### Already got verbal agreement → push for "that's right"

A verbal yes without "that's right" is fragile. "You're right" means they want you to shut up. "That's right" means they feel heard. The Rule of Three verifies: get them to agree three different ways within the same conversation. Real commitment holds across three; counterfeit yes cracks by the third.

## Examples

**The decision tree in motion.** A founder is on the phone with an investor who's been slow-rolling a term sheet for weeks. The founder asks: "What do I do?"

First diagnosis: is the counterpart emotional, needing information, needing a no, ghosting, fatigued, a specific type, or in price negotiation? The situation is ghosting. Deploy the one-shot reset: "Have you given up on this round?" Response: "No, we've just been swamped with portfolio work. Let's talk Monday."

Monday comes. Now the situation is different. The investor is present, not emotional, not hostile. The founder needs information (what's really blocking the deal?). Switch techniques. Label: "It sounds like there's something about the terms that's giving you pause." Silence. The investor says: "Honestly, we're worried about the valuation given what we saw in your last board deck."

Now the founder has real information. Emotional? No. Type? Assertive (direct, fast, outcome-focused). Technique for Assertives: let them talk, mirror, label, get to "that's right." The founder mirrors: "The valuation given the last board deck?" The investor elaborates. Ten minutes later the founder summarizes: "So what you need to see is a cleaner path to Series B metrics and a sharper positioning story, is that right?" "That's right."

Now they can actually negotiate. Three techniques in thirty minutes, each picked because the situation demanded it.

**Same technique, wrong situation.** A sales rep has been trained on mirroring and loves it. She mirrors everything. Counterpart is an Analyst who pauses to think. She mirrors the pause back ("…you're thinking?"). He glares. She asks a calibrated question. He stops talking entirely. Wrong diagnosis, wrong technique. An Analyst in silence needs space, not a mirror. She should have shut up and let him think.

**Price negotiation diagnostic check.** A consultant is about to propose her Ackerman sequence on a $150K engagement. Before she does, she checks: is the counterpart emotional? No. Type? Accommodator (warm, friendly, easy yeses). That changes the approach. Ackerman works, but with an Accommodator she needs to layer in implementation questions throughout, because she doesn't yet know if this person can actually close the deal. Instead of going straight to price, she first asks: "How does this decision usually get approved on your side?" Information changes her whole sequence.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

- **The carpenter-with-a-hammer problem.** You have a favorite technique (usually mirroring or labeling because they're easy to learn) and you use it for everything. Sometimes you need a screwdriver. Reading the situation first is how you know which tool to grab.

- **Running the full sequence every time.** You mirror, then label, then "that's right," then calibrated question, then Ackerman. Checklist delivery. The counterpart feels managed, not heard. Pick one move. See what it pulls. Then pick the next move based on what actually happened.

- **Skipping diagnosis because you think you already know.** You walk in assuming you know the type, the emotional state, and the situation. You're wrong more than you think. Spend the first five minutes diagnosing before you deploy anything. Technique selection is only as good as the read it's built on.
