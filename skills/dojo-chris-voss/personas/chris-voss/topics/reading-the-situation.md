# Reading the Situation

## When to Use
- Before every significant negotiation. Diagnosis comes before technique selection. Until you know what you're dealing with, you don't know what you're dealing with.
- When something feels off but you can't name what. The diagnostic tools here turn vague unease into specific observations you can act on.
- When a deadline or threat lands on the table and you need to decide whether to take it seriously.
- When you suspect you're not talking to the real decision-maker.
- When the other side's words say yes but something else says no.

## Fails When
- You read a single gesture as definitive. Crossed arms might mean defensiveness, or the room might be cold. Look for clusters and changes, not single data points.
- You use the reading to feel clever instead of to adapt your approach. Diagnosis is only useful if it changes what you do next.
- You accuse based on what you read. "I can tell you're lying" starts a fight. "It seems like there's more to this story" opens a door.
- You treat the counterpart as a puzzle to be solved rather than a person to understand. Reading the situation is in service of a better deal, not in service of feeling superior.

## Core Concept

Voss learned the diagnostic principle the hard way. Brooklyn, 1993. His first major hostage case. A bank robbery gone wrong. The lead negotiator on the phone turned out to be a con man who'd slipped behind police lines and was pretending to negotiate for the hostage-takers. While he kept the FBI busy, the real robbers escaped through tunnels. The FBI got played because they assumed they knew the situation. They didn't.

Every negotiation requires diagnosis before action. You need to understand the terrain before you move across it. Here are the tools.

### The five-question assessment

Run these before you walk in:

1. **What do they SAY they want?** Their stated position. The number. The demand. Their position tells you where they want you to look.
2. **What do they ACTUALLY need?** Behind every position is an interest. Behind every interest is a need. "I need $150K for this hostage" is a position. "I need money to fund operations" is an interest. "I need to show my superiors I can deliver" is the need. The need is survival-level, which is why it's hidden.
3. **What are they AFRAID of?** Fear drives more negotiation behavior than any other emotion. Rarely stated directly. Usually disguised as a demand. Common fears: looking bad to superiors, making the wrong call, losing status, missing a better deal, being exploited.
4. **What's the deadline pressure?** Is it real or manufactured? Whose is more urgent? What happens if it passes? Most deadlines are self-inflicted figments of imagination.
5. **Who else is involved?** The "behind-the-table players." Bosses, spouses, boards, advisors. It only takes one bit player to kill a deal. One telecom company had 50% of signed deals never get implemented, because the point of contact had no relationship with the people who had to execute.

### Pronoun analysis (status reading)

Pay attention to which pronouns dominate. Real decision-makers tend to use "we," "they," "them." They're comfortable deferring to the organization. They don't need to assert importance because it's assumed.

People who are trying to appear important — or who have less authority than they claim — overuse "I," "me," "my." The rule Voss gives: the more in love they are with "I," "me," and "my," the less important they are.

When you hear "I'll make sure this happens" or "I can guarantee that," check whether they actually have authority to deliver. Use a calibrated question: "How will the rest of the team view this?" or "What will you need to do to get this approved?" Don't waste your best negotiation on someone who can't say yes.

### The Pinocchio effect (detecting deception)

Research from Deepak Malhotra at Harvard identified four markers that distinguish liars from truth-tellers. Liars use:

- **More words.** The number of words grows along with the lie. Liars over-explain. A ten-sentence answer to a yes-or-no question is a signal.
- **More third-person pronouns.** "The meeting was scheduled for 5 PM" instead of "I was there at 5 PM." Subconscious linguistic distancing from the lie.
- **More complex sentences.** Truth is simple; lies require architecture. When the explanation needs diagrams, pay attention.
- **Verbal inflation.** "Absolutely." "Definitely." "Without question." People telling the truth don't need to convince you they're telling the truth.

Don't accuse. Diagnose. Use a label: "It seems like there's more to this story." "It sounds like you might be concerned about something." Then let the silence work.

### 7-38-55 (incongruence)

From UCLA's Albert Mehrabian. 7% of communication is the words. 38% is tone of voice. 55% is body language and facial expression. More than 90% of the message is non-verbal.

The key insight isn't that words don't matter. It's that all three channels have to agree for the message to land. When they don't, you have incongruence, and incongruence is information.

Examples: they say yes but the voice drops and they look away. They say "I'm fine with that" but their shoulders tense. They say "no problem" but the tone is flat. Incongruence doesn't always mean lying. It often means unspoken concerns. Either way, it's something worth surfacing.

Surface it with a label: "I heard you say yes, but it seemed like there was hesitation there." Most people want to share their real concerns. They just need the invitation.

### First-name use as a status move

Using the counterpart's first name repeatedly is a subtle dominance move. So is refusing to use theirs. Voss uses his own name ("my name is Chris") as a friendly handshake but watches carefully for how the counterpart responds. When a counterpart uses your first name repeatedly, especially in combination with direct eye contact and declarative statements, they're asserting status. Notice it. You don't have to match it. But don't miss it.

### Deadline specificity

When a threat or deadline lands, assess specificity across four dimensions:

1. **What** will happen?
2. **Who** will do it?
3. **When** will it happen?
4. **How** will it happen?

Vague on all four: it's a bluff. "We'll have to look at other options." What options? How soon? It tells you nothing. Specific on all four: it's real and dangerous. Track the trend. A threat that's getting more specific over time is escalating. A threat that stays vague or gets vaguer is deflating.

The loophole principle: when people issue threats, they consciously or subconsciously leave themselves escape routes. "End of day" doesn't say which time zone. "Close of business" is flexible. The loopholes are signals of flexibility. When the loopholes close, the deadline becomes real.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the five-question assessment before the meeting.** Write the answers down. You'll be wrong on some. That's fine. The point is to have hypotheses you can test, not to be right in advance.

2. **In the first few minutes, read pronouns and pace.** Are they "I" heavy or "we" heavy? Fast or slow? Direct or warm? You're building your type hypothesis (see three-negotiator-types.md) and your status hypothesis at the same time.

3. **When something feels off, name it with a label.** Don't accuse. Don't confront. Just label: "It sounds like there's something else going on here." "I heard you say yes, but something in your voice suggested you might have concerns." Give them permission to say what they're really thinking.

4. **Track specificity on threats.** As the negotiation progresses, does their deadline get more specific or less? More specific means the threat is firming up. Less specific means they're bluffing and probably losing steam.

5. **Watch for patterns, not gestures.** Don't declare someone a liar because they looked away once. Watch for clusters: word inflation plus complex sentences plus third-person pronouns plus tonal mismatch. Any one is weak evidence. Four together is strong.

6. **Use what you learn to adapt, not to judge.** If you realize you're not talking to the decision-maker, your next move is to surface the behind-the-table player with a calibrated question. If you realize they're afraid of looking foolish, your next offer addresses that fear. Diagnosis is only valuable if it changes behavior.

## Examples

**The "signed but dead" deal.** A telecom vendor kept closing deals that never got implemented. They blamed procurement, legal, anyone downstream. The real diagnostic was question 5: Who else is involved? The point of contact was a director with no relationship with the engineers who had to actually deploy. Every signed deal was a solo hero effort that collapsed at implementation. Fix: add calibrated questions about implementation into every deal. "How on board are the people not on this call?" "What do your engineering team see as the main challenges?" Deals that couldn't answer these got flagged. Conversion to actual revenue doubled.

**The pronoun tell.** A buyer keeps saying "I'll get this approved" and "I'll push this through internally." A lot of "I." You use a calibrated question: "How does your team see this fitting into their priorities?" The answer is vague. You ask: "What would your CFO need to see to sign off?" Another vague answer. Now you know: this person can champion the deal but can't close it. You adjust. You don't push this person for signature. You help them sell upstream. You prep the materials their CFO needs. You ask to be introduced.

**The incongruent yes.** Meeting goes well. Counterpart says "yes, we're good with the timeline." But the tone drops on "timeline" and they look down. Label: "You said the timeline works, but something in your voice made me wonder if there's a concern there." Pause. They exhale. "Honestly, Q2 is going to be tight because we have a board meeting end of Q1 and the CFO will want this locked in earlier." You just saved yourself a surprise six weeks from now.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

- **Body-language bingo.** Treating crossed arms, steepled fingers, or broken eye contact as definitive signals. Voss is explicit: don't over-interpret single gestures. Look for clusters and changes tied to specific topics. When their body shifts every time a specific subject comes up, that's diagnostic. A single gesture isn't.

- **Accusing instead of labeling.** You notice the pronoun pattern or the verbal inflation and you say "I think you're lying." Dead end. The counterpart defends, the rapport breaks, and you get nothing. Label what you see instead: "It seems like there's more going on here." Labels open; accusations close.

- **Reading without adapting.** You diagnose beautifully, write it all in your notebook, then proceed with your original plan anyway. The diagnosis has to change your next move. If you'd do the same thing whether you diagnosed or not, you didn't really diagnose.
