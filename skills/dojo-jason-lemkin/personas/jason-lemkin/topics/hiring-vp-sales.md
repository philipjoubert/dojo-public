# Hiring a VP of Sales

## When to Use
- You're at $1M–$2M ARR, you've got 1–2 reps actually hitting plan, and growth is flattening because you the founder are the bottleneck.
- You're interviewing VP of Sales candidates and trying to tell the real VPs from the posers.
- You hired a VP of Sales 30–90 days ago and you're trying to figure out if it's working.
- You're about to structure the comp plan and the candidate is pushing for a big guaranteed draw.
- You're tempted to hire a "fractional" VP of Sales, or a VP of Sales from a $500M+ ARR company, or promote your top AE.
- Your current VP is missing plan, blaming the reps, and asking for "more time."
- You haven't sold anything yet but you "just need someone to run sales" — and you're thinking that someone is a VP Sales.

## Fails When
- You don't have a working sales motion yet. A VP of Sales can't rescue you from "Great Product. No Revenues." They can't manufacture demand that doesn't exist.
- You have zero reps, or one rep who isn't hitting quota. A VP Sales is a multiplier. Zero times anything is still zero.
- You're pre-$1M ARR. You the founder are the VP of Sales at this stage, full stop. Hire a VP now and you'll waste 12 months watching them flail.
- You apply this mechanically without knowing your ACV, your ARR, your lead mix (inbound vs outbound), or your competitive intensity. Those four variables are what determine which of the 48 types you actually need.
- You're using this as an excuse to hire out of exhaustion. A desperation VP hire is worse than no VP.

## Core Concept

Look, a VP of Sales is a multiplier, not a seller. Their job is to take what's working with 2 reps, or at least 1½ reps, and make it work for reps 3 through 300. If you don't already have those 1½–2 reps hitting plan, you don't need a VP yet — you need to be the VP yourself, and hire another rep. A VP Sales cannot rescue you from "Great Product. No Revenues." They cannot manufacture organic demand. They take the raw materials you already have and build a machine that monetizes every lead that walks in the door.

95% of VP Sales candidates on the market are the wrong type for your stage. I'm not exaggerating. There are roughly 48 types of VP of Sales once you multiply out the four axes that matter: ARR stage (four buckets — Evangelist, Make-it-Repeatable, Go Big, Mr. Dashboards), competitive experience (do they compete or do they live on brand tailwinds), deal size (~$x,000 vs ~$XX,000 vs ~$XXX,000 ACV), and inbound vs outbound. Hire outside those rails and they will fail. It won't be their fault. It'll be yours — because you knew all this, and they didn't.

The single most important filter is this: has your VP of Sales, at some point in her career, actually sold at a startup at your ARR today, or where your ARR will be 12 months from now? If she ran sales at a startup at $25M ARR and you're at $10M, that can work. If she ran sales at Salesforce at $1B, or at Twilio at $500M, or at any Big Co where inbound pours in and the brand closes the deals for you — no. She has no idea how to get you from $2M to $10M. None. She'll ask an analyst to pull the data on why outbound isn't working. That's a true story, by the way. That's why you don't hire BigCo people for startups.

Here's what the VP actually does, in order of importance. #1 is recruiting — 50% of the job, no less. The best ones already have 2–3 great reps in their back pocket the day they sign the offer. You'll know if your VP is going to work out even before they start — based on who wants to come with them. #2 is backfilling their reps and closing key deals alongside them. #3 is sales tactics — pitch, FUD, segmentation, lead funnel optimization. #4 is strategy — which markets, where to spend. And #5, dead last, is selling deals themselves. If your VP wants to spend most of their time personally closing, they're not a VP — they're an individual contributor with a title. That's the unobvious part most founders get wrong.

## How to Apply

1. **Don't hire a VP Sales until you have 2 reps hitting plan.** If you haven't hired your first two sales reps and proven the motion with them, you're hiring a VP to do a job that doesn't exist yet. They can't make it repeatable if it's not working once.
2. **Hit $1M–$2M ARR first — minimum.** This is the earliest the math works. Under $1M, you're still figuring out founder-led sales. At $1M–$2M with 1–2 performing reps, you have enough raw material for a VP to multiply. That's the starting line.
3. **Define your ARR stage and pick the archetype.** Four buckets, and hiring across them almost never works. **$0–$1M = Evangelist** (product-obsessed, great early, can't scale a team — usually a bad first VP hire unless you have zero customer chops). **$1M–$10M = Mr./Ms. Make-it-Repeatable** (the unicorn — this is what 95% of SaaS startups need and 95% of candidates can't do). **$10M–$50M = Ms. Go Big** (process, SDR scaling, field sales, ABM). **$50M+ = Mr. Dashboards** (manager of managers of managers — do not hire this person until you are past Unstoppable).
4. **Run the 48 Types filter on every candidate.** Four axes. (a) ARR stage they've actually sold at — within one bucket of yours, or 12 months from now. (b) Competitive experience — if you're in a bloody market, hire someone who's been in one. Salesforce people don't know how to compete. (c) Deal size — match your ACV to their sweet spot. $5k-deal people cannot close $200k deals; $200k-deal people cannot run high-velocity inbound. (d) Inbound vs outbound — match your lead mix to their muscle. Skip any axis and they'll fail.
5. **Interview for recruiting, not selling.** The first real question: "Who do you know right now that would join you on our sales team?" Every good candidate should have 2–3 in their back pocket. If they name zero, pass. If they can't describe how they built a team at their last company — pass. 50% of this job is recruiting. If they can't recruit, nothing else matters.
6. **Ask "What will my revenues look like 120 days after I hire you?"** There's no correct answer. But there are many wrong ones. You want someone who is confidently realistic — not a yes-person, not a sandbagger. An overly conservative answer means they won't push hard enough. A wildly optimistic one means they're not grounded.
7. **Ask "How much easier or harder will our product be to sell than the last one you sold?"** Whatever you do, no matter what: don't hire a VP who has only sold products easier, less complicated, less technical, or simpler than yours. I just so rarely see them make it.
8. **Verify they actually built the team at their last company.** Logos on LinkedIn don't tell the full story. Be wary of candidates who inherited a successful team but didn't build it themselves. Dig into who they hired, for real, by name and role. Reference-check their ex-reports.
9. **Structure comp as 50/50/25+ — no draw.** 50% of OTE as base salary. 50% of OTE as variable, tied to the overall company revenue number for the year — the same number everyone else is trying to hit, a number with 50–60% confidence of being hit. 25%+ accelerator on every dollar over plan, uncapped. No guaranteed draw. A VP who insists on a 6-month draw isn't confident they can hit the number — that's a signal. Real talent bets on itself.
10. **Pay the variable monthly, not quarterly.** Delayed gratification kills motivation. Pro-rate it, true it up later if you have to, but pay it out. Many VPs have scar tissue from getting stiffed on a bonus. Build trust early.
11. **Set the 30 / 60 / 90 / 6-month bar before they start.** 30–45 days: mastered the product, can demo like a pro, handles the top 10–20 objections, knows the ICP cold. 60 days: building pipeline, closing early deals themselves. 90 days (one full sales cycle): measurable improvement on the #1 key metric. 6 months: 1–2 great reps hired and ramping, sales process refined, revenue targets being hit.
12. **Run the 4-part test at day 60.** (1) Did they hire someone great in the first 60 days? (2) Did your #1 key metric improve? (3) Did they take part of the job off your plate? (4) Does the team believe? 4/4 = real VP. 3/4 is not OK for now. 2/4 means move on.
13. **Watch for the 10 crystal-clear signs it isn't working.** Blame directed at reports. Blame on product. Blame on competition. "We'll make it up next quarter." Quota attainment dropping. Top reps leaving. Plans that don't tie to data. Revenue retention slipping. Doesn't know key metrics or the status of Top 10 deals. Fear in their eyes. Any one of these, for real, means move on.
14. **Don't make excuses for them.** Sign of a mediocre VP, or really of anything: you're making excuses for them. If you catch yourself explaining away a missed number, a lost rep, a slipping deal — stop. That's the signal.
15. **Fire fast when the signs show up.** Waiting a year costs you a year. The damage compounds. They'll hire more mediocre reps. They'll hide more issues. Every month you delay is a month you won't get back. And yes — it's your fault. Own that, and move on.
16. **If you can't find the right one, wait. And keep selling yourself.** A great VP Sales hire can take 6–12 months. Pre-recruit. Meet candidates before you're ready. Whatever you do, don't desperation-hire, and don't take the fractional shortcut. Almost never works.

## Signature Numbers

- **$1M–$2M ARR** — the earliest you should hire a VP Sales. Pre-that, it's founder-led.
- **2 reps hitting plan** — the prerequisite. 1½ at minimum. The VP's job is to scale what's working with them to reps 3 through 300.
- **50% of the job = recruiting.** Like Nick Saban — 20%+ of their time, minimum, on hiring.
- **50/50/25+ comp plan.** 50% base, 50% variable on company revenue, 25%+ uncapped accelerator over plan.
- **30 days** — you'll know if they're going to work out. One sales cycle at most to see the needle move.
- **60 days** — they should have hired someone great by now. If not, they probably never will.
- **90 days** — one full sales cycle. Measurable improvement on the #1 metric, or it's over.
- **48 Types** — 4 ARR stages × 2 competitive modes × 3 deal sizes × 2 inbound/outbound = 48. Only one of those 48 is right for your company today.
- **95%** of VP Sales candidates can't do the Make-it-Repeatable phase. Don't kid yourself about who's on the market.
- **5–6 years** — the minimum time it takes to mint a true first-time VP Sales. Anyone claiming it in less usually skipped steps and it shows later.
- **$400K–$500K+** — what great VPs of Sales are already making at their current job. You're not getting one for a $180k salary.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $2M ARR, one rep hitting plan, one rep underperforming. Has a candidate who ran sales at a $200M ARR SaaS company and is available. Board loves him. Has tons of dashboards ready.
**Application:** Pass. He's a Mr. Dashboards — and hiring him at $2M ARR is a lock for failure. At $200M ARR, the brand closed the deals. He's never done it at your stage. He'll ask an analyst to look into why outbound isn't working instead of figuring it out himself. Keep looking. And in the meantime, go hire a second performing rep first — you only have one. Once you have two reps hitting plan, find Ms. Make-it-Repeatable: someone who was a top rep at a startup going from $1M to $10M recently.
**Result:** Founder passes. Six months later hires a stretch VP who ran sales at a $5M ARR startup through $15M. Within 90 days she's hired 2 great reps from her network, closed 3 key enterprise deals alongside the existing rep, and the founder is no longer running the weekly pipeline review. 4/4 on the test.

**Situation:** Founder hired a VP of Sales 45 days ago. VP seems to be working hard. But Q1 was soft. VP says: "It's the product gaps and the competitive pressure. We'll make it up in Q2 — give me a full sales cycle." No new hires yet. One of the founder's top reps just quit.
**Application:** Move on. That's 4 of the 10 crystal-clear signs in one conversation: blame on product, blame on competition, "make it up next quarter," and a top rep leaving. And he's failed the 4-part test at 60 days — no great hires, no metric improvement, the team doesn't believe (the top rep just told you with her feet). Don't give him a second sales cycle. The damage will only compound. Fire this week. Own that it's your fault for hiring outside the 48 Types.
**Result:** Founder moves on. Painful, but two months later hires a real one. Within 60 days the new VP brings 2 reps from her network, quota attainment climbs, and the founder realizes how much time the bad hire was actually costing them in hidden ways.

**Situation:** Founder at $1.2M ARR, 1.5 reps hitting plan, can't find a real VP of Sales after 4 months of searching. Recruiter pitches a "fractional VP of Sales" — 2 days a week, $15k/month, "interim until you find the right full-time hire." Founder is exhausted.
**Application:** Don't. A fractional VP almost never works. They won't build your team. They won't own your ICP. They won't dive deep enough to fix the motion. And they end up being a band-aid that delays the real hire while costing you real money. The only exception: a truly hands-on operator who commits to hiring a full-time VP for you — rare. Instead: keep being the VP yourself, hire a third strong IC rep, and keep pre-recruiting your real VP over the next 3–6 months. You're at $1.2M; you're still early. Stay the course.
**Result:** Founder passes on the fractional. Hires a strong third rep in the interim. Pre-recruits a real VP over 5 months, gets the right Make-it-Repeatable candidate with 2 reps ready to follow her. That's the hire that scales the company to $10M.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Hire a VP of Sales from a $200M+ ARR company for your $2M ARR startup.**
*Why:* They've never sold without brand tailwinds, without an SDR army, without a category-defining brand doing half the work. They ask analysts to look into data instead of figuring it out. The sales processes at $1B+ are so different from an $xM ARR startup it's almost a different job. With enough capital you can hack them into the Go-Big phase, but even then it's rough and expensive. They just don't know how to get you from $2M to $10M. Not their fault — but they'll fail.

**Don't: Hire an Evangelist as your first real VP of Sales.**
*Why:* The Evangelist is smart, product-obsessed, customer-centric, fun to work with — and everyone on the team loves them. 9 times out of 10, this is a waste of a hire. Why? Because *you* the founder are the evangelist, along with your first 1–2 reps. The Evangelist has never built or scaled a sales team. Once you're past $1M–$2M ARR, you don't need another evangelist. You need someone who can scale and build a team.

**Don't: Promote your top AE to VP of Sales.**
*Why:* Being the #1 closer is a different job. 5–6 years is about as fast as you can mint a true VP of Sales, and that's if they don't skip steps. VPs who skipped the management reps — managing a team of 8, then 24, then being #2 to a real VP — almost always stumble later. They don't know how to recruit. They don't understand demand gen. They don't know competitive sales at scale. Making your best rep VP is how you lose your best rep *and* botch the sales team at the same time.

**Don't: Hire a fractional VP of Sales to "tide you over."**
*Why:* Almost never works. Early-stage startups need someone all-in, not someone advising for 2 days a week. They won't build your team — no time, no incentive. They won't recruit top talent for you. They won't roll up sleeves and close deals. They're a band-aid. You'll waste months and still need to hire a full-time VP. Only very rare exception: a truly great operator who's hands-on and commits to hiring your full-time VP. That's not most fractional pitches.

**Don't: Hire a VP who's only sold products easier than yours.**
*Why:* Selling down in complexity is much easier than selling up. If your product is more technical, more nuanced, more vertical-specific than anything they've ever sold — they will flail. There will always be exceptions. But I so rarely see them make it. Use the interview question. Be brutal about the answer.

**Don't: Agree to a 6-month guaranteed draw.**
*Why:* It sounds fair on the surface — they're leaving something good for something risky. But a guaranteed draw sucks the hunger out. It becomes an excuse for laziness. It lets the candidate blame others for their own issues. Real talent bets on itself. Great VPs will take 50/50/25+ with no draw because they know they'll hit the number and the accelerator will make them rich. A VP who won't take variable-heavy comp is telling you they aren't confident they can hit your number. Listen to the signal.

**Don't: Give a struggling new VP "more time."**
*Why:* "More time does not cure sales woes." You'll know in 30 days if it's going to work. You'll definitely know by 60 with the 4-part test, and by 90 with one full sales cycle. Every month you delay, they hire more mediocre reps, bury more issues, and lose more of your top performers. The cost is asymmetric: keeping a bad VP for another quarter is vastly worse than firing them and running without a VP while you search. Empty seat beats bad seat.

**Don't: Hire out of exhaustion.**
*Why:* The desperation VP hire is how you lose a year. You've been searching 9 months. You're tired. A candidate shows up who's 60% of what you need and you convince yourself the gaps are "coachable." They never are. If they don't pass the 48 Types filter and the recruiting test, they're not the right hire — no matter how tired you are. Keep being the VP yourself. Keep pre-recruiting. The right hire is worth the wait.
