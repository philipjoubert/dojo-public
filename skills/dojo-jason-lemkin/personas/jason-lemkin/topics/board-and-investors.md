# Board and Investors

## When to Use
- You've raised at least a seed or Series A and you have a real board.
- You're preparing a board meeting and wrestling with format, pre-read, agenda.
- Numbers were soft this quarter and you're deciding what to tell the board.
- Your investors are unhappy — or you suspect they are — and you don't know how to handle it.
- You haven't sent an investor update in three months because the news isn't good.
- You don't actually know where you stand with your investors.
- You're thinking about adding an independent, or replacing a VC who isn't adding value.
- You're post-Series A/B and the board dynamic has shifted — more seats, more expectations.

## Fails When
- You're pre-seed with a SAFE and no real investors. No board to run.
- You're asking about fundraising process itself — decks, term sheets, valuation. That's fundraising.md.
- You're bootstrapped. You don't have this problem.
- You're being removed for cause — fraud, ethics. Different playbook.
- You apply this as a ritual and miss the point. The point is trust. Checking boxes while hiding the ball, you've already lost.

## Core Concept

Look, the post-funding investor relationship runs on one thing: trust. Not charisma, not narrative, not slides. Trust. Trust gets built by being transparent, owning the numbers, and never surprising your board with bad news they should have heard weeks earlier. Almost every founder-investor meltdown I've seen traces to the same root cause. The founder hid something. Soft-pedaled. Kept saying "we'll make it up next quarter" until the math was impossible to hide. By then the investors aren't mad about the miss. They're mad about the surprise. That's what breaks the relationship.

Second thing founders don't want to hear: when you raised VC, a clock started ticking. Your existing investors expect the next round to price at 2x what they paid, ideally 3x for seed, inside 18 months — 24 max. That's not greed, it's fund math. Don't clear the bar and you don't get a clean up-round. You get a round extension, flat round, down round, or no round. Going from $10K MRR to $20K MRR doesn't do it. You usually need Triple Triple Double Double, or close. Know the game — it shapes every board conversation for the next five years.

Third thing: 98% of boards do not want to fire you. Too much work, too much risk. They'll move mountains to make the founder-CEO relationship work. Where they get nervous enough to make a change is when bad news is a big surprise, after another big surprise, really the third big surprise. Miss a quarter? Happens. Hide the miss, hide the next, then tell the board you're out of money in six weeks? That's when even the best VCs panic. The game is simple, even if it's hard: send the updates, share the Zero Cash Date, surface bad news fast, run the meetings like a pro. Do that and 99 times out of 100 you won't have a board problem. You'll have a business problem, which is different, and which the board will actually help you solve.

## How to Apply

1. **Send a monthly investor update. First week of the month.** Not quarterly, not "when there's news." Monthly. 95% complete on the 1st beats 100% accurate on the 31st. Include metrics, wins, losses, top 2–3 concerns, top 3 highlights, Zero Cash Date, specific asks. Send it especially when the news is bad. Not sending always makes it worse.
2. **Run board meetings every 60 days.** Every 60, investors are actively engaged. Every 90, informed but not top-of-mind. Never, and they check out on other portfolio companies.
3. **Get the deck out 2–3 days before the meeting. No exceptions.** Late decks undermine confidence. One of the 9 things that makes CEOs hard to work with — and the easiest to fix.
4. **Talk less. Let your team talk more.** CEO sets the stage 10 minutes. Then each VP presents their own area. The never-hear-from-anyone-else-er CEO tells the board you don't trust your team, or don't have one.
5. **Share top 2–3 concerns and top 3 highlights every meeting.** Put them on a slide. Volunteer it. No one accuses you of hiding when you surface it yourself.
6. **Know and share your Zero Cash Date every month.** The one number that hidden gets you fired, shared honestly almost never does. VCs can handle a missed quarter. They can't handle a surprise runway cliff.
7. **Go around the room at every meeting: "1–10, how are we doing? Would you invest more today?"** Your Investor NPS. 80% of founders don't know where they stand. Most think Green when they're Yellow. Start asking at 12 months of runway, not 4.
8. **When investors are unhappy, speak with data. No "woe is me."** Honest root-cause analysis. Plan to fix. Product roadmap tied to customer needs. Realistic financial model — not a hockey stick needing magic in Q4. Don't blame anyone else. Ever.
9. **Never surprise your board.** Sense bad news coming, share it before it lands. Deal slipping, VP not working, burn creeping — you know before they do. Pre-wiring keeps faith. Surprises kill it.
10. **Re-forecast at most once a year.** Endless reforecasting reads as wiggling. Set the plan, own it, miss it if you miss it, don't rewrite goalposts every quarter.
11. **Use investors for what they're good at: recruiting, customer intros, follow-on signaling.** Come with 3–5 specific asks every meeting. Don't use them for daily hand-holding — they don't have time, you shouldn't need it.
12. **Invite more 5%+ shareholders to board meetings, not fewer.** Meetings are on Zoom — overhead is near zero. They become allies when times get tough. The 2019–2021 "avoid observers" advice aged badly.
13. **Keep the formal board lean.** 3–5 voting seats max pre-$20M ARR. Founders, 1–2 lead VCs, maybe 1 independent. When you're running a real org with VPs, an operator independent is worth their weight.
14. **Replace a board member only when you must — start the conversation early.** Rare but happens. Wrong-stage seed investor still on the board at $50M ARR is often mis-tuned. Use the next round as the natural reshuffle moment.
15. **Audit yourself against the 9 CEO types boards can't stand, quarterly.** Surprise-springer. Third-check-er. Make-it-up-in-the-last-2-weeks-er. Miss-every-plan-er. Schmoozer-upper. Thin-skinner. Blame-someone-else-er. Never-hear-from-anyone-else-er. Deck-late-er. Drifted into 2+? Fix it before the board has to.
16. **Get your comp approved by the board. Pay yourself market, not more.** Overpaying yourself is a top-5 red flag.
17. **Don't cancel board meetings when times are tough.** That's exactly when you need the board in the room. Canceling signals hiding.
18. **Post Series A/B, manage up to your board like you'd want a VP to manage up to you.** Clear communication, no surprises, specific asks, owning numbers. If your VP did with you what you do with your board — would you keep them?

## Signature Numbers

- **18 months** — ticking clock after a VC round. Next round ready inside 18, 24 max.
- **2x valuation minimum, 3x for seed** — what existing investors expect for the next round.
- **Every 60 days** — board meeting cadence that keeps investors engaged. 90, distant. Never, checked out.
- **Monthly investor updates, first week of the month.** No excuses. Especially when news is bad.
- **2–3 days early** — how far ahead the deck goes out.
- **2–3 concerns + 3 highlights** — what you volunteer every meeting.
- **9 CEO types** boards can't work with. Quarterly self-audit.
- **98%** of boards do not want to fire the CEO.
- **Third surprise** — roughly when boards start seriously considering a change. Not the first miss.
- **80%** of founders don't know where they stand with investors.
- **4 VC categories**: Super Green, Green, Yellow, Red. Know your bucket.
- **1 reforecast per year, max.**
- **5%+ shareholders** — threshold for observer invites.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $4M ARR, Series A 14 months ago. Q3 soft, bookings down 15%. Founder thinking: "I'll wait until after December. If we close the two big December deals, Q4 looks normal, I can report the year together."
**Application:** Send the update this week. Your investors have seen Q3 misses at every company they've funded. What they can't handle is a founder going dark three months then dropping a soft quarter, a missed year, and whatever else all at once. That's the Third Surprise pattern. Send: the miss, honest root cause, plan to fix, updated Zero Cash Date, 2–3 specific asks. No blame. No "woe is me." No hockey-stick recovery.
**Result:** Within 48 hours, four replies. Two "been there, here's what worked." One intros a VP candidate. One asks a sharp question that surfaces a churn issue the founder had half-admitted to themselves. Nobody's happy, nobody's panicked. Series B conversation stays on track. Alternate timeline where the founder hides: the Series B lead gets spooked by the info asymmetry, founder spends Q1 fundraising in panic mode instead of fixing the business.

**Situation:** Founder at $8M ARR, Series B closed. Board is trending toward "why are you missing plan every quarter, and why is every forecast more optimistic than the last?" Founder reforecasted Q1, Q2, Q3 — each time nudging the plan lower to hit it. Q4 meeting in two weeks. Tempted to reforecast again.
**Application:** Don't. You've burned that lever. More goalpost-moving reads as wiggling. You're flirting with two of the 9 types — miss-every-plan-er and make-it-up-er. Instead come to Q4 with: original plan, actual, gap, honest root cause, and a new plan you commit to and don't touch for 12 months. Own the misses. Don't blame the macro, the AI competitor, the departed VP. "I set the plan, I missed, here's what I got wrong, here's what I'm doing, I own it." Then go around the room: 1–10, would you invest more today?
**Result:** One investor says "7, maybe." Another "5, show me Q1." One "9, keep going." That's Yellow, not Red — useful. Founder stops quarterly reforecasts, hits the new plan two of four quarters, Yellows move back to Green by mid-year. Another reforecast and the Yellows would have gone Red.

**Situation:** Founder at $15M ARR, 4 board seats. Seed investor on the board hasn't added value in 3 years — late, asks basic questions, never makes intros. Founder wants to replace them with an operator independent.
**Application:** Don't brute-force it. Private coffee with the seed investor, quarters ahead of when you want the change. Acknowledge the early contribution, explain the stage shift, propose they step off the formal board and stay as observer. Most will go gracefully if you do it with respect. Natural moment is the next round. Meanwhile source the operator independent — VP Sales or CRO from a $100M+ SaaS company, gets your stage, network you need. Meet 3–4. Gel or don't.
**Result:** Seed investor steps off at Series C, stays as observer, no grudge. Operator independent joins, drags enterprise sales rigor into the board dynamic, makes three intros that become $500K+ deals year one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Hide bad news hoping it'll improve.**
*Why:* You know before your board does. Every delay you're gambling the news fixes itself — it almost never does. The miss compounds. When you finally surface it, it's the accumulated pile plus the meta-story that you've been hiding. The meta-story is what erodes trust.

**Don't: Cancel the board meeting when the quarter was bad.**
*Why:* Canceling says "I don't want to face this." The board notices every time. The meeting where the quarter was bad is the most important meeting you'll run all year. Show up with data, root cause, plan. Lead through it.

**Don't: Forecast a hockey-stick recovery that needs magic in Q4.**
*Why:* Every VC has seen this deck a hundred times. Three soft quarters, then a miraculous Q4. Nobody believes it. The CFO built the model backwards from the plan instead of forward from the pipeline. Brands you the miss-every-plan-er. A credible lower number beats a fictional higher one every day.

**Don't: Blame anyone — VP, market, macro, competitor — for a miss.**
*Why:* The buck stops at the CEO. The moment you blame someone else, you've told the board you're not in charge of outcomes. Even when there's truth — the macro did turn, the VP did underperform — you own the response. You hired the VP. You picked the market. Boards back founders who own outcomes, dump founders who dodge them.

**Don't: Dominate the meeting by talking the whole time.**
*Why:* Never-hear-from-anyone-else-er. The board learns nothing about the team, can't assess the VPs, and concludes you don't trust them or don't have them. 10 minutes of stage-setting, then hand to the VPs. Your in-meeting job is context, strategic calls, hard questions — not narration.

**Don't: Wait until 4 months of cash to ask "would you invest more?"**
*Why:* At 4 months runway you have no leverage. Existing investors know you're cornered. The check's signal value dilutes if they write it, and if they don't you're out of time to find a new lead. Start asking at 12 months of runway, every meeting.

**Don't: Use your investors for daily hand-holding.**
*Why:* Your VCs sit on 10+ boards. They have 2–4 hours a month for you outside board meetings. Texting your lead daily with product and pricing decisions burns your most valuable chips on low-leverage stuff, and outsources founder judgment. Use them for what capital and network unlock: recruiting, customer intros, follow-on signaling. Specific asks, not vague anxiety.
