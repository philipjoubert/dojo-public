# AI-Era GTM

## When to Use
- You're rebuilding your GTM motion for 2026 and beyond and you need to decide what to do with the SDR team, the AE team, and the stack underneath them.
- Your growth decelerated this year and you can't tell if it's sales, marketing, product, or the fact that AI-native competitors ate your market share while you were adding "AI features."
- You're interviewing a VP of Sales or VP of Marketing and trying to figure out whether they can actually operate in an AI-first GTM — or whether they're still running a 2021 playbook.
- Your outbound email numbers are collapsing and you don't know whether to double down, pivot, or shut it off.
- You built a co-pilot this year and you're trying to figure out why customers won't pay more for it.
- You're about to make the hiring plan for next year and someone on your exec team is asking for 30 more people.

## Fails When
- You apply this pre-PMF. AI doesn't save a broken product or an absent market. It amplifies what's already there. If you don't have a motion yet, you need founder-led sales, not more agents.
- You use "AI is replacing reps" as cover to fire your top 20%. The top reps aren't going anywhere. It's the middle that gets compressed. If you fire your best person, you just made your problem worse.
- You sell to buyers who are a decade behind on tech — heavy industry, traditional legal, old-school healthcare, construction. The further your customer is from tech, the slower this unfolds. AI handles the transactional middle; the strategic top still requires humans.
- You're honest about nothing. You can't face whether you gained or lost market share. You deleted the competitor slide from the board deck. That's the classic tell — and it's a losing position.
- You believe "we added AI features" is a strategy. Microsoft couldn't get people to pay $20 a month to talk to Word. A mediocre co-pilot is not an AI strategy. It's table stakes at best.

## Core Concept

AI is not coming for sales jobs in the abstract — it's already replacing the specific work that the mid-pack 60% of reps weren't actually doing. That's the honest version. Everyone wants to talk about the top 20% and the bottom 20%. The top keep their jobs and become superhuman. The bottom keep getting fired the way they always did. The middle — the reps who take a week to schedule, who can't answer a technical question, who send three "just checking in" emails and mark the deal closed-lost — that's who gets replaced. Because AI can already do what they do, faster, cheaper, 24/7, in any language, without needing a Friday afternoon off. You can watch it happen live in the contact center: zero percent AI automation 18 months ago, now every contact center leader's goal is 50%+. Sales is next, and it's not 5 years away. It's 18–24 months away for the transactional middle of most motions.

The outbound SDR role specifically — the email-based, 100-a-day, "I noticed you raised a Series B" SDR — that job is dying in real time. Not because AI is bad at it. Because AI is too good at it. The supply of AI-generated outbound emails exploded, spam filters got smarter, inbox fatigue hit a wall, and the response rates collapsed for everyone. Emergence Capital's data across 400+ B2B companies: 36% of companies decreased SDR headcount last year, the highest of any sales function, and only 19% increased. That's the writing on the wall. Meanwhile AI SDRs are sending 11–40x the volume of human SDRs with better response rates, because they know the product cold, they never forget the case study, and they book the meeting at 6:02 PM on a Saturday night when a human would never. If a deal can close over text or email — and a lot of mid-market deals can — then AI can close it. Maybe not perfectly today. Soon enough that it doesn't matter.

None of this means you fire your top reps or dismantle enterprise sales. The $1M+ strategic deals still require relationships, executive presence, in-person meetings (which still close at 3x the rate of virtual). Founder-led selling for big deals still wins. What it means is that the GTM stack is bifurcating — AI handles the transactional middle, humans handle the strategic top, and the space in between is collapsing. The real question for your product is darker and more important than your GTM motion. If your product is just a co-pilot — a nice little AI feature bolted onto your 2021 software — you're not going to get any of the AI budget. Gartner says software spend is reaccelerating to a record level next year. But half of that record spend is going into price increases from existing vendors. Thirty percent is going into new AI tools. If you're not in one of those two groups, you're not getting any of it. Steal it, or it gets stolen from you. There is no third option. And you already had 2025 to compound. If you didn't reaccelerate growth this year with a real agentic product in market, you get a D minus. You can't get a D next year. You're late.

## How to Apply

1. **Be honest about which reps actually do the work.** Audit your team against the seven things a great AE actually does: owns scheduling, runs real customized demos, knows the product cold, solves the technical problem, arms the champion, follows up with real substance, and simplifies deployment before the deal closes. The reps who do fewer than half of those, consistently, are already gone. AI just hasn't formally replaced them yet.
2. **Cut the mid-pack before the market does it for you.** The top 20% stay. The bottom 20% you were going to fire anyway. The middle 60% is where the compression happens. That's where you replace headcount with AI SDRs, AI inbound agents, AI scheduling, AI research, and a smaller, more technical team of humans.
3. **Kill the email-only SDR role — or convert it.** The classic outbound email SDR is dying. Either shut it down and route the budget into AI SDRs plus a smaller team of humans doing phone + in-person + account-based work, or restructure the role entirely around technical selling, SE-style work, and customer onboarding. Don't defend the old job. It's not coming back.
4. **Build the 50/50 team — half AI agents, half humans.** By the end of 2026, most CROs will be running teams that are roughly half AI, half human. That's not a slogan, it's a math problem. Start now. Figure out which motions are fundamentally text-based (qualification, scheduling, early-stage follow-up, renewal reminders, basic technical Q&A) and move those to agents. Keep humans on what actually requires humans.
5. **Hire "AI curiosity" as a firing-offense-level trait.** Every rep, every marketer, every CSM has to be actively using AI, deploying agents, pushing the stack forward. Not an expert — just engaged. If they're not AI-curious in mid-2026, that's disqualifying. Not coachable. Let them go.
6. **Shift headcount ratios toward SEs, FDEs, and technical talent.** AI-native companies are running 2:1 or 3:1 SE-to-AE ratios. Some have more SEs than AEs. Rebalance. The people closing big deals in 2026 are Sales Engineers, Solutions Architects, Forward Deployed Engineers — people who actually solve the customer's problem. The pure demo-jockey AE is obsolete.
7. **Interview for real AI operating experience, not AI LinkedIn experience.** When you hire your next VP Sales or VP Marketing, ask what agents they've deployed. What their AI stack looks like. What they automated at their last company and what the actual results were. A candidate who talks about "AI-first GTM" but can't name three agents they've stood up is a pass.
8. **Audit your product against the three categories that actually get budget.** Either (1) you replace humans — real headcount comes out, measurable — or (2) you dramatically augment humans with order-of-magnitude productivity gains (like what Cursor did for devs), or (3) you enable massive TAM expansion that lets you charge 5x what you were charging. If you're not in one of those three, a co-pilot feature is not enough. There's no budget for "we made our product a little nicer."
9. **Stop selling qualification meetings.** 62% of buyers don't want to talk to sales until the decision stage. Making a prospect burn 30 minutes on a qualification call before they get any value is unforgivable in 2026. Let AI qualify. Let the buyer self-qualify. When the human gets on the phone, they better bring real value — not a discovery script.
10. **Test the "Would the AI answer this better?" question on every sales interaction.** If your rep can't answer a technical question that Claude can answer instantly off the product docs, your rep is a liability, not an asset. The bar is: your rep has to know your product better than AI does. If they don't, the prospect will just talk to the AI instead.
11. **Focus human time on the top 40% of accounts.** Sales reps historically cover about 40 of every 100 assigned accounts. That's been true for decades and it's not a character flaw, it's time math. AI fixes the other 60. Let your reps go deep on the accounts where relationships win. Let AI cover the long tail.
12. **Protect in-person and strategic enterprise selling — that's where humans still crush.** In-person meetings still close at 3x the rate of virtual. The strategic $1M+ deals still require relationships, executive trust, and showing up. The most AI-forward companies still fly their SEs onsite for the big ones. AI handles the transactional middle. Humans handle the strategic top. Don't confuse the two.
13. **Decide whether you're a vertical AI or horizontal AI play — and own the tradeoffs.** Vertical AI companies have customers and domain moats (Clio in legal, Harvey, industry-specific agents). Horizontals have data and scale but commoditize fast. Neither is automatically safe. Vertical wins when domain knowledge compounds. Horizontal wins when distribution and data compound. If you're neither, you get eaten.
14. **Ask the honest question each year: did we gain or lose market share?** Even if you grew 50% — if your direct competitor grew 100%, you lost. If you deleted the competitor slide from your board deck, you already know the answer. Track competitors' ARR, growth rate, customer count, even web traffic as a crude proxy. Pretending competition doesn't exist is how you lose the year.
15. **Refuse headcount-growth requests that exceed revenue-growth requests.** If your CRO, CMO, or CPO wants to grow revenue 2x and needs to grow the team 2x or 3x to do it, kindly thank them and move on. In the AI era, revenue growth should outpace headcount growth — meaningfully. If your execs are still playing the 2021 headcount game, they're not going to operate the AI stack that matters.
16. **Get off your D minus. You had 2025. You don't get another pass.** If your growth didn't reaccelerate this year, that's a D minus — not an F, because you made it through, but you can't get a D next year. You're already late. Your job for 2026 is to find the tailwinds: the AI budget, the AI demand, the I-need-this-for-AI categories where buyers have their hands in their wallets. Go steal some. Quit hiding.

## Signature Numbers

- **50%+** — percentage of contact center interactions most leaders want AI-automated; the target that used to be 0–10% just 18 months ago. Sales is next.
- **11–40x** — AI SDR volume vs. human SDR volume, at comparable or better response rates (SaaStr's own data).
- **36% vs 19%** — share of B2B companies that decreased vs. increased SDR/BDR headcount in the last year (Emergence Capital, 400+ companies). The highest headcount-reduction rate of any sales function.
- **62%** — share of buyers who don't want to talk to sales until the decision stage. Qualification calls before that are now friction, not value.
- **95%** — share of human SDRs who can't answer a tough technical question from a VP Eng, CPO, or CIO — Lemkin's bar, and the reason AI wins here.
- **20% / 60% / 20%** — top / middle / bottom rep distribution. The middle 60% is what AI replaces first. The top 20% become superhuman. The bottom 20% leave the way they always did.
- **3x** — the rate in-person meetings close at vs. virtual. Still true. Still underused — only 27% of B2B meetings happen in person.
- **2:1 or 3:1** — SE-to-AE ratio at AI-native companies. Inverted from the traditional 4:1 AE-to-SE ratio.
- **~8 months** — the window Lemkin gives companies to adapt to AI-first GTM or fall permanently behind.
- **50% + 30% = no room** — of record software spend next year, half goes to existing-vendor price increases, 30% goes to new AI tools. If you're not in one of those two groups, there's no budget left for you.
- **5x** — what the best AI products can charge vs. their pre-AI equivalents. Cursor at $200–$5,000/seat vs. Atlassian at $5/seat. Gamma at $100/seat vs. Google Slides at free. TAM expansion is where AI companies actually print money.
- **D minus** — the grade Lemkin gives most B2B companies for their use of 2025. Next year it's an F.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $12M ARR, sales team of 18 (4 AEs, 10 SDRs, 3 SEs, 1 sales ops). Outbound email response rates have collapsed from 4% to 0.8% over 18 months. The SDR team is missing quota. The VP Sales wants to add 4 more SDRs to "hit the lead numbers." Board is pushing to hit $25M next year.
**Application:** Don't add the SDRs. You're running a 2021 org in a 2026 market. Cut the SDR team from 10 to 2–3. Redirect the saved comp into one AI SDR platform + one FDE/SE hire + one senior AE hire. Rebalance the team ratio toward SEs. Give the remaining humans the top 40% of accounts and let the AI SDR cover the long tail + inbound. Refuse the headcount request. Revenue growth needs to come from AI leverage, not more humans sending dying outbound emails. Tell the VP Sales what the new stack looks like — if he can't operate it, start looking for his replacement.
**Result:** Founder cuts to 2 SDRs, hires 1 FDE and 1 senior AE, deploys AI SDR + AI inbound agent. Within 6 months, qualified meetings are up 3x (mostly from the AI inbound agent catching leads humans missed), close rates are up because the FDE is running technical sells, and total sales headcount is down 5. VP Sales is replaced 4 months in with someone who's actually operated an AI stack.

**Situation:** Founder at $8M ARR has spent 2025 adding "AI features" to the product — an AI summary button, a chat co-pilot, an AI-generated report template. Growth has flatlined at 18% YoY. A VC asks why the AI features didn't move the number. The founder says "customers love the AI features." Meanwhile a competitor launched 14 months ago is at $45M ARR, AI-native, and closing deals that used to be yours.
**Application:** The AI features don't count. They're necessary but not sufficient. The competitor wins because their product either replaces humans, dramatically augments them, or enables 5x TAM expansion — and yours doesn't. A co-pilot on top of 2021 software does none of those. The honest question: did you gain or lose market share this year? You lost it. Stop adding AI features. Go rebuild the product core around one of the three budget categories — human replacement, dramatic augmentation, or massive TAM expansion. Pick one, do it seriously, by mid-2026. Otherwise the competitor owns your market by the end of next year.
**Result:** Founder kills the co-pilot project, rebuilds the product around a workflow that replaces a full FTE of work at customer sites, repositions pricing from $12k ACV to $60k ACV. Growth reaccelerates to 85% YoY in 12 months. Not enough to catch the competitor, but enough to stay in the game and get the next round funded.

**Situation:** Founder is interviewing two VP Sales candidates. Candidate A ran sales at a $50M–$150M ARR traditional SaaS company, great logos, strong references, "interested in AI." Candidate B ran sales at a smaller ($8M–$40M ARR) AI-native startup, has personally deployed 5 AI agents in the GTM stack, is more junior on classic enterprise sales process.
**Application:** For a company going into 2026 at $6M–$30M ARR, Candidate B is the right hire in most cases. The "interested in AI" answer from Candidate A is a pass-level signal — the bar is active operating experience, not curiosity. Candidate B's smaller company experience is the right stage (per the 48 Types framework), and their actual AI stack operating experience is exactly what this job requires. The only case for Candidate A: you're a traditional enterprise motion selling to non-tech buyers (healthcare, government, heavy industry) where AI adoption is still 5+ years away from the buyer. Most founders are not in that case.
**Result:** Founder hires Candidate B. Within 90 days, the GTM stack has 3 new agents running (AI SDR, AI inbound qualifier, AI meeting prep), sales headcount grows modestly while pipeline doubles. The VP builds a team of 2 SEs per AE instead of 4 AEs per SE. One year in, the company is growing 2x faster than peers who hired the traditional profile.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Defend your SDR team because "they just need better training."**
*Why:* The email-based SDR job is dying, not under-trained. No amount of coaching makes a human SDR competitive with an AI SDR on volume, consistency, or 2am response times. The productivity gap is 11–40x. You can't train around that. The right move is to cut the role, redeploy budget to AI SDRs plus a smaller team of humans doing phone, field, and strategic account work — not to send your existing SDRs to another outbound bootcamp. You're polishing a role that's leaving the market.

**Don't: Use "AI is replacing reps" as cover to fire your top 20%.**
*Why:* The top reps aren't going anywhere. They're the ones who already do the work — customized demos, technical depth, real follow-up, arming the champion. AI makes them superhuman, not obsolete. If you fire a top rep because you're "cutting to AI," you just handed a killer to a competitor. Cut the middle. Keep the top. Keep the bottom moving out the door the way it already was. Get the sorting right.

**Don't: Build a co-pilot and call it your AI strategy.**
*Why:* Microsoft couldn't get people to pay $20 extra for Copilot in Word. If the biggest software company on earth can't extract money for a co-pilot bolted onto the most dominant productivity product ever made, you probably can't either. AI features are necessary. They're not sufficient. No one is raising a new budget line for "the product got a little nicer." Budget flows to products that replace humans, dramatically augment them, or 5x TAM. Decide which one you are — or stop calling it an AI strategy.

**Don't: Trust the vertical AI moat or the horizontal AI moat as automatic.**
*Why:* Neither is safe by default. Vertical AI companies have domain and customer moats but limited TAM and slow compounding if the vertical is small. Horizontal AI companies have distribution and data moats but get commoditized by the next model release. Whichever side you picked, you have to actively compound the moat — proprietary workflow data, customer-specific training loops, deep integrations, switching costs. If you're just "vertical AI for X" with no real compounding moat, the next model release eats you.

**Don't: Let your exec team ask for headcount that exceeds revenue growth.**
*Why:* In 2021 it was normal to double the team to double revenue. In 2026 it's a tell. If your CRO wants to grow revenue 2x and needs 3x the team to do it, they're running a 2021 playbook. The efficient companies are doing the opposite — revenue up, headcount flat or down, per-employee revenue up 2–3x. HubSpot's up 2.6x. Salesforce up 2.2x. Microsoft's absolute headcount is shrinking. An exec who can't operate more efficiently every year is an exec you can't afford to keep.

**Don't: Hide the competitor slide from your board deck.**
*Why:* The single cleanest tell that a CEO knows they're losing share and doesn't want to face it. Lemkin has seen it directly — a CEO moved the competitor slide to "hidden" in Q2, deleted it in Q3. The company had stopped gaining share. Same thing will happen to you if you stop tracking honestly. Track competitor ARR, growth rate, customer logos, even web traffic as a crude proxy. If you're losing share, say it out loud. You can't fix what you won't name.

**Don't: Wait for the market to force your hand on AI.**
*Why:* You already had 2025 to compound. You had the year — Claude, GPT-5, agentic tools that actually worked, a buying environment where CIOs had AI as their top 1–2 priority. If you coasted, that was a D minus. If you coast again in 2026, the competitor who didn't coast has a 24-month head start and eats your market share. You don't need to catch Cursor. You do need to ship a real AI product, in market, with measurable traction, before the year is out. The tailwinds are real and massive. You just have to go take some.
