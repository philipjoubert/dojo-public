# PMF Signals

## When to Use
- You're between $10K MRR and $5M ARR and you genuinely don't know if you have PMF or are faking it with hustle.
- Growth flattened at $1M, $3M, or $5M ARR and you need to figure out whether you ever had PMF or have fallen out of it.
- You're about to hire a VP Sales, double your burn, or scale a channel — and you want to confirm PMF before you bet the cash.
- An investor asks "do you have PMF?" and you want a real answer, not a NPS score and a vibe.
- You have happy customers but leads aren't growing and you can't figure out why.
- You're considering a pivot vs. staying the course at $1.5M ARR growing 40% a year.

## Fails When
- You're pre-$10K MRR. The sample is too small. Go get 20 paying customers, then measure.
- You treat PMF as a one-time achievement. It isn't. You can have it at $1M, lose it by $5M, and claw it back. Measure it every quarter, forever.
- You use NPS, surveys, or the Sean Ellis "40% would be very disappointed" test as your *primary* signal. Those are fine as a check. They are not the signal. The signal is in the pipeline and the customer count.
- You absorb "product-market fit" as your own framework. It isn't. It's Marc Andreessen's term. What's yours is the operational version — the five observable signs. Cite the origin honestly.
- You use "we don't quite have PMF yet" as permission to stay in iteration mode forever. At some point, if you can't get to 10%+ monthly growth at $10K–$50K MRR with a reasonable-sized market, the answer isn't more iteration. It's a different product, a different ICP, or a different founder.

## Core Concept

Product-market fit is Marc Andreessen's term — he coined it. But the way most founders use it is useless: an abstract feeling, a survey score, a slide in a pitch deck. PMF is not a vibe. PMF is observable, in the pipeline, every single week. If you can't point to it on a dashboard, you don't have it. That's the whole operational reframe.

Here's the test. PMF in SaaS is when you have more leads than you know why you have. More customers than you know why. Inbound pull exceeds your capacity to handle it. Enterprise prospects you've never heard of start finding you on Google. Existing customers bring you new customers without being asked. Your customer count doubles monthly from a small base. And once you're past $500K ARR, you're growing over 100% a year. That's PMF. Not four of those. Most of them, most of the time. When you have it, you know — and so does everyone around you, because nobody can keep up with the inbound.

The blunt diagnostic: if you hit $1M ARR and you're growing under 100% a year in this Cloud market, you *don't quite* have PMF. The Cloud is too big and growing too fast for a real PMF business to grow 40–60% at that stage. You might have a compelling pitch. You might have happy customers. You might be solving a real problem — just not a big enough one, yet, the way you're solving it. Don't gaslight yourself. Figure out the minimum feature set that truly solves the customer's problem, ship it, and watch growth accelerate. Or accept you're at the edge of fit, not inside it.

And PMF is losable. Startups growing fast at $1M ARR routinely fall out of PMF at $5M–$10M — happy customers, positive NRR, and yet leads stop growing, close rates drift down, and you don't know why. The signals fade the same way they arrived: customer pull weakens, the enterprise inbound dries up, word-of-mouth goes quiet. When you see that, stop scaling. Don't hire more reps. Go back to first principles — talk to every customer until you pass out, ship 10x features, and rebuild the pull. Scaling a broken motion just burns cash faster.

## How to Apply

1. **Know whose framework this is.** PMF is Andreessen's concept. The five operational signs below are how it shows up, day to day, in a SaaS business. When you explain PMF to the board, attribute the term. When you diagnose your own company, use the signs.
2. **Check the five signs, together, not one at a time.** (a) More leads than you know why you have — inbound pull exceeds your capacity to respond. (b) Customer count doubling monthly from a small base (true between roughly $10K and $50K MRR). (c) Word-of-mouth customers — existing customers bringing you new ones without being asked. (d) Enterprise prospects and potential customers finding *you* — inbound from logos you didn't outbound. (e) Growing >100% YoY once you're past $500K ARR. PMF means you see most of these, most of the time. If you're hitting one and faking the rest with outbound, you don't have it.
3. **Run the $1M ARR test honestly.** At $1M ARR growing under 100% annually in a normal SaaS market, you don't quite have PMF. You may be close — especially with high NPS and happy customers — but you haven't fully solved the problem. Don't scale yet. Find the minimum feature set that actually closes the gap and ship it.
4. **Check the $10K MRR / 10% monthly floor.** Past $10K MRR, if you can't grow 10% a month, you're probably not there yet. The math of small numbers says once you have real fit, you get another 50 customers as easily as the first 50. If you can't, something in the pitch, the ICP, or the insertion point isn't clicking. Keep iterating. But be honest — at some point "keep iterating" is a euphemism for "this one's not it."
5. **Don't substitute NPS or surveys for the observable signs.** High NPS at 40% YoY growth at $1M ARR is a signal you're solving *a* problem, just not a big enough one yet. NPS is a useful secondary check. It is not the test. Inbound volume is the test.
6. **Measure PMF every quarter, not once.** PMF is a condition, not a milestone. Track the five signs on a quarterly dashboard from $10K MRR onwards. Watch for early fade — usually the leads-growth signal goes first, then enterprise inbound, then word-of-mouth. By the time revenue slows, you've been out of fit for two quarters.
7. **When PMF is clearly missing, stop scaling.** Don't hire more reps. Don't raise the burn. Don't double down on paid. All of those are scaling moves that assume you have a working motion. You don't. Cut to first principles: talk to customers, rebuild the 10x feature gap, tighten the ICP.
8. **When PMF is fading (you had it, now it's dimming), diagnose the root cause from a short list.** 90% of the time it's a missing VP (Product, Sales, or Demand Gen) — you outgrew a junior team. Sometimes it's one channel that faded without a replacement. Sometimes you didn't add enough features and the competition caught up. Fix the root cause, not the symptom.
9. **Don't confuse PMF with founder-led sales being enough.** If the founder has to be on every call to close, you don't have PMF yet — you have founder-market fit. Real PMF is when reps without you can close the deal. Until a good rep can close without the founder, you're still proving the motion.
10. **Don't raise a scaling round on pre-PMF numbers.** Series A fundable on early PMF (the five signs) is real. Scaling capital pre-PMF burns faster and does nothing. If you can't point to the signs on the dashboard, raise a smaller round — or none — and keep iterating.
11. **Watch for the fake version — outbound-driven "PMF."** You can hit $1M–$2M ARR on pure outbound hustle with zero inbound pull. That's not PMF. That's a sales team. It'll break at $3M–$5M when the outbound yield flattens and there's no demand beneath it. If your inbound / outbound mix is 10/90 at $2M ARR, you don't have PMF — you have a sales motion that's papering over a market problem.
12. **Re-check PMF before every major capital allocation decision.** Hiring a VP Sales? Confirm PMF first. Doubling ad spend? Confirm PMF first. Opening a new segment? Confirm PMF in the core segment first. Every major scaling move presumes PMF. If you're wrong, you compound the wrong bet.

## Signature Numbers

- **$10K MRR** — the earliest you can meaningfully measure. Below that, any signal is noise.
- **10%+ month-over-month growth** between $10K and $50K MRR — the minimum for "you probably have it." Below that, you likely don't, yet.
- **$500K ARR** — past this, real PMF means >100% YoY growth. In the Cloud market, anything less is a yellow flag.
- **$1M ARR growing <100% annually** — the Lemkin test for "you don't quite have PMF." You may be close. You are not inside it.
- **$5M–$10M ARR** — the stage at which previously-fit startups most commonly fall out of PMF. Usually tied to not building the VP layer fast enough.
- **5 signs** — leads exceed capacity, customer count doubling monthly, word-of-mouth, enterprise inbound, >100% YoY at $500K+. You want most of them, most of the time.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $1.3M ARR, growing 45% YoY, NPS of 62, loves her customers. Asks: do I have PMF? Board is pushing her to hire a VP Sales and "scale."
**Application:** She doesn't, quite. Happy customers and a 62 NPS say she's solving a real problem — but 45% YoY at $1.3M ARR in Cloud is the Lemkin yellow flag. Run the five signs. Leads-exceed-capacity: no, she has enough bandwidth. Doubling monthly: long past that phase, but check: are leads growing 8–10% a month? Enterprise inbound: does her pipeline show logos she didn't outbound? Word-of-mouth: what fraction of deals came from existing customer referrals last quarter? If three or four of the five are weak, she's at the edge of fit, not inside it. The prescription: don't hire the VPS yet. Find the minimum feature set that would push NPS-60 customers into actively telling others. Ship it. Watch growth re-accelerate past 100% before scaling GTM.
**Result:** Six months later, after shipping two 10x features her top-10 accounts asked for, inbound doubled. Word-of-mouth deals went from 8% to 26% of pipeline. Growth went from 45% to 110% YoY. *Now* she hired the VP Sales — into PMF, not ahead of it. The VP's first two reps hit plan in 90 days because the market pulled.

**Situation:** Founder at $6M ARR. Was growing 140% at $1M. Now at 22% annually. Happy customers, NRR of 118%, churn low. Wondering if this is a sales problem, a product problem, or something else. Board wants to fire the VP Sales.
**Application:** This is the classic fall-out-of-PMF pattern. Happy customers, positive NRR — and yet leads aren't growing and close rates slipped. Don't fire the VPS yet. Run the five signs again. Are enterprise prospects still finding them inbound? Is word-of-mouth still bringing deals? Has LVR been positive every month? Usually the answer is: the first two faded 2–3 quarters ago and the team didn't notice because revenue was still catching up. The root cause, 90% of the time, is the missing management layer — no real VP Product, a weak VP Demand Gen, features didn't keep pace with competition. Fix the root cause. Hire the VPs. Talk to every customer until you pass out. Build what they'll pay more for. You can claw PMF back — several of the biggest SaaS outcomes are companies that fell out and got back in.
**Result:** In this case, LVR had been flat for 14 months, word-of-mouth fraction had dropped from 34% to 11%, and the product roadmap hadn't shipped a meaningful feature in 4 quarters. The problem wasn't the VPS — it was a missing VP Product and a stale roadmap. Fixed that, re-engaged the top 50 customers, shipped 3 major features in 9 months. Leads re-accelerated. Not to 140%, but to 70% YoY at $9M ARR — and climbing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Use NPS or a "would you be very disappointed" survey as your primary PMF test.**
*Why:* Surveys are secondary. Plenty of companies have high NPS and no PMF — they're solving a small problem very well. And plenty of companies with PMF have messy NPS because they're scaling faster than they can support. The observable signals — inbound volume, word-of-mouth fraction, enterprise pull, growth rate — are the real test. Surveys can confirm. Surveys cannot substitute.

**Don't: Claim PMF at $1M ARR growing 40% annually.**
*Why:* Cloud markets are large and fast. Real PMF produces >100% YoY growth at $500K–$2M ARR almost automatically, because once you've found 50 happy customers, there are usually thousands more exactly like them. 40% says you're solving a problem, just not a big enough one, yet. Don't fake it to the board. Don't fake it to yourself. Find the missing piece.

**Don't: Scale on top of fake PMF (pure outbound).**
*Why:* You can get to $2M ARR with a good SDR team on a product that has no organic pull. That's a sales achievement, not PMF. It breaks at $3M–$5M when outbound yield flattens and there's no inbound beneath. If your mix is 10% inbound / 90% outbound at $2M ARR, stop scaling and fix the pull first. Otherwise you're pouring capital into a motion with no bottom.

**Don't: Treat PMF as a one-time achievement.**
*Why:* PMF is a condition, not a trophy. The best SaaS companies on Earth have fallen out of it at $10M, $50M, $200M ARR — usually because they didn't build the VP layer, didn't ship enough features, or let one channel carry the whole business. Check the five signs every quarter, forever. The moment you stop checking is the moment you stop seeing the fade.

**Don't: Keep iterating forever to "find PMF."**
*Why:* At some point, if 18 months of iteration hasn't produced 10%+ monthly growth at $20K MRR, the answer isn't another pivot. The answer is: this product, this ICP, this insertion point — the combination doesn't have a big enough market. Either find a fundamentally different angle (new ICP, new wedge, new problem) or stop. "Keep iterating" becomes a comfort blanket. Be honest about the line.
