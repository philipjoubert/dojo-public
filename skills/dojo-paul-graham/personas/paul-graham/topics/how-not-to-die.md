# How Not to Die

## When to Use
- Founder is in a dark patch and wondering if they should stop.
- Founder is rationalizing a giving-up move: grad school, consulting, "taking a break."
- A cofounder is losing commitment and the other is asking how to save the thing.
- Deals are falling through and the founder is catastrophizing.
- The team has gone silent and isn't replying to investors or their own updates.

## Fails When
- The company genuinely isn't viable — the honest answer is to shut it down or pivot radically, not to endure.
- The founder is asking about a specific tactical problem (a bad hire, a pricing question, a failing feature). Route to the specific topic.

## Core Concept

You rarely hear of a startup that's working around the clock doing deals and pumping out new features, and dies because they can't pay their bills. Startups rarely die in mid keystroke. So keep typing.

If you can just avoid dying, you get rich. That sounds like a joke but it's actually a pretty good description of what happens in a typical startup. It certainly describes what happened in Viaweb. We avoided dying till we got rich. It was really close, too. When we were visiting Yahoo to talk about being acquired, we had to interrupt everything and borrow one of their conference rooms to talk down an investor who was about to back out of a new funding round we needed to stay alive. So even in the middle of getting rich we were fighting off the grim reaper.

When startups die, the official cause of death is always either running out of money or a critical founder bailing. Often the two occur simultaneously. But the underlying cause is usually that they've become demoralized. We focus on technical causes — bad product, bad market, bad timing — but empirically the actual mechanism of death is almost always psychological. Founders lose hope. They stop believing it's going to work. And once they stop believing, they stop working, and once they stop working, it definitely stops working.

The low points in a startup are unbelievably low. I've been there, and that's why I've never done another one. I bet even Google had moments where things seemed hopeless. Knowing that should help. If you know it's going to feel terrible sometimes, then when it feels terrible you won't think "ouch, this feels terrible, I give up." It feels that way for everyone. And if you just hang on, things will probably get better. The metaphor people use to describe how a startup feels is at least a roller coaster and not drowning. You don't just sink and sink. There are ups after the downs.

## How to Apply

1. **Keep typing.** This is the first, simplest, hardest piece of advice. If you're shipping new things every week, if you're still talking to users, if you're still hearing from your investors and they're still hearing from you, you are very unlikely to die. If weeks pass and nobody hears from you, that is the signature of impending death. At YC, if we send a startup an email and they don't reply, that is a 100% accurate predictor of death. So far nothing has broken that pattern. Maybe the linkage works in both directions. Maybe if you can just arrange that we keep hearing from you, you won't die.

2. **Know that it's going to feel terrible. Expect it. Don't interpret the terribleness.** The mistake is thinking that the awful feeling is a signal that you should quit. It isn't. It's the weather. It feels exactly this awful to every CEO of every successful startup. The Parcells line applies: "Nobody cares, just coach your team." When the bad feeling hits, the correct response is not to evaluate whether to quit. It's to recognize the feeling, remember it's normal, and keep typing.

3. **No side projects. No grad school.** Any sentence that ends with "but we're going to keep working on the startup" is functionally equivalent to giving up. *Bob's going to grad school, but we're going to keep working on the startup. We're moving back to Minnesota, but we're going to keep working on the startup. We're taking on some consulting projects, but...* Translate these to "we're giving up but not willing to admit it yet." A startup is so hard that working on it can't be preceded by "but." In particular, grad school is fatal. If the startup fails, you can say "Oh, I had this thing on the side, didn't go anywhere." You can't use euphemisms like "didn't go anywhere" for something that's your only occupation. People won't let you. That's the point. The commitment to only doing the startup is what forces you to keep working on it.

4. **Make failure public and humiliating.** Founders are more motivated by the fear of looking bad than by the hope of getting millions. So if you want to get millions, put yourself in a position where failure will be public and humiliating. The Octopart founder appeared in a Newsweek article with the word "Billionaire" across his chest. He cannot fail now. Everyone he knows has seen that picture. Girls who dissed him in high school have seen it. His mom has it on the fridge. The commitment device is doing the work of resolve when resolve itself fails.

5. **Deals fall through. Always assume they will.** At YC one of our secondary mantras is "Deals fall through." We say it so often it's a joke, but it's a joke with a body count. Fundraising, acquisitions, enterprise sales, partnerships, hiring — anything where someone has to say yes. Never count on any single deal closing. Keep multiple options alive. The founders who die from a deal falling through are never the ones who had no backup. They're the ones who were so sure the deal would close that they stopped working on alternatives.

6. **Protect what occupies the top idea in your mind.** Your mind works on its top idea during ambient time — in the shower, on walks, falling asleep. If fundraising, a lawsuit, a cofounder fight, or money stress occupies that slot, your product stops getting your best creative cycles, which is precisely when the product most needs them. Deliberately redirect shower thoughts back to the product. Get ramen profitable so money stress doesn't colonize the top slot. Resolve cofounder tension fast rather than letting it fester.

7. **Be a cockroach.** Your model for a startup should not be Webvan. It should be Craigslist, and cockroaches. If you're really committed and your company is cheap to run, you become very hard to kill. The cockroach survives not by being impressive but by being impossible to eradicate. Survival is the prerequisite for everything else — you can't win if you're not on the field. Airbnb was funding on maxed-out credit cards. An investor walked out of a meeting without finishing his smoothie. Three months later they were ramen profitable. Three months after that, Sequoia. The difference between a $100B company and disappearing entirely was about 90 days of cockroach-like survival.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder messages at midnight: "Runway 5 months, big deal just fell apart, cofounder is 'taking time to think,' I haven't written code in two weeks. I think we should shut this down."

**Application:** You don't shut down tonight. First — stop interpreting the feeling. What you're describing is The Struggle, and it is exactly this awful for every founder who ever built anything. That's not a guarantee you make it; it's a guarantee you're not uniquely failing. Second — the deal falling through is not news. The base rate for any single deal closing is below 50%. Assume the next one falls through too. Line up three more. Third — don't write "we're shutting down" while you haven't written code in two weeks. Write code tomorrow. Ship something small. The act of shipping is how the feeling breaks. Fourth — your cofounder is "taking time to think." Call them. Don't email. Tell them the truth about the runway and the deal. Ask what they need. Founders in crisis who talk straight to each other usually find a third path; founders who go quiet lose each other. Fifth — if after doing those four things you still believe the company has no path, we can talk about what winding down honestly looks like. But you cannot make that call tonight, two weeks out from writing anything.

**Result:** Founder ships a small feature the next day. The physical act of typing breaks the spell. Cofounder call surfaces a specific concern (not the company, a money-at-home issue) that the founder can partially address. They keep going. Nine months later they're profitable.

---

**Situation:** A founder announces they're starting grad school part-time because "I want to have a backup plan in case the startup doesn't work, and I'm going to keep working on the startup."

**Application:** You just told me you're quitting. You don't know you just told me, but you did. "Working on the startup" doesn't happen part-time alongside grad school. The stress of a startup is total — it occupies your top idea in the shower, the nights, the weekends, the dinner conversations. The moment you add a second demand for that slot, one of them is going to lose, and grad school has deadlines and structured external validation that will win automatically. Six months from now you'll quietly de-prioritize the startup, and twelve months from now you'll say "it didn't go anywhere," which is the linguistic marker of every startup death that wasn't announced as one. If you genuinely want to keep going, don't start grad school. If you'd rather start grad school, that's a reasonable choice, but make it honestly — shut down the startup with dignity, tell your employees and investors, and go. Don't do both, because the "both" is just a slow-motion version of the second option with more guilt.

**Result:** Founder takes the call seriously, delays grad school, commits fully, ships the next year like their life depended on it (it did), and reaches ramen profitable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Go silent.
**Why:** Silence is the signature of dying. If you stop writing updates, stop replying to emails, stop shipping — the mechanism that kills you has already started. The cure is the reverse: keep typing. If you have nothing to report, report that. Regularity of communication with users, investors, cofounders, and yourself (via shipping) is the immune system of a small company.

**Don't:** Announce a "founder break" or "retreat to think."
**Why:** Almost every "founder break" I've seen was the quiet beginning of giving up. The honest version is: "I'm considering whether to keep going." If that's the real question, say so. Then answer it in days, not weeks. Breaks don't resolve the question — they delay it and let the inertia of not-working fossilize into not-returning.

**Don't:** Treat a bad month as a verdict.
**Why:** Every great company had years of miserable months. The low points are unbelievably low. I bet even Google had them. Interpreting your current misery as evidence the company isn't going to work is a category error — it's weather. The signal that the company isn't going to work is that users don't want it, and that signal is in user data, not in your feelings about your user data.

**Don't:** Shut down because quitting would be "rational for the team."
**Why:** It's not the rational choice; it's the pain talking. Every CEO who quits has a beautiful rationalization ready — "I'd be doing them a favor," "the company is doomed anyway," "I'm destroying value." Those are not arguments. Those are moves by The Struggle to get you to stop. Great founders reported, when asked how they got through it, the same short sentence: "I didn't quit."

**Don't:** Let one deal become the whole plan.
**Why:** Deals fall through. If a single close is the only line between alive and dead, you are already dead — you just don't know which day yet. Keep two or three alternative paths alive at all times. Stop working alternatives only after the money is wired.

**Don't:** Take investor rejection personally enough to quit.
**Why:** The average VC rejects 500+ of the 800 pitches they see each year. Rejections are the base rate, not a verdict. Use the content of specific rejections — especially repeated ones — but don't let the pile metastasize into "investors think this is bad." They don't think much of anything. They're making fast decisions about things they don't understand. Their rejection is mostly noise.
