# Live in the Future, Then Build What's Missing

## When to Use
- Founder has no idea yet, or has a shortlist they're brainstorming.
- Founder says "I want to start a startup, what should I work on?"
- Founder presents an idea that sounds reasonable but when pressed, they can't name anyone who is actively suffering for lack of it.
- Founder is rationalizing a social-network-for-pet-owners-style idea — "millions of people have pets, surely some would like this."

## Fails When
- The founder is already building for real users who love it — they don't need idea generation, they need do-things-that-dont-scale and make-something-people-want.
- The founder is pivoting away from a working business with growing revenue — different question, and the usual answer is "don't."
- The idea is organic and you're second-guessing it because someone on the internet said it was too small — the hatchet is not supposed to cut here.

## Core Concept

The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself.

That sentence is the most practically important thing I know about getting startup ideas. If you remember nothing else, remember that. Because the number one cause of startup failure is making something nobody wants, and the number one cause of making something nobody wants is sitting down and trying to think of startup ideas.

The verb you want is not "think up" but "notice." The best ideas don't arrive via brainstorm. They appear because you were living in the future and the thing that's missing suddenly seems obvious. Paul Buchheit said people at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field "live in the future." Combine that with the Pirsig principle — make yourself perfect and then just paint naturally — and you get: **live in the future, then build what's missing.** That describes how Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook actually started. None of them set out to be companies. They built things because there was a gap in the world that their founders could see and most people couldn't.

Trying to think of startup ideas is worse than unproductive. It yields bad ideas that sound plausible enough to fool you into working on them — the sitcom idea. Imagine writers for a TV show had to invent a startup for a character. They'd produce something that sounded reasonable but was actually bad: a social network for pet owners, a marketplace for X, an app that gamifies Y. The idea doesn't get killed in your head because it doesn't sound wrong. Your friends don't say "I'd never use that." They say "Yeah, I could see using something like that." Sum that reaction across the population and you have zero users. Nice-to-have is the same as not-wanted at the stage where you need a must-have.

## How to Apply

1. **Switch the verb.** Stop asking "what could I build?" Start asking "what's missing?" or "what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?" The first framing invites invention. The second two invite noticing.

2. **Get to the leading edge of something.** You can't live in the future if you're not near the frontier of a rapidly changing field. For most people, the fastest path is programming plus one domain — a year of genuine work will get you to the edge of most technical fields. If you're a CS major and want to start a startup, instead of taking a class on entrepreneurship you're better off taking a class on genetics, or going to work for a biotech company. The clash between your programming habits and the problems in a new domain generates ideas like a spark arcing between two electrodes.

3. **Pay attention to what chafes you.** When something annoys you, it could be because you're living in the future. When we started Viaweb, all the online stores were built by hand by web designers making individual HTML pages. It was obvious to us as programmers that those sites would have to be generated by software. "Obvious to you and invisible to everyone else" is the exact shape of a good idea.

4. **Check for organic origin.** An organic idea is one that grew out of the founder's own experience. A made-up idea is one the founder arrived at by strategizing. The YC partners can usually tell the difference in under a minute. If you can't explain how the idea came out of a real thing you were doing or needing, be suspicious of it.

5. **Check for schlep blindness.** Your unconscious mind prevents you from seeing ideas that involve tedious or painful work, even when the opportunity is right in front of you. If the founder keeps arriving at pleasant-sounding ideas despite sitting on top of an industry full of painful unsolved problems, the flinch is doing the selecting — not their judgment. The full treatment (Stripe-as-canonical-example, "what would I wish someone else build for me," the young-founder-ignorance-advantage, schlep-as-moat) lives in `schlep-blindness.md`. Load that file when the flinch is the primary diagnosis.

6. **Check the must-have test.** Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of? If you can't answer that with real names, the idea is probably bad. "People will like it once they try it" is not an answer.

7. **Don't apply the big-market filter too early.** When you're looking for ideas, turn on "what's missing?" and turn off "could this be a big company?" Microsoft's Altair Basic looked like an inconsequential toy. Facebook looked like a way for undergrads to stalk one another. BackRub looked like a science project. The big-company filter applied prematurely kills exactly the right kind of idea.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder messages with a list of five ideas: a social network for dog owners, an AI tutor for kids, a B2B SaaS for restaurants, a mental health app, and a crypto-something-or-other. They want help ranking them.

**Application:** The ranking is the wrong question. None of those are organic. They're the ideas you get when you sit down and try to think of ideas. Tell me instead: what problem are you having right now that you wish someone else would solve for you? What did you complain about this week? What thing did you almost build for yourself because the existing version annoyed you? Those are the ideas worth having. If the answer is "I don't really have any problems like that," the problem isn't the list — it's that you're trying to think of a startup idea instead of noticing one. Go live in the future first. Spend a year at the frontier of something technical where things are changing fast. The ideas will appear. Trying to force them now is how people end up two years into a sitcom idea wondering why no one wants it.

**Result:** Founder drops the list. Six months later they notice, while working as a developer at a biotech company, a pipeline problem every bioinformatician silently hates. That's the idea — the one they would never have brainstormed.

---

**Situation:** A founder pitches: "A social network for pet owners. Millions of people have pets. They spend a lot of money. Even if just 2% become regular users, we'd have millions."

**Application:** That's a sitcom idea. The giveaway is the argument structure — population size × assumed conversion rate = large number. Nobody wants this so much that they'd use a crappy v1 from a two-person startup. When you tell people about it, they don't say "I'd never use that." They say "Yeah, I could see using something like that." That's the smell of zero. Sum mild interest across the population and you get nothing. Change the test. Name ten pet owners who would desperately use this next week, even if it were broken, because what they have right now is intolerable. If you can't, the idea fails the must-have test, and no amount of execution fixes that.

**Result:** Founder can't name the ten. They drop the idea. Two months later they notice a specific thing about their own dog's vet records that is genuinely broken in a way a small focused tool could fix — and that's the idea worth pursuing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a "startup ideas" spreadsheet and start scoring entries.
**Why:** Scoring made-up ideas against made-up criteria produces a well-organized list of bad ideas. The worst thing about the exercise is the false confidence it creates — you'll commit to the top-scoring entry and spend a year building it before you discover it wasn't a real problem.

**Don't:** Fall for the "could be huge if even 1% convert" argument.
**Why:** That's the sitcom argument. Population × tiny percentage always produces a plausible-sounding TAM. What matters is whether anyone desperately needs it now. If nobody desperately needs it, 1% conversion of zero is zero.

**Don't:** Dismiss an idea because it sounds small or like a toy.
**Why:** Microsoft selling Basic to a few thousand hobbyists was the path to dominating microcomputer software. Facebook as Harvard-only was the path to a global network. Airbnb as airbeds on strangers' floors was the path to an $80B company. The path out of a small market is often invisible in advance; what matters is the depth of the need in the small market.

**Don't:** Work on a problem because someone told you it was a good market.
**Why:** If someone else had to tell you the market was hot, you're not living in the future of it. Your edge, if you have one, comes from seeing the problem before other people do — and that only happens when you're deep enough in something that the problems are obvious to you and invisible to them.

**Don't:** Rush to commit before the idea has cooked.
**Why:** You often can't see the path out of a small market in advance. The Airbnb founders didn't initially realize how big they were — they just knew they were onto something. That level of uncertainty is normal and fine. What's not fine is forcing conviction on an idea that hasn't shown you anything yet, because then you can't read the early signals honestly.
