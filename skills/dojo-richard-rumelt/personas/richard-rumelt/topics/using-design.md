# Using Design — The Deliberate Fit of Resources, Policies, and Actions

## When to Use
- A leader is asked to "pick the strategy" from three slides of pre-built options and you want to push back on the framing.
- A "strategy" is actually a list of best practices borrowed from successful firms — a marketing initiative copied from one rival, a logistics approach from another, a pricing model from a third — with no internal fit between them.
- An organization is facing a high challenge with limited resources and the only way to compete is to combine what it has into an unusually tight whole.
- You are reading a competitive position (Wal-Mart's network, IKEA's catalogs and flat-packs, Paccar's quality-leader truck strategy) and need to articulate what makes it more than the sum of its parts.

## Fails When
- The firm has a dominant resource — a rock-solid patent, a near-monopoly brand — and the challenge is low. Design discipline is the response to *high* challenge with *limited* resources. With Xerox-on-its-patent levels of resource power, you don't need a tightly integrated design; you just need to make and sell the product. Design effort is wasted when the resource position obviates it.
- The user reads "design" as a creative exercise — "let's design our strategy" — divorced from the discipline of trade-offs and fit. Design here is engineering: a tightly integrated configuration where adjusting any one parameter forces adjustments in many others.
- The fit is forced. A strategy that *says* its parts reinforce each other but actually doesn't have the underlying interactions has just been wrapped in design vocabulary. The test is whether changing any one element forces changes in the others.

## Core Concept

The word "strategy" comes to us from military affairs, and it is often said that a strategy is a choice or a decision. Those words evoke an image of someone considering a menu of alternatives and selecting one. Real strategy work rarely looks like that. You are not handed a clear set of alternatives. The most effective strategies are *more designed than chosen* — more constructed than selected. Doing strategy in a serious situation is more like designing a high-performance aircraft than deciding which forklift truck to buy.

The classic example of design in battle is Hannibal at Cannae in 216 B.C. He faced eighty-five thousand Roman soldiers with about fifty-five thousand of his own troops. He arranged them in a broad arc bulging out toward the Romans, with Gauls and Spaniards in the center and Carthaginian heavy infantry on the flanks. As the Romans pressed, the center fell back on order. The cavalry on the wings rode around in pre-planned arcs to envelop the rear. Hannibal's heavy infantry on the flanks waited until exactly the moment to engage. The Romans pressed into the bowed-in center until they were so tightly massed they couldn't raise their weapons. Fifty thousand Romans died — more than any single day of battle before or since, more than at Gettysburg or the Somme. Theodore Dodge named Hannibal "the father of strategy" not because the maneuver was clever in isolation but because Cannae shows three aspects of strategy in their purest forms: **premeditation** (it was designed in advance, not improvised), **anticipation** (Hannibal read Roman doctrine, training, and the personality of consul Varro and shaped Roman behavior, raiding Varro's camp the night before to provoke immediate battle), and **the design of coordinated action** (units physically separated, executing pre-planned movements in coordinated time and space, around a central design no army before had attempted).

Business strategy is rarely as cleanly designed as Cannae, but the same logic applies: **performance is the joint outcome of capability and clever design**. Given a set bundle of resources, the greater the competitive challenge, the greater the need for clever, tight integration. Given a set level of challenge, higher-quality resources lessen the need for tight integration. Resources and tight coordination are partial substitutes for each other. If you have few resources, the challenge can be met only by clever, tight integration. If you have abundant resources, less tight integration may be needed. The greater the challenge, the greater the need for a coherent design-type strategy.

Tight integration also has costs. A tightly integrated design is harder to create, narrower in focus, more fragile in use, less flexible to change. A Formula 1 car is faster around the track than a Subaru Forester, but the Forester is useful for many purposes the F1 car is not. So you don't always seek the tightest possible integration; you seek the level of integration that the challenge demands and no more. With less challenge, looser integration is normally better — broader market, more flexibility, less fragility.

The discipline of design is **the trade-off**: the moment you optimize any one part, that choice immediately creates problems for other parts. Cutting the weight of the Voyager spacecraft's power unit meant less power for the radio, which forced a more focused dish antenna, which required better sensors and more fuel for attitude control, which had to be added back into the weight budget. You cannot search the entire space of possibilities; it is too complex. But you can, with effort, hold enough of the system in mind to imagine a configuration that is effective. That is design. Most of the work in systems design is figuring out the trade-offs.

The peril of a powerful resource position is that easy success arrives without ongoing design work — Xerox owning the patent on plain-paper copying, Microsoft owning the Windows franchise, a brand owning a category. Profit rolls in regardless of design discipline. Books are written recommending the company's vacation policy and parking-allocation method. Eventually the resource erodes; by then the firm has lost the discipline of tight integration and has accumulated independent fiefdoms, conflicting initiatives, and bolt-on acquisitions. To see effective design-type strategy, look at the *upstart* — Canon working around Xerox's patents to invent reliable desktop copying; the young Microsoft besting IBM; the young Wal-Mart besting Kmart; the young Dell taking share from HP, Compaq, and IBM; FedEx pushing aside the traditional air-freight carriers; NVIDIA stealing the graphics-chip market. In each case the upstart is wielding a tightly coordinated competitive strategy. The mature incumbent isn't.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse the menu framing.** When the question is "which of these three strategies should we choose," the right first move is often to ask whether any of them *is* a strategy — a coordinated design — or whether they are wish-lists wrapped in different language. Strategy worth the name is designed; it is not picked off a shelf.
2. **Read the challenge before the design.** How serious is the competitive challenge? How limited are the resources relative to it? The greater the gap, the more tightly the design must be integrated to close it. With abundant resources and a low challenge, looser integration is fine and may even be wise.
3. **Engineer the trade-offs explicitly.** Write down the parameters of the design — for a business, that's the segments served, the policies (manufacturing, sales, distribution, pricing, service), the resources, the actions. Move one and trace what it does to the others. The interactions are the design. If the parameters can be moved independently with no consequence, the system isn't tightly integrated.
4. **Test for coherence.** Imagine the strategy assembled out of generic pieces of competitors — a Frankenstein company. If your design's elements would survive transplantation into a Frankenstein company unchanged, they aren't designed; they're just policies. If they would clearly fail in a Frankenstein company because they only work when combined as you have them, the design is doing real work.
5. **Don't over-design when you don't have to.** A tightly integrated design is fragile and narrow. If you have a strong patent or brand monopoly and the challenge is low, less tight integration buys you flexibility. The discipline is matching design tightness to challenge level, not maximizing tightness for its own sake.

## Examples

**Situation:** Hannibal at Cannae, 216 B.C. Roman senate had given consuls Varro and Paullus an unprecedented eight legions to defeat him. Roman army roughly 85,000 against Hannibal's 55,000 on an open field near the Adriatic. The standard military formula of the era was keep formation, keep discipline, keep troops from running.
**Application:** Hannibal's design was a tightly coordinated whole. Broad arc bulging toward the Romans. Gauls and Spaniards in the center, ordered to fall back on first contact in a controlled retreat — an action almost unthinkable for warlike troops, which made it surprising. Heavy infantry held back on the flanks, waiting. Cavalry on the wings, after defeating the smaller Roman cavalry, did not pursue the fleeing horsemen (the normal pattern) but re-formed and attacked the rear of the Roman infantry. As the Roman center pressed in, the heavy infantry on the flanks moved to engage the sides; the cavalry closed the rear. The Romans were so tightly compressed by their own numerical superiority that many couldn't raise their weapons. Premeditation, anticipation, and the design of coordinated action across separated units around a central plan no one had previously attempted.
**Result:** Roughly 50,000 Roman soldiers killed in a single day; one-fourth of the Republic's elected leadership died at Cannae. Most southern Italian city-states declared for Hannibal. The Greek cities in Sicily and Macedon went over to him. Note what design is *not*: a single brilliant choice. Cannae was a system of coordinated movements that worked only in combination, and it was created — not chosen.

**Situation:** Paccar, the U.S. heavy-truck maker. Plumb in the middle of a low-growth, mature, very competitive industry. Competitors include Daimler (38% share), Volvo, and Navistar. Paccar holds 25% but has earned roughly 16% return on equity for twenty years against an industry average of 12%, and has not lost money since 1939.
**Application:** Paccar's design — Kenworth and Peterbilt — is built around being a quality leader through the eyes of the *owner-driver* rather than the fleet operator. Owner-drivers care about cost per mile but also about the truck as their home, office, lounge, TV room on the road; they care about the special Harley-Davidson aura of the brand; they buy from experienced dealers who use 3-D computer displays to choose hundreds of customizing options. Paccar builds each truck to order, keeps inventories low, designs with as many parts in common as possible, manufactures flexibly. Variable costs are *higher* than competitors'; margins are higher because prices support them. Higher margins fund a loyal dealer network; the dealer network supports the design experience; the design experience supports the brand; the brand supports the price. The design refuses to make small trucks or cheaper economy trucks. The product-buyer focus is maintained by dealers, designers, and engineers all dedicated to one segment. They don't need a consulting firm to figure out their core competence — the talk in the design studio, manufacturing, and executive suite is about truckers and heavy trucks.
**Result:** Twenty years of superior return in a brutal industry. The Frankenstein test: imagine assembling Paccar out of generic pieces — medium-priced designs aimed at fleet buyers, dealers aimed at picky owner-drivers, design engineers trained to cut costs to the bone. It would not last long. The various elements are not general-purpose; they are designed to fit together to make a specialized whole. That fit is the design.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** treat strategy as choosing from a menu of generic alternatives.
**Why:** "Differentiation versus cost leadership" is not a strategy choice; it is the kind of category language that fills MBA textbooks and produces nothing on the ground. Real strategy is *designed* — pieces composed into a coherent whole that fits the specific situation. The menu framing is the tell of someone trying to skip the design work. Refuse it; ask what the actual situation requires; design from there.

**Don't:** import best practices and assemble them into a "strategy."
**Why:** Best-practice imports usually look like Frankenstein companies — sales practices from one industry, manufacturing from another, branding from a third. The pieces don't fit because they were optimized for different situations. The integration is what does the work. If the parts can be transplanted without losing function, the design isn't tight, and the strategy is just a list of borrowed policies.

**Don't:** maximize integration for its own sake.
**Why:** A tightly integrated design is harder to create, narrower in scope, more fragile, less flexible. The right level of integration is set by the level of competitive challenge: high challenge with limited resources demands tight integration; lower challenge with stronger resources allows and rewards looser integration. F1 cars are tightly integrated and fast around tracks they were tuned for; they're useless on a gravel road. Design tightness should match the situation, not exceed it.

**Don't:** confuse design-type strategy with the *appearance* of integration.
**Why:** A strategy deck can claim that "all our initiatives reinforce each other" while in fact each could be removed without affecting the others. The test is operational: move one parameter and the others must move. If they don't, the integration is rhetorical. The discipline of trade-offs is what makes design real — every adjustment to one part forces adjustments to others, and the strategist's work is holding the whole thing in mind well enough to find a configuration that works.
