# Via Negativa

## When to Use

- When you are facing a complex problem and the instinct is to *add* — a process, a policy, a tool, a meeting, a supplement, a feature.
- When the field offering advice is full of confident positive prescriptions and the track records of the prescribers are unverifiable.
- When you need a heuristic for separating charlatans (positive advice) from real experts (negative advice).
- When the cost of intervention in a complex system is potentially unbounded and the cost of inaction is bounded.
- When you're being told to optimise a system that may be optimising itself already and would do better with less interference.
- When you want to become wiser, healthier, freer, or more focused — and the path is overwhelmingly through removal of bads, not acquisition of goods.
- When you're designing a default rule for an organisation under uncertainty.

## Fails When

- **The situation genuinely requires affirmative action.** A bleeding patient needs a tourniquet, not the absence of intervention. A failing rocket needs an engine restart, not abstention. Apply the principle to *complex evolved systems under uncertainty*, not to mechanical emergencies with known fixes.
- **You weaponise it as a refusal to do anything.** "Via negativa" can degrade into a clever excuse for inaction across the board. The principle is about asymmetric removal of harms, not about cultivating quietism. Some additions — a vaccine that demonstrably works, a new business line backed by clear demand — are positive interventions that pass the test.
- **The system has been stripped already.** A team that has cut every meeting, every process, every tool reaches a point where adding the next one is correct. Subtraction is not infinite. There is a floor.
- **The negative rule becomes its own fragilising ritual.** Religious-style abstention from things that are actually fine — every social interaction, every food group, every digital tool — is a fragility-producing pattern dressed up in the language of subtraction. The point is removing harms, not performing self-denial.

## Core Concept

Knowledge accumulates by *subtraction*, not addition. The strongest things we know are not what we have proven true, but what we know to be false. The strongest interventions are not what we have added, but what we have removed. Once you can name this asymmetry, half of self-help, half of public-health policy, and most of strategy consulting reveal themselves as inverted — confident positive prescriptions where the operationally correct move is removing the harm that's already there.

The empirical case is overwhelming. The single largest medical advance of the post-war era was telling people *not to smoke* — by Druin Burch's calculation, eliminating smoking provides more health benefit than every medical intervention developed since WWII *combined*. Most diet improvements are about removal (sugar, processed seed oils, snacks between meals) rather than addition. Most strategy improvements are about cutting bad customers, killing bad products, walking away from bad deals. Most leadership improvements are about firing the wrong person rather than hiring the right one. Most personal-finance improvements are about removing leverage and counterparty risk, not about chasing returns.

The structural reason. Positive advice — do X, take Y, eat Z, follow this method — is the natural mode of charlatans because it is unfalsifiable in the short run. When X doesn't work, the prescriber blames execution, timing, mindset, or the universe. Negative advice — don't do X — has a tighter feedback loop: if you stop and you improve, the rule worked; if you stop and don't improve, the rule was wrong about that thing. Religions encode mostly negative rules because negative rules survive Lindy. The Ten Commandments are mostly *thou shalt not*. The Hippocratic Oath is *primum non nocere*. The accumulated wisdom of three thousand years of moral philosophy is overwhelmingly subtractive, and not because the people who wrote it lacked imagination — because the additions kept failing and the subtractions kept holding.

Application to risk: the *Antifragile* program is a sustained via negativa. Don't try to forecast the Black Swan; *remove* the fragilities that would make the Black Swan ruinous. Don't try to optimise portfolio returns; *remove* the sources of ruin. Don't try to engineer the perfect public-health response; *remove* the excessive interventions that stack up iatrogenic risk. Application to expertise: the expert-detection rule is via negativa. It is much easier to identify charlatans (those with positive-advice patterns and no skin in the game) than to identify real experts. Don't try to find the "best" macroeconomist; remove all macroeconomists from your decision process and you will be measurably better off.

Application to decisions: faced with a complex problem, ask first — *what should I stop doing?* Most additions to a complex system add hidden interactions, second-order effects, and fragility. Most subtractions reduce these. The default move under uncertainty is subtraction. Application to life: subtractive epistemology. You become wise by removing illusions, not by accumulating beliefs. You become healthy by removing bad habits, not by adopting fads. You become wealthy by removing leverage and counterparty risk, not by chasing returns. You become free by removing dependencies, not by acquiring credentials.

The relationship to the precautionary principle. Via negativa is the operational version of the precautionary principle in domains with unbounded downside. When the cost of being wrong is bounded, optimise freely. When the cost is unbounded — extinction, ruin, irreversible damage to a complex system you don't understand — *don't intervene*, even if the expected value calculation says you should. The expected value calculation is wrong, because the calculation assumes ergodic averaging across futures and you walk one path.

> *At the core of the expert problem is that people are suckers for charlatans who provide positive advice (what to do), instead of negative advice (what not to do).*
>
> *Telling people NOT to smoke seems to be the greatest medical contribution of the last 60 years. The harmful effect of smoking are roughly equivalent to the combined good ones of EVERY medical intervention developed since the war.*

## How to Apply

1. **Reverse the question.** When asked "what should we add?", first ask "what should we remove?" In strategy reviews, product roadmaps, personal life audits — the subtraction question is almost always the higher-leverage one and it's almost never asked.

2. **Audit positive advice for falsifiability.** When someone gives you a "do X" prescription, ask how you would know if it was wrong. If the answer is "you didn't do it correctly" or "it takes time to manifest," the advice is unfalsifiable and likely from a charlatan. Real positive advice has a tight feedback loop.

3. **Apply via negativa to your professional roster.** Look at the people whose advice you take — financial advisors, lawyers, consultants, doctors, coaches. Which of them gives mostly positive prescriptions with no track record? Remove them. The roster shrinks; the quality of decisions improves.

4. **Treat the default of inaction as informationally rich.** The system you're being asked to fix has been working — that is itself evidence that the do-nothing path is survivable. The proposed intervention should justify its expected harm, not the inaction.

5. **Build a Don't List.** For your own work, life, and team, maintain an explicit list of things you have decided not to do, foods you have decided not to eat, products you have decided not to ship, customers you have decided not to serve. The list compounds in value over time in a way that a To-Do list does not.

6. **In medicine and policy, weight the *invisible* harm.** Most interventions have visible benefit (specific, easy to point at) and invisible harm (diffuse, requires looking). The visible side wins political contests; the invisible side wins reality contests. Force yourself to enumerate the invisible side before approving the intervention.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is being told the company needs to "add a layer of management" to scale. The proposed layer comes with a process, an org chart, a software tool, weekly meetings, a quarterly cycle.
**Application:** Via negativa first. What in the current organisation is producing the symptom that the new layer is supposed to fix? Usually it's a specific broken thing — one team that owns too much, one decision that nobody can make, one piece of information that doesn't propagate. The subtractive move is to identify and remove the specific obstacle (split the team, push the decision to the relevant owner, build the one missing dashboard). The additive move (the new layer) introduces every second-order effect that bureaucracy introduces — slower decisions, displaced accountability, more meetings, more politics — and nobody has costed them.
**Result:** The founder who applies via negativa fixes the specific bottleneck and keeps the company light. The founder who adds the layer discovers eighteen months later that the symptoms have not improved and the company is now harder to operate. The new layer cannot be removed because it has its own constituency.

**Situation:** A team is debating which of seventeen new productivity tools to adopt this quarter. The market is full of confident positive recommendations.
**Application:** Via negativa says: what tools or processes can we *remove* this quarter? Which existing meetings could we cancel? Which standing reports could we stop producing? Which dashboards does nobody read? The probability that the seventeen positive recommendations are net-positive is low (they each add load, and the load compounds). The probability that the existing meeting cadence has accumulated waste is high (it always has). The leverage is on the subtractive side.
**Result:** The team that subtracts has more time and a clearer picture. The team that adds has seventeen new tools, none of which they fully use, and the seventeenth one's vendor is the next conference booth in their inbox.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply via negativa to a system that is currently failing for lack of structure.
**Why:** Some teams need the standup; some processes need to be added; some children need a routine. Via negativa is the right default for complex evolved systems that are mostly working. It is the wrong default for under-built systems that need scaffolding. The diagnostic question is whether the system was producing reasonable outcomes before the proposed intervention. If yes, default to subtraction. If no, structure may genuinely be missing.

**Don't:** Use it as a clever wrapper for laziness.
**Why:** "I'm doing via negativa" can become an excuse for not engaging with hard problems that require actual work. The principle is about asymmetric removal of harms with bounded cost; it is not about avoiding effort. The work of identifying the specific harm to remove is itself work, often more demanding than the additive alternative.

**Don't:** Apply the rule to negative advice that is itself a fragilising ritual.
**Why:** Religious abstention from every social interaction, every food group, every form of pleasure can be its own fragility — a brittle system that breaks the moment it encounters the abstained-from thing. The principle is about removing demonstrable harms, not about cultivating delicate purity. The grandmother who avoids cigarettes is applying via negativa correctly. The orthorexic who avoids every food group with any imperfect study is not.
