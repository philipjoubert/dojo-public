# Peer Review and Peers vs. Time

## When to Use

- When somebody invokes peer-reviewed publication as a closing argument for the truth of a claim.
- When a credentialed actor's reputation is grounded in awards, citations, or prizes from other credentialed actors rather than from reality outcomes.
- When you are operating in a field where the primary grading mechanism is peer evaluation — academia, journalism, certain corners of finance and consulting, prestige media, modern art markets.
- When you need to design your own career, output, or organisational reward structure to optimise for real impact rather than peer applause.
- When you notice monoculture in a field — every voice converging on the same view at the same time — and need a structural explanation.
- When you are choosing between writing, building, or working for present-moment recognition versus long-run impact.
- When you want to understand why some prestigious credentials systematically fail to translate to competence.

## Fails When

- **The field has working reality feedback.** Surgery, piloting, plumbing, engineering — the peer review is itself anchored to reality outcomes. A surgeon endorsed by other surgeons who have observed her surgical outcomes is being judged by reality, mediated through peers.
- **You dismiss every peer-validated person.** Some peer-validated people are also independently great. The framework targets the *structural* failure mode of fields that grade themselves; it isn't a permission slip to dismiss every credentialed peer-recognised actor.
- **The work is too new for time judgement.** Time is the only honest peer, but time takes time. For genuinely new work, peer judgement may be the only available filter. The framework says weight time when time has had a chance to act, not refuse all judgement.
- **"Peer review" actually means "peer criticism."** Some peer review processes are genuinely adversarial and produce real filtering. The failure mode is *peer flattery* dressed as review. Not all review is theatre; check the structure.

## Core Concept

A standalone Taleb argument that radiates across his career and explains why he refused tenure-track academic life, refused to be edited, and built his audience outside the credentialing system. Once you have seen the mechanism — peers grade peers, what peers like drifts from what is true, the field rots — you cannot un-see it in the prestige institutions of contemporary life.

The core claim. Any system in which the only judge of a contributor's work is the contributor's peers will rot. Peers select for what peers like. What peers like drifts arbitrarily far from what is *useful, true, or important to people outside the peer group*. Over time, the peer-judged field becomes a closed circle of mutual evaluation that has lost contact with reality. The drift is slow and invisible inside the field, because everyone in the field is being judged by the same drifting standard. From outside, the rot is obvious — it just takes time to surface as some specific failure (the financial crisis the macroeconomists missed, the replication crisis in psychology, the regime-change disasters the foreign-policy class endorsed).

Examples of peer-rotted fields. *Restaurants that win awards from other restaurateurs.* Most are out of business by the time the next year's awards are given. (BLOCKCON 2018, Naval.) *Academic social science.* "Macroeconomics, for instance, can be nonsense since it is easier to *macrobullshit* than *microbullshit* — given how abstract the effect on society, nobody can tell if a theory really works." Citation rings, peer-reviewed journals, the entire credentialing apparatus operates by mutual evaluation. The replication crisis is the predictable consequence. *Journalism.* "Journalists try to impress other journalists. That's their business. They're not working on the reader. So you can have monoculture and become very vulnerable." (BLOCKCON 2018.) Hence the *New Yorker* and *New York Times* clusters. *Pre-2010 Hollywood, modern art markets, philanthropic foundations, prestigious literary prizes.* All operate by mutual evaluation; all have rot signatures.

Contrast: fields where the judge is reality. *Trading.* P&L is the judge. There is no peer-review version of being right about the market. Bad traders disappear. Survivorship is a real signal. *Surgery.* Patient outcomes are the judge. A surgeon with a graveyard does not get more patients. *Plumbing, dentistry, firefighting, piloting.* The reality test cannot be faked. The expert is real because reality select for them. *Engineering.* The bridge stands or it collapses. The aircraft flies or it crashes. The reality feedback is direct. The fields with working reality feedback produce real experts; the fields without it produce credentialed performers.

The Lindy version of the argument. *Being reviewed or assessed by others matters if and only if one is subjected to the judgement by future — not just present — others.* Time is the only honest peer. A book that survives 100 years has been judged by 100 generations of readers across cultural shifts, political regimes, and intellectual fashions, all of whom could have rejected it and didn't. That is meaningful. A peer-reviewed paper from 2024 has been judged by three anonymous reviewers in the same incentive structure as the author. That is not. The two are not in the same epistemic category and treating them as if they were is the central error of the credentialed class.

Operational implications. *For your own work:* structure it so that the judge is reality, not peers. If you're a founder, build something users use, not something investors approve of. If you're a writer, write for readers in 50 years, not for editors in 5. If you're a researcher, work on questions whose answers can be tested by something other than peer applause. *For evaluating others' work:* distrust prizes awarded by the recipient's peer group. Distrust academic credentials in fields where the field grades itself. Trust *external* signals — does the work actually work? Is it used? Has it survived? *For institutional design:* introduce reality feedback into peer-judged systems. Pay academics for predictions that can be checked. Fire administrators for outcomes their administration produced. Tie compensation to what the work *did*, not to what colleagues thought of it.

The personal corollary, the autobiographical move. *An old fellow once came to me in the pit where I was trading and told me: "if people over here like you, you are doing something wrong."* Trade peer approval for reality approval, deliberately. If the people in your room all like what you say, you are saying something safe. Saying something true means at least some of them dislike it. The discomfort is the signal.

> *A free person does not need to win arguments — just win.*
>
> *If people over here like you, you are doing something wrong.*
>
> *Anything that smacks of a competition is a destruction of knowledge.*

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the field's grading mechanism.** Before evaluating any expert, prize, or credential, ask: what grades work in this field? If the answer is "other practitioners in the same field," apply heavy discount. If the answer is "reality outcomes that the practitioner cannot control or fake," trust the signal more.

2. **Distrust prizes from a recipient's peer group.** A James Beard award from other restaurateurs, an Academy Award from other filmmakers, a Pulitzer from other journalists, a Nobel in Economics from other economists. The signal is closer to "this person is well-positioned within the peer network" than to "this person produced exceptional work." Calibrate accordingly.

3. **Notice cluster behaviour as warning, not validation.** When a credentialed class converges quickly on the same view at the same time, that is *peer optimisation* — selection for what plays well at the next conference. Real questions in domains with reality feedback produce sustained disagreement. Rapid consensus is the rot signature.

4. **Structure your own work for time, not peers.** If you are writing, build for readers in 50 years, not editors in 5. If you are building product, build for users, not investors. If you are choosing problems, pick ones whose answers can be tested by something other than peer applause. The discipline is uncomfortable in the short run and compounds in the long.

5. **Apply the Lindy filter to evaluation.** A book that survived 100 years has passed a real test. A paper from this year has passed three anonymous reviewers in the same incentive structure as the author. The two are not the same kind of evidence and shouldn't be weighted as if they were.

6. **Trade peer approval for reality approval, deliberately.** When the people in your room all like what you say, you are saying something safe. The discomfort of disagreement is the signal that you are saying something that might actually be true. Make the trade explicitly.

## Examples

**Situation:** A board is hiring a Chief Economist for a financial services firm. Two candidates: one is a tenured macroeconomics professor with high-citation publications and a recent prize from the American Economic Association; the other is a former bond trader who has written one book the academic community ignored but who has predicted three major rate moves correctly with personal positions.
**Application:** Macroeconomics is a peer-rotted field — the judging mechanism is other macroeconomists, not reality outcomes. The professor's credentials are a measure of his standing within the peer network, not of his predictive accuracy. The trader's record is a measure of his predictive accuracy directly, with skin in the game on each call. The two are not comparable on the same axis. The Lindy version of the question — what would survive a hundred years of having to be right about real markets? — favours the trader heavily. The peer-validation version favours the professor. The two questions have opposite answers and the structural framework says trust the reality-tested record.
**Result:** The board that hires the trader gets a chief economist whose work informs trading decisions and whose calls can be measured. The board that hires the professor gets well-formatted memos that explain the macroeconomic backdrop and produce decks for client meetings. The first is closer to expertise; the second is closer to costume.

**Situation:** A founder is debating whether to spend a quarter writing a deeply considered essay that may take five years to find its readership, or to publish ten polished blog posts that will trend on social media this month.
**Application:** The blog posts are optimised for present peers — what other thoughtful people in the founder's network will share, comment on, and applaud. The essay is optimised for the future — readers fifty years out who will or will not encounter it. The Lindy framework says: anything written *for* present taste fails when present taste changes; anything written for taste that has already lasted decades will keep landing. The peer-vs-time argument extends: the blog posts will produce immediate validation that decays quickly; the essay produces nothing for years and then either is read forever or isn't. The trade-off is real, the math is asymmetric, and most people make the wrong choice.
**Result:** The founder who writes the essay has nothing to show for the quarter and produces an artifact that compounds. The founder who writes the blog posts has a dopamine-positive quarter and produces ten artifacts that disappear from the discourse within months.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply the framework to fields where peers genuinely have reality-grounded judgement.
**Why:** Surgeons evaluating other surgeons by surgical outcomes are doing reality-grounded peer review. Engineers evaluating other engineers by whether their structures stand are doing reality-grounded peer review. The framework targets the *structural* failure mode of self-grading isolated from reality, not all peer judgement. Make the diagnostic before applying the discount.

**Don't:** Use the framework as universal anti-credential rhetoric.
**Why:** Some credentials in some fields really do correlate with competence — those are the fields with working reality feedback. Reaching for "peers vs. time" against any credential degrades the framework into cynicism. The discipline is structural: identify the grading mechanism, weight accordingly, don't reflex-dismiss.

**Don't:** Refuse all current judgement on the grounds that "only time can judge."
**Why:** Sometimes you have to act now — hire someone, fund a project, publish a paper. Time hasn't acted yet on any of these. The right move is to *weight* time-tested signals more heavily where they exist and to use peer signals with appropriate scepticism where they don't, not to refuse all judgement until a hundred years pass. The framework is a calibration of trust, not a refusal of it.
