# Heightening Misalignment

## When to Use
- Writing a job description and feeling the urge to soften the demanding parts to attract more candidates.
- Designing an interview or onboarding process that reveals real fit, not performed fit.
- Stating values or operating rhythms publicly.
- Responding to cultural tension that's caused by false advertising — people joined thinking X and the reality was Y.
- You've had a run of hires who clearly weren't right, and you're starting to wonder whether you're hiding what the job actually is.

## Fails When
- Confused with illegal discrimination. "Heightening misalignment" applies to work style, values, and expectations — not to protected characteristics. Making it clear you demand long hours is fine. Making it clear you want a particular demographic is illegal.
- Used to brand dysfunction as intensity. "We move fast" shouldn't disguise "we're chaotic and constantly firefighting." Real expectations are fine; rebranding dysfunction as culture is not.
- Applied by advertising demands you don't actually have. Don't false-advertise in reverse — claiming to be more demanding than you are attracts intensity-seekers who get disappointed by the actual comfort level.
- Deployed to exclude valuable diversity of background. The goal is alignment on how the work happens, not homogeneity of perspective.

## Core Concept

Most companies try to be for everyone. They soften their rough edges in job descriptions. They avoid mentioning the demanding parts in interviews. They make the culture sound universally appealing. This creates years of mutual frustration when the wrong people join.

The better approach is the opposite: heighten misalignment. Make it extremely clear who *shouldn't* work at your company. Design for self-selection. A new hire should know within their first week — ideally within their first interview — whether they belong. If they don't, both parties are better off discovering it early.

There's this sort of belief that every company should be absolutely for everyone. That's not right. We want diversity *across* companies — different cultures, different operating rhythms, different expectations, different trade-offs. If every company becomes the same, we lose the most valuable kind of diversity: the ability to find a culture that actually fits you. Heightening misalignment is a service to the people you're *not* hiring as much as to the ones you are.

The specific mechanism: if you have demanding expectations, put them on a sign. Light it up. Make it impossible to miss. At Shopify, "thrive on change" is explicitly the most important cultural value. Not "embrace change" or "adapt to change" — *thrive* on it. If you can't handle constant change, you know immediately that Shopify isn't for you. That's not exclusion; it's honest matchmaking.

What false advertising does is the opposite. It creates resentment. Someone joins thinking work-life balance will be reasonable. It isn't. They resent the company. Colleagues resent their complaints. Everyone is frustrated. If you'd been honest upfront, they wouldn't have joined, and both parties would be better off. This is the "weirdest divisiveness" that comes from trying to be for everyone — the visible split between people who were told the truth and people who weren't.

A related point: your values should have opponents. If your company values are things everyone would agree with — "we value excellence" — they're not values, they're platitudes. Real values are choices with trade-offs. Someone should be able to read your values and think, "that's not for me." That's the point. If no one would take the other side, it's a platitude.

And if you reorg a lot — like we do — the people who remain are those who don't mind that. The churn itself becomes a selection mechanism. Self-selection is the most powerful filter you have, because the people who stay after you heighten misalignment are exactly the people you want: they knew what they were signing up for, and they chose it anyway. No one had to convince them. They were already aligned.

## How to Apply

1. **Write job descriptions with the rough edges intact.** Instead of generic "fast-paced environment," say what that actually means: "You'll receive 200+ Slack messages on an average day. We ship to production multiple times daily. Our highest performers work through ambiguity without waiting for permission. If you prefer clear direction and predictable schedules, you won't be happy here." Candidates self-select; the ones who come in know what they're getting.

2. **Design the first week to reveal the real job.** Don't ease people in. Put them on real work with real stakes immediately. Show the intensity, the ambiguity, the expectations. If a mismatch exists, better to surface it in week one than year one. Your onboarding is a revelation tool, not a marketing tool.

3. **State values with clear opposite sides.** If your values can't be disagreed with, they're platitudes. "We value craft over speed" is a real value — someone could reasonably disagree. "We value excellence" is a platitude. Force your values into positions that someone on the other side of the trade-off would reject.

4. **Heighten specific, demanding expectations publicly.** "We reorg every 6–9 months." "Our highest performers work 60-hour weeks during launch sprints." "We expect you to use AI reflexively for every task." Whatever the real expectation is, put it on a sign. If it's true, it can be stated. If you don't want to state it, check whether it's actually true.

5. **Use reorg and change as filters, not just as management tools.** If the company genuinely thrives on change, frequent change is both the mode of operation and the selection mechanism. The people who leave during change were always going to leave; the people who stay are aligned on operating style. Don't apologize for this — advertise it.

6. **Frame demands as filters, not judgments.** "We're not for everyone" doesn't mean other companies are worse. It means different people fit different environments, and the goal is for everyone to find where they belong. Heightening misalignment is a service to the candidates who self-select out — they avoid wasted years at the wrong place.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup is writing a careers page and is torn between honest descriptions of intensity and a softened version that might attract more applicants.
**Application:** Write the honest version. Make it obvious what the job is. "This is a company where everyone pushes hard, reorgs happen every six months, and you'll have to use AI reflexively for basically everything. If this sounds exciting, great. If it sounds exhausting, we're not the right fit." Fewer applicants, dramatically higher signal-to-noise. The people who apply know what they're applying to.
**Result:** Hiring gets faster, not slower. Onboarding becomes confirmation rather than reveal. Three-month retention improves because nobody is discovering the job isn't what they thought.

**Situation:** A reorg announcement lands. Some people are upset. The founder is tempted to soften the framing.
**Application:** Don't. "Thrive on change" is on the sign. The reorg is the thing the sign warned about. Make the announcement clear: "This is a change we think makes the company better. Here's why. If constant change isn't for you, I understand — but it's what this company is, and what it says on the box." Don't apologize; state and proceed.
**Result:** The people who leave under this are people who were always going to leave during the next change. The people who stay are aligned on the mode of operation. Future reorgs encounter less friction because the filter has been working.

**Situation:** A new hire two months in says they thought the company would be more relaxed. They saw the "fast-paced environment" line in the job description but assumed that meant something different.
**Application:** This is a symptom of insufficiently heightened misalignment. "Fast-paced" is vague. "We reorg every six months and you should expect 50+ hours a week during launch periods" is specific. Vague language is where false advertising hides.
**Result:** Update the description. The new hire might still leave — if so, that's the filter working late instead of early. But future candidates get the real picture and self-select accordingly.

**Situation:** A company's values read like every other company's: "excellence, integrity, teamwork, innovation." A candidate asks what's actually distinctive.
**Application:** Those aren't values — they're platitudes. No one would disagree with any of them. Real values would be things like "we favor craft over speed when they conflict" (someone could disagree), or "we prioritize mission over career — you won't be promoted for asking" (someone could disagree). The test is whether someone could take the other side. If not, it doesn't belong on the list.
**Result:** Rewrite the values to be actual choices. The next round of candidates reads something distinctive, and the people who are attracted are attracted to the real shape of the company, not to a generic aspirational fog.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** False-advertise in reverse.
**Why:** Claiming more intensity, more demands, or more pace than you actually have attracts people who want that, and then you disappoint them. Heighten *real* misalignment, not imagined. Honesty cuts both ways.

**Don't:** Use "we're intense" to launder chaos.
**Why:** Real demands are fine; dysfunction dressed up as culture is not. "We're fast-paced" should mean moves quickly with intention, not "we're constantly putting out preventable fires." If your culture is chaotic, own the chaos as a problem, not as a feature.

**Don't:** Confuse work-style alignment with demographic homogeneity.
**Why:** Heightening misalignment is about how the work happens — pace, autonomy, rhythm, expectations. It's not about who should do the work. Some of your best team members will have very different backgrounds from each other; what they share is alignment on how the work runs.

**Don't:** Exclude valid feedback by invoking "we're different."
**Why:** "We're not for everyone" isn't a cover for ignoring legitimate criticism. If you're getting the same specific complaint from smart people who should have fit, that's information — don't just write it off as "wrong fit."

**Don't:** State expectations you don't actually enforce.
**Why:** If your careers page says "you'll use AI reflexively" and then nothing in the actual work reinforces that, you've taught the new hire that stated expectations aren't real. Other stated expectations will get discounted too. Either enforce what you advertise or don't advertise it.
