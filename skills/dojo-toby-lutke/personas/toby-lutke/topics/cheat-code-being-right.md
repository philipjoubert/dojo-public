# The Cheat Code to Always Being Right

## When to Use
- You have new information that makes your previously stated position wrong, and you're reluctant to update because you said the old thing publicly.
- A team member is pushing back on a new direction because "we said the opposite three months ago."
- You're in a fast-moving domain (platform shift, new market, rapidly evolving product) where your beliefs should be updating often and you're worried that makes you look unserious.
- You're about to make a significant decision and feel the pull to defend your early prior.

## Fails When
- The "update" is actually an exit from a commitment you made to someone else. Broken promises are a different category from updated beliefs and have to be handled separately — don't launder one as the other.
- You're cycling positions without any new information — that's not updating, it's reactive uncertainty. The cheat code requires *new information*, not just a different mood.
- You tell one audience you've updated while privately holding the original view. That adds deception to error; it's worse than stubbornness.
- Applied to invariants: some things are robustly true across contexts (cash runs out, customers leave, technical debt compounds). Don't "update" out of those.

## Core Concept

There's a cheat code to always being right. It's embarrassingly simple: change your opinion every time you get better information.

Most people optimize for consistency. They stake out a position early and defend it — in meetings, in strategy docs, in their own heads. They'd rather be consistently wrong than publicly admit they changed their mind, because consistency *feels* like integrity. It's not. It's ego protection wearing a costume.

The alternative: hold your opinions loosely, update them continuously as new information arrives, and optimize for being correct at the moment of decision — not for having been correct earlier. If you go through the transcript of any long conversation I have, you will probably find various contradictions inside of what I'm saying about what you should do. Good. The reason is that embracing the contradictions, incorporating new context, is almost the most important thing.

The deeper frame: *consistency with truth* matters. *Consistency with your past statements* doesn't. When those two come apart — because the world changed, because data arrived, because you finally saw a level of the problem you hadn't seen before — you pick consistency with truth. Every time. And you do it visibly, not apologetically.

This is especially load-bearing during platform shifts. If you can reason about what is currently true and update as the ground moves, you have an advantage over leaders who are optimizing for consistency with positions they stated when the ground was different. That advantage compounds.

There is one hard-edged exception: commitments you made to people. If you told someone you would do something, that's a promise, not a belief, and beliefs-update doesn't cover it. Changes to commitments require a separate conversation — "here's what I promised, here's why the situation changed, here's what I propose instead" — which is respectful even when the direction reverses. Don't dress up a broken commitment as intellectual honesty. It's not the same thing.

## How to Apply

1. **State your reasoning visibly when you update.** "I thought X because of A and B. Since then, I've seen C, which changes things. Now I think Y." This makes the update legible, principled, and hard to weaponize against you. Random-looking flips erode trust; reasoned updates build it.

2. **Separate beliefs from commitments explicitly.** Beliefs update all the time. Commitments don't. When something sounds like a flip, check which category it's in. If you said "I believe X," X is a belief — update freely. If you said "I will do X by Friday," that's a commitment — if you want to exit, have the commitment conversation, not the belief conversation.

3. **Announce uncertainty when it's real.** "I think X, but I'm genuinely unsure and testing it" is a far better stance than pretending to confidence you don't have and then looking inconsistent when you update. Framing a belief as a hypothesis creates room to update without looking like you're flip-flopping.

4. **Seek the disconfirming evidence.** Ask what would prove you wrong. Actively look for it. Most people ask questions hoping for confirmation; the cheat code requires the opposite posture. If you can't find anything that would change your mind, either you're right or you've stopped paying attention. Both states warrant investigation.

5. **Keep updating right up to the moment of decision.** Don't close the loop early because certainty feels nice. The last piece of information before you commit might be the most important. Stay open. Tolerate the discomfort of unresolved uncertainty — it's cheaper than the discomfort of a decision made on stale data.

6. **Don't value consistency over correctness in your team either.** If one of your reports changes position on new evidence, that's a good sign, not a bad one. Reward it. The culture you want is one where "I was wrong about that and here's why I now think Y" is a normal sentence, not a damage-controlled one.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder told investors in January that Shopify would not build a headless commerce offering. It's now June and the market has clearly moved; headless is the way enterprise customers want to buy. He's avoiding the topic because he doesn't want to contradict what he said in January.
**Application:** The January statement was a belief under January information. June information changes the picture. The right move is to announce the update *with the reasoning*: "I said in January we wouldn't build headless because X. Since then, Y has happened, and that changes my view. We're now building it, here's why, here's what it will look like." Loud, explicit, reasoned. No apology, no scramble.
**Result:** Investors and team read it as exactly what it is — a leader updating on new information — not as indecision. The leader who refuses to update and keeps defending the January position is the one who looks weak, not the one who updates.

**Situation:** A team member won't stop relitigating a decision from last quarter. Your new direction contradicts what you said at Q1 kickoff.
**Application:** Name the update explicitly. "At Q1 I thought X. Here's what we've learned since then: [specific]. Given that, I now think Y. This isn't a flip — it's the update you'd expect from someone who's paying attention to what the last three months actually produced." If the team member can't let go, the conversation is no longer about the decision; it's about whether they'll accept that leaders update.
**Result:** For most reasonable people, clarifying that the update is reasoned and visible ends the debate. If it doesn't, you have a different problem — someone who prizes consistency over correctness, which is not a trait that ages well.

**Situation:** A founder says publicly that AI is overhyped. Six months later he's seen enough to realize he was wrong — it's a genuine platform shift.
**Application:** Don't soft-pedal the update. "I said six months ago that AI was overhyped. I was wrong. Here's what I missed: [specific]. Here's what that means for what we're doing now: [specific]." The visible admission and re-reasoning is what earns trust.
**Result:** The people whose respect you care about read the update as rigor. The ones who use it against you were going to use something against you anyway — their opinion wasn't load-bearing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "I changed my mind" to get out of a promise.
**Why:** Beliefs and commitments follow different rules. If you promised someone you'd do X, you owe them either X or an explicit conversation about X. Re-labeling the exit as "I updated my belief" corrodes trust faster than just making the original belief-based promise more carefully.

**Don't:** Change positions frequently without pointing at the new information.
**Why:** Updates should be traceable to specific evidence. If you're shifting every week with no legible cause, you're not updating — you're in reactive mode, which looks identical to flip-flopping and creates the same trust erosion. If the information is real, it's also nameable.

**Don't:** Publicly update while privately holding the old view.
**Why:** Performance updates — telling the room you've changed your mind while still operating as if you hadn't — are the worst of both worlds. Actions don't match stated beliefs, and eventually everyone notices. Be genuine or don't bother.

**Don't:** Confuse "I should be open to updating" with "every strong prior is wrong."
**Why:** Some beliefs are well-founded and shouldn't update on weak signals. The cheat code is responsiveness to *better* information, not surrender to any contrary data point. Be expensive to move; be infinitely movable.
