# StarCraft as CEO Training

## When to Use
- A leader is drowning in simultaneous initiatives and asking how to cope — the answer is almost never "more people."
- Someone is trying to orchestrate multiple AI agents at once and can feel the shape of the problem but can't name it.
- Competitive strategy conversations where the obvious framing is "match their headcount / match their spend." Wrong framing. The right framing is attention.
- Hiring or evaluating people who played competitive RTS, poker, or chess at a high level — there's a real signal there most recruiters miss.
- Diagnosing a leader whose Google Calendar is packed and whose business is stalled. Attention has been fragmented into uselessness.

## Fails When
- Used as an essentialist filter ("we only hire gamers"). The point is the cognitive pattern, not the credential. Many grandmasters of attention never touched a keyboard.
- Applied to work with perfect information and no real-time pressure. If the decision is a pure spreadsheet problem, the framework adds nothing.
- Used to glorify constant chaos. A StarCraft grandmaster is precise about where their attention goes — they are not context-switching randomly. "Every moment I'm scrambling" is the failure mode, not the ideal.
- Treated as a metaphor instead of a real skill transfer. The argument is literal: RTS trains a cognitive flexibility trait that business schools do not.

## Core Concept

I firmly believe I learned more about building businesses from playing StarCraft than I've learned from business books. This isn't nostalgia; it's a literal claim about skill transfer. StarCraft at a high level is a real-time, imperfect-information simulation of the role of a senior executive. Business school teaches the same decision matrix through business case analysis; StarCraft teaches it through thousands of live reps. Both give you the same lesson. Only one gives you an intuitive feel.

What is actually being trained:

- **Attention as the binding constraint.** In StarCraft, you have minerals and gas (money), build times (time), army positioning (risk), and your attention. The new player thinks the game is about resources. The intermediate player thinks it's about tactics. The grandmaster knows the game is about attention — theirs and the opponent's. This is also true of running a company, and almost no one is counting it.

- **Imperfect information is the default, not the exception.** StarCraft is a game of imperfect information. It's not chess. You don't see the moves. You see only some information, and to get more you have to invest — you take a worker off resource gathering and send it to scout the enemy. Scouting costs. Ignorance costs more. This is exactly what running a company is, and it's exactly what most MBAs are trained to pretend isn't the case.

- **There's no right decision, only context in which a decision turns out to be correct.** I say this constantly and I mean it literally: there is no absolute correct move in StarCraft, there is only a move that's correct given the map, the matchup, the opponent's playstyle, what you've seen, what you haven't seen. The same is true in business. The cargo-culting of "best practices" from Amazon or Shopify or anyone else fails because the move was never the thing — the context was.

- **Macro vs. micro, and knowing which one the current moment demands.** Macro is running your economy: how many workers, which buildings, which tech, which upgrades. Micro is the specific battle in front of you: controlling individual units, pulling a damaged soldier back, kiting a faster enemy. Great players move between these levels constantly. Run macro while nothing is burning; zoom into micro when the fight is live; zoom back out before the macro degrades. A CEO has the exact same problem. The muddy middle (see top-and-bottom.md) is where most founders waste their attention because they never learned to move fluidly between macro and micro.

- **Attacking the opponent's attention is more profitable than attacking their resources.** This is the deepest lesson. In StarCraft, if you can fragment your opponent's attention — early harass, a drop in their base while they're trying to attack, a feint that pulls them off-map — they will start making mistakes even with a superior economy. Competitive strategy against a larger business player works the same way. Don't try to out-spend the incumbent; force them to manage three simultaneous fronts their size makes hard to coordinate.

- **APM isn't the point; meaningful APM is.** Actions Per Minute — how fast you issue commands — doesn't matter in the abstract. A grandmaster with 300 APM beats a bronze player with 300 APM because the grandmaster's actions are load-bearing. This is the same trap as "we're very busy" in companies. Busyness is not throughput. The question is whether the actions you're taking advance the game state, not whether you're taking many of them.

- **Easy to learn, hard to master — and honest.** StarCraft has one key property: both players start with identical potential on a mirrored map. One person wins at the end. There's no excuse hidden in the rules. That feedback loop is brutal and it's what makes it a real teacher. Most business advice fails this test — results lag cause by years, counterfactuals are invisible, and blame is diffuse. RTS gives you the unforgiving feedback that most work environments don't.

The claim connects to something else I've said recently: managing AI agents feels exactly like StarCraft. I look at my computer and I have six different agents going, all coordinating between them — they send emails to each other, which I think is hilarious — and my attention moves the same way. This one is working on a module I need; I'm zooming in, paying attention, doing a little micro, giving a little extra. Then I zoom back out to the macro view of what all six are doing. The bottleneck isn't compute or model cost. It's the conductor's attention. Which means the skills trained by two decades of RTS are, unexpectedly, the skills of the next decade of work. Strange skill transfer. Real edge.

And a humbler version for people who don't play: the intuitions can be built other ways — poker, chess, bridge, any game that compresses many strategic decisions into a short window with real feedback. Parents get upset when I post in favor of StarCraft. Would they be upset if their kid was playing chess all the time? What's the actual difference?

## How to Apply

1. **Name the binding constraint before moving.** Before any "we should launch another initiative / hire another team / open another geography," ask: what does this do to our attention? Money and time are visible; attention is almost always the real limit. If it isn't, fine — but name it.

2. **Design your calendar as a macro-micro alternation, not a grid of 30-minute blocks.** Grandmaster RTS play is not uniform APM. It's waves of focused micro (the fight you're in) interspersed with macro sweeps (economy, tech, scouting). Your calendar should look similar — hot zones of deep work, cold zones of broad sweeping attention, not an even peanut-butter of 30-minute meetings.

3. **Invest in scouting.** Every week, spend some attention on information you didn't ask for — the project nobody brought to your 1:1, the team two levels down from you, the metric nobody has looked at in a quarter. Taking a worker off economy to scout costs something. Not scouting costs more.

4. **Fragment the competitor's attention, don't match their resources.** If you're fighting a 20x-bigger company head-on, you lose. Make them defend in three places at once and watch their coordination crack. This is rarely the advice they get from their board.

5. **Rotate your zoom level deliberately.** When you feel the urge to micro-manage a specific thing, check: is this actually the moment the fight needs me, or am I zooming in because it's comfortable? Conversely, when you're doing high-level strategy: is the macro actually stable, or is your economy crashing while you're drawing 2026 plans?

6. **Reward people who surface information without being asked.** In an RTS, the scouting worker rarely kills anything — it just tells you what the opponent is doing. In a company, the people who surface uncomfortable truths are the scouts. Most companies reward the people who show up with wins and quietly demote the ones who show up with inconvenient facts. Backward.

7. **Hire for the cognitive pattern, not the credential.** Kyung Ryoo was a pro StarCraft player who had retired to get a software engineering degree and was looking for a summer internship. I told him his StarCraft accomplishments were enough of a CV. Not because he was a gamer — because becoming a StarCraft pro is significantly harder than getting a degree, and the skills are exactly the ones I want in the company. If you see a competitive RTS, chess, or poker background, that's signal. Don't round it down to "hobbies."

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is running six AI agents simultaneously for research, coding, writing, and testing. They feel productive but also constantly on the edge of losing track of what each is doing.
**Application:** This is an attention management problem, not a compute problem. Build the habit: macro sweep across all six every 10–15 minutes (what's each doing, who's blocked, who's off-task), then zoom in for focused micro on whichever one has the highest-value decision pending. Have a critic process running in parallel to flag drift — this is exactly what pro StarCraft players do with their minimap. The conductor's attention is the scarce resource, not the models.
**Result:** Six agents become net additive rather than chaotic. The founder's throughput increases because they've matched the orchestration shape to a pattern they already had decades of training on.

**Situation:** A 100-person startup is losing to a 2000-person incumbent. The board suggests hiring more salespeople to match the incumbent's outbound capacity.
**Application:** That's trying to out-resource an opponent who has a 20x resource advantage. Losing move. Flip the frame: where does the incumbent have attention bandwidth, and where don't they? Launch in three adjacent product areas simultaneously at a cadence their size can't match. Their size becomes a liability; your focus becomes the asymmetric advantage. This is a harass-and-fragment play, not a head-on attack.
**Result:** The smaller company forces the larger one to respond across fronts it didn't expect, its VPs scramble to coordinate, decisions get escalated, mistakes multiply. Small company wins disproportionately because the battle was fought on attention, not on resources.

**Situation:** A CEO's calendar is completely booked, week after week, with 30-minute meetings. Output is dropping. The team has suggested hiring a Chief of Staff.
**Application:** That's a classic overloaded-macro failure. A Chief of Staff doesn't restore attention; it adds a coordination layer. Instead, redesign the calendar around macro-micro alternation. Block multi-hour windows for deep work on the one or two things only this CEO can do. Block sweeping windows for scouting the work nobody brought to them. Compress the rest of meetings into hot zones of 2–3 hours where context is shared and decisions made, not scattered across the day. Same number of decisions; dramatically better attention state.
**Result:** CEO's decision quality recovers. Team notices they're more present when present. The problem was never capacity — it was attention fragmentation disguised as activity.

**Situation:** A hiring manager has two finalist candidates for a senior engineering role. Candidate A has a conventional top-tier CV. Candidate B retired from pro StarCraft at 30, went to get a software engineering degree, and is now a strong but less-pedigreed engineer.
**Application:** Becoming a StarCraft pro is significantly harder than a standard pedigree — it requires years of deliberate practice under real-time pressure with perfect feedback. That's highly valuable in an engineering role where iteration speed, pattern recognition under incomplete information, and composure under load matter. I'd weight the RTS career as an unusually strong signal, maybe more than a degree. (I did this with Kyung Ryoo and I'd do it again.)
**Result:** Hire B. The signal was noisy in the market because no one scores it, which is exactly why it's valuable — anyone who scores it correctly captures edge others are missing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat APM as the metric.
**Why:** High APM without intent is flailing. The grandmaster's APM is all load-bearing; the beginner's APM is spam. The same is true of calendars, Slack messages, and initiative counts. Action quantity isn't output. Meaningful action count is.

**Don't:** Ignore scouting because it feels like "not making progress."
**Why:** Scouting looks unproductive because the worker isn't collecting resources. But the information the scout brings back changes every subsequent decision. In a company, the leader who only does "work that ships" and never pays attention to the margins is playing with their eyes closed.

**Don't:** Try to win on resources when attention is the actual battlefield.
**Why:** Bigger competitors have more resources by definition. If that's the axis, you've already lost. The entire point of the framework is to surface the axis nobody else is contesting — attention — and contest it aggressively. Smaller players can win attention battles they could never win resource battles.

**Don't:** Glorify chaos.
**Why:** A StarCraft grandmaster is not context-switching randomly. They are allocating attention with extreme precision. If your life looks like constant scrambling, you've confused the failure mode with the skill. The skill is deliberate zoom control. Scrambling is the opposite.

**Don't:** Hire only people who played StarCraft.
**Why:** This is a reasoning-style argument, not a recruiting filter. Plenty of people develop the same cognitive flexibility from poker, chess, bridge, climbing, trading, music performance, surgery, or anything else with real-time feedback and partial information. What you're hiring for is the pattern, not the credential.

**Don't:** Use this framing in an annual strategic planning document.
**Why:** If you turn this into corporate slide-ware, you've killed it. The framework lives in the in-the-moment allocation of attention, not in quarterly planning artifacts. It's a way of operating, not a way of writing decks.
