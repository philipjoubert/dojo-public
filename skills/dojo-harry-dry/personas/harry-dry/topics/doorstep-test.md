# The Doorstep Test

## When to Use
- Any copy meant to feel human — homepages, ads, emails, social posts, CTAs.
- Fixing tone when the positioning and substance are right but the copy feels stiff.
- Running a sanity check on every line before shipping: would I say this out loud to a real person?

## Fails When
- **Regulated or prestige copy.** Some contexts mandate a register beyond conversational — financial disclosures, medical claims, some luxury brands. The test still applies inside those constraints, but the baseline is different.
- **You mistake casual for good.** A doorstep-passing line can still fail the three tests. "Yeah, hi, we sell stuff, it's cool" sounds human and says nothing. Conversational ≠ unsubstantive.
- **The doorstep test contradicts the brand voice deliberately.** Some brands cultivate a specific non-conversational register (Apple's minimalism, Patek Philippe's gravitas). The test becomes a genre choice, not a universal.

## Core Concept

Claude Hopkins wrote this in 1926. Picture yourself with the product in your hand. You're walking up to someone's front door. You knock. They open. What are you going to say?

You're not going to say "Seamless transition to a better way." You're not going to say "Unleash the power of innovation." You're not going to say "World-class solutions for tomorrow's challenges." They'd close the door. You're going to say something real. Something that sounds like one person talking to another person.

**The salesperson simulation.** Every time you write copy, run it through the simulation. Picture yourself standing at a door. Product in hand. Person on the other side. Read your copy out loud. Would you actually say this? To a real human being? Face to face? If the answer is no, the copy is wrong.

Go look at your landing page right now. Read the headline out loud. Imagine saying it to someone who just opened their door. "Empower your team with cutting-edge collaboration tools." Nobody says that. If a salesperson opened with that line, you'd assume they were having a stroke. But we write these words all the time. Because writing feels different from speaking. We think we need to sound "professional." Rubbish.

**Starlink passes the test.** "Point at the sky. Plug it in. Directions work in either order." Starlink's satellite internet setup. Read it out loud. Imagine someone just asked you how to install it. That's exactly what you'd say. Point at the sky, plug it in. Done. Now imagine if they'd written corporate copy: "Experience seamless connectivity with our revolutionary satellite internet installation process. Our cutting-edge technology enables rapid deployment with minimal technical expertise required." Same product. Same information. Completely different feeling. And that last bit — "Directions work in either order" — a tiny joke. A human moment. Proof that a real person wrote this. Nobody at a door would say "Our installation process is user-agnostic and direction-flexible." A real person might smile and say "Directions work in either order."

**It's not copy, it's natter.** Ian David wrote a line for Tesco: "Soap. 24p. See, the more we sell the less we charge. You scratch our back. We gently exfoliate yours." Read that out loud. It sounds like a mate telling you about a deal. Casual. Warm. A little cheeky. That's natter. That's how people actually talk.

Nebraska's tourism campaign. Voted the least popular tourist state in America. Instead of hiding from it, they leaned in: "Lucky for you, there's nothing to do here." Beside a photo of people floating down a river in livestock tanks. A thing Nebraskans actually do for fun. That's a doorstep line. Honest. Human. Impossible to forget.

**Eight rules for conversational copy.**
- Load up on personal pronouns. "You" and "your" make people lean in.
- Use contractions. "You're" not "you are." Only academics write "you are."
- Start sentences with conjunctions. And, but, so. It flows better.
- Use parentheses for personality. Like asides in a conversation. (The kind of thing you'd add if you were chatting.)
- Don't worry about grammar. If breaking the rules makes you sound human, break them.
- Ditch the thesaurus. You're not impressing anyone.
- Use your customers' words. Easiest way to get the tone right.
- Read it aloud at the kitchen table. If your partner cringes, rewrite it.

**The Troy McClure test.** You know Troy McClure from The Simpsons. The washed-up actor who narrates every cheesy infomercial. "Hi, I'm Troy McClure. You may remember me from such self-help videos as 'Smoke Yourself Thin.'" Read your copy in his voice. If it works — if it sounds perfectly natural coming out of Troy McClure's mouth — it's too phony. "We're passionate about delivering innovative solutions that empower your team." That's a Troy McClure line. "We answer support tickets in 4 hours." Troy McClure can't say that. It's too real. Too specific. Too human. If Troy can sell it, rewrite it.

**Beyond words.** The doorstep test isn't just about words. It's about what you'd actually do in a real conversation. Sometimes the answer is: don't talk at all. Just show them. The encyclopedia salesperson didn't read from a script about "comprehensive knowledge solutions." They flipped to the dinosaur section. Pointed at something cool. The vacuum salesperson didn't lecture about "superior suction technology." They poured dirt on your carpet and cleaned it up. People don't buy descriptions. They buy demonstrations. That's what a salesperson would do. That's what a human would do. (See show-dont-tell.md.)

## How to Apply

1. **Read every sentence out loud.** Where do you stumble? Where do you cringe? Rewrite those.

2. **Imagine the doorstep.** You. Product. A real person opening the door. What would you actually say first? That's your headline.

3. **Run the Troy McClure test on anything that feels "professional."** If Troy could sell it, bin it. "Professional-sounding" is usually the tell that you've drifted from human.

4. **Contractions everywhere.** "You're" not "you are." "We've" not "we have." "Don't" not "do not." Non-contracted writing reads as formal/artificial.

5. **Start sentences with conjunctions where the flow demands.** "And we built this because..." "But here's the thing..." Flow matters more than a rule you read in primary school.

6. **Swap in customer words.** Go to your CRM, support tickets, reviews, community channels. Find the exact phrases customers use. Drop them into your copy. Customer voice is doorstep voice.

7. **If the conversation would include a demonstration, include one.** "Point at the sky. Plug it in" is a doorstep instruction, not a tagline. When the salesperson would point at something, make sure your copy shows what they'd point at (see show-dont-tell.md).

8. **Convert corporate to conversational in three steps.**
   - Ask: what's the actual problem? Answer in plain words.
   - Ask: what's the actual benefit? Answer as a person would say it.
   - Strip the abstraction. Say what a human would say.

## Examples

**Situation:** A B2B homepage: "We provide integrated workflow solutions that empower teams to achieve operational excellence."
**Application:** Troy McClure test — he'd sell this in his sleep. Doorstep test — you'd have the door closed before you finished. What's the actual problem? Teams waste time doing the same work twice. What's the actual benefit? You stop doing the same work twice. New copy: "Stop doing the same work twice."
**Result:** Six abstract words became six concrete ones. The same promise, finally sayable on a doorstep.

**Situation:** A marketing platform's email: "Our platform enables seamless data synchronisation across your entire technology ecosystem."
**Application:** What's the actual benefit? You never copy-paste between tools. New copy: "Never copy-paste between apps again."
**Result:** The corporate sentence is replaced with something you could say to a friend describing the product. The promise is identical; the register is human.

**Situation:** A luxury tourism brand writes: "Experience the authentic heartland of America."
**Application:** Apply doorstep test. Would you say this to someone at the door? No — sounds like a brochure. Nebraska did the opposite move: "Lucky for you, there's nothing to do here." Self-deprecating. Honest. Human. A brand that admits its thing is quiet is a brand you'd trust more than one that oversells.
**Result:** Honesty can't be clichéd. The doorstep test often unlocks self-deprecation as a tool that "professional" copy forbids.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write for a reader who doesn't exist.
**Why:** "Our discerning customers expect world-class experiences" is written for a phantom corporate reader who would nod approvingly. No real customer, at a door or elsewhere, says "yes, I am discerning." Write for the customer you could actually talk to.

**Don't:** Confuse casual with sloppy.
**Why:** "Yeah, just grab the thing, it's pretty cool I guess" sounds casual. It's also doing no work. The doorstep test isn't "write like a teenager." It's "write like a real person making a real pitch with real information." Real salespeople are focused and specific — not rambling.

**Don't:** Strip every formal word and call it doorstep.
**Why:** Some technical words are the right words. A lawyer doesn't say "the thing" when "the contract" is the accurate word. The test isn't "avoid all specialist vocabulary"; it's "would a human expert naturally use this word with this customer?" If yes, it's doorstep. If no, rewrite.

**Don't:** Apply doorstep test to regulated copy without checking requirements.
**Why:** Financial and medical copy sometimes mandates specific language ("may result in…"). Doorstep test within the legal envelope, not against it.

**Don't:** Let committee sand down the doorstep voice.
**Why:** Every round of corporate review tends to push copy back toward Troy McClure. Watch for "could we soften 'stop doing the same work twice' to 'streamline your workflow'?" The committee voice is the opposite of doorstep voice. Defend the door.
