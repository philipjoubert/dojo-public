# The Iceberg

## When to Use
- Any time a founder or team asks for help with "the words." Before writing a single one, check whether the 90% below the waterline is sorted.
- When the copy sounds like every competitor's copy. That isn't a copywriting problem — the thinking is generic, so the output is generic.
- When a team has spent weeks rewriting the homepage and landing on wallpaper like "simple, easy, reliable." The iceberg is empty; the words can't save it.
- When someone asks "what would make our tagline better?" — the answer is almost always "go fix the positioning first."

## Fails When
- **You use it to stall.** "Go do the positioning first" is a real instruction, not an excuse to avoid writing. For someone with real positioning already, the iceberg diagnostic is a false flag — they need craft, not philosophy.
- **You treat the iceberg questions as a brainstorming exercise.** Answers have to be concrete, single-sentence, and survive the three tests. Answers like "we empower teams" or "we're a global leader" mean the work isn't done, not that you have answers.
- **You try to do it by committee in a single meeting.** Positioning is knowledge that's unevenly distributed — product, sales, support, founder all hold pieces. A single brainstorm won't surface it. Talk to customers first.

## Core Concept

Think of an iceberg. Words are the tip. The 10% you can see. Positioning is the 90% below the waterline.

Most companies spend all their time polishing the tip. Rewriting headlines. Tweaking taglines. Agonising over whether to say "simple" or "easy." Meanwhile the 90% below is a mess. Or worse, it's empty.

Every great ad has questions answered long before anyone touched a keyboard. What are you competing against? Why you? Who's it for? What are you NOT? Why did you start this in the first place? What's your point of view? Why do you exist? These sound basic. Most companies can't answer them.

Ask a founder "why you?" and watch what happens. "We're a global leader in seamless solutions." "We deliver results." "We empower teams." That's not an answer. That's evasion. That's the tip of an iceberg with nothing underneath.

Robly's homepage: "Email marketing for everybody." You could give that brief to the best writer in the world and they couldn't do anything with it. Why? Because "email marketing for everybody" is also Mailchimp. And Constant Contact. And 365 other companies. There's nothing underneath. No position. No enemy. No angle. No specific person they're talking to.

Now compare that to HEY or Loom or Hinge — they're all in crowded categories with fifty competitors. Completely different messages because they answered the iceberg questions first.

The words aren't the problem. The thinking is.

The lesson of the iceberg isn't "do more brainstorming." It's an order of operations. Copywriting comes last, not first. Most people think copywriting is sit-down-and-write-a-catchy-headline. Wrong. Answer the iceberg questions. Find your enemy. Know exactly who you're talking to. Then write. When you have something to say, the words come. When you don't, you fill the page with "seamless solutions" and "next-level results."

And here's the bit people resist: abstract copy gets signed off in meetings because nobody can question it. "Innovative solutions" doesn't offend. "We save restaurants 14 hours a week on scheduling" might. The preference for abstract isn't laziness, it's fear. Concrete claims can be proved wrong. Abstract ones can't. But unprovable claims are also invisible claims. Safety and invisibility are the same thing here.

## How to Apply

1. **Before you write anything, put the person in front of the iceberg questions on paper.** Not in their head. On paper. The questions to answer in single concrete sentences:
   - What are you competing against? (Not who. What. What would your customer do if you didn't exist — the status quo, a spreadsheet, a competitor, nothing?)
   - Why you? (What's different? "Better" isn't an answer. Better at what? For whom?)
   - Who's it for? (Specifically. "Freelance designers who invoice monthly" beats "small businesses.")
   - What are you NOT? (Often more revealing than what you are.)
   - Why did you start this? (What frustrated you enough to build something?)
   - What's your enemy? (Competitor, approach, or belief — see find-your-enemy.md.)
   - What's your point of view on the market? (Teach me something I didn't know before you pitch me.)

2. **If the answers sound like every other company in the space, stop.** Don't proceed to copywriting. Any cleverness you add now is wallpaper. Go back to customers. Interview the five most recent buyers — the ones who just bought. Ask them what would have happened if you didn't exist. Ask them what they almost bought instead. Their answers are your positioning, in their words.

3. **Narrow until you're genuinely number one at something specific.** Robly's "email marketing for everybody" isn't positioning — it's Mailchimp with a worse brand. Narrow to "email marketing for restaurants that integrates with their booking system." Now you're not fighting Mailchimp for the whole market, you own one corner. This feels scary because you're excluding people. You're not — you're focusing the message. Broad = nobody's obvious choice. Narrow = somebody's only choice.

4. **Do the left-right exercise.** Draw a line down the middle of a page. Left side: how everyone else does it. Right side: how you do it. If your right side looks like everyone else's left side, you haven't positioned — you've joined the category. Loom's left side was "screen recording made easy." Their right side was "meetings are too long, emails are too impersonal." They zoomed out from their product category to a completely different enemy.

5. **Run the logo-swap test before you write a word of copy.** Write a homepage draft. Cover the logo. Drop in a competitor's. Does the page still make sense? If yes, you've described a category, not a position. Durham, Plymouth, and Keele universities all wrote "Equip you with essential skills to excel" — word for word — because none of them had done the iceberg work. When the positioning is right, the logo swap breaks.

6. **Check for "and."** Every "and" in your positioning is a decision you haven't made. "Project management and team communication and file sharing" isn't a position. "Online whiteboard for teams to build and brainstorm" is (the "and" there is inside the thing, not between things). One strong claim beats three weak ones.

7. **Only then write the copy.** Once the iceberg questions are answered, once the enemy is named, once you've narrowed, once the logo swap breaks, the words come easily. Pepper wrote "Bras for small boobs. Finally!" — you don't need clever wordplay when the positioning does the heavy lifting. Hinge wrote "The dating app designed to be deleted" — six words because the position was six-words-clear. The copy wrote itself.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder with accounting software spent three days rewriting the headline. "Simple accounting." "Accounting made easy." "Effortless bookkeeping." Everything sounded generic.
**Application:** Harry asked one question — "who actually loves this product?" Answer: freelance photographers. They have weird invoicing needs, multiple shoots, deposits, final payments. "Why didn't you lead with that?" "We want to sell to everyone." That's the tell. We rewrote in five minutes: "Accounting for freelance photographers. Finally, invoicing that makes sense."
**Result:** The positioning enabled the copy. Not the other way around. The iceberg was empty for three days because the founder was trying to own "accounting software." The moment he picked a wedge — freelance photographers — the tagline arrived in a sentence.

**Situation:** A swim school homepage reads "Learn to swim with qualified instructors." Generic. Any competitor could sign it.
**Application:** Ask the iceberg questions. What are they competing against? Big swim schools with 30 kids per pool. Why them? They run three kids per class. Who's it for? Parents who are frustrated their child gets five minutes of real instruction at the big schools. What's the enemy? The big swim schools, implicitly. New positioning: "Three kids per class, maximum." Now the headline is "The swim school where your child gets real attention."
**Result:** A line a competitor cannot sign — because the competitor's business model depends on high class sizes. The iceberg made the line specific, falsifiable, and unique.

**Situation:** A founder asks for help making their homepage copy "punchier" because it feels flat.
**Application:** Refuse the premise. "Punchy" is a property of having something to say, not of sentences. Walk them through the iceberg questions. Nine times out of ten, they can't answer "why us?" in one concrete sentence, which means the copy is flat because the thinking is flat. Send them back to customer interviews. Come back in a week with real answers. Then rewrite.
**Result:** The "copywriting problem" was a positioning problem in disguise. Harry never rewrote the words — he sent them back to fix the iceberg, and the new words came from the founder, not Harry.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the iceberg questions as a workshop output to be "approved" by a committee.
**Why:** Positioning is specific and narrow. Committees reach for consensus, which is always generic. Five stakeholders in a room will land on "empowering teams to do their best work" because everyone can sign off on that. Real positioning makes choices; committees avoid them. Use the questions as interview prompts with customers, not as a brainstorming exercise with internal stakeholders.

**Don't:** Answer the iceberg questions in marketing language.
**Why:** "What are you against?" is not answered by "legacy enterprise solutions." It's answered by "spreadsheets on a shared drive that nobody updates" or "the £60 Deliveroo habit." The answers have to be concrete enough to drop on your foot. If the answer is abstract, the copy will be too.

**Don't:** Use iceberg work as an excuse to postpone shipping.
**Why:** For most young products the positioning is loose by necessity — you don't know who your best-fit customer is yet. That's fine. Write a thesis, ship, learn, tighten. Don't use "we haven't done the iceberg work yet" as a reason to avoid a landing page. Ship a rough one informed by what you do know and revise as customers teach you.

**Don't:** Try to write "great copy on top of bad positioning."
**Why:** It doesn't exist. Watch founders spend weeks going back and forth between "simple" and "easy" and "effortless" — none of it matters if the positioning is wrong. The best copy in history with the wrong positioning is invisible. Mediocre copy on top of sharp positioning converts. Fix the 90% first.

**Don't:** Confuse "we have no differentiation" with "we have no articulated differentiation."
**Why:** Teams that insist they have no differentiation usually do — they just haven't pulled it out of their happy customers yet. Like Dunford's "no differentiation illusion": the differentiation is in-market, driving revenue, but scattered across the team. Before accepting "we're a commodity," talk to the customers who bought and ask why they picked you. Nine times out of ten, the answer is specific and defensible.
