# Find Your Enemy

## When to Use
- Positioning a product in a crowded category where everyone sounds the same. You can't out-feature your way out — you need conflict.
- Writing a landing page or ad where the whole thing is "we do X" and nothing pushes back. The copy is flat because there's nothing to push against.
- Repositioning when the current message is defensive — "we're a good option too" instead of "here's what we're fighting."
- Sponge-cloth products with nothing obvious to say. A sponge cloth doesn't have much to say. Until you pick a fight with paper towels.

## Fails When
- **The enemy is too weak to be a real enemy.** "We're against bad customer service" isn't an enemy. Everyone is against bad customer service. That's a platitude dressed as a position.
- **The enemy is too big or too sympathetic.** Attacking Apple directly as a one-person startup looks desperate. Attacking a genuinely beloved brand makes you the villain.
- **The enemy alienates more customers than it attracts.** Picking an enemy means picking sides. Make sure there are enough heretics on your side to build a business on. If the "wrong belief" you're attacking is held by 99% of your market, you're shouting into the void.

## Core Concept

Without an enemy, you're listing features. With an enemy, you're telling a story. Stories have conflict. Conflict has two sides. Your marketing needs two sides.

HEY isn't just email. It's a protest against what email became. Hinge isn't just dating. It's a rejection of apps that profit from your loneliness. Greenlight Bookstore isn't just a bookshop. It's a stand against corporate empire-building. The enemy gives your copy force. Direction. Urgency. It answers "why should I care?" before the customer has to ask.

Three types of enemy. ABC.

**A — Approaches.** A different way of solving the same problem. Not a company. An entire approach. HEY didn't attack Gmail by name — they attacked email as it exists. Spam everywhere. Companies tracking you. Newsletters you never signed up for. Read receipts spying on you. "Email deserves a dust-off. Email has been stolen from you and we're stealing it back." They spent half their marketing attacking the status quo. Not a company. A broken system. When you attack an approach, you make enemies of behaviours. More people are frustrated by those behaviours than are customers of any single competitor.

**B — Beliefs.** Hardest to pick. Most defensible. Billie makes razors. The entire razor industry believed men and women should use the same product. Pink it up. Charge more. Call it "for women." Billie said no. "Women and men shave differently. So why are our razors the same?" That's not attacking a company. It's attacking a lie the whole industry told itself. Once you name the wrong belief, you attract everyone who secretly agreed but never heard anyone say it out loud. They become evangelists. The risk? You can't market to everyone. Make sure there are enough heretics to build a business on.

**C — Competitors.** A specific rival. Apple vs PC. Simple and direct. DuckDuckGo: "Google tracks you. We don't." Two sentences. Complete positioning. You know exactly what they're selling and why. But direct competitor attacks are risky. You give them free publicity. If they're much bigger, you look desperate. DuckDuckGo pulled it off because the claim was concrete and true. Most of the time, approaches or beliefs are better enemies than specific companies.

Sometimes the enemy doesn't need to be named to be felt. Greenlight Bookstore in Brooklyn: "Buy books from people who want to sell books, not colonise the moon." They never say Amazon. Don't need to. "Colonise the moon" does the work. It turns a purchase into an identity statement. You're not just buying a book. You're choosing what kind of world you want.

Smalls (cat food) describes Big Pet Food: "Think pink sludge getting extruded at extremely high temperatures." Then the contrast: "Smalls is cooked gently, like it would be in your own kitchen." Name the enemy. Nail the enemy. Draw the contrast. The enemy did the selling. You don't need a feature list after that.

## How to Apply

1. **Answer three questions on paper.**
   - What frustrates your customers right now? Talk to them. What do they complain about? "Why does this software have so many features I never use?" "Why is every meeting 30 minutes when it could be two?" Each complaint points to a potential enemy.
   - What do your competitors all have in common that you reject? Go to five competitor homepages. What do they all say? Now ask what they all get wrong. That gap is your opening.
   - What belief does your industry hold that you think is wrong? Every industry has received wisdom. What part of yours do you disagree with?

2. **Write it down. One sentence. "We're against ___."** If you can't finish that sentence, your positioning is too vague. Go back to the iceberg questions.

3. **Pick A, B, or C.** Approaches are the broadest and often the strongest — they make enemies of behaviours rather than of competitors. Beliefs are the most defensible because nobody else in the industry dares voice them. Competitors are simplest but riskiest. Default to A or B unless you have a specific, falsifiable, concrete difference against a named competitor (like DuckDuckGo).

4. **Run the Smalls structure: Name → Nail → Contrast.**
   - Name the enemy. Make it visible.
   - Nail it. Make it visceral. "Pink sludge getting extruded" beats "lower-quality pet food."
   - Draw the contrast. Short. Calm. One sentence is enough. "Cooked gently, like it would be in your own kitchen." You don't need a feature list after that.

5. **Check that the enemy can't attack itself.** Your enemy makes your copy unsignable. Because your enemy can't attack themselves. Gmail can't write HEY's copy. Tinder can't write Hinge's copy. That's the test.

6. **Make sure enough heretics exist.** If you're picking a belief, count the people who secretly agree with you. If it's 2% of the market and you need 20% to run a business, the belief is too niche. If it's 30% of the market, you've got something. Marmite knows half its market hates it; the half that loves it is a real business.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder selling a minimalist task app against a field of maximalist productivity tools (Notion, ClickUp, Asana, Jira, Monday).
**Application:** Pick A — Approach. The enemy is "productivity tools that become second jobs." Landing page opens with: "Most productivity tools make you more productive at managing your productivity tools." That's the nail. Then the contrast: "Three buttons. No tags. No projects. Just what you're doing today." Now the copy has force.
**Result:** Without the enemy, the app is "yet another task tool." With the enemy, it's the calm voice saying what the frustrated customer has been thinking.

**Situation:** A cleaning product wanting to sell against the big brands. Can't afford to attack anyone specific.
**Application:** Pick B — Belief. The belief the industry holds: that household cleaning needs ten different bottles for ten different surfaces. Attack the belief, not the brands. "One bottle. Everything. Stop running a chemistry lab under your sink."
**Result:** A line no incumbent can sign, because every incumbent sells ten bottles. Once the belief is named, the customer spots the absurdity they'd normalised.

**Situation:** Loom (screen recorder) facing fifty other screen recorders all claiming "record your screen in seconds."
**Application:** Refuse the whole category. The enemy isn't other screen recorders. It's long meetings and impersonal emails. "Meetings are too long. Emails are too impersonal." Same product category. Completely different game. Loom isn't competing with screen recorders — they're competing with meetings and emails.
**Result:** Zoom out from product category to customer problem. The entire competitive frame changes. Loom becomes the obvious alternative to something every customer already hates.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick a weak enemy to avoid picking a real one.
**Why:** "We're against bad experiences" isn't an enemy. Nobody defends bad experiences. An enemy has to be real, specific, and controversial enough that some people disagree. If nobody would disagree with your enemy, you haven't picked one — you've picked a platitude.

**Don't:** Attack by naming a specific huge competitor without a concrete, falsifiable difference.
**Why:** "We're better than Salesforce" without proof looks like Dunning-Kruger. DuckDuckGo can write "Google tracks you. We don't" because the claim is verifiable. "We're better than Google" isn't the same thing. If you can't name a specific, provable, differentiated gap, attack the approach or the belief instead.

**Don't:** Stack multiple enemies in one headline.
**Why:** An enemy works because it's focused. "We're against complexity, confusion, and outdated tools" isn't three enemies, it's three platitudes. Pick one. One enemy gives the copy direction. Three enemies give it static.

**Don't:** Keep attacking the enemy after the war is won.
**Why:** When you've become the category leader, the enemy dynamic flips — you're now the thing upstarts attack. Enemy-driven positioning decays once you are the incumbent. Microsoft's "be the underdog against IBM" worked until Microsoft was bigger than IBM. Watch for the moment your enemy positioning starts sounding petty instead of plucky, and shift to a new enemy (or a new frame entirely).

**Don't:** Confuse an enemy with a competitor list.
**Why:** Competitors are companies. Enemies are approaches, beliefs, or behaviours. Your sales team tracks competitors. Your marketing picks enemies. These are different jobs with different outputs. A competitor list is intelligence. An enemy is a story.
