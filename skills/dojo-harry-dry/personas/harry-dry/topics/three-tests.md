# The Three Tests

## When to Use
- Every single sentence of copy you write. Three tests. Run every sentence through all three. Three yeses and you're on to something. Three nos and you've written rubbish.
- Reviewing someone else's copy and needing a structured way to explain what's wrong. The tests turn "I don't like it" into "it failed test 2 because you used an adjective where a number would go."
- Diagnosing why a page isn't converting. If the headline fails all three tests, nothing below it can save the page.

## Fails When
- **Applied too narrowly.** Some great copy uses abstract words on purpose — as setups for a concrete punchline ("Light. Years ahead.") or as deliberate misdirection. The tests are for the claim, not for every individual word.
- **Run as a purity test.** Good copy sometimes has one line that fails test 3 (a category claim nobody else is strongly making) if paired with lines that pass. Don't reject a draft because one line isn't unique. Reject it if the central claim isn't.
- **Run without the zoom-in.** If a line fails a test, the answer isn't "delete and write something else." The answer is to run the zoom-in technique (see zoom-in.md) until the line is concrete, falsifiable, and unique.

## Core Concept

Every sentence must pass three tests.

**Test 1 — Can I visualise it?** Concrete means tangible. It's a word you can drop on your foot. Leg of lamb — drop it on your foot, you feel the weight. "A better way" — drop it on your foot, it floats away. "Seamless transition" — try to pick it up, there's nothing to grip. Most marketing fails this test. "Premium experience." What does that look like? "Innovative solutions." Can you see a "solution" walking down the street? "We deliver results." What shape is a "result"? These words fill space. They don't fill your reader's head with pictures. Memory is image-based. If your customer can't visualise your message, they won't remember it. You're asking them to memorise digits instead of pictures.

**Test 2 — Can I falsify it?** "Leading chef" — leading according to who? Leading in what? Says who? You can't prove it. You can't disprove it. It's an opinion wearing a suit. "New York City chef" — either she's a chef in New York City or she isn't. You could check. There's something at stake. When you write something that could be proved wrong, you're saying: go on, check. I'll wait. That confidence is what creates trust. When you write something nobody can prove wrong — "premium quality," "industry-leading," "best-in-class" — you're saying nothing. You're playing it safe. And safety costs you everything. The simplest move: replace adjectives with numbers. "Tasty ingredients" becomes "all ingredients you would eat." "Packed with chicken" becomes "the number one ingredient is fresh chicken." "Low unsubscribes" becomes "0.4% unsubscribe rate." Every conversion turns opinion into fact. Every fact has stakes.

**Test 3 — Can nobody else say this?** Never write an ad a competitor can sign. The test is simple. Write your headline. Swap in your competitor's logo. Does it still work? If yes, throw it away. Durham University: "Equip you with essential skills to excel." Plymouth University: "Equip you with essential skills to excel." Keele University: "Equip you with essential skills to excel." Three different universities. Same sentence. Word for word. If everyone can say it, nobody is saying anything. Freedom Pay: "Take your payments to the next level." Google that phrase in curly quotes — hundreds of results. Every payments company on earth has written that exact sentence. You paid a copywriter. You held a meeting. You signed off on words that a hundred companies already use for free.

Stack all three on any claim.

"Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio." Can I visualise it? Yes — catwalk and barbecue. Can I falsify it? Yes — check Instagram. Can nobody else say this? Yes — only New Balance. Three yeses. That's the standard.

"Delicious hot pizza." Can I visualise it? Yes. Can I falsify it? Not really. Can nobody else say this? No — every pizza shop says it. One yes, two nos. Wallpaper.

"Ships in 24 hours." Can I visualise it? Sort of. Can I falsify it? Yes. Can nobody else say this? Only if your competitors don't ship in 24 hours. Two yeses if you're unique on speed, one yes if you aren't.

Three yeses = copy. Two yeses = close, push harder. One or zero yeses = wallpaper.

## How to Apply

1. **Read the line. Close your eyes.** Did anything appear? If not, it failed test 1. Find the concrete noun. What's the actual thing you mean? A product on a counter. A person at a desk. A screen with numbers. Find the picture. (If stuck, run zoom-in.md.)

2. **Circle every adjective.** For each one, ask three questions: Prove it to who? Compared to what? According to what metric? If you can't answer, the adjective is worthless. Replace it with a number, a customer quote, a specific fact, or cut it. "Fast delivery" becomes "ships before noon, arrives same day." "Experienced team" becomes "47 years combined, 200 projects." "High satisfaction" becomes "94% buy again within 12 months."

3. **Run the logo-swap test.** Cover your logo. Drop in a competitor's. Does the line still work? If yes, bin it. Run it through three different competitors — the bigger your category, the harder the test.

4. **Run the Google curly-quotes test.** Paste your headline in quotation marks into Google. One result or zero? You've written something. Hundreds of results? You've assembled from the same pre-fab parts bin as everyone else. ("Take your payments to the next level" returns hundreds. "Point it at the sky. Plug it in" returns Starlink.)

5. **If a line fails, don't rewrite randomly — run the zoom-in.** Write "abstract" at the top of the page. Write "concrete" at the bottom. Start with the vague line at the top. Ask yourself: "What do I actually mean?" Write the answer lower down. Ask again. Keep going until you hit something you can drop on your foot.

6. **If a line keeps failing test 3 after rewrites, the problem isn't the copy.** The positioning is generic. Go back to iceberg-positioning.md and find-your-enemy.md. No amount of clever sentence-craft will make a generic position sound unique.

## Examples

**Situation:** A fitness app with the tagline "Regain your fitness."
**Application:** Test 1 — can I visualise it? No. Fail. Test 2 — falsify "regain"? How? Against what? Fail. Test 3 — could any fitness company sign this? All of them. Fail. Three nos. Run the zoom-in. "Regain" — who? Someone sitting on a couch for six months. "Fitness" — what kind? Running. "Running" — how far? Five kilometres. Result: "Couch to 5K." Can I visualise it? A person on a couch. Same person crossing a finish line. Yes. Can I falsify it? Either the plan gets you from couch to 5K or it doesn't. Yes. Can nobody else say this? (It's not original anymore — it's now the name of the most-downloaded fitness app ever — but at inception, only this product was running this positioning.) Yes.
**Result:** The worst tagline in the category became the strongest by running it through the three tests and zooming in.

**Situation:** A consultancy homepage opens with "We deliver innovative solutions to drive growth."
**Application:** Close eyes — nothing visible. Fail test 1. "Innovative" — prove to whom? Fail test 2. "Drive growth" — every consultancy on earth says this. Fail test 3. Three nos. The entire line is wallpaper. The rewrite path: what specifically did they do for their last three clients? Example: "Helped three B2B SaaS founders raise their next round by rewriting the investor deck's thesis." Specific. Falsifiable (check the founders). Nobody else's (their three specific clients, their specific outcomes).
**Result:** The new line has something to push back against — you might disagree with the approach or the outcome — but it isn't invisible. Risk and memory are the same currency.

**Situation:** A B2B dashboard: "Get a 360-degree view of your business."
**Application:** Close eyes. What's a 360-degree view? Nothing visible. Fail test 1. "360-degree" — falsifiable? Meaningless. Fail test 2. Any dashboard can claim this. Fail test 3. Three nos. Rewrite path: what does the dashboard actually show? "See which of your 200 sales reps hit quota this week. Find the three who didn't, before Friday." Specific people. Specific numbers. Specific day.
**Result:** Same product. Same data. Completely different feel. The first line looks like a BI platform from 2014. The second line looks like a founder who actually runs a sales org.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run the three tests on setup lines that are supposed to be abstract.
**Why:** Some great copy opens with a deliberate abstraction to tee up a concrete punchline. "Light. Years ahead." The first word is abstract on purpose. The second collapses it into meaning. Judge the full line, not the fragment. If the claim itself lands concrete by the end, the setup can be abstract.

**Don't:** Obsess over tests 1 and 2 when test 3 is the real problem.
**Why:** Most copy that fails is concrete and falsifiable but still generic because everyone else is saying it too. "Fast, reliable, easy-to-use software" is sort-of-concrete, sort-of-falsifiable, and completely non-unique. The unlock is test 3. Put the logo-swap test at the top of the checklist.

**Don't:** Require test 3 to be "nobody on earth has ever said this."
**Why:** That's impossible and unnecessary. The test is whether a direct competitor could sign it today. New Balance's "supermodels and dads in Ohio" isn't a line no one has ever thought of — it's a line Nike and Adidas couldn't credibly sign. The bar is defensible uniqueness, not novelty.

**Don't:** Use the tests as a final filter only.
**Why:** Baking them into drafting saves rewrites. Close your eyes after every sentence as you write. Replace adjectives with numbers as you go. Logo-swap test at the end of every paragraph. The tests aren't a review stage — they're how you write.

**Don't:** Accept "but our whole category sounds like this" as an excuse for failing test 3.
**Why:** The category sounding like this is exactly why test 3 matters. If your whole category is writing "the comprehensive platform for modern teams," your opportunity to stand out is embarrassingly large — write something concrete and specific while everyone else recycles the parts bin. The excuse is the diagnosis.
