# Copywriting Formulas

## When to Use
- Drafting from scratch. A good formula frees you to focus on substance — you stop worrying about structure and start worrying about message.
- Diagnosing why existing copy isn't working. Often a whole step is missing — the pain isn't agitated, the before isn't vivid enough, the CTA doesn't describe a reward.
- Teaching a team to produce consistent copy. Formulas are patterns with predictable outputs.

## Fails When
- **The formula becomes a prison.** If the content doesn't fit, don't force it. Skip the formula. A good formula is scaffolding; you take it down once the building stands.
- **You fill the formula with wallpaper.** PAS with abstract pain and abstract agitation is still abstract. The formula is the skeleton; the substance still has to pass the three tests.
- **Formula fatigue in audience.** If every piece of your content uses PAS, readers start to notice the pattern. Vary formulas so the shape doesn't become predictable.

## Core Concept

Formulas aren't cheating. They're scaffolding. The best copywriters use them, then hide them. You don't reinvent the wheel every time you write. You use a structure that works and fill it with your words. Most people think formulas make writing robotic. The opposite is true. They free you to focus on the message. You stop worrying about structure and start worrying about substance.

Here are the formulas that keep showing up.

**PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve.** The most common formula in copywriting. Three steps. Name the problem. Twist the knife. Offer the fix. Most people skip straight to the solution. That's the mistake. You have to earn the right to sell by first proving you understand the pain. Billie, the women's razor brand:
- **Problem:** "Fact: Women and men shave differently."
- **Agitate:** "We shave 10x the area, navigating more curves and angles."
- **Solve:** "Not all razors are created equal. Ours are designed for womankind."

Curology:
- **Problem:** "Shopping at the drugstore for skincare is broken."
- **Agitate:** "Buy it, try it, toss it, repeat."
- **Solve:** "One-size-fits-you skincare."

"Buy it, try it, toss it, repeat" — six words in the agitate step. Six words that capture a frustration the reader has felt a hundred times. You don't need to explain the problem. You need to make them relive it. The agitate step is where most people go wrong. They skip it or underdo it. Don't. Name the pain. Rub salt in it. Then hand them the bandage.

**BAB — Before, After, Bridge.** Slightly different angle. Instead of agitating, you paint two pictures. Where the reader is now. Where they could be. Then you connect them. Trainual:
- **Before:** "Onboarding and training problems are killing your growth."
- **After:** "Imagine your business could grow without growing pains."
- **Bridge:** "One powerful playbook to onboard, train, and scale your team."

The before has to feel true. The after has to feel possible. The bridge has to feel easy. Use PAS when the pain is sharp. Use BAB when the dream is vivid. PAS pushes them away from pain. BAB pulls them toward desire. One more thing about BAB — the bridge has to be simple. One sentence. One product. One action. If your bridge needs two paragraphs, the before and after aren't clear enough.

**AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action.** The granddaddy. Since the 1890s. Mirrors how humans make decisions. Young & Rubicam ad:
- **Attention:** "This is a backbone." [with an image of a backbone]
- **Interest:** "You can't run a good advertising agency without it."
- **Desire:** "Very often, the result is outstanding advertising."
- **Action:** "Young & Rubicam, Inc."

Key insight: each stage has to earn the next. Attention without interest is clickbait. Interest without desire is education. Desire without action is daydreaming. The chain only works if every link holds.

**Features to Benefits.** Not exactly a formula — a translation exercise. Pattern: lead with the benefit, contrast the old way, show the new way. Unscreen:
- **Benefit:** "Goodbye Greenscreen."
- **Old way:** "Producing background-free video used to require complex and fiddly techniques like chroma keying and greenscreens."
- **New way:** "With Unscreen you can record anywhere, then simply get rid of the background."

Feather (furniture rental):
- **Benefit:** "Stop spending $6,000 on furniture."
- **Old way:** "It costs ~$6,000 to furnish a one-bedroom apartment."
- **New way:** "With Feather, you rent stylish furniture for a fraction of the price."

Lead with what the customer cares about. Then show the gap between the old world and yours. Most companies do this backwards — they start with features. "Our AI-powered background removal tool uses advanced machine learning..." Nobody cares. Start with the benefit. Then the before/after follows.

**Open Loop / Close Loop.** Open a question. Close it with your product. Huel:
- **Open:** "Who says eating healthy has to be expensive?"
- **Close:** "Only 1.32 per meal. 100% plant based. Complete nutrition. Vitamin B12 to support metabolism."

The open creates curiosity. The close creates conviction. The close must be specific — don't close with "affordable healthy food." Close with "1.32 per meal." Specifics close loops. Generalities leave them dangling.

**Dave Trott's Triangle.** Every ad needs three things. Impact — got to get noticed. Communication — got to tell me what. Persuasion — got to tell me why. Most ads just do communication. "We're a brand agency." Right. I just googled that. Impact without communication is art. Communication without persuasion is information. You need all three. Three layers. Miss one and the whole thing collapses.

**The CTA formula: objection handle + social proof + actionable outcome.** Most CTAs say "Subscribe" or "Sign Up." Functional. Boring. Better: "Join 85,000 marketers. Get one tip every Monday." The objection handle tells you what you'll get. The social proof tells you who else gets it. The actionable outcome tells you what happens next. The best CTAs describe a reward, not an action. Lead with value, not action.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the formula that matches your content's emotional shape.**
   - Sharp, specific pain your customer feels today → PAS.
   - Clear gap between current state and desired state → BAB.
   - Long-form narrative ad, or a sales letter where you need to sequence attention-to-action → AIDA.
   - Feature-heavy product where you need to translate → Features-to-Benefits.
   - Short ad that needs to stop the scroll → Open Loop / Close Loop.
   - Any ad, as a final check → Trott's Triangle.

2. **Write the formula's steps, each as a single sentence.** Don't try to write "the whole paragraph" for each step. A step is a beat. One sentence per beat is usually enough.

3. **Run each beat through the three tests.**
   - Is the problem/before/attention concrete enough to visualise?
   - Is the agitate/bridge step specific (numbers, real pain words, customer language) rather than generic?
   - Is the solve/action/close falsifiable — would the reader recognise it as doable?

4. **Rewrite each step for rhythm once substance is right.** Parallelism between the steps (see rhetorical-toolkit.md) — "Buy it, try it, toss it, repeat" — gives the formula sonic force beyond the structure.

5. **For CTAs, replace "Sign Up" with reward language.** What does the user get when they click? Write that. "Get my first lesson" beats "Subscribe." "Find my tax credits" beats "Start trial." "See my results" beats "Get started."

6. **Check Trott's Triangle on the finished piece.** Impact — does it stop the scroll? Communication — can the reader summarise what you do? Persuasion — do they know why they should care? If any pillar is missing, the formula didn't save you.

## Examples

**Situation:** A newsletter sign-up page with CTA "Subscribe."
**Application:** Apply the CTA formula. What does the user get? One specific email every week. Who else reads it? Write a reward-shaped CTA: "Get Monday's issue (85k marketers already do)." Objection handle ("get the next issue — you're not committing to ten years"), social proof ("85k already do"), reward ("Monday's issue").
**Result:** One clicked the mechanic of a form field; the other promises a specific thing the reader wants. Conversion follows.

**Situation:** A SaaS homepage opens with a product tour. No hook, no agitation.
**Application:** Swap to PAS. Find the sharpest pain your happy customers describe in their own words. "Reconciling invoices takes our finance team ten hours a week." Agitate: "Ten hours. Per week. Of work no human should ever have to do." Solve: "We reconcile in two minutes. Same data. Same accuracy. Different decade."
**Result:** The product tour moved down the page; the PAS hook does the selling above the fold. Readers now see themselves in the pain before they see the solution.

**Situation:** A fintech product pitch to consumers that opens with "We're a modern banking platform for ambitious people."
**Application:** Apply BAB. Before: "Most banks still make you wait three days for an international transfer." After: "Send money to anyone, anywhere, in under 30 seconds." Bridge: "Open a Wise account in one minute." Three beats. No "ambitious people" language survived.
**Result:** BAB forces specifics on both sides. "Modern banking platform" is replaced with two concrete moments (the 3-day wait and the 30-second transfer), and the bridge is a specific action. Drawing the before/after also makes the copy unsignable — every other bank allows that 3-day wait.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Skip the agitate step in PAS.
**Why:** "Skincare shopping is broken. Try us." doesn't work because the reader hasn't relived the problem — they just nodded and scrolled. The agitate step is where the reader sees themselves. Without it, PAS collapses to Problem-Solve, which is a product tour with a hook.

**Don't:** Let the bridge in BAB be abstract.
**Why:** "We help you get there" is an abstract bridge. The bridge must be a specific product, a specific action, a specific commitment. "Sign up in 60 seconds. First workout plan in your inbox by tonight." Concrete bridges close BAB; abstract bridges leave the customer in the before.

**Don't:** Use AIDA as an excuse to pad.
**Why:** AIDA is designed for long-form ads and sales letters where every stage needs room. On a landing page hero, AIDA often collapses to PAS or BAB. Don't force attention-interest-desire-action when the medium is 140 characters.

**Don't:** Write a features-to-benefits section that's just a features section with a sentence prepended.
**Why:** "Unlock productivity — built on advanced ML" is a feature with a platitude on top. Real features-to-benefits does the zoom-in work (see zoom-in.md) — feature ("built on ML") → benefit ("learns your customer's tone") → value ("your replies sound like you wrote them") → who cares ("founders whose support volume is eating their week").

**Don't:** Apply every formula to every piece.
**Why:** A newsletter is not a sales letter. A Twitter thread is not a landing page. Each medium has formulas that fit and formulas that don't. PAS is a sledgehammer — use it when the pain is sharp, not for a light brand update.
