# The New-CEO Reset

## When to Use
- You're considering a fundamental strategic redirection — exiting a core business, killing a defining product line, reorganizing around a new center of gravity — and you find yourself unable to commit, even though you can articulate why the redirection is correct.
- The arguments against the redirection keep coming back to identity ("we *are* a memory company," "we *are* a hardware company," "this is who we are") rather than to economics or to capabilities.
- A founder you're advising can describe the new strategy fluently in private and then walks back into the next staff meeting and defends the old one.
- You catch yourself defending a current strategy by listing how much money, time, or career has gone into it, rather than by what it would produce going forward.
- You suspect the binding constraint on the company's next move is not information — you have enough information — but emotional investment in the past.

## Fails When
- Used for ordinary tuning of a working strategy. The new-CEO question is heavy-duty equipment for fundamental redirections. Applied to routine course-correction, it manufactures false drama and licenses thrash.
- Used as a rhetorical move to push a personal preference rather than as a genuine attempt to import outsider judgment. If you already know what answer you want, the question becomes a manipulation, not a tool.
- Applied before the diagnostic work is done. The reset imports objectivity, but objectivity has nothing to operate on if you haven't first established that an inflection point is in progress, what the 10X force is, and what's changed in the silver-bullet and complementor tests. The reset is a release valve at the end of the diagnosis, not a substitute for it.
- Treated as a one-and-done conversation. You'll almost certainly answer the question, agree intellectually with the answer, and then revert. Plan to ask it again, with the same people, in three weeks, and again three weeks after that, until the actions on calendars and wafer allocations match the words.

## Core Concept

When a company has to cross a strategic inflection point, the binding constraint is rarely information. By the time the question is on the table, the senior team usually has more than enough information to know what to do. What's missing is the willingness to do it. The willingness is missing because the people in the room — including you — have built their identities around the strategy that has to change. They wrote the press releases. They hired the team. They told their families what the company was. They defined themselves in opposition to a competitor who is no longer the right competitor. To redirect, they have to undo a decade of self-definition, and that turns out to be the most expensive line item on the balance sheet.

Lesser leaders are removed and replaced by individuals who are not necessarily more capable but who do not have the emotional investment in the previous strategy. That's the thing that makes the replacement effective: not better judgment, just less encumbrance. The new-CEO reset is a way to import that lack of encumbrance without going through the firing. You step out of yourself, briefly, and answer as someone who didn't build the thing. Then you decide whether to do what the imagined replacement would do.

The follow-up matters as much as the question. Once you've named what the new CEO would do, the next move is, "Why don't we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?" That sentence converts insight into action. Without it, you've had a thought experiment. With it, you've made a commitment — and committed yourself to the awkwardness of telling the rest of the room that the strategy you've been defending in public is the one you're now going to dismantle.

> "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what do you think he would do?" (OTPS ch.5)

## How to Apply

1. **Confirm you're at a fundamental redirect, not a tuning.** Run the inflection diagnostics first — the silver-bullet test, the complementor test, the strategic-dissonance check. If the answer is "we're at an inflection," continue. If the answer is "we're inside a working strategy that needs adjustment," put this tool away. Using it on tuning is destructive.

2. **Pick one trusted partner and pose the question literally.** Two people, behind a closed door, with no audience. Say the words: "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what would he do?" Don't soften it. Don't pad it with qualifications. The cleanness of the question is what makes the answer come out.

3. **Wait for the immediate answer.** In Grove's conversation with Gordon Moore, the answer came back without hesitation: "He would get us out of memories." When the question is correctly posed at a real inflection point, the answer almost always comes fast. If your partner has to think about it for ten minutes, either the question isn't right yet or the inflection isn't really an inflection. The lack of hesitation is part of the diagnostic.

4. **Ask the follow-up.** "Why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?" This is the move that turns a thought experiment into a decision. The first question gives you the answer. The second one assigns the work.

5. **Notice your own equivocation when you try to say it to anyone else.** When Grove tried to discuss the exit with his associates, he had a hard time getting the words out without equivocation. That's expected. The difficulty of saying it out loud is not a sign that you're wrong; it's a sign that you've correctly identified the part of the strategy that's load-bearing for your identity. Notice it, name it to yourself, and say the words anyway.

6. **Don't expect a single conversation to be enough.** The intellectual answer is fast. The emotional acceptance is years. Plan to revisit the new-CEO question with yourself and your partner repeatedly through the crossing — in staff meetings, at offsites, after every major reversal. The discipline is to keep the imagined replacement in the room as a check on your own emotional reflexes.

## Examples

**Situation:** Intel, mid-1985. The company had been a memory chip business for sixteen years; "memories were us" was Grove's phrasing for how deep the identity ran. By 1985, Japanese producers had gutted the memory market structurally. Intel had been losing money for nearly a year. Senior management had held meeting after meeting and produced conflicting proposals — go-for-it strategies, avant-garde technology bets, special-purpose niches that didn't exist — and the wandering had cost a year of cash and morale. Grove and Moore were in Grove's office, looking out at the Ferris wheel of Great America turning in the distance, with no obvious next move.

**Application:** Grove asked the question literally: if we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what would he do? Moore answered without hesitation: he would get us out of memories. Grove sat with that for a moment — "I stared at him, numb" — and then asked the follow-up that converted the insight into a commitment: why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?

**Result:** The decision was effectively made in that conversation, in an afternoon, with two people. Implementation took until mid-1986 to execute and another year to return Intel to profitability. The new-CEO reset didn't shorten the implementation; it shortened the *deliberation* — the wandering-in-the-valley phase that had been costing the company a year of cash. Grove's later reading of the episode is that the intellectual work had been done long before the conversation. The conversation merely removed the emotional barrier to acting on it.

> "If existing management want to keep their jobs when the basics of the business are undergoing profound change, they must adopt an outsider's intellectual objectivity. They must do what they need to do to get through the strategic inflection point unfettered by any emotional attachment to the past. That's what Gordon and I had to do when we figuratively went out the door, stomped out our cigarettes and returned to do the job." (OTPS ch.5)

**Situation:** A founder you're advising runs a company built around a hardware product that defined the brand for a decade. The market has shifted toward a software-and-services model. Internally, the founder has admitted to a few trusted people that the hardware line should be wound down. In public meetings, he keeps defending it. Gross margins keep deteriorating. The founder's stated reason for not pulling the trigger: "We're a hardware company. That's what built us."

**Application:** The defense is identity, not economics. That's the signal that the new-CEO reset applies. Pose the question to him literally: if the board brought in a new CEO tomorrow, with no history at this company, what would she do with the hardware line? The answer comes back fast — wind it down. Ask the follow-up: why don't we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves? Notice that he equivocates when he tries to say "wind down the hardware line" out loud at all-hands. That equivocation is the diagnostic. The answer is correct; the inability to say it out loud is the cost of the identity attachment.

**Result:** The advisor's job is not to manufacture courage but to keep the imagined replacement in the room. Repeat the question every few weeks. Help the founder hear his own equivocations as evidence of the attachment, not as evidence of doubt about the decision. The action — reassigning engineers, rewriting the roadmap, shifting the marketing — follows the words, but the words have to come first, and the words come from the reset.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the new-CEO question with a large audience.
**Why:** The clean answer comes from two people, behind a closed door, with no posture to defend. In a room of ten, everyone is performing for everyone else, and the imagined replacement is contaminated by everyone's interest in the existing strategy. The Grove-Moore conversation worked because it was Grove and Moore. Reproduce that intimacy or don't run the test.

**Don't:** Manipulate the question by framing it to produce your preferred answer.
**Why:** "If a new CEO came in, wouldn't they obviously do the thing I've been pushing for?" is not the new-CEO reset. It is a sales pitch dressed up as the reset. The honest version asks the question with no thumb on the scale and accepts the answer the imagined replacement actually gives — which sometimes will be "she'd keep doing what you're doing."

**Don't:** Treat the answer as the work.
**Why:** Naming what the imagined replacement would do is the easy part. Saying it out loud to your team, reallocating wafers and headcount, telling customers and partners — that is the work. Grove had the answer in an afternoon and spent eighteen months executing it. Plan accordingly. The reset accelerates the deliberation; it does not accelerate the implementation.

**Don't:** Skip the follow-up question.
**Why:** Without "why don't we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?", you've had a useful thought. With it, you've committed. The shift from imagining the replacement to *being* the replacement is the move that converts the thought experiment into action, and it has to be made explicitly. Otherwise the imagined CEO walks out of the room with you, and the actual CEO continues the old strategy on Monday.

**Don't:** Apply the reset before confirming the inflection.
**Why:** The reset is the right tool only when the binding constraint is emotional investment in a strategy that the environment has already invalidated. If the strategy is still working, the reset will manufacture a redirect where none is warranted. Run the diagnostics first.

**Don't:** Expect the reset to fix the cultural carrying-cost of the redirect.
**Why:** Even when the reset works perfectly, you still have to march a tired, demoralized organization through the valley of death on the other side. The reset gets you to the valley's edge. Crossing it is a separate framework. Don't confuse the moment of clarity with the work of the crossing.
