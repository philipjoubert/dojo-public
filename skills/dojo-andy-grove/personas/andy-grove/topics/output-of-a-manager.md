# Output of a Manager

## When to Use
- Defining or redefining what a manager — yours, a report's, your own — is actually being measured on.
- A manager arrives in the conversation with a list of activities (meetings held, decks reviewed, plans drafted) and treats that list as the answer to "what did you produce?"
- Designing a scorecard, a hiring rubric, or an org-design document for any managerial role.
- Diagnosing why a manager who is "always busy" doesn't seem to move the business.
- Auditing your own week before a board update or self-review.

## Fails When
- Applied to a true individual contributor with no reports and no cross-org influence — the second term in the equation is empty and the first collapses to "my own work," at which point the framing adds nothing.
- Used as a stick rather than a diagnostic. The point is to redirect attention to leverage; if it becomes a way to hammer a manager whose team had a bad quarter for reasons outside her control, you've turned a thinking tool into a blame tool and lost both.
- Applied at too short a horizon. Output ripens slowly. A team's output this week is mostly the consequence of a manager's input weeks or months earlier, not the meetings she held this morning.

## Core Concept

A manager's output is not what she does. It is what her organization does, plus what neighboring organizations do because of her influence. Write it as an equation:

> "A manager's output = the output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence." (HOM ch.3)

Two terms, both of them outside the manager's own keyboard. The first covers the team that reports to her. The second covers the people she doesn't supervise but whose work she shapes — the engineer who consults across plants, the marketing analyst whose interpretation guides the company, the lawyer whose permit unblocks a year of research. In a know-how business, that second term is enormous, and ignoring it produces a generation of "individual contributors" who are quietly running the place without title or credit. The definition of *manager* has to widen to fit the work.

The trap that catches almost everyone is confusing activity with output. I asked a room of middle managers what their output was. They gave me: judgments, opinions, direction, resource allocation, mistakes detected, subordinates trained, courses taught, products planned, commitments negotiated. Every one of those is an activity. None of them is output. The output of a wafer-fab manager is fully processed silicon wafers. The output of a high-school principal is educated students ready for the next year. The output of a surgeon is a recovered patient. Output is what comes out the end of the pipeline — what the customer of the work, internal or external, ends up holding. Activities are only valuable to the extent that they generate that output through the team.

This is why a day in a manager's life looks like nothing in particular. Mine breaks into four categories: information-gathering, decision-making, nudging, and being a role model. I read mail, take a call, sit through a staff meeting, lecture at orientation, eat lunch with the training group, ratify a decision over the phone — twenty-five separate activities, two-thirds of them in some kind of meeting. None of those, taken individually, is impressive. Each of them is a vehicle through which I try to affect the output of the company. The point is not to feel important during the meeting. The point is whether the wafer comes out clean a month later.

## How to Apply

1. **Translate the question from verbs to nouns.** When someone tells you about their work in verbs of activity — "I'm running planning, I'm holding reviews, I'm coaching the team" — restate it in nouns of output: "What is the team shipping, to whom, at what quality, on what schedule?" If they can't answer in output terms, that's the first finding. They don't yet know what their output is. Stop there. Don't move on to tactics until output is named.

2. **Compute both terms of the equation.** Make a list of the people who report to you. That's the first term. Then make a separate list of people who don't report to you but whose work you influence — the team you consult to, the cross-functional process you own, the standards committee you sit on, the junior person in another division you mentor. That's the second term. Most managers radically under-count the second term. In a know-how organization, it's often where the largest leverage lives.

3. **Categorize the day into the four activities.** Pick a recent day or week and tag every block on your calendar as information-gathering, decision-making, nudging, or role-modeling. Most of it will be information-gathering — that's correct; information is the base of everything else. Decisions are rare and mostly ratifications. Nudges are far more common than decisions. Role-modeling is invisible to you and constant to your organization.

4. **Test each block against the output equation.** For each block, ask: through which downstream output does this contribute? If the answer is nothing — if you cannot trace a line from this hour to a wafer, a customer, a shipped feature, a trained subordinate, a better-positioned neighboring team — the block is activity without output, and the calendar should be edited.

5. **Treat your own work as input to the team's output, not as an end.** A surgeon spends his time scrubbing and cutting and suturing; that hardly sounds respectable in itself, and yet the cured patient is what he produced. Your hours are scrubbing. They are valuable insofar as they end in the patient walking out. If you can do your "own" job perfectly and the organization still doesn't ship, you have not produced anything as a manager.

## Examples

**Situation:** A middle manager at Intel asked Grove, in the middle of a busy stretch, how he could possibly find time to teach in-plant courses, walk through manufacturing plants, and meet with people several levels below him in the org chart, and still "do his job."

**Application:** Grove turned the question back: what did the manager think his job was?

**Result:** The manager paused, thought, and answered his own question — those things *are* the job. They are not stolen from the job; they are how a CEO, who cannot write a single line of code or process a single wafer, contributes to the output of Intel. The lesson generalizes: every time a senior person catches themselves saying "I don't have time to do my job because I have to do all this other stuff," it is worth asking which of the two — the desk work or the "other stuff" — is more plausibly upstream of the company's output.

> "I asked him what he thought my job was. He thought for a moment, and then answered his own question, 'I guess those things are your job too, aren't they?' They are absolutely my job — not my entire job, but part of it, because they help add to the output of Intel." (HOM ch.3)

---

**Situation:** Cindy, an engineer at Intel, supervises an engineering group inside a single wafer-fab plant. She also serves on a corporate advisory body that sets standard procedures for all the company's plants.

**Application:** Cindy contributes to output through both terms of the equation. Inside her plant, she supervises directly — first term. On the advisory body, she influences procedures used by every other fab in the company — second term. A naive view would treat the advisory body as "extra" work distracting her from her real job. Grove's view is the opposite: the advisory work has higher leverage than her direct supervision, because it touches every plant in the network.

**Result:** A know-how individual contributor — someone with no direct reports — can have a manager-sized output if their expertise propagates across neighboring organizations. This is why Grove insists the definition of "manager" must widen to include knowledge specialists. Their second term dwarfs most line managers' first term. (HOM ch.3.)

---

**Situation:** A founder writes in worried about a new VP whose calendar is "completely full" and who keeps saying she's "swamped," yet her team has missed two delivery dates in a row.

**Application:** Run the activity-versus-output translation. Ask the VP: what is your team shipping, to whom, at what quality, on what schedule? If the answer keeps drifting back into verbs ("I'm coaching, I'm reviewing, I'm coordinating") — and the deliveries are still slipping — the VP is producing activity, not output. Calendar fullness is a symptom of mistaking the medium for the work.

**Result:** Either the VP redirects her hours toward the activities that actually move the team's output (the diagnostic from the leverage equation in the related topic), or she is mismatched to the role. A manager whose calendar is full and whose team is missing dates has either chosen the wrong activities or — worse — has a team that doesn't depend on her at all, in which case the role itself is not a managerial role.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse meeting attendance with managerial work.
**Why:** Meetings are the *medium* through which a manager works, not the work itself. You cannot tell whether a meeting was productive by counting it; you can only tell by tracing it forward to the output it shaped. Two-thirds of my own day is in meetings. That is correct only if those meetings are how I gather information, decide, nudge, and model. The day a meeting becomes a calendar item to satisfy is the day it stops being output.

**Don't:** Reward managers for activity-density on their calendar.
**Why:** Indicators steer behavior. If you praise the manager who is in back-to-back meetings, you'll get more back-to-back meetings; you will not get more output. The performance discussion has to land on the output of the team, with the manager's activities treated only as the means.

**Don't:** Cut a manager's "second term" work — cross-org influence, knowledge sharing, in-plant teaching — because it doesn't appear on her direct-team scorecard.
**Why:** That work is often the highest-leverage thing she does. Reorgs that "tighten focus" by killing cross-org work routinely destroy the largest source of a know-how manager's output. If you have to defend the time, defend it explicitly as part of the equation, not as a hobby.

**Don't:** Let a manager describe her output in verbs after you've asked for it in nouns.
**Why:** This is the most common failure mode in performance conversations. You ask "what did your team ship this quarter?" and the answer comes back "we ran the planning process, we set up the new review cadence, we onboarded two engineers." Those are activities. The conversation cannot move forward until you have nouns. Press, kindly, until the nouns appear. If they refuse to appear, that is the finding.

**Don't:** Treat the output equation as a quarterly metric.
**Why:** Output ripens slowly. A team's results this month are largely the consequence of inputs from one to six months ago. Judging a manager by what shipped this month, against what she did this month, is a category error — the leverage of her current work shows up in next quarter's output. Use the equation as a lens, not a stopwatch.
