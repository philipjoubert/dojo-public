# Layoffs

## When to Use
- You have decided (or are about to decide) that the company has to reduce headcount materially. The financial plan didn't hold. The cost structure is wrong for the business you actually have. This is a company failure and it has to be addressed as one.
- You are drafting the script for the all-hands and you can feel it pulling toward spin. Stop. Write the real version.
- You are in the three-to-six-week window after a layoff, and you are managing survivors who are quieter than they should be and less committed than they were last month.

## Fails When
- **It's really an executive firing.** Different script, different cultural frame, different communication sequence. Route to firing-executives.
- **It's really a performance cleanup.** If the real motivation is that you have twenty underperformers you've been avoiding firing, stop. Don't dress that up as a layoff. That's a management debt problem and calling it a layoff will destroy trust with the people who stay.
- **The founder is too shattered to run the event.** In that case, the first step is stabilizing the CEO psychologically, because a CEO who delivers the layoff with the wrong affect does more damage than the layoff itself. Route to the-struggle for the founder's psychology, then come back here for the execution.

## Core Concept

After we sold Opsware to HP, I sat down with Doug Leone of Sequoia. He wanted to hear the story of how we went from doomed to a $1.6 billion outcome with no recapitalization. I walked him through it — the near bankruptcies, the stock at thirty-five cents, unlimited bad press, and three separate layoffs in which we lost a total of four hundred employees. He stopped me at the layoffs.

He said that in over twenty years in venture capital, he had never seen a company recover from consecutive layoffs and achieve a billion-dollar-plus outcome. He said he would bet against that every time. He asked me how we did it.

I asked him why the other startups failed. He said: *the layoffs inevitably broke the company's culture. After seeing their friends laid off, employees were no longer willing to make the sacrifices needed to build a company.*

We laid people off the right way. That is the whole answer. Doug had seen thirty companies lay people off the wrong way, and every one of them had broken the trust that makes a company a company.

Here is the first thing you have to get right, and it is the thing almost every founder gets wrong: **this is a company failure, and you have to own it that way.**

If you are the founder-CEO, you laid those people off because you fucked up. Your plan was wrong. Your forecast was wrong. Your strategy was wrong. Something was wrong, and it was your responsibility. You must own that reality before you can lead the company through the layoff with any integrity. Board members will sometimes try to make you feel better with positive spin — *this is a great opportunity to deal with some performance issues and simplify the business.* That may even be partially true, but do not let it cloud your message. You are laying people off because the company failed to hit its plan.

The message must be, and I will put this in quotes because it is the exact shape of the sentence:

*The company failed, and in order to move forward, we will have to lose some excellent people.*

Not "we are right-sizing." Not "we are realigning for the next phase." Not "tough decisions were made." Not "the market has changed." Not any of the passive-voice euphemisms that corporate America reaches for when it is trying to avoid responsibility. You decided. You have to say so. Passive voice in a layoff script is a coward's tell and the room can hear it.

Admitting the failure may not seem like a big deal. Trust me, it is. *Trust me.* That's what a CEO says every day to her employees. Trust me, this will be a good company. Trust me, this will be good for your career. Trust me, this will be good for your life. A layoff breaks that trust. In order to rebuild the trust with the people who stay — because the people who stay are the only people who matter now — you have to come clean. There is no shortcut.

During the first layoff at Loudcloud, I was forwarded an email exchange between some of our employees. In the exchange, one of our smarter engineers wrote: *"Ben is either lying or stupid or both."* I remember reading that and thinking: definitely stupid. That was where my learning started.

The second thing you have to understand: the layoff is not primarily about the people you are losing. It is about the people who stay.

My friend Bill Campbell put this to me when I was preparing for the speech. *The message is for the people who are staying.* The people who stay will care deeply about how you treated the people who left. Many of them will have closer relationships with the people you are cutting than you do. They will remember every detail. They will remember what you said, how you said it, who you delegated it to, and whether you showed up at the end of the day. If they see you handle the layoff with integrity, they will stay and rebuild. If they see you dodge it, spin it, or hide from it, they will spend the next twelve months looking for another job, whether or not they tell you.

Then there is the script. The all-hands script. The one you think is just a speech but is actually the single most culturally consequential artifact you will produce as a CEO. Let me give you the rules for the script, because they are simple and almost everyone breaks them.

No passive voice. No "tough decisions were made." Replace with "I decided." If you cannot say "I decided," you should not be doing the layoff.

No euphemisms. Say "we are laying off roughly X people" in the first thirty seconds. Do not build up to it. Do not bury it.

No apologizing too much. This is a narrow line. Acknowledge the failure clearly, own it, and move on. If you spend the entire speech flagellating yourself, the company cannot recover. You are setting the precedent for how they process this event. They take their cues from you.

No sunshine. This is not the moment for optimism that cannot be backed up. Do not close with "we're stronger than ever." Close with something that projects calibrated confidence — a clear, credible statement about what the plan is going forward and what you are asking from the people who stay.

## How to Apply

1. **Get your head right before anything else.** Do not look for the bright side. Do not spin it to yourself. This is a failure, and you must own it. Your focus now — from the moment you make the decision to the moment you finish the last conversation — is on the people who are staying and how they will perceive the event.

2. **Don't delay once the decision is made.** Between the decision and the execution, every extra day is a liability. If word leaks — and it will leak if you delay — you will face an extra set of problems. Employees will question managers. If managers don't know, they look stupid. If managers do know, they have to lie, leak, or stay silent, which creates additional agitation. We mismanaged this on the first layoff at Loudcloud. We sharply corrected on the next two.

3. **Be clear in your own mind about why.** This is a company failure, not a performance cleanup. If individual performance were the only issue, you'd be running a different action. This distinction has to be crisp for you, because it will leak into every conversation you have for the next six months.

4. **Train your managers — and they lay off their own people.** This is the most important operational step in the whole exercise. Managers must lay off their own direct reports. They cannot pass it to HR or a more confrontational peer. You cannot hire one of those outsourcing firms from *Up in the Air*. Why so strict? Because people will not remember every day they worked at your company. They will remember every detail of the day you laid them off. *If you hired me and I busted my ass working for you, I expect you to have the courage to lay me off yourself.* Before the day, brief your managers: explain briefly what happened and that it is a company rather than a personal failure; be clear that the decision is non-negotiable; be fully prepared with the benefits, the severance terms, and the transition support. No ambiguity. No "we're exploring options." The sentence is: *"You are being let go. Your last day is X. Here is what we are providing."*

5. **Address the entire company — the CEO, personally, in person.** Not a Slack post. Not a blog. You, in front of the company. Before the individual conversations in most cases (teams split on this — some prefer the reverse sequence, but the key point is that the CEO addresses the company, clearly, on the day of, and is visible during and after).

6. **Be visible. Be present. Do not hide.** After you make the speech, you will not want to hang out. You will want to go to a bar and drink a fifth of tequila. Do not do this. Be present. Be visible. Be engaging. People want to see you. They want to know if you still care. The people you laid off will want to know if the relationship with you and the company still exists. Talk to them. Help them carry their things to their cars. Let them know you appreciate their effort. This is the part almost every founder neglects and it is the part the survivors watch most closely.

7. **Address the survivors directly, that afternoon, again.** The speech in the morning is the event. The speech in the afternoon is the reset. Acknowledge survivor's guilt explicitly — many of the people who stay will feel worse than the people who left, because they will carry the guilt of being kept. Tell them the plan for the next ninety days. Tell them what you need from them. If you stay, stay all the way. If you can't, tell me, and we'll figure out how to part well.

8. **Expect the next three months to be hard and plan accordingly.** Productivity will dip. Some people will quit — often the people you wanted to keep. The way to minimize this is not more communication in the abstract, but visible, frequent, honest check-ins about how the plan is actually going. Do not go silent on the company after the event. The worst companies I've seen post-layoff are the ones where the CEO disappears into board meetings for six weeks and the company fills the silence with its own worst imagination.

## Examples

**Situation:** Series C startup. The plan assumed $60M in ARR growth this year; they'll end at $28M. Burn is unsustainable. The CEO has decided to lay off 30% of the company — about 120 people. She has drafted an all-hands script that opens: "Given the challenging macroeconomic environment and the need to align our resources to our long-term strategy, we have made some difficult decisions about our team structure."

**Application:** That script is a lie everyone can see through. Rewrite. The opening has to be: "Today we are laying off roughly one hundred and twenty of our colleagues — approximately thirty percent of the company. I want to tell you what happened and I want to tell you the truth about it. We did not build a business that could support the cost structure we built. That is on me. It is not a performance issue. The people we are losing are not the people who weren't good enough. Many of them are people I personally recruited and personally asked to bet on this company. They made good on that bet. We didn't make good on ours." The difference between the two scripts is not stylistic. The first one signals to every remaining employee: *the CEO will spin. When something goes wrong for me next, the CEO will spin about me too.* The second one signals: *the CEO will tell me the truth. I can stay here.*

**Result:** In version two, the survivors know what happened. They do not spend the next three weeks trying to decode whether they are next. Productivity recovers faster. Attrition among the people you most want to keep drops. The company's capacity to endure the next eighteen months of hard work — which is exactly what it needs — is higher because the trust is intact.

**Situation:** Fifty-person company. Founder has to lay off twelve people. She is exhausted and overwhelmed and plans to have her head of HR deliver the individual news to each of the twelve. She'll address the company by Slack that afternoon.

**Application:** No. You are the CEO. You hired these people. You asked them to bet their careers on you. The managers do the individual conversations — their own people, face-to-face — and you do the all-hands in person. If you are geographically distributed and in-person is genuinely impossible, it is a live video call with cameras on, not a written note. The Slack announcement version of this is the worst possible signal: *I couldn't be bothered to say this to your face.* Every person who stays will note it. Your ability to raise the next round of commitment from them will be damaged for years. You do not get to outsource the hardest thing a CEO does.

**Result:** If you do it right, the company's capacity to endure the next crisis — and there will be a next crisis — is materially higher than it was before the layoff. That is the strange, counterintuitive thing: a well-handled layoff can make a company stronger culturally, because it is the first time the CEO has been tested on whether she will tell the truth when the truth is expensive. The people who see you pass that test will follow you through the next one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Frame the layoff as "taking the opportunity to address some performance issues."
**Why:** It is a lie that everyone can see through. It fails to take responsibility for the company failure, and — this is the worst part — it terrifies the people who stay because they assume they're next on the "performance" list. You have taken what could have been a trust-rebuilding event and turned it into a trust-destroying event. If individual performance is really the cause, you're not doing a layoff — you're doing a series of firings, and those are different actions with different scripts.

**Don't:** Use passive voice in the all-hands script.
**Why:** "Difficult decisions were made" is the cowardly tell that every employee hears. By whom? "Tough choices had to happen." Who made them? You did. Say so. The passive voice is trying to protect you from the emotional weight of what you've done, but the company reads the protection as evasion. Passive voice in a layoff script is a coward's tell and the room can hear it.

**Don't:** Outsource the individual conversations to HR or to an outside firm.
**Why:** The person being laid off will remember every detail of the day you let them go. If they got that news from someone who didn't know their work, didn't know their contribution, and wasn't the person who asked them to join, they will remember it as an indignity. And everyone they ever talk to — in this industry, for the next thirty years — will know how your company handled it. Managers lay off their own people. Period.

**Don't:** Disappear after the event.
**Why:** You will want to. You will feel like the only thing you can do is escape, collapse, drink, or go to a dark room alone. Don't. The survivors are watching to see if you still care, and the people you laid off — who will remain part of your industry, your alumni network, and your career — are watching to see whether the relationship with you and this company is over. Stay. Be visible. Help people carry their things to their cars. Talk to them. Take the calls that night. This is the part that costs nothing financially and distinguishes the CEOs who rebuild their companies from the ones who just ran a layoff.

**Don't:** Skip the afternoon reset speech to the survivors.
**Why:** Survivor's guilt is a real and specific thing. It is heaviest in the first week after the event, and it has to be named directly. The survivors will feel like they are betraying the people who left by moving forward. You have to give them permission to move forward. You also have to give them the plan — a clear, credible account of what the next ninety days look like and what you are asking from them. Without the afternoon speech, the company fills the silence with its own worst imagination for the next three weeks, and productivity and morale collapse in ways that are very hard to reverse.
