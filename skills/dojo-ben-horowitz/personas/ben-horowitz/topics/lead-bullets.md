# Lead Bullets

## When to Use
- Customers are choosing a competitor's product over yours, repeatedly, in head-to-head evaluations.
- The smartest people on your team have started proposing clever strategic moves — downmarket play, acquisition, pivot, repositioning, new segment focus — as the answer.
- You are about to greenlight one of those clever moves because it feels easier than nine months of grinding product work.
- You are reviewing a product roadmap and the roadmap is missing the thing your biggest deals are losing on.

## Fails When
- You have genuinely established that the market does not exist for what you built — nobody is buying, from anyone. That's a pivot case, not a lead-bullets case.
- The problem is channel or distribution, not product. If customers want your product but can't find it, you don't need better product, you need better distribution.
- You've been losing deals for two weeks and you're panicking. Lead bullets are a nine-month answer, not a two-week one. Check whether this is a real trend or a small sample.

## Core Concept

Early in my tenure as product manager at Netscape, we benchmarked Microsoft's new web server against ours. Microsoft's IIS had every feature we had, was five times faster, and we knew they were going to give it away for free. Oh snap.

I immediately went to work trying to move the playing field. Partnerships. Acquisitions. Anything to broaden the product line and survive the attack. My engineering counterpart, Bill Turpin, a long-time veteran of battling Microsoft from his days at Borland, looked at me like I was a little kid who had much to learn. He said:

*"Ben, those silver bullets that you and Mike are looking for are fine and good, but our web server is five times slower. There is no silver bullet that's going to fix that. No, we are going to have to use a lot of lead bullets."*

We focused our engineering team on fixing the performance issues. We eventually beat Microsoft's performance and grew the server line to become a $400 million business. We never would have done it without those lead bullets.

I carried that lesson for years. When I was CEO of Opsware, our toughest competitor BladeLogic started consistently beating us in large deals. The smartest people in my company came to me with ideas for avoiding the battle. "Let's build a light-weight version and go down-market." "Let's acquire a company with a simpler architecture." "Let's focus on service providers." Every one of them was elegant. Every one of them was delivered by someone I respected. Every one of them was a way of not doing the actual work.

The issue was that we weren't facing a market problem. The customers were buying; they just weren't buying our product. This was not a time to pivot. So I said the same thing to every one of them: "There are no silver bullets for this, only lead bullets."

They did not want to hear that. But it made things clear: we had to build a better product. There was no other way out. No window, no hole, no escape hatch, no backdoor. We had to go through the front door and deal with the big, ugly guy blocking it.

After nine months of hard work on an extremely rugged product cycle, we regained our product lead and eventually sold Opsware to Hewlett-Packard for $1.65 billion. Without the lead bullets, I suspect we would have ended at about one-tenth that value.

That is the framework. If customers are choosing a competitor's product over yours, the problem is that your product is not good enough. Not your pricing. Not your positioning. Not your segment. Not your brand. The product. And the answer is not a silver bullet. There are no silver bullets. There are only lead bullets, which is to say: build a better product.

Every competitive fight I have ever seen come back to this. Every single one. And every single one has been surrounded by smart people, sincere people, people I respected, proposing elegant strategic maneuvers that would let the company avoid the front door.

Here is the taxonomy of silver bullets. Learn them. You will be offered every one of these, by someone you trust, in the next six months.

**The Downmarket Play.** "Our product is too expensive and complex for the current segment. Let's build a simpler version for smaller customers." Translation: we can't beat the competitor in our core market, so we'll move to a market where the competitor isn't. This is almost always avoidance. The smaller segment won't support your cost structure, the margins are worse, and your best sales people — the ones who can sell into enterprise — will quit because the new deals are too small to work on.

**The Acquisition.** "Let's buy company X — their architecture is exactly what we need." Translation: we can't build it, so let's buy it. The problem: you are now integrating someone else's codebase while still fighting the competitor, and integration will take a year, and during that year the competitor will extend their lead, and when you're done you'll have two products that don't quite work together. This is occasionally the right move — but only when the acquired technology is genuinely core and you have integration capacity. Almost never is it the actual answer to a head-to-head product loss.

**The Pivot.** "We're going to reposition around a new category — let's call it AI-native or cloud-first or platform-as-a-service." Translation: the product is getting beaten, so we'll change the story. This works, briefly, on new prospects who haven't seen the product yet. It does not work on the deals you're currently losing. The competitor will keep eating those deals while you're at an offsite designing the new brand.

**The Niche.** "Let's focus on service providers / public sector / healthcare vertical." Translation: the general market is brutal, so let's find a corner where the competitor is weak. This sometimes works — some companies win by vertical — but almost always it's avoidance, because the vertical is smaller, the deal cycles are longer, and if you can't win in the general market you usually can't win in a vertical either.

Every one of these has the same shape: **avoid the front door.** The front door has a big ugly guy standing in front of it whose name is "build a better product." Every smart person on your team will come to you with a silver bullet idea — mine did — and every one of them is a way of not doing the actual work.

Here's the test. If we walked into a deal next week against this competitor, pulled up both products on the screen, and did a side-by-side demo of the thing customers actually care about — would we win? If the answer is no, none of the silver bullets fix that. The downmarket version would lose the same demo. The acquired company's product would lose the same demo. The repositioned brand would lose the same demo. The only thing that wins the demo is a product that wins the demo.

There is a deeper question underneath this, and it is the one most founders don't want to ask themselves. When you are reaching for silver bullets, you are really asking: **can we avoid being good enough to win?** And the honest answer is no. You cannot. There is no such thing as a successful company whose product isn't good enough to win. So the question becomes:

*"If our company isn't good enough to win, then do we need to exist at all?"*

That is the question that breaks founders or remakes them. Once you accept that the only answer is to build a better product, everything changes. You stop looking for the escape hatch. You start the nine months. You concentrate the engineering organization on the specific features you are losing on. You talk to the last ten customers who bought the competitor and you find the three reasons you lost. You kill the projects that aren't on that critical path. You put your best people on it. You hold the line. And nine months later you have a product that wins the demo.

That is a lead bullet. It's grueling. It's unromantic. It doesn't make for a good strategy offsite. But it is the only thing that has ever worked.

## How to Apply

1. **Talk to the last ten customers who chose the competitor.** Not five. Ten. Find the specific features, workflows, or capabilities that made them choose. Write down the pattern. If you can't identify the specific product gap, you haven't listened long enough.

2. **Be honest about whether this is a product problem.** The tell: customers are buying — they are just buying the competitor. The market exists. The budget exists. The buying process works. Your product is losing the product decision. That is a product problem.

3. **Reject the silver bullets, by name, out loud.** When someone on your team proposes a downmarket play, an acquisition, a pivot, or a vertical focus as the answer to a head-to-head product loss, name it: "That's a silver bullet. There are no silver bullets here." Do it publicly enough that the team internalizes it and stops bringing you new ones.

4. **Concentrate the product organization on the critical gaps.** Not all of them. The two or three that decide most deals. Kill the projects that aren't on that critical path. Every engineer-month spent on something else is a month your competitor keeps eating deals.

5. **Commit to the nine months.** Lead bullets take time. Opsware took nine months against BladeLogic. Netscape took longer against IIS. Tell the board. Tell the company. Set the expectation that there is a grueling product cycle ahead and there is no shorter path.

6. **Put yourself on the front line.** Lead bullets require a wartime posture. The CEO needs to be in the product reviews, talking to the customers who switched, sitting with the engineers on the critical features. This is not a delegation moment.

7. **Watch for the smartest people on the team bringing you silver bullets.** They are not stupid. They are avoiding the fight, gently. They are telling you, in the most reasonable voice, that they don't want to go nine months against the front-door guy. Your job is to lead them into the fight, not to accept their escape plan.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series B founder tells you his sales team is losing 65% of head-to-head deals to a better-funded competitor. His VP Product has proposed pivoting the company to focus on "AI-native observability" as a repositioning. His VP Sales has proposed going downmarket to mid-market customers. His CTO has proposed acquiring a smaller competitor for their architecture.

**Application:** All three proposals are silver bullets. The repositioning doesn't change the fact that in every side-by-side the competitor's product wins on specific features. The downmarket play concedes the enterprise market where the money actually is. The acquisition is integration risk on top of a product problem. The right answer is none of the above. The answer is: talk to the last ten deals, identify the three features that decided them, concentrate engineering on those features, commit to a nine-month product cycle, and send the VP Sales back out to start pipeline building for when the product is ready.

**Result:** If the founder has the stomach for nine months of grueling product work, the company has a shot at winning the deals back. If he takes any of the silver bullets, the company will still be losing deals in nine months — just in a different market, with a different story, or with a half-integrated acquired product making things worse.

---

**Situation:** A founder's product is being beaten in enterprise deals by an incumbent with 10x the engineering team. She is considering positioning the product around a new category — "workflow-native AI" — to reframe the comparison and avoid the feature-for-feature fight.

**Application:** Repositioning is a story problem, not a product problem. It can be the right move when customers are comparing you to the wrong category — when positioning is genuinely mismatched and fixing the story fixes the loss rate. It is the wrong move when customers are comparing you correctly, see both products, and choose the competitor because the competitor's product works better. Test the distinction: is the deal being lost at the positioning stage (before demo) or at the product evaluation stage (after demo)? If after demo, it's lead bullets. The new category name won't change the demo.

**Result:** If the distinction is honest, the founder either ships a better product in the current category or repositions into a category where the product genuinely is differentiated. Either way, the answer starts with the truthful diagnosis of where the deal is actually being lost.

---

**Situation:** A founder tells you his product is being beaten in deals and his board is pushing him to sell the company to one of the bigger players. He's considering it because "the fight is too hard."

**Application:** Before any of that: can you build a product that wins? If yes, the sale is premature. If no, the sale is rational. But the honest form of the question is not "is the fight too hard?" It's: "Is the company good enough to exist?" If it is — build a better product. If it isn't — selling is the honest move. The fight-is-too-hard framing is the silver bullet version of selling, and it produces bad outcomes because you sell at a bad price in a moment of weakness. Sell because the math works, not because you're tired.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let the smartest person on your team talk you into a pivot.
**Why:** The smartest people are the ones who bring the most plausible silver bullets. They construct elegant arguments. They have real data. They are not lying. They are avoiding the fight, and they've convinced themselves it's strategy. Your job is to notice the pattern: when your best person is bringing you a non-product answer to a product problem, something is off. Listen, then say no, and then go build the product.

**Don't:** Reframe a product loss as a positioning loss because it's easier to fix positioning.
**Why:** Positioning work is real work, but it doesn't fix a demo that loses. If you've honestly tested the positioning and customers still choose the competitor in head-to-head product evaluation, the problem is the product. Reframing it as positioning is a way of sending the problem to the marketing team so engineering doesn't have to carry it. It doesn't work.

**Don't:** Commit to the nine months without telling the company it's going to take nine months.
**Why:** If the company doesn't know you're in a lead-bullets phase, every miss along the way reads as failure. Attrition spikes. The board gets nervous. The sales team gives up. Tell the truth: we're in a multi-quarter product cycle, here is what we're building, here is why we will win when it ships, and here is what I need from each of you between now and then. Lead bullets require the company to hold the line with you, and the company can only hold the line if they know the plan.

**Don't:** Convince yourself the competitor is winning on marketing.
**Why:** This is the single most common self-deception in a losing product fight. The competitor has more awareness, so the losses feel like a funnel problem. But losses in the evaluation stage — where customers have seen both products — are product problems. Marketing can put you on the shortlist. It cannot win the bake-off. If the bake-off is where you're losing, lead bullets.

**Don't:** Use "strategic focus" as cover for product avoidance.
**Why:** Choosing a narrower segment is sometimes the right move. But the test is whether the segment choice is driven by your actual differentiated product strength, or by the desire to stop competing against the scary competitor. If the segment plan amounts to "the competitor doesn't care about this corner," you are retreating, not focusing. Retreats are rarely profitable and usually humiliating.
