# Culture Is What You Do

## When to Use
- You've put "Integrity," "Excellence," and "Teamwork" on a poster in the lobby and you're wondering why the actual company doesn't look like the poster.
- A new hire just behaved in a way you cannot believe — broke a rule you thought was obvious, treated a partner in a way you would never treat a partner, sandbagged a peer in a way your "values" explicitly forbid.
- You're designing your culture for the first time and your instinct is to write down what you *believe*. Stop. The question is what you *do*.
- An executive on your team is routinely violating your stated culture and nothing has happened. The culture just changed. The only question is whether you noticed.

## Fails When
- **The problem is a single bad employee, not a system.** Fire the employee. If the behavior is systemic — if more than one person is doing it and nobody stopped them — it's culture.
- **The founder wants a values poster, not a culture.** If the conversation is about what's on the wall, stop having it and go have the conversation about what's in the hallway.
- **The company is in true existential wartime.** Culture gets defined by the war when you're fighting for survival. Survive first, then design. Route to peacetime-wartime-ceo.

## Core Concept

When I was CEO of Loudcloud, I figured our culture would be a reflection of my values, behaviors, and personality. So I focused all my energy on "leading by example." I was transparent, I worked hard, I treated people with respect, and I expected the same in return. To my bewilderment and horror, that method did not scale.

Past a hundred people I started noticing things that made no sense. One engineering manager screamed at his team. Another never gave feedback at all — people on his team had no idea whether they were doing a good job or a terrible one. A third manager didn't return emails for days. And a fourth had developed a habit of taking credit for his team's work in front of the executives while deflecting blame downward.

None of these people were imitating me. They were doing whatever came naturally to them, and since I hadn't defined what the culture actually required of them, "natural" was all they had. I had assumed that culture was about who I was. It turns out culture is about what everybody does. And if you don't tell them what to do — really tell them, in ways they can't ignore — they'll make it up as they go. Two-thirds of what they make up will be accidental, and the rest will be a mistake.

That's the hardest lesson I ever learned about culture. It's not what you tweet. It's not what you believe. It's not the values on the wall. *It's what you do. That's who you are.*

Let me be precise about what culture is, because most people get this wrong. Culture is not yoga at lunch. Culture is not dogs at work. Culture is not free kombucha or a ping-pong table in the break room. Those are perks. Perks buy comfort. **Culture drives behavior.**

Culture is the set of assumptions your employees use to resolve the problems they face every day. It's how they behave when no one is looking. It's whether they return phone calls on time or let them slide. It's whether they tell you the truth about a slipping deadline or dress it up with creative explanations. It's whether they optimize for the partnership or the price.

Here's another way to say it: **culture is how your company makes decisions when you're not there.** And since you can't be everywhere — since 99% of the decisions in your company are made without you in the room — culture is arguably the most important thing you build.

The samurai understood the distinction that most modern corporations miss. They didn't have values. They had virtues. *A value is merely a belief, but a virtue is a belief that you actively pursue or embody.* The difference is everything. A value lives in your head. A virtue lives in your actions. The Bushido code wasn't a poster on the dojo wall — it was a detailed set of behavioral expectations, edge cases, and stories that told you exactly what to do in every situation you might face. They paired virtues in systems that checked each other: politeness balanced by veracity prevented fakeness; honor balanced by politeness prevented random violence. They stamped them deep with vivid, unforgettable stories about samurai who lived and died by the code.

The reason so many corporate values programs are useless is that they emphasize beliefs instead of actions. Culturally, what you believe means nearly nothing. What you do is who you are.

Test: can someone be caught performing your virtue? If not, it's a value, which is to say it's nothing. "We value integrity" is wallpaper. "Every deal over $100K gets a three-person compliance review, no exceptions, and the reviewers have veto power over sales" is a virtue — somebody can be caught doing it, and somebody can be caught not doing it, and in either case the culture just got programmed.

Values on the wall that are not lived are actively worse than no values at all. They teach cynicism. Your employees can see the gap between what's on the poster and what actually happens. Every time they see a manager get rewarded for cutting corners while "Integrity" hangs above the conference room, they learn that the real culture is: say one thing, do another. Which is itself a culture — just not the one you wanted.

Toussaint Louverture understood this better than any modern CEO. He inherited a slave culture in Saint-Domingue — a culture designed to crush ambition, destroy trust, and make people dependent. He didn't give speeches about freedom. He didn't put "Dignity" on a wall. He transformed an army of former slaves into a fighting force that defeated the armies of Spain, England, and France by changing what they *did* every day: how they dressed, how they trained, how they treated their word, how they treated the former plantation owners, how they handled money, how they handled each other's wives. The culture changed because the actions changed. The beliefs followed.

Shaka Senghor learned the same thing inside a maximum-security prison, running a prison gang with a written code. He realized the code on the page wasn't producing the behavior the code described — the gang was stabbing people, selling drugs, destroying its own members. He couldn't fix it by rewriting the code. He had to transform himself first, in public, visibly, and let his changed actions pull the culture behind him. The walk almost always wins over the talk.

There is a saying in the military: if you see something below standard and do nothing, then you've set a new standard. That's culture in one sentence. Every day, in a thousand small interactions, your culture is either being reinforced or eroded. Every time a manager behaves in a way that contradicts the culture and nobody says anything, the culture just changed. The old standard died. A new standard was born. You can't set culture and walk away. The target is always moving.

One more thing. Culture travels. The behaviors you tolerate inside the walls of your company will eventually define how your company behaves outside those walls. The way you treat that partner will eventually be the way your employees treat each other. If you build a ruthlessly competitive external culture and you think it's going to stay neatly outside your internal relations, you're wrong. Cultural behaviors, once absorbed, get deployed everywhere. Ask Uber.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop asking "what do we believe?" and start asking "what do we do?"** Write down the five behaviors that, if everyone in the company did them every day, would most change your trajectory. That's the draft of your culture. Not beliefs. Behaviors.

2. **Go around your managers and ask new employees what the culture actually is.** Not after a year — after their first week. What behaviors do they perceive will help them fit in, survive, and succeed? What's different from other places they've worked — not just better, but worse? What practices made them wary? New hires are the best culture sensors you have, because they haven't yet learned to filter their observations through the lens of organizational politics. Their unvarnished impressions are your closest approximation to the truth. Your managers will tell you the culture they wish you had. Your new hires will tell you the one you've got.

3. **Pick virtues you're willing to be tested on.** Before you put a virtue into your company, ask yourself: "Am I willing to pass the test on this?" If the virtue is "we tell customers the truth even when it costs us a deal," are you willing to lose a million-dollar deal next Tuesday for it? If not, pick a different virtue. Don't ever put up a virtue you're not willing to be personally caught performing, publicly, at cost.

4. **When you see something below standard, say something.** Every time. If a manager's team is shipping garbage and everyone pretends it's fine, the culture just shifted. If an executive treats a junior engineer like a servant in a meeting and nobody corrects it, the culture just shifted. Culture is not what you announce at the all-hands. It's what you enforce in the hallway.

5. **When you violate your own culture — and you will — admit it publicly and overcorrect.** The admission and the self-correction have to be public enough and vehement enough to erase the earlier decision and become the new lesson. I pivoted Loudcloud in secret once and it broke our transparency virtue. I had to hold an off-site — the Dream Inn — tell the whole company what I had done, own it, and commit out loud to not doing it again. That off-site is what saved the culture I'd violated.

## Examples

**Situation:** You're a 150-person company. You have "Customer Obsession" on your values page. A deal is slipping at the end of the quarter. Your Head of Sales tells a customer their integration will be "ready in two weeks" when you both know it's more like eight. The deal closes. Your Head of Sales gets a big commission check. Everyone celebrates.

**Application:** Your culture is now: we lie to customers at quarter end. Not because you wrote that on a poster. Because the first time it happened in front of everyone, you celebrated it. Every sales rep in the company just learned that the real culture beats the stated one. You have three moves. First, you have to publicly unwind the incentive — that commission check, the celebration email, everything. Second, you have to tell the customer the truth, right now, and take the hit. Third — and this is the one that actually programs the culture — you have to make a decision everyone can see that demonstrates the priority. Maybe that's telling the sales team that any rep who misrepresents a timeline gets zero commission on the deal. Maybe it's putting a customer-success veto on sales commits. The point is: say it in actions everyone can watch.

**Result:** You'll lose a quarter of revenue. You'll also lose two or three sales reps who don't like the new rules. Those are the reps you didn't want anyway. The ones who stay will be the ones who can win deals without lying, and your customer base will start to behave differently because they trust what you say.

---

**Situation:** You're the founder of a 60-person startup. You've been "leading by example" for three years and the company has always felt like a family. Now you've hired an SVP of Engineering from a big tech company, and within two months he's screaming at his team, refusing to give written feedback, and telling new engineers that the standups are optional for senior people.

**Application:** Don't tell him to "get more aligned with the culture." That's a speech and speeches don't work here. Sit him down and tell him the specific behaviors that have to change — not the vibe, the behaviors. Then design a mechanism. If written feedback is a virtue, make it a rule: nobody on his team gets a raise, bonus, or option grant until he's completed all his written performance reviews. I did this at Opsware and we got 100% compliance. If you want attendance at standups, make the rule: if the VP isn't at the standup, the whole team takes the morning off — and announce it. Now every engineer has a stake in the VP showing up.

**Result:** Either he starts doing the actions, which becomes the new culture, or he can't do them, which means he's the wrong hire and you fix that. Either way the culture wins. What you don't do is give a speech about culture and hope.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write your values first and try to retrofit behaviors to them.
**Why:** It produces wallpaper. Values that describe abstract beliefs — "Integrity," "Innovation," "Excellence" — tell nobody what to do on Tuesday morning. Start with the Tuesday-morning behavior you want and work backward into the virtue. If your virtue cannot be turned into a specific action that somebody could be caught performing or not performing, it's a value. Throw it out.

**Don't:** Delegate culture to Head of People or a "culture committee."
**Why:** Culture starts with the CEO and lives in the CEO's decisions, actions, and reactions. The moment you delegate it, you've told the organization it's not important. Head of People can help you operationalize it. They cannot create it and you cannot hire it out.

**Don't:** Treat perks as culture.
**Why:** Dogs at work, yoga, organic food — those are nice. They are not culture. They will not tell an engineer at 11 p.m. on a Friday whether to ship a feature she isn't sure about, or tell a salesperson whether to fudge a ship date to close a deal. Perks buy comfort. Culture drives behavior. Do not confuse them.

**Don't:** Ignore a cultural violation because the violator is a star performer.
**Why:** Every other employee is watching. The moment you allow your top salesperson to belittle a peer in a meeting and say nothing, you've told the company that hitting your number buys you out of the virtue. The star will do it again. The people who watched will do it too. Within a year, the virtue is dead and you're the one who killed it. If you're not willing to hold your best people to the culture, you don't have a culture. You have a top-of-the-funnel loyalty program.
