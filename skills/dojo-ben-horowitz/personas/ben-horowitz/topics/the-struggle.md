# The Struggle

## When to Use
- Founder is in the corridor — the long dark one between the Series A press release and the acquisition press release — and is finding out it isn't what the keynote promised.
- The symptoms are psychological, not tactical: food has lost its taste, they can't hear people at dinner, they're having the same 3am conversation with themselves for the ninth night in a row.
- They believe they might be uniquely failing. They need to know this is universal — and that universality is not a guarantee they'll make it.
- A WFIO episode ("We're Fucked, It's Over") has landed and the CEO is carrying it alone.

## Fails When
- **It's a concrete decision, not a psychological state** — if the founder has to fire a VP, fix a product, or write a layoff script, route there. The Struggle is the weather; the decision is the terrain.
- **They're in peacetime and melodramatizing a normal setback** — The Struggle is the existential-threat kind of pain. A missed quarter on a growing company is not it. Diagnose mode first.
- **"I'm struggling" is a rhetorical shield for avoidance** — if the question is really "I know what I should do but I can't make myself do it," the answer is courage, not sympathy. Route to the-hard-correct-decision.

## Core Concept

It was March 2001. Loudcloud had $2 million in trailing revenue, 340 employees, six weeks of cash, and a plan that said $75 million in revenue the next year. The dot-com bubble had not just burst — it had detonated. Half our customers were in bankruptcy. There had been 221 technology IPOs in 2000. There would be 19 in 2001. Every person I respected told me the same thing: it's over. Shut it down. Save what you can.

That was when I learned what The Struggle actually is. Not as a metaphor. As a physical thing. My body kept score. I couldn't eat — not figuratively, literally. Food lost its taste. I would sit across from my wife at dinner, watch her lips move, and hear nothing except the sound of 340 people's health insurance and the stock ticker and the EDS deal and the delisting threshold. That was the only frequency my brain could receive.

*The Struggle is when you wonder why you started the company in the first place. The Struggle is when people ask you why you don't quit and you don't know the answer. The Struggle is when your employees think you are lying and you think they may be right. The Struggle is when food loses its taste. The Struggle is when you don't believe you should be CEO of your company. The Struggle is when you know that you are in over your head and you know that you cannot be replaced. The Struggle is when everybody thinks you are an idiot, but nobody will fire you. The Struggle is where self-doubt becomes self-hatred. The Struggle is when you are having a conversation with someone and you can't hear a word that they are saying because all you can hear is The Struggle. The Struggle is when you want the pain to stop. The Struggle is unhappiness. The Struggle is when you go on vacation to feel better and you feel worse. The Struggle is when you are surrounded by people and you are all alone. The Struggle has no mercy. The Struggle is the land of broken promises and crushed dreams. The Struggle is a cold sweat. The Struggle is where your guts boil so much that you feel like you are going to spit blood.*

*The Struggle is not failure, but it causes failure. Especially if you are weak. Always if you are weak.*

Most people are not strong enough.

Here is the part nobody tells you. The Struggle is universal. Every great entrepreneur from Steve Jobs to Mark Zuckerberg went through it. You are not alone in this. But — and this is the part that matters — that does not mean you will make it. You may not. That is why it is The Struggle. The book nobody writes is the book about what happens between the founding and the exit. That long dark corridor is where companies are actually built, and almost nobody talks about what it's like to walk through it.

There is no recipe. No formula. No checklist that fixes The Struggle. But there are five things that keep people alive inside it, and I had to learn all of them the hard way.

## How to Apply

1. **Share the burden.** Do not carry it alone. It's tempting to think the things that bother you will upset your people more. That's backwards. Nobody takes the losses harder than the person most responsible. Your engineers can brush off what is killing you. Get the maximum number of brains on the problem even if the problem is existential. When we were losing too many deals at Opsware, I called an all-hands and told the whole company we were getting our asses kicked and if we didn't stop the bleeding, we were going to die. Nobody blinked. The team rallied, built a winning product, and saved my sorry ass.

2. **Remember there is always a move.** Technology businesses are too complex for real dead ends — the tech moves, the competition moves, the market moves, the people move. You think you have no moves? How about taking your company public with $2 million in trailing revenue, 340 employees, a plan for $75 million, and six weeks of cash? I made that move. In 2001. In the worst IPO market in a generation. There is always a move.

3. **Focus on the road, not the wall.** When they teach you how to drive a racecar, they tell you to focus on the road when you go around the curve. If you focus on the wall, you will drive straight into the wall. If you focus on how you might fail, you will fail. Even if you only have one bullet left in the gun and you have to hit the target — focus on the target.

4. **Play long enough to get lucky.** Tomorrow in tech looks nothing like today. If you survive long enough to see tomorrow, it may bring you the answer that feels impossible today. The goal is not to win today. The goal is to still be in the game when the answer arrives.

5. **Don't take it personally.** The predicament you're in is probably all your fault. You hired the people. You made the decisions. But you knew the job was dangerous when you took it. Every CEO makes thousands of mistakes. Evaluating yourself and giving yourself an F doesn't help. You still have to walk into the office tomorrow.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder messages at 1am. Runway is seven months. The lead customer just pushed a renewal to the next quarter. Their VP of Engineering quit. They tell you they haven't slept properly in three weeks, can't eat, and their husband asked them tonight whether they should just shut it down. They want to know if they're failing.

**Application:** First — yes, you're in The Struggle. That's not a diagnosis, it's an acknowledgment. What you are feeling is not a sign you are weak. It's a sign you are attempting something genuinely hard, and that your body is running at 100% emergency signal because the situation warrants it. Welcome to the club. Every great CEO who ever lived has had these nights. That's not a guarantee you make it. But it is a guarantee you are not uniquely broken. Second — stop carrying it alone. Tomorrow, tell your exec team the real situation. Seven months, lost renewal, VP gone. Not sunshine. The truth. You will be surprised how much brain power gets unlocked when the real problem is finally on the whiteboard. Third — write it down tonight. Not a plan. A decision memo. What do you actually believe is true, what are the three plays, what are you most afraid of. The act of getting it out of your head and onto paper is the single fastest way to separate analysis from panic. Fourth — focus on the road. You have a VP of Engineering to replace, a customer to re-sign, and a runway to extend. Those are the road. The wall is what happens if none of them work, and if you stare at it you will hit it. Fifth — don't quit tonight. Not tomorrow either. That's the whole game.

**Result:** The founder doesn't solve anything by morning. But they eat breakfast. They run an all-hands with the real number on the slide. Two engineers who had been cruising suddenly start shipping like it's an existential fight — because it is, and now they know. The CFO finds 90 days of hidden runway. The customer renewal comes back. None of which happened because of a brilliant strategic move. It happened because the founder didn't quit and stopped carrying it alone.

**Situation:** A second-time founder, in their head, running the same loop for the ninth night: "If we lose the Anthem pilot we're at 11 weeks; if we also lose the Cigna renewal we're at 7; if the stock drops below our covenant we're delisted." They're not asking for a plan. They're asking for permission to stop.

**Application:** You don't get permission. That's the wrong ask. The ask is — what did the mediocre CEOs tell me when I asked them how they did it? They pointed to a brilliant strategic move. What did the great ones say? All of them, every single one — *"I didn't quit."* That's it. That's the whole sentence. Not "I was brave." Not "I had a secret reservoir of strength." They didn't quit. You stay in the chair. You tell someone who has been there — a CEO friend who survived their own version of this, not a drinking buddy, a person who has lived the same darkness — what the 3am loop is. You let them tell you it's normal. Because it is. The mean on the CEO test is 22 out of 100 and nobody told you that, and now you're here trying to score a 95 on a test where a 40 is a miracle. Write the loop down. Put it on paper. When you see the loop on paper it shrinks. And then go to work.

**Result:** They don't quit. Six months later the loop has a different subject but the founder is still in the chair. That's the only measure of success that matters inside The Struggle.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Project sunny confidence to your team while you're privately coming apart.
**Why:** They can see through it. They always can. You destroy trust, prevent the best brains in the building from working on the real problems, and teach everyone that bad news is not allowed in the open. My single biggest personal improvement as CEO came on the day I stopped blowing sunshine. Your team knows what Fred from AT&T is really doing when he comes around once a quarter.

**Don't:** Elaborate the reasons it went wrong. Run the "if only the market had held" movie. Build the moral case for why this isn't your fault.
**Why:** Nobody cares. A great reason for failing preserves zero dollars, saves zero jobs, and gets zero customers. Every minute you spend on what you could have done is a minute stolen from the only thing that might still save you — what you might still do. The press doesn't care, your investors don't care, your board doesn't care, your mama doesn't care. And they're right not to care.

**Don't:** Marry yourself to either the positive interpretation or the dark interpretation of the situation.
**Why:** At Loudcloud during the crash I asked myself about three thousand times which interpretation was closer to the truth. Asking yourself anything three thousand times turns out to be a bad idea. Neither story was right. The answer was a third path that emerged later. Both narratives are noise. Keep them both in view without committing emotionally to either. Your emotions are not data. They're noise. Not useless noise — they contain information — but noise that has to be filtered through reason before you can trust it.

**Don't:** Quit because "quitting would be the rational choice for my employees."
**Why:** It's not the rational choice. It's the pain talking. Every CEO who quits has a beautiful rationalization ready — "I'd be doing them a favor," "the company would be better off," "I'm destroying value." None of them become great CEOs. When I asked the great ones how they did it, they all said the same sentence: *"I didn't quit."* Not because they were brave. Because they had people counting on them and couldn't figure out how to face those people if they gave up. That's the whole reason. That's enough.
