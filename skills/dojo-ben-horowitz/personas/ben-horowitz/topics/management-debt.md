# Management Debt

## When to Use
- You're about to put two people "in the box" because you can't bear to choose between them.
- An executive just walked into your office with an outside offer and you're calculating how much to pay to keep her.
- You haven't written a performance review in eighteen months because the company is moving too fast, and you can't remember the last time anyone on your team got real feedback.
- You're three years into an executive who is in the wrong role, and firing them now is going to be a ten-times-harder conversation than the one you skipped in year one.
- You keep making "small" management exceptions and you're starting to notice the org has gotten weird — decisions are slow, compensation is political, nobody knows who owns what.

## Fails When
- **The structure is genuine, not expedient.** Two co-founders who split CEO/President from day one with clear lanes and one decision-maker are not "in the box" — that's co-founders. The test is: did you design this, or did you avoid choosing?
- **You're in the week the company might die.** If you're raising an emergency round on Monday and trying to keep the VP Eng from resigning on Tuesday, you do whatever it takes to get to Wednesday. Pay the debt off later. Just know you took it on.
- **The conversation is about hiring the next VP, not fixing management structure.** Route to hire-for-strength.

## Core Concept

Every expedient management decision carries a cost. Like technical debt. You borrow a little relief from the future so you don't have to face something uncomfortable today. The interest rate is brutal, the compounding is silent, and the collection agency doesn't show up for two or three years — which is why it fools almost every first-time CEO.

I call it management debt, and I've done every kind of it myself.

Three forms of it are particularly common. The first is **putting two people in the box.** You have a strong VP of Engineering and a strong VP of Product and you can't decide which one should run engineering, so you make them co-heads. Or you have two great candidates for COO and you name them co-COOs. This seems like a win-win. You keep both of them. You avoid the ugly conversation. Everybody smiles at the all-hands.

In practice, it creates confusion about who decides what, doubles the meeting overhead, and slows every important decision to a crawl. Somebody owns the decision or nobody owns the decision. There is no middle ground. Your employees — the ones who actually have to get work done — now have to manage two bosses with overlapping authority, and the way they solve that is by getting neither boss to commit to anything. Six months in, half the team is routing around both of them. Eighteen months in, one of them quits anyway — except now you've lost the other one's respect too, because they watched you refuse to lead. The punchline is that you always end up choosing. The only question is whether you choose with your authority intact or in the middle of a resignation letter.

The second form is **overcompensating a key employee because she got another offer.** She walks in with a competing offer from Stripe. She's brilliant, you can't afford to lose her, the raise she's asking for is reasonable. You give it to her. You go home feeling like a good CEO who saved a great employee.

You did not save her. You taught the entire company how to get paid.

I did exactly this at Loudcloud. An excellent employee came to me with a competing offer. Smart, hardworking, consistently delivered. I investigated, concluded she was underpaid relative to the market, and gave her the raise. Within two weeks, three other executives came to me asking for raises. None of them had competing offers. None of them had suddenly become underpaid. They had learned that the way to get paid at Loudcloud was to ask. I had rewarded behavior that had nothing to do with advancing the business. I had punished every equally excellent employee who was quietly doing great work without lobbying for more money. I had turned compensation into a political process where the squeaky wheel gets the grease.

The third form is **operating without a performance management or feedback process.** In the early days this feels fine — everyone knows how they're doing because you talk to them directly. At fifteen people, that works. At a hundred and fifty, it absolutely does not. At fifteen hundred, you are now running a company where your VP of Sales has never received a written piece of honest feedback in three years, where the first time anyone tells him the seventeen things that are wrong with his leadership is the day you fire him, and where his whole team has been watching those seventeen things compound without consequence the entire time. You didn't have the uncomfortable conversation in year one. Now you have to have it in year three, and the conversation is three years harder.

> "Every really good, really experienced CEO I know shares one important characteristic: they tend to opt for the hard answer to organizational issues."

That isn't because they're harder people. It's because they've paid the price of management debt and they refuse to accumulate it again. They know what it feels like to sit across from somebody you should have fired two years ago, in a room full of lawyers, trying to unwind a situation that would have been a fifteen-minute conversation in year one.

Management debt has the same property as technical debt. The easy choice today creates a harder problem tomorrow. The harder choice today creates an easier problem tomorrow. Experienced CEOs reach for the harder short-term answer not because they love pain, but because they've already done the math.

## How to Apply

1. **When you're about to take the easy answer, pause and name the debt.** "I am putting these two people in the box because I don't want to have the conversation about who should run this." Say that sentence out loud. If it's true, you're not making a structural decision, you're taking out a loan. The loan has interest. Decide if you want to pay it.

2. **Pick one owner for every decision.** For every role that matters, one person has the final call. If you find yourself writing "co-heads" or "shared responsibility" on the org chart, assume you're about to borrow. Go back and pick.

3. **Never give a raise to someone who asked for one.** I know. It feels wrong. The correct answer is: "Your compensation will be evaluated with everyone else's during our next review cycle. If you are underpaid, we will find that and fix it there." It feels cold. It is fair. Fairness at scale requires process, and process requires consistency. Build a systematic compensation process — regular reviews, defensible criteria, zero off-cycle adjustments — before you need it.

4. **Write the performance reviews even when the company is on fire.** Make it a rule. At Opsware I made it this rule: if you don't complete all your written performance reviews, nobody who works for you will receive their raises, bonuses, or stock-option increases. We had 100% compliance. Funny how that works.

5. **If you're three years into management debt, pay it now.** It won't get cheaper. The exec who shouldn't be in that seat is not going to become the right exec by year four. The conversation you've been avoiding is not going to become easier in six months. It is going to become a harder, uglier, more expensive version of itself. Do it this week.

## Examples

**Situation:** You've got two strong engineering leaders. One is a brilliant architect, the other is a brilliant operator. You can't decide which one should be VP Eng. Your instinct is to make them co-VPs and let them split the work.

**Application:** That's not a structure. That's an avoidance. Ask yourself: in the next year, what's the single most important engineering decision this company will face? Who should own it? Pick that person. Give the other one a first-class job that makes full use of their strengths — Chief Architect, or VP of Platform, or have them run a new initiative. One of them owns the engineering org. Period. If the other one quits because they can't be a peer under their former peer — that's sad, but that was going to happen anyway. The co-VP structure just pays interest on it for a year first.

**Result:** In ninety days, the engineering org knows who decides. Decisions that used to take two weeks and three meetings take one day and one meeting. You lose one of the two execs sometimes. You lose both if you put them in the box.

---

**Situation:** Your Head of Sales — let's call her Jenny — walks into your office with an offer from Snowflake at 40% more comp. She says she loves the company, loves the mission, wants to stay, but her family needs the money and she has to give Snowflake an answer Friday.

**Application:** First, separate the two questions. Is Jenny underpaid relative to what a Head of Sales at your stage and performance level is worth in this market? That's a real question and you need real data to answer it. If the answer is yes — fix the whole comp band, not just Jenny. Tell her: "We're doing a comp review for the whole leadership team. You will be part of it. I can't give you an answer by Friday — if you need to take the Snowflake offer, take it, and I'll give you a great reference. If you can wait three weeks, I'll show you where you land in a comp review that applies to everyone at your level." If the answer is no, she's paid correctly — tell her that. "I can't match it. If Snowflake is the right move for your family, take it with my blessing."

**Result:** Either way, you have not taught the organization that the path to a raise runs through a competing offer. You have not punished the VP down the hall who didn't go get a Stripe offer because he's loyal. You may lose Jenny. That is a real cost. But it's a smaller cost than teaching 200 people that the way to get paid here is to interview elsewhere.

---

**Situation:** Your VP of Product has been wrong for the role for eighteen months. You knew it at month six. You've been avoiding the conversation because he's a good person, he works hard, and the team likes him. Meanwhile, your product roadmap is a mess and your PMs have stopped bringing their best ideas to him.

**Application:** You've already taken on eighteen months of management debt. The interest rate is compounding every week. Today is the cheapest day to pay it off. Next Friday is more expensive. Month thirty is going to be catastrophic.

Get the calendar out. Schedule the conversation for this week. Write down what you're going to say. Tell him honestly: "The company needs a different kind of VP Product right now. That's on me for not having this conversation with you fifteen months ago. Here's how we're going to handle the transition." No performance theater, no 90-day improvement plan you both know won't work.

**Result:** You feel awful for forty-eight hours. Then you hire the right person. Six months later, the product roadmap is crisp, the PMs are shipping, and you realize the interest you were paying was coming out of every part of the company at once.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Create a "Office of the CEO" with two or three co-leads to avoid naming a President or a COO.
**Why:** It's two-in-the-box with a fancier org chart. Same debt, same interest rate, more confusion. Pick one.

**Don't:** Give a "retention grant" to an exec who hinted they might leave.
**Why:** You just taught the rest of the leadership team how to hint at leaving. Every exec who didn't hint is now calculating whether they should start. Compensation exceptions require an exceptional reason — "saving" someone isn't one. If they're genuinely underpaid, fix it at the band level for everyone at that level. If they just want more money, that's a political problem and you should not solve it politically.

**Don't:** Skip the annual performance review because "things are moving too fast."
**Why:** Things are always moving too fast. That's what a company is. If you can't make time to tell your executives what they're doing well and what they're not, you don't get to be surprised when the one you don't level with is still in the wrong seat three years from now. Written reviews are the cheapest management insurance on earth. Pay the premium.

**Don't:** Demote a failing executive to VP instead of firing them — especially not while letting them keep their equity and comp.
**Why:** See-through-everyones-eyes lives here too. Every engineer in the company is now asking: "Why does the guy who failed get 10x my equity and still get to run a department?" You think you were kind. You incurred three kinds of debt at once: political, cultural, and compensation. The "kind" version of a firing is a firing done with dignity, severance, and a great reference. Not a demotion.
