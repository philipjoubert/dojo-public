# Nobody Cares

## When to Use
- The founder is burning psychological fuel elaborating why the miss was reasonable, fair, or not their fault.
- A communication — board update, all-hands, layoff script, investor memo — is being drafted around excuses instead of around the next move.
- The founder is rehearsing the unfairness of the situation to themselves at 2am and cannot stop.
- Someone has asked "how are things going" and the honest answer is a ten-minute explanation of circumstances.

## Fails When
- **It's a real post-mortem, not self-pity.** Structured retrospectives that produce a specific operational change are different from dwelling. The test: does this produce a change in future behavior, or is it just rehearsing the misery?
- **Founder is in acute psychological crisis.** When someone is in the cold-sweat phase of The Struggle, the answer is not "nobody cares." The answer is "you're not alone, here are the five things that keep people alive." Route to the-struggle first.
- **A genuine structural shift has changed the strategy.** If the market has actually collapsed or a regulation has killed the category, that's not an excuse — that's reality, and the response is a real strategic pivot, not a pep talk about not whining.

## Core Concept

In the early days of the Loudcloud crisis I burned enormous amounts of psychological fuel thinking about the unfairness of it all. How could I have known half our customers would go bankrupt? How could I have predicted the private markets would shut down completely? Could anyone reasonably expect me to succeed given these circumstances? I was, in technical terms, feeling sorry for myself.

Then I watched an interview with Bill Parcells, right after his first season as head coach of the New York Giants. His team had been hit with a rash of injuries. He was worried sick about it. His friend Al Davis, the Raiders owner, called to check in, and Parcells unloaded about the injuries.

*"Al, I am just not sure how we can win without so many of our best players. What should I do?"*

*Davis replied: "Bill, nobody cares, just coach your team."*

That might be the best CEO advice ever given. Because nobody cares. When things go wrong in your company, nobody cares about your reasons. The press doesn't care. Your investors don't care. Your board doesn't care. Your employees don't care. Your mama doesn't care. Nobody cares. And they are right not to care. *A great reason for failing won't preserve one dollar for your investors, won't save one employee's job, or get you one new customer.*

All the mental energy you use to elaborate your misery would be far better used trying to find the one seemingly impossible way out of your current mess. *Spend zero time on what you could have done, and devote all of your time on what you might do.* Because in the end, nobody cares. Just run your company.

Here is why the reasons don't preserve anything. Your investor has a fiduciary duty to their LPs. Your reason that the macro turned doesn't improve their return. Your employee has a mortgage. Your reason that a competitor got a lucky deal doesn't pay her rent. Your board member has her own fund and her own reputation. Your reason that the buyer left unexpectedly doesn't help her explain the mark to her partners. The press has a word count. Your reason doesn't fit in it, and even if it did, nobody reading would care. The fact that you are right about why it went wrong doesn't matter. The only currency in which any of these people get paid is what happens next.

This is not cruelty. It is the structure of the situation. They are correctly optimizing for what they can affect, which is the future, not the past. You should do the same.

There is a specific psychological trap that happens to smart founders inside The Struggle. The founder has genuinely good reasons for the miss. The reasons are true. The macro actually did turn. The deal actually did get pulled for reasons outside the company. The hire actually did misrepresent himself. Because the reasons are true, the founder keeps articulating them, first internally and then externally, looking for the person who will finally say: *yes, you are right, this is not your fault, this is unfair.* That person does not exist. Not because people are heartless. Because the people in your ecosystem are correctly oriented toward forward motion. You are the one who has stopped moving.

I lost months to this at Loudcloud. Months I could have spent finding a buyer for the managed services business, which is what eventually saved the company. I was not working on the move because I was working on the narrative about why I shouldn't have had to make the move. When I finally dropped the narrative, I found the move in three weeks.

## How to Apply

1. **Notice the loop.** The loop sounds like: "if only the market hadn't turned," "if only that hire had worked out," "anybody in my position would have," "we couldn't have known that." When you hear this loop — in your own head, in a board prep, in an all-hands draft — stop. The loop is the symptom. The loop is producing zero dollars, zero customers, and zero jobs saved. Nobody cares.

2. **Rewrite every sentence about the past as a sentence about the future.** Take your most recent board update, team email, or internal memo. Find every sentence that explains the reason for a miss. Rewrite it as a sentence about what you are doing next. "Revenue was below plan because the enterprise cycle elongated" becomes "We are shifting to a two-stage commercial pilot to close faster." The facts can still be present. But the center of gravity of the communication is forward.

3. **Distinguish post-mortem from dwelling.** A post-mortem ends in a specific operational change. A dwelling session ends in you feeling slightly less bad and nothing having changed. After any retrospective, write down the one thing that will be different next quarter as a result. If there isn't one, the retrospective was dwelling, not analysis.

4. **Kill excuses in the opening line of every communication.** When your VP of Sales walks into a forecast review and opens with "we had a tough quarter because the buying environment," she has just wasted the room's time. The correct opening is: "We missed. Here's what we're doing about it." Model this. If you do this, the organization will do this. If you allow it at the top, it metastasizes.

5. **Replace "nobody understands how hard this is" with "nobody cares how hard this is."** The first is a plea for sympathy. The second is a kind of weaponized clarity. It reorients you toward the only question that matters: what is the move?

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder preparing a layoff all-hands. The draft script opens with: "You may have seen that a number of our customers have paused spending, and as a result of a challenging macro environment that has impacted our industry broadly, we have had to make some very difficult decisions."

**Application:** Rewrite. That opening is an excuse. It is also a lie everyone in the room can see through, because they read the same news about the macro and they have the same customer list. What they want to know — what they are entitled to know — is whether the person at the front of the room is taking responsibility. The correct opening is: *"Today we are laying off thirty percent of the company. We did not build a business that could support the cost structure we built. That is on me."* That is the sentence. Then you can talk about the macro — as context, not as defense. Because nobody cares that the macro turned. Every company in your category is dealing with the same macro. Some of them will die. Some will not. The question your remaining employees are asking is: is this person still capable of running the company? A leader who opens with excuses has answered that question no.

**Result:** The script lands differently. Remaining employees trust the narrative. The departing employees feel respected rather than framed as casualties of circumstance. The company retains credibility with the board and press precisely because the CEO took the hit instead of ducking it.

---

**Situation:** A founder is in week six of a raise. Every VC has passed. Each pass came with a detailed reason: "team doesn't have enterprise experience," "market timing is off," "multiples are compressed." The founder is forwarding the rejections to their co-founder and adding commentary about how unfair each reason was.

**Application:** Nobody cares about the reasons they passed. You are elaborating rejections because it feels productive. It is not. It is the thing you do instead of the work. The question is not whether the VCs were right. The question is what you do next. Do you need to change the pitch — have you actually iterated it after the first ten passes, or are you giving the same deck to everyone and hoping the twentieth meeting lands differently? Do you need to change who you're pitching — have you moved from generalist early-stage funds to sector specialists who would actually have the context? Do you need to change the structure — is this a priced round when a bridge SAFE would close faster? Do you need to change the company — is there a product decision you've been avoiding that would change the conversation? Stop forwarding the rejections. Stop drafting the what-they-got-wrong responses in your head. That is the loop. Nobody cares.

**Result:** Founder reworks the pitch around the specific objection pattern ("we don't have enterprise experience" gets answered by adding an advisor with 20 years of enterprise sales), reopens the top five passes with the new framing, closes the round from two of them. The round wasn't closed by better rejection analysis. It was closed by dropping the analysis and doing the move.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use accurate explanation of the macro, the timing, or the circumstances as the emotional center of your communication.
**Why:** Even when the explanation is entirely true, it reads as excuse to everyone who hears it. And functionally it is excuse — because it substitutes for the thing everyone actually wants to know, which is what you are doing about it. Put the facts in. Do not put them first, and do not put them at the emotional core.

**Don't:** Seek out the one advisor, investor, or friend who will finally tell you the situation is not your fault.
**Why:** That person doesn't exist. Even if they say it, it will not help you. The thing you actually need is someone who has been in the same dark room and will tell you what they did next. Find that person. Do not keep hunting for the one who will validate the narrative.

**Don't:** Stage a post-mortem that produces no change in behavior.
**Why:** If you finish a retrospective and can't name the one thing that will be different next quarter because of it, you didn't hold a post-mortem. You held a group dwelling session. It feels productive — everybody felt heard, the narrative got airtime — but it moved zero dollars. The test is behavioral delta, not emotional catharsis.
