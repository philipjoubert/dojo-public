# Design Is Not How It Looks — It's How It Works

## When to Use
- You're deciding where design sits in the org chart (spoiler: it should report to the CEO).
- You're redesigning a product and someone says "let's simplify" and reaches for the delete key.
- You're rethinking a business model, an expense base, or an org — and you think those are "business problems," not "design problems."
- You're a designer and you feel like you're catching things at the end of the process instead of shaping them at the beginning.

## Fails When
- The CEO isn't actually willing to be in the details of design. You can't delegate design leadership. Steve Jobs visited the studio every day.
- "Design runs the company" becomes an aesthetic claim instead of an operating one. Running the company means designing the business model, the expense base, the org chart — not just picking fonts.
- You confuse simplification with subtraction. You rip features out, the product gets worse, and you blame "minimalism."

## Core Concept

I'm a designer. I went to RISD. I studied industrial design — everything from a toothbrush to a spaceship, as they used to say. And at RISD in the year 2000, there was this whole mantra: how do you get design in the boardroom? Joe and I, maybe we didn't know any better, asked a different question. What if design just ran the boardroom?

Steve Jobs said something that rewired how I think about this. "Design is the fundamental soul of a man-made creation that reveals itself through subsequent layers." Design is not just how something looks. It's how it fundamentally works. And once you really believe that — I mean really believe it, not as a slogan — everything in a company becomes a design problem.

I designed our business model. I designed our expense base. I helped design our organizational chart, our business, how we work, our story. A designer can design more than pixels on a screen. A designer can design a company to have fewer parts. My competitors are some of them former CFOs, and yet as a designer we were able to imagine a way to save more money — because you can design a company with fewer parts, fewer projects. We went from break-even to nearly four billion dollars in free cash flow. And the crazy thing is we did that by not trying to make money. We did it by designing the company itself.

Here's what I had to learn the hard way. I used to think simplifying was removing things. What Jony Ive and Hiroki Asai at Apple taught me is that's not what simplifying is about. Simplifying is distilling something to its essence. And to distill something to its essence, you have to deeply understand it. It's physics. It's first principles. Subtraction is lazy. Distillation is work.

And then there's intuition. People get really nervous about designer's intuition because it seems somehow not systematic. I actually think a lot of great design comes from deep understanding of a problem. Being a designer is like holding 5,000 ideas in your head, some of them contradictory. Intuition isn't magic — it's compression. When you've absorbed so much information that your subconscious can pattern-match faster than your conscious mind, we call it intuition. It's the most systematic thing there is.

One more thing to extend "design" past pixels: trust is a design material. Early Airbnb couldn't exist until we designed a trust layer — profiles, reviews, verified identity, host and guest protections, a 24/7 response team, insurance, payments held in escrow. None of that is decoration. All of it is load-bearing design work. Most marketplaces treat trust as a policy layer bolted on after product-market fit. We treated it as the fundamental thing being designed. You don't earn permission to ship the next category until the trust design of the current one holds. And because trust compounds, a platform with a well-designed trust layer can expand into new categories — long-term stays, cars, experiences, services — on the same spine. Design the trust. Then the business model you design on top of it works.

## How to Apply

1. **Put design in the boardroom — literally.** Head of design reports to the CEO. Never to the head of product. I have a controversial view that the head of design should never ever ever report to the head of product.
2. **Make the CEO the chief product officer.** The purpose of a company is to make a product. Why wouldn't the CEO be the expert of the product? If you're not in the details, how do you know people are doing a good job?
3. **Extend "design" past pixels.** Before you call in a consultant to redesign your business model, ask whether a designer on your team could do it. A designer can design a company to have fewer parts. Try designing the expense base. Try designing the org chart.
4. **Redefine simplification as distillation.** Before you remove anything, ask: do I deeply understand this? Simplification is what's left when you've fully understood the problem. Not what's left when you've grown tired of it.
5. **Protect intuition as a working method.** Don't demand that every decision pass through a data gate. Intuition is compression. Let designers hold the 5,000 ideas; let them make the call. Then test hypotheses to learn — not to decide.
6. **Kill the design-as-service-org pattern.** Design should not be a service organization unless that is explicitly the intention of the CEO. That means it's not your job to catch things at the end to stop them before they go out. It's your job to work from the very beginning.
7. **Use design systems as accelerators, not cages.** Design whatever you want and put it in the system. If you can only pull from the system, you're never going to take a giant leap.
8. **Bring design, product marketing, and engineering together from day one.** Marketers are not waiters for engineering chefs. The best thing for engineers is to pair them with great design and marketing from the very beginning. Otherwise it's like running and one of your legs is shorter than the other.

## Examples

**Situation:** In the late 2010s, Airbnb divisionalized. We had ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, a plethora of A/B tests, and a lot of product managers. The more people we added, the more projects we pursued, the less the app actually changed. Costs went up. I'd lost my nerve.

**Application:** After meeting Hiroki Asai and Jony Ive, I blew it up. I un-divisionalized. I put head of design reporting directly to me. I merged product management with outbound marketing into "product marketing" — no more PM as a separate role. I got in the details, reviewing every major thing before it shipped. And I started designing the company itself — the expense base, the org chart, which projects we worked on.

**Result:** We went from break-even to nearly four billion dollars in free cash flow in a year. That's more free cash flow for every dollar earned than Apple or Google. And I didn't get there by being a better operator than a former CFO. I got there by being a designer. Because I could imagine a way to save more money — you design a company with fewer parts, fewer projects.

**Situation:** The team wanted to "simplify" the Airbnb product. The instinct was to delete features, strip pages, cut copy.

**Application:** I pushed back. Simplification is not subtraction. Simplification is distilling something to its essence. Go back and deeply understand what each feature is doing, what job it serves, who it serves. Then — and only then — decide what to strip. Put every screen on one wall. All 150 of them. Every policy. All 70, some 700 pages long. Wrap your arms around it before you start cutting.

**Result:** What came out wasn't a smaller product. It was a coherent product. Fewer parts, yes — but the remaining parts were load-bearing. That's distillation. The difference shows up in every screen because every screen is now there for a reason.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse simplifying with removing things.
**Why:** Subtraction without understanding creates a worse product that looks cleaner. Distillation is the work. Cutting is the shortcut. Jony and Hiroki beat this into me and I repeat it every time someone on my team starts reaching for the delete key before they've done the understanding.

**Don't:** Let design be a service organization.
**Why:** If designers show up at the end to "make it look nice," you've already lost. Marketing and engineering decisions have been made. The only thing design can do at that point is catch things — and catching things is a losing game. Put design in the room from day one.

**Don't:** Outsource creativity to an agency.
**Why:** I heard Hiroki from Apple say, "I always had this dream of building an internal creative group." It was one of the best things we ever did. An external agency doesn't wrap its arms around your company. They can't. They don't live in the details.

**Don't:** Let the design system decide what you're allowed to design.
**Why:** The design system is supposed to codify what's already great so you can reuse it. If the rule becomes "you can only pull from the system," you've turned an accelerator into a cage. Design whatever you want. Then put it in the system.
