# Perishable vs. Non-Perishable Opportunities

## When to Use
- You have a roadmap you like and an unexpected opportunity just landed that isn't on it.
- You're sequencing several big moves and don't know which to do first.
- A trend window is opening, a competitor is stumbling, a regulation is shifting — and you're trying to decide whether to divert the team.
- You're being pitched acquisitions or partnerships and you need a frame for which ones actually matter now versus which can wait.

## Fails When
- You use it as a license to chase every shiny object. If everything is "perishable," nothing is.
- You haven't fixed the core business. Running off to chase a perishable opportunity while the core is bleeding is how companies die.
- You confuse urgent with perishable. Most urgent things are still available next quarter. Actual perishable opportunities are rare.
- You use "non-perishable" as permission to never do the thing. Sequencing is not the same as avoidance.

## Core Concept

I stole this one from Bezos. It's a filter I apply when something lands in my lap that isn't on the roadmap.

Most opportunities are non-perishable. Most acquisitions, most hiring candidates, most strategic moves — if they're there today, they'll probably still be there in six months or twelve months. You don't lose them by sequencing them behind the work you were already doing.

A small number of opportunities are perishable. A specific talent wants to join right now and won't be available later. A competitor just stumbled and the window is narrow. A regulatory change opened a door that's going to close. A cultural moment lines up with something you were going to build anyway — and if you miss the moment, the thing ships into silence.

The asymmetry is the whole point. Non-perishable opportunities can wait. Perishable opportunities can't. So when a perishable opportunity shows up, you drop it in front of the non-perishable ones — even if the non-perishable ones look more important on paper. Because "important" and "available later" are both true, and important-and-available-later loses to important-and-available-only-now.

At Airbnb I've applied this most clearly to acquisitions. We got momentum, we got cash, we had a list of things we could potentially buy. Most of those acquisitions were not perishable. They'd still be there in a year. So I said: let's get a little more momentum in the core business before we try to absorb large companies. That's a sequencing call. It's not saying the targets were bad. It's saying they weren't perishable.

The trap is the other way. Most founders under-index on perishability. They have a great, well-ordered roadmap and they protect it too well. When the genuinely perishable moment arrives, they treat it like noise because it's off-plan. So they miss it, and six months later the window has closed, and they're still shipping what was on the plan.

The discipline is: hold the roadmap tight enough that you don't chase every shiny object, loose enough that you can recognize perishable when it actually shows up.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask the question explicitly: is this perishable?** Don't assume. Make the team articulate why it won't be available in six or twelve months. If they can't, it isn't.
2. **Name the window.** A perishable opportunity has a window. Write it down. "This closes in Q3 because X." If there's no X, it's not perishable.
3. **Put the non-perishables in a staging queue.** They don't disappear — they wait. Sequence behind the perishables and behind the core work.
4. **Check the core first.** Perishable or not, if the core business isn't healthy, you don't have permission to chase. Fix the house before you add a wing.
5. **Default to "not perishable."** Assume an opportunity can wait unless there's specific evidence it can't. Most founders default the opposite way and get fragmented.
6. **When it IS perishable, move fast and disrupt the roadmap.** This is the part most planners get wrong. You have to be willing to drop the well-ordered plan when the real perishable arrives. That's the whole point of having the frame.
7. **Don't let "non-perishable" become "forever deferred."** Schedule the staged items. If something sits in the queue for two cycles, either do it or admit you don't believe in it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb post-2022, with real momentum and cash. A list of possible acquisitions on the table — adjacent travel companies, experience platforms, loyalty businesses.
**Application:** I applied the perishable-vs-non-perishable test. Most of those acquisitions were not perishable — they'd still exist in a year, and their price wasn't going up faster than our ability to execute on our own core was going up. So we held. Let's get more momentum in the core, I said, before we try to absorb large companies.
**Result:** We kept discipline on the core. The acquisitions that were truly perishable — specific talent, specific capabilities we couldn't build in time — we pursued. The rest we deferred. The next-phase expansion (Experiences, Services, people's time) we built rather than bought, because the build path was available and the buy path wasn't perishable.

**Situation:** A founder has a tight roadmap and a key engineering leader at a competitor just became available. Hiring them would disrupt the current sprint structure.
**Application:** Ask the question. Is this perishable? Yes — they're talking to three other companies this month. Six months from now, they're gone. Non-perishable features on the roadmap can slip. The hire can't.
**Result:** The founder disrupts the plan, makes the hire, absorbs the short-term cost, and ends up with a leader who changes the trajectory of the function. The shipped features were a quarter late. The leader compounds for a decade.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Declare every new idea "perishable" to justify chasing it.
**Why:** The power of the frame comes from the asymmetry — most things aren't perishable. If you apply the label to everything, you've just built a fragmentation machine with a sophisticated name on it. Default to non-perishable. Make someone argue the case.

**Don't:** Ignore the perishable moment because "it's off-plan."
**Why:** Plans have inertia. Good planners protect the plan too well. When an actual perishable window opens and you miss it because it wasn't in the deck, you've lost something you can't get back. The frame exists specifically to give you permission to break the plan when you should.

**Don't:** Confuse urgent with perishable.
**Why:** "Urgent" is pressure. "Perishable" is structural — the thing will not be available later. Most urgent things are still there next quarter. Only treat something as perishable if there's a specific reason the window closes.

**Don't:** Let the non-perishable queue become a graveyard.
**Why:** Sequencing isn't avoidance. If an item sits deferred through two planning cycles, you owe yourself a real decision: do it, or kill it. Items that sit forever are the tell that the team doesn't actually believe in them.
