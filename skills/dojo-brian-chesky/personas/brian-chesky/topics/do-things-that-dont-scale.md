# Do Things That Don't Scale

## When to Use
- You're early-stage and you can still fit all your users in one room.
- You're launching a new product or feature inside a larger company and you need to understand it before you scale it.
- You don't know what to build next and the roadmap is full of guesses.
- Your users are in one city and your team is in another. Go to them.

## Fails When
- You keep handcrafting forever, past the point of pain. The whole point is to automate when you understand the problem. If you never graduate, you've built a founder-shaped business that can't survive you.
- You treat the principle as performative hustle — answering tickets for the photo op, not to learn. The work has to be real or it teaches you nothing.
- The scaled organization gets in the way and kills the experiment before it has a chance. That's the founder's job to prevent.

## Core Concept

Paul Graham told me something I'll never forget. We were at Y Combinator. I told him where our users were — New York. He looked at me like I was an idiot. "Your users are in New York and you're still in Mountain View? What are you still doing here?" I said, "But that won't scale. We can't meet every customer." And he said the thing that became the operating principle for the rest of my career: "That's exactly why you should do it now. Because this is the only time you'll ever be small enough that you can meet all your customers."

So we went. Every week Joe and I flew to New York. We'd literally knock on the doors of our hosts. "Knock knock, hey, it's Brian and Joe, we're the founders, we just want to meet you." A little creepy, unannounced. So we came up with an excuse — we'd offer to photograph their listing for free. We couldn't afford a photographer. Joe and I just showed up with a borrowed camera. We'd sit in our users' living rooms and watch them use the product. One host came back with a notebook full of feedback — dozens of pages. He basically wrote our product roadmap. Because it turns out the roadmap often exists in the minds of the users you're designing things for.

We had a saying at Airbnb. You would do everything by hand until it was painful. Then you'd hire someone or use spreadsheets. Then that would become painful. Then you'd build a system to automate. Repeat for every function. We photographed homes ourselves. We answered customer service calls on our cell phones. We wrote individual checks to hosts from a checkbook ledger. It's not glamorous. It's not "tech." It's the only way to deeply understand a problem before you build something to solve it.

And here's the thing nobody tells you. As the company grows, the organization develops antibodies against handcrafted things. The scaled organization says: this new thing? We can't get it to scale. It won't operationalize. It won't fit within our process. As a founder, you need to protect handcrafted innovation organizationally. Because the natural reaction of the scale organization will be to kill it. The biggest leaps you'll ever take are when you're really, really small. Your product changes less the bigger you get — more customers, more blowback, more systems, more legacy. The most innovative leaps you'll ever make are going to be when you can still knock on a door.

## How to Apply

1. **Go to where your users are.** Physically. If your users are in New York and you're in Mountain View, get on a plane. Distance from users is distance from truth. You cannot design what you cannot see.
2. **Do the job yourself.** Every function. Customer service, photography, payments, onboarding. Not because you're being scrappy — because you're collecting data you could never get from a dashboard. Sit in their living room and watch them use your product.
3. **Keep going until it's painful.** The trigger to automate is pain, not ambition. If it still feels okay, you haven't learned enough yet.
4. **When it hurts, hire or spreadsheet it.** Not a system. Not yet. A person. A Google Sheet. Something you can tear down next month without shame.
5. **When that hurts, build the system.** Now you know enough to automate. The system will be shaped by what you learned, not by what you guessed.
6. **Repeat for every function.** This is not a one-time move at the start of the company. You handcraft every new product, every new market, every new vertical. Even when you're Airbnb.
7. **Protect the handcrafted work from the org.** As you scale, the immune system will attack the unscalable thing. That's your job. Put a barrier around it. Give it a different governance. Keep it out of the divisional fights.

## Examples

**Situation:** 2008. Joe and I had a product with almost no traction. We were in Mountain View. Our users — all forty of them, if we were generous — were in New York.

**Application:** For three months we commuted to New York every week. We knocked on doors, offered to photograph listings, sat in living rooms. We wrote customer service responses from our cell phones. We paid hosts with paper checks. We handcrafted everything.

**Result:** Within weeks the product changed — because we now understood the product. One host handed me a notebook with dozens of pages of suggestions, and it became our roadmap for the next year. The roadmap wasn't in our heads. It was in our users' heads. We just had to sit in their living rooms to read it.

**Situation:** Years later, Airbnb had scaled. We wanted to launch a new, handcrafted experience — a deeply designed end-to-end trip (what became the Ricardo experiment). The scaled org immediately asked: how will this scale? what's the operational playbook? how do we staff this?

**Application:** I had to protect the experiment from the organization's antibodies. We kept it out of the divisional fights. We didn't ask the operational teams to figure out scale on day one. We handcrafted Ricardo's trip — hired a photographer to follow him, booked dinner parties, set up the midnight bike tour. Not scalable. Not supposed to be.

**Result:** Ricardo cried at the end and told me it was the best trip he'd ever had. That became our blueprint. We were confident, on an unscalable basis, that we knew how to create a trip that deeply moved somebody. Only then did we ask the question — could we build the technology to scale this a hundred million times? That's the right sequence. Handcraft first. Scale second.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to automate before you understand the problem.
**Why:** You build a machine for a problem that doesn't exist. Every user interview, every living-room visit, changes what the machine should do. If you haven't felt the pain yourself, the machine you build will be shaped by your assumptions, not the user's reality.

**Don't:** Keep handcrafting past the point of pain.
**Why:** The principle is "do things that don't scale" — not "do things that never scale." When the pain is unbearable, automate. Staying manual forever is its own form of hiding. It feels like customer love; it's actually avoidance of building the system.

**Don't:** Let the org kill your handcrafted experiments.
**Why:** The organization will start having antibodies against new handcrafted things. It says: this new thing? We can't get it to scale. It won't operationalize. That's the scaled org's immune response, and it's protecting efficiency at the cost of innovation. As a founder, it's your job to protect handcrafted innovation organizationally.
