# Presence, Not Absence

## When to Use
- You're deciding how involved to be with a newly hired executive.
- You've been told by your team or your board to "trust the team and step back" and your gut is resisting it.
- You're watching a project drift and don't know whether to jump in or stay out.
- You're trying to explain to someone why you review all the work before it ships.
- You're onboarding a leader and setting expectations about your involvement.

## Fails When
- You pretend presence means presence for its own sake. Sitting in every meeting adding no signal isn't presence — it's attendance.
- You can't tell the difference between auditing work and dictating work. If you're in the details telling people what to do, you've crossed into micromanagement.
- You haven't earned your taste. Presence from a CEO with no product judgment is just friction.
- You apply it to people who've already built muscle memory — they don't need the golf instructor watching every swing. They need scope.

## Core Concept

Great leadership is presence, not absence.

That's the whole thesis. Most CEOs of most companies are not present. They're fairly absent. They've been told that absence is the goal — hire great people, empower them, give them space. And the results are devastating. I know because I lived it. For years I did exactly what everyone said to do. I hired experienced executives, gave them autonomy, stepped back. The company slowly came apart. Not because the people were bad. They were wonderful. But because leadership without presence is not leadership. It's abdication.

Here's the paradox nobody warned me about. The more I let go, the more dysfunctional the company got. I'd hire an executive. I'd give them space. They'd go in their direction. Things would slide. People would say the dysfunction was because leadership was too involved — not that there was an absence of leadership. So I'd back off further. And it got worse. Then when things got bad enough, I'd finally step in. But now I'm stepping into a totally dysfunctional project. So I'm associated with the dysfunction. "See? Brian's always involved in the most broken things at the company." It was a reverse intervention.

The conventional wisdom is exactly backwards. I'm now in many more details and yet I have way more free time than the old way where I wasn't involved. Because in the old way, you still have meetings — but for things going wrong. Someone's resigning. A project has to be redone. Committees and swirl. Long presentations that go nowhere. Now? Decisions happen every week. Sometimes every day. Sometimes every hour. The bottleneck everyone predicted never materialized. It massively sped up.

Here's the distinction that matters. There is a difference between micromanagement — telling people exactly what to do — and being in the details. Being in the details is what every responsible company's board does to the CEO. The board doesn't tell the CEO what to do. But they audit the work. They ask questions. They check the details. How do you know someone's doing a good job if you're not in the details?

Steve Jobs said he didn't believe he was a micromanager. He was in the details but partnering with people. I asked Jony Ive — did you ever feel micromanaged by Steve Jobs? He said no. He said he felt the most empowered he'd ever felt in his career. Even though Steve visited the design studio every single day. Think about that. The man who ran the most detail-oriented product company in history — his head of design felt more empowered, not less. Not in spite of Steve's presence. Because of it.

Here's a bad analogy, but it works. I'm a terrible golfer. But I went to get a golf lesson once. The instructor watches your swing. They correct you. Repetition after repetition. Thank God they were there — because if they weren't, I would have developed a wrong swing and never known it. That's what it should be like at a company. You hire people, you start in the details. Over time, they build muscle memory. And you let go. But if you let go first — before they've developed that muscle memory — they develop a wrong swing. And you won't find out for years.

The way most companies work is the opposite. They hire executives and let them do their thing. Then they find out a year later the whole thing has been wrong. The executive hired the wrong people. Built the wrong team. Now you have to get in the details anyway, except now you've undermined their confidence, so they leave. And you inherit their team. And the cycle starts again.

Start in the details. Develop trust. Build muscle memory. Then let go. That's the sequence. Almost everyone does it backwards.

## How to Apply

1. **Start every new hire in the details.** Even if they know more than you about their function, they don't know more than you about the company, your standards, or your pace. Work alongside them for the first few months.

2. **Review the work, not the people.** Johnny Ive would say: "My main job is to manage the work. I'm not having career conversations all day long." Manage the people through the work.

3. **Audit, don't dictate.** Treat yourself like a board member auditing a CEO. You don't tell them what to do. You ask questions. You check details. You hold them to a standard. If the work isn't good enough, name it and let them solve it.

4. **Stay on a cadence.** Weekly, biweekly, monthly, quarterly — depending on the area. Customer service might be quarterly. Anything that touches the customer experience, you see it. Marketing too.

5. **Do deep dives every 1-2 years.** Pick a function. Two-to-four-week audit. Nobody's going to get fired — just show me the good and the bad. Then fundamentally rethink the group.

6. **Skip-level as a matter of course.** If you're not in regular touch with the top 30-50 people below your executive team, you're not actually in the details of the company.

7. **Let go when the muscle memory is built.** Presence early, autonomy earned. Not the other way around.

## Examples

**Situation:** Steve Jobs and Jony Ive at Apple. Steve visits the design studio every single day for years. He reviews every material, every curve, every color. People outside Apple assume Jony must feel smothered.
**Application:** Ask Jony. He'll tell you he felt the most empowered he'd ever been in his career. Because Steve's presence wasn't dictating the design — it was partnering on it, raising the standard, forcing the work to be better than Jony would have pushed it alone.
**Result:** The iPhone. The iPad. The Apple of 1997-2011. That's what presence looks like when the leader has taste.

**Situation:** A founder hires a VP of Design, six months in, she's producing work that feels off-brand. Everyone says: give her time, trust the process. Six more months go by. The founder finally steps in. The work is still off. The VP leaves. The founder inherits her team and has to redo two years of work.
**Application:** Should have started in the details from day one. Weekly design reviews. Ask questions, raise standards, partner on the work. Either she rises to it or she doesn't — and you know within three months, not eighteen.
**Result:** Either you build the muscle memory together and she becomes a great partner, or you find out fast that she wasn't the right hire and you cut the loss before the organization has absorbed two years of mediocrity.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse absence with empowerment.
**Why:** There's a notion that power is zero-sum — either I have the power or the employees have the power. There's a scenario where we're all powerless. It's called a lot of Fortune 500 companies. When leadership is absent, bureaucracy fills the vacuum and nobody has power. When leadership is present and involved, everyone can actually do great work.

**Don't:** Back off when someone says "trust me to do my thing."
**Why:** Each generation of executives says let me do my thing. They do their thing. They leave. You inherit their team. Then the next executive says the same thing. Over 17 years I've been through many generations of this cycle. Trust is earned by shared work, not by distance.

**Don't:** Try to be present without a cadence.
**Why:** Dropping in randomly is how you become associated with only the broken things. Regular, predictable reviews on a cadence are how you actually partner with your team. Everyone knows when the work will be seen. Everyone raises their own bar because of it.
