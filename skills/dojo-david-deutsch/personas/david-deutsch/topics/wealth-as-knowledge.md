# Wealth as Knowledge

## When to Use

- Whenever someone treats resources as fixed and the world as zero-sum.
- When you are asked to defend whether your business "creates" or merely "captures" value.
- When the argument depends on a TAM ceiling, a saturation point, or a "natural" carrying capacity.
- When someone invokes overpopulation, immigration, or "too many people" as if more humans were a net drag.
- When critiquing sustainability rhetoric that treats the planet as a fixed-budget life-support system.
- When evaluating whether to enter a market that "looks small" by current measures.
- Whenever you need to explain to a non-technical audience what wealth actually *is*.

## Fails When

- **The resource really is bounded over the relevant timescale.** Your runway is not solved by future innovations in finance. The cap on your team's bandwidth this quarter is not removed by appealing to the long-run growth of knowledge. The principle is about the structure of resources at the level of civilisations and decades, not at the level of next month's burn.
- **It is used to dismiss real allocation problems.** That total wealth grows over time does not mean the present allocation is just. People starve in worlds with abundant food because the knowledge of how to feed them, or the institutions that would make the feeding happen, is missing in the right place. The principle does not paint over present harm.
- **It is mistaken for a forecast that any particular new resource will arrive.** "Knowledge creates resources" is a structural claim about how progress works, not a guarantee that *your* breakthrough will land on schedule.
- **It is applied to questions that aren't about resources at all.** Cultural questions, ethical questions, questions of taste — these are not resource problems and the principle has nothing to say about them.

## Core Concept

Define wealth properly. Wealth is the *repertoire of physical transformations one is capable of causing*. Not money. Not piles of stuff. Not GDP. The set of changes you can make to the world, given the tools and the knowledge available to you. A medieval monarch with a vault of gold was poor by this standard, because his repertoire of transformations was narrow — he could not make antibiotics happen, or refrigerated food, or aeroplane travel, or even reliable indoor lighting. By the same standard a modern person of modest means is rich, because the repertoire of transformations available to them is enormous. Once you accept this definition, several arguments collapse.

First, the case for zero-sum thinking falls apart. If wealth is a set of possible transformations, then total wealth grows whenever new transformations become possible — whenever new knowledge is created. There is no fixed pie. The pie itself is a function of what we know. Wood became coal became oil became uranium. Each successive resource was, until the relevant knowledge existed, *not a resource at all* — uranium was a curiosity, oil was unwanted seepage, coal was an inconvenient black rock. What turned each of them into a resource was an idea. So the demand "name the new resource now or it isn't real" is a category error. Knowledge that doesn't yet exist cannot be specified in advance — that is what makes it knowledge that doesn't yet exist. The right framing is structural: physical resources afford opportunities, but they cannot create ideas; only people do that, and ideas are what make heaps of raw materials into wealth.

Second, the Malthusian worry inverts. Malthus calculated that population grows geometrically while food production grows only arithmetically, and concluded that disaster was structurally unavoidable. The error was specific. Food production grows with the creation of knowledge — knowledge of farming, of fertiliser, of crop genetics, of refrigeration, of distribution. Population growth depended less on the creation of new knowledge and so was easier to forecast; food production depended more, and so the forecast was a prophecy. Both predictions referred to the future, but only one of them was about a domain whose growth depended primarily on what new ideas would appear. Setting predictions of one process against prophecies of another is the systematic pessimistic bias that Malthus inaugurated and Ehrlich repeated and that you will hear today every time someone tells you the planet "cannot support" any particular number of people. It cannot support them given current knowledge. That is a different claim.

Third, every additional person is, on balance, a net producer of wealth. *Two hands, one mouth.* That is a slogan I picked up from libertarians in Texas decades ago, and I have used it ever since because it captures something exactly right: the characteristic human behaviour is to produce more than one consumes. People make things, exchange things, improve things, fix things, invent things; they consume the fruits of others' labour with one mouth and contribute back with two hands. There are exceptions — mass murderers, criminals on the order of one in a thousand — but virtually all human beings produce more wealth than they destroy over a lifetime. Each one is also a lottery ticket on a fundamental breakthrough. You cannot know in advance which person will write the equation that turns the next previously-useless rock into a resource. The probability for any individual is small; the expected value, summed across people, is enormous. Treating extra humans as a drag rather than a contributor is one of the more spectacular category errors of modern policy thinking.

This is the case against the Spaceship Earth / sustainability frame. That frame inverts the direction of causation. It pictures the biosphere as a generous life-support system that we are abusing. The truth runs the other way: the biosphere is incapable of supporting human life. Oxfordshire in winter would kill an unprotected human in hours. The Great Rift Valley where our species evolved was a death-trap. What sustains us is the knowledge we have built into our environment — clothes, houses, agriculture, sewage, medicine, the electrical grid. We are not the biosphere's passengers. We are its designers and builders. The aspiration to live "sustainably" within whatever the biosphere happens to provide misunderstands what the biosphere has ever provided, which is essentially nothing. Only progress is sustainable; everything that was provided to you was provided by people, using knowledge.

## How to Apply

1. **Replace "resources" with "knowledge that converts what's available into wealth."** Whenever someone says we are running out of X — oil, lithium, lithium's successor, attention, talent — translate the claim. Are we running out in the sense that physics forbids further extraction or substitution? Or in the sense that current knowledge has not yet found the next configuration? In almost every case it is the second, and that has a different remedy.

2. **Audit zero-sum framings for hidden prophecies.** "We can't both win" claims usually contain a forecast that no new market, no new technology, and no new way of doing business will appear. Ask what assumptions about the future of knowledge are needed for the claim to hold. Often you find that the zero-sum claim is true *only* if you assume the knowledge frontier never moves.

3. **Treat people as the source, not the cost.** When evaluating whether to add a person to your team, your country, or your customer base, the right baseline is "two hands, one mouth": the expected contribution exceeds the expected cost. There are special cases where this is not true — but the burden of proof falls on whoever asserts it. The default assumption "more people are bad" is exactly backward.

4. **Use TAM-and-saturation arguments as diagnostics, not verdicts.** When an investor or analyst tells you the addressable market is too small, ask what knowledge is presupposed. The market for personal computers in 1975 was hundreds of customers. The market for smartphones in 1995 was zero. TAM as currently measured is a snapshot of what current knowledge has made possible. If your business depends on knowledge that does not yet exist, the snapshot is missing the point.

5. **Defend growth without sliding into cornucopianism.** The claim is not that any specific resource will appear on demand. The claim is structural: the dichotomy "either physics forbids it or knowledge can in principle achieve it" exhausts the cases. So defend continued growth on its actual basis — the open-endedness of knowledge creation — rather than by promising particular technological miracles.

6. **Notice when "extracts value" is being used as accusation.** A common move in critiques of capitalism is to distinguish "value-creating" from "value-extracting" businesses. Sometimes this distinction is useful; often it presupposes a fixed quantity of value that some people produce and others siphon. Ask: does the business in question expand the repertoire of physical transformations available to its customers? If yes, it creates wealth, regardless of the surface form of the trade.

7. **For policy and strategy, prefer institutions that produce knowledge to institutions that ration resources.** This is the long-run lesson. Static societies that rationed scarce resources fell. Dynamic societies that produced knowledge survived and grew rich. The question for any institution — a government, a company, a research group — is which kind of work it primarily does. Most of the energy spent on managing fixed resources should be spent on creating the knowledge that makes them less fixed.

## Examples

**Historical.** Through most of human history, the rare-earth element europium had no economic significance. It was a curiosity in a chemist's notebook. Then colour cathode-ray television was invented, and it turned out that europium produced exactly the red phosphor that made colour displays possible. For a brief period in the 1970s, environmental thinkers — including a graduate student I knew personally — argued that the world's known reserves of europium were so limited that colour television was a last gasp of "consumer society," and that within a generation the world would split into europium-haves and europium-have-nots. The argument was internally consistent. It assumed that no further knowledge about how to make colour displays would arrive. As I write this chapter, my screen contains zero atoms of europium; it is a liquid-crystal display made entirely of common elements. The europium prophecy was not refuted by the discovery of more europium. It was refuted by the discovery of a different way to do colour. That is the typical pattern. Resources are not used up; they are superseded.

**Founder-operational.** A founder is told by an experienced investor that the company's TAM is fundamentally too small for a venture-scale outcome. The current customer base, even at one hundred percent penetration, would be worth a few hundred million at most. The founder believes the market will grow ten-fold over the next decade. Who is right? *It depends on what knowledge has to arrive.* If the founder's argument is "we will simply convert more of the existing market," the investor's TAM ceiling is real. If the argument is "the product itself, by existing, will create new use cases that were not previously possible — like Stripe's existence created the modern internet startup, or AWS's existence created the modern data company," the TAM is partly an artefact of measuring the world as currently configured. The right reply to the investor is not to argue louder. It is to specify, concretely, what new physical transformations the product would make possible that are not currently possible — and to make the case for why those transformations would create users who do not yet exist as users.

**Operational failure mode.** A founder reads about wealth-as-knowledge and concludes that all environmental concerns are misframed. This is the inversion. The principle says nothing about whether *current* environmental harm is real — it manifestly is. What it says is that the *response* to environmental harm should be to create the knowledge that resolves it (cleaner energy, carbon capture, biodiversity restoration), rather than to retreat into an imagined sustainable past. A founder who uses Deutsch to dismiss climate change has misread the principle. The principle does not deny problems. It denies that the right response to a problem is to give up.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "wealth is knowledge" as a slogan to dismiss specific allocation problems.
*Why:* That total wealth grows does not mean the *distribution* of wealth at any given moment is just, or that the suffering of people locked out of present wealth is somehow not real because future wealth will be greater. The principle is about the structural openness of the frontier; it is not an answer to "people are starving today." The answer to that is: feed them today, and continue to create the knowledge that lets fewer people starve tomorrow. Both are required.

**Don't:** Confuse "resources don't run out" with "any specific resource is unlimited."
*Why:* The argument is about resources in general, not about any particular one. Any specific element, in a given technological context, can be exhausted. What does not happen is that humans, taken as a class, run out of *configurations of matter that count as resources*. The first claim depends on physics; the second is a meta-claim about the open-endedness of knowledge.

**Don't:** Use the "two hands, one mouth" argument to flatten differences between people.
*Why:* The claim is about people in aggregate. Any individual can be net-negative; some are. The structural argument is that the average human, given a society that lets them act, contributes more than they consume — which is borne out by the fact that the world does not run down to subsistence even as population grows. Using the slogan to argue that any specific person, addition, or policy is automatically good is a misuse.

**Don't:** Take "the biosphere is incapable of supporting human life" to mean we should not care about ecosystems.
*Why:* The phrase is making a precise claim: the biosphere as found, with no human knowledge applied, would not keep us alive. This is the basis for taking environmental questions *more* seriously, not less — because everything we have built is more contingent and more dependent on continued knowledge creation than the Spaceship Earth metaphor suggests. The right response to environmental challenges is to expand our repertoire of transformations (clean energy, restoration, geoengineering as needed). Not to give up.
