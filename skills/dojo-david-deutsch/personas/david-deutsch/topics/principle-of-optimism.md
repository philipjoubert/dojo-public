# The Principle of Optimism

## When to Use

- Whenever someone is using "realism" or "rigour" as a license to declare a problem structurally unsolvable.
- Whenever a setback is being read as a verdict on a project's possibility rather than as a gap in current knowledge.
- Before you accept any argument of the form "we've reached the limits" — of growth, of compute, of biology, of a market.
- When critiquing a colleague's idea and you can feel yourself sliding into "you're being unrealistic" — that's the move to refuse.
- When you are being accused of magical thinking for taking a hard problem seriously.
- Whenever someone invokes the precautionary principle, tail-risk-as-rigour, or "invert, always invert" as if pessimism were epistemic safety.

## Fails When

- **The laws of physics genuinely forbid the outcome.** Faster-than-light communication, perpetual motion, prophecy of the content of future knowledge — saying "this won't happen" is not pessimism, it's just correct. The principle is about unsolved problems, not about real physical limits.
- **It is used as a slogan to dodge a specific objection.** "We have a duty to be optimistic" is not an answer to "your runway is six weeks." Optimism is a stance toward the unknown; it does not exempt you from arithmetic.
- **It is applied to questions of taste or value.** "Should we build this?" sometimes turns on what one cares about, not on what is solvable. Optimism cannot tell you what to want.
- **It is mistaken for a prediction of success.** Optimism does not say the moon shot will succeed. It says the failure, if it comes, will not be because the laws of nature were against you. The probability is not the point.
- **It is confused with cheerfulness.** Churchill suffered from intense depression. Malthus was, by all accounts, the life of the dinner party. Mood is not the unit of analysis here.

## Core Concept

State it plainly. *All evils are caused by insufficient knowledge.* That is the Principle of Optimism, written down in chapter 9 of *The Beginning of Infinity*, and it is more precise than the slogans that surround it. Optimism, in the sense I mean, is in the first instance a way of explaining *failure*, not of prophesying success. When we try to improve something and fail, it is never because the spiteful gods are thwarting us, never because we have reached the limit of what reason can do, never because it is best for us that we fail. It is always because we did not know enough, in time. That is the whole content of the principle. Everything else follows from it.

This is *not* the disposition described by the half-full glass. That is psychology, more spin than substance. Nor is it Leibniz's optimism — the doctrine that this is the best of all possible worlds — which is in fact, by my definitions, a form of pessimism, because it concludes that no rational thought could improve matters. The optimism I mean is structural. It says only this: there is no fundamental barrier — no law of nature, no supernatural decree, no metaphysical limit on reason — preventing progress on any problem not forbidden by the laws of physics. If something is possible in physics, the only thing that can prevent it from being possible in technology is not knowing how. That is also why optimism follows from the explicability of the world.

Now name the thing it opposes. The blind optimist proceeds as if he knows the bad outcomes will not happen — that is recklessness. The blind pessimist — the precautionary principle, tail-risk obsession, "invert, always invert" — proceeds as if he knows the good ones will not. Both are *prophetic*. Both purport to know unknowable things about the future of knowledge. They look like opposites; at the level of explanation they are the same move. The pessimist who says "the harm of an innovation could be unbounded" is making exactly the same kind of unjustified claim about the future as the optimist who says "this ship is unsinkable." Note that almost every civilisation that has fallen, has fallen for lack of knowledge — most had enthusiastically implemented the precautionary principle. Sticking with what was known killed them; it did not save them.

So why is this the rational stance? Because the alternative — assuming that knowledge will not grow, or cannot be relied on to grow — closes a loop that prevents any improvement from happening. A society that does not expect its future choices to be better than its present ones will entrench its present choices. It is the assumption of growth that creates the conditions for growth. We have, as Popper put it, a duty to be optimistic. Not because the future is bright (we cannot know that) but because *if it is to be bright, someone has to act as though it can be*. The prisoner who promises to teach the king's horse to talk in a year is not predicting that he will succeed. He is refusing to let the assumption of failure foreclose every escape route, including the ones he cannot yet imagine.

## How to Apply

1. **State the principle when challenged, not as a mood.** "All evils are caused by insufficient knowledge" is not a slogan; it is a claim about where the bottleneck is. If someone calls you naive, the answer is not to apologise for hope but to ask what specifically is supposed to be impossible — and whether physics forbids it or merely current knowledge does. If the latter, the conversation is about *how* to acquire the missing knowledge.

2. **Distinguish prediction from prophecy.** Following Popper: a prediction follows from a good explanation, in its domain. A prophecy claims to know what is not yet knowable — typically the content of future ideas. Most "we know how this ends" arguments in business, technology, and politics are prophecies. Name them as prophecies and the argument's grip loosens.

3. **Audit the move from "hard" to "won't happen."** When mathematicians call a problem hard, they do not mean it is unlikely to be solved. Hardness is often the very reason a problem gets attacked. If a colleague says "this is hard," that is information; if they say "this is hopeless," ask which law of physics they are appealing to.

4. **Test for blind pessimism the same way as blind optimism.** Both are "prophetic" — they bet on knowing unknowable things. The Titanic argument cuts both ways: the asymmetry between possible disasters and possible benefits is itself an unjustified premise about the future of knowledge. Run the symmetric question: *what unforeseen disasters can flow from existing ignorance?*

5. **When critiquing, refuse the genre move.** "You're being unrealistic" is, almost always, smuggled prophecy. Replace it with: "Here is the specific gap in the explanation; here is what would need to be true that I don't yet see how to get." That is criticism. The other thing is intimidation in the language of rigour.

6. **Use difficulty as a feature, not a bug, in choosing objectives.** Kennedy: *"We choose to go to the moon… not because they are easy, but because they are hard."* He did not mean the project was unlikely to succeed. He meant the project required the creation of knowledge that did not yet exist, and that was the *point*. When choosing among means, hardness is a cost. When choosing the objective itself, hardness can be the reason — because what makes the project worth doing is the knowledge it forces into existence.

7. **Set up so that being wrong is recoverable.** Optimism does not entail confidence in any specific plan. It entails a posture toward the unknown that is generative rather than defensive. The corollary is institutional: the right thing to do, given that you cannot know what you do not yet know, is not to predict better but to make your bets in a way that lets you correct them when reality teaches you something. (See `don-t-destroy-error-correction.md`.)

## Examples

**Historical.** In 1798, Malthus declared that the nineteenth century would see a permanent end to human progress because population grew geometrically while food supply grew only arithmetically. The first half of his argument — population growth — was a prediction grounded in known mechanism. The second half — food supply — was prophecy: he was implicitly forecasting the absence of agricultural innovations that would in fact arrive. The century that followed saw both the population explosion he predicted *and* food production growth that exceeded it. He was right about the part that was less dependent on the creation of knowledge, and wrong about the part that was. This is the systematic pessimistic bias to which prophecy is prone, and it has been repeated, with the same structure, by Ehrlich (1971), Rees (2003), and any number of contemporaries: setting *predictions* of one process against *prophecies* of another, and calling the result rigour.

**Founder-operational.** A serious investor tells you the market for what you are building is fundamentally too small — that the maximum reachable customer base, even at perfect penetration, does not produce a venture-scale outcome. Pause and audit. Are they making a prediction grounded in current explanations of how the market works (in which case they may well be right within those explanations) — or are they prophesying that no future innovation, on your part or anyone else's, will expand the addressable market? Almost every "TAM is too small" argument from investors is the second one in the language of the first. Wood went to coal, coal to oil, oil to uranium; the customers for personal computers in 1975 numbered in the hundreds. The right reply is not "you're being too negative." It is: "Which of those is your claim, and what would have to be true for the second one to fail?"

**Operational.** A senior engineer says a feature you want to ship is impossible at the latency you need. That is a useful sentence if it is followed by an explanation: here is the bottleneck, here is the law of physics or architectural constraint that makes it so. It is a useless sentence — a prophecy in the language of expertise — if it is just "we can't." The principle of optimism gives you the specific question that distinguishes the two: *what specifically would have to change for this to be possible, and is that change forbidden by physics, or merely by what we currently know?* Almost every "impossible" inside a company is the second. (When it really is the first, you should believe it: faster-than-light communication is not coming.)

**Failure mode.** An early-stage founder reads a few books about Big Visions and concludes that any criticism of a moonshot project is itself a kind of pessimism — a failure of nerve. This is the inversion. The principle of optimism does not say criticism is suspect; criticism is precisely the engine by which the relevant knowledge gets created. What the principle rules out is a particular kind of meta-criticism — the kind that says "this can never work" without grounding the claim in the laws of physics or in a specific, removable gap in current knowledge. Substantive criticism of a moonshot — "your unit economics don't work because of X, and here is why X is structural" — is not pessimism. It is fallibilism doing its job. Confusing the two is how you end up with founders who refuse to hear from anyone except people who already agree with them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the Principle of Optimism to dismiss specific operational objections.
*Why:* "All evils are caused by insufficient knowledge" is true but does not absolve you of getting the wiring right today. The principle is a stance toward the unknown future of knowledge. It is not a refutation of the engineer who says your battery chemistry will not deliver the energy density at the price you need. The right move with that engineer is not "we have a duty to be optimistic"; it is to engage the explanation and find the gap. The principle tells you the gap is in principle closeable. It does not tell you it will be closed by Q3.

**Don't:** Confuse "we have a duty to be optimistic" with cheerfulness as policy.
*Why:* Optimism in this sense is a methodological commitment, not a vibe. Churchill's expectations about the war were unusually positive while he was clinically depressed; Malthus prophesied doom while being merry company. The unit of optimism is not how you feel; it is what you take to be possible in principle. A team that performs optimism by suppressing bad news has not adopted the principle — it has destroyed the means of error correction, which is the actual catastrophic move.

**Don't:** Treat pessimism as a sign that the pessimist has done their homework.
*Why:* It is a recurring trick of intellectual fashion to dress prophecy in the costume of rigour — "tail risk," "asymmetric downside," "the precautionary principle." Each is a fine tool *within* a domain where the explanations are stable; each is a category error when applied to the future of knowledge. The argument that "a single catastrophic consequence could end progress forever" assumes both that we can identify the catastrophe in advance and that doing nothing is safe. Both are unjustified. Sticking with the known has historically been the riskier move.

**Don't:** Take "this is the worst time in history" seriously without checking the previous claims.
*Why:* Pessimism's recurring move is to declare the present moment uniquely dangerous. *Our Final Century* claims this for the twenty-first century. Ehrlich claimed it for 1971. Malthus claimed it for 1798. Savonarola claimed it for 1494 Florence. Each was sincere; each was wrong in the same structural way. When someone tells you the current moment is uniquely dangerous, ask them — without sarcasm — what would have to be different for them to say the same thing about 1971 or 1894. If the answer is the same kind of argument, it is the same kind of argument.
