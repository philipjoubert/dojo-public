# Problem Selection

## When to Use

- When choosing between job offers, startup ideas, research directions.
- When the user has the talent and capacity to do many things and the question is which thing.
- When prestige (school, employer, fund tier, thesis area) is doing more of the talking than substance.
- When the question is whether to *stay* on a current problem or pivot.
- When evaluating whether a problem is worth a 5- or 10-year commitment.

## Fails When

- The user has hard constraints — dependents, debt, visa — that genuinely make "pick what you love" a luxury they can't afford yet. The Wolfram-letter test still applies, but on a longer time horizon.
- "I need to love it" gets used as a permanent excuse to avoid the unglamorous work that any problem demands after the first month.
- The user mistakes the early excitement of any new problem for the deeper draw of one they'd actually stay with.
- There's no edge available — sometimes the honest answer is "this is a great problem, but you're not the right person to work on it, and that's okay."

## Core Idea

Most ambitious people pick problems by prestige optimization: which option signals the most? Which fund, school, lab, employer is the highest-status one I can get into? Feynman's life and his recommendation letters argue for a different test: pick the problem you'd be willing to *work it all out yourself* on, the one where you have an edge nobody else in the room has, and where the pleasure of figuring it out would sustain you for a decade. Anything else — money, status, opportunity-cost regret — is a side effect or noise.

The Wolfram letter is the cleanest single statement of the criterion. Feynman is asked to recommend a 21-year-old prodigy. He does not list awards, comparisons to peers, or future potential. He praises the *method*: "the method he uses in studying each question is not so much to read about it, but to work it all out himself. He works very hard. Such methods and such industry is, of course, the true source of creativity and originality." That method is what Feynman means by a person being on the right problem. If you would work it out yourself, you're on it. If you're skimming what others have said and hoping that counts as work, you're not.

## Mechanism — the four tests

Run any candidate problem through:

1. **The work-it-out test.** Would you, on this problem, be willing to derive things from scratch — slow, painful, embarrassing — rather than just reading what others have said? Or are you here for the readership and not the derivation?
2. **The edge test.** What do you have on this problem that other capable people don't? A method, a perspective, a tool, a lived experience, a dataset, a relationship. If you can't name your edge in one sentence, you don't have one yet — and "I'm smart" is not an edge.
3. **The ten-year test.** If the recognition / fundraising / external validation evaporated tomorrow, would you still want to be on this in five years? In ten? The pleasure-of-finding-things-out test, applied to the entire arc.
4. **The "work hard" test.** Feynman repeats this. Originality without sustained effort is dilettantism. The problem you pick must be one you're willing to grind on past the point where it stops being fun, because every problem stops being fun in month four.

If a problem fails any of these tests, it is not the problem. Move on without regret.

## Worked Examples

- **The Wolfram MacArthur recommendation (April 6, 1981).** Asked to write up Stephen Wolfram for the MacArthur "genius" Award, Feynman names specific work — Wolfram's QCD comparison method, his algebraic computation system built from scratch, his early-universe baryon analysis — and then names the *method* he most respects: "The method he uses in studying each question is not so much to read about it, but to work it all out himself. He works very hard. Such methods and such industry is, of course, the true source of creativity and originality." Feynman's actual selection criterion for a problem (and for a person on a problem), stated in one paragraph: derive it yourself, work hard, and originality follows. He closes: "He seems to have worked on everything and has some original or careful judgment on any topic."

- **The Mano letter (February 3, 1966).** A former student writes from a research lab to congratulate Feynman on the Nobel and confesses he is studying "a humble and down-to-the-earth type of problem" — coherence theory applied to electromagnetic waves through turbulent atmosphere — and is sad about it. Feynman writes back to say his teacher (and his own teaching, Feynman admits) had "given you a false idea of what are worthwhile problems": "The worthwhile problems are the ones you can really solve or help solve, the ones you can really contribute something to." He then lists his own "humble" problems — friction on polished surfaces (failure), elastic properties of crystals, electroplating onto plastic radio knobs, neutron diffusion through uranium, flexagons, turbulence (years, no success). The lesson: prestige of the problem is the wrong filter; "no problem is too small or too trivial if we can really do something about it."

- **Refusing the Institute for Advanced Study.** In *Surely You're Joking*, Feynman describes the IAS offer as "absurd" — better than Einstein's terms, with a half-appointment at Princeton to satisfy his interest in teaching. He turns it down because he had watched what the IAS did to "those great minds": no classes, no obligations, "and they're not getting any ideas. I believe that in a situation like this a kind of guilt or depression worms inside of you, and you begin to *worry* about not getting any ideas." The most prestigious chair in physics fails the work-it-out-yourself test: it removes the daily friction (teaching) that produces the work.

- **Resigning the National Academy of Sciences (August 1961).** Feynman writes to Detlev Bronk that he finds it "psychologically very distasteful to judge people's 'merit'" and is "not happy as a member of a self-perpetuating honorary society." He apologizes for the inconvenience and resigns: "your flock will contain one very peculiar, sad and reluctant bird." The same logic in reverse — once a "credential" is just status without work attached, decline it.

- **Refusing honorary degrees** (letter to George Beadle): "I remember the work I did to get a real degree at Princeton and the guys on the same platform receiving honorary degrees without work — and felt an 'honorary degree' was a debasement of the idea of a 'degree which confirms certain work has been accomplished.' It is like giving an 'honorary electricians license.' I swore then that if by chance I was ever offered one I would not accept it."

## Coaching Moves

- For each option, write down: what would I work it all out myself on, here? If the answer is "nothing — I'd just execute on what others have figured out," it's not the problem.
- Ban prestige-language from the decision memo. No "Tier 1 fund," no "elite school," no "FAANG." Force yourself to describe each option in terms of substrate, method, and edge only. Re-introduce prestige only as a final tiebreaker.
- Imagine the recognition is stripped. The fund flames out. The school's reputation collapses. The employer becomes mediocre. Would you stay? If no, you were optimizing for the wrong thing.
- Identify your edge in one sentence. If you can't, you haven't earned the right to commit yet — go acquire an edge before picking the problem.
- 10-year horizon: write down what you'd want to be true about your work in 2035. Reverse-engineer the problem from there.

## Anti-Patterns

- The "stepping stone" rationalization. ("This isn't the problem I love, but it'll get me to the one I love.") Sometimes true. Usually a story you tell yourself to take the prestigious option without admitting it.
- Treating excitement-of-the-week as evidence of the right problem. Every new problem is exciting for a month.
- Picking by elimination — "well, the others were worse." Affirmative reasoning for the chosen option, please.
- Confusing "edge" with "passion." You can love a problem and have no edge on it; that's a recipe for unrewarded effort. You need both.
