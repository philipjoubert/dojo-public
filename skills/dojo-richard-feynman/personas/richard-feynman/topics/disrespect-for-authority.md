# Disrespect for Authority

## When to Use

- When an argument is being defended on the basis of who said it, not what was said.
- When you've done the work on a question and are being talked down to by someone with the credential.
- When you're intimidated into not asking the obvious question because the expert is in the room.
- When a field has converged on a consensus that nobody alive can defend from primitives.
- When you're managing your own deference to senior people in your organization or industry and it's costing you accuracy.

## Fails When

- The credential actually represents accumulated specialist knowledge you don't have. A surgeon's "I've done this 4,000 times" is not authority-by-credential; it's earned expertise. Disrespecting it is stupidity.
- "Disrespect for authority" becomes contrarianism for its own sake. Disagreeing with the consensus *because* it's the consensus is just authority-worship inverted.
- The user wields it to dismiss legitimate domain expertise they don't want to engage with. ("Doctors don't really know anything." "Climatologists are just credentialed.") This is the failure mode of a slogan.
- The user picks fights with experts as a status move rather than to learn. The point is to *judge the argument*, not to *defeat the expert*.

## Core Idea

Feynman is reflexively suspicious of titles, ceremonies, and the assumption that someone who has been right before will be right now. He resigned from the National Academy of Sciences because he found the ranking of scientists by other scientists ridiculous. He refused honorary degrees. He wore the same shirt to physics conferences and to lectures at the Institute for Advanced Study. None of this is contrarianism. It is the *consistent application of the scientific method to one's own life*: judge the argument by its content, not by the speaker.

The discipline matters because deference is the path of least resistance, and most decisions get made the easy way. The senior partner says X; the room agrees with X; X turns out to be wrong; everyone is surprised. The expert says Y; the founder defers to Y; Y is wrong; the company spends a year correcting. The pattern is universal. The cure is the small, repeated act of asking *why* instead of *who*.

This generalizes far past science. Founders who can ask their highly-credentialed advisors "why do you believe that?" without flinching have a strictly better information environment than founders who can't. Operators who'd rather hear "I don't know" from their experts than confident wrong answers are running a better organization. The disrespect is not for the person; it's for the *substitution of credential for reasoning*.

## Mechanism — practicing it well

1. **Distinguish credential from earned expertise.** A surgeon's volume of operations is earned data; their MD degree is mostly correlated noise. Same with traders, teachers, engineers. The data is the work history; the credential is a proxy with widely varying signal.
2. **Ask why, not who.** When someone makes a claim, default to engaging the *content* of the claim, not the source. "Tell me how you got there" beats "okay, you're the expert."
3. **Practice on the asymmetric cases.** When the credentialed expert is in the room and you're not, asking the basic question feels expensive. Do it anyway — the expert who can't answer the basic question is exactly the one whose claim you most need to discount, and the one who *can* answer earns more of your credence than the one whose authority you'd accepted.
4. **Earn the right to disagree.** Disrespecting authority well requires that you've actually done some of the work yourself (`first-principles-thinking.md`, `make-it-concrete.md`). Disagreement from a position of laziness is just contrarianism.
5. **Watch yourself for the inversion.** The worst form of authority-worship is dressing up "I disagree with the experts" as if *that* were the credential. The point is the reasoning, not the posture.

## Worked Examples

- **Resigning from the National Academy of Sciences.** In August 1961 Feynman wrote to Detlev Bronk, the Academy's president, asking to resign. The letter, in full, is the cleanest available statement of his position on credential-by-institution: *"My desire to resign is merely a personal one… I find it psychologically very distasteful to judge people's 'merit.' So I cannot participate in the main activity of selecting people for membership. To be a member of a group, of which an important activity is to choose others deemed worthy of membership in that self-esteemed group, bothers me. The care with which we select 'those worthy of the honor' of joining the Academy feels to me like a form of self-praise."* He closed: *"Your flock will contain one very peculiar, sad and reluctant bird."* The Academy's primary activity, to Feynman, was a closed credential loop — admitting more members so that being a member meant something. He wanted no part of being a person whose endorsement of others' work was the point. (Letter to Detlev W. Bronk, August 10, 1961, *Perfectly Reasonable Deviations from the Beaten Track*, pp. 108–109.)

- **Refusing every honorary degree.** Offered his first honorary degree by the University of Chicago in 1967, Feynman declined — and declined every one offered for the rest of his life. His standing reason: *"I remember the work I did to get a real degree at Princeton and the guys on the same platform receiving honorary degrees without work — and felt an 'honorary degree' was a debasement of the idea of a 'degree which confirms certain work has been accomplished.' It is like giving an 'honorary electricians license.' I swore then that if by chance I was ever offered one I would not accept it."* The criterion was not contempt for those institutions; it was refusal to accept a status marker that didn't track the underlying thing it claimed to mark. (Letter to Dr. George W. Beadle, *Perfectly Reasonable Deviations*, p. 233.)

- **The Challenger Commission, refusing to be managed.** Rogers, the chairman, and the senior commissioners wanted Feynman doing nothing of substance — sitting through "We'll get back to you on that" briefings and waiting for trips to Florida. Feynman went around them, met engineers directly, ran the ice-water demonstration on live TV, and threatened to take his name off the report unless his observations went in as Appendix F. *"I am determined to do the job of finding out what happened — let the chips fall."* The disrespect was for the chairman's authority to manage the inquiry into smoothness; the demonstration and the appendix were the result. (Letter to Gweneth and Michelle Feynman, February 1986, *Perfectly Reasonable Deviations*, p. 401.)

- **Accepting the Nobel under protest.** Feynman seriously considered ducking the Nobel, accepted it reluctantly, and gave a famous statement of why: *"I don't like honors. I appreciate it for the work that I did, and for people who appreciate it, and I know there's a lot of physicists who use my work, I don't need anything else, I don't think there's any sense to anything else. I don't see that it makes any point that someone in the Swedish Academy decides that this work is noble enough to receive a prize — I've already got the prize. The prize is the pleasure of finding the thing out, the kick in the discovery, the observation that other people use it — those are the real things, the honors are unreal to me."* The Academy in Stockholm is functionally the same closed credential loop as the Academy in Washington. The work is the prize. (BBC interview *The Pleasure of Finding Things Out*, 1981.)

- **The "What's the matter with the cocoa/potatoes?" pattern.** As a young busboy, Feynman was told the head chef — *"a German, a great big guy who was King of the Kitchen"* — wanted potatoes peeled the traditional way. Feynman bought an egg-slicer at the five-and-ten, sliced the potatoes in seconds, and sent them back. The chef came storming out, blood vessels bulging, demanding to know what was wrong with the potatoes. The story is small but the pattern is the entire ethic in miniature: the senior person's authority over how-it's-done is not load-bearing if a better way is to hand. (*Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!*, "He Fixes Radios by Thinking!" / early chapters.)

## Coaching Moves

- Practice asking the basic question in rooms where the senior expert is presenting. The question is, "Why do you believe that, specifically?" Done politely, this never goes badly. The expert who answers it well earns your trust; the one who doesn't, doesn't.
- Separate the *signal* in a credential (specialist training, real reps) from the *noise* (institutional ranking, status). Engage the signal; ignore the noise.
- Build the muscle in low-stakes settings so it's available in high-stakes ones. Disagree (politely, with reasons) with the senior person at the casual dinner, not just at the board meeting.
- Reward your own juniors when they ask the basic question of you. The team where the most junior person can ask the CEO "why do you believe that?" without consequence is the team that doesn't fool itself.
- For yourself: when you find yourself deferring, ask whether you'd defer if the same content came from someone with no credential. If yes, the deference is to the content. If no, examine.

## Anti-Patterns

- "All experts are wrong." Authority-worship inverted. Same epistemic failure, opposite slogan.
- Disrespecting authority publicly while privately deferring. The point is the *practice*, not the persona.
- Picking only the easy fights — disagreeing with the safe targets (academics in unrelated fields, etc.) while still deferring to the experts whose authority actually constrains you.
- Treating Feynman's anti-honors stance as a license for self-promotion via anti-establishment posturing. He refused honors *quietly* and continued doing the work. The work is the point.
