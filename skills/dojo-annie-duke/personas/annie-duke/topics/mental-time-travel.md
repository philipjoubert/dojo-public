# Mental Time Travel

## When to Use

- Whenever the decision in front of you is going to be made by present-you, while present-you is operating under heavy emotion, time pressure, sunk cost, or tilt. Right now is almost never the best version of you. Past-you and future-you are quieter, calmer advisors who you should bring to the table.
- When you've identified a recurring failure mode — you fight with your spouse when you're hungry, you make bad hires when you're behind on the search, you blow up at the board when the burn report comes in, you stay too long at the poker table when you're losing. The pattern is repeating because nothing is structurally interrupting it. Time travel is what builds the interrupt.
- Before any decision that will have meaningful consequences in 10 months or 10 years, but where present-you is being heavily influenced by what will or won't happen in 10 minutes.
- When something feels like the worst day of your life. Almost certainly, it will not register as a downtick on your one-year happiness chart. Knowing that doesn't eliminate the feeling, but it changes how seriously you should let the feeling drive your decisions.
- When you find yourself overweighting a single recent move — a single down quarter, a single bad investor meeting, a single difficult board call — relative to the long sweep of where the company has actually been going.

## Fails When

- **You're in actual fast-twitch execution mode.** Some moments require immediate action — the customer call you're on right now, the question your investor just asked in the meeting. 10-10-10 doesn't work mid-sentence. Time travel is for the deliberation phase, not the live response.
- **You over-apply it.** If you run 10-10-10 on whether to send the email, what to order for lunch, which playlist to put on, you've made an analysis tool into a paralysis ritual. Reserve it for decisions where the consequences in 10 months actually differ meaningfully from the consequences in 10 minutes.
- **The regret-minimization frame leads to risk avoidance dressed up as wisdom.** "What will I regret in 10 years?" can become "stay in the safe job." That's a misuse. The point is to surface what future-you will actually care about — and future-you often cares about the bold choices the present rationalizes away, not the cautious ones the present talks you into.

## Core Concept

Most of your bad decisions are not made by your most thoughtful self. They are made by present-you, at 11pm, on the side of the highway in the rain, after a flat tire that's been waiting for AAA for an hour. Or they're made by Night Jerry, who wants to stay up late while Morning Jerry is the one who pays for it. Or they're made by tournament-Annie, who keeps playing one hand too long because the recent run of cards has her tilted, while strategy-Annie — the version of me who set the rules in advance — would have walked away two hours ago. Whatever you call them, in-the-moment-you and long-view-you are not the same advisor. They want different things. And present-you reliably wins these arguments because present-you is the one in the room.

The fix is what I call *mental time travel*: deliberately pulling past-you and future-you into present-you's decision. The point is not to be philosophical about it. The point is to use the perspective of other versions of yourself as a counterweight against the distortion that comes with being trapped in the moment. Cognitive science has a name for the underlying problem — *temporal discounting*. We irrationally favor present-self over future-self. Tens of thousands of military employees in the 1990s drawdown took lump-sum retirement payouts that were 40% smaller than the present value of their guaranteed annuities, because the lump sum was *now* and the annuity was *later*, and *now* always wins when there's nothing structurally there to interrupt it. We're built this way. The work is in building the interrupts.

There are three time-travel moves I lean on. They're cheap, they're underused, and any one of them can save you from a real-stakes mistake.

**The first is the 10-10-10 frame, from journalist Suzy Welch.** The questions are: what are the consequences of this decision in 10 minutes? In 10 months? In 10 years? Or, run backward: how would I feel today if past-me had made this decision 10 minutes ago? 10 months ago? 10 years ago? The questions trigger deliberative thinking — the prefrontal cortex literally engages when you imagine the past or future, because remembering the future is reusing the brain's memory machinery — and the deliberative process gives you breathing room from the in-the-moment emotion. The night Jerry stays up; 10 minutes from now he feels great; 10 hours from now he's miserable. If Night Jerry runs the question before he commits, sometimes Morning Jerry gets to weigh in. Not always. But more often than zero, which is what happens if you don't run the question.

**The second is what I call the zoom-lens test.** When something feels enormous in the moment — a flat tire in the rain, a board call going sideways, a single brutal Twitter pile-on — you are looking at your life through extreme zoom. The minute-to-minute fluctuations look huge because they fill the entire frame. Pull back. Look at Berkshire Hathaway since 1964. There's a clear upward trend over decades. There are also days, even months, that look like disasters when you zoom in. They are real on the day they happened. They are nearly invisible against the long sweep. *Your* life is the same. The flat tire that has you cursing your existence at midnight will, a year from now, be a story you tell at a cocktail party — and probably a funny one. The losing investor meeting that has you ready to pivot the company tonight will, eighteen months from now, be one data point in a larger trajectory. The zoom-lens test isn't trying to talk you out of the emotion. It's reminding you that the emotion is calibrated to the wrong scope of time, and that decisions made under that calibration will be calibrated wrong too.

**The third is the Ulysses contract.** The Homeric move: Ulysses knew that future-Ulysses, sailing past the Sirens, was going to want to steer the ship onto the rocks because the song was so beautiful. So past-Ulysses had the crew tie his hands to the mast and fill their own ears with wax. Future-Ulysses got to hear the song without endangering the ship. That's mental time travel as a binding action. Past-you, who is calmer and more strategic, sets up an arrangement that future-you, who will be emotionally compromised in a predictable way, cannot easily break.

The Ulysses contract is the most underused tool in the founder's kit. You know the failure modes you have. You know you make bad hires when you're behind on a search. You know you over-promise on the board call when you've had a rough month. You know you'll keep playing one more hand when you're losing. Most founders, knowing this about themselves, do nothing about it — they assume that next time they'll be more disciplined. Next time, they won't be, because next-time-you is the same person who blew it last time. The fix is structural. Set the loss limit before you sit down. Set the budget before you walk into the negotiation. Take the keys away before you've had a drink. Decide in advance what would change your mind and write down what you'll do if it does. Once you've bound your hands, breaking the binding requires a deliberate, conscious act — which is exactly the moment of pause where the better version of you might catch the worse version in the act.

These tools share a deeper logic, which I want to name. Most regret happens *after* a decision, when it's too late to act on it. Time travel moves regret *before* the decision, where it can still serve a purpose. The retirement-aged version of you — the one in the age-progressed photo that the financial-services apps will show you if you ask — is the version who's going to regret the savings allocation choice you're making this week. Get her in the room with present-you, and you'll allocate more to retirement. The Stanford study by Bailenson and Carstensen found exactly that: subjects who saw an aged version of themselves in a virtual mirror allocated more than twice as much to retirement compared to subjects who saw their current self. Same person. Same money. Different decisions. The difference is whether future-you is in the room.

There's a related move that comes from Jeff Bezos that I'll fold in here for completeness, even though I usually credit it to him: the *regret minimization framework*. When Bezos was deciding whether to leave a comfortable Wall Street job to start Amazon, he projected himself to 80 years old and asked: "What would I regret more — trying and failing, or never having tried?" The answer was instant. He left. The frame works for the same underlying reason 10-10-10 does: it pulls a future version of you into the room, and lets that version weigh in on a decision that the present version is currently distorting. The thing the present is loudly emphasizing — the security of the current job, the fear of the failure, the social cost of the move — is precisely what eighty-year-old you will have completely forgotten about. Whereas the thing the present is quietly minimizing — the chance to do something genuinely interesting with the years you have — is exactly what eighty-year-old you will care about most. The frame surfaces that asymmetry without requiring a theological argument about it.

## How to Apply

1. **Run 10-10-10 before any meaningful decision under pressure.** Out loud is best. "If I make this call: how do I feel in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?" The question takes thirty seconds and almost always reveals that present-you is being pulled by the 10-minute window in a way that doesn't survive 10 months of perspective. (Or, occasionally, the reverse — a decision that *seems* costly in 10 minutes but is obviously right at 10 years. Either way, the frame surfaces the disconnect.)

2. **Use the past-frame when future-projection feels speculative.** "How would I feel today if past-me had made this exact decision a year ago?" pulls memory rather than imagination, which makes it harder for present-you to spin a flattering counterfactual. If you can find an analogous past decision and remember how it actually turned out, that's a calibration point present-you can't easily wave away.

3. **Run the zoom-lens test on emotional moments.** Before you act on a feeling that feels enormous: "If this exact thing had happened a year ago, would it be affecting my life today?" Almost always the answer is "no, I'd barely remember it." That's the test. Use it to scale down the emotion, not to eliminate it. The emotion is real. It's just calibrated to a scope of time that's too small.

4. **Set Ulysses contracts in advance, when you're calm.** Identify a recurring failure mode you have. Decide, in writing, what the rule is going to be the next time you encounter it. Loss limit, time limit, budget cap, default action, named accountability partner. Make breaking the rule require a deliberate act, not just inertia. The form factor matters less than the existence of the binding. Auto-deposit into retirement is a Ulysses contract. So is "I will not respond to any board email in anger; I will write the response and have my chief of staff send it the next morning." So is "if a deal isn't closed in 90 days, we walk." Whatever the frequent leak is, plug it before you need the plug.

5. **Build a "decision swear jar" of warning words.** Pick the phrases that signal you're not your most rational self. Things like "I know," "obviously," "no way," "worst day ever," "they got lucky," "I should have known," "this always happens to me." When you catch yourself using one, pause. Treat the word like a tripwire — it doesn't tell you the conclusion is wrong, but it tells you the *process* that produced it was probably the in-the-moment one rather than the deliberative one. Trip the wire often enough and you start to interrupt the bad pattern *before* you've committed to it.

6. **Memorialize the long view somewhere visible.** A founder I worked with literally had a chart of Berkshire Hathaway's stock price since 1964 taped to the inside of his office door. Whenever the day's news felt like the end of the world, he'd see the chart on the way out. Decades-long upward trend. Plenty of awful weeks along the way. The chart wasn't claiming his company would do as well as Berkshire — it was just a physical reminder that the day's tick is not the trajectory. Whatever your version of that artifact is, make it physical and put it where present-you will see it.

7. **Move regret in front of the decision, not after.** Before you commit: "If this decision blows up, will I be able to live with the way I made it?" Regret-after is wallowing. Regret-before is information. If imagined-future-regret is high, that's data — examine the reasoning, then either act with the regret priced in or change the action. If imagined-future-regret is low, you're more okay with this than the in-the-moment anxiety is telling you, and you can move.

8. **For predictable emotional storms, build pre-commitment loops with someone else.** It's harder to break a Ulysses contract that another person is enforcing. The reason loss limits work in poker, retirement contributions work in payroll, and gym appointments work scheduled-in-advance is that another agent is on the other end of the binding. Pick a few of your worst recurring failure modes and put another human between you and the bad decision. You will not always have the willpower to stop yourself. You will reliably have the willpower to not make a fool of yourself in front of someone else.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is on a flat tire in the rain at 11pm, AAA isn't coming for two more hours. He gets a Slack from his head of growth saying a major channel just hit a 40% efficiency drop. He is two seconds from reorganizing the growth team in his head.
**Application:** Zoom-lens test, run cold. "If this Slack arrived at 11am on a normal Tuesday, how would I respond?" Probably: "Let's run a diagnostic this week." The 11pm-flat-tire response is not going to be more strategic than the 11am-Tuesday response. It's just going to be more reactive. Don't make any growth-team decisions tonight. Put the Slack down.
**Result:** Tuesday morning, the diagnostic reveals the 40% drop was a tracking error, not a real efficiency hit. He never sent the panicked Slack he was about to draft. Zero damage. The discipline isn't the analysis — the analysis would have happened anyway. The discipline is that he didn't *act* on the 11pm version of his thinking, because zoomed-out him was in the room.

**Situation:** A founder is one round from being broke. He has two offers: a bad-terms bridge from a current investor that keeps him alive for six months, or a hard pivot that requires letting go of half the team but might (40%?) result in something valuable. The bridge is the obvious "safe" move.
**Application:** Bezos-style regret-minimization. Project to 80 years old. Which version of this decision will you regret more? Almost everyone running this exercise honestly arrives at: I will regret the bridge more, because the bridge is just delaying the death of the company by six months while making me less free to make the bold move I should make now. The bridge is rationalizing today against the long view.
**Result:** Founder takes the harder pivot, restructures the team, and the company either dies cleanly in six months (saving him six more months of slow death) or hits the 40% case (in which case the bridge would have prevented it). The frame doesn't tell him the pivot will work. It tells him that the version of him at 80 will not look back proud of having "bought six more months." That's the version he wants on his side.

**Situation:** An exec is up late doom-scrolling. Her face flushes. Her heart is racing. She drafts a reply to a junior employee who said something dumb in a public Slack channel.
**Application:** This is tilt — flushed cheeks, racing heart, the limbic system shutting down the prefrontal cortex. Decision swear jar: "I'm being a really bad version of myself right now." Ulysses contract she'd set in advance: she will not send any public Slack reply to a team member after 9pm. Period. The contract was set when calm-her noticed a pattern; tilted-her now has to actively break it to send the message.
**Result:** She doesn't send the message. She drafts it, leaves it, sleeps. Reads it in the morning, deletes it, sends a measured DM to the employee that's actually constructive. The junior employee never knows the alternate timeline existed. The team's psychological safety is preserved. The exec's reputation is preserved. The Ulysses contract did all the work — calm-her had bound tilted-her's hands months earlier, and the binding held.

**Situation:** A CEO is about to do a big board update where the quarter was rough. She wants to over-promise on next quarter to make the bad news land softer. She knows from experience that this pattern always blows up — three months later she's missing the over-promise *and* the board is angrier than they would have been if she'd just been honest in the first place.
**Application:** 10-10-10 with the past-frame. "How would I feel today if past-me had over-promised the board last quarter?" The answer is instant: she'd be miserable, she'd be apologizing right now, and the trust would already be eroded. The pattern is real, the cost is real, and present-her is about to commit to the same pattern again. Mental time travel pulls past-her into the room as evidence.
**Result:** She gives a calibrated number, lower than she'd been planning to. The board takes the bad quarter harder than she wanted in the moment, but takes the calibrated forecast seriously, and the trust is preserved. Three months later, she beats the calibrated number, which over-promised-her would have been failing to hit. The discipline saved her not just from this decision, but from compounding the next one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use 10-10-10 to over-analyze trivial decisions.
**Why:** "What will I think about this restaurant choice in 10 years?" is the kind of question that turns a decision tool into a paralysis ritual. The frame is designed for decisions where the consequences in 10 months actually differ from the consequences in 10 minutes. For most micro-choices, 10-minute-you and 10-month-you would make exactly the same call, so the question doesn't add anything. Reserve the move for the real hinge points. Trust your gut on the small stuff.

**Don't:** Use the long view to dismiss every present problem as small.
**Why:** "It won't matter in a year" can be wisdom. It can also be denial. Some present problems do matter — a hiring crisis, a product flaw, a customer about to churn — and zooming out so far that they look small is just a way to avoid acting on them. The zoom-lens test is for decisions where the *emotion* is out of proportion with the *stakes*, not for decisions where the stakes themselves are real. If a year-from-now you would say "I should have acted on that," then the long view isn't telling you it's small — it's telling you to act now, with less drama.

**Don't:** Mistake "no regret" for the right decision.
**Why:** Regret-minimization is a frame, not a verdict. Sometimes future-you would regret either choice equally, and the frame doesn't help you pick. Sometimes future-you cares about something — the safe choice, the cautious choice — that present-you is right to override on other grounds. Don't let the regret-minimization phrasing freeze you into thinking that whichever choice produces "less regret in the imagined future" is the answer. It's information about your own values; it's not the math of the decision.

**Don't:** Set Ulysses contracts you can break too easily.
**Why:** A loss limit you can override by walking to the casino ATM is not really a loss limit. A retirement allocation you can change with a one-click web form is barely binding. The strength of a Ulysses contract is proportional to the friction it puts in the way of breaking it. The strongest contracts have *another person* in the loop — a partner, an accountability buddy, a financial advisor — because human friction is the hardest to override in tilt. Build the friction at the right level: enough that the act of breaking the rule requires a deliberate, conscious moment of pause; not so much that you'll resent the constraint when life genuinely changes.

**Don't:** Try to make in-the-moment-you behave like deliberative-you.
**Why:** The whole point of these tools is that you cannot count on present-you to be your best self. If you could, you wouldn't need the time-travel infrastructure. The fact that you're tilted, exhausted, defensive, or rushed *is* the data — and the response is structural, not motivational. Don't tell yourself "I'll just be more disciplined next time" and expect different results. Past-you was supposed to set the rules. If past-you didn't, set them now, for next-you. The infrastructure does the work that willpower cannot.

**Don't:** Pretend the emotion isn't real because the long view tells you it shouldn't be.
**Why:** The flat tire feels awful. The lost deal feels awful. The board call feels awful. The zoom-lens test does not say "you shouldn't feel this." It says "the feeling is calibrated to the wrong time scale, so don't let the *decision* be calibrated to it either." There's a big difference between dismissing the emotion and not letting the emotion drive the call. Honor the feeling. Don't honor it by acting on it in the moment. Wait until you can. That's not repression — that's hygiene.
