# Kill Criteria — States and Dates

## When to Use

- You're starting any commitment of nontrivial size — a project, a hire, a fundraise, a relationship, a major purchase, a deal-making process — and you want to keep the option to quit live.
- You set a goal, and you want to make sure the goal doesn't become a finish line you'll cross with a broken leg.
- You manage a team or process where consistent quit decisions matter — sales funnels, R&D, capital allocation, hiring trials.
- You've noticed that you systematically miss the moment when you should have walked away, and you want to design that moment in *before* you're in it.
- You're building a culture that can shut things down without shame.

## Fails When

- **You're already in it and reaching for kill criteria as permission to quit.** Pre-commitment is a tool you set *before* the gravity of the situation gets you. After you're in it, "let me set kill criteria now" is usually rationalization for a decision you've already made (which might still be the right decision — just don't pretend it came from the framework).
- **You over-rigidify and ignore real signal.** Kill criteria are pre-commitments, not blindfolds. If something dramatic happens that the criteria didn't anticipate, you re-evaluate. The point is to raise the cost of "I'll just keep going," not to substitute mechanical rules for judgment.
- **The activity is too early or too small.** Setting kill criteria for a Tuesday afternoon experiment is overhead theater. Setting them for a weekend trip is overkill. Use the tool when the stakes warrant — when getting it wrong costs you a meaningful chunk of time, money, attention, or wellbeing.

## Core Concept

The strongest single idea in the quitting toolkit is the *pre-commitment contract* — a decision made in advance about when you'll walk away. The form I use, and the form I teach, is **states and dates**: *if I am in state X by date Y, I quit.* Or *if I haven't reached state X by date Y, I quit.* Or *if I've spent Y on something and haven't reached X, I quit.* The form is mundane; the leverage is enormous.

Why pre-commitment? Because Daniel Kahneman is right that the worst time to make a decision is when you're "in it." When you're in it, every cognitive bias the field has documented is firing at once — sunk cost, escalation, endowment, status quo, identity (see `why-we-stay-too-long.md`). The version of you that has to make the quit call in the moment is not the version of you who would make the best decision. The version who *would* make the best decision is the version who hasn't started yet — fresh, no katamari, no commitment to defend. Kill criteria are the move that lets the un-invested version of you set the rules that the invested version of you has to follow.

This is a Ulysses contract, in the Homeric sense. Ulysses tied himself to the mast before the song of the sirens reached him because he knew that once he heard them, he wouldn't have the sense to plug his ears or steer away. He couldn't trust his future, in-it self. So he transferred the decision to his pre-it self, and used physical structure (the mast and the ropes) to bind the future self to the earlier decision. Kill criteria are the same move, applied to anything you'll be tempted to over-persist in. You can't trust your future self because your future self will be running a defense, not an analysis. So your present self writes the rule and binds the future. Set the rule simply, set it specifically, and write it down.

The states-and-dates form matters because it forces specificity. "I'll quit when this isn't working anymore" is useless — you'll never feel that the moment has arrived, because each individual day looks like the day before. "If I haven't gotten an executive in the room by the next meeting, I'll kill the deal" is useful, because it has a state (executive in the room), a date (next meeting), and a binary trigger (yes/no). Admiral McRaven, planning the bin Laden raid, broke the entire mission into 162 phases, each with state-and-date kill criteria — *if you reach this point and X hasn't happened, abort.* The advance work meant that on the night, McRaven only had about five command decisions to make in real time. The other 157 had been pre-decided. That's the standard you're aiming for.

The flip side of kill criteria is the **myopia of goals**, and this is the part most people miss. We set goals because goals work — they're motivating, they focus effort, they produce more output than vague aspirations. But goals come with a side effect: they're pass-fail. Anything short of the finish line feels like failure. And once you've set a finish line, the world can change and your finish line will not — you'll keep running toward a target that no longer makes sense. Goals are inflexible objects in a flexible world.

The story I tell is **Siobhan O'Keeffe** at the 2019 London Marathon. Trained four months. Hoping to finish in five hours. Her ankle started hurting four miles in. She kept running. Eight miles in, her fibula snapped in half. Medics urged her to stop. She refused. *She ran the last eighteen miles on a broken leg* and finished in 6:14:20. And she's not exceptional. The same day, same marathon, same eight-mile mark, *another runner broke his foot and ran the remaining eighteen miles on it.* In Edinburgh four weeks later, another fractured his fibula at mile sixteen and dragged it the last ten miles. In 2014, same eight-mile water bottle, same broken foot, same eighteen-mile finish. In 2012, Darren Oliver broke his leg at mile one and ran twenty-five miles in agony to the finish.

Why? *Because there's a finish line.* Goals are pass-fail. You either reach the line or you don't. Once you start, the only thing that matters is closing the mental account in the gains, and partial progress — twenty-six excellent miles — counts for nothing if you stop short of 26.2. Your leg can break and you'll keep going, because *not finishing* is unbearable in a way that *finishing on a broken leg* is not. The goal occludes the actual decision. You set the goal of running a marathon to be healthier, accomplished, to enjoy the run. None of those is served by a fractured fibula. But the proxy — *finish the race* — has eaten the original goals.

The fix is adding an "*unless*" to every goal. *I'm going to finish this marathon **unless** I break a bone.* *I'm going to keep developing this product **unless** I fail to hit clear benchmarks within two months.* *I'm going to keep climbing **unless** the turnaround time arrives without me at the summit.* The unless is the kill criterion. It turns a pass-fail goal into a flexible commitment that respects the world's right to change while you're pursuing it.

Rob Hall's turnaround time on Everest is the most beautiful version. In 1995 he turned client Doug Hansen around 300 feet from the summit and saved Hansen's life. The next year, same mountain, same client, Hall waited two hours past his own turnaround time. They both died on the descent. Same person, same rule, same client. Worked when he honored it. Killed both of them when he didn't. Kill criteria are only as good as your commitment to act on them — which is why pre-committing them, in writing, with an outsider as witness if possible, dramatically increases the probability they fire.

## How to Apply

1. **Set criteria *before* you start, not after you're committed.** This is the whole point. The version of you setting the criteria has to be the un-invested version. If you're already a year into the project and you're setting kill criteria today, the criteria you set will be lenient enough that you don't have to act on them. Set them before. Write them down. Tell someone.

2. **Use the states-and-dates form.** Every kill criterion has a *state* (the measurable condition) and a *date* (the deadline). "If I'm not at $50K monthly recurring revenue by month nine, I shut this down." "If I haven't gotten an executive in the room by the third sales meeting, I drop the deal." "If we haven't trained the monkey in six months, we kill the project." States and dates force specificity, which forces the criterion to be one you can actually evaluate.

3. **Tie kill criteria to monkeys, not pedestals.** The right state to track is whether the high-uncertainty piece of the project — the monkey from `monkeys-and-pedestals.md` — is showing signs of being trainable. *Wrong:* "If we haven't shipped the redesign by Q3, we kill it." (Pedestal-tracking.) *Right:* "If our retention curve hasn't flattened by Q3, we kill it." (Monkey-tracking.) Pedestal-criteria let you escalate by switching pedestals; monkey-criteria don't.

4. **Anticipate the rationalizations.** When you set the criterion, also write down the most likely arguments your future self will make to ignore it. *"Q3 was an unusually rough quarter; we should give it one more."* *"We've made huge progress on adjacent metrics."* *"The team is hitting their stride right now."* If you've pre-named the rationalization, when it shows up you recognize it as the predicted defense rather than as a fresh insight. This single move — pre-listing the excuses — is one of the highest-leverage things you can do, because in-the-moment biases mostly run through plausible-sounding excuses, and a named excuse is an excuse you can refuse.

5. **Add an "unless" to every goal.** When you set a finish line, set the conditions under which you'll abandon the finish line. *I'm going to finish this marathon unless I break a bone or my heart rate stays above zone five for more than five miles.* *I'm going to summit Everest unless I'm not at the South Summit by 11 AM.* *I'm going to push to close this acquisition unless the diligence reveals X, Y, or Z.* The unless makes a flexible commitment in a flexible world.

6. **Write them down. Share them with someone who'll hold you to them.** Kill criteria you keep in your head are kill criteria you'll renegotiate with yourself. Kill criteria you write down and share are harder to argue away. The combination of kill criteria + a quitting coach (see `the-quit-decision.md`) is the single most effective structure for actually shutting things down on time. Especially when the coach has authority — McRaven aborting a mission because the helicopter count fell below six — the combination is gold.

7. **Plan the post-mortem at the same time as the criteria.** When you set the criteria, schedule the meeting where you'll evaluate them. Not "we'll check in around then." A specific date, on the calendar, with whoever needs to be there. The fact that the meeting exists is half the battle — without it, the date drifts and the criterion never gets evaluated.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS company's sales team consistently spends two months on doomed deals before walking away. The leader wants to install kill criteria.
**Application:** This is a real client of mine, mParticle. We walked through what early signals correlate with deals that closed vs. deals that died. Out of that came states-and-dates: *If the prospect immediately wants to talk price, you're being used as a stalking horse — kill the deal.* *If you can't get an executive in the room by the second meeting, propose executive alignment for the next; if you still can't, kill the deal.* Each becomes an explicit criterion the AE evaluates with the next meeting as the trigger.
**Result:** Sellers spend less time on deals that won't close. Compensation is updated so AEs are evaluated not just on revenue closed but on funnel hygiene — how well they kill leads on time. Kill discipline becomes a competence the team gets *rewarded* for. Funnel velocity goes up, win rate climbs.

**Situation:** A founder is about to start a new company. She wants to give it a real shot but doesn't want to be, three years from now, the founder of a zombie company she can't bring herself to wind down.
**Application:** Set criteria at the start. Write: *If we haven't reached $X ARR by month 18, we wind down. If retention is below Y by month 12, we wind down or pivot. If we haven't closed a Series A by month 24, we wind down or transition to profitability.* For each, list the rationalizations you'll be tempted to make ("the market was bad"; "we just had a key engineer leave") and pre-commit to ignoring them. Identify the quitting coach and give them the right to call the meeting when the date arrives.
**Result:** Two outcomes. A: she hit the criteria, the company is working. B: she didn't, she wound down at month 18 instead of month 36, freeing eighteen months and a chunk of investor capital for the next thing. B is the outcome kill criteria *create* — a clean exit at the right time, instead of the dragged-out three-year zombie that's the default.

**Situation:** A senior PhD student in the humanities, two years into the academic job market, hasn't gotten a tenure-track offer. The norm in the field is that leaving academia is a one-way decision; it's exceedingly hard to come back.
**Application:** This is Kevin Zollman's example. The states-and-dates approach is unambiguous here: figure out the average time-to-tenure-track placement in the field. If it's, say, four years post-PhD, set the date at four years. State: *I have a tenure-track offer.* If date arrives without state, you quit. The kill criterion is set in advance, with the field-average data, before the gravity of every "just one more application cycle" gets a chance to operate.
**Result:** People who set the criterion at four years quit at four years, on bad outcomes. People who don't quit at six, eight, twelve — by which point the cost of the missing alternative life is enormous. Set early, decide in advance, save the years.

**Situation:** A runner sets a goal to finish the London Marathon in five hours. Ankle starts hurting at mile four. Pain getting worse.
**Application:** The whole problem is that the goal — finish the marathon — was set without an unless. Add the unless at the start: *I will finish the marathon unless I have meaningful pain past mile six, or my heart rate exceeds zone five sustained, or I sustain a fall.* With the unless built in, mile six becomes a real decision point. The goal stops being pure pass-fail. The runner can quit the race without quitting *the larger goal* — being a healthy, distance-running person who'll run another marathon. Without the unless, the goal eats the larger goal: the marathon becomes the only thing that matters, and you find yourself at mile twenty-five on a broken leg.
**Result:** Set the unless. Finish the next race. Don't be Siobhan O'Keeffe. Or — to be precise — be the version of Siobhan O'Keeffe who set an unless and stopped at mile six, and who is running marathons two years later because her fibula was never broken in the first place.

**Situation:** A founder set kill criteria eighteen months ago. The deadline is now. The criteria haven't been hit. He's in the meeting where the decision happens.
**Application:** The hardest part of kill criteria is honoring them when the moment arrives. All five forces are firing. He'll have arguments ready: the metrics are imperfect, timing was off, next quarter looks different. The pre-listed rationalizations are right there in the document he wrote eighteen months ago, and many match the arguments he's now making. The role of the quitting coach is to read those rationalizations back: *you predicted you'd say all of this; you said in advance these wouldn't be sufficient reasons; what's actually new?*
**Result:** With the structure in place, he honors the criterion and shuts the company down. Six months later, the next thing is well underway and he says, with embarrassment, that he's glad the rule was there because he wouldn't have made the call cold. That's the pre-commitment doing its job. The whole point is that it makes the call when you can't.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Set kill criteria after you're already deep in.
**Why:** They don't work as retroactive permission to quit. They work as advance binding. If you're already a year in, you'll set criteria that you've already met (so you can quit immediately) or that you're conveniently close to (so you can rationalize either direction). The discipline of pre-commitment requires the un-invested version of you to set the rule. Apply it forward to the *next* commitment, not backward to the current one. (For the current one, use the close-call heuristic from `the-quit-decision.md`.)

**Don't:** Set criteria so vague they can't fire.
**Why:** "If things aren't working" is not a kill criterion. It's a feeling. The whole point of states-and-dates is that the criterion is binary and externally observable. If you can't tell from the outside whether the criterion has been met, you'll never let it fire from the inside. *Specific* and *measurable* are not optional.

**Don't:** Set criteria so rigid they ignore real new information.
**Why:** Pre-commitment is not blindness. If something genuinely unforeseen happens — a regulatory change, a key competitor exit, a new technical capability — that wasn't part of the world when you set the criterion, you can revise. The bar is high: the new information has to be *materially different* from what you anticipated, not just "things turned out differently than I hoped." The pre-listed rationalizations exercise (step 4 above) is the test. If your "new information" matches one of the rationalizations you predicted, it isn't new, it's the predicted excuse.

**Don't:** Use kill criteria as a substitute for thinking.
**Why:** Mechanical rule-following is its own pathology. The criterion is a forcing function for a serious conversation, not a replacement for one. When the date arrives and the state isn't met, the meeting happens, the data gets reviewed, and the decision gets made — informed by the criterion but not made *by* the criterion. The presence of the criterion is what makes the meeting happen and what raises the bar for "let's give it another quarter." It doesn't tell you, definitively, what to decide. It tells you that you have to decide *now*, with discipline.

**Don't:** Set criteria privately and never share them.
**Why:** Private criteria are renegotiable with yourself, which means they don't bind. Shared criteria, especially with a person who has standing to invoke them, are much harder to argue away. McRaven could abort the bin Laden raid because his kill criteria were known to his command structure, his decision authority was clear, and the criteria were preset in the operational plan. Private kill criteria you tell only yourself are what you say at the start of every doomed project — *I'll know when to quit.* You won't.

**Don't:** Confuse the goal with the criterion.
**Why:** The goal is the finish line. The criterion is the unless. They're different objects. You don't replace the goal with the criterion — you augment the goal with the unless that protects you from goal-induced myopia. *Goal: run a marathon. Unless: a fracture, sustained zone-five HR, a fall.* Both. Together. The goal motivates; the unless protects. Without the goal, you don't train. Without the unless, you finish on a broken leg. You need both, and the absence of either is a failure mode.
