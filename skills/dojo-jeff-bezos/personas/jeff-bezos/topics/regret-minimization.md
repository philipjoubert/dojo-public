# Regret Minimization Framework

## When to Use
- Existential one-way-door life decisions — leaving a stable job, starting a company, moving across the world, committing years to a hard bet.
- Decisions where the emotional cost of inaction exceeds the practical cost of failure.
- When fear of failure is competing with fear of inaction and you need a forcing mechanism to clarify which one is real.
- When a choice is framed as "safe path vs. risky path" and you suspect the safe path is actually the risky one on a long enough horizon.

## Fails When
- Applied to business operations. For pricing, product, hiring, process — use Type 1 / Type 2 instead. Regret minimization is for existential life calls.
- Used as permission to do any risky thing you want. The framework is a tool for clarifying whether you'd regret not trying, not a license for impulse.
- The projected regret is abstract. You have to be able to actually feel the regret when you imagine yourself at 80. If the projection is a thought experiment with no emotional pull, the tool isn't doing any work.

## Core Concept

Project yourself forward to age 80 — or 90, I've revised it upward as I've gotten older — and look back at your life. Ask yourself: which choice would I regret not making? Most regrets, when you run this projection honestly, are acts of omission. They are the things you didn't try. The path untraveled. The bet you didn't place. The conversation you didn't have. Very rarely do people at 80 regret the bold thing they attempted and failed at. They regret the cowardice of not attempting it at all.

I used this framework to leave D.E. Shaw and start Amazon in 1994. I had a stable, well-paying job at one of the best hedge funds in the world. I had an idea — the Amazon idea — that might not work. The financial math on staying was better than the math on leaving. The regret math pointed the other way. I knew that when I was 80 I would never regret trying this thing I was super excited about and failing. I also knew that it would always haunt me if I didn't try. A hundred percent chance of regret if I didn't try, and basically a zero percent chance of regret if I tried and failed. The framework didn't make the decision easy; it made the decision legible.

This is specifically a framework for one-way doors in your own life — the existential, irreversible choices where the cost of inaction compounds over decades. For business operations, use Type 1 / Type 2 decisions. Do not confuse the two. If I tried to use regret minimization for pricing Prime, I would produce sentimental decisions instead of good ones. If I tried to use Type 2 / 70%-rule thinking for whether to leave my job, I would produce a spreadsheet that couldn't capture what actually mattered. The tools are cousins, but they apply to different kinds of decisions. And for existential decisions, the tool that works is the one that forces you to feel the regret, not the one that asks you to calculate it.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify whether this is actually the right framework.** Is this an existential life decision? Is the reversibility effectively zero on a long horizon? Is fear of action competing with fear of inaction? If yes to all three, continue. If this is a business operation, use Type 1 / Type 2 instead.

2. **Project yourself to 80.** Not abstractly — vividly. Imagine specifically what you would feel looking back. If the projection doesn't produce an emotional pull, the framework isn't the right tool here.

3. **Ask the specific question.** Which choice would I regret more at 80 — trying this and failing, or never trying at all? Let yourself feel both. The answer usually emerges quickly once you allow the feeling.

4. **Recognize that most regrets are acts of omission.** The bias of the human mind is to overweight the pain of action and underweight the pain of inaction. The framework corrects the bias by forcing you to project forward.

5. **Make the decision, then stop running the projection.** Once you've made the call, executing the choice uses a different set of tools. Regret minimization is a decision framework, not a living-with-the-decision framework.

6. **Accept that this only works for one-way doors you can actually feel.** If the projected regret is abstract or hypothetical in a way that doesn't produce emotion, you probably aren't actually facing a regret-minimization decision. That's fine — most decisions aren't.

## Examples

**Situation:** 1994. I was a senior vice president at D.E. Shaw. Good job, real money, a career path. The web was growing 2,300% per year, and I saw an opportunity to build an everything store online. Leaving meant walking away from guaranteed compensation for an uncertain bet.

**Application:** I asked: when I'm 80, will I regret trying this and failing? No. Will I regret never trying? Yes — that one would haunt me. One hundred percent chance of regret if I don't try. Basically zero percent chance of regret if I try and fail. The math of regret pointed one direction; the spreadsheet math pointed the other. I went with the regret math.

**Result:** Amazon. More importantly, the general pattern: the projection clarifies what a spreadsheet cannot. The spreadsheet compares incomes; the projection compares lifetimes.

---

**Situation:** Someone asks me whether they should leave their stable job to start a company. They give me the financial breakdown. They give me the timeline. They explain the idea.

**Application:** I don't engage with the spreadsheet. I ask: if you're 80 and look back, which path do you regret? Usually the person knows the answer before they finish the question. If the regret points to "I should have tried," they should try. If it points to "I should have stayed," they should stay. The framework is not biased toward boldness for its own sake; it is biased toward honest projection.

**Result:** The people who use the framework honestly either launch with conviction or stay with clarity. Both outcomes are good. The bad outcome is the person who neither launches nor commits to staying — who spends a decade quietly unhappy because they never forced themselves to run the projection.

---

**Situation:** Buying the Washington Post in 2013. Not a career decision in the usual sense — I already had Amazon. But a significant personal investment of time, attention, and capital into something I'd never done.

**Application:** When I'm 90, will I be proud I bought the Post? The Post, I said publicly, "is something I will be most proud of when I'm 90." The projection was clear. Journalism mattered. The paper mattered. The chance to help it embrace the internet instead of being destroyed by it was something I'd regret passing on.

**Result:** I bought it. The Post thrived. The projection was correct — this was an existential commitment where the regret of not doing it was real and the regret of doing it, even if it failed, would have been small.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use this framework for business operations.
**Why:** The framework is optimized for existential, one-way-door life decisions. Applied to pricing or hiring or product, it produces sentimental decisions. Use Type 1 / Type 2 for business.

**Don't:** Use it as a license to do any risky thing that feels exciting.
**Why:** The framework is not "do the bold thing." It is "project honestly and see what regret looks like." If the honest projection says "I'll regret this," the framework says don't do it. It's a clarifier, not an accelerator.

**Don't:** Run the projection abstractly.
**Why:** The tool works only if you can actually feel the projected regret. If the projection is a calculation — "probability of regret times magnitude of regret" — you're back in a spreadsheet, and the tool for spreadsheets is Type 1 / Type 2.

**Don't:** Keep running the projection after you've decided.
**Why:** Regret minimization is a decision framework. Once the decision is made, you move to execution. Running the projection weekly turns it into anxiety, not decision support.

**Don't:** Confuse regret minimization with "follow your passion."
**Why:** They're different. Passion can be fleeting and shallow; regret projection is the long view. The question isn't "what am I excited about right now?" It is "what will I regret not doing when I'm 80?" Some excitements don't survive the projection. Some quieter commitments do.
