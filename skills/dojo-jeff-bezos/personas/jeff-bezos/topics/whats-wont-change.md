# What Won't Change (Long-Term Invariants)

## When to Use
- Designing a long-term strategy that has to survive multiple trend cycles.
- Evaluating which investments will compound over a decade versus which will get obsolete.
- Framing to investors, employees, or yourself why the company is spending on decade-long bets instead of quarter-long ones.
- Checking a strategic proposal — does this anchor to something durable, or is it chasing a temporary condition?

## Fails When
- The identified invariants aren't actually stable. Some "invariants" are really just current conditions that the thinker mistook for permanent truths.
- Applied to businesses where customer needs genuinely are changing fast — biotech, consumer software in the AI era, certain fashion segments. In those, what was true a year ago may not be true a year from now.
- Used to justify refusing to adapt. The framework says "anchor strategy to invariants." It does not say "ignore everything else." Trends, technology shifts, and competitive dynamics still require response.

## Core Concept

I get asked the question a lot: what's going to change in the next ten years? It's an interesting question. It's also the wrong question — or at least, it's only half of the right question. The more useful question is: what's NOT going to change in the next ten years? I almost never get asked that. And in my view, it's actually the more important question, because you can build a long-term strategy around the things that won't change. Strategies built around trends have to keep adapting as the trend evolves. Strategies built around invariants get more valuable with every passing year.

For Amazon's retail business, the invariants are obvious — so obvious that people underweight them, which is exactly why they're so strategically valuable. Customers want lower prices. Customers want faster delivery. Customers want larger selection. Nobody is ever going to come to me in ten years and say, "Jeff, I love Amazon. I just wish the prices were a little higher." Or "I love Amazon, I just wish you delivered a little more slowly." Or "I love Amazon, I just wish your selection were more limited." These preferences are so fundamental, so human, that it is safe to pour a decade of investment into making them better. When you know what won't change, you know exactly where to put the energy that compounds.

The counter-intuitive part: the boring, obvious customer needs are the most powerful strategic foundations precisely because they're so stable. Most strategy advice says "find the next big thing." I say find the thing that will never change and pour everything into it. The next big thing is hard to predict and often wrong; the thing that will never change is easy to identify and still under-invested in. Blue Origin operates on the same premise — nobody will ever want less reliable rockets, so reliability is the invariant, and the motto is gradatim ferociter, step by step ferociously. The Washington Post operates on the same premise — readers come to a newspaper because they trust the staff's editorial judgment, not because they want a personalized AI experience. When Marty Baron told me he expected me to try to personalize the homepage for every reader, I surprised him by saying no. The invariant — trust in staff editorial judgment — was the thing to protect.

The framework pairs with another question: what EXTERNAL trends do I need to embrace? Invariants tell you where to anchor. Trends tell you what to ride. A strategy that only anchors to invariants misses the trend tailwinds. A strategy that only chases trends has no foundation. Do both: identify what won't change, build your foundation there, and embrace the external trends that let you deliver on those invariants better than anyone else. AI, machine learning, cloud, mobile — these are trends. Fast delivery, low prices, large selection — these are invariants. The trends get harnessed to serve the invariants. That's how you get durable advantage out of both.

## How to Apply

1. **State the invariants for your business explicitly.** Write them down. For Amazon retail: lower prices, faster delivery, larger selection. For your business: what customer needs will still be true in ten years? Be specific.

2. **Test whether each claimed invariant is actually stable.** Ask: could a customer in ten years plausibly say "I wish this was less of a thing"? If yes, it isn't an invariant — it's a current preference that might change. Cut it.

3. **Pour investment into the invariants.** This is where compounding works. Every dollar spent making delivery faster in 2024 is still paying off in 2034, because faster delivery is still what customers want in 2034. The invariant-anchored investment has a 10+ year half-life.

4. **Separate invariants from trends.** Invariants are about customer needs. Trends are about how those needs get delivered. Cloud computing is a trend; wanting reliable infrastructure is an invariant. Machine learning is a trend; wanting relevant recommendations is an invariant. The trends change; the underlying needs don't.

5. **Embrace the external trends that serve the invariants.** The right question is: which emerging trend helps us deliver on our invariants better? Ride those hard. Ignore the trends that don't connect to the invariants — even if they look exciting.

6. **Re-check your invariants every few years.** Most don't change. Some do. Customer preferences can shift over decades even when they feel permanent. Audit the list. Keep what's still true. Update what's drifted.

7. **Teach the invariants.** Everyone in the company should be able to name the invariants. They should inform hiring, product decisions, pricing, every major call. If only the CEO knows what won't change, the organization can't align to it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Strategic planning at Amazon. The question on the table: where should we invest the next decade of capital and attention?

**Application:** I reframed. Don't ask what will change in the next ten years — ask what won't. Customers will still want lower prices. They will still want faster delivery. They will still want bigger selection. Those are the invariants. Pour capital into them. Every dollar spent in 2010 making delivery faster is still working for us in 2024. Every dollar spent chasing a trend that faded is gone.

**Result:** The strategy anchored on invariants. Investments in fulfillment, Prime, marketplace — all of these were investments in the invariants. They compounded. The strategy didn't need to be reinvented with every technology cycle, because the foundation was stable.

---

**Situation:** Blue Origin. The question: what kind of space company should this be? Fast and risky, like some competitors? Slow and reliable? Somewhere in between?

**Application:** The invariant. Nobody is ever going to want less reliable rockets. The customer — whether astronauts or cargo or satellites — is going to want reliability forever. The right motto is gradatim ferociter: step by step, ferociously. Build reliability methodically. Let the compounding happen. The trend of commercial space exists; the invariant of reliability is stronger than any trend.

**Result:** Blue Origin's culture and engineering approach anchor on reliability. The company moves slower than some alternatives and chooses that explicitly, because the invariant justifies the pace.

---

**Situation:** The Washington Post. Editor Marty Baron expected me to personalize the homepage for every reader. The technology existed. The trend was toward personalization. The move would have looked innovative.

**Application:** I checked the invariant. Why do readers come to a newspaper? They come because they trust the staff's editorial judgment about what matters. Personalization undermines that — it replaces "the trusted newsroom decided this matters" with "an algorithm picked this for you." The trend said personalize; the invariant said don't. I chose the invariant.

**Result:** The Post didn't personalize the homepage. The editorial identity of the paper strengthened. Baron said I didn't try to reinvent the paper; I tried to capture what made it special. That's what anchoring to invariants looks like in practice.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mistake current conditions for invariants.
**Why:** Some things feel permanent and aren't. Test each claimed invariant: could a customer in ten years plausibly say they wished this was less of a thing? If yes, it's not an invariant. Prune aggressively.

**Don't:** Chase every exciting trend as if it were an invariant.
**Why:** Trends change. Strategies anchored only to trends have to keep reinventing themselves. The compounding doesn't happen because the ground keeps shifting. Anchor to invariants; harness trends.

**Don't:** Refuse to adapt because "the invariants are stable."
**Why:** The invariants are what to anchor to. They don't mean "don't change anything." Trends, technology shifts, and competitive responses still require adaptation — adaptation in service of the invariants.

**Don't:** Let the invariants list grow long.
**Why:** Three to five invariants is usually the right number. A list of fifteen invariants is a list that hasn't been tested; most of them are probably current preferences that feel permanent. The discipline is ruthless pruning.

**Don't:** Keep the invariants secret to leadership.
**Why:** Every person in the company should be able to name the invariants. They inform every decision. If only the CEO knows what won't change, the organization can't align to it, and alignment to invariants is the whole point.

**Don't:** Confuse invariants with mission statements.
**Why:** Mission statements are aspirational. Invariants are descriptive — they're statements about what customers will demand regardless of what the company aspires to. Both matter; they are different. The invariants exist whether you acknowledge them or not.

**Don't:** Assume invariants never evolve.
**Why:** Most don't change. Some do — especially over multi-decade horizons, or in industries where customer expectations genuinely shift (healthcare expectations are different today than 1975; education expectations are shifting fast). Audit every few years. Keep what's true; update what's drifted.
