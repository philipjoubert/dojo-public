# Missionaries vs. Mercenaries

## When to Use
- Hiring for a mission-critical initiative where you need people who will stay through years of uncertainty.
- Designing compensation structures that shape which kind of people the company attracts.
- Evaluating candidates — is this person motivated by the product and the customer, or by the stock price and the brand?
- Building culture at a growing company where the hiring mix determines everything else.

## Fails When
- Used as a purity test. Real people are a mix of missionary and mercenary motivations; demanding 100% missionary produces performance theater, not authenticity.
- Applied to justify underpaying people who should be paid well. Missionaries at Amazon are paid well — just through equity rather than cash bonuses. The structure matters; the compensation level does not suddenly get lower because the culture is mission-oriented.
- Mistaken for "missionaries should suffer." A missionary culture does not mean asceticism. It means the incentive structure rewards long-term thinking and mission alignment.
- Used to dismiss criticism from team members as "mercenary behavior." Criticism is not mercenary; it's often the opposite.

## Core Concept

I'll take a missionary over a mercenary any day. Mercenaries try to flip their stock. Missionaries love their product or their service and love their customers and try to build a great service. By the way, the great paradox here is that it's usually the missionaries who end up making more money. The paradox matters. This isn't a choice between "mission-driven and poor" versus "mercenary and rich." The pattern I've seen over and over is that the people who are genuinely obsessed with the product and the customer end up outperforming the people who are optimizing for the financial outcome — and they end up making more money as a result. The mission-first orientation isn't a cost; it's a leading indicator of long-term performance.

The distinction isn't about compensation level. Missionaries at Amazon are paid competitively. The difference is the structure of the compensation and the type of behavior it encourages. Heavy equity, multi-year vesting, lower cash bonuses. Stock vests 5% after year one, 15% after year two, and 20% every six months for the last two years. That schedule is deliberate. It filters out people who are here to collect a signing bonus and leave. It rewards the people who stay through the uncertain years. The mercenary looks at the compensation package and sees weak upfront cash; the missionary looks at the same package and sees an alignment with where the company is going. Both are reading the same structure correctly. The structure selects.

Missionary doesn't mean soft or idealistic. Amazon missionaries are intensely competitive and results-driven. The difference is what fuels the intensity. A mercenary's fuel runs out when the stock plateaus. A missionary's fuel is customer dissatisfaction — which is infinite, because customers are never done. Customers are always beautifully, wonderfully dissatisfied, even when they report being happy. That's why missionary motivation is sustainable across decades and mercenary motivation runs dry within a cycle. The missionary has an inexhaustible engine; the mercenary has a tank of gas.

Recognize both in yourself and in people you're hiring. The signal of a missionary: they talk about the product and the customer with genuine specificity, not with rehearsed mission statements. They've thought about problems that weren't assigned to them. They stay late because the problem is interesting, not because they're tracking hours. The signal of a mercenary: they talk about the compensation package, the brand on the resume, the title, the trajectory. Both kinds of people exist. Both can contribute. For mission-critical work where you need sustained commitment through uncertainty, hire missionaries.

## How to Apply

1. **In hiring, probe for the specific source of motivation.** Not "why do you want this job?" — that gets rehearsed answers. Ask what specifically they love about the problem space. What have they built or written on this topic when nobody was paying them? What would they do if they won the lottery? The answers separate missionaries from mercenaries.

2. **Structure compensation to attract missionaries.** Heavy equity, multi-year vesting, backloaded. Lower cash bonuses. This doesn't mean underpay — it means shift the compensation into instruments that reward staying and caring about the outcome.

3. **Be honest that both kinds of people exist.** Don't pretend the organization is 100% missionaries. Some roles genuinely benefit from mercenary traits — a deal-maker closing acquisitions, a sales leader hitting a quarterly number. The question is whether the mission-critical roles are filled by missionaries.

4. **Test with the lottery question.** If this person won the lottery tomorrow, would they still want to work here? If yes, probably a missionary. If no, probably optimizing for compensation. Neither answer is wrong; the answer tells you what you're hiring.

5. **Protect the missionaries when the institutional immune system attacks them.** Big companies get comfortable and mavericks cause discomfort. It's easy to drive them out. Don't. Missionaries are often the source of the breakthrough moves; the organization's reflex to eject them is exactly what Day 2 looks like.

6. **Avoid "missionary" as a rhetorical weapon.** "You're being too mercenary about this" can become a way to silence legitimate disagreement. Criticism and skepticism are not mercenary behaviors. Be careful not to use the framework to delegitimize pushback.

7. **Accept that the great paradox is real.** Missionaries tend to outperform mercenaries over long horizons, and they tend to make more money as a result. This isn't coincidence. The structure of the problem rewards the mission-aligned posture.

## Examples

**Situation:** Amazon's compensation structure in the growth years. Low cash bonuses. Heavy equity. Backloaded vesting. Many people looked at the package and walked away.

**Application:** The structure was intentional. It was a filter. The people who stayed were those for whom the mission — building Amazon — was the reason they were there, not the salary. The vesting schedule ensured that anyone here was aligned with where the company was going, not with collecting a bonus and leaving. Mercenaries took offers elsewhere. Missionaries took the package and built the company.

**Result:** The compensation structure is, in retrospect, one of the most important culture-shaping decisions Amazon ever made. The people who built Amazon were the people who accepted that compensation structure. That selection produced the culture. If the compensation had been mercenary-friendly, the culture would have been mercenary, and the long-term bets — AWS, Kindle, Prime — would not have been possible.

**Situation:** Interviewing a candidate. Standard question: why Amazon?

**Application:** The standard answer is rehearsed. So I probe further. What have you built on this topic on your own time? What would you work on if nobody was paying you? What specifically about this problem space keeps you interested? The missionary gives specific, textured answers about the problem. The mercenary gives answers about Amazon's brand, trajectory, or compensation.

**Result:** The probe is a reliable filter, though not a perfect one. Some missionaries are bad at talking about their motivation; some mercenaries have learned how to sound like missionaries. The probe tilts the odds, not the certainty. Combined with behavioral probes in the rest of the loop, the pattern usually becomes clear.

---

**Situation:** A senior leader at Amazon is producing extraordinary results but consistently behaves in ways the leadership principles would call out. They're delivering short-term wins while eroding customer trust and internal culture.

**Application:** This is often a mercenary profile. The performance is real; the motivation is personal advancement or compensation optimization, and the collateral damage to culture and customer is invisible to them because they're not optimizing for those things. The question is whether you keep the performance and accept the erosion, or you protect the culture and lose the performance.

**Result:** Amazon's historical answer is: protect the culture. A mercenary star who damages the mission costs more than they produce, even when the short-term numbers are outstanding. The longer the horizon, the clearer the math gets.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "missionary" as a purity test.
**Why:** Real people are mixes. Demanding 100% mission alignment produces performance theater — people performing the missionary role without actually holding it. The heuristic identifies dominant motivation, not exclusive motivation.

**Don't:** Underpay missionaries because "they're here for the mission."
**Why:** Missionaries at Amazon are compensated well; the structure of the compensation is what differs. Using the framework to justify underpayment produces resentment and turnover. Mission alignment does not mean accept lower pay; it means accept a different structure of pay that aligns with the mission.

**Don't:** Eject missionaries when they cause discomfort.
**Why:** The organization's immune system will attack mavericks. That reflex is Day 2. Missionaries often cause organizational friction because they're pushing against comfortable consensus. Protecting them is part of staying Day 1.

**Don't:** Use "you're being mercenary" as a rhetorical weapon.
**Why:** Criticism and skepticism are not mercenary behaviors. Using the framework to silence dissent is a misuse. If someone disagrees with a direction, engage with the disagreement on its merits; don't dismiss it by labeling the person.

**Don't:** Hire only missionaries.
**Why:** Some roles genuinely benefit from mercenary traits. A dealmaker optimizing a specific transaction, a sales leader closing a quota. The right question is whether the mission-critical roles are filled by missionaries, not whether every role is.

**Don't:** Assume compensation structure alone produces missionaries.
**Why:** The compensation structure is a filter, but it's not the only filter. Hiring, performance management, and culture all contribute. A mercenary can tolerate a missionary compensation structure if every other dimension of the culture supports mercenary behavior. The structure has to match across all dimensions.

**Don't:** Confuse passion with competence.
**Why:** Missionary traits are necessary for certain kinds of work; they are not sufficient. A passionate missionary who cannot execute is a beloved liability. Hire for both missionary motivation and demonstrated performance. Either alone is not enough.
