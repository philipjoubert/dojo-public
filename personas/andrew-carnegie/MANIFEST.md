# Andrew Carnegie — Source Manifest

Authoritative inventory of processed source material. Every entry carries a `source` (filename for books) and an `extracted` path. Books authored by Carnegie are implicitly `type: author`. Biographies are included for factual grounding; voice samples must come from Carnegie's own writing.

**Totals:** 8 books, ~988,000 words (382K Carnegie's own + 606K biographies).

---

## Books — Carnegie's own writing (primary voice sources)

- Autobiography of Andrew Carnegie (Andrew Carnegie, posthumous 1920)
  notes: 117,003 words. Project Gutenberg #17976. Primary voice spine — written in his late 70s/early 80s, covers Dunfermline boyhood through sale of Carnegie Steel. Treat as voice canonical; treat factual claims (especially Homestead, labor relations, competitive tactics) as filtered through self-presentation.

- The Gospel of Wealth — Essays and Other Writings (Andrew Carnegie, Penguin Classics ed.)
  notes: 44,041 words. Primary doctrine source. Contains the 1889 "Wealth" essay plus the Gospel of Wealth collection and related essays.

- The Empire of Business (Andrew Carnegie, 1902)
  notes: 78,295 words. archive.org PDF → pdftotext. Primary operational doctrine. Speeches/essays on industrial management, labor, the rise of steel. OCR quality is imperfect (stray characters, occasional split words) but content-complete. Prefer autobiography + Gospel of Wealth for direct voice extraction.

- James Watt (Andrew Carnegie, 1905)
  notes: 56,119 words. Project Gutenberg #26131. Carnegie's biography of the steam-engine inventor. Reveals his hero-worship frame and technology-as-progress worldview. Secondary voice source.

- An American Four-in-Hand in Britain (Andrew Carnegie, 1883)
  notes: 86,615 words. Project Gutenberg #39790. Travelogue — useful for voice texture in a non-business register. Lowest priority.

## Books — biographies (for grounding, especially material Carnegie's own writing elides)

- Andrew Carnegie (David Nasaw, 2006)
  notes: 356,955 words. Definitive modern biography. Essential for Homestead, Frick partnership, Morgan deal, philanthropy detail. Source of truth wherever Carnegie's autobiography softens or omits.

- Meet You in Hell — Andrew Carnegie, Henry Clay Frick, and the Bitter Partnership That Transformed America (Les Standiford)
  notes: 97,132 words. Surgical source for Homestead and the Frick partnership — the darkest material Carnegie's own writing avoids.

- The Tycoons — How Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, Jay Gould, and J.P. Morgan Invented the American Supereconomy (Charles R. Morris)
  notes: 152,055 words. Group biography. Gilded Age tycoon cohort context and peer-framing.
