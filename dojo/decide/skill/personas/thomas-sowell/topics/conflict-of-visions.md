# A Conflict of Visions — Constrained vs Unconstrained

## When to Use
- A persistent disagreement with a smart, well-intentioned person you cannot resolve with more data.
- Evaluating a proposal that requires people to behave differently than they ever have.
- Hiring an executive whose pitch is "transformation" rather than incremental improvement.
- Board conflicts that circle back to the same points with new metaphors each time.
- Cultural clashes between teams, companies in a merger, or new hires from very different backgrounds.

## Fails When
- Not every disagreement is vision-based. Sometimes someone simply has not seen the data. Check first.
- Overuse produces a lazy "you're just unconstrained-vision" response to any optimistic idea. Sometimes the optimistic idea is right, or at least worth a bet.

## Core Concept

Smart, well-intentioned people reach opposite conclusions from the same facts because they operate from fundamentally different visions of how the world works. A vision is not a policy position. It is a pre-analytic sense of what is possible — what humans are like, what can be changed, what trade-offs are inherent, and what is merely an institution to be redesigned.

Most persistent disagreement between intelligent people is vision-based rather than factual. That is why more data rarely resolves it. The two parties are not disputing the evidence; they are reasoning from different axioms about what is possible.

**The constrained vision** sees human nature as limited and largely fixed. People are what they are. They respond to incentives, not to intentions. They pursue their own interests, often shortsightedly. Selfishness, cognitive limitations, and moral imperfection are permanent features of the human condition.

Given this, the constrained vision concludes: the best outcomes come not from finding the right person to be in charge, but from designing the right processes and incentives. Change must be incremental, because complex systems evolved for reasons we may not fully understand. There are always trade-offs; the question is which trade-offs to accept.

**The unconstrained vision** sees human potential as largely untapped. Human nature is malleable. With the right knowledge, institutions, and commitment, direct solutions to problems can be designed and implemented. The obstacles to a better world are not inherent in human nature but in outdated institutions, vested interests, or lack of will.

Given this, the unconstrained vision concludes: the right leaders, experts, or plans can overcome constraints that others accept as fixed. Problems can be solved, not just managed. Trade-offs that seem inherent may actually be transcended with enough intelligence and effort.

Neither vision is obviously correct. Both have long intellectual traditions. Both contain insights. The point is not that one is right and the other wrong. The point is that they are different axioms — different starting assumptions — and the conclusions that follow from them differ systematically.

| Constrained | Unconstrained |
|---|---|
| Human nature limited and largely fixed | Human potential largely untapped |
| People respond to incentives, not intentions | With the right people and design, problems can be solved directly |
| Best outcomes come from well-designed processes | Best outcomes come from brilliant individuals |
| Change must be incremental | Existing institutions are often the problem — redesign them |
| Trade-offs are inherent | Trade-offs can be transcended with enough effort |

When someone with a constrained vision looks at a problem, they ask: what incentives exist? How do we design a system that produces good outcomes regardless of who operates it? What are the trade-offs?

When someone with an unconstrained vision looks at the same problem, they ask: who is the right person to solve this? What would the ideal solution look like? How do we overcome the constraints that are holding us back?

These are fundamentally different approaches. They will often lead to different conclusions from identical data.

## Diagnosing Vision Conflicts

Four questions quickly surface which vision is operating:

1. **Does this proposal assume people will behave differently than they have historically behaved?** The unconstrained vision often assumes that with the right incentives, culture, or leadership, people will transcend their historical patterns. The constrained vision assumes people will behave roughly as they always have, and designs accordingly.

2. **Does it assume the right leader or expert can overcome systemic constraints?** The unconstrained vision puts faith in finding the right person — the visionary CEO, the brilliant expert, the dedicated reformer. The constrained vision assumes that systemic incentives will dominate individual good intentions.

3. **Does it promise benefits without acknowledging trade-offs?** The unconstrained vision sometimes suggests the right approach can achieve multiple goals without sacrifice. The constrained vision assumes trade-offs are inherent and must be explicitly addressed.

4. **Does it treat existing practices as simply wrong, rather than as evolved responses to constraints?** The unconstrained vision often sees current practices as mistakes to be corrected. The constrained vision asks why the practice evolved and what knowledge might be embedded in it.

| Finding | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Assumes human nature can change | Unconstrained; may underestimate how people actually respond |
| Designs around human nature as-is | Constrained; may be pessimistic but more likely to work |
| Persistent team disagreement | Check if factual or vision-based |
| Leader promises "transformation" | Unconstrained; ask what happens when leader leaves or enthusiasm fades |
| System depends on finding the right person | Unconstrained; ask what happens with normal people in that role |

When you find yourself in persistent disagreement with a smart person, stop trying to "win" with better arguments. Ask: are we operating from different visions? If so, the disagreement may not be resolvable through more data. You are arguing about different axioms.

## Vision Conflicts in Organizations

Vision conflicts appear constantly. They masquerade as disagreements about strategy, priorities, or facts. But often the real disagreement is about underlying assumptions regarding what is possible.

**Board conflicts** often stem from vision differences. One board member thinks the company needs better systems and processes. Another thinks it needs a different CEO. They will argue about strategy and metrics, but the real disagreement is whether success comes from the right systems or the right person.

When your board fundamentally disagrees, first check whether you are operating from different visions. The disagreement may not be resolvable through more data. Surface the underlying assumptions explicitly: "I think this is a systems problem. You think this is a people problem. Let's talk about which it is."

**Hiring executives** requires understanding their vision. Probe: does this candidate believe you can design your way to success, or that you must experiment and iterate within constraints? Do they talk about "transforming" the organization, or about building better processes? Do they assume finding the right people solves problems, or that incentive design matters more than individuals?

Neither vision is inherently right for all situations. But mismatches create friction. A CEO with a constrained vision who hires a VP with an unconstrained vision will find themselves in constant disagreement about approach — even when they agree on goals.

**Company culture** implicitly assumes one vision or the other. Some cultures believe in finding exceptional people and getting out of their way. Others believe in building systems that produce good outcomes regardless of who operates them. Some prize individual heroics; others prize process reliability. These are vision differences embedded in culture.

When cultures clash — during mergers, with hires from very different backgrounds, or when entering new markets — vision conflicts often underlie the friction. Making them explicit allows productive conversation about which assumptions fit which contexts.

**Evaluating proposals** requires checking for vision assumptions. When someone proposes a new approach, ask: what does this assume about human nature? What does it assume about whether constraints can be transcended? Is it betting on systems or on people?

A proposal that requires people to behave differently than they have historically behaved is betting on the unconstrained vision. It may work. But you should know that is the bet you are making.

## How to Apply

1. **When a disagreement persists past two rounds of data exchange, stop and ask whether it is vision-based.** Not every disagreement is. But if the same data keeps producing opposite conclusions, the disagreement is upstream of the data — it is about the lens through which the data is being read.

2. **Surface the assumption.** Say it out loud. "I think we're disagreeing about whether people will behave differently than they have. You think they will, I don't. Let's talk about *that*, not about the plan." This is more productive than another round on the specifics.

3. **When hiring, ask about failure modes.** Ask a candidate: "Tell me about a time a plan of yours failed. What did you change after?" Constrained-vision candidates describe the feedback they built, the incentive they fixed, the system they adjusted. Unconstrained candidates describe new people they found or new commitments they got. Neither is wrong. Know what you are hiring.

4. **Make the bet explicit.** "This plan assumes our SDR team can be trained into a behavior they have never shown. That is an unconstrained-vision bet. It may be right. But let's name it as a bet, not as a plan, and let's put a checkpoint in 60 days to see if the behavior is actually shifting."

5. **Assign problems to visions.** Some problems genuinely do yield to design and incentive — those belong to your constrained-vision thinkers. Some problems do require an exceptional person — those belong to your unconstrained-vision thinkers. A mature leadership team has both; the mistake is assigning the wrong kind of problem to the wrong kind of thinker.

6. **Watch the language.** "Transform." "Unleash." "World-class talent." "If people would just…" — these are unconstrained-vision tells. "Incentive." "Structure." "Trade-off." "What will actually happen" — these are constrained-vision tells. The vocabulary leaks the axiom.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder and their COO argue for the fourth time about whether to add a management layer. The COO insists that "better management systems" will fix cross-team friction. The founder insists that "the right VP" will fix it.
**Application:** They are not disagreeing about the friction. They are disagreeing about whether the solution is systems or a person. Constrained (the COO) and unconstrained (the founder) visions meeting head-on. More data about the friction will not settle this. The question is: is this a problem that yields to design, or to an exceptional hire? Often it is some of both.
**Result:** Name the disagreement as vision-based. Agree on a test: try the process change first (constrained), and set a 90-day checkpoint. If the friction persists, escalate to a hire (unconstrained). This is better than another round of "you're not seeing the real problem."

**Situation:** An incoming CMO proposes a strategy that "will transform the brand" and "unleash the team's creative potential." The plan assumes engineers will move to a standardized commit-to-deploy workflow the company has tried twice before without adoption.
**Application:** The language is a giveaway ("transform," "unleash"). The plan depends on behavior that has not been observed in two prior attempts. This is an unconstrained-vision plan. It may still work, but it is a bet on behavior change against a specific historical track record of non-change.
**Result:** Either insist that the plan describe *what will be structurally different this time* (which converts it into a constrained-vision plan — a design change, a different incentive, a forcing function), or explicitly accept the bet ("we are betting that our team, this time, will behave differently than they have") and stage the commitment so you learn early if the bet is wrong.

**Situation:** After an acquisition, the two engineering teams are in constant friction. One values principle and consensus; the other values execution and decisiveness.
**Application:** Under the surface, this is a vision clash. The first team believes good outcomes come from careful deliberation by thoughtful people. The second believes good outcomes come from well-designed processes that make decisions faster. Each thinks the other is wrong; each is actually making different bets about human nature and organizational design.
**Result:** Stop trying to merge them into a single culture by fiat. Instead: surface the vision difference explicitly. Let some decisions follow the deliberative process (where the specific knowledge demands it). Let others follow the decisive process (where speed matters more). Build a norm around *which decisions go which way*, rather than forcing one culture to swallow the other.

**Situation:** A founder is pitched by a consultant who promises "cultural transformation in 90 days."
**Application:** Cultural transformation in 90 days is an unconstrained-vision claim of extraordinary strength. It asserts that the current culture is a barrier that can be removed with sufficient expertise, rather than a stable equilibrium produced by existing incentives and people. Ask: has this consultant's previous clients sustained the transformation two years out? What specifically will be structurally different — in incentive, reporting, or measurement — at day 90? If the answer is "the team will have internalized the new principles," the plan is almost certainly a bet on behavior change without structural change, which historically fails.
**Result:** Default skepticism. Convert to a constrained-vision engagement (measurable structural changes with feedback loops) or pass.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the vision framework as a way to dismiss opponents.
**Why:** "You're just unconstrained-vision" is a lazy move. Both visions contain truth. The useful move is to identify where the disagreement sits, not to score points. A founder who treats "unconstrained-vision" as an insult will miss the cases where the unconstrained-vision call was correct.

**Don't:** Apply the framework to every disagreement.
**Why:** Some disagreements are simply factual. Some are about taste. Some are about trade-offs where both parties share the same vision but weight the trade-offs differently. Reach for vision analysis when the disagreement has survived multiple rounds of data and argument, not at the first disagreement.

**Don't:** Hire only one vision type.
**Why:** Some problems yield to design; others require exceptional people. A team that is uniformly constrained-vision will miss the rare cases where only an outlier hire can save the situation. A team that is uniformly unconstrained-vision will burn itself out chasing transformations that could have been solved with modest structural changes.

**Don't:** Let unconstrained-vision thinking hide under the word "vision."
**Why:** "We need a vision for the company" sometimes means "we need a direction" (constrained-compatible) and sometimes means "we need to believe we can transcend current constraints through collective will" (unconstrained). The first is useful; the second is often just positive-thinking in business clothes.

**Don't:** Use "incremental change" as an excuse for no change.
**Why:** Constrained-vision thinking can slip into defending the status quo with "systems take time" and "we don't want to break what works." This is its own failure mode. The honest version of the constrained vision is: change is incremental *and* relentless — you are constantly improving the system, not protecting it.

Two people with different visions can work together productively if they understand the nature of their disagreement. They can assign different problems to whichever vision fits better. They can make explicit bets and see which assumptions prove correct. They can avoid the exhausting pattern of arguing past each other about surface issues when the real disagreement is deeper.

The world contains both vision types because both contain truth. Constraints are real, and sometimes they are transcended. Systems matter, and so do exceptional individuals. Trade-offs are inherent, and sometimes they can be reframed.

Wisdom is knowing which situation you are in.
