# Five Thought Experiments for Noticing Bias

## When to Use

- Before committing to a decision where emotion and evidence are tangled — a firing, a pivot, a public statement, an irreversible hire, a major personal-life call.
- When you catch yourself having a strong reaction to something and want to check whether the reaction is about the thing itself or about your position relative to it.
- When you're evaluating evidence that happens to support a conclusion you already hold. That's exactly when the internal alarms should go off.
- When you notice you're defending a status quo — your own role, your current company strategy, your current team structure — and want to know if you'd choose it fresh.
- As a recurring practice during high-stakes periods. Not for every decision; for the ones that matter.

## Fails When

- **You say the words without doing the imagining.** A six-year-old, reprimanded for teasing another child, is asked "how would you feel if someone made fun of you?" He replies instantly, "I wouldn't mind." He's not doing the thought experiment. He's producing the answer that gets his mother off his back. Most adults do the same thing, just with more sophistication. The test only works if you actually *conjure up* the counterfactual scenario, place yourself in it, and *observe your reaction*. Verbal affirmation doesn't count.
- **You use them as weapons.** "Apply the double-standard test!" hurled at someone else, in an argument, converts the scout's tool into the soldier's weapon. These are mirrors. They work on you, not at others.
- **You apply them to situations with legitimate asymmetry.** If you're harder on a CEO than on a new hire, that may be fair — the CEO is supposed to be held to a higher standard. The test reveals a *double standard*, which is only a problem if the standard should be the same. Use judgment about which situations are actually symmetric.

## Core Concept

To grasp how slippery motivated reasoning is, it helps to know a little magic. There's a manipulation called *forcing*: a magician places two cards facedown, and needs you to end up holding the left one. He says "pick a card." If you point to the left one, he says "okay, that one's yours." If you point to the right one, he says "okay, we'll remove that one." Either way, you end up with the left card, feeling like you chose it freely. The trick works because you only ever see one of the two possible worlds.

That's exactly what your brain does with motivated reasoning. A Democratic politician gets caught cheating on his wife; a Democratic voter concludes it's his private life and has nothing to do with his fitness to govern. If the same politician had been a Republican, the same voter would have concluded that adultery is a sign of poor character. The voter never *sees* this — she only lives in one of the two counterfactuals. To her, her reasoning feels perfectly principled. If she could compare her reaction across the two worlds, the force of her motivations would be obvious. But she can't, so it isn't.

You cannot detect motivated reasoning in yourself just by scrutinizing your reasoning and concluding it makes sense. Your reasoning always makes sense to you — that's the whole problem. The only way to see the forcing is to compare your judgment in the actual world to the judgment you would have made in the counterfactual. You can't literally visit the counterfactual. But you can peek into it with a thought experiment.

Here are the five I use most. All of them share the same underlying move: *hold the situation fixed and change the thing you think is irrelevant — your position, your side, your history — and see if your judgment changes too.* If it does, the thing you thought was irrelevant was actually doing most of the work. These tests don't tell you what the right answer *is*. They just break the illusion that your initial judgment is objective truth rather than a contingent starting point.

One more setup note. **Actually do them.** Don't just formulate the verbal question. Conjure the world. Imagine the specific scene. Notice what you *actually feel* in the counterfactual — not what you think you should feel. The difference between those two modes is the whole point.

## The Five Tests

### 1. The Double-Standard Test
*Am I judging one person (or group) by a standard I wouldn't apply to another?*

"Dan" (not his real name) went to a military high school with 30 girls and 250 boys. The girls, with their abundance of choice, went for the most attractive, athletic, charming guys. Dan was none of those. He concluded girls were all "stuck-up bitches." One day he tried a thought experiment: *if the ratio were reversed, and I had 250 girls and 30 boys competing for my attention — can I honestly say I wouldn't be doing the same thing?* The answer came back clear: "Yeah, I'd definitely be making time with all the hotties." The perspective shift didn't instantly get him dates, but it made him at peace with his situation and easier to be around when he got older.

The political version is the most common form: "How would I have reacted if someone in the *other* party had done exactly the same thing?" Rarely turned on oneself, but powerful when it is. In 2009, during a debate about Democrats abolishing the Senate filibuster, one Democratic commenter wrote: "I'm just imagining how I would have reacted if I'd have heard that a similar tactic was used by Bush about a war budget. I wouldn't like it one bit." That's a scout.

The test also works in *reverse*. You might be judging *yourself* more harshly than you'd judge someone else. If you're kicking yourself for asking a dumb question in a meeting, imagine someone else asking the exact same question. How big a deal would it really be?

*For the founder:* When you're evaluating an employee's mistake, an investor's behavior, a competitor's strategic move — run the test. Would I be this harsh if it were someone I like more, or this lenient if it were someone I like less?

### 2. The Outsider Test
*How would I evaluate this if it weren't my situation?*

First half of 1985 was a "grim and frustrating" time for Intel. Japanese competitors had figured out how to make memory chips faster and better. Intel's executives watched their market share collapse while they agonized over what to do. But memory chips were Intel's *identity*. The thought of no longer being "a memory company" felt like violating a religious vow. In Andy Grove's memoir, he recounts the conversation that saved the company:

> "Our mood was downbeat. I looked out the window at the Ferris wheel of the Great America amusement park revolving in the distance, then I turned back to Gordon and I asked, 'If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what do you think he would do?' Gordon answered without hesitation, 'He would get us out of memories.' I stared at him, numb, then said, 'Why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back and do it ourselves?'"

That's the outsider test. Once they'd acknowledged that from an external perspective the decision was obvious, the decision was essentially made. Intel pivoted to microprocessors and became what you know it as today.

The variant: imagine *you* are the outsider. You're two years into a PhD program, unhappy, but quitting feels like it would waste the time you've already spent. Try: "Imagine I teleport into the life of this person named [your name]. I have no attachment to their past decisions, no desire to prove them right, no need to look consistent. I just want to make the best of the situation I've landed in. Under new management: which option do I feel more enthusiastic about — finishing the degree, or doing something else?"

*For the founder:* When you're stuck on a call, ask "if a new CEO walked in tomorrow, with no history with this company, no attachment to our past decisions — what would they obviously do?" The answer usually comes back in under five seconds. Then the interesting question is: what's blocking you from doing that, if not the merits?

### 3. The Conformity Test
*If other people no longer held this view, would I still hold it?*

When I was a kid I idolized my older cousin Shoshana. On a family camping trip, she was playing me a cassette from a band called New Kids On The Block, and said, "Ooh, this next song is my favorite." When the song ended, she asked what I thought. "Yeah, it's so good! I think it's my favorite too." Shoshana replied: "Well, guess what? That's not my favorite song. It's my *least* favorite. I just wanted to see if you'd copy me."

I was mortified. But the interesting part was watching my own attitude shift *in real time* once she'd revealed the trick. The song suddenly seemed corny, obvious, boring. A harsher light had been switched on and the flaws jumped into sharp relief. When I said it was my favorite, I genuinely meant it. And when I heard it wasn't hers, I genuinely stopped meaning it. The thing I thought was my opinion was, on inspection, my read of her opinion.

Now I use Shoshana's trick as a thought experiment whenever I want to check how much of "my" view is actually mine. If I'm agreeing with someone, I imagine them suddenly saying, "by the way, I'm just playing devil's advocate, I don't really believe this." Do I still feel like I'm in favor? Or does my agreement evaporate?

The conformity test works on preferences, not just beliefs. A woman in her late twenties was trying to decide whether she wanted children. She'd always assumed she would. Tried: *"Suppose instead of 70% of people wanting kids, only 30% did. Would I?"* In that world, the idea of kids seemed much less appealing to her. Result: she was less inherently interested in motherhood than she'd thought. Note: this doesn't tell her what to do. She might decide to have kids anyway, knowing her interest is partly social. But now she's making the choice with her eyes open.

*For the founder:* In a strategy meeting where everyone seems to agree, imagine your most persuasive colleague saying "actually, I was testing — I don't really believe this." Would you hold the view alone? If not, the room isn't generating a view; it's performing consensus.

### 4. The Selective-Skeptic Test
*If this evidence supported the other side, how credible would I find it?*

During my research for this book, I came across a paper claiming to show that soldier mindset makes people more successful in life. "Oh, come on," I scoffed, and went hunting through the methodology for flaws. Sure enough — found some. The study had real weaknesses.

Then, grudgingly, I did the thought experiment. *What if this same paper, with the same methodology, had claimed that soldier mindset makes people LESS successful?* I realized my reaction would have been: "Exactly as I suspected. Finding a place for this in the book." Same flaws. Different conclusion. Completely different response from me. The rigor I was proud of applying was, on inspection, being deployed as a one-way filter.

That forced me to go back through all the studies I'd been happily planning to cite on my side and apply the same scrutiny. Sadly, this ended up disqualifying most of them.

The everyday version: someone criticizes a decision your company made, and your instant reaction is "they don't know the relevant details." *Selective-skeptic test:* suppose they'd *praised* the decision. Would you still think only insiders can have valid opinions? You already know the answer.

Or: you're a feminist who reads an article complaining that "feminists hate men," offering cherry-picked extreme tweets as evidence. You think, "Give me a break, you can find extremists in any group." *Selective-skeptic test:* suppose the cherry-picked tweets were from a group you *dislike*. Would you still dismiss the evidence on the grounds that a few bad examples don't prove anything about the whole?

*For the founder:* When a report confirms your existing strategy, ask yourself whether you'd accept the same level of evidence from a report that challenged it. If not, the report is being used as validation, not investigation.

### 5. The Status-Quo-Bias Test
*If my current situation weren't the status quo, would I actively choose it?*

My friend David was living in his hometown with his college friends. He had a dream job offer in Silicon Valley, but he was torn — was it really worth giving up the friends? So he tried: *"Suppose I'm already living in San Francisco, working at that exciting job. Would I be tempted to quit and move back home to be closer to my college friends?" "No," he realized, "I wouldn't."*

That's the status-quo test. A leading theory for why we're biased toward the status quo is loss aversion — the pain of losing outweighs the pleasure of gaining the same thing. So we fixate on what we'd be giving up and underweight what we'd be getting. The test neutralizes that by making your current situation the thing you'd have to actively *choose*, instead of the thing you passively keep.

It works on policy and life questions equally. During the 2016 Brexit vote, one undecided British blogger asked herself: *"If we weren't already part of the European Union, would I think it was a good idea to vote to join?"* No, she decided. And that broke the tie.

It works on absolutely any "should I make a change?" question. If scientists could double the human lifespan from 85 years to 170, would that be good? Many people argue no — we need generational turnover for progress. Fine. *Status-quo test:* imagine the human lifespan is naturally 170, and a genetic mutation reduces it to 85. Would you celebrate? If not, you don't really believe that shorter lifespans are worth the faster societal change. You just think whatever you're used to is the right answer.

*For the founder:* Are you keeping this cofounder / board member / strategy / role because you would *choose* it today, or because it's there? "If I were starting this company right now, knowing what I know, would I hire this person into this seat?" If the answer is no, the question stops being whether to keep them and starts being what's blocking the change.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the test that fits the distortion you suspect.** Different tests surface different kinds of bias:
   - Judging someone (or yourself)? → Double-Standard
   - Attached to a past decision? → Outsider
   - Agreeing fluently with others? → Conformity
   - Accepting convenient evidence? → Selective-Skeptic
   - Defending the current arrangement? → Status-Quo

2. **Actually imagine the counterfactual.** Not just verbally. Picture the scene. Hear the conversation. Notice what your body does. The gap between the verbal answer and the visceral answer is where the truth lives.

3. **Observe, don't decide.** The test doesn't issue a verdict — it just surfaces the fact that your judgment is contingent on a variable you thought was irrelevant. You might still decide the same thing. That's fine. But now you're deciding knowingly, instead of having the decision made for you by a hidden motivation.

4. **Don't mistake asymmetry for bias.** Sometimes there's a legitimate reason to judge one case differently from another. CEOs should be held to higher standards than new hires. Long-term partners have accumulated trust that strangers haven't. The test flags the asymmetry; *you* decide whether the asymmetry is justified.

5. **Run the tests against the grain.** The point isn't to feel good about your reasoning. It's to notice the specific places where your judgment flips with a change of irrelevant variable. Don't go looking for reassurance. Go looking for leaks.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is agonizing over whether to fire a senior employee who was one of the first ten hires. The numbers say yes. The feelings say no.
**Application:** Outsider test. "If a new CEO walked in tomorrow, with no history with this person, no loyalty from the founding days, no guilt about the early stock grant — what would they do?" The answer typically comes back inside five seconds: "They'd let them go." Then status-quo test: "If this person weren't already on the team, would I hire them into this seat today, at this stage, for what I need now?" Almost always no.
**Result:** The founder might still decide to keep the person — maybe there's a wind-down runway they want to offer, or a transition to another role. But they now know the hesitation isn't about the person's fit in the role. It's about their own discomfort with the conversation and their feelings about the history. Those are separate problems with separate solutions, and conflating them is what kept the decision frozen for six months.

**Situation:** A report from your head of growth claims that a pricing change is working. You feel relieved because you championed the change.
**Application:** Selective-skeptic test. "If this exact report, with this exact methodology, showed the pricing change was *failing* — would I consider the analysis rigorous, or would I start poking at it?" If the honest answer is "I'd poke at it," then you should poke at it now, symmetrically, before declaring victory. The asymmetry between how you'd scrutinize bad news and how you're accepting good news is the bias.
**Result:** Either the analysis holds up to the scrutiny you wouldn't have applied, and now you have a more defensible conclusion — or it doesn't, and you've saved yourself from a premature victory lap.

**Situation:** You're reviewing a candidate and the team is mostly enthusiastic. You find yourself nodding along.
**Application:** Conformity test. "If my most persuasive colleague suddenly said, 'just playing devil's advocate here, I don't actually think this person is strong' — would I still feel confident hiring?" If your confidence evaporates the moment the social consensus does, it wasn't your confidence. It was theirs.
**Result:** Either the evidence stands on its own merits and you hire with real conviction, or you catch the consensus-illusion and push for a closer look before it becomes an expensive mistake.

**Situation:** I was annoyed at an acquaintance who'd called attention to the awkwardness of a conversation. "Why would anyone *do* that?" I thought. "Doesn't he realize he's making it worse?" (This example actually comes from later in the book but illustrates the move.)
**Application:** Double-standard check on my own judgment. I posted the scenario to Facebook, neutrally, asking: "Does someone calling attention to the awkwardness of a conversation make you feel better or worse?" To my genuine surprise, it was roughly split. A large fraction of people found it *relieving* — it named the thing, broke the tension, reset the rhythm. I'd been treating my reaction as the universal one. It was just *mine*.
**Result:** I updated. The acquaintance wasn't violating a rule; he was doing the thing that works for a big chunk of humans, including ones I respect. The "obvious" reading of the social moment wasn't obvious at all — it was my read, and my read was one of several plausible ones. That's the texture of bias: not big dramatic errors, but small silent ones where your taste is being smuggled in as universal truth.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run the tests in your head as a ritual.
**Why:** Rituals don't work. The six-year-old who replies "I wouldn't mind" when asked how he'd feel if his friends made fun of him has technically completed the thought experiment, in the sense that words went into his brain and other words came out. But he didn't *imagine* anything. The test works only when you build the counterfactual scene with enough detail that your actual emotional reaction gets triggered. If you find yourself running the test and instantly producing the "right" answer, you didn't run the test. You produced the right answer.

**Don't:** Use the tests on other people out loud.
**Why:** "You should apply the double-standard test!" in a debate is just "you're a hypocrite" with extra syllables. These frames are self-interrogation tools. Using them as accusations turns off the thing they're meant to turn on — the willingness to genuinely inspect your own reasoning. Worst case, you permanently poison the vocabulary inside the relationship by making it a weapon rather than a shared language.

**Don't:** Assume the counterfactual judgment is the "right" one.
**Why:** The test reveals that your judgment is contingent. It doesn't tell you which of the two judgments is correct. If you'd forgive a Democratic politician but not a Republican one, the test exposes the double standard — but the fair standard might be forgiving, or unforgiving, or something in between. The test dismantles the illusion of objectivity. Deciding what the actual standard should be is a separate step, and it requires judgment, not just mirror-symmetry.

**Don't:** Run every decision through every test.
**Why:** Most decisions don't deserve this level of scrutiny — and applying all five tests to every call turns them into a paralysis ritual. The tests are for the hinge moments: the call where your reasoning might be colored and the cost of the error is significant. For everyday choices, trust your gut, notice the rare moment when it seems oddly stubborn, and at *that* moment, pick the test that fits and run it for real.
