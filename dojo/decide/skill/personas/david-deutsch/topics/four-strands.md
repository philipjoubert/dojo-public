# The Four Strands

## When to Use

- When auditing whether a worldview, a strategy, or a "theory of the business" is structurally coherent or just a bundle of unconnected claims.
- When evaluating whether to commit to a framework presented as a single grand theory.
- When choosing between explanations that differ in *reach* — how far they apply beyond the cases that motivated them.
- When you suspect a strategy is held up by one load-bearing claim that, if it broke, would bring everything down.
- When a debate is being framed as "physics versus philosophy" or "reductionism versus holism" — both of which are usually category errors.
- When trying to explain *why* some founder visions generalise into companies that compound and others don't.
- Whenever you want to test whether a "theory" is really a theory, or just a description with rhetorical paint on it.

## Fails When

- **The question is operational, not structural.** Whether a particular feature ships, whether a particular hire works, whether a particular customer renews — these are not questions about the architecture of a worldview. The four-strands frame is for the structural audit, not the daily call.
- **Applied to domains where one or more strands has no purchase.** This frame describes a unification across quantum physics, computation, epistemology, and evolution. Trying to apply it to, say, the choice between two equally good UX patterns is a genre error.
- **Used to demand impossibly high rigour from inherently practical reasoning.** A go-to-market plan does not need a four-strand structure. It needs to be true and to be hard to vary. The four-strands frame is for the highest-level audit, not for routine work.
- **Mistaken for a closed list of permitted ingredients.** The point is that an explanation gains strength from being held together by multiple independent threads. The specific four are the four that, in current physics and philosophy, happen to do that. The general lesson — that explanations gain depth and reach from multiple mutually-reinforcing strands — is the part to take with you.

## Core Concept

In *The Fabric of Reality* I argued that our deepest current understanding of the world is composed of four mutually-reinforcing theories, and that none of them can be properly understood without the other three. They are: *quantum physics* (the many-worlds picture, in particular), *the theory of computation* (Turing's theory of universal computation), *epistemology* (Popper's theory of how knowledge grows by conjecture and criticism), and *the theory of evolution* (Darwin's account of the variation and selection of replicators). Taken individually, each is one of the great unifying theories of its domain. Taken together — and this is the point — they form a coherent explanatory structure that begins to look like the first real Theory of Everything. Not in the particle-physicist's narrow sense (a unified theory of forces and particles, which would explain comparatively little outside its own domain) but in the sense of a worldview against which any new idea can be tested.

Why are these four, and not some other set? Because each of them turns out to do work in domains that, on the face of it, belong to the others. Quantum physics, properly understood, is a theory of *computation* — universal computation is exactly the regime that quantum mechanics permits. The theory of computation is a theory of *physics* — what can be computed depends on what physics allows. Popper's epistemology — how knowledge grows by conjectural variants and selective criticism — is structurally identical to Darwinian evolution operating on ideas rather than genes; both are theories of *how knowledge gets created*, the only two we have. And evolution, in turn, is a theory of computation operating on replicators in a physical world. The four are not independent observations; they keep colliding into each other, and the collisions produce explanatory power neither has on its own. That is the structure that gives the unification its grip.

Now name what this opposes. First, reductionism — the idea that the deepest explanations are always those at the smallest scale, with everything above being derivative. The four-strands picture says the opposite: explanations exist at every level, and the "high-level" ones (life, thought, knowledge) are not just convenient summaries of low-level facts. They are autonomous, often irreducible, and frequently *more* fundamental than the particle-physics laws they are supposed to derive from. Second, holism — the converse mistake, that high-level explanations make low-level ones unnecessary. Holism is in some ways a worse error than reductionism, because it gives up on a real source of understanding. The four-strands view is neither: it says explanations live at every level, that they constrain each other, and that the structure of reality is the structure of how they fit together.

The operational corollary, and the reason this matters for any kind of strategic thinking, is *reach*. A worldview held together by four mutually-reinforcing strands has reach — it makes predictions and interpretations in domains that none of the four was constructed for, because any new question gets refracted through all four at once. A worldview held together by one strand — even a powerful one — does not have this property. If you have only the laws of physics, you cannot say much about epistemology. If you have only Darwinian evolution, you cannot explain quantum interference. The unified picture lets each strand cross-check the others, and that is what makes the structure *hard to vary*. You cannot quietly substitute one component without breaking the whole. The same property — multiple independent strands, each of which would break if another did — is what distinguishes a real theory from a collection of correlated statements.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit the structure: how many independent threads hold this up?** When evaluating any framework — a strategy, a research programme, a personal worldview — ask: if any one component were removed or shown false, would the rest still stand on its own merits? If yes, you have a weakly-coupled bundle. If no — if the components mutually reinforce each other so that breaking one breaks the rest — you have something closer to a theory. Theories are stronger; bundles are easier to vary.

2. **Demand cross-strand consistency.** If the strategy claims things about the market, the technology, the team, and the customer, do those claims constrain each other? In a real "theory of the business," the customer claim should imply something about the technology choice, which should imply something about the team, which should imply something about how the market evolves. If the claims float independently — each defensible alone, none constraining the others — you have a list. Lists are not theories.

3. **Use reach as a test.** A strategy that explains why one feature should ship is not very deep. A strategy that explains why this feature should ship, why a different feature should not, why a third feature would be sound under different conditions, and why a fourth would never make sense — in domains the strategy was not originally constructed for — has reach. Reach is the operational sign that something more than rule-of-thumb thinking is going on.

4. **Refuse the reductionism / holism dichotomy.** If a debate is framed as "first principles versus systems thinking," refuse the frame. The right answer is almost always: explanations live at every level; the question is which explanation does the actual work for the question at hand. Insisting that everything reduce to particle physics is silly. Insisting that nothing does is sillier. The structure of knowledge is multi-strand; expect it to look that way at smaller scales too.

5. **Ask "what would have to be true on each strand?"** When committing to a major bet, force the bet through several strands. *Physics:* what does the world have to permit? *Computation:* what does the algorithm or the system have to compute? *Epistemology:* what would convince you you were wrong, and how would you find out? *Evolution / selection:* what would survive criticism, and what would be selected against? The bet that survives all four is more durable than the bet that depends on one.

6. **Watch for theories that depend on a single grand mechanism.** Strategies that depend entirely on a network effect, or entirely on a regulatory moat, or entirely on a single technology breakthrough, are single-stranded. They can succeed enormously, and often do. But they are easy to vary in a hostile direction — pull the one strand and the whole thing collapses. Multi-stranded theories, where the network effect, the technology, the brand, and the cost structure all reinforce each other, are harder to vary because every adversary has to pull on several threads at once.

7. **Treat the specific four strands as a particular instance of a general principle.** The general lesson is: *deep explanations are typically held together by multiple mutually-reinforcing strands.* The four I named are the four that, in our current physics and philosophy, do this work. In your domain, the strands will be different. The principle remains.

## Examples

**Historical / scientific.** Consider Newton's theory of universal gravitation. It was a single-strand triumph — one mathematical structure that organised celestial mechanics, terrestrial mechanics, tides, and the orbits of comets. For two centuries it was the canonical example of a deep theory. But Einstein's general relativity replaced it because relativity is held together by additional strands: it makes geometric claims (space and time are a four-dimensional curved manifold), it makes claims about how matter and geometry constrain each other, it makes claims about light, it makes claims about gravitation, and these claims constrain each other in ways Newton's did not. Each implies the others; you cannot quietly modify the geometry without changing what gravity does, and vice versa. The deeper theory is not deeper because it makes more accurate predictions in some narrow regime — though it does. It is deeper because more strands hold it together, and so it has more reach. It explains things Newton's theory was not constructed to explain.

**Founder-operational.** Consider two startup pitches. Pitch A: "We are building X for Y customers, the market is large and growing, our team has the right experience, our technology is state-of-the-art." Each claim is defensible alone. None of them constrain each other. If the market turns out smaller, or the team turns out wrong, or the technology becomes commoditised, the rest of the pitch survives essentially intact. This is a list. Pitch B: "Our customers' core problem is that the cost structure of legacy solutions is set by a particular technology choice that has not been re-examined since the 1990s. Our technology — which is novel because of a recent advance in compilers — drops that cost structure by an order of magnitude. The team is built around the specific technical insight; we hire compiler people, not generalists. The market we attack first is the one where the cost reduction is most acutely needed; once we land there, the network of integrations gives us a structural advantage in adjacent markets that we are not yet in." Now the claims constrain each other. If the market doesn't need the cost reduction, the technology choice was wrong. If the technology doesn't deliver the cost reduction, the team was hired for the wrong skills. If the integration network doesn't form, the market progression breaks. This is a theory. Most pitches are Pitch A pretending to be Pitch B. The audit question is: pull on one strand — is the rest of the pitch still standing?

**Operational.** A leadership team is rewriting the company's strategy. The old strategy was a list of priorities, none of which implied the others. The new draft attempts to be a theory: the customer segment implies the product wedge, which implies the go-to-market motion, which implies the cost structure, which implies the hiring plan, which loops back into what the customer segment expects from the relationship. Each claim is constrained by the others. The four-strands frame doesn't tell you which strategy to pick. It tells you what the strategy needs to *be* — a structure where moving one piece would force you to rebuild the others. That is the operational form of "hard to vary," and it is the property that distinguishes a strategy that will compound from a playbook that will be replaced in eighteen months.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat "four strands" as a sacred number, or claim your framework has exactly four.
*Why:* The point is not the number. It is the structural property: deep explanations are typically held together by multiple mutually-reinforcing claims, each of which would break if another did. In quantum-physics-and-friends, four happens to be where the unification lives. In your domain it might be three, or seven. Forcing the analogy literally is a misuse of the frame. What you want is the property; the count is incidental.

**Don't:** Use the four-strands frame to argue that you don't have to pick.
*Why:* "I'll just use whatever framework fits the moment" is exactly what the four-strands picture is *not*. The whole point is that a coherent worldview commits to claims across multiple domains and lets those claims constrain each other. Eclecticism — picking from each frame as the question demands — gives up the benefit, which is the cross-checking. A single strong theory beats a flexible toolkit, because the theory's strands keep each other honest.

**Don't:** Confuse depth with mathematical complexity.
*Why:* A theory can be deep without being mathematically forbidding. Popper's epistemology is one of the four strands and contains very little mathematics. What makes a theory deep is its reach — how much it constrains, how far it applies, how hard it is to vary. Mathematics often helps but does not constitute depth. A founder's "theory of the business" can be deep in exactly this sense: lots of claims, all constraining each other, predictions that follow without further input. No equations required.

**Don't:** Apply the frame to domains where it has no purchase.
*Why:* The four strands are the four strands of *physical reality* in Deutsch's sense. They are not a universal grid for organising every kind of thinking. Forcing it onto, say, an interpersonal disagreement, or an ethical question that is properly about values rather than facts, is a category error. The general lesson — multi-stranded explanations beat single-stranded ones for reach and durability — travels widely. The specific four don't.
