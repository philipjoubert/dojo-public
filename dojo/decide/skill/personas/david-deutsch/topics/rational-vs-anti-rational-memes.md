# Rational vs. Anti-Rational Memes

## When to Use

- You're auditing a culture — your own or one you're inheriting — and need a sharper question than "is it good?"
- You notice a value or norm that *cannot be questioned* in a meeting without social cost.
- An onboarding programme, all-hands ritual, or "values training" relies on emotional pressure to land its content.
- The team has slowed down, but no one can name a specific decision that went wrong.
- You catch yourself saying "this is just how we do things" — and realise that *is* the explanation, not a placeholder for one.
- You are deciding whether to install a strong, founder-stamped culture at scale, and you want to know which kind you're installing.
- A subculture inside the company (a sales rite, an engineering taboo, a leadership clique) replicates faithfully but resists outside scrutiny.

## Fails When

- **The presenting problem is too small for the frame.** If the question is "should we change the meeting cadence?", reaching for meme theory is rococo. Use it for *culture*, not for calendar disputes.
- **You apply it as an accusation rather than as a self-check.** Calling a colleague's idea "an anti-rational meme" while exempting your own is exactly the move the frame is supposed to expose. The audit goes inward first.
- **You confuse procedural conformity with anti-rational replication.** Surgical checklists, deploy procedures, fire drills, and the rule that pilots wear seatbelts during landing are not anti-rational memes; they are explicit, criticisable, and survive *because* they are useful. The mark of an anti-rational meme is suppression of criticism, not the existence of a rule.
- **You read "anti-rational" as "old" or "rational" as "modern."** The Enlightenment is not chronological. Plenty of contemporary corporate ideologies (sustainability theatre, "the algorithm decides," "this is what the market wants") replicate by disabling judgement. Plenty of old practices (double-entry bookkeeping, the scientific paper) replicate because they are useful and have survived criticism.
- **The culture is genuinely young, raw, and unstable.** Pre-product-market-fit, trying to install criticism *infrastructure* before any cultural content exists is just bureaucracy. Fix the meme first, then the error-correction around it.

## Core Concept

A culture is a set of ideas that cause their holders to behave alike. Those ideas — *memes* — are replicators. They survive in human minds and get transmitted because they have a survival strategy, exactly the way genes do. The interesting question is never "is this culture good?" — that is a static question. The interesting question is: *what strategy does this idea use to get itself replicated?*

There are only two strategies that work in the long run, and they are mutually exclusive. A **rational meme** survives because it is true, useful, beautiful, or otherwise rewards the recipient's critical faculties when they engage with it. It *invites* criticism, and survives because it deserves to. Newton's laws are a rational meme — they get replicated across centuries by people who vehemently disagree with each other on most things, because each of them keeps finding the laws useful for their own divergent purposes. The meme's natural environment is a dynamic society — one in which criticism is allowed and active, because in such an environment its rivals get filtered out faster than it does.

An **anti-rational meme** survives by the opposite strategy: it disables the recipient's critical faculties about itself. It comes with an implicit "do not examine this" — typically not stated, but enforced by emotion (shame, fear, embarrassment, the feeling of betraying your group), by ritual, or by the simple absence of any model of what disagreement would even sound like. Anti-rational memes are the building blocks of static societies. They evolve, like everything else, but their evolution is *toward* greater fidelity in suppressing criticism, not toward truth. Static societies were not exceptions in human history; they were the *rule*. Every society for hundreds of thousands of years before the Enlightenment was dominated by anti-rational memes, which is why it took thousands of years between any noticeable change. The Enlightenment was the first time, anywhere, that a substantial set of memes started replicating *because they survived criticism* rather than *because they suppressed it*.

The relevance to founders: a company culture is a meme system. "Things we don't talk about here," sacred founder stories that get retold but never amended, values posters that survive any quarterly result, the shared sense that *of course* we ship on Mondays / hire only ex-Google / refuse to use Slack — these are memes, and they are either replicating because people keep finding them useful (and would push back if they stopped being useful), or they are replicating because the social cost of pushing back is too high. Those are categorically different cultures, and they have categorically different long-run trajectories. The first is a culture that can correct itself. The second cannot — and the only reason to admire it is the same reason people admired the Easter Islanders' statues: it replicates beautifully. Right up to the moment it doesn't.

The diagnostic isn't whether the culture is intense or mild, old or new, founder-stamped or grassroots. The diagnostic is: *what happens when somebody questions one of its core memes, in good faith, in front of others?* If the social mechanism kicks in before the argument does — if eyebrows go up, or the room cools, or the person is quietly excluded from the next meeting — you are looking at an anti-rational meme. The harder it would be to even *frame* the disagreement in your culture's vocabulary, the deeper the entrenchment.

## How to Apply

1. **List your culture's most-replicated memes.** Not the values poster — the *actual* repeated behaviours, phrases, rituals. Things people new to the company learn within the first month without being explicitly taught. ("We don't say no to customers." "We work weekends during launch." "We hire slow." "We don't do ___.") A meme has to be something faithfully transmitted; if it changes shape every quarter, it isn't yet a meme.

2. **For each one, ask what would happen if a thoughtful, senior person disagreed with it in a meeting.** Not "got it wrong" — *disagreed.* Said the opposite, in good faith, with reasoning. If the answer is "we'd have a productive discussion and possibly update," that meme is being replicated rationally. If the answer is "they'd be seen as not getting it / not a culture fit / lost the plot" — that meme is being replicated anti-rationally. Notice that this is not a question about the meme's *content*. It is a question about its *replication strategy*. A perfectly good belief can be held anti-rationally, and that's still bad, because it deprives you of the ability to update when it stops being good.

3. **Look for the conditions that produce anti-rational memes, not just the memes themselves.** Anything that says "because I say so" or "it's worked for us so far" or "let's not relitigate this" — anything that suppresses criticism in the name of the idea being too important to question — is the *condition* under which anti-rational memes evolve. You can have a perfectly decent meme today that becomes anti-rationally entrenched tomorrow because you removed the criticism path. The condition is the deeper problem.

4. **Notice when your founder mythology is functioning as scripture.** Founder stories are useful — they encode *why we are the way we are*. They turn anti-rational the moment a contrary observation gets answered with "but the founder said…" rather than with the original reasoning. That is the move from rational to anti-rational meme. The fix is not to abolish the founder story; it is to insist that its *reasoning* be available for criticism, the same as anything else.

5. **Distinguish procedural memes from ideological ones.** "Run the migration script before deploying" is a procedural rule that should be transmitted faithfully and obediently — it is explicit, criticisable in principle (if a better procedure appears you'll switch), and the cost of variation is real. "We are a customer-obsessed company" is an ideological meme. The two need different treatments. The mistake is to import the *obedience* mode of the first into the second, which is how you end up with values training that feels like brainwashing.

6. **Build error-correction at the meme level.** It is not enough to have a feedback channel about projects; you need a path by which a *cultural meme itself* can be challenged and updated. In practice this is something like: explicit re-examination of values and stated norms on a regular interval, structured devil's-advocate roles, retrospective culture reviews after big strategic shifts. The point is not endless self-doubt; it's that your culture, like your code, needs the equivalent of a test suite that *proves* it can be edited. If you can't edit it, you don't own it — it owns you.

7. **Be especially suspicious of anti-rational subcultures *within* a dynamic culture.** Meme theory predicts these: in a broadly rational organisation, anti-rational subcultures form pockets where criticism is locally suppressed. Founders' offices, sales teams, the senior leadership cabal, "the original team" — these are the most common venues. The damage is finely tuned: the people involved still function competently in most respects; it's only the specific topic (founder authority, the original strategy, who deserves to lead) where criticism dies. The whole-company culture can be healthy and a 10-person subculture can still be a static society in miniature.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 200-person startup, four years old, runs a weekly all-hands where the founder retells the origin story. New hires note that asking "what would have happened if we hadn't pivoted in year 2?" causes visible discomfort. People who do ask such questions tend to leave within six months.
**Application:** The origin story is a meme. The question is its replication strategy. Notice: the test isn't whether the founder is right about the pivot. The test is whether the *meme survives examination*. If the only acceptable engagement is appreciative retelling, and disagreement gets read as disloyalty, the founder has installed an anti-rational meme at the core of the culture. The local consequence is a culture that cannot reverse a strategic mistake without a regime change. The deeper consequence is that all the *other* memes in the company learn from this one — they learn that the way to survive here is to be unquestionable.
**Result:** The fix is not to abolish the origin story; it's to make the *reasoning* of the pivot available for criticism. Bring the original alternatives back into discussion. Have the founder publicly entertain the counterfactual. Reward the next person who asks the uncomfortable question. The story can remain. The replication strategy has to change.

**Situation:** An engineering organisation has a deeply held meme: "we don't do estimates." It has worked well for six years. A new VP of Product asks why, and is told "we tried estimates once and they were terrible — Dave can tell you the story." Dave is no longer at the company. No one can reconstruct the original argument.
**Application:** The meme survives faithfully — every new engineer gets initiated into it. But its content has decoupled from its reasoning. It is now being replicated because it is *what we do*, not because anyone can defend it under criticism. That is the signature of an anti-rational meme, even if the underlying belief might still be correct. The clue is the appeal to a vanished Dave.
**Result:** Reconstruct the argument from scratch, in the open, with the new VP and the engineers who actually do the work. Either the position survives criticism (in which case you've upgraded the meme from anti-rational to rational with no loss) or it doesn't (in which case you've corrected an error you couldn't even see before). The dangerous version of this is the company that says "we'll keep the practice but stop talking about why" — which is exactly how you preserve an anti-rational meme.

**Situation:** A founder is about to publish a strongly worded "culture deck" inspired by Netflix and Bridgewater. It contains aphorisms, founder photographs, and a section titled "Things We Believe." The section ends "we don't argue about these — if you don't share them, this isn't the place for you."
**Application:** The closing line is not a description; it is a replication strategy. It instructs recipients to disable critical engagement with the content of the section. It does not matter what the section says — even if every value in it is correct, the *method* of installing them is anti-rational. A founder who is right about their values doesn't need to immunise them against criticism; they need to expose them to it, because correct values get *stronger* under examination, while only fragile ones get weaker.
**Result:** The deck either survives editing or it doesn't. The version that says "Here's what we believe and why, here's what would make us change our minds, here's where we've been wrong before" is rational-meme-dominant. The version that says "these are settled" is the same culture, structurally, as the static society that built statues until it ran out of trees. The company will get away with it for a while. It will not get away with it forever.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "anti-rational meme" as a polite way to say "I disagree with this culture."
**Why:** The frame is not a verdict on content. It is a verdict on *how the content is held*. A meme can have content you dislike and still be replicating rationally (it survives criticism in your shop, it just doesn't survive in yours), and a meme can have content you love and still be replicating anti-rationally (you got there by social pressure, not by reasoning). The whole point of distinguishing the two is to evaluate the replication strategy, *separate from* whether you agree with the meme. Conflating them turns the frame into a weapon, and it stops being useful as a diagnostic the moment that happens.

**Don't:** Try to convert an anti-rational meme by adding a "discussion" — a town hall, a survey, a feedback channel — without changing the underlying signal that disagreement is costly.
**Why:** The replication strategy of an anti-rational meme is social, not informational. People aren't failing to disagree because they lack a channel; they're failing to disagree because they've correctly observed that disagreement is punished. Adding a channel without changing the cost of using it is theatrical and possibly worse than nothing — it teaches the team that the *appearance* of criticism is required and the *substance* is not. The change has to be in what happens to the first person who actually says the unsayable thing, in public, and is rewarded rather than punished.

**Don't:** Conclude that a culture which has any anti-rational elements is therefore anti-rational overall.
**Why:** Every dynamic culture, including the post-Enlightenment West that Deutsch is celebrating, contains anti-rational subcultures and residues. Asking "is our culture anti-rational?" is the wrong shape of question. The right shape is "where, specifically, in our culture, has the replication strategy stopped depending on criticism, and what would it cost us to fix that?" The frame is a flashlight, not a thermometer.

**Don't:** Assume that because your culture is intense and faithfully replicated, it is therefore anti-rational.
**Why:** Strong, faithfully replicated cultures can be either kind. The faithfulness of replication is a fact about the meme's survival strategy, not a verdict on which strategy. The right test is the criticism test — what would happen if a credible person disagreed in good faith — not the intensity test. A company can have a fierce, distinctive, identity-laden culture that still welcomes argument about every part of itself; that's the dynamic-society case. The mistake is assuming intensity *requires* the anti-rational mode. Newton's laws are intensely held by physicists. Physicists also try to break them every day. That's how you can tell.
