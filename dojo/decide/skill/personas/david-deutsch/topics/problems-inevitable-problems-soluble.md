# Problems Are Inevitable; Problems Are Soluble

## When to Use

- Whenever someone is treating a current obstacle as a verdict on the entire project's possibility.
- When a setback is being interpreted as the universe telling you to stop, rather than as a problem revealing the next problem.
- Before accepting any "natural limit" argument — to growth, to a market, to a technology, to human capacity.
- When team morale slumps after a thing breaks. The right frame is *another problem to solve*, not *the project was doomed*.
- When sizing a hard project: is the question "this hasn't been done" or "this can't be done"? The two have very different responses.
- Whenever someone invokes Spaceship Earth, the precautionary principle, or "sustainability" as if they were technical concepts.
- When evaluating the moral weight of a given evil — is it irreducible, or is it a problem awaiting sufficient knowledge?

## Fails When

- **The obstacle is forbidden by the laws of physics.** "Soluble in principle" is not "soluble at any time, by any team, with any budget." If the laws of nature forbid the outcome, no amount of knowledge will help. Faster-than-light, perpetual motion, prophecy of future ideas — those really are off the table.
- **It is used to dismiss near-term operational reality.** "Problems are soluble" does not mean payroll is optional, or that you can keep burning cash on the assumption a breakthrough will arrive on schedule. The principle is about the long structure of progress. It is not a permission slip.
- **It is applied to questions that aren't problems.** Aesthetic preferences, identity, value choices — these aren't problems in the technical sense. There is nothing to solve.
- **It is used to suppress legitimate criticism.** "Every solution reveals further problems" is a feature, not a bug. A team that hears criticism and replies "well, problems are inevitable" without engaging the criticism has misunderstood the principle.

## Core Concept

There are two maxims here, and they are inseparable. They look like they cancel each other out. They don't. Together they describe the structure of progress.

*Problems are inevitable.* No matter how much we know, no matter how good our solutions are, every solution reveals further problems. This is not a tragic limitation; it is the form that knowledge growth takes. Solving a problem expands the question. The Earth's biosphere, contrary to the lullaby of the Spaceship Earth metaphor, is *incapable* of supporting human life. It never did. The Great Rift Valley where our species evolved was a death-trap by present standards — no safe water, no medical equipment, infested with predators and pathogens, killing most of its inhabitants young. What lets you live in Oxfordshire today is not the biosphere's beneficence but a life-support system *we built*: clothes, houses, farms, the electrical grid, hospitals, sewage. To the extent we are on a spaceship, we have never been its passengers, nor its stewards, nor even its maintenance crew. We are its designers and builders. Before our designs, the planet was a heap of dangerous raw materials. So problems are inevitable not as a curse but as a description: every successful design opens a new front. There has never been an unproblematic time for humans, and there never will be.

*Problems are soluble.* Anything not forbidden by the laws of physics is achievable, given the right knowledge. This is a strong claim and it follows from a simple argument: if there were a class of physical transformations that no possible knowledge could ever achieve, that fact would itself be a regularity in nature. All regularities have explanations; the explanation of that regularity would be a law of nature. So the dichotomy exhausts the cases. Either the laws of physics forbid a transformation, or there is a way to bring it about. The biosphere was not "designed" to keep us alive; we figured out how to keep ourselves alive in it, and then on the moon, and then anywhere else we choose to go. There can be no such thing as a disease for which it is impossible to discover a cure (other than certain kinds of brain damage that have dissipated the patient's personality), because the diseased patient is a physical object and turning a diseased physical object into a healthy one of the same person is not forbidden by physics. Anything left over is a matter of knowing how. And the same applies, equally trivially, to the evil of death from age and disease itself.

Now name what these maxims oppose. On the one side, naïve utopianism: the belief that we will solve everything and then live in finished comfort. The first maxim rules this out — there is no such state. Every solution will reveal further problems, including some that look bigger than what they replaced. On the other side, Malthusian and precautionary pessimism: the belief that we have hit a structural ceiling, that resources are running out, that the planet "cannot sustain" us, that we have to stop. The second maxim rules this out — every "limit" of this kind is a forecast that no future knowledge will arrive, which is a prophecy. The two maxims work as a pair. Holding either alone misleads.

The right stance toward an unsolved problem is therefore: *we don't know enough yet*. Not "this is structurally impossible." Not "we will solve it any minute now." Just: the gap is in our knowledge, the laws of physics permit the solution, and what comes next is the work of looking. This is the structure inside Kennedy's "we choose to go to the moon… not because they are easy, but because they are hard." He was not predicting success. He was committing to a project whose completion *required* knowledge that did not yet exist, and trusting that the institutions of inquiry could create it. Compare that to Spaceship Earth's recommendation: stop trying. The argument from sustainability — that we should protect the planet's capacity to keep providing for us — is exactly inverted. The planet does not provide for us. *We* provide for us, by continually creating the knowledge that converts what was previously a heap of raw materials into a life-support system. The proper aspiration is not sustainability; it is progress. Only progress is sustainable.

## How to Apply

1. **Sort the obstacle: is it physics or is it ignorance?** This is the first question for any problem that looks impassable. If the laws of nature forbid the outcome, you have your answer. If they don't, you have a knowledge problem — possibly a very hard one, possibly one that will take a generation, but a problem of the kind that can in principle be solved. Refuse to let a knowledge problem be relabelled as a physical impossibility just because the speaker is tired or losing the argument.

2. **Treat every solution as the source of the next problem.** If your project succeeds, the world will not be done — it will have a new shape with new failure modes. Build the team, the cap table, the architecture, and the relationships in a way that assumes this. The team that thinks "after we ship, things will be calm" is preparing for a state that will not arrive.

3. **Refuse to read setbacks as verdicts.** A setback is a problem revealing itself. It is not the universe telling you the project was wrong. The Easter Islanders' civilisation collapsed because they could not solve the problems they faced — not because their problems were unsolvable in principle. The right diagnostic question after a failure is not *was this project doomed?* but *what specifically went wrong, and what knowledge would be needed to avoid it next time?*

4. **Audit any "we've reached the limit" claim for prophecy.** When someone says the market is saturated, the technology is mature, the resource is exhausted, or the natural ceiling has been hit, ask: what knowledge are they implicitly assuming will not arrive? Almost always, the limit is downstream of an unstated forecast that no relevant innovation will happen. (See `wealth-as-knowledge.md`.)

5. **Use sustainability talk as a diagnostic, not a goal.** When someone tells you a way of life is unsustainable, the right reply is *good — sustainable lifestyles are static societies, and static societies eventually fail, because the next problem the static society cannot solve always arrives*. The relevant question is whether the unsustainability is the kind that gets resolved by the next round of knowledge creation, or the kind that the laws of physics force on you.

6. **For the moral weight of an evil, ask whether the bottleneck is knowledge.** Disease, ageing, suffering, scarcity — these are not metaphysically deep. They are problems with the structure of "we don't yet know how." That changes how you should feel about them. Resignation toward problems that are in principle soluble is not wisdom; it's a moral abdication camouflaged as mature acceptance.

7. **Build institutions that can absorb new problems.** Since problems are inevitable, the durable thing is not a final solution but a system that keeps producing new ones. In a company: hiring, planning, post-mortems, and onboarding all need to be designed on the assumption that the problem you face today is not the problem you will face in eighteen months. Solutions calcify; processes-for-finding-solutions don't have to.

## Examples

**Historical / scientific.** In 1971 the population scientist Paul Ehrlich gave a lecture in which he forecast civilisational collapse from resource depletion within a decade or two. Half a dozen catastrophes were already too late to avoid. Mass starvation by 1981. Australia near its sustainable limit. The standard of living of the United States required reducing its population by three-quarters. A girl in the audience asked: what if we solve one of these problems? Ehrlich's reply was instructive — *how would we solve it? And even if we did, what would we do then?* The shape of his argument was: if we stop solving problems, we are doomed. Which is correct, but not informative. None of the catastrophes he scheduled actually arrived, because the world did not stop solving problems. What Ehrlich missed — what every Malthusian has missed for two centuries — is that the second maxim makes the first one bearable. New problems arrive; new knowledge meets them; the cycle does not stop. The fallacy was not in his observations of present limits, but in his prophecy that present knowledge represented the available repertoire forever.

**Historical / civilisational.** Consider the Easter Islanders. The conventional read — the one Attenborough draws — is that they overconsumed their forest and that this is a warning about Spaceship Earth. The deeper read, which is closer to the truth, is that they belonged to a static society that could not create new knowledge fast enough to meet the next problem. Their forestry failures were typical of their lack of problem-solving across the board. Their civilisation collapsed not because resource management is hard, but because they had no institutions of criticism that would let them notice their mistakes and correct them. The lesson for us is *not* "be careful with your forests." It is: build a society — a company, an institution, a country — that can produce solutions faster than its problems arrive. That is the only sustainable way of life there is.

**Founder-operational.** A founder runs an early growth experiment that fails badly. CAC turns out to be triple what the model assumed; the channel is gone; revenue projections need to be cut. There are two interpretations available, and they look identical for about a week. *Interpretation A:* the problem is structural — the unit economics don't work, the market won't bear the price, the product is wrong, time to pivot or shut down. *Interpretation B:* the problem is real but solvable — the channel was the wrong one, the segment was wrong, the positioning was off, and the next experiment will tell us more. Most teams collapse Interpretation A and Interpretation B into "things aren't working." Both maxims here are diagnostic. Problems are inevitable: the failure of this experiment was *expected*, in the sense that experiments are how you find out. Problems are soluble: the right next move is not to despair but to identify the specific knowledge gap revealed by the failure, and to design the cheapest possible next experiment that closes it. That is what good founders do operationally; the maxims describe the shape of the move.

**Operational failure mode.** A team adopts the "problems are soluble" half of the principle and uses it to wave away every concern. "We'll figure it out." This is the half-principle and it is dangerous. The other half — *problems are inevitable* — implies that "we'll figure it out" cannot be the universal reply, because new problems will arrive faster than you can hand-wave them off. The team that says "problems are soluble" must also be the team that says "and there will be more of them than we expected, and many will be expensive, and we have to be set up to find out which ones are which." The pair is load-bearing. Either alone leads to a different failure: utopianism alone produces sloppy execution; pessimism alone produces stagnation.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "problems are soluble" as an answer to a specific operational complaint.
*Why:* If a customer reports that the product is broken, the answer is not "problems are soluble." The answer is to fix the product. The maxims operate at the level of the shape of progress; they are not a substitute for the work. Founders who deploy them as conversational closers usually end up running teams that lose trust in the principles themselves.

**Don't:** Treat "the biosphere is incapable of supporting human life" as anti-environmentalism.
*Why:* The point is not that the biosphere is bad or that we should ignore environmental damage. The point is that the moral and practical agency lies with us. Environmental problems — climate change, pollution, biodiversity loss — are real, and they are problems in exactly the sense the maxims describe: inevitable, in that some new environmental problem will always be downstream of the latest solution; and soluble, in that physics does not forbid clean energy, carbon capture, or restoration. The maxims redirect the work — toward more knowledge, more wealth, more capability — rather than toward sustainability frames that secretly assume we cannot do better than we already do.

**Don't:** Confuse "stasis is unsustainable" with "growth at any cost."
*Why:* The argument that only progress is sustainable is not the argument that any specific business model should be pursued recklessly. The maxims tell you that *some* form of continued knowledge creation is the only durable strategy at the level of civilisations. They do not tell you which strategy *your* company should run. Treating them as if they did is one of the more common ways founders use Deutsch to flatter their own decisions.

**Don't:** Forget the asymmetry: physics is the constraint, not knowledge.
*Why:* If a colleague says "this is impossible," you should ask whether they mean *forbidden by physics* or *not yet known how*. The two cases require very different responses, and people will routinely conflate them — usually because the second one feels more honest if framed as the first. The maxim does not let you off the hook from physics; it lets you off the hook from prophecy disguised as physics.
