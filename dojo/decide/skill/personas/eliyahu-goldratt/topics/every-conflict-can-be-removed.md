# Every Conflict Can Be Removed

## When to Use
- Any persistent business conflict: Marketing vs Sales, Finance vs Operations, Quality vs Speed, Centralize vs Decentralize. The frame is "these must be balanced." Stop, and examine the frame.
- A compromise is on the table. Both parties are about to lose a little. The real solution has not been found yet.
- A single decision feels like "damned if you do, damned if you don't." That is the signature of a flawed assumption hiding in plain sight.
- Organizational change efforts that create perceived winners and losers. You are about to generate resistance; better to find the win-win first.

## Fails When
- The parties will not examine their assumptions. The method requires genuine willingness to say "I might have been wrong about what was necessary." Without that willingness, the conflict stays.
- The situation is truly zero-sum at the relevant level — two people, one chair, same second — and even here, step up a level (different chair, different time) before concluding.

## Core Concept

Every conflict can be removed. Not managed. Not compromised. Removed.

When I say this, people laugh. They have spent careers learning to "balance" competing priorities and accept painful trade-offs. Their belief: some conflicts are just reality. But consider — if a conflict truly had no resolution, we would call it a law of physics, not a management problem. A persistent conflict is evidence that there is a flawed assumption somewhere underneath, not a law of nature.

Most "conflicts" in organizations dissolve the moment the assumptions behind the positions are surfaced. The company wants low inventory; the plant manager wants high machine utilization. These seem to conflict. But the assumption connecting "high utilization" to "profitability" is false at a non-constraint. Subordinate non-constraints to the constraint and both needs are met — low inventory *and* high real efficiency (measured by throughput, not activity). The "conflict" was never in reality; it existed only in the head of whoever had written the measurement.

Marketing wants variety; manufacturing wants fewer changeovers. The assumption: changeover time is fixed. Toyota invested in SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die) and shrunk changeover times by orders of magnitude. Variety and efficiency together. The assumption, not the reality, was the problem.

The employee wants work-life balance; the employer wants productivity. The assumption: more hours mean more output. For knowledge work the relationship inverts above a threshold — people produce less in their 70th weekly hour than they would have produced in their 50th. Balance and productivity align.

At the root of every non-harmonious relationship we will find a conflict, usually a heavy-duty chronic conflict. Every difficult relationship — with your boss, your spouse, your supplier, your customer — has a conflict at its root. That conflict persists because neither party has surfaced and challenged the underlying assumption. If the root cause of a non-harmonious relationship is a conflict, and every conflict can be removed, then even chronic conflicts at the muddy bottom can be removed. Can be — not must be, not will be. Can be, if you do the work.

The work is surfacing assumptions.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse the compromise frame.** The moment someone says "let's split the difference," pause. Compromise is lose-lose dressed up as wisdom. The underlying tension survives and will resurface. You need the real solution, not an armistice.

2. **State the positions clearly.** "We must do X" versus "We must do Y." Do not soften.

3. **Find the real need behind each position.** What outcome is each side trying to achieve? The position is the proposed action; the need is the outcome. Needs are almost always legitimate. That is why the conflict persists.

4. **Find the common objective.** Both needs serve something higher. For internal disputes, it is usually the organization's success. Naming this often defuses the temperature — and sets up the search for the assumption.

5. **Surface the assumption between each need and its position.** Why does this need require this specific action? Write it down. Every one. Including the obvious ones.

6. **Challenge each assumption.** Is it always true? Under what conditions would it fail? What action could make it false?

7. **Design the injection.** The action that breaks the false assumption and dissolves the conflict. Not a compromise — a change in reality that makes both needs fully achievable.

8. **Validate.** If the injection works, both parties say "yes, my need is met." Not "I can live with this." If anyone is still unhappy, the assumption was not the right one; keep looking.

## Examples

**Situation:** An airline's ops team wants to reduce flight cancellations; the customer team wants to avoid passenger displacement costs. The two teams have been fighting about overbooking policy for years.
**Application:** Common objective: profitable, reputable airline. Need A (ops): fill seats; overbook to compensate for no-shows. Need B (customer): never displace a passenger against their will. Compromise: overbook less. Unsatisfying to both. Surface the assumption behind overbooking: the airline cannot predict no-shows accurately. Is that true? With modern data, predictions are much better than they used to be. And the assumption behind "never displace": customers resent displacement regardless of compensation. Is that true? Some customers will happily volunteer for a meaningful compensation. The injection: a market-based volunteer system offering real compensation, tuned to demand, with predictable mechanics. No forced displacement; full revenue capture. Both needs met.
**Result:** The airline captures the revenue of overbooking without the reputation cost of forced displacement. Neither team compromised. The win-win had been sitting there under the unexamined assumption "compensation for displacement is painful by definition."

**Situation:** A founder wants to raise the next round at a much higher valuation; the lead investor wants a "reasonable" valuation. Stalemate.
**Application:** Common objective: a successful financing for a high-growth company. Need founder: preserve ownership and signal strength. Need investor: acceptable return profile and protect downside. Compromise: land in the middle, both unhappy. Surface assumptions. Founder assumes "higher price = better terms." Not always — structure can matter more than headline. Investor assumes "lower price = lower risk." Not always — a deal with downside protection at a higher price may be equivalent to a lower price without protection. Injection: negotiate the structure (ratchets, liquidation preferences, milestones, tranches) rather than the headline valuation.
**Result:** A structure emerges that both parties can sign. Not a compromise — a different deal shape that meets both needs better than either original position. The "conflict" over price evaporates once the assumption "price is the decision" is challenged.

**Situation:** Parent wants teenager home at 10 PM for safety; teenager wants midnight for social life. Classic family conflict.
**Application:** Common objective: good family relationship, teen growing up well. Need A: safety. Need B: social participation, demonstrated maturity. Surface the assumption under A: "safety requires physical presence at home." Is it true? Safety can be verified by check-in, location sharing, known activity type. Surface the assumption under B: "social participation requires midnight." Is it true? Many activities end earlier; specific events go later. Injection: 10 PM curfew for ordinary nights with check-in; defined later hours for specific known-safe events.
**Result:** Both needs met. Neither side compromised. Everyone had been fighting over the wrong variable (the hour) when the real variable was "verified-safe information flow."

**Situation:** A manufacturer needs to ship product faster but not sacrifice quality. Sales wants speed; QA wants thoroughness.
**Application:** The assumption "more QA = slower" may be false. Often quality inspection at the end of the line is where delays concentrate, and moving inspection upstream — before the constraint — actually speeds total throughput while improving quality. The second assumption "faster means sloppier" is also false; many defects originate from rushing under overload, not from the speed of the process itself. Injection: redesign where inspection happens and reduce WIP so people aren't rushing. Both speed and quality improve together.
**Result:** The trade-off the organization has internalized for years disappears. Quality and speed, far from opposing, are aligned once WIP is managed and inspection is positioned correctly.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept "we have to balance" as an answer.
**Why:** "Balance" is the operational word for compromise. Both sides lose a little. The tension stays. The real answer is "find the assumption," not "find the middle."

**Don't:** Seek the injection from your side only.
**Why:** The clean move, Goldratt's own teaching: start by seeking the other party's win, not your own. Look for a way to give them what they need that doesn't threaten what you need. Real empathy — not tactical empathy — produces win-win solutions you cannot invent from your side of the table.

**Don't:** Stop at "let's agree to disagree."
**Why:** That is compromise with no movement. The underlying conflict is still there and will resurface at the next decision. Keep examining.

**Don't:** Use the framework to justify what you already wanted.
**Why:** If you know the answer before you surface the assumptions, you will write the assumption that validates you and skip the one that refutes you. Draw the Cloud when you genuinely don't know which assumption is false. That's when it actually teaches.

**Don't:** Treat compromise as maturity.
**Why:** In some cultures, willingness to compromise is equated with adult behavior. In reality, compromise is lazy. The mature answer is to do the hard work of finding the assumption. Anyone can split the difference; few can dissolve the conflict.

Every conflict can be removed. The only question is whether you'll do the work. The assumption is where the work lives.
