# Strategy and Tactic Trees (S&T Trees)

## When to Use
- A strategy exists on paper — a vision, a transformation plan, a five-year horizon — and the organization is not executing it. Work is happening; the right work is not.
- Too many initiatives. Management attention is the real scarce resource and it is being sprayed across dozens of projects, most of which contribute nothing to the top objective.
- A top executive "gave clear focus" and the organization produced twenty interpretations. The gap between "what we want" and "how to get there" is being filled with guesses.
- A Viable Vision or ambitious objective — 4x in four years, a market-constraint move, a Mafia Offer rollout — needs to be decomposed into synchronized changes without losing the causal logic.
- An existing strategy has to survive the handoff from the C-suite through middle management to the people who actually do the work, without distortion at each layer.

## Fails When
- The organization refuses to write down its assumptions. The whole power of an S&T tree is in the *three whys* — necessary, parallel, sufficiency — and those require honesty about what you are betting on. Without them, the tree is a decorated org chart.
- Executives insist strategy is their exclusive property and will not accept reality-checks from the levels below. The S&T tree is explicitly two-way: responsibility for the tactic stays at the higher level, but contribution from below is mandatory. Refuse that, and the tree will be logically hollow.
- The top objective is itself unclear. S&T is for achieving an objective, not for discovering one. If you do not yet know whether you're trying to make money, dominate a market, or survive the quarter, decide first.
- People treat it as a project plan. It is not a Gantt chart and it is not a WBS. It is a map of the *changes required* to close the gap between the current state and the objective — nothing more.

## Core Concept

Dr. Alan Barnard's masterclass tells the story cleanly: Goldratt, in the early 1980s running Creative Output, watched his own company resist change faster than his clients did. His hypothesis — and this is the Goldratt move — was that the culprit was his own poor communication. He had been making two mistakes.

First, he was mixing the *what for* (the objective, the strategy) and the *how to* (the tactic) inconsistently. Sometimes he gave the objective and neglected the method: "get to the top of the mountain by Friday." The organization split — some people drove, some flew, some climbed. Sometimes he gave the method without re-grounding in the objective: the objective had changed from "sell products" to "sell value," but he was still describing process changes as if the goal were the same. Second, he presented decisions as done deals, which prevented people from contributing — and when people cannot contribute to a change, they do not own it.

From that came a deceptively simple redefinition. **Strategy is the answer to the question "what for?" — what are you actually trying to achieve. Tactic is the answer to the question "how to?" — of all possible ways, which way have you chosen and why.** The insight that followed: strategy and tactic always exist as *pairs*. For every objective there must be a way of achieving it, or you would not have listed it. For every action there must be a reason, or you would not be taking it. Pairs, at every level of the organization, all the way down to the person on the shop floor.

That produces the tree. Each box on an S&T tree is a *change that needs to be made* — a weak link in the chain that is not yet at the level of good enough. Not every part of the business. Only the changes. Level 1 is the top objective, the ever-flourishing company through profitable growth (or whatever the specific goal is). Level 2 is the Decisive Competitive Edges that will close the gap. Level 3 is what needs to be built, capitalized on, and sustained for each edge. Level 4 down to actionable information — the point at which a person knows what to do on Monday morning.

Two axes. **Going down** you add detail by asking *how*. **Going from left to right** you check sequence — *when* is this required, is there a dependency, what is the biggest-bang-for-buck first step. **Going up** you validate by explaining *why* this is needed to achieve the level above.

What makes the S&T tree different from every other strategy tool Goldratt read — and he said he read them all — is that any strategy or tactic is only as valid as its underlying assumptions, and those assumptions are never made explicit in conventional strategy documents. So he added three gray boxes alongside each strategy-tactic pair. **The necessary assumption** — why is this change needed at all? What is the gap, the pain, the pot of gold? This is the sell; without it nobody commits. **The parallel assumptions** — under what conditions is this objective possible, and what makes it difficult or risky? These justify the specific tactic you chose over the alternatives. If somebody reads your strategy and the tactic comes as a surprise, your parallel assumptions are weak. **The sufficiency assumption** — why is this level of detail not yet enough? What warning prevents the people below from making predictable mistakes in execution — forgetting a box, adding a wrong one, sequencing them badly? The sufficiency assumption is also what drives the need to go one level deeper.

The structure has one more property that matters: the S&T tree maps organizational responsibility. Level 1 is the CEO's. Level 2 belongs to direct reports. The person accountable for a tactic at any level is the person at that level — they can and must take inputs from below, but the decision is theirs. "Are we getting to the top of the mountain by climbing, driving, or flying?" is decided by the person who owns that tactic, not delegated.

The consequence: you do not improve a system by listing everything it does and optimizing each piece. You identify the few weak links whose improvement closes the gap between current and desired performance, you define the change pair for each, you make the assumptions explicit, and you synchronize the sequence. Everything the organization does that is not on the tree is, by definition, not needed to achieve the objective. That is the discipline of focus.

## How to Apply

### Step 1 — Name the top objective, precisely
The Level 1 box is a change statement, not a slogan. Go from current state X to target state Y by when. For a business: "ever-flourishing company through profitable growth" means net profit growing year on year, not just absolute numbers. For a project: a specific deliverable, a specific date, a specific quality bar. If you cannot verbalize the gap between where you are and where you want to be, you have not defined the objective cleanly enough to build a tree on.

### Step 2 — Draft Level 2 as Decisive Competitive Edges (or the equivalent weak links)
For a for-profit organization, Level 2 is almost always the two or three edges you will build that together close the gap. For other systems, ask: what are the few weak links that if all brought to the level of good enough, would make the top objective achievable? Not ten. Not twenty. Two or three. If you have more, you have not thought clearly about leverage.

### Step 3 — Decompose each Level 2 box
For each edge or weak link, Level 3 typically has three questions: what do we need to **build** to create it, what do we need to do to **capitalize** on it, what do we need to do to **sustain** it. Build–capitalize–sustain is a useful reminder scaffold; it is not sacred, but it pushes you to think about the whole lifecycle instead of only the construction.

### Step 4 — Go deeper until "actionable information"
Keep asking *how* and adding child boxes until a person reading the leaf can say, without interpretation, what they will do differently on Monday morning. The test Barnard recommends: ask them to come forward and state, in their own words, what they will change. Can they see any negatives or risks? If they can see none, either the solution is perfect (unlikely) or they do not understand it (likely).

### Step 5 — Write the three whys for every box
- **Necessary assumption** — why this change is needed. The gap, the pain, the pot of gold. Imagine selling the change to a skeptical audience and what you would feel pressure to say first.
- **Parallel assumptions** — under what conditions is the objective possible, and what makes it difficult or risky. These narrow the space of tactics to the one you have chosen. The tactic must not come as a surprise after reading them.
- **Sufficiency assumption** — why this level of detail is not yet enough. Warnings against predictable execution mistakes: missing boxes (insufficiency), extra boxes (unnecessary), wrong sequence (when-mistakes).

### Step 6 — Validate horizontally (the *when*)
Left to right is sequence. Sometimes it is dependency — B can only happen after A. Sometimes it is priority — A is far higher leverage than B, so start with A. Barnard recalls Goldratt asking a room of TOC experts "what's the first thing you do when you arrive on-site?" They all said "identify the constraint." He said "no — choke the release first." Because with too much work-in-process, you cannot see the constraint clearly. Sequence matters. Check every left-to-right step.

### Step 7 — Validate vertically (the *why*)
Bottom-up, for every box, read the three whys and the parent. The logic must hold: *if this box is achieved, plus its siblings, then the parent is achieved*. Walk the tree upward to the top. If anywhere the chain breaks, you have either a missing box, an unnecessary box, or a parent that was not correctly decomposed.

### Step 8 — Do the sticky-note exercise
Barnard's field test: get the managers to write every current change initiative on a sticky note and place each one on the tree where they think it belongs. Two things happen. First, orphans — initiatives that don't fit anywhere on the tree. They are consuming management attention without contributing to the objective. Kill them. Second, bare boxes on the tree with no initiatives underneath — gaps where the tree says work is needed and nothing is happening. Fill them.

### Step 9 — Assign responsibility at each level
The person at the level of the box is accountable for the tactic. Inputs from below are required but the call is theirs. Make this explicit. An S&T tree without a name on every box is a wish list.

### Step 10 — Run it as a living document
The tree is not finished when drawn. As execution reveals which assumptions were wrong, rewrite the boxes and the gray assumption boxes. The tree is the organization's current best theory of how to get from here to there; theories get updated.

## Examples

**Situation:** A mining company is five months into a year with a 12-million-ton target and has only produced 3 million tons. They insist they "know TOC" and have already done everything a good TOC practitioner would do. The leadership team lists, when asked, about a hundred active initiatives consuming management attention and investment.
**Application:** Draft the top objective: 12 million tons this year, from a current run-rate of 7 million. That is the gap. Level 2 — what are the one or two Decisive Competitive Edges that would close it? For this mine, throughput per production day. Level 3 for that edge — build (exploit the actual bottleneck in the extraction chain), capitalize (eliminate non-bottleneck work that starves the bottleneck), sustain (policy changes to keep the bottleneck fed through shifts and maintenance windows). Now run the sticky-note test: of the hundred initiatives, how many would actually produce more tons? When the mining team ran this, the answer was two. Those two were receiving about 2% of management attention. The other 98% was being consumed by initiatives that, when tested against the tree, had no logical path to more tons.
**Result:** Kill 98 initiatives. Allocate management attention to the two. Define the three whys for each so the middle layers do not drift back. Closing the gap becomes arithmetic, not heroic effort.

**Situation:** A premium food manufacturer — bread and flour — is implementing a Viable Vision transformation and realizes its organizational structure was designed for the old strategy and is now misaligned.
**Application:** Build an Organizational S&T tree — the same structure applied to job responsibilities. For each role, the necessary assumption answers "why is this job needed for the organization's goal?" The strategy answers "what measurable outcomes must this role produce on an ongoing basis?" The tactic answers "what are the things that take your time, and why are they necessary and sufficient to produce the outcomes?" The sufficiency assumption asks "what kinds of responsibility-versus-authority fires consume your time?" — and those fires, if written down, reveal the structural misalignment.
**Result:** The tree becomes the blueprint for restructuring. Roles whose tactics no longer link to the top objective are redefined or cut. The rewriting is driven by logic, not politics.

**Situation:** A software company has a five-year vision but every quarterly plan drifts into firefighting. Executives say they communicated the strategy; the VPs say they executed it; frontline engineers say they never saw a coherent plan.
**Application:** Draw the S&T tree with the strategy as Level 1. Level 2 decisive edges — pick two (e.g., unmatched reliability in a category where competitors are flaky; sub-week delivery where competitors take quarters). Level 3 build-capitalize-sustain for each. Level 4 actionable information for each Level 3 box. Then show the tree to each VP and each engineering lead. For every active project they own, ask them to place it on the tree. Projects that don't fit on the tree go into a review queue. Projects on the tree with no ownership need an assignment. Three whys are written for every box. The quarterly plan is now what was on the tree for that quarter, and nothing else.
**Result:** Management attention — the scarce resource — is concentrated on the changes that actually close the gap. Firefighting drops because most fires were in the orphan-initiatives column.

**Situation:** A car manufacturer is chronically late to market because changes flood in between the original design and production. Each change delays further.
**Application:** A Content S&T tree — the same structure applied to the product design itself. Level 1 is the new model with its target market and release date. Level 2 is the major components — chassis, body, powertrain, electronics, interior — with an explicit statement for each of what changes from the previous platform and what stays. Level 3 is the specific modifications per component. All the way to actionable information: the engineer reading a leaf knows exactly what to change and what not to. The three whys on every box catch the hidden assumptions early. The parallel assumptions — "why is this modification possible but difficult?" — surface manufacturing or supplier risks at design time instead of six months in.
**Result:** Late-stage changes collapse because the logic of every modification was stress-tested at the start. The tree became the design blueprint, which then flowed into an SAP implementation scope that was correspondingly focused rather than boil-the-ocean.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the S&T tree as a process map or a complete organizational chart.
**Why:** Each box is a *change required*, not everything the organization does. If every department and every task is on the tree, you have built a wall chart, not a strategy. The leverage comes from what is *not* on the tree — the work that stops because it was not needed to close the gap.

**Don't:** Allow strategy without tactic or tactic without strategy anywhere on the tree.
**Why:** This is Goldratt's original diagnosis of his own failure. Objective without method produces confusion (driving, flying, climbing). Method without objective produces drift (still running the old process after the goal has changed). Every box must carry both — the what-for and the how-to, as a pair.

**Don't:** Delegate the tactic decision to the lowest level "because they know best."
**Why:** The person at the level of the box is responsible. They must take inputs from below — the people closest to the work often see constraints the level above misses — but the call is theirs. Management-by-objective without a tree means every subordinate picks their own method, and the organization fragments.

**Don't:** Skip the three whys because you are in a hurry.
**Why:** The tree without the assumption boxes is a wall chart. The assumptions are where the thinking lives. Skip them and the first piece of contrary evidence knocks the whole plan over because no one remembers what you were betting on.

**Don't:** Build a tree with twenty Level-2 boxes.
**Why:** Level 2 is the Decisive Competitive Edges — the few weak links that when closed, close the gap to the top objective. If you have twenty, you have not identified the constraints; you have listed wishes. Force convergence. Two or three. Rarely more.

**Don't:** Treat the tree as final.
**Why:** It is your current best theory of how to get from here to there. Execution will falsify some of the parallel assumptions. When it does, rewrite the relevant branch — don't patch around it. A tree that is never revised is a tree that has stopped thinking.

**Don't:** Mistake actionable information for full autonomy.
**Why:** "Actionable" means a person knows what to do on Monday. It does not mean they own the whole tree. They execute the leaf; the accountability for the parent still lives one level up. Without that discipline, the synchronization across branches collapses.

The S&T tree is not a planning artifact. It is a thinking discipline that forces an organization to state, publicly and in writing, what it is trying to achieve, what changes will close the gap, why each change is needed, why the chosen method is the one chosen, and what the warnings are. Everything else the organization does is, by the tree's own logic, unnecessary. That is the whole point.
