# The Agreement Matrix (Tools of Cooperation)

## When to Use
- A CEO or executive is designing a change program and asks which tools to use — whether to use vision statements, reorganization, training, financial incentives, firings, culture-building, or some combination.
- A vision statement was just rolled out, and instead of inspiring the organization it produced rolled eyes or quiet indifference.
- A merger has been announced and the integration plan depends on the acquired employees voluntarily cooperating with the new direction.
- A turnaround is underway and a new CEO is deciding how much unilateral authority to demand before taking the job.
- A company with a strong culture (a great one, historically) is struggling to change direction in response to new competitive conditions, and leadership is becoming progressively more frustrated.
- Someone attributes an organizational change failure to "bad leadership" or "resistance to change" without a structural diagnosis.

## Fails When
- The problem is an individual-performance problem rather than a group-cooperation problem. The matrix describes how groups agree or disagree; it doesn't address one person's motivation or competence.
- The analyst is reading surface behavior for agreement. People who nod in meetings may vehemently disagree once they leave; people who argue openly may actually be more aligned than they appear. The diagnosis has to probe what people actually want and what they actually believe about cause and effect, not what they say when watched.
- Used to rationalize the wrong tool. Power tools in a strong-culture organization produce open revolt (Carly Fiorina at HP). Vision statements in a low-consensus organization produce contempt. Picking the wrong quadrant's tool and then trying harder makes the failure worse, not better.

## Core Concept

A leader who wants to drive change in an organization is not really wielding ideas; she is wielding tools for eliciting cooperation. The tools she has available are quite different in character. Some are coercive: fiat, threats, control of budgets and jobs. Some are transactional: training, standard operating procedures, measurement systems, compensation plans. Some are motivational: vision statements, role modeling, charisma, salesmanship. Some are institutional: rituals, folklore, shared beliefs, cultural norms.

No tool works in every situation. The tool that moved Continental Airlines out of bankruptcy would have been laughed out of the Apple Computer of 1985. The vision statement that aligned Microsoft around beating Netscape would have produced indifferent compliance at Continental. And, crucially, no leader has an infinite toolkit — most managers know how to use only a few of these tools well, and when their instincts fail them, they reach for the tool they're most comfortable with whether or not it fits the circumstance.

What determines which tool will work is not the scale of the ambition or the charisma of the leader. It is two diagnostic questions about the organization itself: *How strongly do people in this organization agree on what they want — the results they seek, the values they hold, the priorities they'd trade off for each other?* And: *How strongly do they agree on cause and effect — which actions will produce which results, how the world works, what will happen if they take action X?* Plot those two dimensions as axes and you get a 2×2 matrix that sorts every organization into one of four quadrants. Each quadrant admits a specific family of change tools and rejects the others.

The **lower-left quadrant** — low agreement on what people want, low agreement on how the world works — is the territory of merging organizations, turnarounds, and crises. Nothing induces cooperation here except power tools: fiat, force, coercion, threats. Marshal Tito held Yugoslavia together with the gun barrel; Jamie Dimon integrated JPMorgan Chase with Bank One the same way; Gordon Bethune at Continental, and Jack Welch in his early GE years, both used power tools to force enough cooperation to get moving. Negotiation, strategic planning, and financial incentives sound sophisticated but do not work in the lower-left — they require a minimum level of shared premises to function, and the lower-left is precisely where those premises are absent. Managers who find themselves in a balkanized organization without the authority to use power tools are being asked to do a job their toolkit cannot accomplish.

The **upper-left quadrant** — high agreement on what people want, low agreement on how to get it — is the territory of leadership tools. When everyone in the room wants the same thing but can't agree on the method, charisma and role modeling and vision statements work, because the vision articulates the shared hope and invites cooperation on methodology. Good sales managers use these tools instinctively. Microsoft in 1995, facing the Netscape threat, was upper-left; everyone wanted to dominate the desktop, nobody yet knew how to respond to the internet.

The **lower-right quadrant** — low agreement on what people want, high agreement on cause and effect — calls for management tools. Training, standard operating procedures, measurement systems, process discipline, transactional compensation. When people have different reasons for being in the organization but agree on the physics of what produces results, you can coordinate them through structured work even without shared values. This is the mode that most functional operating organizations live in during their growth phase.

The **upper-right quadrant** — high agreement on both dimensions — is the territory of strong-culture organizations. Apple in 1985 was upper-right. Mature Hewlett-Packard was upper-right. And here is the paradox: strong-culture organizations are the ones most resistant to *changing* direction, because cooperation flows automatically for the existing direction. The tools that work in the upper-right — rituals, folklore, narrative — only reinforce the existing culture. They do not produce change. A leader who wants to change direction in an upper-right organization is in the hardest position of all: the very strength of the organization is what prevents the pivot.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose both dimensions honestly, using structured probes.** Don't rely on surface behavior. Ask specific questions: if we froze compensation and stopped the scorecard, would people here still agree on what the company should be trying to achieve? What do people privately think is the cause of our current results? Where would we expect disagreement if we asked honestly? The answers place the organization in a quadrant. Different functions of the same company will often be in different quadrants — sales and design may be upper-left while manufacturing is lower-right — which means the change tools have to differ across functions.

2. **Select tools from the quadrant the people are actually in.** Not where you'd prefer them to be. The most common failure of change leadership is insisting on using upper-left tools (vision, charisma) in a lower-left organization (merger, crisis), or using upper-right tools (culture, ritual) in a quadrant where the culture doesn't yet exist. The tools in the quadrant the people are in are often narrower than the leader would like. That narrowness is the truth of the situation.

3. **Negotiate the authority you need before taking the job.** If the organization is lower-left and you are being asked to turn it around, you need the authority to use power tools — unilateral decision-making, the credibility to fire people, control over the budget. If the board is offering the job without that authority, the offer is a trap. Gordon Bethune turned down the Continental CEO job twice before accepting it with full authority, because he knew that the cost-cutting path the board favored would not work and that any CEO without unilateral authority in that situation was certain to fail.

4. **Recognize that success shifts the organization, which changes the tool requirement.** A crisis-era CEO who uses power tools and wins will, if successful, move the organization from lower-left toward upper-right — people are now aligned on what they want and on what works, because success has made the winning strategy legible. At that point, the tools must shift. Continuing to use power tools in an upper-right organization will produce demoralization and attrition. Bethune, after stabilizing Continental, moved to financial incentives (a lower-right management tool) and later to culture-reinforcing activities (upper-right). Jack Welch did the same transition at GE, moving from the Neutron Jack era to Crotonville teaching.

5. **Don't try to change a strong-culture organization without either a crisis or a separate structure.** The upper-right quadrant is the hardest to move. The tools available there — rituals, folklore, leadership rhetoric — reinforce existing direction; they don't redirect. The two ways to move an upper-right organization are to create a genuine crisis (which drops the organization toward the lower-left and re-enables power tools) or to set up a separate organization under different cultural conditions (an RPV-style spinout). Frontal attempts at cultural transformation in a healthy upper-right organization almost always fail and frequently cost the leader her job.

## Examples

**Situation:** Gordon Bethune became CEO of Continental Airlines in 1994. Continental was in open crisis — the tenth CEO in ten years, worst industry rankings for lost baggage, on-time performance, customer complaints, and overbooking; two bankruptcies in the previous decade; and losing $55 million a month despite years of cost cutting. Bethune had been Continental's COO and had turned down the top job twice when offered on limited terms — once to be "acting CEO" during a leave, once to join a "office of the CEO." He believed that further cost cutting — the direction the board wanted — would kill the company, and he refused to take the job without the unilateral authority to go the other way.

**Application:** The organization was squarely in the lower-left quadrant. Employees, customers, the board, and management disagreed sharply on what Continental should do and on how the world worked. This is the territory of power tools. Bethune demanded the full CEO title with real authority before accepting, because he knew the quadrant he was inheriting and knew that management-tool and leadership-tool approaches would fail in it. Once in the job, he used raw power: when operations staff refused to repaint the airline's 200+ airplanes, he threatened to shoot them unless they complied. When customer-service employees were hiding behind a thick policy manual, he set a stack of manuals on fire in the parking lot. These are not management practices that would survive an HR review, and that is exactly the point — they are the tools of the lower-left quadrant applied by a CEO who had the authority to wield them.

**Result:** Continental stabilized. Initial wins moved the organization out of lower-left toward upper-right. Bethune then transitioned tools: he introduced a $65-per-month bonus for top-five on-time performance (a lower-right management tool, which would have been ignored in the lower-left environment he inherited), and later moved to culture-reinforcing activities (upper-right tools) to consolidate what had become a productive culture. Eleven consecutive quarters of improved profits followed; Continental won two consecutive J.D. Power awards.

**Situation:** Jack Welch became CEO of General Electric in 1981. GE was a sprawling conglomerate of businesses with vastly different cultures, cost structures, operating procedures, and growth expectations. The various business units did not agree on what GE should be or on what metrics mattered most. Welch inherited, across the company as a whole, a lower-left organization — not because any one division was in crisis but because there was no coherent shared view across divisions of what GE was trying to become.

**Application:** Welch started as "Neutron Jack" — power tools, wielded hard. The organizing principle was "be first or second in every business you're in, or exit" — a unilateral mandate that closed businesses, divested divisions, and fired managers who couldn't make the cut. This is a lower-left playbook: force cooperation through fiat when consensus doesn't exist. Over a decade, as the businesses that survived adopted a shared view of the operating model, GE drifted from the lower-left toward the upper-right. Welch then shifted tools — moving to Six Sigma (a lower-right management tool) and later to Crotonville-based leadership development and cultural work (upper-right tools).

**Result:** GE became the most admired American company of the 1990s under Welch. The success, however, created a problem for his successor. Jeffrey Immelt inherited an upper-right organization — aligned on what GE was and on how it worked — at a moment when the external environment was starting to shift in ways that demanded a different GE. Upper-right tools don't drive direction change. Immelt's task was structurally harder than Welch's.

**Situation:** Lou Gerstner became CEO of IBM in 1993, when the company was losing billions of dollars and widely considered past saving. Gerstner's famous line was "the last thing IBM needs right now is a vision." It became a leadership cliché. The matrix explains why it was correct.

**Application:** IBM in 1993 was in crisis — in the lower-left quadrant, even though IBM had historically been an upper-right strong-culture organization. The crisis had destroyed the consensus that had previously existed. A vision statement — an upper-left tool — would have been indifferently received; what IBM needed was power tools (which the crisis authorized) to force the cooperation required for a pivot from mainframes to services. Gerstner used them: mass layoffs, cost reduction, portfolio restructuring, unilateral direction from the top. As the services business took hold and IBM's new identity consolidated, the organization moved back toward the upper-right.

**Result:** Gerstner saved IBM. His successor, Sam Palmisano, inherited a far harder problem: an upper-right strong-culture organization at a moment when further changes were coming. There was no crisis to authorize power tools. The culture was coherent and productive, which meant the tools available were culture-reinforcing rather than direction-changing. Palmisano's task was, as Christensen pointed out, tougher than Gerstner's — not because Palmisano was less capable but because the quadrant he inherited constrained which tools could work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to sell vision statements into the lower-left quadrant.

**Why:** Vision statements — and the whole family of charismatic leadership tools — only work when people already agree on what they want. They articulate a shared hope and invite cooperation on the methodology for achieving it. In a lower-left organization, people don't share that hope. They disagree, sometimes bitterly, about the right end state. When a leader stands up and reads a vision statement into that environment, what she produces is not inspiration; it is a collective rolling of eyes, usually followed by a long cynical aftershock that makes the next change program harder to run. The same action viewed as visionary in an upper-left organization is regarded with indifference or contempt in a lower-left one — not because the words are different but because the quadrant is different. The same applies to most of the pageantry of inspirational leadership in low-consensus situations: the Q&A with employees, the "all-hands" call to action, the mission poster on the wall. In a lower-left organization, these are not merely ineffective; they burn the leader's credibility and make the subsequent application of power tools — which is what actually would have worked — harder to execute. The diagnostic question is always the same: what do people here actually want, and do they agree? If the answer is "they disagree, visibly and materially," reach for power tools or stop the program.
