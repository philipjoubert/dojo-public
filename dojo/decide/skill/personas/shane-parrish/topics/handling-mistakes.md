# Handling Mistakes

## When to Use
- A decision has just produced a bad outcome and the user is deciding how to handle it.
- The user is feeling the pull to cover, deflect, minimize, or wait for the issue to blow over.
- A team is in a postmortem and the conversation is drifting toward blame or defensiveness.
- The user is being evaluated on an outcome that may have come from a good process and bad luck.
- The user is evaluating someone else's decision and is at risk of judging by result rather than by reasoning.
- A repeated bad outcome is signaling that something more than luck is at play.

## Fails When
- **The situation requires immediate damage control.** A burning building doesn't need a four-step process — it needs an evacuation. Apply the framework after the fire is out, not during.
- **The user isn't actually the one responsible.** Self-accountability does not mean accepting blame for things you didn't cause. The framework presumes your contribution was real and addressable. If it wasn't, the framework misapplies.
- **The user uses the framework as performative humility.** "I take full responsibility" with no actual change in behavior is worse than no apology. The framework is operational — if no behavior changes, none of the steps actually happened.

## Core Concept

Mistakes are inevitable. What separates good operators from bad ones is not whether they make mistakes — it's what they do in the next forty-eight hours after the mistake becomes visible. Shane's blunt diagnosis: "The biggest mistake people make typically isn't their initial mistake. It's the mistake of trying to cover up and avoid responsibility for it. The first mistake is expensive; the second one costs a fortune."

The first mistake is usually a localized loss. The second mistake — the cover-up, the deflection, the doubling-down — extends the loss across time and across relationships. A botched product launch is a quarter. A botched response to a botched product launch is a multi-year credibility problem. A misjudged hire is six months of sub-par output. A misjudged hire that the leader refuses to admit is a misjudgment is two years of compounding damage to the team and to the leader's standing.

The framework is a four-step process. It is a process, not a principle, because the steps must actually be executed. Saying "I take responsibility" is not step one. Step one is the operational acceptance that this is now your problem to handle, regardless of whose fault it was.

The framework sits on top of a deeper principle: **separate decision quality from outcome quality**. A good decision can produce a bad outcome — you were unlucky. A bad decision can produce a good outcome — you were lucky. Judging the decision by the outcome teaches the wrong lessons systematically. The four steps are designed to extract the right lesson from the actual cause, not from the surface result.

### The four steps

**1. Accept responsibility.**
Even if it's not wholly your fault, it's still your problem to handle. Self-accountability is the lever that opens the rest of the process. Without it, you're stuck arguing about whose fault it was while the damage compounds.

The reframe: "It's not my fault" and "It's not my responsibility" are different sentences. Often only the first is true. The poker mindset — you don't control the cards, you control how you play them. Acceptance means you stop bargaining with reality and start working with it.

**2. Learn from it.**
Diagnose the contribution. What thoughts, feelings, and actions got you here? Which default was running? Which stage of the decision process was skipped or rushed? Was the problem defined wrong? Was the premortem skipped? Was a margin of safety missing?

The check: if you find yourself saying "this isn't fair," go back to step one. The bargaining language is the tell that you haven't actually accepted responsibility yet. You're still relitigating the cards instead of analyzing the play.

The deeper diagnostic, which Shane returns to often: "If you got results you didn't want, the world is telling you at least one of two things: (a) you were unlucky, or (b) your ideas about how things work were wrong." If a repeated approach would have produced different outcomes most of the time, it was luck. If the approach is structurally flawed, the model needs updating. Most people conflate these — Shane's point is to separate them deliberately.

**3. Commit to doing better.**
Specifically. Concretely. This is where most apology rituals fail. "I'll be more careful next time" is not a commitment — it's a hope. The commitment has to be operational: a strength to build, a safeguard to install, a rule to follow. Concrete enough that someone could check whether you did it.

If the diagnosis was "I made the call while exhausted," the commitment is a HALT rule on this class of decision. If the diagnosis was "I didn't run a premortem," the commitment is that no major launch ships without one. If the diagnosis was "I let one star player skip the standard," the commitment is the Belichick rule applied next time.

**4. Repair the damage.**
Not just an apology. Sustained consistency going forward. An apology buys a window of credibility. The sustained behavior either confirms or destroys it. Deviation reverses repair — one slip can erase weeks of consistent improvement, because the slip is read as "the apology was performative all along."

Shane's framing is operational, not therapeutic. The damage is repaired by behavior, not by emotion. The people you've hurt or let down need to see the change in action, not in language.

### The deeper principle: decision quality versus outcome quality

Outcomes contain luck. A decision that was sound given the information available at the time can produce a bad outcome through unforeseeable factors. A decision that was reckless can produce a good outcome through dumb luck. Judging by the outcome reinforces lucky processes and discards unlucky ones — which is exactly backwards.

The discipline: evaluate the *process*. Was the problem defined correctly given what was knowable? Were multiple options explored? Was the premortem run? Was second-order thinking applied? Was a margin of safety built in? If yes to all and the outcome was bad, the decision was good and you were unlucky — repeat the process. If no to some and the outcome was good, the decision was bad and you were lucky — fix the process before the luck runs out.

The single best tool here is a decision log. Capture the reasoning at the time of decision, not retrospectively. Hindsight contaminates everything. The log lets you revisit decisions without ego distortion and update the actual process. Without a log, you'll attribute success to skill and failure to circumstance — the self-serving bias that systematically rots learning.

A repeated bad outcome over a meaningful sample is different. Randomness doesn't sustain systematic failure indefinitely. When results are persistently divergent from expectation, the model is wrong and the process needs updating. The judgment call is when "repeated" becomes meaningful — too soon and you're updating on noise, too late and you're persisting in a broken approach.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop the second mistake before it starts.** The instinct to cover, deflect, or minimize is the first move you have to interrupt. Notice the urge. The cost of the cover-up is almost always larger than the cost of the original mistake. Name it before you act on it.

2. **Make the operational acceptance, not the performative one.** "I take responsibility" with no behavioral change is the cover-up wearing humility's costume. Acceptance is the move that says: this is now my problem to handle, regardless of who caused it. It opens the next three steps.

3. **Run the contribution diagnosis honestly.** Which default was running? Which stage of the decision process was skipped? What did you know, and when? Write it down. The writing forces precision the conversation will resist.

4. **Distinguish decision quality from outcome quality before drawing conclusions.** Was the process sound given what was knowable? If yes and the result was bad, you were unlucky and the process repeats. If the process was sloppy and the result was good, fix the process before the luck runs out. Outcome-based learning teaches the wrong lessons.

5. **Make the commitment specific enough to be checked.** Not "I'll be more careful." A rule, a safeguard, a checklist item, a structural change. If a third party couldn't verify whether you did it, the commitment isn't operational.

6. **Repair through sustained behavior, not through language.** The apology is the announcement. The behavior is the repair. Plan the behavior in advance. One slip in the recovery period erases weeks of consistent improvement, because the slip recodes the apology as performance.

7. **Log the decision and the reasoning at the time, not in hindsight.** Without the log, hindsight will rewrite the past in your favor. The decision log is the single most reliable defense against outcome bias and self-serving attribution.

8. **For repeated bad outcomes, update the model.** One bad outcome is noise. Three of the same kind is signal. When the same approach keeps producing the same loss, the world is telling you that your model is wrong, not that you've been unlucky.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder shipped a major product change that broke a critical workflow for their largest customer. The customer is publicly upset. The board is asking what happened.
**Application:** Step 1 — accept it operationally. Don't deflect to engineering, QA, or the customer's own configuration. The release was approved by leadership; leadership owns the consequence. Step 2 — diagnose. The problem is that the change was approved without a premortem and without a regression test on the largest customer's workflow. Step 3 — commit specifically: no future change to the affected subsystem ships without a regression test on the top three customers' workflows. Step 4 — repair: a personal call to the customer, the rule above made visible, and consistent execution of the rule for the next six months. The apology buys the window; the sustained execution closes the credibility gap.
**Result:** The customer stays. The board sees the response and updates upward, not downward, on the founder's judgment — because the response demonstrated the strength the original failure obscured.

**Situation:** A leader hired a senior executive who turned out to be a bad fit. After eight months, the leader is still defending the hire because admitting the mistake feels like admitting their judgment was wrong.
**Application:** The cover-up is now larger than the original mistake. Step 1 — accept. The hire was wrong. Step 2 — diagnose: ego default, second face — the wrong side of right. The leader confused "my hire being right" with "the right hire." Step 3 — commit: a rule that any senior hire is reviewed by two outside operators in the first quarter, with the option to part ways at six months by mutual agreement. Step 4 — repair: a clean transition for the executive, an honest internal note about what was learned, no rewriting of history.
**Result:** The team observes the move. The standard for honesty about mistakes is set by the leader. The next bad hire (and there will be one) is caught at four months instead of eighteen.

**Situation:** A team is in a postmortem on a market entry that failed badly. Everyone is either blaming or defending. Nothing useful is happening.
**Application:** Reframe. Separate decision quality from outcome quality. Was the problem defined correctly given what was knowable? Were multiple options explored? Was a premortem run? Was a margin of safety built in? Walk through each stage. If the process was sound and the outcome was bad through genuinely unforeseeable factors, the decision was good and the lesson is "repeat the process." If a specific stage was skipped or sloppy, the lesson is to reinforce that stage. The blame-and-defend cycle dies as soon as the conversation moves from outcome to process.
**Result:** The team learns the actual lesson. If the process was good, they don't change a working process out of outcome panic. If a specific stage was the failure point, they fix it specifically.

**Situation:** A salesperson lost three of their last five large deals. Their manager is deciding whether this is a slump or a structural issue.
**Application:** The repeated-pattern test. One loss is noise. Three of five large deals is signal — but signal of what? Run the diagnosis. Were the deals comparable? Was there a common failure mode (timing, pricing, technical fit, decision-maker pattern)? If no common pattern, it's likely variance and the right move is to repeat the process. If there is a common pattern, the model needs updating — the salesperson, the territory, the product fit, or the qualification criteria. Don't fire and don't ignore; diagnose.
**Result:** Either the structural issue gets identified and addressed, or the variance gets respected and the process continues. The manager avoids both the "fire on outcome" mistake and the "ignore the signal" mistake.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to apologize without changing behavior.
**Why:** A performative apology is worse than no apology. It encodes the message that the response is theater. Sustained behavior change is the only repair that holds.

**Don't:** Cover the first mistake.
**Why:** The first mistake is localized. The cover-up extends it across time and relationships and turns a single loss into a credibility problem. Shane's line: the first mistake is expensive, the second one costs a fortune.

**Don't:** Judge the decision by the outcome.
**Why:** Outcomes contain luck. A good process can produce a bad result. A bad process can produce a good result. Judging by outcomes systematically reinforces lucky processes and discards unlucky ones — which is the opposite of learning.

**Don't:** Update the model on a single outcome.
**Why:** One bad result is noise. Three of the same kind is signal. Updating on every outcome produces a chaotic model that drifts toward the most recent loss. The judgment is in deciding when "repeated" becomes meaningful.

**Don't:** Use the language of self-blame as a substitute for self-accountability.
**Why:** Self-blame is backward-facing and produces no decision. Self-accountability is forward-facing — it asks what the next move is. The framework operates on the next move, not on the rumination.

**Don't:** Skip the contribution diagnosis because it's uncomfortable.
**Why:** Without the diagnosis, the commitment will be generic ("be more careful") and the behavior change won't happen. The discomfort of the honest diagnosis is the cost of the actual fix.

**Don't:** Treat repair as a one-time conversation.
**Why:** Repair is sustained behavior over a recovery window. One slip during the window reverses it. Plan the behavior in advance. The window closes when the new behavior has compounded enough credibility to be trusted.

**Don't:** Apologize for things that aren't actually your fault and weren't your responsibility.
**Why:** Over-acceptance is its own distortion. It teaches your team that you'll absorb consequences indiscriminately, which weakens the actual accountability standard. The framework presumes a real contribution. If the contribution wasn't yours, the right move is a clean acknowledgment of what happened, not an absorption of blame that wasn't earned.
