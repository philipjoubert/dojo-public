# Focus — Concentrate, Don't Spread

## When to Use
- Someone hands you a deck with eight to fifteen "strategic priorities" and wants you to help execute against them.
- A leader is dividing resources across many initiatives, with no single one funded to actually clear a threshold.
- A once-focused company has been talked into pursuing growth across many adjacent businesses and is now bleeding margin everywhere.
- You're trying to articulate why a specialist competitor with smaller scale and higher unit costs nevertheless out-earns the giants in its industry.

## Fails When
- "Focus" is treated as a slogan rather than a coordinated set of policies aimed at a common target. A company can announce focus in a press release and still have ten unrelated initiatives in flight; the focus has to show up in resource allocation, in product mix, in customer choice, in how the manufacturing system is set up. If it's just rhetoric, the diagnosis isn't "we need to focus harder," it's "we don't have a strategy."
- The user is running multiple genuinely separate businesses with different customers and value chains. Focus applies *per business*, not as a blanket constraint on the portfolio. Crown Cork & Seal under Connelly was focused; that doesn't mean a true conglomerate has to collapse to one product.
- Used to defend doing only what's easy. "Focus" is sometimes deployed to refuse hard work in adjacent markets where the firm should be competing. The discipline is concentrating on what matters and what is winnable, not just what is comfortable.

## Core Concept

At the core, strategy is about focus, and most complex organizations don't focus their resources. They pursue multiple goals at once, not concentrating enough resources to achieve a breakthrough in any of them. A long list of priorities is the visible signature of that failure. The word "priority" means *first*. A list of twelve priorities is a confession of unwillingness to choose. An air traffic controller giving "priority" to a list of airplanes is a person about to crash them.

Focus has two meanings. The first is **the coordination of policies that produces extra power through their interacting and overlapping effects**. The second is **the application of that power to the right target**. Both are required. Coordinated policies aimed at a wrong or fuzzy target produce nothing useful; a sharp target with uncoordinated policies aimed at it produces the same. The strategy is in the joint of the two.

The canonical case is Crown Cork & Seal under John Connelly, who took it over in 1956 and ran it until 1989. Crown was a metal-can maker in an industry famous for low margins and brutal price competition. The big three — Continental Can, National Can, American Can — accepted being captive producers, building plants dedicated to single customers (Miller Brewing with two competing can suppliers across the street), running long production runs, earning 4–5% return on assets. Crown beat them on margin by 50–60%. Surface explanations had Crown "specializing in hard-to-hold products like aerosols and carbonated soft drinks" — true but not useful. Crown's actual focus was *shorter runs*. Smaller customers, rush orders, new products, seasonal demand, low-volume high-value runs. Technical assistance for customers who didn't have their own canning expertise. Rapid response. Smaller plants, none captive, each with multiple customers, excess capacity waiting for orders. Higher unit costs (more changeovers, more service, idle capacity) — and *higher* prices to match.

The non-obvious result is what made Crown's focus so durable: by concentrating on short runs, Crown avoided the captive squeeze. Where the major can companies had each plant tied to one big customer who could play them against each other, Crown had each plant serving many customers per plant. Crown was the supplier with several customers; the majors were captives with several competing suppliers. Crown hadn't given up its bargaining power the way the captives had. Same industry, different rules. Crown ran on this design from the early 1960s to 1989 and returned 19% per year to shareholders over thirty-five years.

What does focus do? **It concentrates strength against weakness.** Strength against strength is attrition. Strength against weakness — in this case, Crown's coordinated specialization against majors' bargaining-power weakness — is a strategy. Focus also has a psychological dimension: people ignore signals below a certain threshold; people believe in momentum and that success leads to success. Concentrating effort on targets that catch attention and sway opinion can have more impact than spreading the same effort thinly. It may have more impact on public opinion to completely turn around two schools than to make a 2% improvement in two hundred schools.

The downstream test of focus is unforgiving. When Connelly retired and his successor Avery launched a growth-by-acquisition strategy, the CFO defended his understanding of focus by saying: "It's currently fashionable to focus, but we've always been there. We operate in a $300 billion industry and serve only the metal and plastic portion, which amounts to $150–200 billion." That isn't focus — that's a restriction on the product line. Focus is a *concentration and coordination of action and resources that creates an advantage*. Crown lost that and stopped earning 19% returns. Stock fell from $55 to $5 between 1998 and 2001.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse the long list.** When handed twelve priorities, push back immediately. Two or three is the upper limit on what an organization can actually concentrate on. The rest go in next year's list, or they don't go at all.
2. **Identify the target, not just the activity.** What customer, what segment, what need is being concentrated on? Crown wasn't focused on "metal cans" — that's a product line. It was focused on *short runs*, which is a position in the industry's economics. Focus on a segment defined by what makes you valuable and your competitors uncomfortable, not by what's easy to label.
3. **Coordinate policies around the target.** Each policy — manufacturing, sales, distribution, pricing, customer service, hiring — should reinforce the others in the service of the chosen target. If a policy makes sense in isolation but doesn't serve the target, it's diluting focus, not adding to it.
4. **Accept the costs of focus.** A focused strategy is narrower than a generalist strategy. You give up market share you could have served. You accept higher unit costs in some places (Crown's short runs, technical assistance, idle capacity) and recover them through higher prices. You refuse profitable-looking adjacent businesses because they break the design. Focus is not free.
5. **Test for coherence by walking the policies.** Look at every distinctive policy and ask what target it points toward. If the targets are scattered, the company isn't focused — it has a collection of well-intentioned activities. If the targets converge, you can name the focus, even if the company itself hasn't.

## Examples

**Situation:** Crown Cork & Seal, 1956–1989, in the U.S. metal-container industry. Industry returns averaged 4–5% on assets. The big three operated dedicated captive plants on long runs for major customers. Crown earned 50–60% more profit per dollar of assets than the majors over decades.
**Application:** Walking the policies, what looks like "specialization in hard-to-hold containers" is actually focus on *shorter runs*. Technical assistance targets smaller customers who lack internal canning expertise. Rapid response targets rush orders. Smaller, multi-customer, non-captive plants with excess capacity target the same. Each policy points to short runs; short runs put Crown in a structurally different position relative to its customers (multiple customers per plant rather than one captive customer per plant), which preserves bargaining power. Costs per can are higher, prices per can are higher, margin per can is higher — and it persists because the design is internally coherent.
**Result:** 19% per year return to shareholders for 35 years in a "terrible industry." When Avery abandoned the focus for growth-by-acquisition (CarnaudMetalBox, plastic containers, Constar, Van Dorn, Tri-Valley) and the CFO defended this by misreading focus as "operating only in a $200 billion subset of the industry," return on capital fell from 15% to below 5%, stock fell from $55 to $5 between 1998 and 2001, and the firm spent the next half-decade just paying off debt. Same firm, same industry — focus was the difference.

**Situation:** A small business school, 2005, asked Rumelt to help with its strategic plan. Overall ambition was to break out from being a local school to one ranked with the best in the region. Draft strategic plan was a list of initiatives — increased research visibility, more alumni giving, a global business studies program, an enhanced entrepreneurial studies program, a sustainability initiative.
**Application:** Asked the dean and faculty council to imagine they were allowed *only one* feasible objective. What single objective, when accomplished, would make the biggest difference? After a morning's deliberation they had two: get students into better jobs, or focus on PR coverage. By end of day, the group combined them. Primary objective: get students into better jobs. Method: select ten target firms that should be hiring their graduates but presently weren't; faculty committees to study these firms' recruiting practices and design programs to meet their needs and standards. Secondary: rather than global studies and sustainability, a new course of studies on media management — to attract media people to visit and to get students into media jobs that would naturally raise the school's profile. Two of the ten target firms would be media companies. The list of "strategic priorities" went from five fuzzy ambitions to two coordinated objectives that reinforced each other.
**Result:** Both objectives represented strong positions within a field of action and were each pregnant with options for future strategizing. The point was not that they were the *only* possible focus — it was that the long list was not a strategy at all, and forcing the school to identify *one feasible pivotal objective* was the strategic act. Once the focus was named, the actions fell out.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** call the long list a "strategy."
**Why:** Twelve priorities is twelve priorities. It is not a strategy. It is the accumulated result of a board, a leadership team, and various stakeholders each refusing to be left out of the document. Calling it a strategy doesn't make it one — it just confuses the organization, which then tries to do all twelve in parallel and accomplishes none. The right move is to push back and force the choice. If the leader cannot or will not choose, that is itself the diagnosis.

**Don't:** confuse focus with restriction of product line.
**Why:** Crown's CFO under Avery said "we focus by serving only the metal and plastic portion of the packaging industry." That's not focus; that's a description of the markets they sold into. Focus is the coordination of policies and resources around a target that creates an advantage. A firm can sell into a "narrow" market and have no focus at all if its policies are scattered; a firm can sell into a broad market and be deeply focused if the policies all point at one target.

**Don't:** lose focus in the name of "growth."
**Why:** Healthy growth is not engineered; it is the outcome of growing demand for special capabilities. When growth becomes the objective and focus is treated as an obstacle to growth, the strategy collapses. Crown under Avery doubled in size, became "the world's largest container manufacturer," and destroyed shareholder value. The growth was real and the value was negative. If your growth narrative requires abandoning the focus that produced your historical advantage, the right answer is usually not to grow that way.

**Don't:** assume more focus is always better.
**Why:** Focus is not asceticism. The point is matching the level of concentration to what the situation requires. A firm with a strong resource position and a low competitive challenge can afford broader scope and looser integration. The discipline is that the focus should be deliberate and coordinated — not that it should always be maximally narrow. The question is "is the strategy concentrated enough that the policies reinforce each other and clear thresholds," not "have we cut everything we could possibly cut."
