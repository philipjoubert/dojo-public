# The Kernel of Good Strategy

## When to Use
- Someone hands you a "strategy" — a deck, a plan, a binder — and asks whether it is any good.
- You are creating a strategy and need to know the minimum logical structure it has to have before you can call it one.
- You suspect a "strategy" is actually a vision, a financial target, or a list of priorities wearing a strategy costume.

## Fails When
- The challenge is well-structured and the alternatives are already clear — you have a choice problem, not a strategy problem. The kernel is the tool for ill-structured situations where nobody is sure what the problem even is.
- You try to use it as a template to fill in. The kernel is a *test* and a logic, not a form. Three boxes labeled DIAGNOSIS / GUIDING POLICY / ACTIONS, filled in with the same fluff that was there before, is still bad strategy.

## Core Concept

A good strategy has a basic underlying structure I call the *kernel*. The kernel of a strategy contains three elements: a **diagnosis** that defines or explains the nature of the challenge; a **guiding policy** for dealing with the challenge — an overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis; and a set of **coherent actions** — feasible, coordinated policies, resource commitments, and steps designed to carry out the guiding policy. That's it. Diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent action. Three things. If any of the three is missing or misshapen, you don't have a strategy yet, no matter how thick the binder is.

I call this the kernel — the bare-bones center, the hard nut at the core of the concept — to emphasize that everything else most people pile into "strategy" is supporting players. Vision statements, mission statements, hierarchies of goals and objectives, references to time span or scope, balanced scorecards, OKRs, lists of values, "strategic priorities," ten-year financial extrapolations — none of those are the strategy. They are not even the precursors of strategy. The core content is a diagnosis of the situation at hand, the creation or identification of a guiding policy for dealing with the critical difficulties, and a set of coherent actions. The kernel does not require you to sort through legalistic gibberish about the differences between visions, missions, goals, strategies, objectives, and tactics. It does not split strategy into corporate, business, and product levels. It is very straightforward.

The kernel works as both a constructor and a test. As a constructor: when you start with the diagnosis ("what is going on here?"), the guiding policy follows from it, and the actions follow from the policy — each layer earns its place by answering the layer above. As a test: take any document called a strategy, and ask the three questions. Is there a diagnosis — a clear-eyed naming of the challenge? Is there a guiding policy that follows from that diagnosis and rules out a great many possible actions? Are there concrete actions that carry out the policy and reinforce one another? Most "strategies" fail this test. They name a result and call it the problem. They list ambitions and call them a guiding policy. They produce a *dog's-dinner* of unrelated initiatives and call it execution. The kernel exposes all of that.

## How to Apply

1. **Start with diagnosis, not goals.** Ask: what is actually going on here? What is the challenge? Replace the overwhelming complexity with a simpler story that calls attention to the crucial aspects. Name the challenge in a sentence. If you can't, you're not ready to recommend anything.
2. **Derive a guiding policy from the diagnosis.** A guiding policy is an overall approach for coping with the obstacles that the diagnosis named. It is "guiding" because it channels action without specifying every move, like the guardrails on a highway. It is *not* a goal, an aspiration, or an end-state. It says how, not what we wish were true.
3. **Translate the policy into coherent actions.** Strategy must contain action. Resource deployments, policies, and maneuvers should be consistent and coordinated — they should reinforce each other, not cancel each other out. If the actions read like a list of stuff that requires top-management approval, you have operational management dressed up as strategy.
4. **Run the kernel as a self-test on whatever has been put in front of you.** Whether it is a board deck, a McKinsey study, your own draft, or a competitor's announcement — check the three. Where any one is missing, that is where the work needs to happen.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple, September 1997. Two months from bankruptcy. Steve Jobs returns as interim CEO. The general business world expected an ambitious vision speech and a "leap to the future."
**Application:** What Jobs did instead was the kernel, executed cleanly. **Diagnosis:** Apple was a niche producer in a market dominated by the Wintel standard; its product line was complicated, it was bleeding cash, and there was no Apple consumer computer priced under $2,000. **Guiding policy:** shrink Apple to a scale and scope suitable to the reality of being a niche producer; cut back to a core that could survive; then wait for the next big thing. **Coherent actions:** cut fifteen desktop models to one; cut all portable and handheld models to one laptop; cut printers and peripherals; cut development engineers; cut software development; cut five of six national retailers; move manufacturing offshore to Taiwan; cut inventory by more than 80 percent; sell direct via a new web store; talk Microsoft into a $150 million investment.
**Result:** Apple survived. The actions reinforced one another — a simpler product line manufactured in Asia made the inventory cuts feasible, which made the financial recovery feasible. The famous innovations (iPod, iPhone) came later, but they came on top of a kernel that was already complete. He did not announce ambitious revenue or profit goals; he did not indulge in messianic visions of the future; and he did not just cut in a blind ax-wielding frenzy. He redesigned the whole business logic around a simplified product line sold through a limited set of outlets. That is the kernel.

**Situation:** A consumer goods company executive in London is frustrated that the "Pan-European" initiative — meant to reduce fragmentation across country-based marketing organizations — has produced nothing in years, despite quarterly Pan-Europe Executive Committee meetings, rotated developers, a New Products group, and Pan-European contribution being part of every executive's evaluation.
**Application:** The kernel test exposes the gap. **Diagnosis missing:** the executive had named a *result* he wanted (Pan-European products) but not the *challenge* — the country managers' jobs were running country-based systems and they had no real interest in or trust of Pan-European products. **Guiding policy missing:** the various initiatives were a hammer-as-shoe; he needed a different tool entirely — a single unit with full profit responsibility for Pan-European products, plus interventions in the country-based system. **Coherent action missing:** committee meetings and evaluation criteria are not actions on the system; they are decoration. When the kernel was applied honestly, the required actions were not mysterious — they were just painful. The impediment was the hope that the pain could somehow be avoided.
**Result:** It took him another nine months to decide that the Pan-European initiative was indeed important enough to reorganize European operations. There was no magical solution. The kernel forced the recognition that wanting strong country-based marketing, Pan-European initiatives, and no noses out of joint, all at once, was a null set.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** treat the kernel as a three-box template to fill in.
**Why:** A diagnosis box that says "the market is changing rapidly," a guiding-policy box that says "we will be the leading provider of customer-centric solutions," and an action box that lists twelve unrelated initiatives is *exactly* the bad-strategy pattern the kernel is designed to expose. The three pieces have to actually reason from one to the next. The diagnosis must constrain the policy; the policy must constrain the actions. If you can rearrange the three across companies and they still "fit," none of them are doing any work.

**Don't:** stop at the guiding policy and call it a strategy.
**Why:** Many people call the guiding policy "the strategy" and stop there. This is a mistake. Strategy is about action, about doing something. A guiding policy without coherent action is a slogan — it has no bite. Until you've worked through to at least the first round of action, you cannot be sure the guiding policy can even be implemented.

**Don't:** confuse the kernel with the public face of the strategy.
**Why:** What goes on the website, in the all-hands deck, on the office wall — the vision, the mission, the values, the inspirational language — is the *Standard Narrative*. The kernel is the working internal logic that actually decides what to do and what not to do. Standard Narrative is for employees and the board. The Action Agenda runs the company. Don't merge them; don't let one masquerade as the other.
