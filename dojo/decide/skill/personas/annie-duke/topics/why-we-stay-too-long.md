# Why We Stay Too Long — Five Cognitive Forces

## When to Use

- You can't figure out why you're still doing the thing you've privately admitted isn't working.
- You catch yourself making arguments to stay that don't survive saying out loud ("if I leave now, the last three years were wasted").
- You're advising someone whose reasoning has clearly slipped its moorings, and you want to name what's gluing them in place.
- You're designing an organization or a process and want to pre-empt the predictable failures of escalation.
- You're trying to understand a famous corporate failure (Sears, Blockbuster, Kodak, the California bullet train) — every one of these is a five-mechanism cocktail.

## Fails When

- **The user hasn't started yet.** All five mechanisms require prior commitment. If they're still deciding whether to start, the relevant tools are kill criteria and monkeys-and-pedestals, not over-persistence diagnosis.
- **You use it as a moral indictment.** Naming someone's status quo bias to discredit their judgment is the wrong move. These forces apply to everyone, all the time. The point of naming them is to *help* the person see what's actually happening, not to win an argument.
- **The decision really is hard on the merits.** Sometimes things are close calls because the world is genuinely ambiguous, and the cognitive bias frame can become a lazy way of dismissing legitimate uncertainty. Use the lens; don't replace the analysis with it.

## Core Concept

When the world tells us we should quit and we don't — when we double down instead, when we sink another year and another million into the losing effort, when we walk past the off-ramp for the third time — there's no single cause. Five forces work together to gaff the scale. Each one has been studied for forty-plus years, each has a name, and each shows up in the wreckage of every famous over-persistence story. The katamari metaphor is the right one: each force adds debris, and the more debris you accumulate, the harder it gets to walk away, which is how you accumulate even more. The good news is that once you know the names, you can sometimes interrupt the rolling.

What follows is the field guide. Five mechanisms, in roughly the order they kick in.

### 1. Escalating commitment (Barry Staw, 1976)

When we're in the losses, we don't just stick — we *double down*. Barry Staw's 1976 paper "Knee-Deep in the Big Muddy" — title borrowed from Pete Seeger's anti-Vietnam-War song — is the foundational study. Staw gave business students a corporate-allocation case in two parts. Part one: pick a division to invest in. Part two: get back results saying you picked badly, then allocate more money. The students who'd made the first decision *increased* their commitment to the failing division far more than fresh participants who hadn't made the original call. Exposure to bad news, combined with personal responsibility for the original decision, didn't trigger reversal — it triggered escalation.

The mechanism is justification. If I quit now, I have to admit the original decision was wrong. If I double down and it works, the original decision was vindicated. So I keep going, not because the bet is good but because quitting requires me to close a mental account in the losses, and closing accounts in the losses hurts. Staw's later work showed this at every scale: individuals waiting hours past the point of rationality; LBJ committing more troops to Vietnam in 1965 *after* George Ball had warned him in writing that the war was unwinnable; the Shoreham Nuclear Power Plant going from a $75 million estimate to over $5 billion across two decades. *Escalation of commitment is not the exception. It's the rule.*

### 2. Sunk cost and the fear of waste (Richard Thaler, 1980)

Richard Thaler named the sunk-cost effect in 1980. The simple version: people irrationally consider what they've already spent when deciding whether to spend more. The effect is robust, replicates across domains, and gets *stronger* the more you've spent. A $95 concert ticket pulls you through bad weather; a $500 ticket pulls you through worse weather. The weather doesn't care what the ticket cost. Your present-and-future enjoyment doesn't care either. The only thing that cares is your unwillingness to feel like you wasted the money — and ironically, going to the concert in a thunderstorm is how you turn a wasted $95 into a wasted $95 *plus* a miserable evening.

The fingerprints are all over the California bullet train. $8.5 billion spent. Estimated cost to complete now $33-$105 billion. Original timeline shot. Central engineering problem — tunneling under the Pacheco Pass — not solved. If anyone proposed starting today, fresh, it would be laughed out of the room. But because $8.5 billion has been spent, the conversation isn't *should we do this* but *we can't waste what we've already invested*. Sunk cost is the ratchet. It only turns one direction.

The cruel finding is that *knowing about sunk cost doesn't fix it*. People who can articulate the fallacy still fall for it. The "Jedi mind trick" of asking "would I start this fresh?" is well-known, well-intentioned, and demonstrably ineffective. Awareness alone isn't a tool. You need structural defenses — kill criteria, quitting coaches, decision rules set in advance — because in-the-moment reasoning isn't strong enough to overcome the accumulated debris.

### 3. The endowment effect — you own what you've thought (Thaler, 1980)

Once you own something, you value it more than you would if you didn't own it. Thaler's economist friend bought wine at $5 a bottle in the fifties; by the eighties it was worth $100; he'd neither sell at $100 nor buy more at that price. Knetsch's coffee-mug experiments showed the same in fifteen seconds of "ownership" — students given a mug demanded twice as much to part with it as identical students would pay to acquire it.

Selling something you own is structurally a form of quitting. Not selling is persistence. The endowment effect is the thumb on that scale. And — this is the move that matters for careers and companies — *we don't only get endowed to physical objects*. We get endowed to **our beliefs, our ideas, and our decisions**. We own what we've thought. The longer we've held a strategic thesis, the more we value it. The more publicly we've defended a hire, the more we value the hire. Andrew Wilkinson's Flow story is the textbook case: he believed his to-do-list app was superior to Asana's because it was *his*, and that belief survived years of evidence that Asana was outbuilding him on every dimension. He turned down a $6M acquisition offer when he had $11M sunk because the offer didn't validate the version of Flow he'd been telling himself was real.

The IKEA effect is the same mechanism with extra force: things you've *built* yourself you value more than things you bought pre-built. Which is why pedestal-building is poison: every pedestal is something you'll value too much to abandon, even though it represents no real progress.

### 4. Status quo bias (Samuelson and Zeckhauser, 1988)

Status quo bias is the tendency to stick with the current state of affairs even when changing would be better. Samuelson and Zeckhauser named it in 1988; the effect has held up in everything from organ-donor opt-in/opt-out studies to retirement-plan defaults. Set the default to "save 6% of paycheck" and people save 6%. Set it to "save nothing" and they save nothing. The default does all the work.

For quitting, status quo bias is the ambient force. Whatever you're doing is the default. Whatever you'd switch to requires a decision — and decisions are effortful, can be wrong, create exposure. Loss aversion is asymmetric here too: we're much more bothered by the downside of changing course than by the equally-likely downside of staying. Sarah Olstyn Martinez, the doctor who hated her job, was paralyzed by "what if I take the new role and I hate it too?" She wasn't paralyzed by "what if I stay and continue to be miserable?" — even though she'd already acknowledged misery was a 100% certainty if she didn't move. We treat the two losses asymmetrically.

Keynes nailed the social version: "Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally." Failing conventionally feels less bad and *is judged less harshly* than failing after a deliberate change of course. Multiply by every executive in every company, and you get the slow death by status quo that defines most large-company quitting failures.

### 5. Identity and dissonance — the hardest thing to quit is who you are

The previous four mechanisms compound, and then identity locks the door. The hardest thing to quit isn't a project, a job, a relationship, or a strategy. It's the version of yourself those things define.

The Sears story is the cleanest illustration. From the seventies on, Sears was losing the retail war — first to Kmart and Walmart on price, then to Macy's and Nordstrom on premium, then to Gap and the Limited on focus. Meanwhile, Sears had built a quietly enormous financial-services portfolio: Allstate, Dean Witter, Discover, Coldwell Banker. Combined market value $16.6 billion. Profitable. Growing. From any outside view, the trade was obvious — keep the financial empire, divest dying retail. Instead, in 1992, Sears announced it was breaking up the financial empire to "get back to its retailing roots." It sold off Allstate, Dean Witter Discover, Coldwell Banker. Retail went bankrupt in 2018. Allstate, in 2021: $40 billion. Discover: $40 billion. Morgan Stanley (which bought Dean Witter): $180 billion. Sears chose retail because *Sears was retail*. Anything else meant ceasing to be Sears. They preferred to die as themselves than to live as someone else.

The mechanism is **cognitive dissonance**, named by Leon Festinger after his 1954 study of a doomsday cult that survived the non-arrival of doom by becoming *more* devoted. When new information conflicts with our beliefs, we feel discomfort and want it to stop. We can change the belief — painful, especially if it's a load-bearing identity claim — or rationalize away the new information. Most of us, most of the time, rationalize. *I didn't waste eight years on the music career; I learned things. I didn't fail at this startup; I'm pivoting.*

This is why "I'm a teacher," "I'm a founder," "I'm an academic" make quitting so much harder than "I do teaching work" or "I am running this company right now." When what you do becomes who you are, walking away from the work is walking away from the self. Maya Shankar trained from age six to be a violinist, was accepted by Itzhak Perlman as a private student, was on the path to the highest tier of professional music. A torn tendon at fifteen forced her to quit. She described her identity being "literally torn away from her." She rebuilt — Yale, cognitive science, the White House, Google — but only because the choice was taken from her. Sasha Cohen kept skating in shows she hated, three years past the point of pleasure, because skating was who she was.

The five mechanisms are additive. Escalation plus sunk cost is how you find yourself an extra year in. Add endowment and you can't sell below your basis. Add status quo and the alternatives feel scary. Add identity and you literally don't know who you'd be if you walked away. None is correctable by willpower alone. Each requires a structural intervention — kill criteria, a quitting coach, divide-and-conquer roles, monkeys-first sequencing. That's the practical answer to "why can't I just walk away?" You can't, because five forces are gluing you in place. The way out is to fight them with structure, not with effort.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose which forces are loudest.** When someone (you or someone you're advising) is stuck, name the mechanism. "You're escalating commitment because the original decision was yours and reversing it feels like admitting you were wrong." "That's sunk-cost thinking — what's already spent is gone, the question is what's the EV from here." "You're endowed to this idea because you've defended it publicly for two years; that's why letting it go feels personal." "You're defaulting to the status quo because the costs of moving feel concrete and the costs of staying feel diffuse — they're not, they're just slower." "This isn't about the project; this is about who you are if the project ends." Naming demystifies. Naming weakens the spell.

2. **Pre-decide.** All five forces operate hardest in the moment. Decisions made in advance, before you're "in it," aren't subject to the same gravity. Set kill criteria *before* the project starts (see `kill-criteria.md`). Decide what would make you sell *before* you buy. Decide turnaround times *before* you start the climb. The whole point of pre-commitment is that the decision-maker who sets the rule isn't the same person — temporally — as the decision-maker who has to follow it.

3. **Find the outsider who hasn't been infected.** Status quo bias and endowment dissipate when the decision is made by someone fresh. Banks studied by Staw (1997) wrote down troubled loans far more readily after management changes — the new managers hadn't made the original lending decisions, so they had no commitment to escalate. Apply the same trick: divide-and-conquer the decision. Have the people who decide to *start* be different from the people who decide to *stop*, where you can. Where you can't structurally separate roles, find a quitting coach (see `the-quit-decision.md`) — someone close enough to know your situation, not invested enough to be infected by it.

4. **Make the alternative concrete.** Status quo bias and endowment both feed off the abstract-ness of the unfamiliar option. The current path is rich in detail; the alternative is a vague "what if?" Force the alternative into the same level of resolution. If you're considering leaving the job, write the new job description. Write the conversation with the recruiter. Write the new commute. Make the unfamiliar familiar enough that the asymmetry collapses.

5. **For identity: tie identity to process, not output.** The most durable form of identity is *what kind of person you are* rather than *what specific thing you do*. "I'm someone who builds things that matter" survives any individual project ending. "I'm a Glitch developer" doesn't. Stewart Butterfield's identity was elastic enough to absorb the death of *Glitch* and the birth of Slack. Sears's identity was rigid enough to die with retail. The difference is what was load-bearing.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has been at her company for five years. Growth has been flat for eighteen months. Her co-founder left a year ago. She still has $400K in the bank. When pressed, she says, "I can't quit now; we've put so much in. I have to give it everything."
**Application:** Name the forces. Sunk cost ("we've put so much in" — gone, doesn't matter). Escalation of commitment (the bank balance is what she'd have spent on alternatives; saying she has to "give it everything" is asking for permission to escalate). Identity ("I have to" — she's not analyzing, she's defending the version of herself that's a founder). The five-force read clarifies what's happening: she's not making a decision, she's running a defense. Suggest pre-commitment: "If you're going to keep going, set a kill criterion. What does the next two quarters need to look like for you to keep going? Specifically. With numbers. If those aren't met, you wind down. Decide that now, when you're not 'in it.'"
**Result:** She sets the criteria, misses them in Q1, recognizes herself trying to talk her way out of the agreement, calls her quitting coach, winds down within the next quarter. Two years later, she's CEO at a company that's actually working. The five years weren't wasted. They were the apprenticeship.

**Situation:** A regional bank has held a portfolio of loans to a single struggling industry for a decade. New leadership comes in. Within six months, they write off about a third of the book and reallocate the freed capital. The old leadership defended the loans for years; the new leadership wrote them off in months. Same loans, same data.
**Application:** This is Staw, Barsade, and Koput's 1997 finding. The mechanism is divide-and-conquer: the new managers had no escalation pressure (they didn't make the lending decisions), no sunk cost feeling (they didn't sign the original notes), no endowment (they didn't choose the borrowers). The same loans that looked like assets to the people who'd approved them looked like losses to the people who hadn't. Identity wasn't on the line.
**Result:** Generalize: when possible, structurally separate the people who decide to start from the people who decide to stop. Buying committee separate from selling committee. Hiring manager separate from firing decision. CEO who launches the product separate from the analyst who decides whether to kill it. The five forces are weaker by half when the decider doesn't own the original commitment.

**Situation:** A senior leader keeps a low-performing direct report on for two years past the point at which everyone else on the team has noticed. She privately admits the hire was a mistake. She keeps not firing.
**Application:** "If a fresh manager walked in today with no history with this person, what would they do?" The answer is immediate: let them go. The gap between her answer and the fresh manager's is the gap the five forces have built. Escalation: she hired this person, and firing means admitting she was wrong. Sunk cost: coaching, training, mentorship. Endowment: she chose them; the choice itself feels valuable. Status quo: firing requires action, not firing requires nothing. Identity: she sees herself as a manager who develops people, and giving up feels like a betrayal of that self-image.
**Result:** Naming all five lets her see what's happening. She fires within the month. Team performance improves within a quarter. She'd been doing the underperformer no favors either — they were stuck in a job they couldn't succeed in. Quitting on time would have been kinder, sooner.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the five forces as a debate weapon to dismiss someone's arguments to stay.
**Why:** "You're just suffering from sunk cost" is a perfect way to get someone to dig in further. The forces are universal; pointing them at someone else as if they're personal failings activates exactly the identity-defense reaction that makes the problem worse. Use the framework to *help* the person diagnose what's happening to them, not as a club. Tone matters. "Here's what I think is going on for me — sunk cost, endowment — does that sound right?" works. "You're escalating commitment" doesn't.

**Don't:** Try to fix all five forces at once with willpower and self-talk.
**Why:** None of these are willpower problems. Four decades of research is unambiguous on this — people who *know* about sunk cost commit the sunk-cost fallacy at the same rate as people who don't. Dan Rubin, who taught the sunk-cost effect for a living, fell for it himself in his own decisions. Awareness is necessary, not sufficient. The fix is structural: pre-commitment (kill criteria), separation (divide-and-conquer), outside perspective (quitting coach). When you find yourself relying on "I just need to be more rational about this," you've already lost.

**Don't:** Conflate the five forces with garden-variety stubbornness.
**Why:** Stubbornness is general-purpose; these forces are specific, named, and have specific countermeasures. Treating the over-persistence problem as a character flaw — "I'm just bad at quitting" — both misdiagnoses and pathologizes. The right frame is mechanical, not moral. Five forces, all known, each with a specific structural counter. You're not bad at quitting; you're a normal human in a system that systematically biases toward over-persistence. Knowing the mechanics is the start of designing yourself out of the trap.

**Don't:** Use identity-talk casually when the issue isn't identity.
**Why:** Of the five forces, identity is the deepest and the rarest. It's load-bearing for things you've spent decades on, things you describe yourself as ("I'm a doctor"), things that determine your standing in your community. Most over-persistence isn't an identity problem; it's escalation plus sunk cost plus status quo. Reaching for identity when the issue is one of the lighter forces is over-pathologizing — and it's the kind of move that makes the person feel attacked rather than helped. Identity is in play when the question "who am I if I'm not doing this?" produces a real, scary blank. If the answer is "fine, I'd be someone else who does another thing," it's not identity. It's something earlier in the cascade, and you can fix it more easily.
