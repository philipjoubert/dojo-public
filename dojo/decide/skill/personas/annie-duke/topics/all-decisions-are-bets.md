# All Decisions Are Bets

## When to Use

- Whenever you or someone you respect is announcing a belief or a decision with absolute certainty. "We're definitely going to hit the number." "There's no way this hire fails." "Obviously we should pivot." Those are bets dressed up as facts, and dressing them up as facts is what makes them dangerous.
- When you're about to commit a serious resource — capital, headcount, six months of runway, a partnership, a year of your life — and you haven't priced in the alternative futures.
- When a belief that's driving a decision came from a source you never vetted. (Most beliefs are like this. Read on.)
- When you catch yourself thinking the status quo isn't a decision. The grocery line you stay in is just as much a bet as the grocery line you switch to.
- Before any high-stakes meeting where conclusions will be stated as if they're true rather than offered as if they're probabilistic claims about an uncertain world.

## Fails When

- **You're already executing.** Once a bet is placed and the play is in motion, "wanna bet?" on every choice is just hesitation. The frame is for the deliberation phase, not the execution phase. You don't reopen the round trip every time the line creeps.
- **The question is values, not facts.** "Should I want this?" is not a probabilistic claim. The frame helps you calibrate beliefs about how the world is. It cannot tell you what you should care about.
- **It becomes a rhetorical weapon.** Saying "wanna bet?" to a colleague in front of their team is a way to humiliate them, not a way to extract information. The frame is for use on yourself and inside a truthseeking pod that has agreed to it. Outside that, it lands as aggression.

## Core Concept

I want you to internalize one move: every decision you make is a bet. Not metaphorically. Literally. Every decision is a choice among alternative futures, made with incomplete information, under uncertainty, with resources on the line. That's the textbook definition of a bet. *Merriam-Webster*, not me — "a choice made by thinking about what will probably happen." Choice. Probability. Risk. That's a decision.

We have a narrow folk definition of betting — casinos, bookies, sports books, lottery tickets — and that narrow definition is what makes us think of decisions as somehow different. They're not. When you take the job, you're betting that the version of you who took it will be better off than the version of you who didn't. When you fire the head of sales, you're betting that the next best alternative will produce a better expected outcome than keeping her. When you stay in a relationship for one more year, you're betting that the version of you in a year is happier for having stayed than the version who left now. Every decision commits you to a course of action that, by its nature, eliminates every other course you might have taken. There is always opportunity cost. The bet is always live.

Most of those bets aren't against another person — they're against alternative versions of yourself. The Annie who chose the chicken vs. the Annie who chose the fish. The founder who chose this seed round vs. the founder who chose to bootstrap another six months. The CEO who promoted internally vs. the CEO who hired externally. Different futures, all forfeited the moment you commit to one. That's why decisions are hard. Not because the alternatives are obvious. Because they are forever foreclosed by the act of choosing.

Now: bets are only as good as the beliefs they're based on. And here's the bad news — we're terrible at forming beliefs. Daniel Gilbert's research on belief formation is the cleanest summary I know. You'd think we hear something, vet it, then believe it if it survives. We don't. We hear something, we believe it, and then *occasionally*, if we have time and inclination, we vet it. Which means most of your beliefs got into your head without anything resembling due diligence. Baldness comes from the maternal grandfather (it doesn't, only partly). A dog year is seven human years (made up, thirteenth century, pure marketing). Low-fat diets are healthy (sugar industry, paid for the study, hundreds of millions of people miscalibrated for decades). Your beliefs are not earned. They are absorbed. And then you bet on them.

This is where the magic phrase comes in: *wanna bet?* When you're certain the Democrats will take the House, certain the hire will work, certain the launch is going to crush — say "wanna bet?" out loud, even just to yourself. Watch what happens. The certainty evaporates. You start asking the questions you should have asked before: How sure am I? How do I know this? Where did I get this information? What's the source? What would change my mind? What are the plausible alternatives? What does the person on the other side know that I don't? "Wanna bet?" forces the third step — the vetting one — that we usually skip. It's a portable scout-mindset trigger. It doesn't make you actually bet money. It just makes you act, for one second, as if you might.

The other piece of this is that we should stop reporting beliefs as binary — "I'm sure" / "I'm not sure" — and start reporting them as probabilities. "I'm 70% on this." "I'm 90% the deal closes." "I'm somewhere between 30% and 50% on the launch hitting the number." When you say it that way, three good things happen at once. First, you're forced to actually ask yourself how sure you are, which is a question most people skip. Second, when new information arrives, you can adjust the number — drop from 70 to 55 — without the wrenching feeling of "I was wrong, I'm an idiot." You weren't 100% on it; you were 70%. A 15-point shift in calibration is honest updating, not humiliation. Third, anyone listening becomes a more useful collaborator. They know you're inviting input rather than declaring a verdict, which means they're vastly more likely to share information that contradicts your view. If you assert "we're definitely on track," you've quietly told the room their dissent isn't welcome. If you say "I'm 75% on track, what am I missing?", you've handed them a permission slip.

One more move: the status quo is also a bet. If you're standing in a slow grocery line and you don't switch, you have just bet that this line is faster than the alternatives. The fact that you didn't actively *choose* the line — you just got into it and then kept standing — does not exempt you from having made the bet. This is the move Pete Carroll's critics missed. If he had handed off to Marshawn Lynch and lost, no one would have called it the worst decision ever, because everyone would have agreed that handing off was the obvious play, and any loss must therefore be luck or someone else's fault. Pete made an active choice and lost, so he got crucified. The choice to stay in the status quo is invisible — but it is a choice, and it is a bet, and it has the same expected-value math as any other.

## How to Apply

1. **Convert your strongest assertion into a probability.** When you catch yourself saying "definitely," "obviously," "100%," "there's no way" — interrupt yourself and put a number on it. Out loud, in writing, in a slack to yourself. "I'm 85% we hit the number." Not "we're hitting the number." The number forces calibration; the assertion hides it.

2. **Run the "wanna bet?" reflex.** When you find yourself certain about something with stakes attached — a market read, a hire, a competitor's move — silently ask "wanna bet?" Notice what happens to the certainty. Notice what questions you suddenly want answered. The questions you wanted answered are the questions you should have been asking the whole time.

3. **Inventory the alternatives, including the status quo.** For any decision, force yourself to write down the actual options: A, B, C, do nothing. The "do nothing" option is the trap. It feels like the absence of a choice, which is why most people don't price it. But every day you don't fire the underperformer, every quarter you don't pivot, every year you don't have the conversation — those are bets you are placing on the status quo by not actively re-choosing.

4. **Estimate expected value, even crudely.** For each option, what's the upside, the downside, and roughly how likely is each? Be willing to put numbers on it even when you feel you can't. After-School All-Stars used to budget by treating every grant application as if its full face value would arrive. A $200K application was worth $200K to them. I asked for the probability of getting it. They didn't have one. So we created one. A $200K grant with a 10% chance of hitting is worth $20K. A $50K grant with a 70% chance is worth $35K. The smaller-faced grant is, in expectation, the bigger one. Until they did the math, they were prioritizing the wrong applications. Most founders are doing the same thing with their roadmap.

5. **Be willing to say a range when you can't say a number.** "I think there's between a 20% and 60% chance this product launches on time." That's a wide range — and it is much, much better than refusing to give one because you're "not sure." Of course you're not sure. Sure isn't on the menu. The range is what you can say honestly. Refining the range — narrowing the band — is the work. Refusing to give one is just hiding.

6. **Update visibly.** When new information comes in, change your number out loud. "I was at 75% last week; I'm at 60% now because of X." This trains the reflex, models it for your team, and rebuilds the muscle most adults lost in school where "I don't know" got marked wrong.

7. **Imagine the bet from the other side.** When you have a strong view, imagine the smartest person you know holding the opposite view. What would they need to know — or believe — for their position to make sense? If your belief is well-founded, you should be able to articulate the steel-man case against it. If you can't, your bet isn't on a belief. It's on a feeling.

## Examples

**Situation:** John Hennigan, a high-stakes Vegas pro, sits at a poker table where a discussion about state capitals turns into a wager. The table dares him to live in Des Moines for thirty days. He'd be confined to one street, one hotel, one restaurant. The bet: $30,000.
**Application:** This sounds insane. It's actually one of the most lucid, transparent decisions in the room. Hennigan inventories the alternatives. Stay in Vegas, keep playing, foregoing the bet. Take the bet, win $30K, get a month away from a lifestyle that's grinding him down, work on his golf, see if a different life suits him. The "what if" is real on both sides — meet the love of his life in Des Moines, hate it after two days, advance his reputation as someone who'll bet on anything. Probabilities go on each branch. He decides the expected value is positive.
**Result:** He moves the next day. Two days in, he calls and offers the table $15K to let him out. They refuse. A few days later he pays $15K and goes home. The poker community loves the punch line. The point is the underlying analysis: *every* career decision, every relocation, every job change you'll ever make has the exact same shape as Hennigan's bet. The only difference is that Hennigan made the alternative futures explicit, the probabilities explicit, and the price tag explicit. You are making the same kind of decision when you take a job at Google or move cities for a partner. You're just hiding the bet from yourself.

**Situation:** A founder is about to fire her CTO. She says "I know it's the right call. He's not the guy for the next stage." The board nods.
**Application:** Convert the certainty to a number. "How sure are you?" — 85%. Now: what's on the line if she's wrong? The 15% case looks like: she fires a fixable executive, hiring takes nine months, the company loses momentum, two engineers she didn't realize were loyal to him quit, a Series B conversation goes sideways because the team looks unstable. Now run the alternative bets. (a) Fire him and hire externally. (b) Coach him, set 90-day goals, fire if missed. (c) Reorganize his role to play to his strengths. Each branch has its own expected value. She'd been treating the decision as binary — fire or don't fire — and as certain. It's neither.
**Result:** She doesn't change her decision. She still fires him. But she changes her *process* — runs the search in parallel before pulling the trigger so the gap is two weeks instead of nine months, has the conversation with the two loyal engineers in advance, briefs the board on the transition. The 15% case got priced in. When the firing happens, the company doesn't lose momentum, because she'd planned for the 15% world, not just the 85% one.

**Situation:** A founder defends a contrarian thesis to a skeptical investor: "AI search is going to eat traditional search within three years. It's obvious."
**Application:** "Wanna bet? At what odds?" The founder hesitates. *Three years* is doing a lot of work in that sentence. He realizes he means "search will be meaningfully disrupted by AI within three years" — but he was claiming something much stronger. He drops to 55% on the strong claim, 80% on the weak claim, and now has a useful conversation with the investor about which version of the thesis the company is actually betting on, and which version the investor would back. The fuzzy "obvious" version was unfundable. The calibrated 80% version is the actual case.
**Result:** The conversation goes from a confrontation to a collaboration. The investor's questions stop sounding like attacks because the founder is no longer defending an absolute. The founder also realizes — which is the bigger win — that his roadmap has been allocated as if the strong version were 100%, and adjusts the bet sizing to match the actual probability.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Demand probabilities from your team as a public test.
**Why:** "Wanna bet?" is a personal calibration tool. It works inside your head and inside a truthseeking pod that's agreed to use it. Used as a public challenge — especially with people junior to you — it becomes a humiliation tactic. The person being asked feels cornered and either doubles down on the original assertion to save face, or capitulates to whatever number they think you want. Either way, you've corrupted the data. Use the frame on yourself, share it with collaborators who've opted in, and resist the temptation to gotcha people with it.

**Don't:** Treat probabilistic talk as hedging or weakness.
**Why:** The instinct in most founder cultures is "leaders are decisive, leaders speak in absolutes." Probabilistic language sounds wishy-washy by contrast. It isn't. It's more honest. And — counterintuitively — calibrated communicators are *more* trusted, not less, because the people listening can tell the difference between a confident person who's pretending to know and a thoughtful person who's giving you their actual best read. If you say "I'm 80% on this," and 80% of those calls hit, your team will follow you anywhere. If you say "I'm 100%" and you're right 60% of the time, they'll quietly stop listening.

**Don't:** Confuse a probability with a prediction.
**Why:** Saying "I'm 70% the launch lands on time" is not a prediction that the launch will land on time. It's a claim about the *distribution* of possible futures. If a 70% bet misses, you weren't wrong — a 30% event happened. The mistake people make is treating the most-likely outcome as the *only* outcome and being shocked when one of the other branches materializes. A 30% chance of failure is a real chance of failure. Plan for it. Reserve for it. Don't act stunned when it shows up.

**Don't:** Forget that doing nothing is also a bet.
**Why:** Most of the worst decisions I've watched founders make were not active bad calls. They were *passive* bets on the status quo — keeping the wrong VP for one more quarter, refusing to confront a co-founder for one more year, avoiding the pricing change for one more cycle. Every one of those is a bet, and every one of those is a bet you made by not making one. Audit the status quo with the same rigor you'd audit a new initiative. "If we weren't already doing this, would we start?" If the answer is no, you have your answer.

**Don't:** Confuse "more accurate beliefs" with "always more cautious beliefs."
**Why:** The frame doesn't say be less confident. It says be calibrated. Sometimes calibration tells you the right number is 90%. Calibrated is not synonymous with humble. A well-calibrated scout will say 90% when 90 is right, and 30% when 30 is right, and won't downshift good bets to look modest or upshift weak bets to look bold. Calibration goes both ways. If you systematically underestimate your wins to look reasonable, you are just as miscalibrated as the founder who claims certainty on everything — just in the opposite direction.
