# Evaluating Businesses

## When to Use
- Evaluating a business to acquire, invest in, or compete against. The first question is not "what's the multiple" — it is "what pattern of extreme success, if any, does this fit, and what is the actual structural advantage?"
- Diagnosing your own company honestly. Most founders believe they have a moat. Most don't. The ones who do can name the specific structural thing preventing a competent competitor from replicating them. If you can't name it, you don't have it.
- Understanding why a specific competitor has a long run of success, or why a former leader collapsed. Competitive destruction, commodity economics, niche domination, scale advantages — they each leave different fingerprints.
- Designing strategy. If you cannot identify which of the four patterns of extreme success your business is built on, you do not have a strategy. You have a hope.

## Fails When
- **Inventing moats that don't exist.** "Our moat is our team" or "our moat is our execution" — these are not moats. A moat is a structural reason a competent competitor cannot replicate you. If you can't articulate what the structural thing is, there is no moat, and pretending there is will get you killed the first time a real competitor shows up.
- **Ignoring competitive destruction.** You have the finest buggy whip factory. The horseless carriage is coming. No amount of "execution" saves you. The question is whether you see the destruction coming and reposition, or ride it into the ground while working harder on the wrong thing.
- **Assuming "pretty good at everything" is a strategy.** It is not. Extreme success comes from one of four specific patterns. Being moderately competent across many dimensions is a recipe for a mediocre business, not a great one. The discipline is identifying the pattern and committing hard.
- **Confusing current profits with durable advantage.** Current profits can come from a temporary edge that will evaporate. Moats compound; temporary edges don't. The Red Queen test — where do the gains go, owner or customer — separates them.

## Core Concept

Extreme business success is not random. It follows a small number of patterns, and once you see them, you can diagnose almost any great company. I was using a simple search engine in my mind to go through checklist-style, and I was using some rough algorithms that work pretty well in a great many complex systems. Four patterns account for most of the extreme success in business history. Each is a structural answer to the same question — why does this business earn more than its cost of capital, year after year, while its competitors don't?

Pattern one: extreme maximization or minimization of one or two variables. Costco is the canonical example — minimize price at maximum quality, with maniacal consistency across decades. Everything else at Costco serves those two variables. The trade-offs are brutal and the results are extreme. The pattern is not to be "competitive on price." The pattern is to be, by a wide margin, the lowest-priced quality operator in the category, and to let that one thing compound.

Pattern two: combining multiple success factors in nonlinear ways — a lollapalooza, in the good sense. Several reinforcing advantages stacked, each of them modest alone, but multiplied at the intersection. Scale plus brand plus distribution plus switching costs plus network effects — when they compound, the moat is extremely hard to breach. Coca-Cola. Wrigley. A daily newspaper in a monopoly town, back when those existed. The question is never "do you have one advantage" — it is "do several reinforcing advantages combine into a lollapalooza."

Pattern three: extreme good performance across many factors simultaneously. Toyota. Les Schwab. Not one variable maximized, but many variables all executed unusually well — quality, cost, delivery, service, labor relations, supplier management, engineering. The rare company that can do this gets a long run of outperformance because competitors can copy one or two dimensions but cannot copy the whole stack.

Pattern four: catching and riding a big wave. Oracle. Microsoft. The Japanese tire manufacturers in their day. A structural shift changes the economics of an industry, and the companies that position early and build comprehensive advantages during the ride end up with durable positions. But remember — the wave is the setting, not the strategy. What matters is what you build during the ride.

Now, the flip side. Competitive destruction — when technology moves as fast as it does in a civilization like ours, entire industries get wiped out. You have the finest buggy whip factory and in comes this little horseless carriage. Before too many years go by, your buggy whip business is dead. Brilliant, disciplined people at IBM got bounced off the wave after surfing successfully for sixty years. No position is permanently safe. And commodity businesses — the Red Queen trap — run to stand still, because every productivity gain flows through to customers. In commodity textiles, all the gains flow to buyers. Not to us.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify which of the four patterns applies, or admit none do.** For any business you're evaluating — yours or theirs — name which pattern fits. Extreme max/min of one or two variables? Lollapalooza of combined advantages? Extreme performance across many factors? Wave-riding with structural advantages built during the ride? If none of them clearly fits, the business is mediocre. It may survive, it may even be profitable, but it will not be extreme and should not be modeled as if it will be.

2. **Name the specific structural thing preventing a competent competitor from replicating.** That is the moat. Not "execution." Not "team." A structural thing. Scale economies at a level competitors can't match. Distribution that took decades to build. Network effects past critical mass. Brand trust established over generations and reinforced by social proof. Switching costs that make customers costly to move. Regulatory capture. A cornered resource. Name the specific thing. If you can't, there is no moat.

3. **Run the Red Queen test. Where do the gains go?** If you spend $1M to improve productivity, who captures the benefit — you or your customers? In commodity businesses, the gains flow through. The loom story. In differentiated businesses with pricing power, the gains stay with you. This one test separates durable businesses from treadmills. Most founders never run it, and many do not realize they are on a treadmill until the treadmill has already consumed the decade.

4. **Look for critical mass dynamics and winner-take-all cascades.** Things tend to cascade toward winner-take-all in many businesses. Daily newspapers in their heyday — once I get most of the circulation, I get most of the advertising, and once I get most of the advertising and circulation, why would anyone want the thinner paper? Network effects compound. Scale economies compound. Social proof compounds. In such markets, it pays to be number one, acceptable to be number two, and murderous to be number three. Know which market you're in.

5. **Watch for competitive destruction.** Is there a technological or structural shift that changes the economics of your industry? If yes, your current advantages may not transfer. The buggy whip factory had a great business until it didn't. IBM had a great position until it didn't. The question is not "will disruption ever come" — it will — but "can I see it coming early enough to reposition, or am I going to be the last one to notice?"

6. **Price the disadvantages of scale honestly.** Bigger isn't always better. Large organizations develop bureaucracy, territoriality, perverse incentives, and slow decision-making. You get big, fat, dumb, unmotivated bureaucracies. Sears had layers and layers of people it didn't need. Walmart poked a hole through them. At some point the disadvantages of scale exceed the advantages, and the nimble competitor wins. Know where that point is in your industry.

7. **Consider niche specialization as a legitimate play.** Not every business needs to be huge. Just as animals flourish in niches, people who specialize in the business world and get very good because they specialize frequently find good economics they wouldn't get any other way. Trade publications that went narrower and deeper beat the generalists. Motorcross Magazine is a small company with loyal, nut-case readers, and it makes money. The niche is a real pattern. But remember — niches can dry up. Saturday Evening Post had a niche too, and the niche went away.

## Examples

**Situation:** Costco. A retailer that has outperformed most competitors for decades.
**Application:** Pattern one — extreme max/min of one or two variables. Minimize price at maximum quality. Everything at Costco serves those two variables. Limited SKUs so inventory turns are extreme. Membership model that captures fixed cost before the customer walks in. Private label driven by volume. Employee wages above industry average because turnover costs more than higher wages. Every decision is subordinated to the two variables. The moat: at Costco's scale and discipline, competitors either can't match the pricing or can't match the quality, and the ones who try to split the difference lose on both.
**Result:** Munger called Sinegal one of the top five retailers of the past century. Costco still outperforms. The pattern is stable because the commitment is fanatic. Imitators who tried to be "low price, decent quality, broad assortment" failed because they diluted the two variables. You can't split the focus and get the extreme result.

**Situation:** Sears versus Walmart, 1970s-1990s.
**Application:** Sears had the scale advantages of its era — distribution, brand, purchasing power, advertising efficiency. Should have been unbeatable. But scale brought its own diseases. Layers and layers of people it didn't need. Bureaucratic. Slow to think. If you poked your head up with a new thought, the system turned against you. Walmart, smaller but leaner, exploited every weakness. Scale advantages without scale diseases. Sears' gains from scale were being eaten by its own bureaucracy.
**Result:** Walmart took the category. Sears declined for thirty years, too large to turn, too bureaucratic to reform. The lesson is not that scale is bad. It is that the disadvantages of scale can exceed the advantages, and when they do, a smaller, disciplined competitor will run circles around you. Bigger isn't automatically better. Life is an everlasting battle between scale advantages and scale disadvantages.

**Situation:** The textile mill Berkshire owned, facing a productivity-enhancing loom purchase.
**Application:** The math was positive on paper — the loom would double productivity. Munger ran the Red Queen test. Low-end textiles are a commodity. Every mill that adopted the loom would lower prices by approximately the productivity gain. Customers would capture the surplus. We'd run to stand still.
**Result:** Munger said, I hope this doesn't work — because if it does, I'm going to close the mill. The investment that looked positive by NPV was negative in reality because of the structural economics. Who captures the gains? In a commodity business, not you. That single question saves decades of wasted capital.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Claim a moat you can't name.
**Why:** "We execute better" is not a moat. "Our team is stronger" is not a moat. A moat is a specific structural thing preventing a competent competitor from replicating you. Scale at a level competitors can't match. Distribution that took decades to build. Patents. Network effects past critical mass. Brand established through years of consistent performance. If you can't name the structural thing in one sentence, there is no moat. Pretending there is will get you killed.

**Don't:** Assume because you were successful in the old structure, you'll be successful in the new one.
**Why:** IBM was brilliant, disciplined, and dominant in mainframes. That competence didn't transfer to PCs or internet. Industry shifts change which competencies matter. Old winners who don't recognize the shift become new losers. The discipline is to honestly assess whether your advantages translate or whether you're a buggy whip factory that just hasn't noticed the horseless carriage.

**Don't:** Diversify across all four patterns of extreme success hoping one sticks.
**Why:** Extreme success comes from commitment to one pattern, not hedging across all of them. Costco did not also try to be a niche specialist and a wave-rider. Patterson at NCR did not also try to minimize one variable extreme-style. The patterns are ways of concentrating, not of spreading. A founder who says "we want a little of each" has no strategy, only a list.

**Don't:** Confuse current profitability with durable advantage.
**Why:** A business can be profitable today because it has a temporary edge — early timing, a regulatory quirk, a founder with unusual connections — that will evaporate. Durable advantage compounds; temporary edges don't. The test is the Red Queen test, combined with an honest look at whether the structural conditions that created the current profits will persist. If they won't, the current profits are rent from a position you're about to lose.

**Don't:** Ignore the disadvantages of scale.
**Why:** Growing is not automatically good. Past some point, bureaucracy and complacency exceed the operational advantages of size. Sears got there. GE got there. Many large tech companies are there now. Either reform internally — very hard, most can't — or accept that you've become a target for the nimble competitor coming whether you like it or not.

Four patterns. Name which applies. Name the specific structural thing that makes it durable. Run the Red Queen test. That's the whole evaluation. Of course.
