# Iron Prescriptions

## When to Use
- Any pattern of repeating failure where discretion has demonstrably failed to prevent it. You keep agreeing to things you should decline, investing outside your circle, trusting enthusiastic advisors, acting under peer pressure. Discretion isn't working. The answer is an iron rule — non-negotiable, structural, applied without exception.
- Company communication discipline. Carl Braun's rule — always tell people why. Make it iron. People who skip the why get fired. That's how you get a communication culture that actually works.
- Personal habits that protect you from your own biases. Never act under four biases pointing the same way. Never invest outside your circle without explicit humility. Never trust advice from someone paid more when you act. Never let envy, resentment, or self-pity drive a decision. These are not preferences. They are iron.
- Designing checklists for recurring high-stakes decisions. Pilots don't fly without a checklist no matter how experienced they are. Why would you acquire a company, hire an executive, or raise a round without one?

## Fails When
- **Treated as a preference.** "I usually follow this rule" is not an iron prescription. Braun's rule was "you get fired the second time." That bindingness — the no-exceptions enforcement — is what makes it work. A rule you break when inconvenient is not a rule; it's a mood.
- **Over-proliferated until everything is "iron."** If everything is a hard rule, nothing is. Reserve iron prescriptions for the patterns where you have real evidence that discretion has failed — and where the cost of the failure is high. A dozen hard rules is too many.
- **Used to replace thinking about novel situations.** Iron prescriptions cover the repeating patterns. They do not cover the genuinely new decision that requires fresh analysis. The discipline is knowing which is which, and not using rules to avoid the harder work of real thinking.
- **Imposed on others without being followed yourself.** A founder who tells the team to adhere to rules they personally violate will produce cynicism, not compliance. Iron prescriptions that apply to you apply to you first.

## Core Concept

Carl Braun built CF Braun Engineering into a great company partly on one rule. If you wrote a letter or directive in the Braun company telling somebody to do something, and you didn't tell him why, you could get fired. In fact, you would get fired if you did it twice. Always, always, always tell people why. It is an iron rule of worldly wisdom. If you always tell people why, they'll understand it better, they'll consider it more important, and they'll be more likely to comply. Even if they don't understand your reason, they'll be more likely to comply. That's the reason-respecting tendency in human psychology, and Braun used it to build one of the great American engineering firms. The rule worked because it was iron. "Usually tell people why" is not Braun's rule. Braun's rule was: do it once, we talk. Do it twice, you're out. That binding quality is not incidental — it is the mechanism. Of course it is.

The principle generalizes. Most human failures are not failures of knowledge. They are failures of application. You know you should not act under four biases pointing the same direction — but in the moment, with the social proof going and the deadline pressing and the authority figure urging, you do it anyway. You know you should not invest outside your circle of competence — but the pitch was exciting and your ego supplied the confidence, and you did it. You know you should have a margin of safety — but you were sure, and sure people do not think they need one. In each case, discretion failed because the bias overrode the knowledge. The only defense is to make the rule non-negotiable — to remove the decision from the in-the-moment self and pre-commit the other self to the right answer.

That is what an iron prescription is. A self-imposed rule that removes the bad option from the choice set entirely. Not "I'll try to avoid it." Not "I usually don't do this." An actual structural commitment that makes the bad behavior impossible or at minimum costly enough that the in-the-moment self will balk. Franklin knew this — his list of virtues was a schedule of self-discipline applied daily. Darwin knew it — he deliberately gave priority attention to evidence that would disconfirm his cherished theories, because he knew his brain would otherwise weight it less. Munger's own life was built on a small number of these. Never envy. Never resent. Never indulge self-pity. Never act under lollapaloozas. Sit on your ass when there is nothing meaningful to do. Be fanatically reliable. Tell the truth. Read everything.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the recurring failure patterns in your actual behavior.** Not in theory — in the specific ways you, and your company, have failed in the past. Did you take a deal you shouldn't have because of FOMO? Did you hire too fast in a hot market? Did you stay in a project long past the point you should have killed it? The pattern is your data. Write it down.

2. **For each pattern, design a rule that makes the bad behavior structurally harder.** Not "I'll be more careful." An actual rule. No acquisition over $X without a 30-day cooling-off period. No hire without three independent reference checks, no exceptions. No investment outside a pre-defined circle of competence. No decision made in the twelve hours after a high-emotion meeting. The rule has to be specific enough that you can tell, instantly, whether you're following it.

3. **Make the rules iron — non-negotiable.** The whole point is that discretion failed. If you reserve the right to override the rule when you feel it is warranted, you have not fixed the problem. You have a preference, not a rule. The binding quality is the mechanism. Braun fired people the second time. Ulysses tied himself to the mast. You need to engineer the bindingness in, not just intend it.

4. **Install the "always tell them why" rule in company communication.** Every directive, every policy, every memo includes the why. Not because it's polite. Because people who understand the reason comply better, think more clearly about the edge cases, and don't need to be told the same thing again next week. If the why is not in the memo, the memo is incomplete. Munger named this an iron rule of worldly wisdom. Treat it as one.

5. **Use the Granny's Rule — do the unpleasant necessary work first.** Munger used Franklin's discipline. Do the hard task before the easy one, reward yourself only after the hard thing is done. Applied to founders — address the hardest problem in the business first thing in the morning, every morning, before you let yourself do the fun stuff. The rule is iron because discretion fails — in the moment, you always want to do the easy thing first. The sequencing must be pre-committed.

6. **Adopt Darwin's discipline on disconfirming evidence.** Give priority attention to evidence that would disprove your cherished beliefs. Not equal attention — priority attention. Write the disconfirmer down when you find it. Weight it heavily, against your own instinct to discount it. Darwin did this his whole working life. He wrote On the Origin of Species. It worked.

7. **Build checklists for recurring high-stakes decisions.** No wise pilot, no matter how great his talent and experience, fails to use his checklist. The pilot is not stupid. The pilot knows that human memory fails under stress, and that the cost of missing one item is catastrophic. Hiring, acquisitions, product launches, fundraising, firings — these are decisions that repeat. Build the checklist. Use it every time. No exceptions, including the time you "don't need it."

8. **Sit on your ass when there is nothing meaningful to do.** Most of Berkshire's best decisions were decisions not to act. If you actually figure out how many decisions were made in the history of Berkshire Hathaway that were meaningful, it wasn't very many per year. The normal human allotment is not many great opportunities per lifetime. The iron discipline is to refuse the small opportunities that consume attention and capital, so you can act decisively when the rare great one appears. Activity is not progress.

## Examples

**Situation:** Carl Braun's engineering company, running on the five Ws rule.
**Application:** Every directive specified who, what, where, when, and why. The why was not optional. If you skipped the why, you got a talking-to. If you did it again, you got fired. That was not cruel. It was disciplined. Braun knew that compliance collapses without reason, and that he could not trust his people to always remember to include it — so he made it an iron rule enforced structurally.
**Result:** Braun built one of the great American engineering companies. Compliance improved dramatically. Memos were clearer. Decisions were better understood throughout the organization. Munger called it an iron rule of worldly wisdom and recommended it to every founder who would listen. The rule worked because it was iron. Make it a preference and it wouldn't have worked.

**Situation:** Berkshire's disciplined holding of long-term positions despite apparent overvaluation.
**Application:** Many conventional managers would sell a long-held position when it becomes "too expensive." Munger and Buffett largely do not. The iron prescription is: sit on your ass in great companies. This is enforced by understanding the tax math (selling triggers huge capital gains), the difficulty of identifying truly better alternatives, and the behavioral cost of breaking compounding chains. The rule is not re-evaluated every month. It is iron.
**Result:** If you sit on your ass for long, long stretches in great companies, you can get a huge edge from nothing but the way income taxes work. A 15% pre-tax compounded return, paid once at the end, gives you a 13.3% net return. Turnover churns that down to 9.75% even with the same underlying returns. The sitting is worth 350 basis points per year, which is enormous over decades. The rule is iron. The result is compounding.

**Situation:** A founder who repeatedly hires quickly in hot markets and regrets most hires within a year.
**Application:** Discretion has failed. The iron prescription: no hire without three independent reference checks, asked specifically what the candidate is bad at. No exceptions, including "we need to move fast" — that reason is exactly when discretion fails and the rule must hold.
**Result:** Hiring slows slightly. Quality rises considerably. Bad senior hires cost far more than slow hiring does. The rule works because it is iron.

**Situation:** Munger's personal iron prescriptions on envy, resentment, and self-pity.
**Application:** Categorical refusal of all three. Not regulation — refusal. The rule is not "manage envy"; it is "refuse to entertain it." Once iron, the in-the-moment temptation has no decision to make — the answer is settled.
**Result:** Munger called these among the most important disciplines of his life. Time not spent on envy, resentment, and self-pity was time available for actual thinking. Judgment improved because the biases that accompany those emotions were not corrupting it. Discretion fails where these emotions are concerned; only categorical pre-commitment holds.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Call it an iron prescription if it bends.
**Why:** The binding quality is the whole mechanism. If you reserve the right to override the rule when you think it's warranted, it is not iron — it is a suggestion, and suggestions fail under exactly the biases you designed the rule to counter. Braun's rule wasn't "usually explain why." It was "do it twice, you're out." Design the consequence in, or don't bother.

**Don't:** Over-proliferate rules until the company is a rulebook.
**Why:** If everything is iron, nothing is. Reserve iron prescriptions for the handful of places where you have real evidence that discretion fails, and where the cost of the failure is high. Three to seven non-negotiable rules is about right. Twenty is bureaucracy, and bureaucracy produces its own kind of stupidity.

**Don't:** Impose rules on others that you don't follow yourself.
**Why:** Cynicism. You will get compliance for a week and cynicism for years. If the rule is iron for the team, it is iron for you first. If the rule is "always tell people why," every memo you send includes the why, without exception. Violating your own iron rules is how you destroy trust in all of them, including the ones that matter most.

**Don't:** Use rules as a substitute for thinking about genuinely novel situations.
**Why:** Iron prescriptions cover the recurring failure patterns. They do not cover the new decision that requires real analysis. The discipline is knowing which is which. A founder who tries to reduce every decision to a rule will either over-proliferate rules, or will apply a rule in a situation where it doesn't fit, and both are failures. Rules for the patterns; judgment for the novel.

**Don't:** Skip the why when you communicate.
**Why:** Every time you skip it, you erode the reason-respecting compliance Braun built his company on. People rely on instruction rather than understanding — they cannot adapt, cannot handle edge cases, cannot push back when the instruction is wrong. Always, always, always tell people why. An iron rule of worldly wisdom. Treat it as one.

Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there. Then write the rule that keeps you out of that place. Make it iron. And follow it. That is the whole discipline.
