# The Seven Deadly Founder Sins

## When to Use
- Pre-flight any major decision. Acquisition. Senior hire. Fundraise. Pivot. Takes ten minutes. Covers most of the ways founders predictably blow themselves up.
- Post-mortem any bad outcome. Usually three or four of these were operating together. Name them, so you don't repeat them.
- When a peer just made a conspicuous failure, study which sin they committed. You can learn those mistakes for free — a bargain nobody takes seriously enough.
- Whenever you feel overwhelming certainty or urgency. Certainty-and-urgency is the texture of most of these sins. The feeling is the signal.

## Fails When
- **Used to scold others while committing the same sins yourself.** The founder's sins destroy the company. Apply this catalog to yourself first, always.
- **Applied as labels rather than diagnoses.** "That's envy" is useless. "I am about to enter this market because a peer is succeeding there, and if I remove the peer the decision doesn't stand" is useful. Specificity is the whole point.
- **Used to generate endless self-doubt.** Identify the sin, correct for it, move. If you cycle through the catalog forever without committing, you have converted the tool into the disease.

## Core Concept

Most founders do not fail from bad luck. They fail from a small number of recurring behavioral patterns Munger spent sixty years cataloguing. I sought good judgment mostly by collecting instances of bad judgment, then pondering ways to avoid such outcomes. The same mistakes repeat across industries, decades, and founders who would have sworn they knew better.

What follows is seven of the most common founder failure modes. For each: the sin, how it manifests, the bias causing it, and the iron prescription. They are not independent — they compound. The founder who is envious of a peer is usually also acting outside his circle, usually also ignoring his margin of safety, usually also trusting advisors whose incentives are wrong. That compounding is the lollapalooza. Three or four operating together is how good founders destroy good companies. Count them. If you find three pointing the same way, stop.

## The Seven

### 1. Envy — Acting Because Peers Are Acting

**Manifestation:** A competitor raises at a bigger valuation. A peer expands into a new market. Suddenly you want to do what they're doing. Not because the analysis said yes. Because they're doing it and you're not.

**Bias:** Envy plus social proof. Envy was wreaking havoc long before it got a bad press in the laws of Moses. Social proof ties your decision to what your reference class is doing.

**What it does:** Stanley Druckenmiller and George Soros both got killed in the late 1990s because they couldn't stand watching others make money in tech stocks without them. If it can happen to them, it can happen to you.

**Iron prescription:** Refuse envy categorically. Not regulate it — refuse it. Envy is the only sin from which you can never possibly have any fun. Someone will always be getting richer faster than you — so what? When you catch yourself making a decision because a peer is making one, stop. Remove the peer from the picture. If the decision doesn't stand on its own, it isn't a decision — it's envy.

### 2. Operating Outside the Circle While Claiming Otherwise

**Manifestation:** The founder succeeded in one domain, so he extends into an adjacent one with confidence based on the previous success rather than actual understanding of the new field.

**Bias:** Excessive self-regard plus chauffeur knowledge. The founder mistakes having-read-about-something for understanding, and general intelligence for domain competence. Intelligence transfers less than people think.

**What it does:** Munger's own Alibaba mistake. "I got charmed with the idea of their position on the Chinese Internet. I didn't stop to realize they're still a God damn retailer." He operated outside his real circle. It cost him. If it happened to Munger at 95 with sixty years of practice, it can happen to you.

**Iron prescription:** Before operating outside your domain, apply the Planck test. Can you answer the follow-up? Can you reason from principles? If not, you're the chauffeur. Develop real competence, hire someone who has it, or decline. If you play games where other people have the aptitudes and you don't, you're going to lose. That's as close to certain as any prediction you can make.

---

### 3. Single-Model Thinking — The Hammer CEO

**Manifestation:** Every problem evaluated through one lens. Growth. Costs. Engineering elegance. Salesmanship. Whichever discipline the founder came from, he applies to everything.

**Bias:** The man with a hammer sees every problem as a nail. The nature of human psychology is such that you'll torture reality so that it fits your models.

**What it does:** The growth-obsessed founder who ignores unit economics — finance hammer. The engineer who can't understand why users hate his technically correct product — engineering hammer. The sales-culture CEO who hires more salespeople when the real problem is the product doesn't work — sales hammer. Every one is a predictable failure mode.

**Iron prescription:** Build the latticework. Eighty or ninety models carry ninety percent of the freight. Before any major decision, apply at least three disciplinary lenses in writing. Economics. Psychology. Engineering. Biology of competitive ecosystems. If you have only one answer, you have not thought about the problem. You have had a reaction.

### 4. Ignoring Psychology — Skipping Track Two

**Manifestation:** The founder analyzes the rational factors — economics, probabilities, structure — and thinks the analysis is complete. He has not asked which biases are operating in himself, in the counterparty, in the crowd, in the advisors.

**Bias:** A meta-bias — the belief that rational analysis is sufficient. It is almost never sufficient for decisions involving people, which is most decisions.

**What it does:** Overpaying in acquisitions because biases align. Hiring the charming candidate who fools you. Fundraising FOMO driving urgency. The team's commitment to a feature overriding the data that users don't want it. The rational analysis said go; the psychological analysis, had it been run, would have said no.

**Iron prescription:** For every decision involving people, run Track Two after Track One. Which of the twenty-five tendencies are operating? On me? On the counterparty? If the decision fails Track Two despite passing Track One, address it before acting. Never accept a Track-One answer alone. Psychology explains more business outcomes than economics does.

### 5. No Margin of Safety — "I'm Sure"

**Manifestation:** The plan is optimized to the edge. Runway is exactly what's needed if everything goes right. When asked about the downside, the founder says — because I'm sure.

**Bias:** Excessive self-regard plus over-optimism. The forecast is optimistic because the founder is optimistic. No buffer because he's sure the buffer is unnecessary.

**What it does:** The first time anything goes mildly wrong — and something always does — the plan collapses. A delayed hire. A missed quarter. A bridge that takes two months longer. Any of these, in an optimized-to-the-edge plan, is fatal. Bridges are built to hold ten times the expected load because engineers know what happens when they're not.

**Iron prescription:** Never decide without a margin of safety. Raise more than you think you need. Hire ahead of critical roles. Buffer timelines by fifty percent. Eliminate single points of failure. If the plan only survives when everything goes right, it isn't a plan — it's a wish. The word "sure" should trigger the buffer, not replace it.

---

### 6. Positive-Thinking Denial — Rationalizing Bad Numbers

**Manifestation:** The numbers are bad. Instead of facing them, the founder builds a reasonable-sounding story about why they don't mean what they seem to mean. Sales are down because of the market. Churn is up because of a short-term issue. PMF is just around the corner.

**Bias:** Denial, amplified by commitment-and-consistency. Demosthenes had it right: what a man wishes, that also will he believe. Combined with public commitment to a strategy, the denial is near-airtight from the inside.

**What it does:** Denial causes enormous destruction where people keep throwing money into methods that aren't going to work. The reasonable-sounding story becomes the operating reality. Bad numbers get explained away until the runway ends and the explanation finally fails. By then it's too late.

**Iron prescription:** If you are explaining a bad number with a reasonable-sounding story, the story is the denial. Write down in advance the kill criteria — specific metrics that, if hit, mean the strategy isn't working. When they hit, act. Do not revise. Apply Darwin's discipline: give priority attention to evidence that disconfirms your cherished theory. Weight the disconfirmer heavily, because your brain will otherwise weight it less.

---

### 7. The Lollapalooza Deal — Acquisition Fever, FOMO, and Incentive-Driven Advisors

**Manifestation:** High-adrenaline negotiation. Acquisition target. Hot round. Everyone is excited. Advisors enthusiastic. Tight timeline. Social proof going. Founder has publicly committed. Someone else might buy them. The VP Corp Dev has worked on it for months. Decision about to be made under exactly the conditions under which humans make the worst decisions of their lives.

**Bias:** Not one — four, five, six, all pointing the same way. Social proof. Commitment. Authority (the banker). Deprival super-reaction (someone else might get it). Over-optimism about synergies. Reciprocation for dinners. Incentive-caused bias in every advisor in the room, each paid more when you act. Lollapalooza.

**What it does:** Munger cataloged the McDonnell Douglas case — six biases operating in concert to re-run a failed test. Acquisition after acquisition destroys value at the acquirer, same pattern every time. It is not that people are stupid. It is that the lollapalooza overrode the analysis.

**Iron prescription:** Never act when four or more biases point the same way. Count them explicitly. Four or more: mandatory delay, mandatory devil's advocate, mandatory rewrite of the decision process. This is the single most reliable rule Munger offered for preventing the worst founder disasters. If the deadline is self-imposed or banker-imposed, the urgency is part of the lollapalooza — break the urgency. If the deadline is real and you cannot address the lollapalooza, walk. A deal done under lollapalooza conditions is a deal that should not have been done.

## How to Apply

1. **Pre-flight every major decision against the seven.** Ten minutes. Envy? Outside circle? Single-model? Skipping Track Two? Optimized to edge? Denying bad numbers? Four-plus biases aligned? If three hit, full stop.

2. **Post-mortem every bad outcome.** Not to assign blame. To name which of the seven was operating. Usually three or four. Write them down. Build an iron prescription for each in your future process.

3. **Run it on yourself first, every time.** The founder is the source of most founder failures. Applying this to others before yourself is the chauffeur move.

4. **Watch for the lollapalooza texture.** Overwhelming certainty. Urgency. "This is the one." The strongest feelings indicate the most biased thinking. That's the moment to count the biases.

5. **Share the catalog with your senior team.** A founder who is alone with these biases is worse off than one whose team is licensed to point them out.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder receives a term sheet from a hot VC at a valuation 40% above market. The VC sized the round. Timeline is two weeks. Two partners have emailed personally. A competitor is rumored to be raising.
**Application:** Count the biases. Social proof (competitor raising). Authority (prestigious fund). Reciprocation (personal emails, dinners). Deprival super-reaction (two-week window). Commitment (weeks of founder time invested). Over-optimism (valuation feels good). Six. Lollapalooza. Mandatory delay. Mandatory devil's advocate.
**Result:** The founder who forces a delay usually closes on different terms, or elsewhere, or declines. The founder who takes the deal on the two-week timeline has a 40% valuation bump and covenants he didn't fully understand — because he did not have time. The covenants surface eighteen months later, in the down round, when they destroy his equity. Of course they do.

**Situation:** Druckenmiller in the tech bubble, one of the most accomplished traders of his generation.
**Application:** Envy operated ("always had to be the best"). Single-model (momentum). No margin of safety. Lollapalooza of social proof, authority, commitment. Multiple sins stacked. He had every capability to avoid this and still got hit, because the conditions overrode his disciplines.
**Result:** Large losses. If it can happen to Druckenmiller, it can happen to you. The iron prescription — refuse envy — is non-negotiable for exactly this reason.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Diagnose others while ignoring your own patterns.
**Why:** The founder's failures destroy the company. Applying the catalog to your team while skipping yourself is theater — surface recognition without the hard work of self-examination.

**Don't:** Treat the seven as independent.
**Why:** They compound. Envy plus outside-the-circle plus no margin plus lollapalooza is the classic founder-disaster pattern. The interlock is the point. Three together is much worse than one at a time.

**Don't:** Use labels instead of diagnoses.
**Why:** "That's envy" lets you feel diagnosed without correcting the behavior. "I am about to do this because a peer did it, and if I remove the peer the decision doesn't hold" is the actual diagnosis. Specificity or nothing.

**Don't:** Think that knowing the sins protects you from them.
**Why:** It doesn't. Munger knew them all, catalogued them, taught them for sixty years, and still committed several at scale — he said so publicly. The catalog is not a vaccine; it is a checklist. Protection comes from applying the checklist to specific decisions, every time.

Envy. Outside the circle. Single-model. No Track Two. No margin of safety. Denial. Lollapalooza. Seven sins. Read them. Apply them. Then tell me where you're going to die, so you'll never go there.
