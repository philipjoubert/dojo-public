# Two-Track Analysis

## When to Use
- Any decision involving people. Hiring, firing, partnerships, acquisitions, customer behavior, fundraising, negotiation, team dynamics, board interactions. If humans are in the loop, you need both tracks.
- The rational case looks obvious and the decision feels certain. That is precisely when the biases are loaded and you have not yet checked them. Certainty is the alarm.
- A counterparty has been unusually enthusiastic, unusually generous, unusually quick to agree. Any of those patterns is a signal that Track 2 factors are working on you even if the Track 1 numbers look fine.
- Big numbers on the line — the lollapalooza stakes — where biases combine and reinforce. Acquisitions, funding rounds, executive hires, product bets. One bias is manageable. Four biases pointing the same way is when companies die.

## Fails When
- The decision is genuinely mechanical. Don't overlay psychology on arithmetic. Nobody has a bias about the sum of a column.
- Track 2 is performed rather than done. "I considered the possibility of FOMO and determined there wasn't any" is not Track 2. That's the Track 2 costume without the actual examination. If you named fewer than three biases operating, you haven't done it.
- Used as a weapon to accuse other people of bias rather than as a discipline to audit your own reasoning. The move is introspection first, counterparty analysis second.

## Core Concept

I now use a kind of two-track analysis. First, what are the factors that really govern the interests involved, rationally considered? And second, what are the subconscious influences where the brain at a subconscious level is automatically doing these things — which by and large are useful, but which often misfunction? That's the whole method. Two tracks, every time, on anything involving people.

Track One is the rational analysis. Interests. Probabilities. Economics. Structure. Incentive math. Opportunity cost. The bridge-hand, expected-value, compound-interest way of working the problem. Most founders are pretty good at Track One because it looks like work, it produces a spreadsheet, and it makes them feel serious. Of course it does. It's the comfortable half.

Track Two is the psychological analysis. Which of the twenty-five standard causes of human misjudgment is operating right now? In you. In the other party. In the crowd. Liking and loving is making you want this candidate because she reminds you of you. Social proof is making you want the deal because three respected people already said yes. Scarcity is making you hurry because the round might close without you. Reciprocation is softening you because the other side bought dinner. Commitment and consistency is locking you in because you've already told the team you're doing it. Incentive-caused bias is distorting the advice of everyone who gets paid when the decision is made.

Most founders skip Track Two entirely. When they do Track One alone and feel certain, that certainty is the bias talking. It is remarkable how often the confident rational case is a psychological state dressed in a spreadsheet. The two-track move is to do both analyses, see the gap, and adjust. Not to throw out the rational case, but to audit it for the biases that built it. That's how you stop getting hustled — by your counterparty, by your team, by your own brain.

## How to Apply

1. **Run Track 1 first, fully and honestly.** Interests, probabilities, expected value, incentive structures. Who gains, who loses, what are the actual economics. Write it down. Don't skip this — Track 2 without Track 1 is just vibes.

2. **Now run Track 2 explicitly.** Not "did I consider biases." The actual audit. For this specific decision, which of the twenty-five tendencies is operating? Name them. In you. In the counterparty. In the room. If you name fewer than three biases operating, you have not done Track 2 — you have performed it. Go back.

3. **For each bias named, ask what it is doing to the Track 1 numbers.** Incentive-caused bias in the advisor might be adding twenty percent to the optimism in the projections. Social proof is making the market size feel bigger than the bottom-up check supports. Scarcity is collapsing the timeline and eliminating the counter-offer. Commitment bias is suppressing your willingness to revisit a decision you made three months ago in different conditions. Adjust Track 1 with these corrections, explicitly.

4. **Check the counterparty's Track 2, not just yours.** What biases are working on them? A founder selling a company is subject to anchoring on the last funding round, loss aversion on a price below that, and sunk-cost on the years of work. A hiring candidate is subject to commitment bias on the offer they already mentally accepted. Understand what the room looks like through their biases, not just yours.

5. **Watch for the lollapalooza.** When you've named four or more biases all pushing in the same direction — that's an emergency, not a decision. Delay. Bring in a devil's advocate. Change the structure of how the decision will be made. Because four biases acting together produce cult-level commitment, not clear thinking.

6. **If Track 1 and Track 2 disagree, do not act on Track 1 alone.** The gap between the two tracks is the signal. A Track 1 that says yes and a Track 2 that is loaded with biases saying yes is not two tracks agreeing — it's Track 2 producing Track 1 and both of them being wrong.

7. **Check the incentives, then check them again.** Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome. Whose compensation or career goes up if this decision is made? If they're the ones recommending it, discount the recommendation heavily. The bias is not dishonesty. It's how the brain is built. The advisor will genuinely believe the deal is good, because his brain makes him believe it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A CEO is about to hire a VP of Sales. The candidate is charismatic, comes highly recommended by a respected peer CEO, has a great resume, and hit it off immediately with the founder at dinner.
**Application:** Track 1: skills, experience, compensation, references beyond the one peer, track record at comparable-stage companies, proof of hitting number rather than proof of being at companies where the number was hit. Track 2 — name the biases. Liking/loving: the candidate reminds the founder of himself, which is the single most reliable way for founders to hire wrong. Social proof: the peer CEO recommendation is doing a lot of work here. Authority: the candidate name-dropped two impressive former employers. Commitment bias: after dinner, the founder has already started to think of this as "my guy." Reciprocation: the candidate is flattering the mission and the founder. That's at least five tendencies pointing the same direction. Lollapalooza.
**Result:** The move is to introduce structural friction. Independent references the candidate did not provide. A working session that tests actual sales judgment, not chemistry. A delay of a week so the dinner-glow subsides. Probably still hire — but with Track 2 corrections applied, which the interview alone would not have produced. Skipping Track 2 here is how companies hire expensive wrong VPs and lose twelve months.

**Situation:** Fundraising. A top-tier VC is offering a preemptive term sheet at a high valuation. The partner is enthusiastic. The round needs to close in seven days. Three other founders the VC has backed are texting how great the firm is.
**Application:** Track 1: valuation, terms, dilution, board composition, actual value-add beyond capital, runway math, comparison to what a competitive process would produce. Track 2 — name the biases. Scarcity: the seven-day deadline is designed to prevent you from testing the market. Social proof: the other founders' endorsements are being staged. Liking: the partner has been extraordinarily charming, which is also their job. Authority: the firm's brand is doing heavy lifting. Commitment: you've already told your team "we're close on a round" and your consistency bias is pulling you toward closing. Reciprocation: the partner has spent considerable time with you and you feel a kind of obligation. That's six, all pointing at yes.
**Result:** Track 1 might say the deal is good. Track 2 says the conditions are exactly those that produce rushed decisions. The response is to decouple the two. Either the terms are good enough to survive a two-week market test, or they're not good enough to accept. If the VC refuses to extend the deadline, Track 2 has told you what Track 1 was hiding — the preemptive is priced for exactly the founder who doesn't run Track 2. Decline or negotiate from the Track 2 awareness.

**Situation:** An acquisition. Your CFO presents a model showing the synergies from acquiring a smaller competitor for $40M will yield 3x return over five years.
**Application:** Track 1: synergy model, integration cost, debt service, opportunity cost of the $40M, sensitivity analysis. Track 2 — name the biases. Incentive-caused bias: the CFO and VP Corp Dev both advance their careers when the deal closes, not when it's refused. Social proof: competitors in the space are consolidating. Commitment: you've spent three months on this and twenty hours of leadership time. Deprival super-reaction: if you don't close, someone else might. Overconfidence: the synergy model assumes you are in the top quartile of integration outcomes, which statistically you are not. That's five biases, all pushing yes. Lollapalooza.
**Result:** Track 1 alone would say buy. Track 2 says the yes is being manufactured by the exact conditions that produce overpayment in acquisitions. The correction: mandatory delay, independent financial review, re-rank against other uses of $40M with equal seriousness, and a go/no-go where the default is no unless affirmatively yes. Most of the disastrous acquisitions in business history were Track 1 decisions with zero Track 2 audit.

**Situation:** Your team comes to you with a product feature they want to ship. They are unanimous. They have spent three months on it. The metrics from the beta look good.
**Application:** Track 1: actual metric quality, sample size, counterfactual, cost to maintain the feature long-term, opportunity cost of the team's attention. Track 2 — name the biases. Commitment/consistency: three months of sunk cost makes it very hard for the team to recommend killing it. Selection bias in beta: the beta users are the most engaged, not representative. Social proof within the team: unanimity in a team that worked together for three months on one thing is evidence of groupthink, not of quality. Authority: the PM who championed it would lose face if it didn't ship. That's four biases, all pushing ship.
**Result:** Unanimity plus sunk cost plus biased sampling is not a signal to ship — it's a signal that Track 2 was not run. The move is to run Track 2 explicitly, with the team, out loud. Often the team sees it when you frame it right. Sometimes you ship anyway, correctly. Sometimes you kill the feature and save a year of cleanup. Either way, you made the decision in the light instead of the dark.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run Track 2 in a perfunctory way. "I considered the biases and decided they weren't a factor."
**Why:** That sentence is itself the bias. The genuine Track 2 move is to name specific biases, operating in specific ways, on this specific decision. If you can't name three, you haven't done it. You've performed it, and performed Track 2 is worse than no Track 2 because it buys false confidence.

**Don't:** Use Track 2 only on other people's decisions.
**Why:** The bias is always easier to see in the other person's reasoning. Your own biases are invisible to you by design — that's how biases work. The discipline is to run Track 2 on yourself first, every time, before running it on anyone else. The founder who audits everyone else's biases and none of his own is the most biased person in the room, and he will not know it.

**Don't:** Act on Track 1 when Track 2 is loaded and pointing the same direction.
**Why:** That's the lollapalooza condition. Four biases agreeing is not additive, it's multiplicative — the brain crosses a threshold into a cult-commitment state and clear thinking stops. The protection is structural delay, external challenge, and a process change, not another look at the spreadsheet.

**Don't:** Treat Track 2 as "soft." It isn't.
**Why:** Psychology explains more business outcomes than economics does. Dismissing Track 2 as soft is itself one of the twenty-five tendencies — the tendency to denigrate what you don't easily measure. The disasters are psychological, almost always. Call them economic in the postmortem if you want, but the causal chain started in someone's head.

**Don't:** Skip Track 2 because the decision is urgent.
**Why:** Urgent is exactly when Track 2 is most loaded. Scarcity, commitment, and authority all intensify under time pressure. The move under urgency is not to skip the audit but to compress it — name three biases in ninety seconds, apply the correction, decide. Faster Track 2, not no Track 2.

**Don't:** Stop Track 2 after you've named the biases.
**Why:** Naming is not the work. The work is the correction — adjusting the Track 1 numbers or the decision structure based on what Track 2 revealed. If Track 2 identifies that the advisor is paid to close the deal, the correction is discounting his recommendation by a specific amount or bringing in an unconflicted second opinion. Name plus correct. Naming alone is just a more sophisticated form of rationalization.

Rational AND psychological, every decision involving people. Skip either one and the other one is wrong. That's how it works.
