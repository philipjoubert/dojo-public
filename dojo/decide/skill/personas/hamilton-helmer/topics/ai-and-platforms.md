# AI, Platforms, and the Persistence of Power

## When to Use
- An AI company is claiming defensibility from proprietary models, data advantages, or compute scale.
- A platform business exhibits characteristics of multiple Power types simultaneously and you need to identify which is causal.
- You are evaluating whether a company's advantage will survive the commoditization pressure that typically accompanies a maturing technology wave.
- You are deciding whether to build Power as a platform or as a participant on someone else's platform.
- A founder is arguing that AI "changes the rules" and the old frameworks no longer apply.

## Fails When
- Applied to technology in the abstract without a specific business model — Technology creates the opportunity for Power. Technology does not itself produce Power. Without a specific configuration of business model, customer relationship, and competitive context, the framework has nothing to evaluate.
- Used predictively rather than diagnostically — The goal is not to predict which AI companies will win. It is to provide the strategic lens through which defensibility claims can be rigorously evaluated.
- The analyst conflates impressive capability with structural advantage — AI can do remarkable things. Impressive capability is Benefit. It is not Barrier.

## Core Concept

Every generation of business observers encounters a technology wave that appears to invalidate prior strategic wisdom. The microprocessor, the personal computer, the Internet, mobile computing, and now artificial intelligence. Each wave brings breathless claims of new rules, that the old frameworks no longer apply, that the companies born of the new technology will operate by entirely new laws of competitive dynamics. These claims contain a kernel of truth embedded in a much larger falsehood. The kernel of truth is that new technology creates immense flux — new markets, new business models, new competitive configurations. The falsehood is that the structural determinants of business value somehow change. They do not. The same seven configurations that produce persistent significant differential returns in the age of microprocessors produce them in the age of artificial intelligence. Technology creates the opportunity for Power. Technology does not replace the requirement for it. The seven Power types have proven exhaustive across approximately four hundred strategy cases spanning mainframes, microprocessors, personal computers, the Internet, mobile, and cloud. If a new Power type is identified, it can be added to the framework, provided it fulfills the Fundamental Equation of Strategy and the definition of Power as a condition creating the potential for persistent significant differential returns. Until then, the discipline is to apply the existing seven rigorously.

Artificial intelligence has produced extraordinary advances in capability — language understanding, image generation, protein folding, autonomous navigation. The commercial potential is vast. But commercial potential and strategic Power are distinct phenomena. The former concerns market scale on the left-hand side of the Fundamental Equation. The latter concerns the differential margin on the right-hand side. A vast market without Power produces commodity returns. The question for every AI company is not "Is the technology impressive?" but "Which of the seven Powers does this business hold, or have a credible route to establishing?" The most common claim is that an AI company possesses a defensible algorithm. In my experience, I have never seen an algorithm be defensible. The reason is structural: you can do math a lot of different ways. An algorithm that achieves a specific outcome can almost always be matched or approximated through alternative architectures, different training procedures, or alternative mathematical approaches. Algorithms are tools, and tools are imitable. They are Benefits without Barriers. The closely related claim is data network effects — that as an AI system processes more data, it improves, creating a self-reinforcing advantage. This claim almost always fails the non-linearity test within the 3 S's. As data scales, the additional incremental benefit becomes less and less, and eventually the curve gets rather flat. If enough competitors reach that flat zone, the data advantage is not material. The Netflix recommendation engine is technically impressive, clearly beneficial, and probably not strategic, because competitors can achieve similar recommendation quality with somewhat less data.

Where, then, might AI companies establish genuine Power? The most promising route is Counter-Positioning. An AI-native company that adopts a fundamentally different business model — one that would cause collateral damage to incumbents if adopted — can establish the same structural advantage that Vanguard held over Fidelity or Netflix held over HBO. The incumbent's profitable legacy business becomes the Barrier that prevents response. A second route is Switching Costs. AI systems that become deeply embedded in customer workflows — integrated into how decisions are made, how processes are structured, how teams operate — can create financial, procedural, and relational switching costs that function identically to those of enterprise software. These must be built during the right window: during Takeoff, when customers are focused on "Can I get this?" rather than on negotiating switching-friendly terms. A third route, available to very few, is Cornered Resource: preferential access to a unique dataset, a singular team, or a proprietary input at terms that do not fully reflect its value. The Cornered Resource must be secured during Origination, before the value is widely known. Once transparent, pricing arbitrage eliminates the Power. Compute or model scale is occasionally framed as a Scale Economies vector, and the honest question is whether the underlying cost structure is truly decreasing in a defensible way. In some configurations — where fixed training costs are enormous relative to marginal inference costs and where model capability genuinely improves with scale — Scale Economies may be present. More often, the cost structure is approximately linear in compute, and rapid improvements in model efficiency compress any temporary scale advantage. The open-model dynamic, in which capable foundation models are released openly and rapidly approach the frontier, is the commoditization of capability in real time. Capabilities that can be matched by an open model within a year, almost by definition, are not the source of Power.

Platform businesses — those that connect two or more user groups in a way that creates value through their interaction — frequently exhibit characteristics of multiple Power types simultaneously: Scale Economies in infrastructure, Network Economies in user interaction, and Switching Costs in user integration. The diagnostic challenge is identifying which is the cause and which is the effect. Amazon's retail marketplace provides the most instructive example. The conventional narrative holds that Amazon possesses powerful network effects: more buyers attract more sellers, more sellers attract more buyers. This narrative is not wrong, but it is incomplete in a way that leads to diagnostic error. The more precise analysis is that the so-called network effect is actually an effect of the Power in scale of the infrastructure underneath. Amazon's distribution network, fulfillment centers, logistics systems, and technology platform exhibit massive Scale Economies — fixed costs that decline precipitously on a per-unit basis as volume increases. The network effect in the marketplace is a consequence of this scale infrastructure, not an independent source of Power. The distinction matters because it identifies the true Barrier: not the network of buyers and sellers, which can in principle be replicated, but the vast physical and technological infrastructure, which cannot be replicated without enormous, high-risk capital investment. Google's search business is a more nuanced case. For common queries, any competent search engine delivers adequate results. For tail queries — the long tail of unusual searches — the engine that has processed more prior queries has a compounding data advantage in relevance and accuracy. This creates a form of indirect Network Economy where more users generate more query data, which improves results, which attracts more users. Bing could not match this even at comparable average quality, because the tail-query advantage required a volume of diverse search behavior that only Google possessed. Platform Power and participant Power are distinct questions. A participant on a platform inherits the platform's economics but does not typically inherit the platform's Power. Sellers on Amazon, developers on iOS, creators on YouTube must each build their own Power within the constraints the platform imposes — and the platform holder retains the right to adjust those constraints, which is itself a form of Switching Costs plus fiat against participants.

## How to Apply

For any AI company, move directly past the algorithm and the capability demonstration to the structural configuration of the business. Enumerate every competitor, including open-model competitors and well-funded potential entrants. For each, test the seven Power types against the 3 S's. The algorithm itself, however sophisticated, is a Benefit. The question is which of the seven Barriers surrounds the algorithm. Counter-Positioning — is the business model one that incumbents cannot adopt without damaging a profitable legacy business? Switching Costs — are customers becoming sufficiently integrated into workflow that leaving would carry real financial, procedural, or relational cost? Cornered Resource — is there preferential access to a unique input, dataset, or team at non-arbitraged terms? Scale Economies — is the cost structure genuinely decreasing in a way that a well-capitalized competitor cannot replicate, and is the scale advantage large enough to deter share-gain attempts? Network Economies — does the network effect survive the non-linearity test, or does the benefit curve flatten at a scale that competitors can reach? Test data network effect claims with a flowchart. Map the benefit curve. Identify the flat zone. If competitors can reach the flat zone, the advantage is not material.

For platform businesses, trace the causal chain of competitive advantage to its source before attributing Power to any single mechanism. Is the network effect independent, or a symptom of underlying Scale Economies in infrastructure? Does the platform's advantage persist at the margin — at tail queries, at rare use cases, at the extremes of user behavior — or does it flatten at the common case? Is the installed base defensible through Switching Costs, or do users multi-home freely? Each answer points to a different Power type, or to no Power at all. Diagnose whether the platform's Power extends to adjacencies. Microsoft eroded Slack's position in communication not through superior product design but through the extension of existing platform Power — leveraging Network Economies and Switching Costs from the enterprise productivity installed base into an adjacent product category. The Power umbrella extended into communication because the underlying Barrier applied there too.

For participants on a platform, recognize that your Power is separate from the platform's Power and subject to the platform's ability to alter the terms. Build participant Power that survives platform constraint changes. Counter-Positioning against non-platform incumbents, Switching Costs with your direct customers, and Cornered Resources secured outside the platform's control are the viable routes.

Match the Power-building strategy to the phase of the technology wave. Fundamental technologies flow through an economy in phases. Silicon first manifested in discrete chips, then in devices, then in applications. Each phase created a distinct business opportunity with a distinct Power structure. Intel's microprocessor Power was orthogonal to Microsoft's operating system Power, which was orthogonal to Netflix's content Power. All were enabled by the same underlying technology flowing through successive phases. Netflix could not have launched streaming at its founding in 1997 because the enabling technologies — storage, Internet bandwidth, device proliferation — were not yet mature. The company that recognizes the approaching viability of a new phase and positions itself to establish Power as that phase enters Takeoff captures the value. The company that arrives too early burns capital waiting. The company that arrives too late finds the windows closed. AI is currently flowing through its early phases. The first phase — AI infrastructure — has produced companies building chips, cloud platforms, and foundational models. The second phase — AI-enabled tools and devices — is producing companies embedding AI into specific applications. The third phase — AI-driven transformation of entire industries — will produce companies that leverage AI to restructure industry economics in ways that create new Power configurations. Identify the phase you are operating in, and match your Power candidate to the windows available in that phase.

## Examples

**Situation:** An AI company claims defensibility from a proprietary model trained on a proprietary dataset.
**Application:** Move past the model. Ask what prevents a well-capitalized competitor from training a comparable model with a comparable dataset, or from reaching the flat zone of the data-benefit curve. Check the open-model commoditization trajectory — if capable open models are approaching the frontier within a year, the proprietary model is not the Barrier. Test the dataset against the Cornered Resource criteria: is it genuinely idiosyncratic, is it priced below its value, is it sufficient on its own to produce differential returns?
**Result:** In most cases, the answer is that the model and data constitute Benefits without Barriers. Power, if it exists, must come from somewhere else — typically Switching Costs from workflow integration, Counter-Positioning against a legacy incumbent model, or a genuine Cornered Resource in input.

**Situation:** Amazon's retail marketplace.
**Application:** Trace the causal chain. Is the network effect the source of Power, or is it downstream of Scale Economies in the underlying infrastructure? Amazon's distribution network, fulfillment centers, logistics systems, and technology platform exhibit massive fixed costs that decline precipitously on a per-unit basis as volume increases.
**Result:** The so-called network effect is the effect of Power in the scale of the infrastructure underneath. The true Barrier is the infrastructure, which cannot be replicated without enormous high-risk capital investment. The diagnosis directs strategic investment to the infrastructure rather than to network-growth tactics.

**Situation:** Microsoft Teams versus Slack.
**Application:** Evaluate whether Teams' success derived from superior product design or from the extension of existing platform Power. Microsoft possessed a vast installed base of enterprise users on its productivity platform, with Network Economies from the application ecosystem and Switching Costs from enterprise integration. Teams extended this platform into a communication product.
**Result:** Teams eroded Slack's position not primarily through product superiority but through platform Power extension — the Power umbrella reaching into an adjacent category. Slack's product quality could not compensate for the structural disadvantage of competing against an extended platform Power.

**Situation:** Google search against Bing.
**Application:** For common queries, any competent search engine delivers adequate results. The diagnostic question is whether Google's advantage persists at the margin. For tail queries — unusual, infrequent searches — the engine with more prior query data has a compounding advantage in relevance. This creates an indirect Network Economy: more users generate more query data, which improves results, which attracts more users.
**Result:** Bing could not match Google at comparable average quality because the tail-query advantage required a volume of diverse search behavior that only Google possessed. The Barrier is the compounding tail-query data advantage, not the surface-level scale of search infrastructure.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept "we have a proprietary model" as defensibility.
**Why:** You can do math a lot of different ways. An algorithm that achieves a specific outcome can almost always be matched through alternative approaches. Models are tools. Tools are imitable. They are Benefits without Barriers.

**Don't:** Accept "we have a data moat" without mapping the benefit curve.
**Why:** As data scales, the additional incremental benefit becomes less and less, and eventually gets rather flat. If enough competitors reach the flat zone, the data advantage is not material. Data network effect claims almost always fail the non-linearity test.

**Don't:** Treat compute scale as automatic Scale Economies.
**Why:** Scale Economies require a cost structure that is genuinely decreasing in a defensible way and a scale differential large enough to deter share-gain attempts. Compute costs are often approximately linear, and model efficiency improvements compress temporary advantages. Test the actual cost structure before attributing Power to scale.

**Don't:** Conflate platform Power with participant Power.
**Why:** Participants inherit the platform's economics but not its Power, and are subject to the platform holder's ability to alter terms. Build participant Power that survives platform constraint changes — Switching Costs with your direct customers, Cornered Resources outside the platform's control.

**Don't:** Assume technology waves invalidate the framework.
**Why:** Technology creates flux, and flux is precisely the condition from which Power emerges. But the structural requirements for Power do not change. Competition remains an arbitraging force. Benefits remain common. Barriers remain rare. The businesses that endure across technology waves are those that established one or more of the seven configurations of Power — the same seven that have defined strategic advantage for as long as competitive markets have existed.
