# The Power Progression

## When to Use
- You need to decide whether it is still possible to establish a given Power type in a given business at a given moment.
- You are evaluating a company that is growing rapidly but cannot name the Barrier that will protect its margins when growth slows.
- You are considering entering, acquiring into, or investing in a mature market and need to assess whether the structural windows have closed.
- You are a founder in Takeoff and trying to decide between maximizing current growth metrics and winning the share race that will determine Power.
- You hold Power from one era and are trying to diagnose whether it is eroding while a new window opens for a different Power type.

## Fails When
- The business cannot be honestly placed in a growth stage — The Power Progression is keyed to business (not industry) growth rate, using the roughly 30 to 40 percent annual unit growth break between Takeoff and Stability. Without a credible stage diagnosis, the framework yields nothing.
- Treated as a product life cycle relabeling — Introduction/Growth/Maturity/Decline uses different breakpoints and different analytical purposes. Origination can include extended pre-product periods. Stability features considerable growth and overlaps what the product life cycle would call Growth, Maturity, and early Decline. Confusing the two leads to categorical errors about which Powers remain available.
- Used to justify complacency during Takeoff — rapid growth with attractive financials can generate precisely the false positive The Power Progression is designed to expose. Absent an established Barrier, the favorable numbers reflect flux, not Power.
- Applied across businesses as if Power transfers — Power is specific to a strategically separate economic activity. Existing Power from one business does not carry into a new one, which must begin at Origination regardless of how established the parent company is elsewhere.

## Core Concept

The Power Progression is a mapping of each of the seven Power types to the specific growth stage during which it can first be established. This mapping is not an empirical pattern stumbled upon through observation. It follows directly from the structural requirements of each Power type's Barrier mechanism. Three stages — parsed by business growth rate — define the opportunity set.

Origination is the period before compelling value has been achieved. Sales are minimal or nonexistent. The product is taking shape. The market is not yet proven. In this stage, two Power types are available: Counter-Positioning and Cornered Resource. Takeoff is the period of explosive growth, roughly greater than 30 to 40 percent annual unit growth. Customers are flooding in and the market is fluid. In this stage, three Power types are available: Scale Economies, Network Economies, and Switching Costs. Stability is the period after growth has slowed below that threshold. The market has matured and competitive positions are largely established. In this stage, the two remaining Power types — Process Power and Branding — can first emerge.

The critical and practically consequential insight is that if unrealized during the appropriate stage, these Power opportunities typically disappear forever afterward. The window opens, and then it closes. Counter-Positioning cannot be retrofitted onto a market after takeoff — the business model that causes collateral damage to incumbents must have been in place to produce the compelling value that drove takeoff in the first place. A Cornered Resource cannot be purchased once the market has priced its value — the mispricing that permits Power exists only before the resource's worth is widely known. Scale Economies, Network Economies, and Switching Costs all share a structural requirement for differential market position, and differential share can only be gained on favorable terms during the high-flux period when customers are focused on "Can I get it?" rather than "What is the best deal?" Process Power and Branding are both hysteresis barriers — decades of bottom-up process evolution, years of consistent brand cultivation — and cannot be rushed into the compressed timeframe of Takeoff.

This introduces an element of irreversibility into strategic thinking that conventional frameworks lack. Competitive advantage is not something that can be built at any time given sufficient resources and determination. There are critical moments, structurally determined by the growth stage of the business, when decisions are made that inexorably shape a company's trajectory. The strategic question at every stage is not whether you are growing or executing well, but whether you are establishing the specific Barrier whose window is currently open.

## How to Apply

Begin by diagnosing the stage of the business. Use business growth rate — not industry narratives, not product maturity, not management's preferred framing. If unit growth is negligible and compelling value has not yet been achieved, the business is in Origination. If unit growth exceeds roughly 30 to 40 percent per year, the business is in Takeoff. Below that threshold, it is in Stability.

With the stage established, identify which Power types remain available. In Origination, ask two questions. First: is the business model configured to cause collateral damage to incumbents — would a competent incumbent's rational response to adopt this model harm their existing business more than ignoring it? That is the test for Counter-Positioning. Second: is there a coveted, value-enhancing asset that can be secured on terms that do not fully reflect its future worth — a patent, a property right, a founding team whose future collaboration cannot yet be priced? That is the test for Cornered Resource. In Takeoff, ask: are you winning the share race on terms favorable enough that the resulting advantage will survive competitive arbitrage? The specific diagnostic depends on the underlying Barrier. For Scale Economies, the question is whether the volume lead will translate into a structural per-unit cost gap that competitors cannot close. For Network Economies, the question is whether the installed base differential is pushing competitors toward the point where they effectively throw in the towel. For Switching Costs, the question is whether customers are being acquired before the lifetime value of the relationship is fully priced into acquisition economics. In Stability, ask: are you cultivating, patiently and continuously, the processes or brand associations whose value derives from the time and scale that only this stage permits?

Operational excellence matters enormously during Takeoff in a way it does not during Stability. This is the critical resolution of an apparent contradiction. In Statics, operational excellence is not strategic — it can be matched. In Dynamics, during the compressed timeframe of Takeoff, execution quality directly determines whether Power is established before the window closes. Intel's Operation Crush — the aggressive sales and marketing blitz that secured the IBM PC design win — would have been mere competitive noise in Stability. In the microprocessor Takeoff, it was decisive. Apple's botched Apple III, a recoverable error in any other period, was terminal in the PC Takeoff.

The Mantra — a route to continuing Power in significant markets — includes the word "continuing" deliberately. The discipline is not one-time. Technology shifts can sweep away Switching Costs. Competitive dynamics can compress Scale Economy intensity. New business models can neutralize Counter-Positioning as the collateral damage diminishes. Periodically ask: is the Barrier of our current Power type eroding? When one begins to weaken, you must already be establishing the next, during whatever window is currently open.

## Examples

**Situation:** Cromemco in the late 1970s was the largest manufacturer of S-100 bus computers, growing at triple-digit rates, cash-flow positive, operationally impressive by every conventional metric.
**Application:** The business was in Takeoff in the emerging personal computer market. The Powers available to it were Scale Economies, Network Economies, and Switching Costs. The question was not whether Cromemco was growing — it clearly was — but whether it was establishing a specific Barrier that would survive the market's eventual maturation.
**Result:** Cromemco established no Power during its Takeoff window. When the PC shakeout arrived and the market entered Stability, competitive arbitrage did its work and the favorable early returns proved fleeting. Attractive financials were the flux of rapid growth, not the presence of structural advantage.

**Situation:** Intel in 1969 reacquired microprocessor rights from Busicom, three years before the personal computer Takeoff began.
**Application:** The microprocessor's future value was uncertain and therefore mispriced. Securing preferential access on terms that did not reflect its eventual worth established a Cornered Resource during Origination — the only stage in which such pricing could exist.
**Result:** The Cornered Resource gave way, during Takeoff, to a Switching Costs Power built on the IBM PC design win secured through Operation Crush. Software was written to Intel's specific chip architecture and rewriting for a different architecture was prohibitive. Over the following decade and a half, as software was gradually abstracted from chip-level specifics and Switching Costs eroded, Scale Economies — established during the same Takeoff — took over. One Power type succeeded another, each established during its appropriate window. A single design win, then a decade and a half of very high Switching Costs, then Scale Economies.

**Situation:** Tiffany, Toyota.
**Application:** Tiffany's Branding was cultivated over 178 years. Toyota's Production System was fashioned bottom-up over decades. Both are hysteresis-based Barriers that could not have been compressed into the Takeoff stage of their respective industries — the time constants are unyielding and require sustained operating history to accumulate.
**Result:** These are the archetypal Stability-stage Powers. They validate the structural claim that Process Power and Branding cannot be rushed and cannot be bought; they can only be earned through the patient investment that only Stability permits.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat a well-heeled competitor entering your market during Takeoff as welcome validation.
**Why:** In Takeoff, you and your competitor are in a race for relative scale, and the structure typically supports only one winner on favorable terms. Apple's 1981 Wall Street Journal advertisement — "Welcome, IBM. Seriously." — reflected exactly this misunderstanding. IBM's entry, coupled with Intel's microprocessor scale and Microsoft's operating system, was not market validation but a direct threat to Apple's ability to establish Scale Economies in personal computing. Apple's share declined for decades afterward.

**Don't:** Pursue a Power type outside its window.
**Why:** Each Power's Barrier mechanism is tied to conditions that obtain only during a specific stage. Attempting to build Branding during Origination, when brand associations cannot yet have accumulated, or Scale Economies during Stability, when the cost of gaining share has already been arbitraged, is structurally impossible. Resources spent pursuing an out-of-sequence Power are resources not spent on the Power that is actually available.

**Don't:** Mistake attractive Takeoff financials for a solved strategic problem.
**Why:** If a company has not established Power, competitive arbitrage will catch up as soon as growth slows. The question is not whether the financials look attractive today but whether you can name the specific Barrier that will protect your position when the market enters Stability. If you cannot name it, your attractive numbers are temporary.

**Don't:** Assume existing Power transfers to a new market or new business.
**Why:** Power is specific to a strategically separate economic activity. Netflix's DVD-by-mail Counter-Positioning over Blockbuster did not transfer to streaming, where the industry economics and potential competitors were orthogonal. Any new business begins at Origination, regardless of the parent company's standing in its existing business.
