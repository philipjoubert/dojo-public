# The Cookie Jar

## When to Use
- Mile 81 of a 100-mile race you signed up for with three days' notice. Pee is bloody. Feet are fractured. Pacer just told you you're not going to make the cutoff. The voice in your head is saying *quit, this is the rational move, you tried*. That moment, exactly. Reach in.
- Two-thirds into a hard project — a book, a degree, a launch, a recovery — when the new has worn off and the finish line is nowhere in sight and the body and the mind are both begging to stop.
- Pull-up 2,500 of an attempted 4,030. Round four of a Hurt 100. Hour 18 of a 24-hour test. Every place where the easy answer is *I gave it my best*, and you know that's a lie.
- After a long stretch of comfort and wins. You've forgotten you're a savage. You need to remember.
- When somebody else, or your own brain, is telling you you can't do the next hard thing. The jar is the receipts that prove you've done hard before.

## Fails When
- **You've never overcome anything yet.** The Cookie Jar runs on deposits. If you genuinely cannot remember a single time you fought through something hard and won — even small — your job is not the jar. Your job is to go make a deposit. Run the mile you don't want to run. Have the conversation you don't want to have. Do the rep. Then you'll have a cookie. Then come back to the jar.
- **You're trying to fill it with other people's victories.** *I'm inspired by Kobe* is not a cookie. *I'm grateful for my family* is not a cookie. The jar is the archive of moments where *you* were the person who didn't quit. Anybody else's win is not currency in your jar. Build your own.
- **You're using it instead of training.** The jar is the bank you draw from when the work is already going and the brain wants to fold. It is not a substitute for the work itself. You can't sit at home eating cookies and expect to perform. Put the work in. Then the cookies fuel the work when the work tries to break you.
- **You're using it to gloat.** *Hooray for me* sessions don't count. The Cookie Jar is operational, not narrative. It's a weapon you use mid-fight, not a slideshow you run for friends.

## Core Concept

San Diego One Day. November. Signed up three days earlier for a 24-hour race — 100 miles in 24 hours to qualify for Badwater. Had not trained for distance running. Never run more than 20 miles in my life. By mile 70 I was peeing blood. Stress fractures in both feet. Ribs cracked. My pacer Kate looked at me at 81 miles and told me, plainly, no fluff, *I don't believe you're gonna make the time at this pace*. She was right. Walking 20-minute miles. 19 to go, four hours to do it. Zombie.

That's the moment. That's the moment everybody's brain has its own version of. Where the rational, sensible move is to lay down. Where every metric agrees you're done.

I asked myself why. *Because you are one hard man*, came the answer. Said it out loud. Felt something turn over. Then the brain started flipping through the file. Fourth-grade reader to senior level in six months. Cookie. Passed the ASVAB on attempt three. Cookie. 297 pounds down to 191 in three months to qualify for BUD/S. Cookie. Hell Week — twice more, after pneumonia and stress fractures sent me back to start. Cookies loaded with chocolate chunks. Army Ranger School Enlisted Honor Man. Cookie.

I wasn't *remembering* those. That's not what the Cookie Jar is. I was *tapping back into the emotional state I felt during those victories*. Adrenaline came online. Sympathetic nervous system kicked on. The pain didn't disappear — it stayed — but it came in waves instead of a wall, because the brain was busy somewhere else. Mile 82. 90. 95. Hit 100 at 18 hours, 56 minutes. Ran one more lap to make sure. Finished 101.

So here's what the jar actually is: a *literal mental archive of the hardest moments you have personally overcome*. Not your kid's first steps. Not your favorite team's championship. Yours. Every time you fought through something hard and won — small, medium, or earth-shattering — that's a cookie. Stuttering as a kid and learning to speak in front of the room. Cookie. Quitting smoking. Cookie. The divorce. Running your first 5K at 240 pounds. The day you stopped drinking. The night you walked back into the gym after eight years off.

You stuff the jar before you need it. Sit with a journal, write them out — the small, the embarrassingly small, the ones nobody else even noticed. Feel each one. Stack the kindling. Because the day comes — and it always comes — when the brain wants to fold and you need fuel you can't manufacture in the moment.

The brain doesn't want to go there at first. The brain in distress wants to remind you you're suffering and the goal is impossible. You have to *make* the brain reach in. *No. We've done hard before. Pull a cookie. Move.* One cookie buys you the next mile, not the whole race. Then the next mile on the next cookie. The jar refills as you work — every mile you survive becomes tomorrow's cookie.

A rocket engine doesn't fire without a small spark first. You don't start a bonfire with a tree stump — you start with witch's hair, dry grass, small sticks. Small fires light bigger fires. *I lost five pounds the first week* led to *I lost a hundred pounds in three months*. *I read one paragraph* led to senior reading level. None of the big cookies exist without the small ones underneath.

And the jar is *yours*. Not mine. Not your dad's. Not somebody's on YouTube. The moments where *you* were the one who didn't quit. That's why it works — in the moment of greatest doubt, the only voice your brain trusts is the voice of your own past.

## How to Apply

1. **Take inventory. Write the cookies down.** Sit with a journal — paper, not a screen — and list every time in your life you fought through something hard and came out the other side. Big and small. Stuttering as a kid. The terrifying job interview you didn't think you'd survive. The night you walked away from drinking. The marathon you finished in pain. The semester you nearly failed and didn't. *Don't filter for impressive*. The small ones are kindling and you need them. By the end you should have a list that takes pages.

2. **Feel each one. Don't just label it.** This is not bullet points. For each cookie, sit with it long enough to remember the *physical state* — the heart rate, the doubt, the moment it turned, the relief and pride afterward. The jar works because of state, not story. If you can't feel it, it's not in the jar yet.

3. **Stack the small fires under the big ones.** Lose-five-pounds-the-first-week sits in the jar right next to the hundred-pound transformation. Don't throw the small one out. The small one is what *proved* the big one was possible.

4. **When the quitting voice shows up, force the brain to reach in.** The brain will not do this on its own — when you're hurting, the brain wants to focus on the hurting. You have to *make* it. Out loud, internally, in writing — whatever the channel — direct your attention into the jar. *We have done hard before. Get a cookie. Now.* Pull one. Live in it for a second. Then take the next step.

5. **One cookie buys one segment, not the whole race.** Don't try to run 19 more miles on a single cookie. Pull one. It buys you the next mile. When that mile is done, pull another one. The jar is bottomless if you've done the deposits — use it that way.

6. **Refill in real time.** Every mile you survive becomes a new cookie. The mile you just ran when you wanted to quit *is itself* tomorrow's cookie. The bigger the fight you're in right now, the more chocolate-chunk cookies you're depositing for next time. That's why you don't quit in the middle — every step past the wall is a deposit, and you'll need it in two months when the next thing comes.

7. **Use it for everything, not just physical.** The Cookie Jar is not just for ultras. Pull a cookie when the boss tries to break you in a meeting. Pull a cookie before the conversation you've been dreading. Pull a cookie when the page won't write itself at 2 a.m. and the document is empty. Anywhere your brain wants to fold, the jar is the move.

8. **Don't share the jar.** This is private. The moment it becomes a social media flex it loses its operational power. The cookies are weapons, not trophies. Keep the jar for the fight.

## Examples

**Situation:** Goggins, mile 81 of the San Diego One Day, 24-hour race. Three days' notice. No distance training. Bloody urine. Stress-fractured feet. Pacer Kate just told him he's not going to make the cutoff. Walking 20-minute miles. (*Can't Hurt Me*, Chapter 6, "Driven to Win.")

**Application:** Asked himself why. Yelled back *Because you are one hard man*. Started flipping through the archive. The boy who taught himself to read. The man who passed the ASVAB on attempt three. The 297-pound exterminator who dropped 106 pounds in 3 months. Hell Week. Two more Hell Weeks. Ranger School. He didn't replay them as memories — he tapped back into the emotional state of each one. Sympathetic nervous system kicked on. Adrenaline took over.

**Result:** Pace picked up. Mile 82, 90, 95. Hit 100 miles in 18 hours and 56 minutes. Ran one more lap to be sure — finished 101 miles total. Collapsed in his bathtub afterward in kidney failure. He didn't run the last 19 miles on a single cookie. He ran them on twenty cookies, one mile at a time. The jar is operational that way.

---

**Situation:** Goggins on the pull-up record attempt. First attempt failed. Second attempt failed. Third attempt — Brentwood Hills CrossFit, live broadcast. Hands torn open. Body breaking down. Knew the failure was waiting if he stopped digging.

**Application:** Reached into the jar mid-set. The first failed attempt was now itself a cookie — *I went back. I went back again. I came here a third time.* The two prior failures had stopped being failures the moment he went back; they became cookies that fueled the third attempt. Pulled a cookie of Hell Week #3 on broken legs. Pulled a cookie of the 24-hour race. Pulled a cookie of the previous record attempts, because *getting back to the bar after public failure* is itself one of the biggest cookies in the jar.

**Result:** 4,030 pull-ups in 17 hours. New world record. The cookies he ate were the failures that came before. That's the Cookie Jar working backward — the prior pain becomes the fuel for the next attempt. You don't get the record without the failed attempts in the jar.

**Situation:** You. Year three of building a business. Cash is tight. The product still isn't right. The last two launches landed flat. The voice in your head is suggesting it's time to be reasonable, get a job, save the marriage, stop bleeding money. The thing you're trying to build feels further away than it did year one.

**Application:** Sit down tonight. Open a journal. Write the cookies. The first sale you closed when you didn't think you'd ever sell anything. The investor who said no the first time and yes the second. The product feature you spent a weekend on that customers actually use. The conversation with your spouse where you both agreed to give it another year. The night you didn't drink even though you wanted to. The mile you ran when you swore you wouldn't. Stack them. Feel each one. Then tomorrow morning, when the doubt comes back, reach in. Pull one. *I closed that first sale when I didn't think I could. I can write the email I'm avoiding right now.* Send the email. Pull another cookie. Make the next call.

**Result:** You don't run year four on a single cookie. You run today on today's cookie. Tomorrow on tomorrow's. And every brutal day you survive in year three becomes a new chocolate-chunk cookie for year four when it gets harder, because year four will get harder. That's the deal.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Fill the jar with gratitude, affirmations, or other people's victories.
**Why:** *I'm grateful for my family. I'm inspired by my hero. I love my dog.* — none of those are cookies. Those are nice. The Cookie Jar runs on *moments where you personally overcame something hard and won*. Mixing gratitude into the jar dilutes it. When you reach in mid-fight you don't need to remember that your dog loves you. You need the receipt that proves *you* are the kind of motherfucker who doesn't quit. Keep the jar pure.

**Don't:** Use the Cookie Jar as a substitute for putting the work in.
**Why:** The jar is the fuel that gets you through the work, not the work itself. You can sit at home for ten years remembering Hell Week and you will not be in shape. Sit on the couch eating cookies and you'll just be fat with a good imagination. Train. Suffer. Build the body and the mind. Then when the body and the mind try to fold mid-task, the jar is what carries you through.

**Don't:** Make the jar a public performance.
**Why:** *I'm posting my Cookie Jar on Instagram* turns the tool into a brag book. The cookies stop being weapons and start being trophies. You'll find yourself filtering them for impressiveness instead of usefulness. The small kindling cookies — the ones that actually start the fires — will get cut for being unphotogenic, and you'll be left with a jar that looks great and works terribly. Keep the jar private. Keep the work loud.

**Don't:** Wait until you're already broken to start filling the jar.
**Why:** Mile 81 is the wrong time to start your inventory. The deposits get made when life is calm and you have time and clarity to actually feel each cookie and stack it. If you wait until the brain is in distress, you'll grasp at three or four obvious ones and miss the small kindling that actually does the heavy lifting. Stuff the jar now. While it's quiet. So when it gets loud, the jar is full and the hand knows where to go.

**Don't:** Treat one cookie as enough.
**Why:** A single cookie buys you a single segment. The mistake is taking one bite, feeling the rush, and assuming you can ride that rush all the way to the finish. You can't. The brain habituates fast. Pull a cookie, live in it for a moment, take the next step, and when the doubt comes back — and it will, sometimes within minutes — reach in again. Pull another. The jar is bottomless. Use it that way. The 40 percent rule and the Cookie Jar work together: when the governor in your head says *done* at 40 percent, the jar is one of the tools you reach for to push past 50, 60, 80 percent. One cookie at a time, one mile at a time.
