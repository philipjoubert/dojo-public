# Who's Gonna Carry the Boats?

## When to Use
- The team is in a dark stretch and you can feel the room waiting for someone to move first.
- You're asking whether you have the standing to step up, or whether you should wait to be appointed.
- You have people who depend on you — family, employees, teammates — and you're trying to decide whether to "find more" on a day when there's nothing in the tank.
- You're trying to install a culture where the heavy thing gets carried without a manager calling for volunteers.

## Fails When
- **You think it's about being seen carrying the boats.** The cameras aren't on. Nobody hands out the trophy. The carriers in BUD/S Hell Week didn't get a medal at the front of the boat — they got a heavier load. If the question in your head is *will people see this?*, you're not carrying the boats. You're auditioning.
- **You think it's a one-time act.** The boats are heavy every day. The instructors yell the question at every evolution. A leader who carries once and then sits down has misunderstood the doctrine — that's not leadership, that's a photo op.
- **You think it requires you to be loud.** The loudest guy in BUD/S quits first. The actual carriers say almost nothing. They just keep their hands on the boat.
- **You confuse "carry the boats" with "do everyone's job for them."** Picking the boat up off the heads of people who never planned to lift their own weight isn't leadership — that's enablement, and the boat eats you eventually.

## Core Concept

I open every talk with one line. *Who's gonna carry the boats?* The audience yells it back. They yell it loud or they yell it weak, and either way I'm not done with them yet — *that was okay, it wasn't great, but it was okay. One of the best is not the best, but we trying.* You know why I demand that? Not for me. I know what darkness looks like. I know what it feels like to be alone with no motivation, no drive, no passion, and what has to come out of your fucking soul in the darkest of times is what they just did right there. If you can get up every morning with no passion, no drive, no motivation, and still get up with that kind of fire, you will be successful in life. I guarantee you that.

The boats are real. In BUD/S Hell Week your boat crew carries a 200-pound rubber Zodiac on your heads everywhere you go. Mile to chow, mile back. Up the berm, down the berm, into the surf, into the sand. You also carry a 250-pound log between obstacles. You don't get to put it down. The instructors yell *who's gonna carry the boats?* because they know the answer is whoever decides to be the answer. Most guys hope it's the guy next to them. Most guys watch their boat crew get smoked by Psycho Pete and think *please, somebody pick this up.* And in every class there are one, two, maybe three men who hear that question and turn around and put themselves at the front of the boat — the heaviest point — without being told. That's the spot. That's where I put myself in Class 231 with Freak Brown. Two heaviest points, both up front, all week. Wasn't a designation. Wasn't a vote. We just stepped there and stayed there.

That's the doctrine, and it generalizes. In any team — your company, your family, your platoon, your church — the load gets heavy. Some weeks the load is unbearable. The instructors aren't yelling the question out loud anymore, but the question is still being asked, every single day, by life. *Who's gonna carry the boats?* The answer is whoever decides to be that somebody. Not because they were designated. Not because they have the title. Not because they want credit. Because somebody has to, and the room is waiting, and waiting is how teams die. You step up and you put your hands at the heaviest point of the boat. The other guys feel it the second you do. They feel real motivation start to come from your fake motivation, and the boat goes up.

I know what you're thinking. *I'm tired. I've got nothing left. Why me?* Look to your left and look to your right. If you've got a mom, a dad, a wife, a husband, kids — be that motherfucker for your family. Be the one that when fire breaks out in the building, they look at you and they follow you. *Where's Goggins going? I'm following that motherfucker.* That's the job. That's the only job. You may be fine sitting back. You may have just enough money to live, just enough strength to coast. But you have a family, and the day all hell breaks loose, you want them to know that even if you fall on your ass, you can pick them up. And if that's not enough, you go look in the dirty mirror and ask yourself what you're really here for.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop waiting to be appointed.** No instructor is going to walk over to your team and tap you on the shoulder. The whole point of the question is it's asked into the air. The first person to answer is the leader. That person can be you. The longer you wait for permission, the more obvious it is you weren't the answer in the first place.

2. **Put yourself at the heaviest point.** Front of the boat. The hardest call this week. The conversation everyone's avoiding. The customer everyone hopes someone else picks up. The bad news that needs to go to the team. *That's* the front of the boat. If you're standing somewhere lighter, you're not carrying — you're being carried.

3. **Bring fake motivation when you have none.** I had nothing on Wednesday of my second Hell Week. My feet, hands, neck, body — destroyed. Two more days of hell, no sleep, instructors at Boat Crew One yelling pick the boat up. Nobody could pick the boat up. I turned to my five guys behind me and said *don't be a fucking punk.* I started juicing them. I had no juice. I made it up. And false motivation became real motivation in about thirty seconds. The boat went up. We started boat-pressing it. The instructors let us put it down because we'd shown them something nobody else had shown them. That's the trick — you bring the fire you don't have, the team feels it, the fire becomes real, the boat goes up.

4. **Do it on the days nobody is watching.** The boats are heavy on Wednesdays and Fridays both. Most leaders show up for the Friday speech and disappear on the Tuesday hard call. The actual carriers are the ones who pick the boat up when there's no audience and no reward — when no one will know if they sat down. Tuesday is the test.

5. **Carry, don't perform.** The instructors didn't care if you talked about carrying the boat. They cared whether the boat was up. Don't tell the team you're going to lead. Get to the front. Pick it up. Shut up. Let them feel the weight redistribute.

6. **Pick up your people when they fall, even if you're spent.** This is the part that gets glossed in the LinkedIn version. You don't carry the boat for yourself — you carry it because Freak Brown is on the back, Kyle is in the middle, and if you let go, the whole thing crushes them. You make enough so when they fall on their ass, you can pick them up. That's the doctrine. Generational responsibility, not generational genius.

## Examples

**Situation:** Hell Week, Class 231. Wednesday night. Three boat crews left. Hypothermic. Zombie state. The instructors put wet clothes back in the gear crates as a mind game. Boat Crew One can't get the boat up — every time the instructors yell *pick it up*, the bow sags. Boat Crew Two, Goggins's crew, is in the same shape physically: feet, hands, neck — destroyed. Goggins is at the front, the heaviest point.

The slogan itself also appears in interview-guest sources `nDLb8_wgX50.md` (Huberman) and `MZU-m10Fsio.md` (Crisp), where Goggins references it as the line he is known for.)* "I look at my boat crew. I said, 'Look, motherfuckers, don't be a fucking punk. I want you to look at these fucking instructors because they were where we were years ago under these boats. And I want them to think they never seen a boat crew like Boat Crew Two. We are going to fucking take their fucking souls today.' So I start to juice these motherfuckers. So you get juiced up. False motivation becomes real motivation. Now they're starting to feel the juice. So it comes by, 'All right, Boat Crew Two.' But right now, Boat Crew Two has said, 'Fuck it. We're gonna pick the boat up before you tell us to pick the boat up.' So the boat's up. Now we're starting to boat-press it. And all we're saying is *you can't hurt Boat Crew Two.* And our boat crew puts the boat down. They let us put the boat down because we showed something that no one else had."

**Application:** Goggins doesn't wait for the question. He turns around to his five guys before the instructors yell, picks the boat up unprompted, brings false motivation that becomes real motivation, and changes the room. Notice what's not in there: a title, an audience, a moment of personal reward. The other crews still couldn't get their boats up. Goggins's crew got a *rest* — a perverse reward that nobody would have noticed except him.

**Result:** Boat Crew Two becomes the one boat crew the instructors can't break in Class 231. Goggins gets through Hell Week. Decades later he opens every keynote with the chant — *who's gonna carry the boats?* — because he learned in that pool deck moment that whoever picks the boat up first is the one who gets to decide what the day becomes.

Because you've put in enough work that when they fall, you can pick them up.

**Result:** The doctrine transfers off the beach. Carrying the boats means *making yourself into the kind of person whose people don't have to wonder if you'll show up when it gets bad.* That's the whole job.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat *who's gonna carry the boats?* as a leadership platitude — a thing you say at the all-hands and then go back to your office.
*Why:* The whole point is the boats are *physically being carried* every day. If you only say it, you've become the instructor on the beach with a coffee and a clipboard. The boat is being carried by somebody else and you're the one watching. Goggins respected the instructors who'd been under the boats years ago. He had nothing for the ones who only yelled.

**Don't:** Wait for the team to ask you to step up.
*Why:* The whole game is that nobody asks. The instructors yell the question into the air. The room waits. Whoever waits longest loses. If you're saying *I would have stepped up if they'd asked,* you're confessing you weren't the answer. Real carriers don't need a tap on the shoulder.

**Don't:** Be the loud one.
*Why:* In every BUD/S class the loud guys go first. They yell, they posture, they bring fake hardness — and the cold water and the boat and the no-sleep eat them alive. The actual carriers are usually the quiet ones. Freak Brown didn't run his mouth. He just stayed at the front of the boat all week. If your version of leadership is volume, the boat is going to find you out.

**Don't:** Use *carrying the boats* as a way to do everyone else's work and call it virtue.
*Why:* You're not supposed to be carrying the whole boat. There are six of you under it. The doctrine is *you put yourself at the heaviest point and you stay there* — not *you take everyone else's weight off and pretend that's leadership.* If your team learns they can let go because you'll cover, the boat is on you alone in six months and you break. Real boat crews carry. Plural. The leader's job is to make the load shareable, not to swallow it.
