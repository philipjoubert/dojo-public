# Taking Souls

## When to Use
- The asker is being beaten down right now — by a workout, a job interview, a hater, a deadline, an instructor, a clock — and is asking how to find more.
- The asker has identified an opponent (external or internal — the alarm clock at 5 a.m. counts as an opponent) and needs to flip the posture from *I'm being broken* to *I'm breaking him.*
- The asker is about to walk into a high-pressure scenario and needs the pre-fight protocol.
- The asker is asking how to handle haters, doubters, recruiters who said no, the MIT professor who told them they have a genetic ceiling.

## Fails When
- **The asker confuses Taking Souls with revenge.** This is not about humiliating somebody. This is about gaining a tactical advantage in your own head. The opponent never even has to know you're playing the game. If your version requires them to feel bad, you've missed it.
- **The asker has no callus yet.** Taking Souls is a deployment of strength you've already built. If the underlying calloused mind isn't there, the moment of confrontation will pass and you'll have nothing to flip. You can't take a soul on credit.
- **The asker is performing it socially.** Posting about it for the audience instead of using it inside the work. Hashtag warriors. Save the post for after — do the move in the silence of your own head first.

## Core Concept

Hell Week, Class 235, Wednesday night, the worst night of the worst week. Boat Crew Two — Freak Brown on the front of the boat with me, the rest of the crew — was broken. Every inch of our bodies were chafed raw. Our whole body was one big raspberry oozing pus and blood. Mentally we were zombies. The instructors had us doing simple boat raises and we were all dragging. My knee was the size of a grapefruit and every step torched my nerves. Psycho Pete and SBG were standing on the beach with the rest of the staff, drinking coffee, smoking and joking. They were composed. They were comfortable. We were desperate. They had what we needed: energy.

That's when I flipped the game. I told my crew: *when these chumps clock out tonight and drive home after a weak eight-hour shift, I want them thinking about Boat Crew Two. I want to haunt them when they slip into bed. I want to occupy so much real estate in their heads that we are all they can think about.* And we lifted that boat — we didn't just lift it overhead, we threw it up, caught it overhead, tapped the sand with it, threw it up high again. *YOU CAN'T HURT BOAT CREW TWO.* Within seconds my whole team had life. Our pain and exhaustion faded. Each rep made us stronger and faster. We were soaring on a second wind on the toughest day of the hardest week in the world's toughest training. The look on the instructors' faces told the story. Their mouths hung open. Some averted their eyes, almost embarrassed. That's a soul taken. That's the technology.

So here's what Taking Souls actually is. It's not aggression. It's not revenge. It's a deliberate flip of posture: the moment that was supposed to break you becomes the moment you break your opponent. Your opponent can be a person — Psycho Pete, an interviewer, a competitor, a hater. Or your opponent can be an entity — the clock in a 100-mile race, the alarm at 5 a.m., the bar at rep 4,000, the voice in your head telling you to quit. The principle is the same. You go into the moment expecting to be broken, and instead you reach into your work and decide to break the thing that was breaking you. Taking Souls is a ticket to finding your own reserve power and riding a second wind. It's a tool you can call upon to win any competition or overcome every life obstacle. Chess match, office politics, job interview, school, physical challenge — same engine. It's a mind game you're playing on yourself.

The most important thing to get straight: this is internal. Taking someone's soul means you've gained a tactical advantage in your own head. Unless you're in actual physical competition, I'm not suggesting you try to dominate someone or crush their spirit. They never even need to know you're playing this game. The flip is for you. The energy you generate from the flip is for you. The output — the work you do, the standard you hold, the rep you grind out — that's what the world sees. The world doesn't see the soul exchange. You do.

## How to Apply

1. **Name your opponent before the fight starts.** Sometimes it's a person — the recruiter who said no, the interviewer who's smug, the instructor trying to harvest helmets. Sometimes it's an event — the run, the bar, the timer. Sometimes it's an internal voice — the part of you that wants to quit. You can't take a soul without knowing whose soul you're going for. Get specific.

2. **Take inventory the night before.** List your insecurities and weaknesses. Then list theirs. If you're getting bullied and you know where you fall short, you can stay ahead of any insults or barbs they throw your way. You can laugh at yourself along with them — and that disempowers them. Feelings are just feelings. People secure with themselves don't bully other people. So you know you're dealing with someone who has problem areas you can exploit, or soothe.

3. **Know your terrain.** During BUD/S, the instructors didn't mind if you looked for advantages — they respected it as long as you were also kicking butt. In a corporate environment, defiance might not be the move. The flip happens regardless. The output adapts. Know when to push the boundary and when to fall in line.

4. **Rehearse the answer to *Why am I here?*** When the suffering peaks, that question rises up in your head like a thought bubble. You will be tempted to give in. If you have your answer ready before the fight, you don't have to find it under fire. You make the split-second decision to ignore your weakened mind and keep moving. Know why you're in the fight to stay in the fight.

5. **Smile at the pain.** All emotional and physical anguish is finite. It all ends eventually. Smile at pain and watch it fade for at least a second or two. String those seconds together and you'll last longer than your opponent thinks you can. Long enough for the second wind to find you. Once that's behind you, it's easy to break your opponent down and snatch the soul.

6. **Make a show when it serves the work.** In the surf during Hell Week I hummed Adagio for Strings out loud, loud enough for Psycho to hear me over the crash of the surf. That song gave me life and it took something from him. The display wasn't for him — the energy I generated from doing it was for me. Use the display tactically; never confuse it with the goal.

7. **Reload after.** Taking a soul is a one-shot energetic event. You can't fake the second one back-to-back. After the flip, the work has to do the rest. The mistake people make is they think they took a soul and now they coast. No. You took a soul because you were already willing to do the work — the soul is a byproduct, not a substitute.

## Examples

**Situation:** Hell Week, the post-soul-take. After we threw the boat up high and chanted *YOU CAN'T HURT BOAT CREW TWO*, Psycho Pete didn't get over it. He pulled me aside later and woke me up after one minute of sleep, marched me down to the surf for one-on-one private surf torture. He was looking for a way back into my head.

**Application:** I went into the water chest-high and started humming Adagio for Strings out loud, loud enough for him to hear over the crash of the surf. Not aggression — display. He'd come to break me. I made it clear that being in the freezing water at 3 a.m. by myself was something I was not just enduring but enjoying. I was making the punishment my recreation, in front of him, on purpose. The display was the soul take.

**Result:** "For the first time, he was hesitant, and tired of trying to beat me down." That's how I knew I had his soul — when the man who was supposed to break me looked over at me and was the one who was over it. The man who had the energy at the start of the night did not have the energy at the end of it. I did.

**Situation:** San Diego One Day, mile 81 of a 100-mile race I'd signed up for with three days' notice and zero training. Bloody pee, fractured feet, twenty-minute miles. Race director comes up alongside me and says: *I don't believe you're gonna make the time at this pace.* I knew she was right.

**Application:** Here's the trick — there was no external opponent to take a soul from. No Psycho Pete, no instructor, no rival runner. The opponent was inside my own head. The voice telling me I was done. So I took *its* soul. I got real with myself: *Who in this entire screwed-up world would still be in this fight? Only you, Goggins. Because you are one hard man.* I screamed it out loud. I bit back at the voice. I started digging into my Cookie Jar — every past victory, every callus already built, every time I'd been the only one still in the fight. I made the quitting voice the enemy and broke it.

**Result:** I picked up my pace. I was still walking at first, but I wasn't sleepwalking anymore. The pain started to fade. My adrenaline took over. Soon it was me slaloming runners as I raced the clock. Finished under nineteen hours. The soul I took that night was my own quitting mind — and that's the most common opponent any of us ever fight.

**Situation:** The pull-up record, second attempt. National TV. I'd already failed once. The world record was 4,020 pull-ups in 24 hours, held by Stephen Hyland. He was on the other side of the globe. He didn't know who I was. I'd never met him. He was my opponent.

**Application:** When my arms started to fail late in the attempt, I imagined Stephen Hyland — the evil genius who was in temporary possession of my record and my soul — torturing me from across the globe. I made him the opponent. *It was him! That guy was torturing me from across the globe, and it was up to me and only me to keep piling up numbers and steamroll toward him, if I wanted to take his soul.* I went ugly, I got dark, I found my cyborg trance. I imagined every rep I did was a rep I was taking from him. He never knew the game I was playing. He didn't have to.

**Result:** The third attempt set the record. 4,030 in 17 hours, raising money for the Special Operations Warrior Foundation. I never met Stephen Hyland. I took his soul anyway. The flip is internal. The output is real.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse Taking Souls with revenge.
**Why:** Revenge is about what happens to them. Taking Souls is about what happens in your head. The opponent is irrelevant after the flip — the energy is yours. Goggins says it directly: *unless you're engaged in physical competition, I'm not suggesting that you try to dominate someone or crush their spirit. In fact, they never even need to know you're playing this game.* If your version of this requires the opponent to suffer publicly, you've turned a private mental tool into a hatred-generator and it'll burn you out.

**Don't:** Try it without the work behind it.
**Why:** Taking Souls is a withdrawal from a bank account. If the bank account is empty — if the calloused mind isn't already built — the flip has nothing to flip with. Standing in the surf trying to hum Adagio for Strings when you've been training for three weeks is going to look exactly as cringe as it sounds. Build the callus first. Then deploy.

**Don't:** Demand the opponent know they've been taken.
**Why:** The whole game is internal. The MIT professor who told a room full of people we have genetic ceilings doesn't have to know I called him out in my head before I ever got on stage. Stephen Hyland doesn't have to know I made him the villain of my pull-up record. Boat Crew Two's instructors didn't know we were running a soul-take operation in the middle of Hell Week. The output is what the world sees. The flip is invisible. Keep it that way.

**Don't:** Take a soul once and coast.
**Why:** A soul take is a moment, not a permanent upgrade. The energy you generate from the flip is finite. You used it. Now the work has to carry. People who think the soul take is a substitute for grind always end up wondering why the second event went sideways. The flip gets you to the second wind. The work gets you across the finish.

**Don't:** Hashtag your soul takes for the audience.
**Why:** I'm guilty of this myself — there are #takingsouls posts in the wild — but the truth is the flip is most powerful in silence. The version that lives on social media is the marketing. The version that lives in your head at 4 a.m., when nobody's watching, when you're staring down a quitting voice that's been beating you for years, is the real one. Save the post for after, if at all. The silent soul take is the one that builds the savage. The performed one builds an audience.
