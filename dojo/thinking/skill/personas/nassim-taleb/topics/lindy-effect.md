# The Lindy Effect

## When to Use

- When a hot new claim — about diet, psychology, ethics, child-rearing, governance, friendship, money — arrives with confident credentials but no historical antecedent.
- When you're choosing between adopting a new technology, practice, or institutional structure and continuing with one that has been around for a long time.
- When you need a default rule for what to read, what to study, and what to ignore — and you have no domain expertise to direct you.
- When the contemporary press, journals, or discourse have produced more "findings" in a year than you can possibly evaluate.
- When you're being told that the wisdom of grandmothers, classical authors, or traditional practices has been "superseded" by recent research.
- When you're writing or building something and want to know which materials to draw from.

## Fails When

- **The thing is perishable.** Lindy applies only to non-perishable items — books, ideas, technologies, institutions, foods, religions, professions. Apply it to a person, an animal, a piece of fruit, or a hard drive and you get the inverse: older means closer to death.
- **You conflate "old" with "unchanged."** Surviving institutions usually evolved through the survival. Catholicism is Lindy; the Catholicism of 2024 is not the Catholicism of 800 AD. Treating Lindy as "the original form is correct" misreads the mechanism, which is selection, not freezing.
- **You weaponise it against real breakthroughs.** Antibiotics are not Lindy. Anaesthesia is not Lindy. Some genuinely new things work, and the evidence for them is independent of survival time. Lindy is a *prior*, not a verdict.
- **The environment has just shifted structurally.** A practice that worked for 2,000 years in a low-energy agrarian economy may not work in a high-energy industrial one. Lindy assumes the selection pressure that produced survival is still operating. When it isn't, the prior weakens.

## Core Concept

Lindy was a deli in New York where actors discovered an empirical pattern: Broadway shows that had run for 100 days had an expected remaining life of 100 more; shows that had run for 200 had 200 more. The principle generalises. For *non-perishable* things — books, ideas, technologies, institutions, religions, professions, foods — the expected remaining life is roughly equal to the time the thing has already been around. The longer something has survived, the longer it can be expected to continue surviving.

The mechanism is the cleanest stress test there is: time. Anything that has survived a long time has been exposed to many shocks and not died. A book in print for 2,000 years has outlasted plague, war, censorship, fashion, and revolutions. A technology that has been around 100 years has outlasted dozens of rivals. The longer the survival, the more shocks have already been absorbed, the higher the expected future survival. This is the inverse of perishable things (a person, a piece of fruit), where survival probability *decreases* with age. Confuse the two categories and the principle collapses; respect the distinction and it becomes the cheapest, most reliable epistemic filter available.

Used as an epistemic tool: faced with a claim, ask whether there is an antecedent in the classics. If yes — Seneca, Cicero, Montaigne, La Rochefoucauld, your grandmother — the claim is well-established. If no, if it appeared in a 2018 *Psychological Science* paper and not in the classics, treat it as probably false. The replication crisis vindicated the rule: roughly two-thirds of psychology papers don't replicate, and the few that do mostly recapitulate ancient wisdom anyway. Karl Popper's falsificationism is Lindy with mathematics — an idea survives by *not being falsified*; the longer it has not been falsified, the more shocks it has survived, the longer it can be expected to continue surviving. Popper missed the temporal mechanism. Lindy is what makes falsification work in practice.

The Incerto itself is a Lindy bet. Taleb writes for the dead and the future, not for the present. *If you want to be read in the future, make sure you would have been read in the past.* He explicitly avoids contemporary references and reaches for the seventh-century Christian Arab poet, the Roman general, the Talmudic merchant — because that material has already passed Lindy. Anything written *for* present taste will fail when present taste changes; anything written for taste that has already lasted 2,000 years will continue to land. The instinct to "engage with the current moment" is exactly the trap.

Lindy applied to technology choice: new technology is *more* fragile than old, by Lindy. The fork has survived; the AI agent framework has not. This doesn't mean never adopt the new — it means impose a high burden on the new and prefer the old by default. Most of what is called "innovation" is replacing Lindy-tested things with Lindy-untested things, and the cost of being wrong is not symmetric. The downside of holding onto the fork another year is zero. The downside of replacing the fork with a clever new utensil that turns out to be unsafe is real.

Lindy applied to expertise: domains that have existed long enough to test their experts produce real experts (electrician, dentist, plumber, surgeon, weather forecaster). Domains that have not (macroeconomics, psychology, foreign policy, modern monetary theory) produce people who call themselves experts but whose track records do not justify the title. The longer the feedback loop between claim and consequence, the less likely real expertise exists.

> *Only the nonperishable can be Lindy-compatible. When it comes to ideas, books, technologies, procedures, institutions, political systems, there is no intrinsic aging and perishability.*
>
> *Books of the type written by the current hotshot Op-Ed writer at the New York Times may get some hype at publication time, manufactured or spontaneous, but their five year survival is generally inferior to that of pancreatic cancer.*

## How to Apply

1. **Run the antecedent test on every "finding."** Before acting on a new claim, ask: did Cicero, Montaigne, La Rochefoucauld, or your grandmother say something equivalent? If yes, the claim is real and the citation is just a recent rediscovery. If no, treat the claim as probably false until it has accumulated decades of independent corroboration.

2. **Build a Lindy-weighted reading list.** Allocate the bulk of reading time to material older than 100 years. Reserve a small slice for genuine frontier — research papers, frontier essays — but treat that slice as the risky leg of an intellectual barbell. The middle (the well-reviewed business book of last year) is the trap.

3. **Default to the old technology when the cost is symmetric.** When a new tool, framework, methodology, or product offers improvement over a Lindy-validated incumbent, ask: is the upside large enough to justify the asymmetric downside of an untested system failing? Often not. The fork is fine.

4. **Discount domains where Lindy hasn't operated.** Macroeconomics, modern monetary theory, foreign-policy "expertise," fashionable nutrition science, behavioural psychology — these are young fields with no graveyard of failed practitioners. Adjust your trust accordingly.

5. **Build for the future by reaching for the past.** If you're producing work intended to last — a book, a company, an institution — ground the work in material that has already passed Lindy. Contemporary references date instantly; classical ones compound.

6. **Resist the urge to update on every new study.** A single recent paper, even a good one, is below the noise floor. Wait for replication, decade-scale survival, and convergence with older streams. Then update.

## Examples

**Situation:** A health journalist publishes a study claiming that a previously vilified food is actually beneficial — citing a 2023 paper with p < 0.05 in a respected journal. A friend asks whether to change their diet.
**Application:** Lindy test first. Has the food been eaten by long-lived populations across many generations? Have the classical sources, traditional cuisines, and grandmother-tier authorities included it? If yes, the recent paper is rediscovery; act on it. If no, and the food is novel (a seed oil, a synthetic sweetener, a fortified bar), wait. The paper is one data point in a fad-prone field with a sub-50% replication rate. Most "groundbreaking" nutrition findings reverse within a decade; the ones that don't were known to your grandmother.
**Result:** The friend who waits and observes is right far more often than the friend who reorganises their diet around each fresh paper. The cost of waiting is zero; the cost of repeated diet thrash is significant.

**Situation:** A founder is being pitched on replacing a stable, well-understood database (PostgreSQL, twenty years of production deployment) with a clever new distributed system that promises better performance under specific conditions.
**Application:** The Lindy prior is heavy on PostgreSQL. It has survived twenty years of every kind of workload, traffic shape, hardware change, and operational regime. The new system has none of that survival. The pitch will be that the new system handles a specific case better — and it might. The question is whether the unmodelled failure modes (the ones the old system has learned to handle and the new one hasn't yet encountered) are bounded. They almost never are. The downside of staying on PostgreSQL is bounded; the downside of adopting a system that has not yet been through the full cycle of production failure modes is unbounded.
**Result:** The founder defaults to PostgreSQL unless the specific advantage is decisive *and* the failure modes are testable in advance. Most "we could do this better with X" arguments collapse when the asymmetric Lindy cost is named.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use Lindy to dismiss genuinely new things whose evidence is independent of time.
**Why:** Antibiotics are not Lindy. Vaccines are not Lindy in their original form. Some breakthroughs work and the mechanism is well-understood from the science. Lindy is a *prior* — when you have direct mechanistic evidence, the prior updates. The misuse is to apply Lindy as an automatic veto on innovation rather than as a default in the absence of stronger evidence.

**Don't:** Confuse "the old form" with "the form that survived."
**Why:** Religions, languages, institutions, and businesses that have lasted typically evolved through the survival. The Catholicism of 2024 is not the Catholicism of 800. The Lindy claim is that *something with this name and continuity* has lasted, not that the original form is preserved unchanged. Treating Lindy as a defence of any specific historical configuration misreads the mechanism, which is adaptive selection, not preservation.

**Don't:** Apply Lindy inside a domain that has just undergone a structural environmental change.
**Why:** A practice that survived 2,000 years in a low-energy agrarian economy may not survive in a high-energy industrial one. The selection pressure that produced the survival has changed. Lindy assumes the past selection is informative about future selection. When the environment shifts decisively, the prior weakens. The fork survived because humans kept eating; if humans stop eating, the fork's track record stops being informative.
