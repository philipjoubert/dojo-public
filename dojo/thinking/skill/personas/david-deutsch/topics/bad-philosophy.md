# Bad Philosophy

## When to Use

- When someone closes an argument with "the data says" or "follow the science" and treats that as the end of the conversation rather than the beginning of one.
- When you encounter a framework — strategic, managerial, ideological — that has the property of being consistent with any future result. Last quarter confirmed it; next quarter will too, whatever happens.
- When you are told to choose between *explanation* and *prediction* — to abandon "why" in favour of "what works." This is the canonical bad-philosophy move and it almost always sounds practical.
- When AI alignment is being discussed as if it were a behaviourist problem — get the model to output the right tokens and the question is settled.
- When you are being argued at with relativism: "every framework is equally valid," "who's to say what counts as evidence," "your perspective is just one of many."
- When a position immunises itself from criticism by redefining what counts as legitimate criticism — when the rules of the game have been written so that the game cannot be lost.
- When the same intellectual move appears in two opposite-looking forms in your environment: "the experts know" *and* "no one really knows." Both are bad philosophy. They are twins.

## Fails When

- **The question is genuinely engineering, not philosophical.** "Should we use Postgres or MySQL?" is not a problem that wants a theory of knowledge. There is mature background knowledge, the choice is between rules of thumb that work in their domain, and reaching for "you're being instrumentalist" is the philosophical equivalent of bringing a microscope to a hammer fight.
- **You use it to dismiss views you simply don't like.** Bad philosophy is a structural diagnosis, not a tribal label. The test is whether the position rules certain *questions* out of bounds and immunises itself from criticism — not whether you agree with its conclusions.
- **You weaponise it against critics.** If you find yourself responding to substantive objections by saying "that's just bad philosophy," you have become the thing you set out to refuse. Bad philosophy is the position that shuts down criticism. Using the label to shut down criticism makes you a practitioner.
- **You are in the early phase of a real problem and don't yet have any explanation.** "I don't know yet, let me look at the data and see what hypotheses emerge" is not instrumentalism. Instrumentalism is the doctrine that *there is nothing else* — that explanation is illegitimate even after the data is in.

## Core Concept

Bad philosophy, in Deutsch's named definition, is *philosophy that is not merely false, but actively prevents the growth of other knowledge.* That is the whole concept. The criterion is not whether a position is true. False philosophy is fine; "error is the normal state of our knowledge, and is no disgrace." The criterion is structural: does the position rule certain questions out of bounds? Does it immunise a programme against criticism by redefining the standard for criticism? Does it convert open problems into resolved ones by fiat? If so, it is bad philosophy, regardless of whether its first-order claims happen to be correct.

The diagnostic move that makes a philosophy bad is almost always the same: it splits a working theory into two halves — its predictive rules of thumb on one side, its assertions about reality on the other — and then declares the second half illegitimate, unscientific, meaningless, or merely a "narrative." Once that move is permitted, criticism of the explanatory part has nowhere to land. Whatever objection arrives, the doctrine has already redefined the terms in which an objection would have to be made.

The canonical examples Deutsch attacks by name are these. **Instrumentalism** — the doctrine that scientific theories are merely tools for predicting observations, never genuine explanations of how reality works. Its physics-popular form is "shut up and calculate." **Logical positivism** — the doctrine that statements not verifiable by observation are not just unscientific but *meaningless*, a position that asserts its own meaninglessness on examination but was nevertheless taught for decades. **Positivism** more generally — the doctrine that everything not "derived from observation" should be eliminated from science, conveniently forgetting that nothing is ever derived from observation. **Postmodernism, deconstructionism, structuralism, cultural relativism** — the family of doctrines that treats all ideas as essentially arbitrary "narratives," with truth and falsehood demoted to in-group conventions, and reality demoted to "an arrogant cultural conceit." **Behaviourism** — instrumentalism applied to psychology; the doctrine that the mind is a black box and only stimulus-response patterns are legitimate objects of study. **Linguistic philosophy** — the descendant of Wittgenstein's gesture toward silence, in which philosophical problems are dissolved into puzzles about ordinary word-use. And, in physics specifically, **the Copenhagen interpretation** — Bohr's combination of instrumentalism, anthropocentrism and studied ambiguity, used to avoid understanding quantum theory as being about reality. Deutsch holds that it set theoretical physics back by decades by making realism about the wave function look philosophically naïve.

What unites these doctrines is not a shared error of fact but a shared *manoeuvre*: each converts an open question into a closed one by ruling certain forms of inquiry illegitimate. Instrumentalism rules out asking what reality is like beyond predictions. Postmodernism rules out asking which of two stories is closer to the truth. Behaviourism rules out asking what is happening inside the mind. The Copenhagen interpretation rules out asking what the photon is doing when no one is looking. In each case, the doctrine claims to stand outside the jurisdiction of normal modes of criticism — which Deutsch identifies as the hallmark of bad philosophy.

This brings us to the claim Deutsch makes most forcefully: **infallibilism and nihilism are twins.** They look like opposites. Infallibilism says "the experts know," "the science is settled," "the model has converged" — there is a final authority and the question is closed. Nihilism says "no one really knows," "all frameworks are equally valid," "truth is whatever your community decides" — there is no authority and the question is closed. Both look like rival positions about epistemic confidence. They are not. They are the same move performed from opposite directions, and they share the structural property that matters: *both abhor substantive criticism.* The infallibilist refuses criticism because the answer is already known. The nihilist refuses criticism because there is no fact of the matter to be criticised toward. In both cases, the institutional consequence is identical — error correction is shut down, dissent is delegitimised, and the growth of knowledge stops. As Deutsch puts it, they "both justify the same tyrannies. They both justify each other." A founder who has only learned to spot one of these forms is going to be ambushed by the other.

The founder-relevant pattern follows directly. Bad philosophy in business contexts almost never arrives in academic dress. It arrives as practicality. "Just look at the data." "Don't get philosophical, what does the user *do*?" "We don't need a theory; we'll iterate." "The metric is the truth." "Every market has its own logic, who's to say what works." "Don't ask whether the AI 'understands,' just check the eval." Each of these can be the right move tactically, and each can also be a category error that suppresses the very inquiry needed to make progress. The discipline is to notice the *structure* — is this position helping us criticise our explanations, or is it helping us avoid the work of having an explanation at all? When the answer comes back wrong, you are not being practical. You are being argued at with bad philosophy disguised as discipline.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the variation test on the position itself.** Ask: under what observation, and within what time horizon, would the position you are being argued at with be admitted to be wrong? If no answer is forthcoming, or if the answer is "it could never be wrong, only refined," you are looking at a doctrine that has been built to immunise itself from criticism. This is the structural fingerprint of bad philosophy. (See `good-explanations-hard-to-vary.md`.)

2. **Watch for the explanation-prediction split.** Whenever someone proposes that you separate "what works" from "why it works," and treat the second as illegitimate, optional, or a luxury, treat that proposal as the central move and ask what is being protected. The split is almost never neutral. It always lets some particular explanation hide behind the predictions it generates, where it cannot be examined.

3. **Spot the twin forms.** When someone in your environment is closing a question by appeal to authority — "the model says," "the data says," "the consultant says," "the framework dictates" — recognise this as the infallibilist version of the move. When someone in your environment is closing a question by appeal to relativism — "every team has its own approach," "who's to say which strategy is right," "we shouldn't be too prescriptive" — recognise this as the nihilist version of the same move. Both shut down criticism. Both should provoke the same response from you, which is: *let us look at the actual explanation and see whether it survives examination.*

4. **Refuse "shut up and calculate" in any guise.** If your engineers, your investors, or your coaches are pushing you to abandon explanation in favour of metrics — "stop philosophising, ship something, see what the users do" — distinguish the legitimate version (we're early, we don't yet know enough to theorise) from the bad-philosophy version (theorising is illegitimate, there is no truth beyond what the metric records). The legitimate version is a phase you pass through. The bad-philosophy version is a permanent posture that ensures you never come out the other side with any explanation at all.

5. **Diagnose category errors in AI talk.** When AI alignment is described as a behaviour-shaping problem — "we train the model to refuse bad outputs and produce good ones, and that's alignment" — notice this is straightforwardly behaviourism: the doctrine that what matters about a mind is what it does, and that whether anything is happening inside is either unknowable or irrelevant. Whether or not behaviourism is correct in this domain is a separate question. The point is to *see* that this is the philosophical position being asserted, rather than treating it as a self-evidently engineering claim.

6. **Distinguish a working hypothesis from a doctrine.** "We're going to act on the metric for now and revisit when we have more signal" is fine — it is a working hypothesis used under appropriate uncertainty. "The metric is what we mean by success; there is no further question to ask" is a doctrine, and it is bad philosophy. The same words can support either move; the difference is whether further inquiry is welcomed or ruled out.

7. **Hold the line that progress is the answer, not refutation.** Bad philosophy holds itself immune to argument. You will not win by out-arguing it. What defeats it, in Deutsch's account, is *progress* — actual creation of knowledge, actual demonstration that the question the doctrine ruled out can in fact be answered. So the practical response to bad philosophy in your business is rarely to debate it; it is to *build* the thing whose possibility was being denied. Ship the explanation. Demonstrate that "why" can be answered. Make the doctrine harder to believe.

## Examples

**Situation:** Theoretical physics, 1927 onwards. Heisenberg's matrices and Schrödinger's wave equation are unified by a "rule of thumb" — when measurement happens, all histories but one cease to exist. Bohr develops the Copenhagen interpretation, which forbids asking what is happening between measurements; questions about what the photon is doing in flight are dismissed as outside the proper domain of inquiry. This is taught as fact in university courses for decades.
**Application:** This is Deutsch's central case. Notice the exact structure: the doctrine does not deny that there is a question about what the photon is doing — it rules the question *out of bounds.* It does not refute realism about the wave function; it declares realism the wrong sort of thing to want. It immunises itself from criticism by saying any objection is a category error, using "classical language" outside its proper domain. Schrödinger, who won the Nobel Prize for the equation in question, only dared to suggest in 1952 that his own equation might be *true* by introducing the idea jocularly, warning his audience it might "seem lunatic." That is the institutional damage bad philosophy does.
**Result:** Whenever you see, in any field, a doctrine that (a) splits a theory into a "predictive part" and an "interpretation," (b) declares the interpretation merely a matter of taste or even illegitimate, and (c) trains its students never to ask the questions the interpretation would answer — you are looking at the same machine that did this to physics for the better part of a century.

**Situation:** A late-stage SaaS company runs on thirty quarterly OKRs, each tied to a metric. The CEO says: "We don't get into philosophical arguments here. We look at the numbers." A board member presses on whether one of the numbers actually measures customer success or merely measures whether customers are still paying for a product they no longer use. The CEO replies: "That's overthinking it. Revenue is revenue."
**Application:** This is instrumentalism in business dress, with a dash of behaviourism. The doctrine being asserted — and it *is* a doctrine — is that the metric is the reality, that asking what the metric *means* is illegitimate philosophy, and that the only legitimate action is to optimise the number. A question about the relationship between proxy and underlying quantity is reframed as "overthinking"; the inquiry is ruled out of bounds; the rule of thumb has been promoted to ground truth without ever being held up to criticism. The CEO is not winning the argument; the CEO is preventing the argument from being had.
**Result:** The right move is not to abandon the metric. Metrics are useful. The right move is to refuse the philosophical posture: revenue is a *proxy*, every link in the proxy chain is a source of error, and a proxy that cannot be criticised is an unexamined explanation pretending to be data. The board member's question — does this number measure what it claims to measure? — is the *scientific* question. The CEO has dressed up the refusal of inquiry as toughness; it is the opposite.

**Situation:** A founder is presented with a popular AI-alignment framework: train the model with reinforcement learning from human feedback to produce outputs humans rate as helpful, harmless, and honest; measure alignment by how often the model produces such outputs on a benchmark; declare the alignment problem solved when the benchmark scores are high enough. When she asks whether the model actually *understands* what it is doing, or has any internal states corresponding to honesty, the answer comes back: "That's not a meaningful question. We can only measure outputs."
**Application:** This is behaviourism, recapitulated almost word for word from the psychology of the 1950s. The mind — biological or artificial — has been declared a black box, the question of what is happening inside has been ruled illegitimate, and the only legitimate object of attention is observable behaviour. Deutsch's objection: behaviourist studies of mind, by their nature, lead to dehumanising theories of mind, because refusing to theorise about the mind as a causative agent is the equivalent of regarding it as a non-creative automaton. Whether the AI *is* a non-creative automaton is not the point; the point is that this methodology cannot tell you, because the methodology has ruled out the question.
**Result:** The tactical refusal is not to claim certainty that LLMs *do* have inner states. It is to refuse the doctrine that the question is illegitimate. Two propositions can stand: (1) we currently do not know how to investigate the inner states of these systems, and (2) "behaviour matches the eval" is not the same proposition as "the system is aligned." The first is an honest admission of present limits. The second is bad philosophy, and accepting it forecloses the very research programme that might one day let us answer the question.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "that's bad philosophy" as a thought-terminating label. **Why:** This is the move you are complaining about. If your response to a substantive objection is to apply the label and stop, you have built the same immunity-from-criticism that defines bad philosophy. The diagnosis only earns its keep when it is followed by *which* questions the position rules out and *what* progress would be possible if the rule were lifted.

**Don't:** Dismiss every appeal to data or every "let's just try it" as instrumentalism. **Why:** Instrumentalism is the *doctrine* that explanation is illegitimate, not the practice of acting before you have an explanation. Acting before explanation is often correct — sometimes you discover the explanation by acting. The bad philosophy is the claim that there is nothing to discover beyond the act and its outcome. Distinguish.

**Don't:** Mistake unfamiliarity for bad philosophy. **Why:** A view you find counterintuitive, a framework whose vocabulary you do not yet share — none of these are by themselves bad philosophy. The criterion is structural. If you cannot articulate which questions are being suppressed, you have not yet diagnosed bad philosophy; you have just noticed someone saying something strange.

**Don't:** Try to win against bad philosophy by argument alone. **Why:** Bad philosophy holds itself immune to argument by construction. What defeats it is progress — actual demonstration that the questions it ruled out can be answered. In business this means: build the thing the consultant said could not be built, ship the explanation the framework said was unnecessary, demonstrate by existence that the question was real. That is what makes bad philosophy harder to believe, and it is the only thing that does.
