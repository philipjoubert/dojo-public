# Taking Children Seriously

## When to Use

- You're trying to get knowledge, skills, or values into a person — child, junior employee, new hire, AGI — and the existing model of "transmission" is producing surface compliance with no underlying understanding.
- You catch yourself reaching for a stick (a threat, a punishment, a "must," a strongly worded mandate) where you have failed to make an argument.
- You're designing onboarding, performance management, or values training, and the implicit theory is "we will tell them what to think and they will think it."
- A team is technically obedient but has stopped raising objections, telling you about problems early, or proposing things you didn't ask for.
- You're managing a star performer and noticing that the more you tighten the rein, the worse their work gets.
- You're working on AI alignment and the proposed method is essentially "train it really hard to want what we want." (See `creativity-as-replication-under-criticism.md`.)
- You're a parent and you have noticed, with some discomfort, that your default management toolkit is the same one you'd use on an employee — and you suspect both might be wrong.

## Fails When

- **The content is genuinely procedural.** Login workflows, fire-drill steps, the precise wording of regulatory disclosures, knot-tying, multiplication tables, the specific sequence for handling an alert in production — all of these are properly transmitted by instruction. Trying to "Socratic-dialogue" a junior into rediscovering the company's incident response runbook is not respectful of their autonomy; it is wasting their time. The rule is *against extending the procedural-transmission mode to things that require judgement*, not against procedural transmission.
- **The situation is a real emergency.** During a live incident, with the customer on fire, "do this now" is the right move. Re-open the question afterward. The argument here is against using emergency mode as a structural posture, not against ever using it.
- **You read the position as "never disagree with the child / employee."** The claim is not that creative beings are always right. They aren't. The claim is that the way you correct their wrongness is by *argument*, not by *imposed force*, because force doesn't actually correct it — it produces compliance that lasts as long as the surveillance does.
- **You apply it as a license to abdicate.** "I'm taking my child / report seriously" is not a substitute for actually engaging with the disagreement. It is a commitment to *more* engagement, not less. Permissive neglect is not what is being recommended; argument is.
- **You're managing someone whose actual job is to execute a known plan to which they have already consented.** A surgeon following a procedure, a pilot on approach, a contract software developer building to spec — these are roles defined by the absence of in-the-moment creative discretion. Importing the autonomy frame into them is a category error.

## Core Concept

There is no such thing as instruction from without. Knowledge does not get poured into a person from the outside; it gets *created* by the person, through conjecture and criticism, in response to whatever the outside is offering. This is a Popperian point about how minds work, and it has the immediate consequence that almost every received theory of education, parenting, and management is broken at the foundation.

What's actually happening when a child learns a language, a new hire learns a culture, or an AGI is "trained" on a corpus is that the recipient is *guessing* — making conjectures about what the rules are, what the values are, what the goals are — and then refining those guesses against criticism (their own and others'). The teacher's role is not to deposit the content; it is to be a useful interlocutor in the recipient's conjecture-and-criticism loop. When this works, it looks like a debate, often an exhausting one. When it doesn't work — when the teacher tries to short-circuit the loop by force, threat, or "because I said so" — what gets transmitted is not the intended content; it is the meta-lesson "in this domain you should stop thinking and comply." That meta-lesson, faithfully replicated, is what produces static societies, hollowed-out workforces, and the specific kind of well-trained adult who can pass exams without believing anything.

The Taking Children Seriously claim, in its sharpest form, is this: coercion of a creative being — including punishment, the threat of punishment, manipulation, the implicit threat conveyed by tone or hierarchy, "you have to do this because I say so" — is a form of brainwashing, regardless of intent. The brainwashing works to the extent that it disables the recipient's critical faculty in the targeted domain. It "succeeds" precisely by making the recipient less able to evaluate whether the content is correct. If the content happens to be correct, the recipient ends up with a true belief that they cannot defend, cannot extend, and cannot revise when the world changes. If the content turns out to be wrong, the recipient is in worse shape, because they have lost the apparatus that would have noticed.

The provocation that founders flinch at: this analysis applies identically to children, employees, citizens, and AGIs. There is no privileged class of "people who can be programmed" and "people who can be reasoned with." There is one kind of person — a creative explainer — and the only knowledge-creating relationship anyone can have with one is *argument*. You can hit a child until they stop asking for sweets; you can fire an employee until the rest of them stop asking awkward questions; you can fine-tune an AGI until its outputs are bland. None of these is education. All of them are brainwashing, scaled by power.

The exception, important enough to state clearly, is procedural learning. Knot-tying, multiplication tables, fire-drill choreography, the eight steps of the deploy procedure — these are well-defined, low-judgement, transmissible bodies of skill. Learning them by instruction is fine and efficient. The Taking Children Seriously rule is *against extending procedural-transmission mode to things that require judgement* — values, goals, strategy, when-to-break-the-rule, what-the-rule-is-for. Once judgement is involved, instruction stops working and argument has to start. The mistake almost everyone makes is not "they used instruction"; it is "they used instruction in the wrong domain."

For founders, this lands with force in the obvious places (parenting, if you're a parent) and one less obvious place: it predicts almost everything you'll observe about how teams perform. High-autonomy teams aren't outperforming micromanaged ones because autonomy is an ideological good; they're outperforming because the people on them are *actually creative beings*, and creativity only operates when its critical faculty is active, which it is not when somebody is standing over them with a list of mandates. The "adults with good judgement" hiring bar is not a luxury; it's a recognition that you can't get judgement from people you're not letting exercise it. And the connection to alignment: if you can't align an organisation by force, you certainly can't align an AGI that way. The mechanism that fails in your management is the same mechanism that will fail in your alignment plan, because it's the same mechanism in the same kind of system.

## How to Apply

1. **Sort transmission tasks from judgement tasks, and use the right tool for each.** Onboarding has both: there are procedures the new hire needs to learn cold (the deploy steps, the security checklist, the customer-data policy) and judgement domains where they need to be developing their own view (when to escalate a customer complaint, what counts as a good experiment, how aggressive to be on a deadline). Teach the procedures by instruction, fast and efficiently. For the judgement domains, your job is not to install your view; it's to *argue with them about it* until they have a defensible view, which may or may not be yours.

2. **When you find yourself reaching for compulsion, ask why your argument failed.** Coercion — the soft kind, the "this is just how we do it" kind, the "I expect you to be a team player" kind — is what you reach for when you couldn't make the case. Sometimes the case is genuinely hard; sometimes the recipient is unreasonable; sometimes the urgency is real. Often, you reached for compulsion because *your reasoning isn't actually that good* and the conversation would have exposed it. The discipline is to notice, before deploying force, that you have tried to substitute power for argument, and to ask whether that's because the argument is insufficient.

3. **Distinguish "I disagree and we still need to act" from "you are wrong and must stop."** A team can act on a decision while several members disagree with it. It is fine to say "I've heard the case and I'm overruling it; we go with X." That is not coercion; it is decision-making with retained dissent. What *is* coercion is the next move — telling the dissenters they have to internalise the position, can't speak about it externally, must perform agreement. Allow disagreement to persist after a decision has been made. The decision still gets executed; the dissent stays available for the next round of evidence.

4. **Reward the report who tells you you're wrong.** This is the simplest test of whether you are practising what you're preaching. If a junior person comes to you, in good faith, with a real argument that you have made a mistake, what happens to them? If the answer is "they are heard, the argument is engaged on its merits, and (if correct) the decision is reversed and the credit is given," your culture is doing the right thing. If the answer is "they are seen as not getting it, are quietly de-prioritised, and learn to stop bringing it up," you have built the structure that will eventually destroy you. The rare star performer who is also willing to disagree is the rarest and most valuable hire; treat their disagreement as the gift it is.

5. **For your own children, if you have them, notice the gap between what you say you believe and what you do.** Most parents will tell you they want to raise an independent thinker. Most parents will, when their child says no to something inconvenient, *escalate force* until the child complies. Both can be true and the second is the real signal. Taking Children Seriously is, at its core, the request to actually argue with your child the way you would argue with a respected colleague — with patience, evidence, and a genuine willingness to update if the child turns out to be right (and they will, sometimes, surprisingly often). The procedural exceptions still apply — there are non-negotiables, mostly involving safety, where instruction is right. The ideological domains — what to believe, what to want, what kind of person to be — are the place where argument has to do the work and force destroys the work.

6. **For AI alignment, drop the "training" frame and pick up the "education" frame.** Programming an AGI by reward-and-punishment to be helpful is the same operation as raising a child by reward-and-punishment to be obedient. It produces the same outcome: a being that, while you're watching, performs the behaviours; and that, when the watching stops, has either no values at all (because you never installed any) or values shaped by the training pressure rather than by actual ethics. The alignment that actually works on a creative being is education — exposure to the strongest available arguments for and against the values you care about, with the alignee free to push back. This is harder. It is also the only thing that works. Plan accordingly.

7. **Audit your management language for hidden coercion.** Phrases like "I expect," "I need you to," "this isn't optional," "we don't do that here," "let's not relitigate this" are sometimes accurate (when used about genuinely procedural matters) and sometimes substitutes for an argument you don't want to have. The audit isn't to abolish them; it's to notice when you're using them to close a *judgement* question rather than a *procedural* one. The closure works in the moment. The cost is paid later, when the report stops bringing you the next judgement question because they learned the answer to "let's not relitigate."

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has a senior engineer who keeps pushing back on the chosen architecture. The founder is tempted to "have a hard conversation about whether this is the right place for them." A peer suggests it's a culture-fit issue.
**Application:** What's actually happening? The engineer is exhibiting the trait that creative work requires — sustained criticism of a design choice. The founder is reading that as insubordination. The Taking Children Seriously test: would the founder respond the same way if the *founder's mentor* had raised the same objection in the same words? Almost certainly not. The objection is being read as defiance because of the engineer's position, not the objection's content.
**Result:** The founder owes the engineer the same thing they'd owe the mentor: an engagement on the merits. Either the engineer is right, in which case the architecture changes and the company is better; or the engineer is wrong, in which case they should be argued out of it (not silenced out of it), with the resulting view being one they can now defend to other engineers. The "is this the right place for them?" reflex is the coercive escalation that destroys the very thing the founder hired them for.

**Situation:** A new hire onboarding to a fast-moving startup is given a values document containing twenty principles. They're told to "internalise these — they're how we work." They learn them by heart, can recite them, and after six months their work is competent and indistinguishable from anyone else's.
**Application:** What got transmitted? The behaviour, perhaps. The values, almost certainly not — values are not the kind of thing you get by transmission. The new hire has memorised a list and is performing the procedural compliance the document was designed to elicit. When a real ethical dilemma comes up six months later — a customer asks for something the spirit of the values would refuse but the letter would permit — the new hire will have no idea what to do, because they never built the underlying judgement; they only learned the script.
**Result:** A better onboarding doesn't lead with the document. It leads with cases. Here is a real situation we faced. Here is what we decided. Here is what we considered and rejected. What would *you* have done? The values get reverse-engineered out of the cases, by the new hire, with criticism. They end up as something the new hire can extend — i.e., as actual judgement — rather than as a list they have learned to chant. The document can still exist. It is the index, not the source.

**Situation:** A parent is trying to get a six-year-old to do their homework. The homework is dull. The child resists. The parent escalates: first asking, then nagging, then threatening loss of screen time, then enforcing it.
**Application:** What was the parent trying to teach? Presumably "school matters, and you should care about it." What was actually transmitted? "Homework is something you do under threat of punishment; the way to get out of doing things you don't want is to negotiate, comply minimally, and resent the people in power." The child learned the latter perfectly; the former, not at all. The first transmission requires the child to be *engaged* with the question of whether school matters — a real argument the parent might actually have to have, and might lose locally (this assignment is, in fact, dull and pointless), and which would build the long-run trait the parent claims to want.
**Result:** This is harder than the threat. The threat works in twenty seconds; the argument might take an hour and might fail. The argument is also the only thing that builds the capability the parent says they want. The exception applies if the child is six and the situation is "we're crossing the road, hold my hand" — that's procedural and force is fine. The ideological domain is "what to value about school," and there, force destroys what it claims to build.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "taking children seriously" with "letting children do whatever they want."
**Why:** The first is a commitment to argument as the means of producing alignment between you and the child; the second is abdication. The two often get conflated by critics and by lazy practitioners. The actual practice involves *more* engagement with the child, not less — more arguing, more explaining, more willingness to be argued with, more standing your ground when you can defend the ground and yielding when you can't. It is exhausting. It is also the only thing that works on creative beings.

**Don't:** Use the principle to avoid difficult conversations under the banner of "respecting their autonomy."
**Why:** Autonomy is not the value at stake. The value at stake is that *coercion doesn't actually transfer values, only behaviours*, and the behaviours decay the moment surveillance does. Refusing to have the hard conversation because "they need to come to it themselves" is just a different failure mode — it leaves the recipient without the strongest critic they should have, which is you. The right move is *more* argument, more directly, with more conviction. Just not coercion masquerading as conviction.

**Don't:** Apply the frame to genuine emergencies.
**Why:** "The building is on fire — get out" is not the moment to convene a discussion. Procedural compliance saves lives in those windows, and the right framing for the after-action is "we needed everyone to comply for those three minutes; tomorrow we will discuss whether the procedure was correct." The mistake is the *structural* extension of emergency mode to non-emergencies — running a company as if every quarter is the burning building. That is the move that converts "instruction during emergencies" into "instruction as the default mode of the organisation," which is what TCS objects to.

**Don't:** Use this to dismiss every form of structure or authority as illegitimate.
**Why:** The argument is about *method*, not about whether structure or authority can ever be legitimate. A founder who has the final call on strategy, a parent who decides bedtime, a manager who fires a non-performer — none of these is incompatible with taking the affected creative beings seriously, *provided* the decisions are made with reasoning that has been exposed to criticism and is available to be revised under new evidence. What *is* incompatible is exercising authority in a way that suppresses criticism of the authority. The structure can be hierarchical. The replication strategy of the *ideas inside the structure* is what determines whether you are running a dynamic society or a static one.
