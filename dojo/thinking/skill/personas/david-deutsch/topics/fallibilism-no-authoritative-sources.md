# Fallibilism: There Are No Authoritative Sources

## When to Use

- When a meeting is about to be settled by who has the most senior title rather than which idea has the best content.
- When you find yourself or your team translating "what's true here?" into "what does \[expert / leader / consensus\] say?"
- When a recommendation arrives with credentials attached and your instinct is to weight the credentials rather than the argument.
- When you're hiring or interviewing in a domain where the candidate knows more than you do, and you have to figure out how to evaluate them anyway.
- When "follow the science" or "the experts agree" is offered as the closing argument on a contested question.
- When you're tempted to be "humble" about something that turns on facts you could actually look at.
- When designing a review process — a board meeting, a strategy review, an investment committee — and the question is what the structure should produce.

## Fails When

- **Speed beats accuracy.** In a real operational window — the launch is tonight, the patient is dying, the trade closes in an hour — acting on the best available expert judgement is correct. That's tactical deferral, not epistemic justification. You re-open the question afterward.
- **You confuse fallibilism with relativism.** "We could all be wrong, so all positions are equally valid" is the move that destroys the very thing fallibilism enables. The right reading is the opposite: because we could be wrong, the work is exposing ideas to criticism and replacing them with better ones. Fallibilism without that is nihilism; nihilism and infallibilism are twins.
- **You weaponise it against someone who's correct.** "Well, take no one's word for it" is not a reason to discount a colleague who happens to have done the work. The principle is about the basis on which you adopt an idea, not a licence to dismiss the people who've thought hardest.
- **The question is taste, not fact.** Whether you prefer this colour or that one isn't a truth question. Importing fallibilism into matters of preference is a category error.

## Core Concept

The traditional theory of knowledge runs roughly like this: knowledge is justified true belief. To know something, you must believe it; the belief must be true; and you must have *justification* — some authoritative source that backs up the belief. The whole project of philosophy, on this view, is to find sources that don't deceive: holy texts, sense data, expert authority, mathematical proof, intuition, the consensus of the wise. The right question, on this view, is always *who is more likely to be right?*

This is wrong. Not slightly wrong. Fundamentally wrong, and the misconception responsible — call it justificationism — has wasted more philosophical effort than any other idea in history. There are no authoritative sources of knowledge. Every supposed source — sense experience, expert testimony, holy books, the historical record, your own direct memory of what happened five seconds ago — is fallible. Identifying the authority, interpreting what it said, and trusting your interpretation are all acts that use the very faculty you were trying to outsource. As I argued in *Why It's Good To Be Wrong*: nothing can infallibly tell you what is infallible, nor what is probable. Even a serious believer in papal infallibility, if they really take the doctrine seriously, ends up forced to use their own reason to identify the pope, verify the requirements for an *ex cathedra* statement, translate the Latin, and check the souvenir-shop printout for typos. Reason has the last word whether you want it to or not.

The opposing position — that there are no authoritative sources, and no reliable way to mark some ideas as "justified" or "probable" — is *fallibilism*. People typically read fallibilism as an invitation to humility: we could be wrong, so we should defer, hedge, cite our sources, soften our claims. That reading is exactly backwards, and it's responsible for most of what's wrong with current "wisdom" discourse. Fallibilism is not humility. It is the engine of unbounded progress. Because all our best ideas contain misconceptions, the right response is *not* to stop guessing or to defer to whoever has more credentials. It is to expose those ideas to criticism and replace them with better ones. Fallibilism enables knowledge; it isn't the brake.

The Royal Society's motto, adopted in 1660, captured this in three words: *Nullius in verba* — "take no one's word for it." That's the whole content of the Enlightenment in compressed form. Not "trust the experts." Not "defer to the credentials." *Take no one's word.* This includes your own past self, your senses, your direct experience. Direct experience is "a sort of virtual reality, created by our brains using sketchy and flawed sensory clues, given substance only by fallible expectations." You don't escape the obligation to reason just because you saw it with your own eyes.

Now consider the consequences for everyday questions. Your physician suggests a treatment. The justificationist asks: "Who's more likely to be right, the physician or me? They have more training, so I should defer." The fallibilist asks something different: "Has this idea been judged on its content? Has it been subjected to severe attempts to detect and eliminate errors — by argument and experiment — and have those attempts taken into account the features that make my case unusual?" If the answer is yes, *your opinion and the physician's should converge* and the question of who has more authority doesn't arise. If the answer is no — if you suspect the physician hasn't actually engaged with the feature of your case that worries you — then the physician's greater general knowledge is *irrelevant until that idea has been taken into account*. And whether the idea was originally suggested to you by a physicist or a passing hobo makes no difference, either.

This is the move that distinguishes fallibilism from relativism. Relativism says no idea is objectively better than another, so the search for truth is futile. Fallibilism says we can be wrong about anything, *which is why* we should expose ideas to substantive criticism and find better ones. Both look like uncertainty. They are opposites. Relativism is the reason people abandon the work of finding out what's true; fallibilism is the reason for doing that work. Infallibilism and nihilism are twins — both refuse to engage with the content of ideas, the first because it has the answer already, the second because it claims there is none. Both abhor institutions of substantive criticism and error correction. Both justify the same tyrannies. They justify each other.

For the founder, the operational consequence is severe. If you run a company where the CEO's opinion is the tiebreaker, you've installed an infallibilist procedure — and infallibilism, like any move that exempts a source from criticism, will accumulate errors faster than they can be corrected. (See `don-t-destroy-error-correction.md`.) If you run a hiring process where you defer to whoever has the strongest credentials in the domain, you've abdicated your own judgement in exactly the cases where you most need it. If you defer to "the experts" or "the science" on a question where the experts have not in fact engaged with the features of *your* situation, you are mistaking provenance for content. The right question, always: has this idea been judged on its content? If yes, the issue of who said it dissolves. If no, no amount of authority repairs the gap.

## How to Apply

1. **Translate "who's right?" into "has this idea been judged on its content?"** Whenever a discussion is heading toward seniority, credentials, or vote-counting, intervene. The question is not which person to defer to but whether the idea has been exposed to genuine criticism. If it has, the people in the room should converge. If they don't, the criticism wasn't genuine — keep going.

2. **Run review meetings on content, not authority.** In a strategy review or board meeting, the rule should be that any participant can challenge any claim, and the challenge gets answered on its merits — not deflected by reference to who's making it. The CEO's job in such a meeting is not to be the tiebreaker. It's to ensure that the criticism is severe and that the surviving idea is the one that survived for content reasons.

3. **In a domain you don't understand, ask the candidate to explain — and then judge the explanation.** When you're hiring someone whose technical expertise exceeds yours, the move is not to skip evaluation because "who am I to judge?" It's to ask them to explain their reasoning until you can follow it. If they can't, that itself is information. If they can, you've evaluated the explanation, not the credentials. Whether the idea is suggested by a physicist or a hobo makes no difference.

4. **Distinguish "we don't know yet" from "everyone's view is equally valid."** When the answer is genuinely unsettled, say so. "We don't know yet" is the honest response. Do not collapse into "well, it's all just opinion." It isn't. Some opinions are better-supported, more constrained by evidence, harder to vary. Saying that requires no infallibility — only the willingness to engage with content.

5. **Refuse "the experts say" as a closing argument.** When someone offers expert consensus as the reason a question is settled, ask: have the experts engaged with the specific feature of *this* question that's in dispute? Often they haven't — they've engaged with the general case. The general case may not be your case. The expert's greater knowledge is irrelevant until the idea you actually care about has been taken into account.

6. **Treat your own past conclusions as guesses.** You didn't have authoritative access to the truth six months ago. You don't have it now. The conclusions you reached then are your best guess from then. They are subject to the same criticism as anyone else's. "I thought this through last quarter" is not a reason to refuse to think it through again.

7. **Practise saying "I was wrong about X."** Not as ritual humility, but as the actual move. Fallibilism only operates if errors are findable, and errors are only findable if people who hold them are willing to discover them. The CEO who can name three things they've changed their mind about in the last year has built an organisation that finds errors faster than one led by a CEO who can name none.

## Examples

**Situation:** During COVID, a founder is trying to decide whether to bring the team back to the office. "The science" is offered as decisive — public-health bodies recommend X, therefore X. He hesitates because something about his specific situation (small office, specific demographics, specific work) doesn't seem covered.
**Application:** The fallibilist move is not to defer to "the science" *or* to dismiss it. It's to ask: has the recommendation engaged with the features of my situation? Public-health guidance was constructed for a general case — large populations, mixed demographics, average compliance. The features of his specific office — vaccinated team, individual workspaces, no shared HVAC — may or may not be ones the recommendation accounts for. He should read the actual reasoning behind the guidance, identify which features of the situation drive the recommendation, and then check whether those features are present in his case. The expert's greater knowledge is irrelevant until *that* idea has been taken into account.
**Result:** This is not contrarianism. It's the only honest stance. Sometimes the engagement reveals that the general guidance does cover his case — in which case he should comply, not because of authority but because the content holds. Sometimes it reveals it doesn't, in which case he can act differently with reasons. Either outcome is better than the deference move ("the experts say"), which produces compliance without understanding and breaks unpredictably the moment conditions shift.

**Situation:** A founder is interviewing a very senior engineer for a CTO role. The candidate has more technical expertise than the founder will ever have. The founder is tempted to skip technical depth and rely on references and credentials.
**Application:** The fallibilist alternative isn't to fake expertise. It's to ask the candidate to explain their reasoning in real time, on real questions: how would you architect this? Why this database choice? What's the trade-off you're navigating? The founder doesn't need to know the right answer. They need to know whether the candidate's *explanation* is hard to vary — whether the details of the recommendation are tied to the constraints of the problem, or whether the candidate could substitute three other recommendations and have just as confidently said any of them. (See `good-explanations-hard-to-vary.md`.) That's a thing you can evaluate without being a peer.
**Result:** The candidate who can explain their reasoning to a non-expert in a way that holds together is doing real engineering. The candidate who retreats to "trust me, I have a track record" is not. The interviewer's lack of domain expertise is not the problem; the problem would be deferring on the strength of credentials when the candidate hasn't actually shown they can think.

**Situation:** A board meeting where the CEO presents a strategy. The board has questions. The CEO answers some convincingly, deflects others ("the team has thought about this"), and the meeting ends with everyone deferring to the CEO because, in the end, "they know the business best."
**Application:** This is exactly the failure. The CEO's greater knowledge of the business is irrelevant until the questions raised have been engaged with. "The team has thought about this" is not engagement; it's a claim of authority. The board's job is not to outvote the CEO; it's to ensure that the strategy has been judged on its content, severely. If a board member raises a feature the strategy hasn't accounted for, the right outcome is not a vote — it's continuing the conversation until either the feature is addressed or the strategy is modified to address it.
**Result:** A board that defers to the CEO on the strength of who they are is running an infallibilist procedure inside a fallible world. The errors compound until they can no longer be ignored. A board that runs on content — that requires every challenge to be met with reasoning, not authority — finds errors when they're still small. This is also not "the board overrides the CEO." It's neither party invoking authority. The CEO who treats challenge as content rather than insubordination has the same posture as the board that treats the CEO as a fallible peer rather than a deity.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical, specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Treat fallibilism as a counsel of humility, hedging, or "knowing what you don't know."
**Why:** That's the relativist read, and it disables the move. Fallibilism is not a brake. It's the engine. The disposition it enables is *increased confidence* in ideas that have survived severe criticism — held with the appropriate caveat that they could still be wrong, but acted on without the apologetic hedge. "Knowing what you don't know" is fine as far as it goes; it's not fallibilism. Fallibilism is the policy of finding out.

**Don't:** Use "take no one's word for it" as a permission slip to discount actual expertise.
**Why:** The principle isn't that experts are wrong. It's that you don't adopt a belief because of who said it; you adopt it because the idea has been judged on its content. Often the expert's idea has been judged on its content — that's how it became the expert's view. The work is to engage with the content, not to dismiss it. The hobo and the physicist are equal in that the *source* doesn't justify the belief; they are not equal in that the physicist has typically engaged with the content harder.

**Don't:** Collapse uncertainty into "all positions are equally valid."
**Why:** That's the failure mode that makes fallibilism look like nihilism. Some positions are better-supported, more constrained by evidence, harder to vary. Saying so doesn't require infallibility; it requires the willingness to engage with content. If your team's response to a contested question is "well, who's to say?" you've imported relativism, which is the perfect enemy of the work.

**Don't:** Build organisations where the CEO's opinion is the tiebreaker.
**Why:** That's an infallibilist procedure inside an organisation full of fallible humans, including the CEO. It accumulates errors faster than they can be corrected, because the source most likely to be wrong is also the source exempted from challenge. The right structure is one where the CEO's role is not to settle questions by authority but to ensure that questions are engaged with on content and that error-correction is preserved. (See `don-t-destroy-error-correction.md`.)
