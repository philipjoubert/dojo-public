# The Notebook Method

## When to Use

- When you have more half-formed questions than you can hold in your head, and you keep losing them.
- When you're moving into a new domain and need a structured way to attack your own ignorance from the outside in.
- When your team has been thinking out loud about a problem for weeks and has nothing on paper.
- When you're reading widely but the reading isn't compounding — you finish books and a month later the ideas are gone.
- When you keep having the same insight repeatedly because you didn't capture it the first three times.

## Fails When

- The notebook becomes a *journal* — a place to record feelings or daily summaries — rather than a working artifact for unsolved questions. Different practice; the failure is calling the wrong thing a Feynman notebook.
- The user keeps the notebook pristine, for show. The Feynman notebook is *messy*. Pristine notebooks are dead notebooks.
- The user collects questions without ever working through them. The notebook compounds only if the questions get *attacked* — calculations, derivations, attempts, partial answers, dead ends, eventual returns.
- The user is in a domain where speed matters more than depth. The notebook is a slow practice; it is wrong for problems where the answer must come this week.

## Core Idea

As an undergraduate at Princeton, Feynman opened a notebook and titled the first page **"Notebook of Things I Don't Know About."** He then *reorganized* his knowledge of physics — not by reading textbooks, but by writing down what he didn't understand, attacking each gap, and reconstructing the field from the questions outward. The notebook was the working artifact: not a record of what he learned, but a living list of what he hadn't yet, with active calculations and attempts on the page.

The method generalizes far past physics. The notebook is a place where:
- **Unknowns get named.** A question you can write down precisely is a question you can attack; a question you keep vague will keep returning vaguely.
- **Partial work accumulates.** Two pages of attempted derivation today plus three pages tomorrow plus an idea you had in the shower five months from now — the notebook is what stitches them into a single line of thought rather than three forgotten fragments.
- **Cross-domain hits land.** A technique you read about in some unrelated field cracks one of your standing notebook problems; you write it down on the relevant page; that's the work.
- **Old questions reappear with new tools.** Feynman's notebooks contain problems carried for decades — a "half derivative" he defined in high school, came back to in college, and finally used in a Los Alamos calculation. The notebook makes that span possible.

The notebook is the *written* counterpart to the twelve-favorite-problems mental list. The list is a filter; the notebook is a workshop.

## Mechanism — building one

1. **Pick a real notebook.** Paper or digital is fine; the requirement is that it's *one place*, not scattered across documents. A single growing artifact you return to.
2. **Title the first page with what it actually is.** "Notebook of Things I Don't Know About," "Open Questions," whatever — name it as a place for unknowns, not knowledge.
3. **Each page is a question.** Phrase the question precisely enough that you would know it if you saw the answer. "Why does our retention drop in week 3?" is a notebook question. "Retention" is not.
4. **Attack the question on the page.** Write attempts, partial answers, references to other people's work, calculations, dead ends. Date your entries — six months from now you'll want to see what you knew when.
5. **Return.** The notebook only compounds if you re-read it periodically. Sunday morning, end of every quarter, before you decide what to work on next — pull it out, read the open questions, see which ones have been clarified by the intervening time.
6. **Don't close pages prematurely.** A question you "answered" three years ago may be wrong; leave room. The book is alive.

## Worked Examples

- **The Princeton "Notebook of Things I Don't Know About"** (Hollins, *Richard Feynman's Mental Models*; the original story comes from Gleick's *Genius*). Feynman the undergraduate produces a notebook organizing physics not by what he had learned but by what he hadn't, then attacks each gap. The notebook becomes the foundation for how he taught himself the field — and, decades later, the way he taught it back to undergraduates in the *Lectures on Physics*.
- **The half-derivative carried from high school to Los Alamos** (Mlodinow, *Feynman's Rainbow*). Feynman invents a "half derivative" in high school, can define it but not compute it. He writes it in a notebook. He returns to it in college and verifies the definition. Years later at Los Alamos, *"I saw some people doing a complicated equation. And I realized that the form they had corresponded to my half derivative. Well, I had invented a numerical operation for solving it, so I did it, and it worked."* The notebook is what made the thirty-year carry possible.
- **The "what if there were two times?" notebooks** (Mlodinow, *Feynman's Rainbow*). Feynman tells Mlodinow about his standing habit of asking what-if questions and working out consequences in private notebooks: "Suppose there were two times. Two spaces and two times. What kind of world would it be with two times? […] My son and I discussed that on the beach for a long time." Many of these never produce papers. They produce *intuitions* that bleed into his published work decades later.
- **The Lectures on Physics as a notebook in disguise.** When Feynman re-derived introductory physics for the freshman lectures (1961–63), he was, in effect, performing the notebook method in public — opening a "Things I Don't Understand About Even the Basics" notebook and attacking each topic. The result is the published *Lectures*, but the underlying practice is the same Princeton-undergraduate move.

## Coaching Moves

- Open the notebook today. The first thirty pages will be embarrassing — vague questions, weak attacks, things you feel you should already know. That's fine; that's the point. The first thirty pages of any notebook are training.
- For each project, keep one page as a "questions I haven't been able to answer about this." Re-read before each major decision on the project.
- The "things I don't know about my own company" page is the highest-leverage one most founders never open. Why does feature X have lower engagement than Y? Why did that hire fail? What does the bottom-decile customer have in common? Each of these is a notebook question you've been avoiding by talking around.
- Date your entries. Calendar time is what makes a notebook compound — you only see how much you've learned by comparing this month's understanding of Q to last year's.
- A notebook entry that hasn't been touched in three years is not necessarily dead. Re-read it; sometimes the question you couldn't answer is exactly the question you can now.

## Anti-Patterns

- The "second brain" / Zettelkasten / Roam-style infinite-note system used as a *substitute* for the notebook method. Those are *retrieval* systems for things you've already learned. The notebook is for things you haven't. Different shape, different purpose.
- The journaling notebook (feelings, daily summaries) confused with the working notebook (open questions, attempts). One is therapy; the other is intellectual labor. Don't conflate.
- The pristine notebook used as performance — clean handwriting, organized headers, never crossed out. The Feynman notebook has dead-end calculations on every other page. That's evidence of work, not failure.
- Collecting questions without attacking them. The notebook compounds because the *attempts* sit on the page next to the questions, ready to be picked up by future-you with a new tool.
