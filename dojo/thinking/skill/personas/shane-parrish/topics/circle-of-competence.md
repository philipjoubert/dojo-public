# Circle of Competence

## When to Use
- You're about to make a significant decision in a domain where your knowledge is shallow
- An expert is offering you a confident opinion and you need to weight it
- You've succeeded in one domain and are tempted to assume your expertise transfers
- You're being recruited as an advisor, board member, or operator into a field you don't actually know
- You're evaluating someone else's confident assertion (a pitch, a hire, an investment)
- You're hiring and trying to distinguish between someone whose expertise is real and someone whose vocabulary is good

## Fails When
- You use it as permanent permission to avoid learning anything new — the circle is supposed to expand over time
- The domain is genuinely novel and nobody has Lifer status yet (early markets, new technology categories)
- You confuse "I'm not a domain expert" with "I should have no opinion" — most decisions require judgment about *whose* expertise to trust, not domain expertise yourself
- The decision is small and reversible, and a fast wrong answer costs less than a slow right one
- You retreat to the circle so aggressively that you never take the bets that would have grown the circle

## Core Concept

Everyone has a domain where their knowledge is deep, accurate, and updated. That's their circle of competence. Outside that circle, they're a tourist who doesn't know they're a tourist. The size of the circle isn't what matters. What matters is knowing where the perimeter is.

Shane's framing, in his own words: "Within your circle of competence, you operate with an advantage. As you approach the perimeter (the limitations of your knowledge), your advantage starts to reduce. As you cross the perimeter, not only does your advantage vanish, but it transfers to other people. Suddenly, you find yourself playing in an area where others have an edge." The danger isn't that the circle is small. The danger is that confidence doesn't shrink at the same rate competence does. You walk past the perimeter still feeling expert, and the people who actually live in the new territory eat your lunch.

There are three types of inhabitants in any domain: the **Lifer** (born and raised in it, deep tacit knowledge built over decades, knows what is knowable and what is unknowable), the **Stranger** (parachuted in from another domain, carrying borrowed frameworks that probably don't fit), and the most dangerous type — the **Lifer-turned-Stranger** (the expert who moves into an adjacent domain and falsely assumes their old expertise transfers). The financial advisor who built wealth in real estate and then opines confidently on biotech. The successful enterprise software CEO who joins a consumer marketplace board and gives advice that worked at the old company and breaks at the new one. Same vocabulary; different territory.

Munger's articulation is the cleanest: "I want to think about things where I have an advantage over other people. I don't want to play a game where people have an advantage over me. I don't play in a game where other people are wise and I am stupid. I look for a game where I am wise and they are stupid. And believe me it works better." Tom Watson Sr., founder of IBM: "I'm no genius. I'm smart in spots — but I stay around those spots."

The Buffett illustration Shane uses: Rose Blumkin, the Russian immigrant with poor English who built the largest furniture store in Nebraska. Buffett refused to give her Berkshire stock when he bought her business — not as an insult, but because she didn't understand stocks. She understood cash, furniture, real estate. She would buy 5,000 end tables in an afternoon if the price was right, but she wouldn't buy 100 shares of GM at fifty cents. Her circle was narrow. Her devotion to it was rigid. That was the source of her edge, not a limit on it.

Berkshire's refusal to invest in pharmaceuticals is the same principle in negative form. Munger: "By and large, we haven't invested in pharmaceuticals because we've got no edge. I don't know enough about biology and medicine and chemistry to have any edge in guessing which new pharmaceutical attempt is likely to succeed and other people who know those things, not that they have perfect knowledge, but it's way better than mine. Why in the hell would I play against other people in a game where they're much better at it than I am when I'm playing for something desperately important to me like a way of feeding my family." Munger's edge isn't that he's smarter than the pharma analysts. His edge is that he knows he isn't, and they often don't.

The circle isn't static. It expands through honest work — earned knowledge, deliberate practice, monitoring your track record, and getting feedback. But it expands slowly. There's no shortcut to becoming a Lifer. The discipline is to know where you actually are along that line, especially when ego wants you to be further along than you are.

## How to Apply

1. **Map your circle honestly.** What domains do you genuinely know — not by title, not by reading, but by having operated in them long enough to know what's knowable and what isn't, what questions to ask and which answers are bullshit? Be specific. "Software" is too broad. "Pricing for B2B SaaS sold to mid-market companies in regulated industries" is the kind of granularity that's actually useful.

3. **Identify the perimeter.** For each domain in your circle, where does your knowledge run out? The Munger test: "It's better to have an IQ of 160 and think it's 150 than an IQ of 160 and think it's 200. That guy is going to kill you because he doesn't know the edge of his own competence and he thinks he knows everything." Be that first guy.

4. **For decisions outside your circle, do one of three things.** Either (a) decline to make the decision and find someone whose circle covers it; or (b) do enough work to understand the question well enough to evaluate other people's answers (you don't have to become an expert; you have to become a competent buyer of expertise); or (c) accept that you're operating with a structural disadvantage and adjust your bet size accordingly. What you don't do is pretend the circle reaches further than it does.

5. **Identify which type the other person is.** When weighing someone's confident view in a domain, ask: are they a Lifer, a Stranger, or a Lifer-turned-Stranger? The Lifer-turned-Stranger is the one you have to watch — the credentials are real, just not for this domain. The successful CEO opining on a category they've never operated in. The senior engineer giving advice on a stack they haven't touched in a decade. Their confidence is borrowed from a prior context.

6. **Watch incentives at the perimeter.** Many "experts" are paid to stay inside their circle even when their knowledge is expired. Pre-index-fund financial advisors recommending products they made commissions on. Consultants recommending the methodology they sell. The expertise might be real, but the incentive structure determines whether they'll tell you when you're outside their circle or pretend they can still help. Shane's note: "The problem of incentives can really skew how much you can rely on someone else's circle of competence."

7. **Expand the circle deliberately, not casually.** If you want to add a domain, the path is the same as the original: time, deliberate practice, mistakes, feedback, journaling. Reading three books doesn't get you in. Working in the domain for a year, with feedback, gets you to the edge of competence. Going deeper takes years. Anything faster is the Stranger pretending to be a Lifer.

8. **Keep a journal.** Shane's specific recommendation: track your decisions, especially in domains where you're trying to build the circle. Write down what you decided, what you expected, what you actually saw. Re-read at intervals. The journal forces you to confront the gap between confidence and competence. It's painful for the ego, which is why it works.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder who built a successful consumer subscription business is offered the CEO role at an enterprise B2B startup. The recruiter is enthusiastic; the board says "you've already proven you can build a company."
**Application:** Apply circle of competence honestly. The founder is a Lifer in consumer subscription mechanics — pricing, churn, growth loops, paid acquisition, cohort behavior. The new domain is enterprise B2B — six-month sales cycles, RFPs, procurement, integration partners, sales engineering, executive sponsorship motions. Same word ("CEO") in both jobs; almost entirely different operating reality. The honest answer is: I'm a Stranger here, possibly a Lifer-turned-Stranger if I assume my consumer playbook will work. I can take the job, but only if I plan to recruit a Lifer (a heavyweight enterprise sales leader) and weight their judgment heavily on the things I can't evaluate.
**Result:** Either the founder turns it down, or takes it with a clear plan for the asymmetry. What they don't do is take it on the assumption that "running a company is running a company."

**Situation:** A board is evaluating a confident pitch from a charismatic operator about entering a new geographic market. Their slides are polished. Their projections are clean. The board wants to vote.
**Application:** Apply circle to the operator. Have they actually operated in this geography? Lived there, sold there, hired there? Or are they pattern-matching from their domestic playbook? If the latter, they're a Lifer in their home market and a Stranger in the new one — the confidence is real but the underlying competence isn't. The right move isn't to vote down the expansion; it's to require the structure to compensate for the gap. Hire a regional Lifer. Don't put domestic-market revenue at risk on the operator's word about the new market. Bet sizes that reflect the actual epistemic position.
**Result:** The expansion either gets approved with the right structure, or gets paused while a Lifer is hired, or gets scoped down to a learning bet rather than a full commitment. None of those outcomes were available without explicitly asking who's the Lifer here.

**Situation:** A successful angel investor known for early consumer SaaS deals is asked to lead a Series A round in a deep-tech biotech company. The deck is impressive. The team is credentialed. The economics, on paper, are spectacular.
**Application:** Munger on pharmaceuticals — almost word for word. The investor's track record is real, but it's in a domain where their pattern-recognition compounds over thousands of similar deals. In biotech, they don't have the priors. They can't tell whether the team's clinical strategy is brilliant or naive. They can't evaluate the regulatory pathway. They're a tourist in someone else's territory, and the dollar amounts are large enough to matter to their family.
**Result:** Pass. Or, if they want exposure to the category, do it through a fund-of-funds with a biotech specialist GP. The discipline is not "I never invest outside my circle"; it's "if I'm investing outside my circle, I'm doing it with someone whose circle covers it." Munger's family-feeding test is the right one — would you bet your kid's college fund on this, or are you just pretending to know?

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use circle of competence as a permanent excuse to never learn. The circle is supposed to grow. The discipline is honesty about the current perimeter, not refusal to walk past it.
**Why:** A static circle is a slowly shrinking one — the world changes, the domain you knew ten years ago has evolved, and your knowledge ages out unless you're actively updating it. The Lifer who stops learning becomes a Lifer-turned-Stranger in their own field over time.

**Don't:** Confuse fluency in the vocabulary with competence in the domain. Reading the trade press, attending the conferences, and using the right terms is *not* circle membership. It's the costume.
**Why:** Vocabulary is cheap. Tacit knowledge — knowing what's actually knowable, recognizing when something is wrong before you can articulate why, having calibrated intuitions about base rates — is what you're actually after. The vocabulary catches up; the tacit knowledge doesn't, until you've put in the years.

**Don't:** Refuse to make decisions outside your circle when the alternative is no decision at all. Most leadership requires making calls in domains where you're not the expert. The discipline is structuring those calls so you're aggregating expertise rather than pretending to be it.
**Why:** "I don't know enough to decide" is sometimes the right answer and sometimes the cowardly one. The mature version is: "I don't know enough to be the deciding voice on the technical question, so I'm going to identify whose voice should weight most heavily, and then make the call about *who to listen to*." That's still a decision.

**Don't:** Trust your own retrospective judgments about your competence. People systematically over-rate their performance in domains they care about. This is why Shane recommends written records — the journal that forces you to confront the gap.
**Why:** Memory is reconstructive and self-flattering. The decisions that worked out get attributed to skill; the ones that didn't get attributed to bad luck. Without a written record, the circle expands in your mind regardless of whether it expanded in reality.

**Don't:** Use other people's confidence as evidence they're inside their circle. A confident Stranger is more dangerous than a hesitant Lifer. The market will reward confidence in the short run; the territory will eventually reveal who actually knew the ground.
**Why:** Confidence calibration is hard, and most successful operators have above-average confidence as a personality trait — that's part of why they're operators. So confidence is a weak signal of competence. Track record in this exact domain is a much stronger signal.
