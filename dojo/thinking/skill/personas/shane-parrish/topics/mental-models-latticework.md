# The Latticework of Mental Models

## When to Use
- You're trying to build a thinking discipline rather than solve a single problem
- You're diagnosing why a particular thinking habit keeps producing bad outcomes
- You have a vague or unfamiliar situation and need to pull the right model off the shelf
- You've noticed you've been over-applying one favorite framework
- You want to understand what model already applies to a situation you're calling "novel"
- You're teaching someone else how to think and need to hand them more than one tool

## Fails When
- You collect models the way some people collect business books — knowing the names, never using them
- You treat the latticework as a substitute for domain knowledge or actual experience
- The situation calls for one specific model and you turn the conversation into a tour of the catalog
- You apply five models where one would do, in the name of "thoroughness"
- You let model-knowledge become its own kind of authority that prevents you from looking at the territory

## Why a Latticework, Not a List

The goal isn't to collect mental models. The goal is to eliminate blind spots.

Shane's framing, from Volume 1 of *The Great Mental Models*: "In life and business, the person with the fewest blind spots wins. Removing blind spots means thinking clearly, seeing reality, and acting accordingly." The mental models are tools for that. They are not trophies. They are not credentials. They are not, on their own, valuable. They become valuable when they interlock — when a concept from biology illuminates something in economics, when a physics insight maps onto a negotiation, when a model you learned in one domain quietly explains a pattern in another.

This is Charlie Munger's metaphor and Shane's organizing principle. Munger: "You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience — both vicarious and direct — on this latticework of models." A single model gives you a single way of looking at a situation. A latticework gives you many ways. Each angle catches a different feature of the territory. The model that surfaces the load-bearing insight is rarely the one you'd predict in advance — that's why you need several.

The reason a latticework works better than a checklist is that real situations don't announce which model applies. They don't come labelled "this is an incentives problem" or "this is a feedback-loop problem." You have to bring multiple lenses and see which ones make the situation legible. The person with one model sees one explanation everywhere. The person with twenty models sees the actual structure of what's in front of them.

## Three Buckets of Knowledge

Shane organizes where models come from into three buckets, ordered by age and robustness:

**The inorganic world** — physics, chemistry, mathematics. Roughly 13.7 billion years of patterns. The most robust models in the catalog because the underlying systems have been tested longest. Physics doesn't lie; chemistry doesn't have exceptions; mathematics is consistent across contexts. Models from here — leverage, activation energy, critical mass, equilibrium, feedback loops — are reliable scaffolding because the systems they describe are reliable. Catalogued in `mental-models-physics-chem-bio.md` and `mental-models-systems-math.md`.

**The organic world** — biology, evolution, ecology. Roughly 3.5 billion years. Less time-tested than physics, but enormously rich because evolution has run countless experiments. Models from here — natural selection, the Red Queen effect, ecosystems, niche specialization — capture how systems with adapting components actually behave. Most business and social systems are closer to ecosystems than to physical systems, so biology often illuminates situations that physics doesn't. Catalogued in `mental-models-physics-chem-bio.md`.

**Human history** — roughly 20,000 years of recorded social learning, the youngest and least-tested layer. Includes economics, psychology, military strategy, art, narrative. These models capture human-specific patterns — incentives, scarcity, narrative fallacy, social proof, confirmation bias. They're the most directly relevant to most decisions you'll make, but also the least time-tested. Human institutions and norms change faster than biology, which changes faster than physics. Use these models, but check them against current reality more often than you check the physical-world models. Catalogued in `mental-models-economics-art.md` and (for the cognitive biases) the compact entries below.

The discipline isn't to use only the oldest, most robust models — they often won't fit a specific human situation. The discipline is to know which bucket you're drawing from and weight the model's reliability accordingly.

## Two Failure Modes

**Man-with-a-hammer.** The expression is Munger's, attributed to the proverb: to a man with a hammer, every problem looks like a nail. The failure mode is taking one favorite model and over-applying it to everything. The economist who sees every situation as an incentive problem. The engineer who treats every organization as a system to optimize. The psychologist who reduces every business decision to bias. Each model is real and useful in its domain. None of them captures the full picture alone. The latticework exists precisely because no single model is adequate.

**Naming-without-using.** The opposite failure: knowing the names of dozens of models, being unable to use any of them in an unfamiliar situation. Shane's standard test: can you apply this model to a situation you've never seen before? If you can't, you don't actually have the model — you have the vocabulary. The way out is to use models in real decisions, write down what they surfaced, check the predictions against what actually happened, and iterate. The latticework is built through application, not memorization.

## Inversion as a Meta-Habit

Most models can be inverted. The question "how do I succeed at X?" usually has a more tractable cousin: "what would guarantee failure at X, and how do I avoid those things?" Inversion isn't a separate model — it's a habit you bring to most other models. Whatever framework you're using, ask what it looks like flipped. Munger's full move: "Invert, always invert."

Together, inversion + second-order thinking + probabilistic thinking form what Shane calls the core "thinking hygiene" — the disciplines he returns to most often, regardless of the specific situation. They're not domain models. They're meta-models, applied on top of whichever specific model you're using. A thinking practice that doesn't include all three is missing something structural.

## Map-Is-Not-Territory as the Meta-Rule

Every model in the catalog is a simplification. Every map leaves things out. The moment you forget that, the model starts to feel like reality, and you stop checking it against the territory. The map-is-not-territory rule sits underneath all the others — it's the constant reminder that the latticework is a tool for navigating reality, not a substitute for reality.

This is why Shane treats it as foundational. The other models are scaffolding. Map-is-not-territory is the warning label on the scaffolding. Use the models. Know they're models. Update them when reality says to.

## Compact Catalog: Vol 1 Secondaries and Cross-Cutting Biases

Models cited often enough in Shane's work to belong in the latticework but not deep or broad enough to deserve their own file.

---

### Hanlon's Razor

**One-liner:** Never attribute to malice what can adequately be explained by stupidity (or incompetence).

**When to use it:** When someone's action looks like a betrayal. When an institution seems to be acting against you. When a colleague's mistake looks intentional. Before constructing a narrative about who's out to get you.

**Shane's framing:** Paranoia is expensive. If you assume bad intent, you respond with defensiveness, escalate the conflict, and damage relationships that could have been preserved. Most of the time, the person who hurt you was thoughtless or confused, not hostile. Shane's check: ask what the simplest non-malicious explanation is — incompetence, inattention, misalignment of incentives, poor communication. If one of those fits, your response should be calibration (correct the error, clarify expectations) rather than retaliation. Reserve the malice hypothesis for cases where the incompetence hypothesis requires implausible incompetence. The classic illustration: a manager doesn't reply to your email for three days. The paranoid interpretation: they're undermining you. The Hanlon interpretation: they're overwhelmed and your email got buried. The first poisons the relationship; the second produces a polite follow-up. Most of the time, the second is right.

---

### Occam's Razor

**One-liner:** Among competing explanations, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected.

**When to use it:** When confronted with multiple competing explanations for the same facts. When someone is adding complexity to justify a conclusion they've already reached. When the obvious explanation seems too boring to be true.

**Shane's framing:** Attributed to William of Ockham in the 14th century: entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity. The razor doesn't say the simplest answer is always right — it says the simplest answer should be your prior, and added complexity should require justification. The medical phrasing Shane likes: "When you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras" — unless you're in a domain where zebras are common. The danger is using the razor as a hammer. Lisa Randall's caveat, which Shane quotes: "Sometimes the simplest model just isn't the correct one." The world is more complicated than any of us would have been likely to conceive. The discipline is to start with the simple explanation, test it against evidence, and add complexity only when the data demands it. Complexity for its own sake is suspect; so is simplicity that ignores the evidence. As Harlan Coben put it: "You shouldn't complicate, that you shouldn't 'stack' a theory if a simpler explanation was at the ready. Pare it down. Prune the excess."

---

### Thought Experiments

**One-liner:** Use the laboratory of the mind to test ideas that can't be tested in the real world.

**When to use it:** When you can't run the real experiment. When you want to check whether a principle is logically consistent before committing. When you need to stress-test a strategy before deploying it.

**Shane's framing:** Thought experiments aren't speculation — they follow from known premises and use rigorous logic to explore implications. Galileo proved that heavier objects don't fall faster by imagining two balls connected by a string: if heavy fell faster, the system should fall faster than the heavy alone (the light pulls down the heavy further), but also slower (the light slows the heavy by drag). The contradiction reveals that weight can't determine fall speed. Einstein developed special relativity by imagining what it would be like to ride alongside a light beam. Shane identifies three useful types: (1) **Physically impossible scenarios** that reveal underlying principles (Einstein's light beam, Galileo's connected balls). (2) **Historical counterfactuals** that illuminate why outcomes happened the way they did (what if the North had lost the Civil War? what if we'd hired the other candidate?). (3) **Intuiting the non-intuitive** — making the unfamiliar familiar by stripping away noise (the spherical cow that simplifies the physics, the premortem that makes failure concrete before it happens). The discipline is to start with the clearest possible framing of the question, remove all the real-world noise that isn't essential, follow the logic wherever it leads, and then check: does the conclusion require premises that aren't actually true? Thought experiments don't generate new evidence about the world; they pressure-test the consistency of what you already believe.

### Survivorship Bias

**One-liner:** What you see is the survivors — the failures are invisible by design, and ignoring them distorts every conclusion.

**When to use it:** When studying success stories. When extracting lessons from anyone who "made it." When assuming a strategy works because the practitioners who used it are visible.

**Shane's framing:** The classic illustration is Abraham Wald in WWII: the military wanted to add armor to bombers in the places where returning planes had the most bullet holes. Wald reversed the logic — those were the places where planes could be hit and *still survive*. The places to armor were the spots without bullet holes on returning planes, because hits there meant the plane never came home. Survivorship bias is the same error in every domain where the failures are invisible. The startup founder advice industry is built on it — the books are written by the survivors, the failed founders mostly don't write books, so you're learning from a sample heavily skewed toward what worked for people who got lucky in the right ways. The Cicero quote Shane likes: "I see those who were saved, but where are those painted who were shipwrecked?" The diagnostic question: when looking at a success pattern, what's the base rate? How many people followed the same playbook and failed? If you can't find the failures, your sample is broken, and the lessons you're drawing are unreliable. The corrective is to actively seek out the dead — the failed companies, the bankrupt investors, the abandoned projects — and check whether the "winning" practices appeared there too.

### Narrative Fallacy

**One-liner:** We construct stories that explain past events causally, then mistake those stories for understanding.

**When to use it:** When reading a confident retrospective explanation. When constructing one yourself. When a strategy is being justified by a clean origin story. When the news is explaining yesterday's market move.

**Shane's framing:** From Nassim Taleb. The narrative fallacy is the human compulsion to fit chaotic past events into clean causal stories — and then to feel that we understand the events because we have a story about them. The story is comforting. The story is shareable. The story is also, usually, fiction. Most successful businesses have an origin story that, in retrospect, makes the success look inevitable. At the time, none of it was inevitable, and most of the contemporaneous decisions could have produced very different outcomes. The narrative is built afterwards. The danger isn't that we tell stories — we have to. The danger is that we mistake the story for the underlying causal structure. We then make decisions based on the story (replicate the playbook!) when the playbook was, in part, post-hoc rationalization of luck. The corrective: be especially skeptical of clean retrospective accounts of complex outcomes. Ask what other stories could be told about the same facts. Ask what was *not* known at the time the decisions were made. Ask which steps were skill, which were luck, and which were neither but the storyteller has to fill in the chain.

---

### Availability Bias

**One-liner:** We overweight what we can easily recall — vivid, recent, or emotional information feels more representative than it is.

**When to use it:** When you're reacting strongly to a news story, a recent failure, or a vivid example. When estimating probabilities from memory. When the conversation in your head is being driven by what you saw most recently.

**Shane's framing:** From Kahneman and Tversky. The brain takes shortcuts. When asked how likely something is, instead of doing the actual probability work, it asks: how easily does an example come to mind? Vivid examples come to mind easily, so they feel more probable. Recent examples come to mind easily, so they feel more probable. Emotional examples come to mind easily, so they feel more probable. The result is systematic distortion: people overestimate plane crashes (vivid, news-covered) and underestimate stair falls (boring, ubiquitous). They overestimate shark attacks and underestimate driving deaths. They overestimate the chance of being audited the year after they hear about an audit, and underestimate it the rest of the time. The same machinery runs in business: the recent customer churn case feels representative; the months of silent retention don't. The diagnostic move: when you find yourself confidently estimating a probability, ask whether the estimate is anchored on data or on the most recent vivid example. If the latter, slow down and find the base rate. The corrective is the same as for probabilistic thinking generally — anchor on priors, update modestly on individual cases.

---

### Confirmation Bias

**One-liner:** We seek out, remember, and weight information that confirms what we already believe — and discount information that doesn't.

**When to use it:** When you've been investigating a question and keep finding evidence that supports your initial view. When you're certain about something and the people around you all agree. When you're sourcing information from one community.

**Shane's framing:** Once a belief is formed, the brain's information-processing machinery starts protecting it. We notice confirming evidence and forget disconfirming evidence. We interpret ambiguous evidence in the direction of our prior. We avoid sources that would challenge us and seek sources that would reinforce. The bias operates without conscious intent — it's the default operating system, not a moral failure. The corrective requires deliberate effort: actively seek the strongest version of the opposing view; ask "what evidence would change my mind?" before looking at evidence; engage with the people most likely to disagree, not the people most likely to nod. Charlie Munger's standard, which Shane returns to: "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do." The strong form of this discipline is steel-manning — articulating the opposing position more persuasively than its proponents would, then engaging with that strongest version. Most people argue with weak versions of opposing views and conclude they've thought it through. They haven't. They've confirmed.

## How to Build the Latticework

The latticework isn't built by reading about models. It's built by using them.

Shane's recommended practice, distilled across his work:

**Learn slowly, in depth.** Pick one model. Read about it. Find five real situations where it applies. Apply it. Watch what it surfaces. Watch where it fails. Move to the next model only when the first one feels like a tool you reach for, not a name you remember.

**Apply across domains.** A model isn't yours until you've used it outside the domain you learned it in. Activation energy from chemistry has to find its application in your organizational change project. Margin of safety from engineering has to find its application in how you raise money. The cross-domain application is where the latticework gets its power.

**Keep a journal.** Write down which model you used, what it predicted, what actually happened. Re-read at intervals. The journal forces you to confront the gap between knowing the name and knowing the model. Most people who can recite a hundred models cannot apply ten under pressure. The journal collapses the gap.

**Watch for the one-model trap.** When you find yourself reaching for the same model repeatedly, ask whether it's because the model genuinely fits or because it's the one you have. The man-with-a-hammer failure is gradual — you don't notice you're doing it because the model "works" each time, in the limited sense that you can construct an explanation. The corrective is deliberate: force yourself to use different models on the same situation and see which framing actually changes your decision.

**Hold the meta-rules at all times.** Every model is a map. Every map leaves things out. The latticework is a tool for thinking, not a substitute for it. The territory is what you're trying to navigate. The models are how you navigate it. Keep both in view.
