# Surfing Waves of Change — Find a Wave, Ride It With Skill

## When to Use
- An industry that has been stable is showing signs of structural shift, and the leader is trying to figure out what to do about it.
- A leader is being pressed to produce a ten-year vision in an environment where the next two years are not knowable, and you want to push back without seeming evasive.
- You're diagnosing why a particular upstart (Cisco, NVIDIA) ran past entrenched giants in a way that wasn't really about better execution.
- You're stress-testing an investment thesis that depends on extrapolating current trends in a straight line — particularly for durable goods or platform businesses.

## Fails When
- The industry is genuinely stable and the user is treating ordinary cyclical change as a "wave." Most industries, most of the time, are fairly stable. Mistaking noise for signal produces cargo-cult strategy.
- The user wants forecast certainty. The point of wave analysis is not predicting precisely; it is being *more right* than rivals about the underlying forces. If you can peer into the fog of change and see 10% more clearly than others, you may gain an edge. That is the bar — not omniscience.
- "Riding the wave" is treated as a slogan for "do whatever is trending." The discipline is identifying the underlying forces — escalating fixed costs, deregulation, predictable forecasting biases, structural incumbent response, attractor states — and using *those* to make positioning bets.

## Core Concept

In classical military strategy the defender prefers the high ground. It is harder to attack and easier to defend. Much of academic strategy is intricate explanation for why certain types of economic high ground are valuable. But these discussions sidestep the more important question: how do you attain such an advantaged position in the first place?

There are two ways. One is to create new high ground through pure innovation — Gore-Tex, FedEx's overnight delivery system. The other — usually more important — is to **exploit a wave of change**. Waves of change are largely *exogenous*; they are mostly beyond the control of any one organization. They are the net result of shifts in technology, cost, competition, politics, and buyer perceptions. They behave like an earthquake: they create new high ground and level what had been high ground. Existing competitive positions can be erased; new ones become possible. **Good strategy is more like surfing a wave than having a clear vision of the future.** A leader's job is to provide the insight, skill, and inventiveness that harnesses raw exogenous power to a purpose.

Some first principles. Most industries, most of the time, are stable. The 1875–1925 period saw electricity, the streetcar, the telephone, the automobile, the airplane, modern advertising and retailing, mass-produced consumer brands, IBM's first tabulating machine, and dozens of empires founded. The 1962–2012 period was much less transformative for everyday life, despite the buzz. Don't believe the assumption that we live in unprecedentedly turbulent times; sometimes we do, often we don't. Historical perspective helps you make judgments about importance.

After a wave has passed, its effects are obvious. By then it is too late to take advantage of its surge or escape its scour. The strategist's job is to perceive and engage a wave of change in its early stages — and there the challenge is not forecasting but **understanding the past and present**. The evidence of an emerging wave usually lies in plain sight, waiting to be read. When change occurs, most people focus on the main effects (spurts in growth of some products, falling demand for others); you have to dig beneath the surface to understand the second-order and derivative changes. When television appeared in the 1950s, the obvious effect was competitive pressure on movies. The subtler effect was that traditional Hollywood studios specialized in producing B-grade Westerns couldn't lure audiences off the family-room sofa with "just another Western"; this killed the studio system and produced the rise of independent production with studios as financiers and distributors. Same wave, two effects, very different timescales.

There is no simple framework for analyzing waves. The discipline is using a small number of mental guideposts — observations or ways of thinking that warrant attention. Here are five:

**Guidepost 1: Rising Fixed Costs.** The simplest form of transition. When development or capital costs escalate beyond what most competitors can bear, the industry consolidates. Photographic film moved from black-and-white to color; the R&D escalation forced Ilford and Ansco out, leaving Kodak and Fuji. IBM's rise in 1960s computing came from surging costs of computer and OS development. The transition from piston to jet aircraft engines cut the field to GE, Pratt & Whitney, and Rolls-Royce. The diagnostic: identify whether fixed costs in this industry are escalating, who can absorb them, and who can't.

**Guidepost 2: Deregulation.** Major changes in government policy — aviation, finance, banking, cable, trucking, telecom. Several patterns recur. Regulated prices almost always subsidize some buyers at the expense of others; when price competition arrives, the subsidies vanish but the previously-favored players keep chasing what *used* to be profitable segments because of inertia in routines and mental maps. Highly regulated companies don't know their own costs — they have built systems to justify costs and prices, hiding their real costs from themselves. It takes years for them to wring excess staff and arbitrary overhead allocations out of the system. Expect formerly regulated firms to invest in some products that earn no real return and to wind down some that are actually profitable.

**Guidepost 3: Predictable Biases in Forecasting.** People rarely predict that a business or trend will *peak and decline*. The standard forecast extrapolates current growth, with the rate gradually declining to "normal" levels. That works for frequently-purchased products and fails badly for durable goods (flat-screen TVs, fax machines, power mowers): rapid expansion when first offered, then everyone-who-wanted-one has one, then a sharp drop, then sales tracking population growth and replacement. The faster the uptake of a durable, the *sooner* the market saturates. Many managers find this counterintuitive ("Professor, if you can't get that bump out of the forecast, I can find a consultant who will"). Other predictable biases: assume a "battle of titans" between current market leaders (frequently wrong); assume future winners will look like current apparent winners. Consultants advised airlines after deregulation to copy Delta's hub-and-spokes — but Delta's profits had come from regulated cross-subsidies that were vanishing. They advised emulation of WorldCom's fiber-ring strategy until WorldCom crashed. They advised Web start-ups to build "portals" like Yahoo and AOL. The current apparent winners are usually riding particular conditions that are about to change.

**Guidepost 4: Incumbent Response.** Most incumbents resist transitions that threaten the complex skills and valuable positions they have accumulated. The patterns of incumbent inertia — routine inertia, cultural inertia, inertia by proxy — are usually predictable. When you read a wave, ask: which incumbent strategies are now structurally trapped? Which competitors *cannot* respond effectively without cannibalizing their own profitable franchise? Microsoft would not develop a serious browser-based Office competitor because doing so would cannibalize Microsoft Office. Diamond Multimedia would not give up its 25% margins to NVIDIA's unified driver architecture even when the alternative was extinction. Incumbent inertia is often the wave's primary accelerant for an upstart.

**Guidepost 5: Attractor States.** An industry attractor state describes how the industry "should" work in light of technological forces and demand structure — meaning an evolution in the direction of efficiency. Cisco's "IP everywhere" was an attractor state: in this future, all data moves as IP packets over a standardized data pipeline, with intelligence supplied by the devices at the endpoints rather than by carrier-controlled "intelligent network" services. Other firms envisioned futures where carriers provided proprietary protocols and special hardware for value-added services. The "IP everywhere" attractor won because it was more efficient and eliminated the margins attached to a mishmash of proprietary standards. Attractor-state analysis lets you tell the difference between a corporate "vision" (where one company captures most of the pie) and a structural pull toward efficiency (gravity-like, even if no specific company benefits). Identifying the attractor state, and the accelerants and impediments to movement toward it, gives you a sense of direction even when timing is uncertain. Demonstration effects (Napster making everyone simultaneously aware that a song was a 2.5MB file) are accelerants; convoluted licensing and litigation processes (U.S. nuclear power) are impediments.

**A leader does not need to get it totally right.** The organization's strategy merely has to be *more right* than rivals'. If you can see 10% more clearly into the fog of change than others, you may gain an edge. That is the bar.

## How to Apply

1. **Test for a real wave.** Is there an underlying force — escalating fixed costs, deregulation, a technology shift — that's reshaping the industry's structure? Or is the "change" you're being asked to address ordinary noise? Most industries are stable most of the time. Use historical perspective.
2. **Get into the gritty details.** Riding a wave of change requires intimate feel for its origins and dynamics. Leaders who stay "above the details" do well in stable times and fail in transitions. Talk to the engineers, the sales managers, the customers; hold the technical specifics in mind well enough to question the experts.
3. **Look for second-order effects.** The first-order effect is usually obvious (TV competes with movies). The second-order effects (independent film production rises because traditional studios can't make Westerns good enough to compete with TV) are where the strategic openings are. Where else is this wave breaking — in a place no one is yet looking?
4. **Run the five guideposts.** Are fixed costs escalating? Is deregulation moving the regulatory boundaries? What predictable biases are clouding rivals' forecasts? Which incumbents are structurally unable to respond? What is the attractor state, and what are the accelerants and impediments?
5. **Position for asymmetric exposure.** Channel resources and innovation toward positions that will *become* high ground as the dynamics play out — not positions that are obviously high ground today (those are usually overpriced or about to be leveled). Consider the wave's natural beneficiaries: incumbents whose advantages aren't structurally trapped, upstarts with no legacy to protect, suppliers to the wave (NVIDIA selling to gamers riding 3-D adoption, Cisco selling routers to corporations riding IP backbone adoption).
6. **Don't pretend you have a ten-year vision.** You don't. The more dynamic the situation, the poorer your foresight; the more uncertain the future, the more proximate your operating objectives must be. Take a strong position; create options; ride the wave with skill.

## Examples

**Situation:** Cisco Systems, 1988–1994. Founded by two Stanford staff members (Len Bosack and Sandy Lerner) refining a "blue box" router that linked incompatible Apple, Alto, and DEC networks. After paying Stanford $167K and a discount commitment for the rights, the expectation was that Cisco would sell some boxes to research universities. Industry incumbents were giants — IBM, AT&T, Alcatel, NEC, Siemens — who had spent decades building scale advantages in networking equipment.
**Application:** Cisco rode three simultaneous waves and then a fourth. Wave 1 — rise of software as the central skill: routers were really computers, and the differentiator was firmware. A 100,000-line chunk of cleverly written code by a small team created the product's edge. Cisco outsourced manufacturing and concentrated on software, sales, and service. Wave 2 — corporate networking: corporations were discovering they needed to connect mainframes, PCs, printers using different protocols, and Cisco's multi-protocol routers had the right shape. Wave 3 — IP networking: most network protocols had corporate owners (IBM's SNA, DEC's DECnet, Microsoft's NetBIOS, Apple's AppleTalk, Xerox's Ethernet); IP was vendor-neutral, free, originally from ARPANET. As corporate IT departments standardized on IP backbones, Cisco made IP its central hub protocol. Crucially, no incumbent jumped to occupy this space — each had its own proprietary protocol and was loath to abandon it (Guidepost 4: incumbent response, structurally trapped). Wave 4, hitting in 1993 — public Internet adoption: corporate users wanted always-on direct access; IP won the protocol war; Cisco captured two-thirds of the corporate router market and the high-speed routers handling Internet backbone traffic.
**Result:** For a brief moment in 2000 Cisco was the most valuable corporation in the world. The story is not "Cisco executed brilliantly" (though they did). It is that the wave structure made Cisco's small-team software-centric approach nearly unbeatable, and that key incumbents like IBM (still pulling its punches after thirteen years of antitrust litigation) and the major telecom equipment makers (each defending a proprietary protocol) could not respond. Absent the waves, Cisco would have remained a small niche player.

**Situation:** NVIDIA, 1997–2001, designing 3-D graphics chips. Founded 1993 by Jen-Hsun Huang, Curtis Priem, and Chris Malachowsky. First product (NV1, 1995) — meant to be the "Sound Blaster of multimedia" — was a commercial flop. The competitive landscape included Intel and an aggressive 3dfx Interactive, which had set a de facto standard with its Glide language and Voodoo board.
**Application:** Huang reformulated. Diagnosis: the wave was the rise of 3-D action gaming on PCs (Doom, Quake), and within that, the structural insight that demand for graphics performance was effectively limitless (unlike CPU performance, which was bottlenecked by other things — graphics performance was experienced directly by users, gamers would buy any improvement). Anticipation: graphics performance could be advanced *three times* faster than Moore's Law if NVIDIA used density gains to add parallel processors instead of cutting cost (the path everyone else took). Guiding policy: deliver a substantial graphics upgrade every six months instead of every eighteen, by running three overlapping development teams. Coherent actions: heavy investment in simulation and emulation tools (formal logic verification plus electrical-characteristic simulation, because a two-month delay in a six-month cycle is far worse than a two-month delay in an eighteen-month cycle); a unified driver architecture taking driver development out of board makers' hands and back to NVIDIA; emulation hardware mockups allowing driver development to start four to six months before the first real chips; bypassing brand-incumbent board makers (Diamond Multimedia, defending 25% margins) by going direct to Dell with a deal where Celestica Hong Kong manufactured boards under Dell's brand. Bet on Microsoft's DirectX standard (after meeting the team and concluding they were graphics enthusiasts who wanted to push the frontier).
**Result:** NVIDIA jumped from nowhere to dominance, achieving 157% average annual gain in graphics performance during 1997–2001 and 62% per year through 2007 — about as fast as semiconductor technology allows. *Forbes*' 2007 Company of the Year. The story is the kernel applied to a wave: clear diagnosis (limitless demand for graphics, structural opening for a faster cycle), guiding policy (six-month cycle), coherent actions (the teams, the simulation, the unified driver, the channel bypass) — all riding the wave of microprocessor-enabled software-as-advantage that was simultaneously deconstructing the broader computing industry. Diamond should have made the deal with NVIDIA but defended the 25% margins; companies do not slide quietly into the darkness.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** treat normal industry change as a wave that requires action.
**Why:** Most industries are stable most of the time. The buzz-speak that "we live in an age of continual revolution" is usually wrong. Mistaking ordinary change for a wave produces frantic action, abandoned focus, and bolt-on initiatives that the firm can't afford. Use historical perspective: compare current changes to the 1875–1925 period before deciding the world is unprecedentedly fluid.

**Don't:** forecast by extrapolating current growth in a straight line.
**Why:** People rarely predict peaks. For durable goods, the faster the uptake, the sooner the saturation. The "consultant who will get the bump out of the forecast" is selling you the answer you want to hear; the bump is usually real and timing is hard but the existence is predictable. Apply the same skepticism to "battle of the titans" predictions and to "future winners will look like today's apparent winners." Each is a predictable bias.

**Don't:** assume incumbent inertia is overcome by talking about it.
**Why:** Incumbents that are structurally trapped — Microsoft on browser-Office, Diamond on margin defense, IBM on SNA, every regulated telco on its cost-justification accounting — will pull their punches, defend their margins, and refuse to cannibalize, even when the strategic logic says they should. Counting on an incumbent to "wake up and respond effectively" usually loses money. Plan on the inertia, don't argue with it.

**Don't:** confuse a corporate vision with an attractor state.
**Why:** A vision is what one company hopes will happen, usually for self-interested reasons. An attractor state is where the industry is being pulled by structural forces (efficiency, demand, technology) regardless of any single company's preferences. "IP everywhere" was an attractor; the carriers' "intelligent network" was a vision. The attractor wins because it's more efficient and eliminates margin attached to proprietary standards. The discipline is identifying the structural pull, not advocating for the outcome you'd prefer.

**Don't:** demand certainty about the wave before acting.
**Why:** You don't need to be right; you need to be more right than rivals. 10% better visibility into the fog is enough to gain edge. Waiting for clarity often means waiting until after the wave has passed and the high ground has been re-graded. Take a strong position, create options, accept that the future is uncertain — and do not pretend you have a clear ten-year vision, because if you do, you're a psychotic.
