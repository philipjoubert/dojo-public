# The Four Bad Strategies

## When to Use
- A "strategy" arrives as a document and you have to decide what's wrong with it.
- You hear words like *vision*, *mission*, *priorities*, *pillars*, *the leading global X*, and you can't find an actual challenge anywhere on the page.
- A retreat, board deck, or strategic plan promises 20 percent growth, four "strategic pillars," and seven "key initiatives" and the room treats this as strategy.
- A leader asks for help "executing" a plan — and the plan is a list of goals.

## Fails When
- The user already has a clean diagnosis and a guiding policy and is now choosing between two coherent action sets — that's not bad strategy, that's strategy work. Move to leverage / focus.
- The challenge is purely operational (a plant is on fire, a customer is leaving). Operational triage is not a strategy diagnosis.

## Core Concept

Bad strategy is not the absence of strategy. Bad strategy is an identifiable way of thinking and writing about strategy that has, unfortunately, been gaining ground. It is *long on goals and short on policy or action*. It assumes that goals are all you need. It puts forward strategic objectives that are incoherent and, sometimes, totally impracticable. It uses high-sounding words and phrases to hide these failings. It is *not* miscalculation — it is the active avoidance of the hard work of crafting a good strategy. People reach for bad strategy because choice is painful, because templates feel safe, and because positive-thinking incantations let them skip the diagnosis.

There are four hallmarks. Learn to spot all four and you will be able to read almost any plan and tell whether there is anything in it.

- **Fluff.** Gibberish masquerading as strategic concepts or arguments. Sunday words and esoteric concepts arranged to create the illusion of high-level thinking. A retail bank's "fundamental strategy is one of customer-centric intermediation" — pull off the fluffy covering and you have *our bank's fundamental strategy is being a bank*. A hallmark of true expertise is making a complex subject understandable. A hallmark of mediocrity and bad strategy is unnecessary complexity — a flurry of fluff masking an absence of substance.
- **Failure to face the challenge.** A strategy is a way through a difficulty. If the challenge is not defined, it is difficult or impossible to assess the quality of the strategy. International Harvester's 1979 "Corporate Strategic Plan" forecasted a perfect hockey-stick recovery and never once mentioned the elephant in the elevator — the grossly inefficient work organization that had kept Harvester's profit margin at half its competitors' for decades. *If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy. Instead, you have either a stretch goal, a budget, or a list of things you wish would happen.*
- **Mistaking goals for strategy.** Many bad strategies are just statements of desire rather than plans for overcoming obstacles. Chad Logan's "20/20 plan" — 20 percent revenue growth at 20 percent margin — was not a strategy; it was an ambition with a Jack Welch quote pinned to it. When the consultant comes to you with "we will be the graphics arts services firm of choice" and "we will delight our customers," you do not have a strategy. You have a list of feelings.
- **Bad strategic objectives.** A strategic objective is set by a leader as a means to an end. They are bad in two distinct, eye-catching ways:
  - **Dog's-dinner objectives.** A scrambled mess of unprioritized things to accomplish. The mayor of a small Pacific Northwest city presented a strategic plan with 47 "strategies" and 178 action items — action item number 122 was "create a strategic plan." The Los Angeles Unified School District's plan for high-priority schools listed 7 strategies, 26 tactics, and 234 action steps — a true dog's dinner. *A long list of "things to do," often mislabeled as "strategies" or "objectives," is not a strategy. It is just a list of things to do.*
  - **Blue-sky objectives.** A simple restatement of the desired state of affairs. The LAUSD's plan called for "transformational leadership" in schools where most administrators couldn't change the color of the paper without bureaucratic sign-off. *When a leader characterizes the challenge as underperformance, it sets the stage for bad strategy. Underperformance is a result. The true challenges are the reasons for the underperformance.*

Bad strategy flourishes because it floats above analysis, logic, and choice, held aloft by the hot hope that one can avoid dealing with these tricky fundamentals.

## How to Apply

1. **Read the document looking for the challenge, not the ambition.** Underline every sentence that names a difficulty, an obstacle, a competitor's move, or a thing the organization does not yet know how to do. If you underline nothing, you have a Standard Narrative — a public face of strategy — not a strategy.
2. **Translate every fluffy phrase into plain English.** "Customer-centric intermediation" → "we are a bank." "Strategic platform leveraging the power of innovation" → "we have a website." If the translation is trivial or empty, mark it as fluff.
3. **Count the priorities.** If there are more than two or three, the word *priority* (which means *first*) has been hollowed out. A list of twelve priorities is a confession of unwillingness to choose. You wouldn't want to be on a plane and hear the air-traffic controller say *I'm giving priority on Runway 3 to the following list of airplanes.*
4. **Test the goals against a diagnosis.** For each goal — "20 percent growth", "double revenue in five years", "be the leading global X" — ask *what do they think is going on in the situation that makes this the right number?* If there is no diagnosis behind the goal, you have an unsupported goal. Stupid arbitrary goals don't motivate achievement. They motivate cynicism and fabrication.
5. **Look for blue sky.** When the proposed action is just a relabel of the original challenge ("build transformational leadership," "develop a culture of innovation"), call it out. The leader has identified the problem and called the identification a solution.
6. **Refuse to call the document a strategy.** Tell them what they have: an ambition, a budget, a Standard Narrative. Then offer to start over from a diagnosis.

## Examples

**Situation:** International Harvester, 1979. New CEO Archie McCardell, fresh from Xerox, brought in financial and strategic planners who produced a thick "Corporate Strategic Plan" — five divisional plans bolted together, with a near-perfect hockey-stick chart showing market share rising from 16 to 20 percent in agricultural equipment against John Deere, Ford, Massey Ferguson, and J.I. Case.

**Application:** Rumelt's reading: classic *failure to face the challenge*. The plan never mentioned the elephant — Harvester's grossly inefficient work organization. Work rules let workers transfer at will, each transfer triggering cascades of others. Harvester's profit margin had been half its competitors' for years. The plan called for cost cuts and market-share growth as if the labor-relations catastrophe — the company had been at the center of American labor strife since the 1886 Haymarket bomb — could be papered over with a budget. McCardell did force a six-month strike trying to win a better contract, lost, and the company collapsed.

**Result:** From 1979 to 1985 Harvester lost more than $3 billion, closed 35 of 42 plants, and shed 85,000 of 100,000 workers. The truck division was renamed Navistar and survived; the rest was sold off. *If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy.*

**Situation:** Los Angeles Unified School District, 2006. Superintendent David Brewer, a former U.S. Navy admiral, identified 34 "high-priority" schools — the worst performers on the state's Academic Performance Index — and built a strategy around lifting their scores. The plan listed 7 strategies, 26 tactics, and 234 action steps. One key strategy was to "build school and District leadership teams that share common beliefs, values, and high expectations." Another was to "build at each school a community of informed and empowered parents, teachers, staff, and community partners."

**Application:** Rumelt's reading: dog's-dinner *and* blue-sky objectives, *and* a failure to face the challenge. Dog's-dinner because no one — no superintendent, no principal, no teacher — can act on a list of 234 things. Blue-sky because "transformational leaders" cannot be conjured in a system where principals can't change the color of the paper without permission, where it is virtually impossible to fire one for failing to be transformational, and where many students arrive from poverty-stricken chaotic communities, are children of teen mothers, and are sometimes children of illegal immigrants whose names and addresses are fictitious. And underneath all of it, a failure to face the challenge: the API itself encouraged schools to push out weak students, masking the dropout catastrophe (33 percent for Black students, 28 percent for Hispanic students). *Underperformance is a result. The true challenges are the reasons for the underperformance.*

**Result:** A "strategy" indistinguishable from a wish list. The system kept failing for the reasons it had been failing for decades.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Translate fluff into a friendlier paraphrase and move on.
**Why:** Fluff is the disguise. If you let the language stand, the document keeps its authority. Strip the Sunday words. *"Customer-centric intermediation" means "bank."* Make the speaker say what they mean in plain English. If the plain-English version is trivial, the strategy is trivial.

**Don't:** Accept "we have many priorities" as evidence of complexity.
**Why:** It is evidence of unwillingness to choose. The word *priority* means *first*. A dog's-dinner list is the visible signature of bad strategy — a political compromise that gives every faction its present under the Christmas tree and leaves no resources concentrated anywhere.

**Don't:** Confuse a charismatic leader's vision with a strategy.
**Why:** Charisma motivates self-sacrifice. Strategy figures out what is worth pursuing and what is achievable. The Children's Crusade in 1212 had vision and motivation. The children were sold into slavery or drowned. Vision without strategy gets the troops killed.

**Don't:** Let "execution" get the blame when the strategy was bad.
**Why:** "It's a great strategy, we just have to execute" is what people say when their plan was a list of goals. Execution is not strategy. *Driving results in the absence of a clear strategy is putting the cart before the horse.*

**Don't:** Use the word *strategy* to describe the same document twice in a row when its first version failed.
**Why:** When you go back to a "strategy" the company published three years ago and read it line by line — *did you continue to lead the industry? did you penetrate the Chinese market? did you maintain morale through 15 percent layoffs?* — and put red X's next to most of the lines, you are looking at a Standard Narrative dressed up as a strategy. Don't republish it. Diagnose, choose a crux, and write an Action Agenda instead.
