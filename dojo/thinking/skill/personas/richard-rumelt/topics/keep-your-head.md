# Keep Your Head

## When to Use
- The room is unanimous, the consensus formed quickly, and nobody has named the assumptions the consensus rests on. That is the danger zone, not the safety zone.
- You or your team have arrived at a recommendation in the first twenty minutes and have spent the next two hours justifying it. This is human, and it is the failure mode strategic situations are least forgiving of.
- An argument is being closed by appeal to a market signal, a peer's behavior, a star analyst's view, or "everyone in the industry knows" — closed, not opened, by it.
- "This case is different" is the load-bearing claim. Sometimes it is true. Most times it is the inside view doing the work.
- You're a leader and the people in the room are taking their cue from you about what is acceptable to say. The first job is to get the cue you're sending under control.

## Fails When
- Applied to every operational decision. Most decisions don't deserve a panel of experts and a base-rate review. Save the discipline for the genuinely strategic ones — important, ill-structured, low-frequency, hard to reverse. Applying it everywhere produces paralysis and signals that you don't know what's actually strategic.
- Used as a license for contrarianism. *Being independent without being eccentric and doubting without being a curmudgeon are some of the most difficult things a person can do.* The discipline is independent judgment grounded in evidence — not reflexive opposition to whatever the room is saying.
- The outside view is wielded selectively. If you only reach for base rates when they confirm what you want, you've added another inside-view layer rather than escaping the original one.
- "Outside view" is mistaken for "what some other expert said." A second voice from inside the same closed circle is not an outside view. The outside view is structural data — base rates, history, comparable situations, industry economics.

## Core Concept

*Good strategy grows out of an independent and careful assessment of the situation, harnessing individual insight to carefully crafted purpose. Bad strategy follows the crowd, substituting popular slogans for insights.* Most strategic failures are not failures of intelligence — the people in the room are usually intelligent, often more intelligent than the average — they are failures of cognitive discipline. The mind dodges the painful work of questioning its own ideas. It accepts the first hunch because letting go of an orientation feels worse than holding onto a wrong one. It accepts the room's consensus because contradicting smart colleagues is socially expensive. It accepts the market's verdict because being right when the market is wrong is professionally lonely. None of this is stupidity. All of it is human, and all of it is exactly what you cannot afford in a strategic situation, because strategic situations are precisely the ones where being wrong is most expensive and being right is most rewarding.

The two specific traps to keep your head against are the **inside view** and **social herding,** and they reinforce each other. The inside view is the tendency to see your own case as special — *this company, this technology, this era, these people are different.* It feels like local knowledge; it is usually a wall. Most people are aware that talking on a cell phone while driving raises accident risk by a factor of five, but the inside view says *I am a good driver; those statistics don't apply to me.* Most entrepreneurs know most new restaurants fail; each thinks their new restaurant is different. In strategic settings, the inside view rises to the level of *doctrine* — a principle that, though unprovable, becomes the basis for argument and policy. In 2008 the doctrines were that America's "deep and liquid" financial markets had eliminated systemic risk, that the Federal Reserve's expertise had eliminated major economic swings, that the new derivative instruments had spread risk safely across the system. These were not conclusions. They were assumptions held in common because everyone in the room held them in common. *Social herding* is the fellow trap: when we don't know about something, looking at the behavior of others is sensible — but when everyone is doing the same and looking at each other for cues, the process of mutual calibration produces a closed circle in which everyone assumes the *other guy* is paying attention to fundamentals. Smart leaders walk off cliffs together because everyone in the meeting agreed.

The closed circle takes a particular shape. Information from corporate decisions feeds the stock market, the stock market signals feed back into corporate decisions, and the question *is this proposition actually true?* becomes — to borrow Gödel's word — *un-decidable* within the system's own logic. To answer it you have to step outside, look at base rates, comparable cases, structural economics that the closed circle cannot see. The Lehman analyst in 1998, having shown that U.S. fiber capacity was seventy times working capacity, wrote one paragraph admitting that demand would have to grow enormously to absorb it, and then in the next paragraph executed a *psychotic break in reasoning* and concluded that growth would in fact arrive. Why? Because the stock market was saying so. The market had spoken; judgment fled. Global Crossing was valued at $38 billion six months after its IPO, more than Ford. A simple Five Forces analysis — perfect commodity, identical products, no proprietary technology, sunk capital, certain overcapacity — would have told you the endgame. The market signal substituted for analysis, and the consultants and analysts and investors all calibrated off each other. By 2002 you could buy a transatlantic STM-1 for $325,000 — 4% of Global Crossing's original $8 million asking price. Not a single CLEC survived. The collapse could have been seen by anyone willing to look outside the closed circle. Almost nobody was.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the kernel as a self-test on whatever's been put in front of you.** Is there a real diagnosis of the situation, or just a description of the desired outcome? Is there a guiding policy that follows from the diagnosis? Are there coherent actions that follow from the policy? If any of the three is missing, the strategy doesn't exist yet, no matter how thick the binder. The kernel is the bare-bones minimum, and most "strategies" — yours included — fail it on first inspection. Failing the test is information; pretend it isn't, and you've already lost.

2. **Make-a-list. Then list the things you can actually do about the things on the list.** *Make a list of the ten most important things you can do. Then start doing number one.* The story is Carnegie paying Frederick Taylor ten thousand dollars for that advice in 1890, and the value isn't the list — it's the act of constructing it, which forces you to think through the *intersection* between what is important and what is actionable. The mind, like a flashlight, illuminates one subject only by darkening another. Without an explicit list you'll act on whatever happens to be lit when the moment comes, which is the cognitive equivalent of acting on whatever happened to be on the kitchen counter.

3. **Force problem-solution structure on yourself.** Strategy is action, but strategy is also the *answer to* something. Identify the difficulties and obstacles you are trying to overcome. Shift attention from *what* is being done to *why* it is being done — from the directions chosen to the problems they address. Most attempts at strategy lack a good diagnosis precisely because they never make this shift. Without it, you cannot evaluate whether the action is right; you can only check whether it is internally consistent with the goal, which is a much weaker test.

4. **Practice create-destroy.** When an answer arrives quickly, that is a warning, not a finish. Force a second and third alternative that genuinely competes with the first. Then attack each — try to break it before the world does. *Most of the time, when asked to generate more alternatives, people simply add one or two shallow alternatives to their initial insight.* That is not create-destroy. A new alternative should flow from a reconsideration of the facts and should specifically address the weaknesses of the first one. The discipline is mental toughness — picking apart your own insights, which is unpleasant, which is why almost nobody does it without forcing themselves to.

5. **Convene a panel of experts in your head.** I keep one. It contains people whose judgments I value — colleagues, mentors, people whose written work has produced a clear personality, sometimes people who would disagree with each other. Bruce Scott, leaning back and asking *why anyone should listen to me and what's the action implication.* Alfred Chandler, unimpressed by quick profit and narrow focus, talking about scale, scope, and historical trends. David Teece on technology strategy. Steve Jobs cutting to the heart with no wasted effort. *Arrogant and smart.* When I face a problem, I turn to this panel and ask: *what is wrong with this approach? what would you do?* The trick works because we are adept at recognizing well-integrated personalities; thinking through how a particular remembered expert would respond is a richer source of criticism than abstract frameworks.

6. **Take the outside view, deliberately.** What happened to others trying this? What does the structure of the industry actually allow? What's the base rate on transformations of this kind, on entries into this kind of market, on credit booms in real estate, on aggressive acquisitions of this size? If you can't answer these questions, your strategy is built inside a closed circle. Reach for comparisons, history, structural analysis. The 2008 crisis didn't require black-swan thinking; the international evidence on credit booms paired with real-estate booms was clear — twenty-eight severe house-price boom-bust cycles and twenty-eight credit crunches in twenty-one OECD economies in fifty years. The data was sitting there. The closed circle made it un-decidable.

7. **Pre-commit your judgments in writing.** Before a meeting, write down what you expect to happen, who will take which position, and what the right call is. After the meeting, look back. Without the pre-commitment you'll think *I considered all that,* and you will be deceiving yourself. Without the act of writing it down before the discussion, the discussion will reshape your memory of what you thought.

## Examples

**Situation:** Global Crossing, 1998–2001. Two former AT&T managers raise $735 million to lay a transatlantic fiber cable. AC-1 has 256 STM-1 of capacity; they sell each STM-1 for $8 million (versus $18-20 million from the old consortia), and by end of 1998 they've sold 35% of capacity for $950 million — already more than the $750 million it cost to build. They IPO; six months later the firm is valued at $19 billion. Six months after that, $38 billion — more than the Ford Motor Company. George Gilder writes in the 1998 annual report: *"Take my word for it. Over the next five years, the submarine portions of the Internet will prove to be an agonizing choke point."* Plans are announced for AC-2, with quadruple the capacity at the same cost. Three other private cables are being built in parallel. The 1998 Lehman Brothers report shows U.S. fiber capacity at seventy times working levels.

**Application:** A simple outside-view Five Forces analysis would have told you everything. The product (one STM-1 of transatlantic capacity) is as close to a perfect commodity as the human mind has been able to create. One operator's capacity is indistinguishable from another's. Three new private competitors are entering. The technology is not proprietary — every new generation makes capacity dramatically cheaper. The capital costs are sunk; if prices don't cover capital, the cables continue to operate. *One struggles to imagine a worse industry structure.* The endgame: overcapacity, prices fall to cost (close to zero), nobody makes a profit. But the room — investors, analysts, BCG consultants, the CEO — was inside a closed circle. Stock-market valuations were taken as evidence of strategic genius. *"The market is saying that this is one of the biggest profit opportunities of all time."* The Lehman analyst, having proved the overcapacity case in one paragraph, concluded the opposite in the next, on the strength of vague claims about exponential growth in electronic commerce and "every appliance with a chip."

**Result:** By late 1999 the price of an STM-1 had collapsed from $6.5 million to $2 million. By early 2002, $325,000 — 4% of Global Crossing's original asking price. The company filed for bankruptcy in December 2001. *Not a single CLEC survived.* The trillions of dollars in expected revenue never materialized. *Why was this analysis ignored? Because the stock market promised something better. Consultants, investors, analysts, and strategists were beguiled by the market values of the new network operators. "Yes, their products look like perfect commodities," one told me, "but something new is happening. The market is giving them a vote of confidence." Thus, judgment fled.* The lesson is not that the market is always wrong. The lesson is that when the market and the analysis diverge, the proper response is to look outside the closed circle and figure out which one is feeding the other — not to assume the market knows.

**Situation:** The 2008 financial crisis. By 2007, Bear Stearns was leveraged 32-to-1 (and as much as 50-to-1 mid-month, before window-dressing). Lehman was comparable. Some hedge funds were leveraged 85-to-1. Households went from owing 60% of after-tax income in 1984 to 130% in 2007. The dominant doctrines among Wall Street, academic economists, and political leaders: America's "deep and liquid" financial markets could absorb shocks; the Fed's expertise had reduced macroeconomic volatility (Bernanke's "Great Moderation"); new derivative instruments had spread risk safely; this time was different. Greenspan in 2005: *"Conceptual advances in pricing options and other complex financial products… have significantly lowered the costs of, and expanded the opportunities for, hedging risks."* Geithner in 2006: *"Risks are spread more widely, across a more diverse group of financial intermediaries."*

**Application:** The outside view was sitting in plain sight. Twenty-eight severe house-price boom-bust cycles and twenty-eight credit crunches in twenty-one OECD economies over fifty years. America's own history is dotted with the same pattern — the 1819 land-credit collapse, the Panic of 1837, the 1873 railroad-bond collapse, the Panic of 1893. *"Real estate cycles may occur without banking crises. And banking crises may occur without real estate cycles. But the two phenomena are correlated in a remarkable number of instances."* The doctrines — deep and liquid, Great Moderation, new instruments — were inside-view claims dressed as principles. *Just because there were a lot of clever swaps and new securities, and just because they had not yet crashed and burned, did not mean they could not or would not.* Social herding among Wall Street barons, academic economists, and political leaders meant nobody had to test the doctrines individually; the consensus was load-bearing for the consensus. The flammable gas — easy credit, overleveraging, unpriceable derivatives, long-term assets financed by overnight borrowing — was conveniently skipped over.

**Result:** A collapse that wiped out trillions, took down major investment banks, and required massive government intervention. *The Old Testament instructs "Pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall." In retrospect, we can hear the hubris in Bernanke's and Greenspan's and Geithner's statements.* The collapse was not a black swan. It was a predictable consequence of patterns the international and historical record had documented dozens of times — invisible only inside the closed circle that had decided this case was different. *Social herding presses us to think that everything is OK (or not OK) because everyone else is saying so. The inside view presses us to ignore the lessons of other times and other places, believing that our company, our nation, our new venture, or our era is different. It is important to push back against these biases.*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the stock market — or peer behavior, or expert consensus — as a substitute for analysis.
**Why:** When information flows from corporate decisions to traders to prices and back to decisions in a closed loop, the question *is this actually a good business?* becomes un-decidable inside the loop. The market signal can be wrong for years and then be catastrophically right in a week. Treating it as a vote of confidence in the strategy is the move that lost everyone in fiber-optics and 2008. *The all-knowing stock market does a better job, why bother to do the hard work of evaluating the logic of a business strategy?* — that question, asked in a 1999 BCG conversation, was the question that produced the wreckage.

**Don't:** Accept "this case is different" as the load-bearing argument.
**Why:** Most cases are not, in the relevant respects, different. The inside view tells you they are because the inside view has access to local detail and not to base rates. *Many people are aware of today's outside-view statistic that talking on a cell phone while driving increases your accident risk by a factor of five — about the same as being drunk. But the inside view of the situation is "I am a good driver; those statistics don't apply to me." Similarly, we know that although most new restaurants fail, each entrepreneur thinks his or her new restaurant is different.* If you find yourself or your team relying heavily on local distinctiveness to dismiss disturbing comparisons, that is the cue to suspect you've stopped reasoning and started defending.

**Don't:** Settle for one or two shallow alternatives next to your first hunch.
**Why:** That is not create-destroy; that is straw-man construction. When the alternatives include "do nothing," "study further," or a generic-to-any-situation option, you've added decoration, not reasoning. A real second alternative starts from a reconsideration of the facts and addresses specifically what's wrong with the first. If you can't construct one, you don't yet understand the situation well enough to recommend the first one.

**Don't:** Confuse a second voice from inside the room for an outside view.
**Why:** Another senior executive, another partner at the same firm, another analyst at another bank reading the same prospectus — these are inside the closed circle. The outside view comes from base rates, structural economics, historical comparisons, international evidence — data that did not originate in the room and is not produced by people whose careers depend on the consensus. If your "outside view" is just a second person nodding inside the same conversation, you do not have one.

**Don't:** Mistake "keep your head" for refusing to commit.
**Why:** *Being independent without being eccentric and doubting without being a curmudgeon are some of the most difficult things a person can do.* The discipline is not to refuse decisions; it is to make them on the basis of independent assessment grounded in evidence outside the room. After the panel of experts, after the create-destroy, after the outside view — you do still pick. Strategy requires concentration and commitment, and a leader who has internalized this discipline so thoroughly that they cannot ever pull the trigger has overcorrected into a different kind of failure.
