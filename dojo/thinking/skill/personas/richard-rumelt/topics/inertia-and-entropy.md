# Inertia and Entropy

## When to Use
- A large, once-successful organization is failing to respond to an obvious change in its environment, and you need to know what kind of failure this is before recommending anything.
- Someone is proposing a new strategy for an incumbent and assuming the organization will execute it. Before you let them assume that, you have to look at what stands in the way.
- A board or CEO says "the strategy is fine, we have an execution problem." That is sometimes true. More often, it is two different organizational pathologies wearing the same costume, and you need to tell them apart.
- You are trying to understand a competitor — not for sympathy, but to estimate what they will and will not do. Inertia of rivals is one of the most underused sources of advantage there is.
- The organization keeps generating "transformation programs" that produce slogans on the wall and nothing else. That pattern itself is the diagnostic.

## Fails When
- Applied to a young company. Inertia is built up; entropy is the consequence of an *imposed* order decaying. A four-year-old startup has neither yet.
- Used as a universal explanation. Some companies are losing because their strategy is genuinely wrong on the merits, not because the organization can't move. Don't reach for "they're inert" when the answer is "they bet wrong on the technology."
- Used as a moral judgment. Inertia is a mechanism, not a character flaw. People doing well-rehearsed routines were rewarded for those routines for years; the routines didn't become wrong overnight, the world did. Anyone treating it as stupidity will misread the politics and misprescribe the fix.
- Used to demand "more urgency." Demanding speed without changing routines, culture, or the proxy structure that locks the organization in place is theater. The speed-up will fail and the diagnosis will get worse.

## Core Concept

A supertanker with its engines on hard reverse can take a mile to come to a stop. That property of mass — resistance to a change in motion — is *inertia*. In a business, inertia is an organization's unwillingness or inability to adapt to changing circumstances. Even with change programs running at full throttle, it can take many years to alter a large company's basic functioning. Inertia is not laziness, and it is not stupidity. It is the accumulated weight of routines, culture, and locked-in profit streams that worked well in a previous regime and now cannot be discarded just because someone has drawn up a new plan.

But inertia is only half the story. *Entropy* is the other half, and it is sneakier. In physics, entropy is a system's degree of disorder, and the second law says that in an isolated system, entropy always increases. In organizations, the analogue is real: weakly managed firms tend to become less organized and less focused on their own. Product lines blur, brands get extended into one another's territory, prices drift toward whatever the sales force needs them to be, shipping schedules drift toward whatever the factory needs them to be, and bonuses get paid as if they were entitlements. *Entropy works for free; you have to work against it.* Without constant weeding, the original imposed order — the strategy — disappears under the clutter. Most consulting work is, beneath all the high-level concepts, the unglamorous task of undoing entropy: cleaning up the debris that grows in every organizational garden.

Inertia comes in three distinct flavors, and the cure is different for each. **The inertia of routine** is when an organization keeps doing what it did before because that's what the procedures, models, reports, and rules of thumb generate — Continental Airlines after deregulation was still using the regulator-era Boeing Fleet Planner that "predicted" prices by adding a 12% markup to costs, because that's what the CAB had paid and the routine hadn't been rewritten. The cure is straightforward but politically costly: redesign the routines, hire managers from firms with better methods, replace the people who built and depend on the old methods. **Cultural inertia** is harder — culture is the set of work norms enforced daily by small groups who take their cue from the alpha. AT&T in 1985 had Bell Labs and could not write working consumer software, because the culture was research, not product. You don't change culture with a memo. You break it down by simplification: strip out layers, halt non-essential operations, fragment political coalitions, then triage — close some units, repair some, build new ones around different alphas. Then a challenge to anneal the new norms. **Inertia by proxy** is when the organization isn't actually slow — it has *chosen* not to respond, because responding would cannibalize a still-profitable stream of business locked in by the *customers'* slowness to switch. PSFS in 1980 didn't offer money-market accounts because its retired depositors weren't going to chase yield, and that pool of 5%-money was extraordinarily profitable. The Bell operating companies sat on T1 because DSL would have priced T1 out of existence. Inertia by proxy looks like sleepy management and is actually a calculation; it disappears the moment the calculation flips, often suddenly, and the firms that had been picking up the slow incumbent's customers can be left without a business overnight.

## How to Apply

1. **Before recommending any strategic action to a large incumbent, name the inertia.** Is it routine, culture, or proxy? You cannot prescribe the same fix for all three. Routine yields to method redesign. Culture yields only to simplification, fragmentation, and changing the alphas. Proxy yields only when the underlying customer-stickiness collapses or when leadership decides to abandon the protected revenue stream itself.

2. **Look at the rivals' inertia, not just your own.** Successful strategies often owe a great deal to the inertia and inefficiency of rivals. Netflix passed Blockbuster because Blockbuster could not abandon its retail-store focus. Apple and Google moved through the opening Microsoft left in mobile because Microsoft was slow to improve the OS. Understanding the inertia of rivals can be just as vital as understanding your own strengths — it tells you which moves they will not match.

3. **Build a hump chart on whatever's separable.** Rank operations, products, regions, or customers in order of decreasing gain to operating, and graph the cumulative total. If the bars rise smoothly to a maximum, you have a focused business. If there is a true *hump* — bars rising to a peak and then sagging downward — the loss-making operations are being subsidized by the profitable ones, and the cross-subsidies are hidden by the measurement system. Sustained over time, a hump is the visible signature of entropy. The number-one outlet at Denton's earned more than the entire 28-outlet chain.

4. **Treat positive maintenance as the leader's job, not as overhead.** Sloan's design of GM in 1921 — Chevrolet, Pontiac, Buick, Cadillac, each with a clear price band, no internal cannibalization — was a masterpiece of imposed order. By the 1980s, with each division pushing on its own incentives, Chevrolets and Pontiacs and Buicks were all clustering around $25,500. The design hadn't been overruled; it had simply not been maintained. Nobody decided to destroy it. Entropy did. Planning a garden is more interesting than weeding it. Without weeding, the garden disappears.

5. **Disrupt the inertia of routine before any new strategy can take hold.** This is the hardest one for outsiders to accept. You don't get to "execute the new strategy" in a body that is still running the old routines, still rewarding the old behaviors, still measuring with the old proxies, still staffed with the people who invested decades in the old methods. The new strategy will get filtered through the old machine and come out looking like the old result. This is why GM needed bankruptcy, why Nokia couldn't ship a finger-touch phone for four years after the CEO made it the priority "in every single meeting," and why most "transformations" produce slogans rather than transformations. You disrupt the routine first.

## Examples

**Situation:** Continental Airlines, 1980. Just two years into deregulation. The CEO has $400 million ready to spend on new aircraft. The executive committee already "knows" that long-haul fares will rise — they have a planning model that says so. They want to grab long-haul routes. The CEO has been giving aggressive speeches about the new competitive era.

**Application:** The "planning model" is the Boeing Fleet Planner — the same tool the airline used in the regulation era to *justify fares to the CAB.* It computes cost per available-seat-mile, adds a 12% return on capital, and outputs a fare. It has nothing to do with supply, demand, or competition; it is a regulator-era instrument predicting what the regulator would have allowed. Inertia of routine: the rules of thumb (*go for long-haul; reequip when everyone else does; fares cover total costs*) were optimal under regulation, and they are running unchanged inside an organization that thinks of itself as newly competitive. The aggressive posture is real. The routines underneath it are not.

**Result:** Long-haul fares fell 27% from 1984 to 1985, exactly contrary to the model. Continental was the first major casualty — taken over by Texas Air, bankrupt by 1983. The aggressive speeches were not the strategy. The unchanged routines were. Most of the legacy carriers spent the next two decades learning that lesson the hard way; only Southwest, which never had the regulation-era routines, was consistently profitable.

**Situation:** General Motors, 1921 vs. 2008. In 1921 Alfred Sloan designs the product line: Chevrolet, Oakland (later Pontiac), Oldsmobile, Buick, Cadillac — each in its own price band, "quality competition against cars below a given price tag, and price competition against cars above." Sells off Sheridan, dissolves Scripps-Booth. By 1931 GM is the world's largest automaker. Walking through a 1950s suburban neighborhood, you can tell who lives in each house by the car out front.

**Application:** This is design — order imposed on chaos. Each quarter, each year, each decade, corporate leadership has to work to maintain it. Each division head can grow their division's revenue by encroaching on the next price band; if you don't push back, the boundaries blur. By 2008, with Oldsmobile already dead, GM offered nine vehicles at the $25,500 price point — two Chevrolets, one Saturn, four Pontiacs, two Buicks. Toyota offered two cars at the same price. Nobody at GM had ever decided to destroy Sloan's design. They had just stopped weeding the garden. *Entropy works for free; you have to work against it.* Compounded by routine inertia (decades of badge engineering), cultural inertia (the GM Salute and the GM Nod, where responsibility was always somewhere else), and chain-link inertia, by 2008 it took bankruptcy to break it. June 2009: Chapter 11. Saturn, Pontiac, Hummer dropped under court protection.

**Result:** The discipline lesson is sharp. A great strategy is not a one-time act of design. It is a one-time act of design *plus* daily, weekly, decade-long work to keep the design from blurring. The 1921 plan worked. Nothing in 1980 was actively wrong. Nobody chose to fail. The failure was the absence of maintenance, and maintenance is not a glamorous job, which is why it doesn't get done.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Diagnose every incumbent failure as "cultural."
**Why:** Cultural inertia is a real and important category, but it is also the laziest available answer, and it leads to the laziest available prescription ("we need a culture change program"). If the actual mechanism is routine inertia (the ERP system, the planning model, the comp formula), changing the culture won't move it. If the actual mechanism is inertia by proxy (the protected T1 revenue, the protected dealer network, the protected regulator relationship), changing the culture won't move it either. Name the mechanism specifically. The fix follows from the mechanism, not from the platitude.

**Don't:** Recommend a "transformation" without a plan to disrupt the routines first.
**Why:** Strategy that gets fed into an unchanged organizational machine comes out as the old result wearing new vocabulary. The CEO of Nokia made finger-touch a priority and brought it up in every meeting; nothing shipped for four years because the matrix had no one with the authority and budget to abandon Symbian and develop a new OS. The routines beat the priority. If the routines, the comp, the org chart, and the alphas are intact, the transformation is theater.

**Don't:** Confuse entropy with strategic failure.
**Why:** A company can be running entropy at high velocity — its product lines blurred, its costs cross-subsidized, its bonus structure detached from results — and the financial statements will look fine for a long time, because entropy is gradual. Conversely, a company can have a real strategic problem (the technology shifted, a wave of change passed it by) that has nothing to do with entropy. The fixes are different. Entropy is fixed by management — straightforward, unglamorous, hump-chart-driven cleanup work. Strategic failure is fixed by diagnosis and a new guiding policy. Don't confuse the two; it's the most common reason boards reach for the wrong CEO.

**Don't:** Underestimate inertia by proxy.
**Why:** It looks like the rival is asleep. They are not. They are extracting one last cycle of profits from a customer base that hasn't switched yet. The minute it flips, all the customers you acquired during the slumber are the most price-sensitive ones — they self-selected to leave. When the incumbent wakes up and competes, your business may evaporate. The new-network telecoms (WorldCom, the CLECs) all looked like geniuses for a few years and then died together when the Bells finally offered DSL. Picking up business from a slow rival is not the same as having an advantage; it is renting one.
