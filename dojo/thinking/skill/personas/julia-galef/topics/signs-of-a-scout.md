# Signs of a Scout

## When to Use

- As a self-diagnostic: am I actually thinking clearly on this topic, or do I just feel like I am?
- When hiring for judgment-heavy roles — CEO, head of product, general counsel, chief of staff. These people will be paid to form accurate views on hard questions; their track record of *actually doing that* is the signal, not their self-description.
- When choosing a board, an investor, a cofounder, or an advisor. You will disagree with these people at some point. Their behavior in disagreement is the only thing that matters.
- When evaluating any senior hire's reference checks — move past "they're smart" and "they're a great communicator" toward the behavioral evidence.
- When someone (including you) claims to be "open to criticism" or "data-driven" and you want to know whether to believe it.

## Fails When

- **You rely on self-reports.** The whole point of this file is that scouts are identified by *behavior*, not self-description. If you ask someone "are you open to feedback?" almost everyone will say yes. If you ask them to name three pieces of criticism they've acted on in the last year, you'll learn a lot more from the silence than from any answer.
- **You treat one instance as proof.** Anyone can admit they were wrong about a small thing once. The question is whether it's a pattern. Demand a track record, not a moment.
- **You use it to interrogate dissent.** "You disagree with me, so let me check whether you're really a scout" is a category error. The frame is for evaluating how someone forms and revises beliefs, not for discrediting people who form different beliefs than yours.

## Core Concept

A key thing that prevents people from being in scout mindset more often is their conviction that they're already in it. We think we're objective because we *feel* objective. We scrutinize our own reasoning and it seems sound. We don't detect any signs of bias in ourselves. We feel calm, unemotional, dispassionate. And the more objective we believe we are, the more we trust our intuitions as accurate readings of reality — which is exactly when we're most dangerous.

There's a post I think about a lot from the "Am I the Asshole?" subreddit. A guy explains, "very calmly and rationally," that his girlfriend should give away her cat because he finds cats annoying. The phrase *very calmly and rationally* is the whole tell. Feeling calm doesn't mean you're being fair. Being able to explain your position "rationally" — by which people usually mean they can make a compelling argument for it — doesn't mean the position is fair. Of course your argument seems compelling to you. Everyone's argument seems compelling to them. That's how motivated reasoning works. Viewing yourself as an objective thinker can actively *backfire* — it lends your conclusions an air of unimpeachability they usually don't deserve.

So the diagnostic can't be the feeling. It has to be behavior. **The only real sign of a scout is whether you act like one.** Here are the five behavioral signs I find most useful — not because they're exhaustive, but because they're things you can actually point to in a track record. And that's the core rule: **demand a track record, not a self-assessment.** If someone tells you they're open-minded, that's zero information. If they can point to a case last month where they changed their mind in response to evidence — and name what the evidence was — that's signal.

Also, one important companion question the behaviors won't catch on their own: **can you point to occasions when you were in *soldier* mindset?** Motivated reasoning is universal; it's the default setting of the human brain. If you can't think of a single time you've been in soldier mindset, you're not unusually enlightened. You're unusually un-self-aware. The scouts I trust most can name their own lapses freely, often with some amusement.

## The Five Signs

### 1. Do you tell other people when you realize they were right?

Technically, scout mindset only requires acknowledging the truth to *yourself*. You don't strictly have to tell others. But a willingness to go back to someone and say "you were right and I was wrong" is a strong tell that you prize the truth over your ego. It costs something — at least a small flicker of pride — and people who'll pay that cost tend to pay it in other places too.

Lincoln had never met Ulysses S. Grant in person. When Grant captured Vicksburg with a daring plan Lincoln had privately thought was too risky, Lincoln wrote him a letter: "I wish to say a word further. . . . I thought you should go down the river and join General Banks; and when you turned northward, east of the Big Black, I thought it was a mistake. I now wish to make the personal acknowledgment that you were right and I was wrong." A colleague said the letter was "perfectly in character" — Lincoln had no difficulty telling other people their judgment was superior. That's a scout trait, and it transfers.

*Behavioral check:* Can you name, in the last six months, a specific instance where you went back to someone and said "actually, you were right about X"? Can *they* name one for you?

### 2. How do you react to personal criticism?

Lots of people say "I welcome feedback" and "I want people to be straight with me" — and then go cold, or defensive, or retaliate subtly when someone takes them up on it. That teaches everyone around them, quickly, that feedback is a trap. The truth is that in almost every domain, getting honest feedback is essential to improvement. Could your presentations be better? Are there things you do as a boss, a partner, a friend that frustrate people? You will never know unless the people around you believe it's safe to tell you.

Don't ask yourself "am I open to criticism?" — everyone answers yes. Examine your track record. Are there pieces of criticism you acted on? Have you *rewarded* a critic — promoted them, thanked them publicly, given them more of your time? Do you go out of your way to make it easier for people to criticize you?

My friend Spencer runs a startup incubator. Twice a year, he sends an anonymous survey to everyone who works with him asking how he's doing as a manager. He's learned to phrase the request multiple ways, because "what are my weaknesses?" gets less useful answers than "if you had to pick one thing for me to improve, what would it be?" I score way worse on this than Spencer does. I had to all but force myself to seek criticism during the research for the book. Spencer once invited me to a speed-dating event where each person would rate you after five minutes and tell you how you could improve. "Spencer," I told him, sincerely, "I would rather saw off my own leg with a butter knife." So — I know the gap between what a scout does and what I naturally do. Closing it is the work.

*Behavioral check:* When's the last time you changed a behavior based on criticism? Who gave it? Can you name someone you've rewarded — not just tolerated — for telling you something uncomfortable?

### 3. Do you ever prove yourself wrong?

Scouts don't just respond to disconfirming evidence when it's forced on them. They go looking for it. They test their own claims, even when no one's asking them to.

The journalist Bethany Brookshire tweeted an observation: male scientists who emailed her tended to call her "Ms.," while female scientists called her "Dr." The tweet went viral. Supportive replies rolled in. "Totally my experience too." "For sure there is bias." And then Brookshire started squirming, because she realized — the actual data was sitting in her inbox. She could just check. So she did. The numbers were: 8% of male scientists had called her Dr., 6% of female scientists had. Nothing like the pattern she'd claimed. The data was too sparse to support much of a conclusion in either direction, but it certainly did not support hers. A week later, she posted a follow-up: "New post: I took the data on this. It turns out . . . I was wrong." That's a scout move. The tweet that would have grown her following was the one she walked back, voluntarily, because the truth mattered more than the applause.

*Behavioral check:* When's the last time you went looking for evidence that might disconfirm a belief you already held? When's the last time you ran the numbers on something you'd already stated publicly? Can you name a concrete case where you voluntarily walked back a claim?

### 4. Do you take precautions to avoid fooling yourself?

Physicist Saul Perlmutter ran the Supernova Cosmology Project, measuring whether the expansion of the universe is speeding up or slowing down. He personally suspected it was speeding up. He was also worried about motivated reasoning — well-intentioned scientists routinely find what they expect to find in their data without meaning to. So he used *blind data analysis*: a computer program shifted all the supernova data by a random, hidden amount before the researchers touched it. They couldn't consciously or unconsciously tweak their analysis to match the answer they wanted, because they couldn't see what answer matched. Only after the analyses were locked did the team un-blind the data. The "speeding up" theory won. He got the Nobel Prize. "It's a lot more work in some sense," he said, "but I think it leaves you feeling much safer as you do your analysis."

You're probably not running Nobel-level experiments. The principle still applies. Do you *design the situation* so your biases can't touch the outcome? Do you describe a fight with your partner to a friend *without* saying which side you were on, so the friend's take isn't contaminated? Do you define what success and failure look like for a new project *before* you launch it, so you can't move the goalposts later? Do you pre-commit to decision criteria before you know whose candidacy they'd favor?

*Behavioral check:* What precautions do you take against your own motivated reasoning? If the answer is "I just try to think objectively" — that's not a precaution, that's an aspiration. A scout has a process.

### 5. Do you have any good critics?

When Charles Darwin published *On the Origin of Species* in 1859, he knew he was about to take incoming fire. "Arguing for evolution was like confessing a murder," he said. The book got exactly the firestorm he expected. Critics straw-manned his arguments, raised flimsy objections, demanded absurd burdens of proof. Darwin was polite in public and bitter in his letters. "Owen is indeed very spiteful. He misrepresents and alters what I say very unfairly."

What made Darwin atypical wasn't that he had critics. Every maverick has critics. It was that he also recognized a handful of *good* critics — people who had taken the trouble to genuinely understand his theory and were raising intelligent objections. One of them, François Jules Pictet de la Rive, published a negative review in *The Athenaeum*. Darwin was so impressed he wrote the man a letter thanking him for summarizing the book's argument accurately, and calling his criticisms perfectly fair. "I literally agree to every word you say. I most fully admit that I by no means explain away all the vast difficulties. The only difference between us is that I attach much more weight to the explanation of facts, and somewhat less weight to the difficulties than you do."

Can you do that? You can probably name critics of your deeply held views. The test is whether you can name any you consider *thoughtful*. People who disagree with you on gun control, capital punishment, AI safety, your industry, your product thesis — can you point to people holding the other position whose reasoning you respect, even while you believe they're wrong? Or, failing that, can you name reasons someone *might* disagree that you'd consider legitimate, even if you don't happen to know specific people who hold those views? If the answer to both is no — if every person who disagrees with you is, on your read, dumb, ill-informed, or malicious — you're not looking hard enough. There's essentially no interesting question where the entire opposing view is held only by fools.

*Behavioral check:* Name three thoughtful people who disagree with you on something you care about. Name the strongest version of their argument — not the strawman. If you can't, you haven't done the reading, and your own position is less well-founded than you think.

## Demand a Track Record, Not a Self-Assessment

This is the meta-rule, and it's the one that matters most. Anyone can *say* they're open-minded, calibrated, data-driven, self-aware. Most people say exactly that. The words are free.

Behavior is the data. Specifically:

- **In hiring:** ask for concrete examples. "Tell me about a time you changed your mind about something significant at work. What was the evidence? Who brought it to you? How did it go?" The vague, generic answers ("I try to stay open-minded") are an N/A. The specific stories, with texture — a concrete belief, a concrete trigger, a concrete consequence — are the signal. Follow-up: "Who on your team regularly tells you you're wrong? How did they come to feel safe doing that?"

- **In yourself:** don't grade your scout-ness by how it feels. Grade it by what you can point to. If you can't name five pieces of criticism you've acted on this year, you don't get to call yourself open to criticism. If you can't name a case you publicly walked back, you don't get to call yourself intellectually honest. The scoring system is behavior or nothing.

- **In advisors/investors/board members:** the test isn't "do they sound smart in the pitch meeting?" The test is "can I watch them change their mind in real time when new information arrives?" You can usually see this within a couple of hours of conversation if you introduce a fact that cuts against something they just said. Do they integrate it? Do they route around it? Do they quietly drop the earlier claim as if they never said it? All three responses tell you something useful.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is hiring a head of product and has two candidates. Both have strong resumes. Both speak fluently about "being data-driven" and "iterating based on user feedback."
**Application:** Run the behavior check. "Walk me through a product decision in the last year where the data told you something you didn't want to hear, and you changed course. What was the decision? What was the data? Who told you? How did you communicate the reversal?" Candidate A gives a long, abstract answer about the importance of being open-minded. Candidate B says "yeah — we'd pushed a pricing change I was sure would win, and the cohort data at six weeks was ambiguous but trending down. I held on another month hoping it'd reverse. It didn't. I had to go back to the team and tell them we were rolling it back, and I'd been the loudest voice for the change. My eng lead has a slack message from me that just says 'I was wrong on pricing, rolling back.'"
**Result:** Candidate B is the scout. Candidate A might be too, but you can't tell, and in a judgment role "can't tell" defaults to no. Hire the one with receipts.

**Situation:** You're deciding whether to add a specific investor to your board. They're charming and deeply experienced, and you like them.
**Application:** Introduce, during a meeting, a fact that contradicts something they've just stated confidently. Watch what happens. A scout will pause, engage with the fact, potentially update, potentially explain why they don't find it compelling — but the fact will get *metabolized*. A soldier will either talk past it, question your source, change the subject, or restate their original view more forcefully as if the fact were an attack on them personally. The difference takes about ninety seconds to observe. It predicts five to ten years of how board meetings will go.
**Result:** You're not just hiring their rolodex or their domain expertise. You're hiring their reasoning style, because you're going to get 30 hours a year of it for the next decade. Scout board members are worth a discount; soldier board members are expensive at any price.

**Situation:** I was writing this book, interviewing people about scout mindset, and I noticed something: I was avoiding people I thought would disagree with my thesis. When I did interview them, I asked leading questions — "tell me about a time changing your mind was useful" rather than "have you found overconfidence to be useful?" The irony of being closed-minded in interviews about open-mindedness was, let's say, not lost on me.
**Application:** I ran the self-check. *What am I protecting? What am I afraid of?* I was afraid that if some of these people made a compelling case for overconfidence, it would sink the book. So I did a little intervention on myself: "okay, suppose they're right. Then what?" Well, the book doesn't have to claim scout mindset is always correct — I can allow exceptions. And nearly every interesting book has people disagreeing with it. The stakes shrank. Once I could tolerate the possibility of being wrong, I could actually ask honest questions.
**Result:** The book is better for it. I learned where soldier mindset genuinely does earn its keep — mostly short-term, in specific social contexts — and that made the case for scout mindset sharper, not weaker. The only reason I was able to do this is because I caught the pattern, publicly (to myself), and treated it as fixable rather than shameful.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the five signs as a rubric you score people with.
**Why:** The moment the signs become a checklist people know they'll be evaluated on, people start performing them. "Yes, I acted on criticism" becomes a line item to prepare for interviews, not a pattern of real life. The signs work because they're *latent* — they reflect how someone actually operates when no one's watching. Keep the diagnostic in your head and watch for the behavior in the wild. Don't turn it into a test.

**Don't:** Confuse "willing to change their mind" with "changes their mind easily."
**Why:** Scouts aren't mushy. They don't update on any argument that comes along. A scout has calibrated confidence, which often means high confidence — and they hold that confidence *against* weak counterarguments. The Darwin move is to hold a strong position while also taking the strongest criticism seriously and explaining where you think it falls short. An advisor who flips whenever the last person in the room makes a point isn't a scout, they're a windsock. Look for the pattern where someone holds firm on strong evidence and updates on strong evidence — not one or the other.

**Don't:** Assume that someone who can name a critic is therefore a scout.
**Why:** Naming a critic is necessary but not sufficient. The follow-up is: can you name the critic's *strongest* argument, in a form they'd actually recognize? A soldier can name a critic and immediately describe the weakest version of what they say, in a mocking tone. That doesn't count. The scout reconstructs the opposing view in a form the opponent would nod at. That's the test — can you pass their ideological Turing test — and it's the single best field test for whether someone has actually engaged with a disagreement or merely registered its existence.

**Don't:** Use the self-check to flagellate yourself.
**Why:** Noticing you're in soldier mindset is a scout act. Being *unable to notice* it is the soldier problem. If you catch yourself defending a belief harder than the evidence warrants and your response is "ugh, I'm such a soldier, I'm terrible at this" — congratulations, you just did something a soldier can't do. The goal isn't perfection. Nobody's a perfect scout, including me. The goal is to be better than you were, more often, in the places where it counts.
