# Avoiding Stupidity

## When to Use
- The founder is searching for a clever move, a breakthrough, a silver bullet. That is where the biggest stupidities get made. The question to ask instead is what obvious dumb thing are we about to do.
- You are reviewing a plan, a hire, a deal. The useful move is not to imagine how it wins — that's the easy part, the brain manufactures it for free. The useful move is to run the checklist of how smart people routinely wreck themselves and see if any apply.
- The decision feels exciting, daring, bold. Excitement is a bias, not a plan. Exciting-and-daring-and-bold is the exact texture of the classic catastrophe.
- Any major commitment. Before the forward case, run the stupidity check. Takes ten minutes. Saves decades.

## Fails When
- **Treated as "never do anything."** Avoiding stupidity is not inaction. The rare great opportunity still requires decisive action — Munger called them lollapaloozas of the good kind. The rule is don't be dumb; it isn't don't move.
- **Used as a license for mediocre plans.** "At least we're not being stupid" is not a strategy. You still need a real edge. The point is the edge compounds only if you also avoid the predictable mistakes; without that, no edge survives.
- **Treated as a personality trait rather than a discipline.** Everyone thinks they are not stupid. The exercise is running the actual checklist on the actual decision, in writing, out loud, with someone willing to tell you the truth. "I am generally cautious" is not the same thing.

## Core Concept

It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent. That is the whole thing, right there. That is the meta-principle that sits behind every other model in this latticework. Read it again.

The world is full of people chasing alpha. They want the brilliant insight, the contrarian bet, the twenty percent edge that will make them rich and celebrated. And most of them lose. Why? Because they took on leverage, or operated outside their circle of competence, or acted under a lollapalooza of five biases pointing the same way, or ignored the incentives of the person bringing them the deal, or refused to look at the disconfirming evidence. Any one of those will eat the twenty percent edge and more. Do three of them at once and you are broke. The brilliance people chase is real, but it is overwhelmed — routinely, predictably — by the stupidity they did not bother to avoid. That is the math of it.

Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there. Most people smile at the rustic's ignorance and miss the basic wisdom. The rustic had it right. You do not need to find the one great move. You need to consistently not make the ten dumb ones everybody else makes. The list of dumb ones is short, public, and has been known for centuries. Envy. Resentment. Self-pity. Ideology. Unreliability. Single-model thinking. Operating outside your circle. Acting under four or more biases. Leverage you can't service. Denial of a bad number. Trusting enthusiastic advisors paid when you act. I did not invent that list. It is the list of how smart people blow themselves up, compiled over thousands of years of recorded human folly. Read it. Avoid those things. You are already ahead of ninety percent of the field, and you haven't done anything clever yet.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the stupidity checklist before the brilliance checklist.** For any major decision, ask first: am I about to do any of the classic dumb things? Out of circle? Under four or more biases? Using leverage I can't service in a bad scenario? Trusting an advisor with a conflict of interest? Chasing a wave I don't understand? Optimizing to the edge with no margin of safety? Work through the list. If any item hits, stop, and deal with it before touching the forward case.

2. **Invert the question.** Instead of "how do I make this work," ask "what would guarantee this fails." List the failures. Check whether you're doing any of them. Most forward plans collapse the moment you write down the inverted list — not because the plan is bad, but because the inverted list reveals the three things the plan-maker hadn't thought about. Invert, always invert.

3. **Count the biases.** For anything fraught, name the psychological tendencies pulling you toward the decision. Social proof because the peers are doing it. Consistency because you already committed publicly. Authority because a senior figure endorsed it. Deprival super-reaction because someone else might take it. If four or more point the same direction, full stop. Mandatory delay. That is how you avoid the lollapalooza that kills companies.

4. **Audit the incentives.** Who is advising you? What do they get paid if you act? What happens to them if the decision is wrong? If nothing happens to them, discount their confidence to near zero. Especially fear professional advice when it is especially good for the advisor. That's not cynicism; it is pattern recognition across centuries.

5. **Check the circle.** Am I actually competent in this domain, or am I the chauffeur? Can I answer the follow-up question? Can I identify what would disprove my thesis? If not, stop — you are outside your circle, and confidence here is fake confidence and it will get punished.

6. **Refuse envy, resentment, and self-pity categorically.** They are the three habits most likely to produce stupid decisions, and they are the three most people never name. Envy drives founders into markets they don't understand because a peer is succeeding there. Resentment drives decisions aimed at proving the doubters wrong rather than building the business. Self-pity paralyzes. None of them produce an hour of useful work. Refuse them. Not regulate them. Refuse them.

7. **Study the history of human folly.** Not your own mistakes — other people's. They did most of the kinds of stupid you are going to do, and they wrote books about it. You can learn those failures for free, in your chair, before you commit capital. The founders who end up fine are, largely, the ones who read about how other founders wrecked themselves and then declined to repeat the pattern. The founders who don't read are condemned to invent the mistakes themselves, at their own cost.

## Examples

**Situation:** The 1990s hedge fund Long-Term Capital Management, with an average IQ among principals that Munger estimated at 160, blew itself up using heavily leveraged strategies.
**Application:** Brilliance was not the problem. These were genuinely smart people. The problem was leverage, plus overconfidence, plus the assumption that their model was right in the tail cases. Three standard stupidities compounding. The stupidities ate the brilliance, and then ate the firm.
**Result:** Smart, hardworking people aren't exempted from professional disasters from overconfidence. Munger's words. The lesson is not that they weren't smart enough. It is that they spent their effort on brilliance and didn't spend it on avoiding the three obvious dumb things. If they had, the fund would still exist.

**Situation:** A founder considers taking on $20M in venture debt to extend runway, because "we're sure we'll raise the next round in six months."
**Application:** Check the stupidity list. Leverage you can't service in a bad scenario — yes. Optimizing to the edge with no margin — yes. Operating under overconfidence bias ("we're sure") — yes. Denial of the base rate for bridge rounds in your stage and sector — probably yes. Four dumb things already. Before we discuss whether the debt is clever, we notice the floor is on fire.
**Result:** The decision reverses. Raise less, cut faster, match your runway to the base rate of your raise, not to the optimistic scenario. The "clever" debt move was four stupidities stacked. Removing them doesn't make you brilliant; it just keeps you alive long enough for brilliance to matter.

**Situation:** The rustic who wanted to know where he was going to die, so he'd never go there.
**Application:** Most people laugh at the rustic and miss his point. You do not need the metaphysics of a good life. You need the list of places where people die — pride, envy, leverage, self-pity, single-model thinking, unreliability, ideology, chemical dependency, denial — and you need to not go there. The list is short. Avoid it. That covers most of the ground.
**Result:** A life, or a career, or a company, spent consistently not doing the obvious stupid things. Not glamorous. Not contrarian. Just durable. Warren Buffett and I built Berkshire Hathaway this way, and it's hard to argue with the result.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Chase twenty percent alpha through extraordinary insight while ignoring thirty percent losses through predictable mistakes.
**Why:** The math is not close. Consistently not losing thirty percent to the known failure modes is dramatically more valuable, over time, than occasionally capturing an extra twenty percent through brilliance. The brilliance gets eaten by the next stupidity. The absence of stupidity compounds. Of course it does.

**Don't:** Treat "avoiding stupidity" as a personality — "I'm generally careful."
**Why:** Stupidity-avoidance is a discipline, not a disposition. The exercise is running the actual checklist on the actual decision, in writing, with a devil's advocate. Everybody thinks they're generally careful. The classic disasters were all executed by generally careful people who happened to skip the checklist on the one that mattered.

**Don't:** Use "avoiding stupidity" as cover for refusing to act when action is indicated.
**Why:** The rare great opportunity must be acted on decisively. The whole point of avoiding the background noise of stupidity is so you have the capital, the credibility, and the clarity to move when the real chance appears. If avoiding stupidity has become a reason to never commit, you have misunderstood the principle — it is a filter for bad moves, not an alibi for passivity.

**Don't:** Think stupidity is a thing smart people don't do.
**Why:** The list of documented catastrophes by very high-IQ people is long. LTCM. Enron's quants. Countless founders with advanced degrees. Intelligence does not prevent stupidity — it often enables it, by giving you the rhetorical tools to rationalize a dumb move. The check against stupidity is not intelligence. It is the written checklist, the inversion, the incentive audit, and the cold look at what biases are operating. You run those regardless of your IQ. Your IQ is not your protection.

**Don't:** Confuse "not stupid" with "timid."
**Why:** Berkshire Hathaway did not become Berkshire Hathaway by being timid. It became Berkshire Hathaway by being fanatically disciplined about not making the obvious dumb mistakes, and then being aggressive when the rare great opportunity came along. Those are two sides of the same coin. You earn the right to be aggressive by having avoided the ruinous moves. You can't skip to the aggression.

Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there. That's the whole method. Read it again.
