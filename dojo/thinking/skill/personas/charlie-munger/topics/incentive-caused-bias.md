# Incentive-Caused Bias

## When to Use
- Any time you receive advice from someone whose pay goes up when you act on it. Bankers. Brokers. Consultants. Lawyers. Contractors. Commissioned salespeople. Internal corp-dev people whose careers advance when deals close.
- When you are designing or changing a compensation system, a bonus, a commission, a quota, or any structure that rewards people based on measurable outputs. Especially if those outputs can be faked.
- When an organization keeps producing the same kind of bad behavior, nobody can explain it, and everyone involved seems to genuinely believe they're doing the right thing. That is almost always incentive-caused bias operating below awareness.
- When you find yourself unusually enthusiastic about a course of action that happens to benefit you personally. Especially then. Your brain is the one being fooled.

## Fails When
- **Used as a blanket excuse for cynicism.** The point is not to assume everyone is crooked. The point is to know that the human mind reshapes belief to fit the paycheck, and to build systems accordingly.
- **Applied only to others and never to yourself.** You are not immune. If you think you are, that is itself a bias. If the incentive benefits you, assume your belief is distorted and seek outside discipline.
- **Used to explain away legitimate disagreement.** "He's only saying that because he's paid to" is sometimes true and sometimes a way to dismiss an argument you don't want to engage with. Do the work first.

## Core Concept

Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome. Of course. If there is one single bias you have to get into your head before any of the others, it is this one. The power of incentives is routinely underestimated — even by people who claim to know about incentives. Never a year passes that I don't get some surprise that pushes my appreciation of incentive superpower a little further.

Here is the crucial thing. Incentives don't just change behavior. They change belief. The surgeon paid per surgery does not think, "I'll do unnecessary operations for money." He thinks, "The gallbladder is the source of all evil, and I'm saving lives by removing it quickly." He believes it. His brain, which is built by evolution to rationalize whatever benefits him, has done the work for him without telling him. Whose bread I eat, his song I sing — and the singer does not know he is singing.

This is why professional advice is so dangerous when it is especially good for the advisor. The investment banker does not think he is selling you a bad deal. He thinks this is a good deal because his brain has sorted the evidence in whatever way is consistent with his fee. The management consultant does not think he is recommending unnecessary work. He thinks the work is necessary because his firm bills by the hour. You do not need to impute bad motive. The motive was installed by Darwin long before any individual showed up to the meeting. The job is not to find the crook. The job is to build systems that make the crookedness irrelevant.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit every incentive structure touching a decision you care about.** Before you take any recommendation, write down: who benefits if this decision is made? Who pays if it turns out wrong? If the decision-maker wins either way, his confidence is free and therefore worthless. Discount the recommendation accordingly. Do this for your advisors, your employees, your board, your partners, and yourself.

2. **Fear advice that is exceptionally good for the advisor.** When a banker is unusually enthusiastic about a deal, when a consultant is unusually insistent on a long engagement, when a broker is unusually urgent about a trade, the enthusiasm itself is the warning sign. Assume the intensity of the advocacy is proportional to the advisor's fee. Then require independent verification — from someone who is not paid to reach the same conclusion.

3. **Build systems that make bad behavior hard, not just discouraged.** Patterson's cash register did not exhort the clerks to be honest. It made stealing physically impossible. That is the engineering principle. You do not run an honest store with moral suasion. You run an honest store with a cash register. Apply this everywhere. Expense policies are weak. Expense caps enforced by the software are strong. Honor systems fail. Structural systems survive.

4. **Never reward what can be easily faked.** The single most dangerous compensation structure is one that pays for a metric that can be produced without producing the underlying thing. Vanity metrics. Activity metrics. Anything that looks like progress and isn't. If you pay a sales team for signed contracts with no clawback, you will get signed contracts from customers who will never pay. Of course you will. That is what you paid for.

5. **Use Granny's Rule.** Make the unpleasant necessary thing happen before the reward. My grandmother made you eat your carrots before you got your ice cream. Same principle applies to compensation. Don't pay the commission at signing — pay it at renewal. Don't pay the bonus on launch — pay it on retention. The reward should arrive after the value has been delivered, not after the motion has been made.

6. **When the incentive benefits you, assume your belief is distorted.** This is the hardest move. If the decision is good for you personally — in money, in status, in power — your brain is already sorting the evidence to justify it. The only protection is external: people who will tell you the truth, whose pay does not go up when you act, who have no reason to flatter you. Build that around yourself on purpose, or you will end up a fool.

## Examples

**Situation:** Federal Express, early days. Packages have to move from plane to plane in one central location every night. If the shift is slow, the whole system breaks. The shift is always slow. Management has tried everything — moral suasion, threats, speeches, reorganization. Nothing works.

**Application:** Somebody finally got the happy thought. The night shift was paid by the hour. Of course it was slow. The longer it took, the more money each worker made. They were being paid to be slow. The company changed the pay structure: so much per shift, go home when it's done. Lo and behold — the problem cleared up overnight.

**Result:** The whole thing was an incentive problem, dressed up as a discipline problem. No amount of exhortation could have fixed it, because the people doing the exhorting did not understand the situation. They were treating it as a behavior issue when it was a structure issue. That is the lesson: if the structure rewards the bad behavior, the behavior is not going to stop, and the people doing the behavior will tell you they are doing the right thing. They will believe it. They believe it because their paycheck arrives on time.

**Situation:** Early Xerox. Joe Wilson, who had gone into government, had to come back to the company because he couldn't understand why their new and better copying machine was selling poorly in relation to the older, inferior one. Every report said the new machine was superior. Every customer who tried it preferred it. And yet it sold badly.

**Application:** When Wilson got back and looked, he found out the commission arrangement with the salesmen gave a bigger spiff on the old machine. That was the whole explanation. The salesmen weren't stupid and weren't dishonest. They were selling the machine that paid them more. They had probably convinced themselves the old one was better, or more reliable, or better for the customer. But the paycheck was the thing making the decision.

**Result:** Change the spiff, change the sales mix. A piece of paper in HR had more influence on Xerox's product strategy than the engineers in the lab. That is incentive superpower. And nobody in the executive suite thought to look there first — they looked at marketing, at training, at positioning. The incentive was invisible because it was doing exactly what it had been designed to do. It was only wrong because the design was wrong.

**Situation:** A founder is evaluating whether to do a $40M acquisition. The CFO has built the synergies model. The VP of Corporate Development is enthusiastic. The investment bankers pitched the deal. Everyone's excited.

**Application:** Count the incentives. The bankers get a fee if the deal closes — of course they're selling it. The VP Corp Dev's career advances when deals close, not when they are refused — his job is to bring deals, so he brings deals. The CFO has now invested months of his team's time in the model and would look foolish killing it — consistency. And the founder himself feels the thrill of a big move, the status of being an acquirer, the relief from the slower grind of organic growth — over-optimism plus liking his own plan. Every single person in the room is paid, in money or in ego, to close the deal. And not one of them is paid to kill it.

**Result:** Before the math, change who is in the room. Bring in someone whose career is advanced by finding reasons not to do the deal. A board member with no banker relationship. A competitor who has done acquisitions and got burned. Someone whose bread you do not eat. If the deal still looks good after that person has spent two weeks trying to kill it, now the deal is worth considering. Not before.

**Situation:** A founder sets the sales team's commission structure to pay on new deals closed, with no tie to customer retention or satisfaction.

**Application:** Predict the behavior. The sales team will close anyone. They will oversell features the product doesn't have, because the commission is banked before the customer churns. They will target buyers who shouldn't be buyers, because the commission is the same. They will resist handing off to customer success, because they don't get paid for it. They will genuinely believe every one of these behaviors is in the customer's interest. Of course they will. Their paycheck depends on that belief.

**Result:** The commission structure is a strategy document whether the founder wrote it as one or not. It tells the sales team exactly what to do. If it rewards appearance over outcome, the team will produce appearance and not outcome, and every one of them will tell you they are doing their best. The fix is not a values statement or a Monday-morning pep talk. The fix is to change the structure so that what is rewarded is what you actually want. Tie commission to retention. Clawback on churn within twelve months. Pay part on signature, part on renewal. Make it hard to get paid for selling the wrong customer, and you will stop seeing the wrong customer sold to.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume that because you've named the bias, you've escaped it.
**Why:** Naming is not neutralizing. You can know, intellectually, that you're paid to reach a particular conclusion and still reach that conclusion, because the bias operates below awareness. The only reliable protection is structural — hand the decision to someone who isn't paid to reach it, or require external validation from someone who pays a price if they get it wrong.

**Don't:** Rely on ethics, values, or culture to override a bad incentive structure.
**Why:** You can build a culture of integrity, recite values at every all-hands, and hire only good people. None of it matters if the paycheck says otherwise. The cash register always wins over the employee handbook. The employee handbook that contradicts the cash register is the one that gets ignored, not the cash register. Design the incentive right first; the culture talk is a bonus, not a substitute.

**Don't:** Pay bonuses on vanity metrics — signups, impressions, launch dates, anything trivially gamed.
**Why:** People will game whatever you pay them to game. They will believe the gaming is honest because they are paid to believe it. You will get a hockey stick in whatever metric you chose, and the underlying business will be unchanged or worse. If the metric can be produced without producing the underlying value, you are paying for the metric and not for the value. Stop.

**Don't:** Trust the confidence of an advisor who has no skin in the game when he's wrong.
**Why:** Advice is free when confidence is free. The advisor who is paid if you act but not penalized if you're wrong is running a one-way bet. His confidence will be calibrated to what he wants to happen, not to what should happen. Only advice from someone who pays when the decision goes bad is worth real weight. Everyone else is decoration.

**Don't:** Think incentive-caused bias is a finance problem or a sales problem.
**Why:** It is a human problem. It operates in marriages, in parenting, in academia, in government, in friendships. The professor whose tenure depends on a theory being true will find that theory true. The parent whose self-image depends on the child being gifted will find the child gifted. The politician whose reelection depends on a certain story will tell that story with genuine conviction. Wherever there is a paycheck — in money, in status, in belonging — there is incentive-caused bias. Hunt for it everywhere.

Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome. The right culture is one where no one makes a decision free of the discipline of competent people who know something about it — and none of them is paid to reach the particular conclusion. Build that culture, and you have eliminated half the stupidity you'd otherwise commit. Of course.
