# Low-End vs. New-Market Footholds

## When to Use
- A founder has a disruptive idea and is trying to decide which customers to launch with. Getting this choice wrong does not just hurt growth — it invalidates the disruptive framing entirely, because without a proper foothold the entrant ends up in a head-on sustaining fight it cannot win.
- An investor is diligencing the entry strategy of a startup claiming to be disruptive. "Who is your first customer?" is the right question, and the answer must fit one of two shapes.
- A corporate strategy team is evaluating where the next disruptive attack on their industry might originate. Low-end and new-market footholds are the only two places it can come from, and knowing which one is which sharpens the defensive posture.
- A product manager inside an established company is trying to scope a disruptive internal venture and needs to choose a cost structure, a distribution channel, and a pricing point — each of which differs meaningfully between the two foothold types.

## Fails When
- The user's product does not meet the disruption preconditions in the first place. Neither foothold is available for a product aimed at an incumbent's best customers with a better version of what they already buy. That is sustaining, and the foothold question does not apply.
- The user mistakes noncustomers for nonconsumers. A nonconsumer is someone who has a job to get done and is currently cobbling together an inadequate solution or doing without. Someone who simply has no job to be done is not a nonconsumer; they are a nonprospect, and no product, however accessible, will pull them in (Innovator's Solution ch. 4). The failed late-1990s internet-appliance products — cheap sub-$200 devices aimed at nonowners of PCs — failed because the nonowners had no job for which a cheap computer was the answer, not because the products were bad.
- The user tries to combine the two footholds into one launch strategy. Each has its own cost structure, distribution model, and performance metric. A blended launch usually means neither foothold is done well.

## Core Concept

Disruptive innovations originate in one of two places, and only two ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", HBR 2015; Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The first is a *low-end foothold* — existing, overserved customers at the bottom tier of the established market, who would be happy to buy a product with less performance if they could get it at a lower price. The second is a *new-market foothold* — nonconsumers who previously could not access the product at all because it was too expensive, too complicated, or required expertise or equipment they did not have, and who consequently went without or cobbled together a workaround. These are the two types of markets incumbents systematically overlook, because serving them requires a business model that earns attractive profits at lower price points and because the incumbents' own processes are focused on moving upmarket toward higher-margin customers. A disruptive innovation, by definition, starts from one of these two footholds. If it starts anywhere else, it is not a disruption — it is a head-on sustaining attack, and the theory predicts the incumbent will win.

Low-end disruptions take root inside the existing value network, at the bottom. The canonical example is steel minimills attacking rebar (Innovator's Dilemma ch. 4; Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Rebar specifications are loose, so low-quality minimill steel was acceptable there. Gross margins on rebar hovered near 7 percent — about 4 percent of industry tonnage — the least attractive tier the integrated mills produced. The integrated mills were almost relieved to exit. The minimills, with a 20 percent cost advantage, enjoyed attractive profits in rebar until they drove the last integrated mill out, at which point rebar prices collapsed and they had to move up — to angle iron, to bars and rods, to structural beams, finally to sheet steel. Each upward step found overserved mainstream customers happy to trade down a little quality for substantial price savings. Each upward step produced the same response from the integrated mills: flight. Low-end disruptions motivate incumbents to flee the attack (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Discount retailers versus full-service department stores followed the same pattern. Korean automakers entering the US market. MCI and Sprint versus AT&T's long-distance business. In every case, the entrant entered at the low end of the incumbent's existing value network with a cost structure built to earn attractive profits at discount prices, and the incumbent retreated upmarket toward higher-margin territory rather than defending the ground being invaded. The entrant then migrated upward behind them.

New-market disruptions compete against *nonconsumption* rather than against the incumbent ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015; Innovator's Solution ch. 4). The product is affordable and simple enough that it enables a whole population of people who historically lacked the money, equipment, or skills to begin doing something they were already trying to do. In the early days of photocopying, Xerox served large corporations with high-speed machines at premium prices; school librarians, bowling leagues, and small offices were priced out and made do with carbon paper or mimeographs. In the late 1970s, challengers introduced personal desktop copiers — slow, limited, inferior by Xerox's standards, but affordable and convenient enough to pull nonconsumers into the market. Sony's first battery-powered transistor pocket radio, 1955, targeted teenagers who could not afford the big vacuum-tube radios their parents bought; its sound was tinny and static-laced, but teenagers were thrilled, because their alternative was no radio at all (Innovator's Solution ch. 4). Honda's Super Cub motorbike competed against nonconsumption of motorcycles in North America — not against Harley-Davidson (Innovator's Dilemma ch. 7). The telephone competed against nonconsumption of short-distance communication, not against the telegraph (Seeing What's Next, Introduction). In each case, new-market disruptions induce incumbents to *ignore* the attacker — because the attack is not, at first, visible to them. The new-market disruptor is not taking their customers; they are building a different market on a new value network. By the time the disruptor's performance improves enough to pull incumbents' customers out of the original value network into the new one, the disruptor has already built the business model, the distribution, and the cost structure to win there.

Both footholds exploit the same mechanism — asymmetric motivation — but through opposite routes. Low-end disrupts the bottom of an existing market and motivates incumbents to flee. New-market builds outside the existing market and motivates incumbents to ignore. A disruptor must commit to one, because the operating logic of each is distinct. A low-end disruption requires a cost structure engineered for the existing market's lowest tier; a new-market disruption requires channels that can reach nonconsumers in contexts the incumbent does not even watch. Many real disruptions are hybrids, sliding along a continuum between the two, but the designer must know which end their strategy anchors on (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Southwest Airlines is a hybrid — it targeted customers who previously drove or took buses (new-market nonconsumers) while also pulling some low-end customers out of the major airlines. But Southwest's operators knew their primary opponent was not United; it was nonconsumption — highway traffic.

## How to Apply

1. **Determine whether the idea passes the disruption threshold at all.** If the product is aimed at an incumbent's most demanding mainstream customers with a better version of what they already value, stop. It is sustaining. No foothold logic applies and the theory predicts the incumbent will win. Go back to the drawing board.

2. **Run the low-end test.** Ask two questions (Innovator's Solution ch. 2): Are there customers at the low end of the market who would be happy to purchase a product with less performance if they could get it at a lower price? Can you create a business model that earns attractive profits at those discount prices? If both are yes, low-end disruption is available. Often the business-model innovation is about reducing overhead costs and turning assets faster — earning attractive returns on lower gross margins by operating differently, not by being worse at the same thing.

3. **Run the new-market test.** Ask two questions (Innovator's Solution ch. 2): Is there a large population of people who historically have not had the money, equipment, or skill to do this thing for themselves — and have gone without or hired an expert? To use the product today, do customers have to go to an inconvenient, centralized location? If the technology can be developed so that a less-skilled or less-affluent population can begin owning and using, in a more convenient context, something that was previously accessible only to specialists in centralized settings, new-market disruption is available.

4. **Apply the "all incumbents" test.** Whichever foothold you choose, verify that the innovation is disruptive to *all* of the significant incumbent firms in the industry. If it appears sustaining to even one major player, the odds are stacked in that firm's favor and the entrant is unlikely to win (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The late-1990s internet retailers failed here: the internet was sustaining for Dell, so entrant internet retailers could not disrupt Compaq effectively — Dell absorbed the opportunity instead.

5. **Design the business model to match the foothold.** A low-end disruption requires a cost structure that can profit at the discount prices required to win overshot customers from the incumbent. A new-market disruption requires a channel and product experience simple enough to reach people who have never bought the product before, often in new contexts the incumbent does not cover. Do not design one and launch the other.

6. **Predict the incumbent's response accordingly.** Low-end attackers provoke flight — the incumbent moves up and abandons the lowest tier. New-market attackers provoke indifference — the incumbent does not see them at all, because they are not competing for the same customers. Both responses are desirable for the disruptor; both give time to build out the trajectory. An incumbent that instead turns and fights is a sign you have misclassified the foothold, or that the incumbent has understood the threat more clearly than usual — either way, the disruption playbook is at risk.

## Examples

**Situation:** (Low-end foothold.) In the mid-1960s, steel minimills become technologically viable. They melt scrap steel in electric arc furnaces at 20 percent lower cost than integrated mills. But the quality of their output is poor because the chemistry of scrap varies.
**Application:** The minimills could not compete in high-quality markets. But the rebar market had loose specifications, 7 percent gross margins, and represented the least attractive 4 percent of the integrated mills' portfolio (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The minimills entered rebar with a cost structure — cheap electric arc furnaces, short rolling lines, a small town workforce — that earned attractive profits at rebar's discount prices. The integrated mills, whose cost structure required 12, 18, 25 percent margins on higher-value products, could not profitably defend rebar and were relieved to exit. This is a pure low-end disruption: overshot customers in the existing market (rebar buyers who did not need integrated-mill quality), a cost structure that could make money at the low end, and incumbents motivated to flee.
**Result:** Nucor drove the last integrated mill out of rebar by 1979. Prices collapsed. The minimills then moved up to angle iron (12 percent margin), then bars and rods, then structural beams (18 percent margin), then eventually sheet steel. Each step was another low-end attack, each produced the same flight. Nucor's market cap now dwarfs US Steel's. Bethlehem Steel went bankrupt. The whole sequence was set in motion by the rebar foothold — a market the integrated mills themselves considered worthless.

**Situation:** (New-market foothold.) In 1955, Sony introduces the world's first battery-powered pocket transistor radio. The sound is tinny and static-laced. Compared with the tabletop vacuum-tube radios that RCA dominates, it is plainly inferior.
**Application:** Sony did not target RCA's customers. The tabletop radio buyer who already owned an RCA cared about sound fidelity and would have rejected the transistor radio on that dimension. Sony instead targeted teenagers — the rebar of humanity, in Christensen's phrase (Innovator's Solution ch. 4) — few of whom could afford big vacuum-tube radios. The portable transistor radio offered teenagers a rare treat: the chance to listen to rock and roll with friends, in places out of the earshot of their parents. The teenagers were thrilled to buy a product that was not very good, because their alternative was no radio at all. This is a pure new-market disruption: nonconsumers (teenagers without radios), a new context of use (portable, social, away from the home), and a performance dimension (portability, battery power, low cost) that the incumbents did not value.
**Result:** From the transistor radio, Sony moved to portable black-and-white televisions in 1959 — same pattern, same new-market foothold, nonconsumers who lacked space for floor-standing TVs. When solid-state electronics finally became good enough to handle the power required in large televisions, Sony and its retailers vacuumed out the customers from RCA's original value network. Within a few years, RCA had vaporized. The disruption ran through a nonconsumption foothold that RCA never saw as a threat because RCA's customers were never involved in it.

**Situation:** (New-market foothold.) In the 1870s, the telephone emerges. Western Union, dominant in telegraphy, dismisses it — telephone signals carry only a few miles, while the telegraph spans continents.
**Application:** This is frequently cited as Western Union's great blunder. In fact, by the theory, Western Union's rejection was locally rational. The telephone did not compete against the telegraph. It competed against nonconsumption — against walking to someone's house, against writing letters, against the short-distance communication that most people wanted but could not perform over the telegraph network (Seeing What's Next, Introduction). Telegraphy required a central facility, an expert operator, Morse code. Most people had none of these. The telephone enabled ordinary individuals to communicate over several miles without any of that apparatus. The fact that it could not replace the telegraph in long-distance, professional contexts did not matter, because in the new-market foothold it had no competition to beat — the alternative was no communication at all.
**Result:** The telephone's trajectory improved. Range grew. Reliability improved. Switching networks developed. Eventually the telephone absorbed much of what the telegraph did, and then vastly more. Western Union, having refused to buy the patent, became a historical footnote. The lesson is that a new-market foothold looks, from the incumbent's perspective, like an irrelevance. That is its defining feature.

**Situation:** (Low-end foothold.) In the 1960s, Wal-Mart and Kmart attack the low end of department stores' business — nationally branded hard goods such as paint, hardware, kitchen utensils, toys, and sporting goods (Innovator's Solution ch. 2).
**Application:** The department stores' business model required 40 percent gross margins at three inventory turns per year — 120 percent annual return on capital invested in inventory (ROCII). Wal-Mart's discount model ran at 23 percent gross margins but turned inventory more than five times annually — also about 120 percent ROCII. Different formula, comparable returns. The department stores' customers in the branded-hard-goods segment were overshot: the product was familiar in use, it sold itself, and they did not need well-trained floor salespeople to help them buy a can of paint. Classic low-end disruption: an overserved segment, a different cost structure that earned attractive profits at 20-percent-lower prices, and a rational incumbent response of fleeing upmarket into cosmetics and high-fashion apparel at 50 percent gross margins.
**Result:** Discount retail ate department stores from the bottom up. Sears, Macy's, and JCPenney retreated into the higher-margin tiers and were then squeezed again from below by the next generation — category killers like Home Depot and Toys 'R Us, then online retailers, then Amazon. Each generation a low-end foothold. Each generation the same flight response.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat "low-priced" as a synonym for low-end disruption.
**Why:** New-market disruptions are also low-priced, but the pricing sits in a fundamentally different context. A new-market product may be cheaper than the incumbent's product while still being expensive on an absolute scale — the first mobile phones, personal computers, and cameras were all expensive by the standards of anyone not already rich, yet they were significantly more affordable than the available alternative (e.g., for mobile phones, giving everyone you wanted to call a CB radio) (Seeing What's Next ch. 1). The relevant price comparison in a new-market disruption is not the incumbent's product; it is the cobbled-together workaround or the null option. Design the value proposition relative to nonconsumption, not relative to the mainstream product.

**Don't:** Assume nonconsumers exist just because the current product is expensive.
**Why:** A nonconsumer must have a job to get done — an outcome they are actively trying to achieve — for which the current product is inaccessible (Innovator's Solution ch. 4). Oracle and others tried in the 1990s to turn "households that don't yet own a computer" into consumers by offering a $200 internet appliance. They failed, because the nonowners did not have jobs in their lives for which a cheap computer was the answer. A real new-market foothold requires identifying a population that is currently doing something inconvenient, expensive, or unsatisfying to get a job done — not a population that is simply not doing the thing at all.

**Don't:** Enter a low-end foothold without a cost structure designed for it.
**Why:** A mainstream business model cannot make money at discount prices. When a company tries to take a higher-cost business model down-market to sell at lower price points, almost none of the incremental revenue falls to the bottom line — it gets absorbed into overhead (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Low-end disruptions require engineered cost structures: leaner operations, higher asset turns, different channel economics. If you do not have that cost structure, you do not have a low-end foothold — you have a loss leader.

**Don't:** Let the disruptive unit share distribution with the mainstream organization.
**Why:** When Honda began its disruption of the North American motorcycle market with the Super Cub, it had to build a new channel because Harley-Davidson dealers made their commissions by selling Harleys, not Super Cubs. Trying to plug the Super Cub into the existing motorcycle channel would have produced nothing — the salespeople in the dealerships had no incentive to sell it (Innovator's Solution ch. 5). The same structural mismatch exists whenever a disruptive new-market product is pushed through a mainstream channel: the channel's incentives are tuned to the old product, and the new product cannot get oxygen. Build the channel the foothold requires, or the foothold does not exist.

**Don't:** Hedge across both footholds in one launch.
**Why:** The two footholds have different cost structures, different channels, different performance metrics, and different customer experiences. A launch that tries to serve both overshot low-end customers and nonconsumers simultaneously typically does neither well — the product is too complicated for nonconsumers and too expensive for the low end. Pick one. If the idea genuinely straddles (as Southwest Airlines does), still commit to a primary foothold and design the business model for it; the other segment can come along as overflow, not as a co-equal target.

**Don't:** Forget to check that the innovation is disruptive to *all* significant incumbents.
**Why:** The "all incumbents" rule is the most commonly skipped test (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The internet in 1998 looked disruptive to Compaq but was sustaining for Dell, and entrant internet retailers got squeezed as Dell absorbed the opportunity. If even one large incumbent can integrate your innovation as a sustaining improvement, that incumbent will likely win, and your foothold is not safe. Do the check for every major player before committing.
