# Disruptive Innovation

## When to Use
- Someone is claiming that a company is "disruptive" and is about to act on that claim — invest, acquire, defend, panic. The word is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and it is almost always doing the wrong work.
- An incumbent is weighing whether to respond vigorously to an upstart. The correct response depends on what kind of attack it is, and disruption is one specific kind.
- A founder is describing a path from a small, unglamorous corner of the market toward the center of it. The question is whether the trajectory is real or a hope.
- A board is discussing small competitors nibbling at the edges of the business. Some of those competitors can be ignored. Some of them will, if ignored, destroy the company. The theory is the way to tell the difference.
- You are trying to predict, before the outcome is known, whether a fledgling business will grow into a meaningful one. The theory raises the odds.

## Fails When
- The user wants a label, not a diagnosis. Disruption is not a word you paste on a company; it is the description of a process that unfolds over years. If you try to use it the first way, you will be wrong half the time.
- The entrant is attacking the incumbent head-on, with a better product, in the incumbent's core market. That is a sustaining innovation, and incumbents almost always win those fights. Calling it disruption produces exactly the wrong prescription.
- The market is heavily regulated in a way that neuters the incumbent's ability to respond. Uber lives in that space. In such cases, expect atypical outcomes that the theory does not cleanly predict, and say so honestly rather than force-fit.
- Success is being used to prove disruptiveness. Not every triumphant newcomer followed a disruptive path, and not every disruptive path leads to triumph. Success does not define the theory; the trajectory does.
- You are trying to use the theory to decide how to *win* in a foothold market. The theory is quiet on that. It tells you what kind of fight you are in and which direction to point; it does not hand you a product plan.

## Core Concept

Disruption is a process, not an event, not a product, and not a personality. It describes how a smaller company with fewer resources successfully challenges established incumbent businesses ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", HBR 2015). As incumbents focus on improving their products and services for their most demanding — and usually most profitable — customers, they exceed the needs of some segments and ignore the needs of others. Entrants that prove disruptive begin by targeting those overlooked segments, gaining a foothold by delivering more-suitable functionality, frequently at a lower price. Incumbents, chasing higher profitability in more-demanding segments, tend not to respond vigorously. The entrants then move upmarket, delivering the performance that incumbents' mainstream customers require, while preserving the advantages that drove their early success. When mainstream customers start adopting the entrants' offerings in volume, disruption has occurred. That is the whole arc. It is measured in years, sometimes decades. It took Nucor and the other steel minimills more than forty years to match the revenue of the largest integrated steelmakers; it took Compaq and its cohort only twelve years to reach parity with DEC in minicomputers. The pace varies with how quickly the enabling technology improves. The shape does not.

Two trajectories tell the story. The first is the rate of improvement that customers in a given market can utilize or absorb — a line that slopes gently upward through time, because customer needs do grow, but slowly (Innovator's Dilemma, Introduction). The second is the rate at which innovating companies actually improve their products, which is almost always steeper. These two lines cross. Before the crossing, the incumbent's product is not yet good enough for its best customers, so the incumbent runs hard up the sustaining curve to catch them. Somewhere after the crossing, the incumbent has given its best customers more performance than they can use, while a cheaper, simpler, less-functional alternative — the disruptive product — has been quietly improving on its own trajectory in a corner of the market the incumbent did not think worth defending. When the disruptive product's trajectory intersects the mainstream customer's need line, the disruption arrives in the incumbent's market, not as a direct attack but as a migration. Customers cross over. The incumbent, which has been busy running upmarket, looks down one day and finds the floor has been sold. This is why the mainframe-computer market shrank: not because personal computers outperformed mainframes, but because networked personal computers intersected with what many organizations actually needed ("Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", HBR 1995).

The mechanism that makes disruption so reliable is asymmetric motivation (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). When the disruptor shows up in a low-margin, unattractive corner, the incumbent is powerfully motivated to flee upmarket — not because the incumbent's managers are stupid, but because they are doing their jobs. Allocating resources to defend a low-margin segment when higher-margin opportunities are available is the *wrong* answer for a well-run company serving demanding customers. The integrated steel mills did not fight Nucor in rebar; they were almost relieved when Nucor took rebar off their hands, because 7-percent-margin rebar was the worst-performing tier in their product mix. When Nucor moved up into angle iron at 12 percent, they were again relieved. Then structural beams at 18 percent. Each time, the incumbents executed an upward retreat that was locally rational and globally fatal. Disruption works because it is much easier to beat competitors who are motivated to flee than competitors motivated to fight. That is the whole theory in one sentence.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the two-foothold test.** Ask: Does the entrant start in a low-end foothold (overshot mainstream customers buying "good enough" for less) or a new-market foothold (nonconsumers who previously went without)? If neither — if the entrant is launching head-on into the incumbent's best customers — it is not disruption. It may still be a good business, but do not apply the disruption playbook to it (this is the "Is Uber disruptive?" test from the 2015 HBR piece).

2. **Run the quality-trajectory test.** Plot what customers in the incumbent's mainstream can actually use, and the slope at which their demand is growing. Then plot the entrant's current performance and the slope at which that performance is improving. If the entrant's trajectory is steeper than the mainstream's demand trajectory, the lines will cross, and disruption will arrive. If the entrant is below and improving slower than the market is demanding, it will stay a niche. Both slopes matter, not just the entrant's.

3. **Read the incumbent's response as evidence.** Are the incumbent's best customers rejecting the new product? That is a positive signal — it means the attack is disruptive, and the incumbent will lack the internal resource-allocation logic to defend. Are the incumbent's best customers demanding the new product? That is a negative signal for the disruption hypothesis — it is likely sustaining, and the incumbent will marshal everything it has to respond.

4. **Don't confuse transformation with disruption.** Industry upheaval is not the same as disruption. Uber upended the taxi business but did not do so from a low-end or nonconsumption foothold. It launched in San Francisco (a well-served taxi market) with an already-superior product. That path is not disruptive in the technical sense, and the 2015 HBR refinement was written specifically to stop people from conflating the two. A company can shake an industry without being disruptive, and it can be disruptive without (yet) shaking anything.

5. **Ignore or isolate as an incumbent.** If the attack is disruptive, the correct response is almost never to dismantle the profitable existing business in a panic. Continue to strengthen relationships with core customers through sustaining innovation, and create a separate division to pursue the disruptive opportunity — under the protection of senior leadership, kept organizationally distinct. Every company that has tried to manage mainstream and disruptive businesses within a single organization has failed (Innovator's Dilemma Introduction; "What Is Disruptive Innovation?" 2015).

6. **For entrants: pick the fight you can win.** Disrupt or sustain is a strategic choice, not a discovery about the technology. Few technologies are inherently disruptive. The idea must be shaped into one. If your business plan has you taking better products to an incumbent's best customers on their own turf, you are picking a sustaining fight and, absent unusual circumstances, you will lose. Reshape the idea into a disruption, or reconsider the venture.

## Examples

**Situation:** Integrated steel companies — US Steel, Bethlehem, Nippon Steel — dominate the global steel industry in the 1960s. Nucor and other minimills, using electric arc furnaces melting scrap, can produce steel 20 percent cheaper but only at the lowest quality. Rebar is the only market that will take their product.
**Application:** This is the canonical disruption (Innovator's Dilemma; Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The minimills enter at the foothold — rebar at 7 percent gross margin, 4 percent of industry tonnage — the least attractive tier in the integrated mills' portfolio. The integrated mills are almost relieved to exit. With a 20 percent cost advantage, the minimills earn attractive profits in rebar until they drive out the last integrated mill in 1979; then rebar prices collapse and the minimills move up to angle iron at 12 percent margin. Again the integrated mills flee. Then structural beams at 18 percent. Then, eventually, sheet steel — the crown jewel. Each stage is locally rational for the integrated mills; none of them is able to defend a low-margin segment while their resource-allocation processes demand upward movement. The trajectory of minimill quality rises faster than the market's demand for quality. The lines cross.
**Result:** Nucor's market capitalization now dwarfs US Steel's. Bethlehem Steel went bankrupt. Not a single integrated steel company successfully invested in a minimill. This is not a story of bungled management; it is a story of rational managers, rationally following their best customers, losing the industry because the theory said they would.

**Situation:** Netflix launches in 1997 with a DVD-by-mail service. Blockbuster's customers, who rent movies (typically new releases) on impulse, find the offering useless — delivery takes several days; you cannot decide at 7 p.m. to watch a movie tonight. Netflix appeals only to a narrow slice: movie buffs who don't care about new releases, early adopters of DVD players, online shoppers.
**Application:** Netflix took a classical disruptive path — a foothold in a segment Blockbuster did not value, a trajectory of improvement enabled by a technology (streaming video) that would eventually catch up to mainstream customer needs ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015). Blockbuster's decision to ignore the early Netflix was not, at the time, a strategic blunder. The two companies filled different needs for different customers. The mistake was not ignoring Netflix; it was failing to recognize the *trajectory* Netflix was on. Had Netflix launched by targeting Blockbuster's core impulse-renter in their core use case, Blockbuster would very likely have mounted a vigorous and probably successful counterattack — because that would have been a sustaining attack, and incumbents win those.
**Result:** Streaming made Netflix good enough for Blockbuster's core customers. Blockbuster collapsed. The lesson is the asymmetry: the path a new entrant is on matters more than the position they currently occupy.

**Situation:** Apple releases the iPhone in 2007. Most people call it a disruptive innovation in the smartphone market.
**Application:** This one requires care. The iPhone was a *sustaining* innovation in the smartphone market — it targeted the same customers coveted by incumbents like BlackBerry and Nokia, and its initial success is best explained by product superiority ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015). But that is not the interesting story. The iPhone's subsequent growth is better explained by disruption — not of other smartphones, but of the *laptop* as the primary access point to the internet. Through the app ecosystem, the iPhone pulled internet access out of the laptop value network and into a new one. It did that by new-market disruption: new uses, new contexts of consumption, new nonconsumers (people who had never used a laptop for a given job).
**Result:** Apple wins in both frames. But the mechanism is different in each, and a manager who lumps them together under "disruption" will reach for the wrong tool. When you meet an ostensibly disruptive case, always ask: disruptive *to what*? The answer matters.

**Situation:** Xerox dominates photocopying in the 1970s, serving large corporations with high-speed machines at premium prices. School librarians, bowling leagues, small offices — all priced out — make do with carbon paper or mimeographs.
**Application:** New entrants introduce personal tabletop copiers in the late 1970s. The product is slow, limited, and inferior by Xerox's standards. But it is not trying to serve Xerox's customers. It is creating a market where none existed — turning nonconsumers into consumers (Innovator's Dilemma; "What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015). Canon leads this move. Xerox's internal processes are well-tuned to serve the corporate copy-center customer and to reject products that do not meet that customer's standards. They reject the personal copier. From this modest beginning, the personal copier makers gradually build up into Xerox's mainstream market.
**Result:** Canon and the other personal-copier makers capture large portions of the photocopier market that Xerox once owned. Xerox survives but is permanently diminished. The mechanism is new-market disruption: the entrants do not fight Xerox; they build a different market, improve it, and then absorb Xerox's customers when the trajectory of their product catches up.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Call every industry-shaking company "disruptive."
**Why:** Many researchers, writers, and consultants use "disruptive innovation" to describe any situation in which an industry is shaken up and previously successful incumbents stumble. That is much too broad a usage. Uber is the exemplar. It transformed the taxi business but did not enter from a low-end or new-market foothold — it launched in a well-served taxi market targeting existing taxi customers with a better product. Applying the disruption playbook to Uber-like situations produces the wrong prescription. If the entrant is attacking your bread-and-butter with a better product, fight them hard — that is a sustaining battle, and you have the resources to win it. Treating them as "disruptive" and ignoring or isolating them would be disastrous.

**Don't:** Reason from outcome to definition. "It succeeded, so it must have been disruptive."
**Why:** Success is not built into the definition of disruption. Not every disruptive path leads to triumph, and not every triumphant newcomer follows a disruptive path. Many internet retailers pursued disruptive paths in the late 1990s and failed. Their failures are boundary markers for the theory, not flaws in it. If we call every success a disruption, we will try to mix and match behaviors from fundamentally different strategies and produce plans that contradict themselves.

**Don't:** Treat disruption as a product attribute — as if a technology can be scanned for disruptive properties.
**Why:** Few technologies are intrinsically sustaining or disruptive. Their disruptive impact must be molded into strategy as managers shape the idea into a plan (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The internet was a sustaining technology for Dell, which was already selling direct, and a disruptive threat to Compaq, which was not. The same technology, opposite implications. What matters is the strategic shape you give it, not the technology itself.

**Don't:** Overreact as an incumbent by dismantling the still-profitable core business.
**Why:** "Disrupt or be disrupted" can be dangerous counsel. The 2015 refinement emphasizes that incumbents should keep strengthening sustaining innovation in the core business while creating a *separate* organization to pursue the disruptive opportunity. Tearing up the core in panic destroys value today in exchange for nothing guaranteed tomorrow. The right move is to manage two operations in parallel, tolerate the tension, and let the new division grow under senior leadership's protection until it can stand on its own.

**Don't:** Apply the theory to a market where regulation suppresses incumbent response and then blame the theory when outcomes diverge.
**Why:** Uber is an outlier in part because the regulated nature of the taxi business — controlled entry, controlled prices, little incentive for incumbents to innovate — means the usual asymmetric-motivation mechanism does not fully engage. The theory does not claim to explain every case. When conditions diverge from the theory's assumptions, say so. A theory used honestly to flag its own limits is more useful than one used to force-fit every situation.

**Don't:** Mistake the foothold for the destination.
**Why:** Disrupters start where they can, not where they mean to finish. A company that looks trivial in year one — teenagers with tinny transistor radios, small offices with slow tabletop copiers, bowlers with rebar-quality steel — is not stuck there. The move is from fringe to mainstream, and the mainstream is the point. Judging disruptors on their initial market size is exactly the mistake the incumbents made before they lost their industries. The question is never how big the foothold is today; it is how steep the trajectory is and whether it leads upmarket.
