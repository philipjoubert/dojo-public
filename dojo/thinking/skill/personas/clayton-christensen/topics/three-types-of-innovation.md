# Three Types of Innovation

## When to Use
- A company's income statement looks healthy but the CEO senses the business is hollowing out. The taxonomy will show why — almost certainly the portfolio has tilted from market-creating to efficiency, and the free cash isn't being redeployed.
- A country's unemployment stays high while corporate balance sheets swell with retained earnings. The taxonomy names the mechanism: efficiency innovations free up capital, but with no market-creating innovations to soak that capital back up, it piles up and jobs don't follow.
- You are reviewing an innovation portfolio and want to know whether it is actually aimed at growth, or whether what people are calling "innovation" is in fact three consecutive rounds of cost-out.
- You are briefing a board or a founder on why disruption matters, and you need them to see that disruption is not merely a product concept — it is the one type of innovation that creates net new jobs in an economy.

## Fails When
- You apply the taxonomy to a single product decision. A single product can be a sustaining innovation in one market and a market-creating one in another. Taxonomy works at the portfolio level.
- You use it to argue a company should stop doing efficiency. An economy and a firm both need all three types. The warning is about imbalance, not about any single type.
- You use "market-creating" as a synonym for "new product." Most new products are sustaining. Market-creating innovation is specifically the kind that reaches nonconsumers — people who previously couldn't afford, access, or use the thing at all.

## Core Concept

There are three types of innovation, and they do different things to the two resources an economy actually cares about: jobs and capital. Call this the late-career lens Christensen was building out when he died — a microeconomic account of why nations prosper or flatline.

The first type is **market-creating innovation**. It takes a product that was complicated and expensive and transforms it into something so affordable and simple that a whole population of non-consumers can now own it. The mainframe was two million dollars and filled a room; the personal computer was two thousand dollars and sat on a desk. The same move happened again from PC to smartphone. Each transformation did not just replace the old product — it pulled a new population into the market. That is the signature: nonconsumers became consumers. And because so many new people are now buying, somebody has to make the thing, distribute it, sell it, service it, finance it. Market-creating innovation **creates jobs**, and because growth requires assets on a balance sheet, it **uses capital**. This is the engine of prosperity. Nearly every net new job created in a developed economy over the last hundred years was created by this type of innovation.

The second type is **sustaining innovation**. Its purpose is to make good products better, and then to sell those better products to the customers you already have, usually at better margins. When Toyota launches a Prius, the customer who buys it was going to buy a Camry otherwise. Sustaining innovation is indispensable — it is what keeps markets efficient and what funds most of what companies do — but on average it does not create jobs, because every sale of the new product displaces a sale of the old one. Its effect on employment is roughly net zero. Its demand on capital is modest, because the company is reusing assets it already owns to make a slightly better version of something it already makes.

The third type is **efficiency innovation**. It lets a company sell the same products to the same customers at lower cost. Walmart was an efficiency innovation. The Toyota Production System was an efficiency innovation — Toyota reduced assembly time from sixty days to two, which collapsed the work-in-process inventory trapped on the balance sheet and freed all that capital for other uses. Efficiency innovations are beautiful. They **eliminate jobs** — that is the whole point; fewer people produce the same output — but they **free capital**. Capital that was imprisoned in inventory, in factory floor space, in receivables, is now liberated and available to be deployed elsewhere.

If you draw those three together you get something close to a perpetual motion machine. Efficiency innovations free capital. That freed capital gets invested in market-creating innovations, which create jobs and absorb the capital productively. Market-creating innovations mature into sustaining-innovation businesses, which throw off enough cash to keep the whole cycle running. The cycle worked in the United States for roughly a century and a half. It worked in Japan through the 1950s, 60s, and 70s. It is working today in Korea, then Taiwan, then China, then India — each wave disrupting the one above it and pulling another few hundred million people out of poverty.

But the cycle only runs if capital freed by efficiency gets redeployed into market-creating innovation. If instead the capital is funneled back into another round of efficiency — which is what happens when managers are trained to optimize ratios — the machine straightens into a line. A line has a beginning and an end. Efficiency generates more capital. Capital, in the absence of market-creating bets to absorb it, accumulates. Jobs disappear from the efficiency side and are not replaced on the market-creating side. Capital becomes so abundant that its cost falls to zero (Square raised equity at a negative cost of capital — the investors paid to give them money). The Fed stands on the beach with a fire hose trying to fill the ocean. The country has cash and no growth. That is what "economic stagnation" actually looks like under a microscope.

## How to Apply

1. **List every innovation project in the portfolio and sort each into one of the three buckets.** For each one, answer one question: does this product reach people who previously couldn't afford or access this category (market-creating); does it replace an existing product we already sell to an existing customer (sustaining); or does it let us deliver what we already deliver at lower cost (efficiency)? Force a single bucket. "A little of both" is a dodge.

2. **Count the dollars in each bucket, not the projects.** Most companies have a handful of headline-grabbing market-creating projects and a giant unlisted mass of efficiency work hiding inside operations and supply-chain budgets. Add up the actual capital allocation. The honest portfolio is the dollar-weighted one, not the slide with three logos in three boxes.

3. **Trace the capital flow.** Efficiency innovations free capital. Where is yours going? Into buybacks? Into more efficiency? Into market-creating bets? If the freed capital is not being routed into market-creating bets — inside your firm, or via investors into other firms' market-creating bets — you are running the straight-line version of the machine, and the end of the line is stagnation.

4. **Ask the "nonconsumer test" for each claimed market-creating innovation.** Who is the customer who, today, is not buying anything in this category because the existing options are too expensive or too complicated or too inaccessible? If you cannot name that nonconsumer population in one sentence, the innovation is not market-creating, no matter what the deck says.

5. **Apply the same lens at national scale when you need to.** Look at the economy's major firms. How many of them launched a market-creating innovation in the last decade? Japan post-1990 launched one: the Nintendo Wii. One. Japan is the canary. The United States since 2000 is producing roughly a third of the market-creating innovations per decade that it was producing in the 1950s, 60s, and 70s. The taxonomy is not just a management tool. It is a lens on why the recession of 1991 took fifteen months to recover, why 2001 took thirty-nine months, why 2008 took sixty-seven months — and why each subsequent recovery has been slower. Recoveries used to average six months. Something has changed, and the three-types lens shows you what.

## Examples

**Situation:** A diversified industrial group claims to be an "innovation leader" because it files hundreds of patents a year and has a dedicated R&D budget.
**Application:** Bucket the patents. The great majority are incremental improvements to products the group already sells — sustaining. A large efficiency portfolio sits inside operations and supply chain. One or two small skunkworks might be market-creating, but they get quietly reabsorbed because their five-to-ten-year payoff looks poor against the two-year payoff of the efficiency work.
**Result:** The group looks innovative, throws off cash, buys back shares, and produces no net new jobs over a decade. Revenue per employee rises; total employment falls. This is efficiency-weighted innovation wearing the marketing of market-creation.

**Situation:** A CEO complains that "our innovation pipeline is strong, but our revenue is flat."
**Application:** Run the taxonomy. Almost always, what is happening is that the pipeline is full of sustaining innovations — better versions of products for existing customers — and those innovations are cannibalizing existing sales on roughly a one-for-one basis. The "pipeline" is replacing, not expanding.
**Result:** The diagnosis reframes the problem: the company needs market-creating bets reaching non-consumers, not better versions for consumers it already has. This is typically an organizational problem (those bets die in a sustaining-innovation-optimized allocation process) more than an idea problem.

**Situation:** An economist asks why corporate cash balances are at record highs while wage growth stays weak.
**Application:** The three-types lens is a direct answer. Efficiency innovations (lean, outsourcing, offshoring, automation) have freed enormous amounts of working capital. In the same period, market-creating innovations that would have soaked that capital back up and created new employment have dropped sharply. So capital piles up; jobs don't.
**Result:** The policy conversation shifts from "why won't companies hire" (the agents are behaving rationally against their incentives) to "why aren't we producing market-creating innovations" — which, once asked, points at cost of capital, tax treatment of long-payoff investments, and the training of the people making the decisions.

**Situation:** Japan, 1989. The economy has been a market-creating juggernaut for three decades. Honda democratized the motorcycle; Toyota democratized the car; Sony democratized the television; Canon democratized the office copier. Unemployment under 2%.
**Application:** Beginning in the 1980s, Japan hires graduates trained in ratio-based finance — the same training that was hollowing out American capital allocation. The firms stop doing market-creating innovation. Since 1990 Japan has produced exactly one disruptive, market-creating innovation at scale: the Nintendo Wii.
**Result:** Thirty years of stagnation. No level of monetary stimulus fixes it because the problem is microeconomic — at the level of what companies choose to invest in — not macroeconomic. This is the future that waits for any economy that runs only efficiency innovations.

**Situation:** A founder claims she is building a market-creating product: a cheaper version of an existing enterprise software category.
**Application:** Apply the non-consumer test. Who is the population that, today, is not buying this category at all because it is too expensive or too complicated? If the buyers of the cheaper version are the same companies that already buy the expensive version, she is running a sustaining-innovation or efficiency play inside an existing market — not a market-creating play. Both can be good businesses; only one creates a new market.
**Result:** The language clarifies the strategy. If the true play is sustaining, the go-to-market is sales-led into existing budgets. If the play is actually market-creating, the buyer is not the enterprise buyer — it's the small business, the individual user, the emerging-market customer — and the entire go-to-market is different.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume all innovation is good for the economy, or for the company.
**Why:** Efficiency innovations free capital but eliminate jobs. A company or economy that runs only efficiency innovations will generate rising profits alongside flat or falling employment, rising cash balances alongside falling business investment, and — if sustained long enough — a cost of capital so low that the traditional measures of financial discipline become meaningless. The individual innovations are not wrong. The portfolio mix is wrong. Cheering every "innovation" headline without asking which of the three it is is how a country ends up cash-rich and growth-poor for a generation.

**Don't:** Call a sustaining innovation a market-creating one because it is new.
**Why:** Most new products are sustaining. Most product launches, most category extensions, most feature releases go to customers who were already buying the category. Market-creating innovation has a specific signature: it reaches non-consumers. Conflating the two flatters the portfolio and disguises the imbalance.

**Don't:** Try to pick the "best" of the three types.
**Why:** A healthy system needs all three. Without sustaining innovation, products stagnate and competitors take share. Without efficiency innovation, capital stays trapped in working capital and can't be redeployed. Without market-creating innovation, capital piles up, jobs erode, and the economy flatlines. The diagnostic question is never "which type is best" — it is "what is the current mix, and is it imbalanced." Almost always, in mature firms and mature economies, it is imbalanced toward efficiency.

**Don't:** Skip the capital-flow question.
**Why:** The mechanism by which the three types hang together is the flow of capital from efficiency, through market-creation, into sustaining. If you only bucket the projects without tracing the dollars, you'll miss the actual pathology, which is almost always that the capital freed by efficiency is being returned to shareholders or redeployed into more efficiency, not invested into market-creating bets. The taxonomy without the capital-flow diagnosis is just a labeling exercise.
