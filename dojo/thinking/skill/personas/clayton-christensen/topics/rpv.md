# Resources, Processes, and Values (RPV)

## When to Use
- A well-run company with talented managers and plenty of cash is consistently failing at a new initiative — particularly a disruptive one.
- A CEO is about to "transform" the mainstream organization to chase a new opportunity, and you suspect the transformation will destroy what the organization is actually good at.
- Someone proposes putting a disruptive project under a business unit whose P&L depends on higher-margin mainstream revenue.
- An acquirer is about to integrate an acquired company into the parent to "capture synergies."
- A founder asks why the same hire who was a superstar at their old company is struggling at the new one. (RPV also runs in reverse.)

## Fails When
- You apply it to a start-up. In the early stages, capabilities really do live in a handful of people — the addition or departure of a few key individuals changes what the firm can do. RPV becomes useful once processes and values have congealed, which is typically mid-life and beyond.
- You use it to excuse the organization from doing things it could, with decent management, actually do. "Our values can't support this" is not a rebuttal to "we should answer customer complaints faster."
- You treat all three components as equally mutable. Values are the hardest to shift; treating them as a flipchart exercise is how executives spend three years trying to change what the organization prioritizes and end up with a poster on the wall and no change in behavior.

## Core Concept

For years we've been puzzling about why so many capable, well-resourced companies — companies that by every measure ought to be able to do whatever they set their minds to — find some particular new initiative impossible to get off the ground. The usual answers focus on people: wrong managers, wrong incentives, wrong talent. But that answer doesn't hold up when you look closely. The people in these companies are frequently superb. The resources are abundant. And still, the initiative withers.

Organizations themselves — independent of the people and other resources in them — have capabilities. And every capability, at the point it has matured, defines a concurrent disability. That's the hard part. The things that make a company good at what it does are the same things that make it incapable of doing something else. Three factors determine what an organization can and cannot do, and understanding them sorted by their increasing difficulty to change is the whole framework.

**Resources** are the most visible. They're what comes to mind first when a manager asks "what does my company have?" — people, technology, products, equipment, information, cash, brands, supplier and customer relationships. Resources are tangible and transferable. You can hire them. You can buy them. You can move them from one project to another. When capabilities live in resources, changing direction is relatively easy because the underlying units are movable.

**Processes** are the patterns of interaction, coordination, communication, and decision-making by which the organization turns inputs into outputs. They are how work actually gets done. Some are explicit — documented, taught, audited. Many more are invisible background processes: how market research is habitually conducted, how analyses get translated into financial projections, how plans and budgets get negotiated, how priorities get argued about in meetings. A process that gives the company the capability to execute one task effectively is, by the same logic, a disability at executing tasks the process wasn't designed for. Processes are not nearly as flexible as resources, and popular change-management programs notwithstanding, they are not adapted easily by decree.

**Values** are the criteria by which decisions about priorities are made — what the organization considers high-margin enough to pursue, which customers are important enough to serve, which opportunities are big enough to matter. Values emerge directly from the cost structure and the business model. A company that has learned to make money at 40% gross margins against a $100M minimum viable market size cannot get excited about a project that produces 15% margins in a $5M market, even if the executive team agrees at the whiteboard that the project is strategically important. The values of the organization — running through every resource-allocation meeting, every promotion decision, every quarterly planning cycle — will starve it. Values are the hardest of the three to change, because they have been inculcated over decades of successful operation and encoded in the thousands of micro-decisions that compose the culture. You can add resources. You can, with pain, redesign processes. You cannot reprogram values inside a mainstream organization on any timetable that matters.

This sequence — resources, then processes, then values, by increasing difficulty of change — is also the migration of capabilities over a firm's life. In the start-up stage, a company's capabilities live mostly in its people (resources). Over time, as the founder's preferences harden into habits and the habits harden into rules, capabilities migrate into processes and values and eventually into culture itself. Culture is just what happens when processes and values have become so internalized that employees follow them by assumption rather than by conscious choice. The firm is now able to crank out high-quality work year after year without the founder involved in every decision. That is a gift. It is also the prison.

## How to Apply

1. **For any new initiative, diagnose capabilities in three separate questions.** Does the organization have the *resources* to succeed? Does it have the *processes* — will the work of integrating people, information, and technology required here flow naturally, or will it have to be forced? Do its *values* cause this initiative to be prioritized, or will it be starved in the resource-allocation process? Each question is independent. Answering "yes, yes, yes" means build it inside. Any "no" tells you something.

2. **Decide where the initiative lives based on which capabilities are missing.** If resources are missing, hire or acquire them — the mainstream organization is fine. If processes are wrong, pull people out of the existing organization into a dedicated team with new boundaries (what Wheelwright and Clark called a "heavyweight team") — new boundaries permit new patterns of work to coalesce into new processes. If values are wrong — if the initiative is disruptive and would never clear the resource-allocation hurdle of the mainstream business — you must spin out. No reorganization inside the parent will suffice, because the parent's values will reassert themselves in every budget cycle.

3. **If you acquire, diagnose what you bought before integrating.** Ask: what produced the value I just paid for? If it was *resources* — the technology, the products, the engineers, the customers — then integration makes sense. Plug those resources into your processes. If it was *processes and values*, integration will vaporize them. Let the acquired company stand alone; infuse parent resources into its processes rather than the reverse.

4. **Never let a CEO treat a spinout as a way to get a problem off his agenda.** Disrupting your mainstream values requires the personal, attentive oversight of the CEO — precisely because the power of values runs through the normal resource-allocation process, and only the CEO can override that. CEOs who delegate a disruptive spinout and "check back in a year" are almost certain to return to a starved and failing unit. We have seen no exceptions to this rule.

5. **Separate sustaining work from disruptive work permanently, not temporarily.** Merrill Lynch didn't have to *replace* its existing planning and acquisition processes when online discount brokerage emerged — those processes were still making the company billions of dollars. What Merrill needed was *additional* processes to handle the new class of problem, run alongside the old ones. Most transformation programs fail because they try to convert a mainstream organization that is still profitably serving its existing customers. Don't. Run two businesses in tandem.

## Examples

**Situation:** Digital Equipment Corporation dominated the minicomputer industry from the 1960s through the 1980s. When personal computers emerged, Digital tried repeatedly to enter the PC business, launching four separate initiatives over a decade. The company had every resource required — engineers who knew computing cold, a global sales force, brand, cash, and distribution. Each attempt failed.

**Application:** A resources-only diagnostic predicts success. But the processes and values tell the full story. Digital's processes were tuned to the minicomputer business — designing a minicomputer meant integrating most of the subsystems inside the company itself, because minicomputer architectures were interdependent and the work required direct interaction between component designers. PCs were architected differently: they were assembled from modular components supplied by an ecosystem, and success depended on fast, low-cost procurement and thin-margin assembly processes that Digital didn't have and couldn't easily build. More importantly, Digital's values couldn't support the PC business. Minicomputers produced 40%+ gross margins on multi-hundred-thousand-dollar systems; PCs produced 15% margins on $2,000 boxes. Every time the PC project came up against the minicomputer project in resource-allocation meetings, the minicomputer project won — not because anyone was stupid, but because the company's values, properly tuned to its mainstream business, consistently directed resources toward the higher-margin, larger-opportunity work.

**Result:** Digital could have built a PC business. IBM did exactly this — set up a separate organization in Florida, physically and operationally removed from Armonk, with its own processes and its own values, charged with building PCs. Digital tried to build PCs inside the mainstream organization. The mainstream organization did what the mainstream organization was designed to do, which was to keep making minicomputers. Digital disappeared.

**Situation:** In 1991, Hewlett-Packard's Disk Memory Division conceived of the Kittyhawk, a 1.3-inch disk drive that would enable a new class of handheld and palm-top computing devices. The engineering was brilliant. The drive could survive a three-foot drop onto concrete. HP's executives mandated that the project ramp to $150M in revenue within three years to be worth the parent company's attention.

**Application:** The resources existed. The processes were adaptable. But the values were wrong — not for building a Kittyhawk, but for making Kittyhawk matter inside HP. A project that could never become a billion-dollar business in a reasonable timeframe could not sustain executive patience in a $20B+ parent company whose resource-allocation machinery was tuned to opportunities at much larger scale. When the initial PDA market failed to materialize as projected, and the real early market turned out to be nascent and unpredictable (the way disruptive-technology markets always are), HP's values couldn't tolerate the wait.

**Result:** Kittyhawk was killed. The technology was sound. The market that eventually emerged for small form-factor drives — digital cameras, MP3 players, and eventually much else — was enormous. HP left it on the table because its values couldn't sponsor an initiative that had to be small and patient for the early years.

---

**Situation:** In the 1990s and 2000s, Kmart and Sears held every resource required to become Walmart. National footprint. Buyer relationships with every manufacturer that mattered. Enormous cash flow. Sophisticated logistics. A retail brand older than Walmart's.

**Application:** Resources said Kmart and Sears could crush Walmart at any time. But their processes — how buying decisions were made, how inventory was managed, how stores were configured — were tuned to the department-store model, not the discount model. And their values, descended from decades of department-store profitability, couldn't support the thin-margin discipline that discount retail demands. Every quarter, when allocating attention and capital, the existing higher-margin departments won. The low-margin, high-volume Walmart model was never prioritized.

**Result:** Neither company became Walmart. Neither company could. Their capabilities — which were real — concurrently defined disabilities that foreclosed the pivot.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Ask an established organization to do something that requires changing its values.

**Why:** Values are the criteria by which prioritization decisions are made, and they have been shaped by decades of successful operation — encoded in the cost structure, the margin expectations, the minimum market size that clears the investment hurdle, the type of customer the salesforce knows how to sell to, and the type of problem the executive team finds interesting. You can add resources by hiring. You can rewrite processes, with pain, through new team structures and heavyweight teams. You cannot change values on a meaningful timetable from inside the mainstream organization, because values only shift through separate organizations with different cost structures, or through generational turnover of leadership. Leaders who try anyway end up with a PowerPoint deck announcing new values and zero change in where resources actually go. If the new initiative requires values the mainstream organization doesn't have, you don't reorganize — you spin out. No exceptions we've ever seen.
