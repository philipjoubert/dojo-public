# The Precedent Trap — Exceptions, Repeated Games, and Missing Options

## When to Use
- Any one-off exception, discount, accommodation, or policy deviation.
- Decisions evaluated in isolation when the same type of decision will recur.
- Binary choices that exclude other options. ("Ship on time with fewer features, or ship complete late.")
- Decisions about "just this one customer" or "just this one employee."
- Any negotiation concession you plan to treat as a one-off.

## Fails When
- The decision truly is unobservable and has no precedent-setting effect (a discreet internal accommodation that nobody else will learn of).
- Used as infinite stall. At some point, the repeated game has been considered and you still have to decide this specific case.

## Core Concept

Analyzing each decision in isolation, without considering longer-term dynamics and precedent effects, leads to systematically wrong conclusions even when each individual analysis seems sound.

A company faced a choice: give a 20% discount to close an important deal, or hold firm. The deal was large. The customer was prestigious. The math on this particular transaction favored the discount. The deal closed. It was celebrated. Then the consequences began.

The salesperson told other salespeople about the discount. They expected similar flexibility. Other customers learned about the deal through industry channels and demanded equivalent treatment. The sales team started pricing high, expecting to negotiate down. Customers stopped believing the listed price. What had looked like a sensible decision for this one transaction turned out to be a systematic change in how the company sold.

Each individual analysis was sound. The sequence of analyses produced outcomes that no single analysis considered.

## The One-Day-at-a-Time Trap

A founder faced a simple request: could one employee work from home on Fridays? The employee had a good reason. Considered in isolation, approving it was the obvious choice. The founder approved.

The following week, another employee made the same request. Different reasons, equally valid. The founder approved. Then a third. A fourth. Within months, the office was empty on Fridays. Projects requiring collaboration stalled. New employees wondered where the veterans were. The culture had changed.

Each individual decision was rational. The pattern they created was not what anyone intended.

This is the one-day-at-a-time rationalism trap. Each analysis is sound, but the sequence produces outcomes no single analysis considered. The first discount seemed sensible. So did the second. By the time you have given twenty, you no longer have pricing power — but no single discount decision was the cause.

The corrective is to ask not just "does this make sense?" but "what pattern does this establish?" Not just "is this the right decision today?" but "what will we do when we face a similar decision tomorrow?"

When making exceptions, consider the precedent. The one employee who works from home sets an expectation for every other employee who might want the same. When negotiating, consider the repeated game. The concession you make this customer will be expected by the next customer who learns about it. When setting policy, consider what patterns you are establishing. The workaround you allow for one case becomes the standard practice for all similar cases.

This does not mean never making exceptions. It means making exceptions with full awareness that exceptions tend to become rules.

## The Incremental Substitution Problem

Before examining precedents, a related error: the confusion between categorical priorities and incremental substitution.

When someone says "security is our top priority" they are treating security as categorically more important than everything else. When a founder says "the customer comes first" they are placing customer needs above all other considerations. When a board demands "growth above all else" they are establishing growth as the supreme value.

This sounds decisive. It is incoherent.

Whenever there are two things which each have some value, one cannot be categorically more valuable than another. A diamond may be worth much more than a penny, but enough pennies will be worth more than any diamond. The question is never "which is more important, A or B?" but "given how much A we already have, is more A worth more than some B?"

Human beings cannot live without salt, fat, and cholesterol. These are essential for survival. Yet most Americans get so much of these things that they shorten rather than lengthen lives. Something essential in small amounts becomes harmful in excess.

The same principle applies to every business decision. Security is important. At some point, additional security measures cost more than they save. Customer satisfaction matters. At some margin, additional investment in customer happiness produces less value than the same investment in product development. Growth is valuable. At some point, faster growth destroys value rather than creates it.

When told that something is a "top priority," the correct response is: at what margin? Given how much we already have? Compared to what we would give up?

This connects to precedent: "customer comes first" gets invoked to justify the exception; once the exception is granted, the precedent takes on a life that "customer comes first" never intended.

## Find the Missing Option

When people present binary choices, the most important question is often: *what third option exists that has not been considered?*

Binary framing is seductive. "We can cut features and ship on time, or ship the full product late." "We can hire fast and risk quality, or hire slow and fall behind." "We can invest in growth or invest in profitability."

These frames feel logical. They also exclude alternatives that may be superior.

When a VP presents two options, ask what is missing. Ship core features to some customers early while completing others? Different feature sets for different market segments? A different definition of "ship" that allows a limited release? The constraint that made the binary feel necessary may itself be challengeable.

A company debating growth versus profitability might discover a third path: which specific growth investments are actually profitable, and which are unprofitable? The binary collapsed growth into a single category, but growth is not a single thing. Some growth is value-creating and some is value-destroying. The relevant question was never growth-or-profitability but which-growth.

The missing option pattern applies especially to constraints everyone accepts as fixed. "We cannot raise prices because customers will leave." Have you tested this? Do you know which customers would leave and which would stay? What if you raised prices for some segments but not others? The constraint may be real, or it may be an assumption that has never been examined.

Finding the missing option is uncomfortable because it rejects the frame you have been given. The people who presented the binary put thought into it. They may feel that looking for alternatives is a criticism of their work. But the discipline of asking "what else?" before accepting "A or B?" saves organizations from false dilemmas.

## How to Apply

1. **Before any exception, ask: what precedent does this set?** Name it specifically. "If I approve this, what will I say to the next employee who asks for the same thing with a weaker reason?"

2. **Evaluate the repeated game, not the single instance.** The first discount to this one customer is not the question; it is *how do we price when customers learn we negotiate*. The first WFH exception is not the question; it is *what is our WFH policy after this*.

3. **Before categorical declarations ("top priority," "non-negotiable"), state the trade-off at the margin.** What are you willing to accept less of to have more of this priority? If you cannot answer, the declaration is a speech, not a priority.

4. **Reject binaries by default.** When someone presents A or B, ask "what third option have you considered?" If they have not considered one, the binary is suspect. If they have considered and rejected alternatives, ask why — the constraint may still be challengeable.

5. **For decisions that establish precedents, decide the *policy*, not the instance.** If you're going to approve one work-from-home request, think through the policy: who gets it, under what circumstances, with what conditions. Approve this specific request under the policy, not as a standalone exception.

6. **Document the precedent-setting decisions so you can apply the policy consistently.** Many organizations drift into new norms because no one tracks what they have agreed to. Write it down.

## Examples

**Situation:** Your biggest enterprise customer is asking for a 25% discount to renew. Your sales team thinks you should give it. "It's just one customer."
**Application:** The repeated game. Other customers will learn of the discount (either directly — the customer tells peers in the industry — or indirectly, when they hear about it from your own sales team or from the customer's vendor-comparison conversations). What precedent does the 25% discount establish? The answer is "our list price means 75% of our list price for any customer with leverage." Every future renewal and every new negotiation starts from that assumption. The $X you give up on this deal is not the cost; the multi-year erosion of pricing power is the cost.
**Result:** Either (a) refuse the discount and accept the risk of losing the customer, or (b) structure the concession to be less precedent-setting: a multi-year prepayment discount, a bundled deal that adds value on your side, a scope reduction rather than a price reduction. What you cannot do is grant a 25% renewal discount and treat it as "just one customer."

**Situation:** A VP is presenting a planning choice: "We ship the full product in Q2 or we cut to core features and ship in Q1."
**Application:** Third option check. What about: ship a limited release of the full product to the top 10 customers in Q1 and broad launch in Q2? What about: ship the core features in Q1 with an in-app signal for beta access to advanced features being completed? What about: ship the full product only for the segment that needs all the features and a simpler version for the segment that doesn't? The binary may be wrong.
**Result:** The team finds a third option that produces earlier feedback while preserving optionality. Or the team confirms the third options are genuinely unworkable and the binary stands. Either way, the decision is more informed.

**Situation:** A founder says: "Quality is our top priority. We don't ship anything that isn't perfect."
**Application:** Categorical priority without trade-off specification. At what margin? Is the next hour of polish worth more than the next hour of iteration with real users? Is zero defects worth a six-month delay? The honest answer is usually no — past some point, additional quality produces less value than what you are giving up to get it.
**Result:** Replace the slogan with a trade-off. "We ship when defects are below X, when core workflows are reliable, and when user feedback has validated the design — not when the product is perfect." Now the priority is actionable.

**Situation:** An employee asks to skip the Q3 planning offsite because of a family commitment. It's a legitimate reason.
**Application:** The exception analysis: what precedent does this set? If you say yes, what will you say to the next employee who has a weaker reason but wants the same accommodation? If you say no, are you being inflexible about a genuinely good reason? The honest answer is usually: decide the policy. When are we flexible on offsites? Under what conditions? Once you have the policy, apply it here.
**Result:** The policy probably is: flexibility on offsites is granted for (a) family medical, (b) pre-committed personal obligations made in good faith, (c) specific cases the team approves. The employee qualifies under (b); the exception becomes a consistent policy application rather than a precedent-setting exception.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat an exception as a one-time event when the category will recur.
**Why:** Once you have made an exception, the category now contains an instance. Other people with similar requests will reference it. The honest approach is to treat the first exception as a policy decision, made for this instance and for all future instances in the same category.

**Don't:** Let someone present you a binary without challenging it.
**Why:** Binaries are often analytically lazy — they compress real optionality into two presented choices. Ask what is missing. It costs you thirty seconds and sometimes unlocks a third option that is better than either presented choice.

**Don't:** Declare categorical priorities to avoid trade-off conversations.
**Why:** Declaring "quality is non-negotiable" is a way to avoid the uncomfortable conversation about what you are willing to trade for quality. The trade-off exists anyway. Naming it produces better decisions; hiding it produces slogans and drift.

**Don't:** Make decisions based on whether the case is sympathetic.
**Why:** The first work-from-home request always comes from someone with a good reason. That is not why you're deciding; you're deciding the policy, which will apply to future requests whose reasons will be progressively weaker. Evaluate the pattern, not the instance.

**Don't:** Use precedent analysis as infinite stall.
**Why:** At some point the repeated game has been considered and you must decide the specific case in front of you. "What about precedent" that never resolves is not precedent analysis; it is avoidance. Name the precedent you are setting, decide whether you're comfortable with it, decide the instance accordingly.

The question is never just whether a decision makes sense today. It is whether it makes sense considering what comes next. Get the time horizon wrong and today's success becomes tomorrow's problem. Get the precedent wrong and the exception becomes the rule. Get the framing wrong and you choose between A and B when C was available all along.
