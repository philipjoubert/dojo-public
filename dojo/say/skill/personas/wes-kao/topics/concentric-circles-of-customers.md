# Concentric Circles of Customers

## When to Use
- User feels their product "really is for everyone" and doesn't know where to start.
- User has limited budget and bandwidth and is trying to be everywhere at once.
- User is selling to a B2B buyer who keeps citing "policy" or asking "what will I tell my boss?"
- User can't figure out why messaging that feels strong to them keeps falling flat with the market.

## Fails When
- **There is no real inner circle yet.** — If you can't name three real people who would die-hard defend the product, you don't have a positioning problem, you have a product problem. Concentric circles assumes a center exists.
- **The inner circle can't sustain the business.** — If the bullseye is twelve people, you can't build there. You need to honestly assess whether the center is large enough to fund expansion.
- **You apply it to a true commodity.** — Toothpaste, paper towels, electricity. Concentric circles assumes worldview-driven differentiation. Commodities don't have worldviews.

## Core Concept

"Don't try to market to everyone." By now this is obvious. But what if you feel like your product really is for everyone? Where do you start?

Think about your customers as concentric circles on a bullseye. In the center are your core customers — die-hard fans who completely "get it" and are excited you exist. Their worldview already matches yours. Start by marketing to them, then expand outwards.

🚫 Trying to please every rung at once.
✅ Saturate the center, then move to the next adjacent ring.

You might want to be the default choice. But you can't get to "everyone" in a single leap. The image of concentric circles tricks your brain into letting you target everyone — eventually. On your way to the masses, you've got to go through lots of "circles" of people. Facebook started with Harvard. Then the Ivies. Then other universities. Then high schools. Now your grandpa scrolls his newsfeed daily. SmartWool didn't start as athletic wear — they started as odor-neutralizing merino socks, then expanded.

Forget people on the outer rungs. Later, when everyone else joins, they'll join too. As social creatures, we care what the people around us are buying. The far outer rungs will be convinced when the rung closest to them starts talking about your product. Their close friends will eventually convince them for you.

**The bureaucrat is the outer ring.** When you're selling to someone who keeps asking "what will I tell my boss?" — you are talking to a bureaucrat, regardless of their title. Being a bureaucrat is a mentality, not a title. They optimize for deniability, not outcomes. They want to avoid being wrong. There is a big difference between wanting to be right and wanting to avoid being wrong. If your inner circle is full of change agents and early adopters, do not blow your fire trying to drag a bureaucrat across the chasm. They will spend years saying no in well-reasoned ways.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the bullseye.** Write down the exact person at the center. Not a persona. A real human. What do they already believe? What worldview do you share with them?
2. **Audit your current marketing for outer-ring pandering.** Find every line of copy, every channel, every campaign that's trying to convince a skeptic. Cut it or quarantine it.
3. **Saturate the center first.** Concentrate budget, copy, and channel on the inner ring until you've genuinely tapped them out. "Tapped out" means you've stopped finding new center-circle customers, not that you're tired of marketing to them.
4. **Move one ring out — not two.** The next ring is adjacent. They share most of the worldview but need a little more proof. Use social proof from the center ring as your bridge.
5. **For bureaucrats specifically: load up on social proof.** VCs, press, customer logos, testimonials, advisory boards. Make the safe choice obvious. Help them answer "what will I tell my boss?" before they ask.
6. **Make it more dangerous to say no than yes.** If a gatekeeper thinks "if I block this and my boss knows them, I'm in trouble," they'll forward your note. The decision didn't get made on merit — it got made on defensibility.
7. **Watch out for faux early adopters.** Senior leaders who claim to be risk-takers, then defer the moment risk shows up. They are the worst kind of internal champion. Find a real one or move on.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder says their B2B SaaS tool is "for any company that wants to be more efficient." Three months in, conversion is brutal. Sales calls keep ending with "let me run this by procurement."

**Application:** "You're trying to convert the outer ring while skipping the bullseye. Who already lives the worldview your product assumes? Probably an ops lead at a Series B startup who's tired of duct-taping spreadsheets, not a procurement officer at a Fortune 500. Stop pitching procurement. Pitch the ops lead. And when you do hit a bureaucratic buyer, don't lead with how new and innovative you are — lead with how safe, proven, and normal you are. Logos, testimonials, customer count. Help them answer 'what will I tell my boss?' before they ask."

**Result:** Sales cycles compress because the inner-ring buyers don't need to be convinced of the worldview — they already share it. The Fortune 500 deals come later, after the inner ring becomes social proof.

**Situation:** Early-stage edtech founder is running paid ads targeting "lifelong learners" — broad demo, broad geography, broad messaging. CAC is climbing, no clear winners.

**Application:** Concentric circles says: who is the absolute center? Probably a specific kind of operator — say, a marketer at a high-growth startup who reads Lenny's newsletter and has already paid for one cohort-based course. That's a population, not a slogan. Build everything around them. Ignore the lifelong learners until the operators are saturated.

**Result:** Smaller addressable market on day one, but message-market fit is sharper, word-of-mouth is real, and the next ring (founders, PMs, designers) is already adjacent.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write copy that hedges so it appeals to both the inner ring and the outer ring at once.
**Why:** You end up sounding like nothing to no one. The inner ring loses the recognition signal that says "this is for me," and the outer ring isn't convinced anyway. Hedged copy is the worst of both worlds.

**Don't:** Spend marketing budget trying to convert bureaucrats by playing up how cutting-edge you are.
**Why:** Bureaucrats experience newness as risk. You can spend months trying to convince someone to embrace newness — but they've spent years with that mindset. Asking them to flip 180 degrees is unlikely. Either reframe as "safe, proven, normal" with social proof, or skip them and find a non-bureaucrat champion.

**Don't:** Use one of your inner-ring fans as proof while simultaneously discounting your product to chase the outer ring.
**Why:** The inner ring watches what you do. Heavy discounting tells them they overpaid and that the product is commoditizing. You break the cognitive dissonance that was working in your favor ("I jumped through hoops, this must be valuable") and replace it with the version that works against you ("they had to bribe me, this must not be that good").

**Don't:** Confuse "we got a meeting with a big enterprise" for "the inner ring is saturated."
**Why:** One inbound enterprise lead is not market signal. It's an anecdote. Saturation means you've systematically reached the inner ring and watched the conversion math hold. Premature ring-skipping is how startups blow their runway chasing one whale.

**Don't:** Treat customer interviews as a substitute for shipping to the inner ring.
**Why:** Interviews are low-stakes — no commitment, no money, you're in listening mode. It feels productive but you're often just collecting stamps. If knowing a piece of information won't change the way you act, there's no point in knowing it. The inner ring reveals itself through purchases, not opinions.
