# Sharper Writing Toolkit

## When to Use
- The user has a draft (email, Slack, doc, blog post, customer message) and is in the edit phase.
- The user's writing sounds vague, corporate, apologetic, or mushy and they don't know why.
- The user is preparing a high-stakes message — investor update, customer reply, board memo, hard feedback — and wants the prose to actually land.

## Fails When
- The user is writing in a regulated context where hedging is legally required.
- The user applies these filters during the brainstorm/first-draft phase. These are editing tools. Use them after you have words on the page.

## Core Concept

Mediocre copy is a missed opportunity. **Bad messaging obscures your value. Good messaging is an amplifier.** It takes a seed of something good and aggrandizes it. The benefit is a transference of emotion — of course you feel excited about your product; with good messaging, you can transfer that feeling to your customer.

Most writing fails in a particular zone. **When writing is obviously bad, you fix it. When writing is obviously good, you ship it. But what if it's not terrible — but it's not good either?** That's limbo copy. It's just kind of there. Editing it takes a disproportionate amount of mental effort, because the problem isn't usually the copy — it's that the underlying idea is fuzzy.

This file is the tactical toolkit for sharpening writing once you know what you want to say. It's a set of moves to run on a draft.

### 1. The Robot Voice test

Read your draft out loud — but in a robot voice. No emotion. No warmth. No benefit-of-the-doubt.

Why: in person, you have facial expression, tone, pace, mood, pitch — all the cues that show sincerity. **In writing, you have only text. Cold, hard text.** Your reader might be having a bad day, feeling insecure, or skimming on the subway. They'll read your words through their own emotional state, which you have no control over. They might read your note with the emotional excitement of a robot — or worse.

If your draft sounds cold, dismissive, or passive-aggressive in robot voice, that's how it might land. Edit until it works EVEN IF read flat. Often, swapping out a single word is enough to make writing sound friendlier but still accurate.

### 2. Fix the underlying idea, not just the words

If you're stuck editing and the copy still feels off, the problem is probably the idea. **The lack of a point of view expresses itself as mediocre copy.**

Moves to clarify the idea:
- **Finish the knock-knock joke.** Sometimes you wrote the setup and forgot the payoff. Add the "Who's there?"
- **Even a tweet has a narrative arc.** A beginning, middle, and end. If your message feels incomplete, it's missing the tension or resolution.
- **State your assumptions and rationale.** Show your work. This lets readers respond to specific logic gaps instead of dismissing the whole idea.
- **Scarce + relevant = valuable.** Teach something readers don't already know. What's surprising AND true?
- **Clarify your stance.** Have a spiky point of view rooted in evidence.

### 3. Delete these words

Take 3 minutes to delete these and your writing improves forever:

**Delete "fine."** Saying "fine" sounds like you're not fine. Use "good," "great," "on board."

**Delete "however."** Melodramatic way to say "but." Skews corporate and bureaucratic. If you need a negating word, use "but."

**Delete "unfortunately."** It subconsciously accepts blame and makes situations sound worse than they are.

**Delete double negatives.** When you say "not," your reader has to think of what is, then reverse it. "I could not be more proud" → "I am incredibly proud." "He's not wrong" → "He has a point." Short phrases ("couldn't have said it better") are usually fine. Longer or more complex sentences should default to affirmative.

**Delete "can't until."** You're giving up power unnecessarily. State what you CAN do. "I can't meet until 2 PM" → "I can meet at 2 PM and after."

**Delete "honestly" / "to be honest."** If you point out your honesty, you imply you're usually dishonest.

**Delete "very," "truly," "really."** You'll come across just as sincere without them. "I really appreciate you and I'm truly glad to be part of this team" → "I appreciate you and I'm honored to be part of this team." Pro move: swap the filler for a more evocative word ("truly glad" → "honored").

End sentences on a positive word. Your reader's eyes see first and last words more than the middle. "Leisure" beats "no big deal."

### 4. Speak in the affirmative ("Do this" beats "Don't do that")

To understand "Don't do that," the reader has to think about "that," then reverse it. Saying "Do this" saves a mental step. **If you can save people a mental step, you're doing a kind thing.**

It's also kinder when giving feedback — "don't" sounds reprimanding. A good diving coach says "Point your toes," not "Don't point your toes the wrong way." It's simpler to remember what to do — not what not to do.

It also makes you sound confident instead of apologetic. "I can only meet between 11–2pm" implies you think you should be more available. "I'm available 11–2pm" owns it.

### 5. Use accurate, precise language for your level of certainty

Conventional wisdom says sound more confident, drop the "I think." That works WHEN STAKES ARE LOW. If you're tweeting a hot take, sound certain. If your boss is making a $50K decision based on your input, **it's dangerous and irresponsible to sound overly confident.**

Pick words that match your actual certainty. "This will X" and "This tends to X" are not the same thing. Qualifiers like *might, could, tends, perhaps* add real meaning.

When in a position of authority, this matters more — bosses speaking prescriptively make teams less likely to push back. To encourage healthy dissent, speak with accurate conviction. If you're riffing, signal that you're not 100% sure. Use "I" instead of "we" so the team doesn't feel they're hearing an organizational decree.

**Empathizing words:** consider, might, could, suggest, seem, can be, try, probably, likely, in general, "have you thought about," "may want to try," "the story I tell myself is," "my point of view is," "this is A way" (not "THE way"), "sometimes" (not "always"/"never").

**Prescriptive words to use sparingly:** should, must, do this, never, only, always, "it's crucial," "the thing is."

### 6. Sound like a human, not a corporate machine

No one talks like the corporate manuals from your college career center. Avoid jargon. Use a mix of short and long sentences for rhythm. Trim adverbs. Swap weak nouns and verbs for strong ones.

Bad grammar can be a feature. "Free gift" is technically redundant — but seeing the word "free" makes your audience's eyes light up. "Our nation must come together to unite" is redundant but lands hard because it ends on "unite." **Getting people to FEEL SOMETHING always overrides conciseness, technical correctness, and anything else.** Don't be a stickler about useless things. The goal is to win hearts and minds — not to please your 10th grade English teacher.

You can break convention if you know why you're doing it and have an assertion about why it'll work.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the draft.** Don't filter while drafting. Get the idea down.
2. **Read it once in robot voice.** Mark anywhere it sounds cold, passive-aggressive, or unclear.
3. **Run the delete-words pass.** Search for: fine, however, unfortunately, honestly, very, truly, really, can't until, "not [verb]." Replace or delete.
4. **Flip negatives to affirmatives.** Anywhere you said "don't" or "I can't," try the opposite framing.
5. **Check certainty calibration.** Are you over-claiming or hedging? Match the words to the actual stakes and your actual conviction.
6. **Cut filler and corporate-speak.** If a sentence sounds like a manual, rewrite it like you're talking to a friend.
7. **Check the last word of each paragraph.** Is it positive? Strategic? Evocative? Or is it a throwaway like "no big deal"?
8. **Final pass: read it again, robot voice.** Did it improve?

## Examples

**Situation:** A team member is replying to a customer who asked about a livestream date.

**Application:**

> 🚫 "Unfortunately we currently only offer the two livestream sessions on X dates, but all livestream sessions are recorded and distributed to students the following day. All other content can be accessed 24/7."
>
> ✅ "Absolutely. The two livestream sessions are scheduled for X dates, and all livestream sessions are recorded and distributed to students the following day. All other content can be accessed 24/7."

**Result:** Same information. Different emotional register. "Absolutely" opens with abundance and certainty. "Unfortunately" opened by accepting fault for nothing.

---

**Situation:** A founder writing a launch announcement opens with: "However, we'll be launching on Tuesday. The only exceptions are early-access users, who'll get it Monday. However, this is a soft launch, so no big deal if you miss it."

**Application:** Run the delete pass. Two "howevers" become "and." "Only exceptions" becomes "early-access users get it Monday." "No big deal" becomes "you can join any time after."

> "We're launching Tuesday. Early-access users get it Monday. You can join any time after."

**Result:** Three sentences instead of three "howevers." Crisp, confident, no apologizing for things that aren't problems.

---

**Situation:** A manager giving feedback opens with "I really appreciate everything you do, and I just want to say truly, you've been very helpful. However, I'm not trying to be discouraging, but I have some concerns about the project."

**Application:** Robot-voice this. It sounds simultaneously gushing and threatening. Pull out: very/truly/really, however, "not trying to," "concerns." Rewrite affirmatively:

> "Your work on the project has been strong. I want to share some thoughts on where we can sharpen it for the next phase."

**Result:** Same intent, no inception, no hedging, no melodrama. The recipient hears the message instead of bracing for an attack.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply these rules during the first draft.
**Why:** They're editing tools. Filtering while drafting kills momentum and produces stilted writing. Get the idea down first; sharpen second.

**Don't:** Strip every qualifier and end up sounding falsely certain.
**Why:** "Drop the 'I think'" works only when stakes are low. In high-stakes business contexts, calibrated language IS the goal — you want your "I'm sure" to mean something different from your "I think this could work." Match certainty to reality.

**Don't:** Confuse "tight writing" with "short writing."
**Why:** Concise means an economy of words for the idea you have. Some ideas need space. The point of the toolkit is to remove what isn't pulling weight, not to truncate everything to 30 seconds.

**Don't:** Use bad grammar randomly.
**Why:** "Break convention if you know why and have an assertion about why it'll work." Random grammar mistakes sound careless. Strategic ones (ending on a strong word, redundancy for emphasis) sound deliberate. Know which you're doing.

**Don't:** Lecture readers with corporate softeners that mean nothing.
**Why:** "Going forward," "moving the needle," "circle back," "at the end of the day" — these phrases are cognitive sugar with no nutrition. They make you sound like the manual, not like a human. Cut.

**Don't:** Skip the robot-voice read because you "wrote it warmly."
**Why:** You wrote it warmly because you were warm WHILE writing. Your reader will read it in whatever state they're in — possibly cranky, distracted, or threatened. Robot voice mimics that. If it works in robot voice, it works.

**Don't:** Treat "fine" or "no problem" as neutral.
**Why:** They're not. "Fine" reads as resentful. "No problem" implies there could have been one. Replace with words that actually convey what you mean — "good," "happy to," "glad to."
