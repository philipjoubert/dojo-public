# Brand vs Performance Marketing — The Spectrum

## When to Use
- User keeps pouring more budget into paid ads and CAC keeps rising.
- User's leadership team can't agree on what marketing is actually for.
- User is starting a marketing function and needs to set the philosophical baseline.
- User has been doing only-brand or only-performance and hit a wall.

## Fails When
- **The runway doesn't exist.** — Brand marketing pays back over months and years. If the company has 30 days of cash, you're doing performance whether you want to or not. Brand-only is a luxury of solvent businesses.
- **The product genuinely is one-shot impulse.** — A tourist gift shop on a busy street really does live or die on conversion at the moment of foot traffic. Brand investment has no compounding effect.
- **The team has no taste.** — Brand work without taste is just expensive performance work that doesn't convert. Sometimes the right call is to do performance well until you can hire a brand person who's any good.

## Core Concept

There are few philosophical stances that will influence everything about your marketing thereafter. Deciding to prioritize brand versus performance is one of those things.

**The Law of Brand vs Performance Marketing:**
> All marketing activities are a trade-off between immediate conversion and brand equity.

🚫 Performance and brand are substitutes you pick between.
✅ Performance and brand are complements that pull on different timelines.

Performance marketing means better short-term conversion, worse long-term brand equity. Brand marketing means worse short-term conversion, better long-term brand equity. Performance harvests demand. Brand creates it. If all you do is harvest, eventually there's nothing left in the field.

Get on the same page about this early with anyone who can veto your work — clients, boss, co-founders. If you don't, you'll get push-back and skepticism downstream. The skepticism will be masked as logical criticism — something feels "off," it doesn't seem "right" — but what they really mean is "I thought we were more brand-driven (or performance-driven) than this."

**Performance-addicted companies hit a ceiling.** One client was squarely in the performance-driven camp. They did whatever converted. They became known for spammy ads and promotional offers. They sacrificed their brand to get short-term conversion. Eventually CAC still got too high, so they wanted to start investing in brand to drive word of mouth and reduce reliance on paid media. *But once you're addicted to throwing money at a problem, it's hard to pull back.* If your default is performance-first but you want to add brand, all your metrics will get worse in the short run. CAC up. Conversion down. Ads more expensive. Brace yourself.

**Brand-only companies have to walk away from money.** Hermès isn't trying to convert you with their Times Square billboard. They'd rather burn excess inventory than discount it. They are betting that with repeated exposure, when you can afford a statement bag, you'll want a Birkin. It seems easy to say "of course we'll prioritize the brand." Then you're walking away from a few million dollars in revenue and it gets harder. Most people want long-term brand equity but aren't willing to live with the worse click-throughs.

**Just because it's measurable, doesn't mean it's important. And just because it's not measurable, doesn't mean it's not important.** A horrible call with a customer service rep who's condescending and hangs up on you doesn't show up in any dashboard — until that customer churns six months later for "no recorded reason." Performance metrics underweight the things that move long-run.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the alignment conversation before you do any creative work.** Ask the team: "If you could only prioritize brand or performance, what would you pick?" Ask the same question of anyone who can veto your work. Get the answer in writing.
2. **Map every current activity onto the spectrum.** SEO, PPC, conversion optimization, lead gen, discounts, affiliates → performance. Podcast appearances, social content, thought leadership, influencers, brand experience, PR, product placement → brand. Email marketing sits in both.
3. **Set timeline expectations per activity.** If you measure brand marketing using performance marketing metrics, you will always be disappointed. Tell leadership upfront: "This podcast tour is a brand activity. Don't expect immediate sales lift. Expect compounding pull over six to twelve months."
4. **Decide your skew explicitly.** Most teams should aim for both, but most teams are still meaningfully skewed one way. Pick one — 60/40, 70/30 — and own the trade-offs. "We are 70% performance for the next two quarters because we need to hit growth targets. We are funding one brand bet — this podcast — and protecting it from performance metrics."
5. **If you're transitioning from performance to brand, warn leadership the metrics will get worse.** Customer acquisition cost will go up. Conversion rate will go down. Ads will get more expensive. This is the cost of weaning off the addiction. If leadership isn't on board with the rough patch, don't start.
6. **For brand-prioritizing teams: define your self-imposed constraints.** At the altMBA we didn't offer discounts, didn't use Seth's photo in paid ads, didn't use affiliates or referral programs, didn't mention how much money we helped students make, didn't dangle silver bullets, didn't use exclusivity plays. The constraints forced creativity in messaging, copy, visuals, and audience segmentation. They raised the bar.
7. **Accept that doing both well is genuinely hard.** On the surface, we made it look easy. Behind the scenes, we were ducks paddling hard under the water. Be patient with yourself and know the tension is normal.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder is spending 100% of marketing budget on Meta ads. CAC is up 60% year over year. They want to add more channels — TikTok ads, Google ads, affiliates.

**Application:** "You're doubling down on the addiction. Adding more performance channels won't fix the underlying issue: nobody is building demand at the top of the funnel. You're harvesting an empty field. Pull 30% of the paid budget into brand work — a point-of-view newsletter, a flagship customer documentary, a podcast tour for the founder. Tell your board CAC will get worse for two quarters. If they can't stomach that, you're stuck on the performance treadmill until something breaks."

**Result:** First two quarters, blended CAC rises and revenue is flat. Quarter three, organic mentions and direct traffic start showing up. Quarter four, CAC drops below the previous baseline because brand is doing work paid was doing alone.

**Situation:** Luxury skincare brand is brand-only. Beautiful Instagram, gorgeous packaging, no e-commerce funnel optimization. Revenue is stalled.

**Application:** "You don't need to abandon the brand stance — Hermès doesn't either. You need to add a performance layer that doesn't dilute the equity. Email sequences with a credibility tone, retargeting to existing site visitors with full-price product (not discount), search ads on your own brand terms. Performance can serve a brand-driven business — you just need self-imposed guardrails so the performance work doesn't undermine the brand."

**Result:** Conversion improves 30-50% on warm traffic without discounting, and the brand position holds.

**Situation:** Two co-founders disagree. One wants to run discount campaigns to hit Q4. The other wants to build long-term brand. The team is paralyzed.

**Application:** This is the alignment conversation Wes warns about. Surface it directly: "If you could only prioritize brand or performance, what would you pick?" The skepticism in your team meetings — "this feels off" — is really "I thought we were more brand-driven than this." Make the trade-off explicit. Put it on a wall. Pick a skew.

**Result:** The disagreement stops being about every individual campaign and becomes an explicit policy decision both co-founders signed.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Measure brand activities with performance metrics in week one.
**Why:** A podcast pre-roll won't convert listeners that week. Combined with other ads, a friend's recommendation, and more research, it might contribute to a sale six months later. If you kill the podcast in week three for "no measurable conversion," you murdered the input before it could pay back.

**Don't:** Quietly let a performance-driven org do "a little brand" with leftover budget.
**Why:** Brand work needs cover and a clear timeline. If it's an unfunded mandate squeezed between performance campaigns, the first time numbers dip, leadership will pull the budget. Get explicit alignment on the trade-off before you start, or don't start.

**Don't:** Do brand-driven creative with performance-driven copy hooks.
**Why:** This is the mismatch that produces sporky marketing — beautiful art direction with "1 weird trick to lose belly fat" headlines. The brand says luxury; the copy says clickbait. The customer's brain rejects the inconsistency. Pick a register and hold it.

**Don't:** Use the Law of Brand vs Performance as an excuse to not measure anything.
**Why:** Brand work is harder to measure but not unmeasurable. Brand-driven KPIs include direct traffic, branded search volume, share of voice, NPS, retention, willingness to pay full price, organic referral rate. Track them on quarterly cadence, not weekly.

**Don't:** Promise leadership "we'll do both equally."
**Why:** "Both equally" is a fudge that satisfies nobody. Most organizations are still either more brand-driven or performance-driven, even when they aim for both. Pick the skew, name the trade-off, and let the team make consistent decisions.
