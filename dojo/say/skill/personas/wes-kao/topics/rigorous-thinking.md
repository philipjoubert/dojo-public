# Rigorous Thinking

## When to Use
- Managing a team that constantly brings half-baked ideas — "Hey boss, can we do [random tactic competitors are doing]?" — and you're bottlenecked correcting them.
- Coaching direct reports to think strategically rather than just execute.
- Diagnosing a culture where everyone hedges, no one commits, and meetings end without decisions.
- Building your own habit (especially as a junior or mid-career operator) of bringing assertions instead of questions.
- Establishing team norms in a new org or team that you want to be evidence-based and decision-oriented.

## Fails When
- Psychological safety is missing. — Rigorous thinking under those conditions becomes interrogation; the team learns to never bring ideas, which is worse than half-baked ones.
- The manager uses it as a power move — sitting back, kicking their feet up, asking to be impressed. — That posture poisons the practice. Rigorous thinking has to be driven by curiosity, not contempt.
- The domain genuinely rewards speed over deliberation (incident response, firefighting). — Don't spend 30 minutes pressure-testing a hotfix. Use the framework where the stakes justify the cycle time.

## Core Concept

Rigorous thinking is where flimsy ideas go to die.

Wes's definition: rigorous thinking is asking critical questions about tactics and having a systematic way of making decisions. It's not a single mental model — it's an approach to problem-solving that lets you deconstruct ideas, gain clarity, and make decisions far more likely to be right.

🚫 **Lazy thinking**: making assumptions you don't even know are assumptions. Having a black box of logic where "suddenly it works and we have thousands of customers." Considering anything you don't want to do (usually the hard part of actually solving a problem) to be "the details."

✅ **Rigorous thinking**: any idea goes, but each team member is prepared to advocate for and defend it — the upside, the downside, data points rooted in reality, how it works given your assets and constraints. Questions are received with gratitude, not defensiveness.

Counterintuitively, rigorous thinking saves time. Instead of jumping to execute the first idea, you stress-test the logic. Less time on avoidable mistakes, more time on ideas worth pursuing. It's a force multiplier — a team of rigorous thinkers gives you a bench of up-and-coming leaders sharing the strategic load.

Three pillars hold the practice together: (1) eliminate "what do you want me to do?", (2) make assertions, not just insights or suggestions, and (3) when you ask a question, try to answer it.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Eliminate "What do you want me to do?"

We don't need more people who can only follow directions. We need leaders who can survive ambiguity — then pave the way by asserting what to do.

🚫 "What do you want me to do?"
✅ "I would like to do this and here's why it makes sense. Does this sound good to you?"

This reframe gets buy-in 10x faster. Why? Because we humans don't like to think. We have too much going on. So when an empathetic, trustworthy, sharp colleague (you) takes the lead and suggests what we should do — we breathe a sigh of relief.

Eliminate the passive question — and all versions of it — from your vocabulary. Everyone around you is waiting to hear what you recommend and why.

### Step 2: Move from insights → suggestions → assertions

The progression Wes drilled into early in her career at Gap (when senior merchant Susie Park kept asking "And? What should we do? What action should we take?"):

**Insight**: pointing out an observation. "I'm seeing a lot of high-end designers using plaid." Response: "Hmm interesting." Nothing changes.

**Suggestion**: putting forth a recommendation, proposing options. You're still off the hook because the boss decides. The decider holds the emotional burden.

**Assertion**: things get real. You're on the hook because there's more of you in what you're positing. You're advocating for your point of view and trying to convince others.

Assertions have three parts:

1. **A bias toward action** — answers the "so what?" Connects dots, interprets, posits what to do.
2. **Personal conviction** — you believe enough to look someone in the eye and try to convince them. Advocacy is built in.
3. **Ownership** — you'll take responsibility for trying. You're saying "If I were in charge, here's what I would do, here's where I would go, here's what I'm betting on."

If your manager just had to follow directions to a tee, they could hire someone on Fiverr. They're cheaper than you. And what's even cheaper? A robot. Any repeatable, clearly defined task can be done better by AI than a human. Wes's framework: do the job only humans can do — connect dots, see patterns, come to a conclusion about what this means and what we should do about it.

You don't need 100% certainty to make an assertion. When you're building something new, no one has the right answer — that's *precisely* why assertions are valuable. There's no historical precedent. You can't just copy someone else (different assets, different constraints). Somehow the project has to move forward. Assertions move it forward even under uncertainty.

Be honest about your level of certainty. Wes's template:

> "Based on my insight of X, I believe that Y is true. Given X and Y, I think we should do Z. I think it'll work because A and B. If it doesn't work, there's a risk of C, and I'll take care of that by doing D."

You're saying: I'm willing to look you in the eye and fight for this idea. I've done the rigorous thinking. I will take responsibility if it goes awry.

### Step 3: When you ask a question, try to answer it

Asking questions = no skin in the game. Many people ask a smart question and stop there. That's step one. Step two is asserting your answer.

When you just ask a question, it puts 100% responsibility on the other person to respond. For hard problems, your counterpart has likely asked themselves smart questions many times before. *If all it took were a question, they would have figured it out already.*

🚫 Asking a question keeps you at arm's length. Ball in their court. Lots of options. No risk.

✅ Answering puts you up close. Ball in your court. Decision made. Skin in the game.

Questions generate new options. Most of us actually want fewer options — too many choices, too much data. You don't get rewarded for adding more options to the pile. You get rewarded for bringing wisdom about how to make sense of it all.

So next time you have a great question, think about how *you* would personally answer it. Share your point of view. Advocate for it. Continue asking questions — but don't *only* ask questions.

### Step 4: Replace "let me know what you think" with rigorous proposals

The lazy version:

🚫 "Hey boss, can we do [insert random tactic competitors seem to be doing]?"

The rigorous version:

✅ "Hey boss, I think we should do [tactic]. The upside and why it's worth the time, budget, and effort: ____. The downside is ____. We can minimize the downside by testing it small, like this: ____. I've seen orgs like X and Y try this — used them as a frame of reference. If you agree, the next step would be ____. I can manage this and report back."

Notice what's there: a recommendation, the reasoning, the failure mode, a low-cost test, references, and ownership of next steps. That single message is the whole shift.

### Step 5: As a manager — coach, don't correct

The dilemma: you want your team to think like owners and bring you fresh ideas — *and* you want those ideas to be defensible. Each time you give them a wrist slap for stepping outside the box, you discourage them from trying new things. Each time you just answer their question, you do their thinking for them and they don't grow.

The escape: ask, don't tell. Use these questions every time someone brings you an idea:

- "This is a great start. How do you see this working?"
- "Who is this for?"
- "Why would they be excited to do the thing you want them to do?"
- "What is this for?"
- "What's the hard part?"
- "What would success look like?"
- "Who do we need buy-in from for this to move forward?"
- "Can we do this ourselves, or do we need engineering, sales, etc.?"
- "How can we experiment with this in a way that requires less overhead? And even less? And even less?" (Wes asks this three times. You'd be surprised how small you can make an experiment while still getting what you need.)
- "What models did you look at? What's your frame of reference? How is our situation different?"
- "How does this tactic play to our assets and brand?"
- "What constraints are we working within?"
- "If we decided to do this today, what would you need to make it happen?"

Result: you rarely say no, because it's not about yes or no. It's about vetting. The employee often realizes themselves that the idea won't work in its current form. They go back to the drawing board, return with a stronger version. They become sharper thinkers over time. You use less brainpower and get better results.

### Step 6: Build psychological safety as the foundation

Rigorous thinking only works if psychological safety is present. Your team has to trust that you care, that you're asking hard questions because it's better for them, the team, and the idea — not because you're trying to make anyone look bad.

This isn't an interrogation. The way you ask matters. Don't sit back, kick your feet up, and ask to be impressed. Ask hard questions in a way that's supportive and driven by curiosity, not contempt. It's two people walking through an idea and scenario-planning what it could look like, with the goal of finding the best solution. Win-win.

Treat questions as a gift. Wes's exact phrasing from Maven's culture: "When questions are asked of them, react positively and see the questions as a gift. A team member's initial reaction may be to feel a little defensive, and that's natural, but that's not the reaction to act on. The way to react is to appreciate when a colleague cares enough to speak up."

### Step 7: Sharpen the analytical layer

Rigorous thinking includes being analytical. Wes's heuristics from her Gap analyst days:

1. **Patterns and pattern breaks**: What's normal? What sticks out? Why? How much does it stick out compared to everything else?
2. **Find the boundaries of an idea**: Where does it apply? Where doesn't it? The power is in seeing nuance where others see binary yes/no.
3. **Absolute numbers AND percentages**: Big numbers seem good in absolute terms; percentages reveal relationships.
4. **Variance**: Change from baseline. Month over month. This year vs. last year. In line with industry growth or not?
5. **Expected vs. actual**: 17% growth seems good — until you remember you forecasted 30%.
6. **Percent contribution to whole**: Tiny sliver or majority of the pie? In dollars, volume, top hits.
7. **Zoom out to broader scale**: 3-5 year peaks and valleys. If white tees have never sold more than X in 5 years, what makes you think they will this season?
8. **Profit margin, not revenue**: $5M revenue with $4.9M cost is not the win it sounds like.
9. **Who benefits from this narrative?**: Critical thinking. What are the incentives of the messenger?
10. **Investigate why**: When you spot a pattern or pattern break, find the cause and the impact. Validate or invalidate the hypothesis until you get closer to the truth.

Being analytical isn't about numbers — it's about developing healthy skepticism and curiosity. Validate your hypothesis. Look at multiple data points. Get closer to truth.

## Examples

**Situation:** A direct report messages: "Hey, I noticed our competitor just launched a referral program. Should we do one too?"

**Application:** Wes's lens: that's a question with no skin in the game. The manager's move is not to answer "yes" or "no" — it's to coach. Ask the questions: Who is the referral program for? What's it for? What does success look like? How would we experiment in a low-overhead way? How is our situation different from theirs? What assets do we have that they don't? Then close with: "Bring me back a one-pager with your assertion." That trains the report. Next time, the report opens with: "I think we should do a referral program. Here's the upside, downside, low-cost test, and ownership."

**Result:** The report gets better. The manager's calendar gets back. The org gets a steady stream of stronger proposals.

---

**Situation:** Junior PM in a meeting with their VP. They've spotted a problem in the funnel and are about to say "Should we look into the drop-off in step 3?"

**Application:** Convert the question into an assertion. Wes's template: "Based on my insight that step 3 has a 40% drop-off (vs. 15% on step 2), I believe the friction is the form length. I think we should run an A/B test cutting it from 11 fields to 6. I think it'll work because [reference to similar test elsewhere in the funnel]. If it doesn't work, the risk is [data quality], and I'll handle that by [filter we can layer in later]." Same problem, totally different signal sent.

**Result:** The VP sees a future leader instead of a future ticket-filer. The work moves forward instead of bouncing back to the VP for direction.

---

**Situation:** A founder writes Maven's company values. One value will be "rigorous thinking." How do they articulate it?

**Application:** Wes's actual draft for Maven (real artifact): "We are relentlessly focused on solving problems and making the best out of less... Decision-making and writing are core skills of everyone on the team. We examine multiple points of view and make decisions in a thorough manner. We're not afraid to be wrong, and we don't delusionally ignore the truth for the sake of speed... We value qualitative data as much as quantitative, because while numbers never lie, they also never tell the full story. We then write down our thoughts because it is both the most efficient and effective form of communication."

Then operationalize it: open Q&A at every all-hands where anyone of any level can ask anyone any question (engineer to PM, sales to Head of Growth, support to CEO). Strategy docs are commentable by everyone. The word "rigorous" itself becomes a compliment used regularly. Outside contractors notice the difference.

**Result:** A culture where evidence-based thinking is rewarded and the founder isn't the only person doing strategic thinking.

---

**Situation:** A team member proposes an experiment that you can already tell won't scale. The lazy move is to say no. The rigorous move?

**Application:** "How can we experiment with this in a way that requires less overhead? And even less? And even less?" Ask three times. By round three, the team member has either reduced the experiment to something so small it's worth running anyway, or realized the idea genuinely doesn't work and has retired it themselves — which is more learning than a no would have produced.

**Result:** Either a $200 test gets run (high-leverage data either way), or the team member walks away with sharper instincts about how to evaluate ideas.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Sit back, kick your feet up, and ask to be impressed.
**Why:** That posture turns rigorous thinking into interrogation. The team will stop bringing ideas. Ask hard questions in a way that's supportive and driven by curiosity, not contempt — this is two people walking through an idea with the goal of finding the best solution.

**Don't:** Use rigorous thinking as cover to avoid making your own assertion.
**Why:** Hiding behind "I'm just asking questions" is the senior version of the same lazy move you're trying to coach out of your team. If you have a point of view, share it. If you don't, do the work to develop one. Asking questions ≠ skin in the game.

**Don't:** Demand certainty before someone makes an assertion.
**Why:** When you're building something new, no one has the right answer. The fact that no one has the right answer is precisely why assertions are valuable. Calibrate language to certainty (use hedges honestly), but don't require 100% confidence — that's a recipe for analysis paralysis and a team that never proposes anything.

**Don't:** Say yes or no without context.
**Why:** A yes or no without context is a missed opportunity for learning. If you only say yes/no, your team comes back with similar-quality ideas. They won't know why a proposal worked or didn't. Invest the time to share your thought process — it's a multiplier.

**Don't:** Confuse rigorous thinking with being a contrarian.
**Why:** The point isn't to disagree for its own sake. It's to pressure-test ideas. Rigorous thinkers can land on the same answer the rest of the room landed on — the difference is they got there by examination, not by inertia.

**Don't:** Wait until the team brings you a fully baked idea before you engage.
**Why:** The whole framework lives in the back-and-forth. If you only weigh in at the end, you've abandoned the coaching. Stay engaged through the messy middle — that's where the rigor gets built into the team's habits.

**Don't:** Forget that rigorous thinking takes longer up front.
**Why:** The trade-off Wes names: you either spend the time upfront training your team, or you spend a few hours every day correcting their work forever. Most managers don't budget the upfront time and stay stuck in the correction loop. Make the investment.

**Don't:** Say "any thoughts?" at the end of a presentation.
**Why:** It's the lazy-thinking version of asking for feedback. It produces vague nods and zero rigor. Replace with: "What's the weakest assumption in this plan?" or "If you were running this, what would you change?" or "What would have to be true for this to fail?" Specific prompts produce specific thinking.
