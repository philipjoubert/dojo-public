# The State Change Method

## When to Use
- Designing or running a live Zoom lecture, workshop, or cohort session — especially with 15+ attendees where personal charisma alone won't carry the room.
- Preparing for a guest lecture or webinar where you only get one shot.
- Diagnosing why your live session is dying — silence, cameras off, chat empty, attendance dropping mid-session.
- Building a recurring training cadence (weekly cohort sessions, internal company all-hands) where attention erodes if every session looks the same.

## Fails When
- The audience is 5-10 people. — At that size, you're having a conversation, not delivering a lecture; the dynamics are different and instructor charisma plus calling on individuals is enough.
- The format is recorded or async. — State changes are about breaking a *live* monologue rhythm; in async video, pacing is the editor's job.
- The user thinks "state change" means random interruption. — A state change punctuates with intent. Switching modality without purpose feels jumpy and confuses students more than monologue does.

## Core Concept

Students don't owe you their attention. Once you internalize this, you can embrace the fact that it's your responsibility to keep your audience engaged.

Listening to one person talk for long stretches is mind-numbing — especially on Zoom, where you have to sit still and stare at a screen. Humans are alert to change, not to monotony. We evolved to scan the savanna for movement: rabbit running, grass rustling, predator approaching. Sitting still listening to a single voice for an hour straight fights every wiring instinct we have.

🚫 Monotony causes audiences to tune out.
✅ Movement causes audiences to become alert.

The **State Change Method**: aim for a state change every 3-5 minutes to break up the monotony of a monologue-style lecture. A state change is anything that punctuates an instructor's monologue and offers a change in pace that causes students to perk up and snap back to attention.

You are 50% instructor, 50% entertainer. If you're 90% instructor and 10% entertainer, you're a college professor — and college professors are the only content creators with a guaranteed captive audience. The rest of us have to earn the attention. If you're 90% entertainer and 10% instructor, you're a BuzzFeed video. The job is to thread both — entertaining enough to keep them in the room, instructional enough that they leave better than they arrived.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Build a state-change toolkit

A state change is anything that breaks the rhythm. Wes's catalogue:

- Breakout rooms (1:1 or small group)
- Asking students to comment in the chat
- Switching from screen-share back to gallery view (or vice versa)
- Asking students to unmute and chime in
- Literally having anyone else talk (TA, coach, guest, alumni)
- Putting a question on screen and asking students to reflect silently
- Cracking a joke / adding humor
- Giving the mic to a student to share
- Asking students to pause and internalize what was just said
- Q&A
- Group discussions
- Hands-on projects
- Critique / feedback rounds
- Role-playing
- Time to hear from other students
- 1:1 pairs
- Analyze a case study, then analyze own situation
- Solo exercises
- Reflection questions

Notice the pattern: you're alternating *who is talking*, *what mode the audience is in* (consuming, producing, discussing, reflecting), and *what's on screen*. Variety on those three axes is what creates the perception of pace.

### Step 2: Plan state changes into the slide deck before you teach

Don't try to improvise state changes in the moment — you'll forget under the pressure of live delivery. Build them into the deck. Look at your existing lecture and ask: where is the next state change? If you go more than 3-5 minutes without one, insert one.

A useful rule: 5 slides with 1 main point each beats 1 slide with 5 points on it. One slide with multiple bullet points has no movement. Our eyes are used to seeing change on screens — TV producers know this, which is why every televised sport has swirling whoosh animations and documentaries zoom into static photos. Speed up the perceived pace.

### Step 3: Convert statements into questions

The simplest, lowest-effort state change: turn what you were going to *say* into what you're going to *ask*.

🚫 **Before:**
You: "Here's why X is important. Reason 1, 2, 3."
Students: Silence. Quiet because they're focused… or asleep? Can't tell.

✅ **After:**
You: "Why do you all think X is important?"
Students: Reason 1!
Students: Reason 2!
Students: Reason 3!
You: "Exactly, these are all excellent reasons." [Next slide that shows your Reasons 1, 2, 3.]

You ADDED one step: you turned a statement into a question. You still have the slide with the answer prepared in case students don't respond — and you should still share your point of view, because that's what they paid for. But the question creates a state change away from your single voice.

This is the Socratic method. Why don't more instructors use it? Because it takes longer than just giving the answer. Use self-restraint. The lesson lands deeper when the student arrives at it themselves.

### Step 4: Hook in the first 5-10 minutes — get to a participatory experience fast

The instructor who started with 5 minutes of his own background put everyone to sleep. Better: a few hooks first, then your background once people care.

Aim for participation in the first 5-10 minutes. Open with a "no-fail" question everyone can chime in on ("What did you have for dinner? Drop it in the chat") — it costs nothing, breaks the ice, and signals that this is an active session worth attending live. Then move to a 1:1 breakout right after housekeeping. That tells students the rest of the session will demand their participation, which makes them lean in instead of multitask.

### Step 5: Vary pace, length, and texture

Humans appreciate rhythm. Sentences that are all the same length are boring. Short sentences, long sentences, different paragraph sizes, different sentence structures — all of these help readers (and listeners) stay engaged. Same for live lecturing.

In music, pop songs change into a slightly different song every 15 seconds or so, but return to a theme to hold it together. In food, processed snacks like Doritos pique your taste buds with complex flavor formulas — you can't quite name what you're tasting, so you keep eating to figure it out. Live teaching works the same way. Keep the audience guessing what's next.

Vary fast and slow. Loud and quiet. Long monologue followed by sharp question. Solo reflection followed by raucous group discussion. The contrast is what wakes people up.

### Step 6: Let anyone else talk

The cheapest, fastest state change: literally let anyone else speak for a bit. Even if it's your facilitator, TA, alumni mentor, or coach. The change of speaker counts as a state change. You don't have to invent a complex exercise — handing the mic to a co-host for two minutes resets the audience.

### Step 7: Add interactivity — and then add more

When you ask students of cohort-based courses what they wish their course had more of, they rarely say "I wish there were more lectures." They say "more chances to meet other students, get feedback, do role-playing exercises."

Reach into your active-learning toolkit and pepper exercises throughout. Hands-on projects, critique sessions, role-plays, 1:1 pairs, case studies — they punctuate the lectures and the time flies by.

### Step 8: Recognize when you've crossed the conversation-vs-performance threshold

You don't lecture to a group of 5. You have a conversation with them. Brady-Bunch grid view, you can read every face, you can call on individuals. Charisma fills the gaps.

But at 100+ people on Zoom, the dynamic flips. You can't read the room — some look bored, some look excited, most are unreadable. Personal attention can't save the session. This is when you need to design like a performer: exaggerate gestures, use the full "stage," act out conversations. Not corporate-robot or D-list-actor mode — still real, still authentic — but with the awareness that 100 eyeballs are on you and you can't just call it in.

If you're moving from teaching small groups to teaching larger ones, this transition is non-obvious. Practice teaching to a group big enough that you can't rely on individual attention.

## Examples

**Situation:** Wes attended a Zoom workshop and left after 15 minutes.

**Application:** Her diagnostic: the instructor opened with 5 minutes of his background — everyone went to sleep. Then 5 minutes of breakout (good), then 5 minutes of lecture that wasn't valuable — Wes was ready to leave 2 minutes in. The instructor had ONE slide with 5 points on it for that lecture stretch. No movement on screen. No question to the audience. No anyone-else-talks moment. The whole session lacked state changes, which compounded as the value of the content dropped.

**Result:** Wes left. The takeaway: "If an instructor stays on one slide for a long time, there's nothing else to distract from the content… That means a higher bar for the quality of insight." Realistically, not every part of a course is groundbreaking. State changes give you cover for the merely-good stretches by keeping attention high while the content recovers.

---

**Situation:** A founder running a 60-minute internal all-hands every Friday and noticing engagement drop quarter over quarter.

**Application:** Audit the monologue ratio. If the founder is talking for 40 minutes straight, the meeting is failing the State Change rule by an order of magnitude. Apply the method: open with a chat-prompt question ("What's one win from this week?"), insert a 3-minute peer-pair on a strategic question 10 minutes in, hand the mic to a different team lead for 5 minutes mid-meeting, end with a Q&A round that takes real questions instead of a final monologue. State changes every 5-7 minutes minimum.

**Result:** Re-engaged audience. Better questions, better information flow, less calendar dread.

---

**Situation:** Cohort-based-course instructor preparing their first 90-minute live workshop session and panicking about how to fill the time.

**Application:** Wes's reframe: don't prepare 90 minutes of lecture. Prepare 30 minutes of high-leverage points and 60 minutes of state changes built around them. Plan: 5-minute hook, 10-minute concept #1, 10-minute breakout discussion, 5-minute synthesis from groups, 10-minute concept #2, 15-minute hands-on exercise with peer critique, 10-minute concept #3, 15-minute Q&A, 10-minute commit-to-action close. Now the workshop has 8+ state changes and the instructor is delivering a fraction of the words but generating more learning.

**Result:** Higher engagement, higher retention, lower instructor cognitive load (you're moderating, not monologuing for 90 minutes straight).

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with your background and credentials.
**Why:** You'll put the room to sleep. Hook them first with a question or a striking idea, *then* explain why they should listen to you. Their care is what makes your background interesting, and they don't care yet.

**Don't:** Build slides with 5 bullet points per slide.
**Why:** One slide with five points has no movement. Five slides with one point each gives you four free state changes (the slide transitions). Wes's rule: speed up the perceived pace by spreading content across more slides with less per slide.

**Don't:** Treat state changes as filler or gimmick.
**Why:** A state change without instructional purpose is a distraction. The polls, breakouts, and questions need to drive learning, not just break up dead air. Random interruption confuses; intentional punctuation engages.

**Don't:** Try to improvise all your state changes in the moment.
**Why:** Live delivery cognitive load is high. Bake the state changes into the slide deck before you go live — write them in the speaker notes, drop them on the slides, give them slide numbers. When the pressure hits, you'll thank yourself.

**Don't:** Default to "Any questions?" as your interactivity tactic.
**Why:** It's the laziest possible state change and it almost always falls flat — students don't want to be the first to break silence. Use specific prompts ("Tyler, what would you do in this case?"), chat questions ("Drop a 1 if you've seen this happen, a 2 if you haven't"), or breakouts. Engineer the participation; don't beg for it.

**Don't:** Skip rehearsing the new format.
**Why:** Moving from a 10-person group-coaching style to a 100-person performance style is a real skill shift. Most instructors don't realize how different it is until they're 20 minutes into a workshop that's dying. Practice teaching to an audience large enough that personal charisma can't carry it.

**Don't:** Use the State Change Method as a substitute for content.
**Why:** State changes punctuate substance — they don't replace it. If your underlying content is thin, the breakouts, polls, and chat prompts feel forced and students leave the workshop with the activities but no learning. Get the content right (Super Specific How), then engineer the rhythm around it.
