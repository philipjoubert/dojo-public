# How to Onboard Yourself

## When to Use
- You just joined a new role — at a startup, a company, a team, or a new scope inside your current company. Especially when there's no real onboarding plan and you're expected to start contributing in week one.
- You're 30, 60, or 90 days in and feel like you're still "getting context" instead of doing the job. The team is starting to wonder why.
- You're a senior hire and your team is too busy to hand-feed you the lay of the land. You need to manufacture the onboarding yourself.

## Fails When
- **You're in the wrong role.** No amount of process can save a structural mismatch. If you genuinely can't do the job, onboarding harder is not the answer.
- **You go too aggressive too early.** Sharing a hunch with calibrated conviction is good. Walking in on day three and announcing what's broken is *not* a fresh perspective — it's a signal that you don't know how to read the room. Use beginner's mind humbly.
- **You skip the human side.** "Take charge of the process" doesn't mean "skip 1:1s and only deliver output." You need allies. People will block you or unblock you based on relationships, especially in startups.
- **You confuse passive observation with active onboarding.** Sitting in meetings and reading docs without forming a POV is the default failure mode. You'll look busy and produce nothing.

## Core Concept

Most companies suck at onboarding. Most startups don't have a formal process at all — and even when they do, it's not designed to give *you* an edge over everyone else who started this month. So if you wait for information to be handed to you on a platter, **it won't happen.**

> 🚫 "I'll wait for them to tell me what to do."
>
> ✅ "No one is as invested in your success as you are. Take the reins."

The reframe: the reason you were hired is your new team was stretched for bandwidth. They couldn't wait any longer. They expect you to start contributing in week one — and if you onboard well, you can. The first 30/60/90 days aren't about being a fly on the wall. They're about **manufacturing your own edge** while you still have outsider eyes.

The seven moves of self-onboarding, in Wes's structure:

1. **Take charge of the process.**
2. **Start developing a point of view.**
3. **Review information with an active stance.**
4. **Pick up on unspoken rules.**
5. **Gather what you need to make recommendations.**
6. **Identify the types of decisions you'll make.**
7. **Assume you'll lead the meeting.**

The unifying principle is *active*, not passive. Every doc, every 1:1, every meeting is something you're using — not something you're absorbing.

## How to Apply

### Move 1: Take charge of the process

Don't wait. Ask yourself, in week one:

- **What info do you need to start contributing?**
- **What do you need access to?** (Tools, docs, data, channels.)
- **What kind of decisions does your manager want to be involved with?** (vs. ones you can run with.)
- **What open questions do you have?**
- **What's an easy win in the first month?**

Write these down. Treat your onboarding as a project you own. *"If your organization doesn't have an on-boarding process yet, you can document your on-boarding — and that's the beginning of a process for the next hire. I've done this and it's a great way to make a situation better than when you found it."*

The dual benefit: you get up to speed faster *and* you've shown you're proactive and self-directed — something every manager appreciates. You reinforce your own self-confidence, which lets you start a new role feeling strong, grounded, and in control.

### Move 2: Start developing a point of view (use beginner's mind while you have it)

Most new employees ask questions. *Wes's contrarian principle:* it's even more important to **share hunches.**

> 🚫 "I'm still absorbing. I shouldn't speak up yet."
>
> ✅ "Your fresh perspective is one of the most valuable things you bring. You only have beginner's mind once."

Write down what you agree with, what you disagree with, what provokes a strong reaction. After a customer interview, don't just write a recap. Write a recap *plus your analysis, hunches, and a forward-looking recommendation.*

The honest reason this matters: *"You work with smart people. If it were really so easy to ask one question to change everything, they would have thought of it themselves already."* Many great questions actually have an insight buried inside them — and it's the insight, not the question, that pushes the conversation. So bring the insight forward.

But — and this is the calibration — you preface with conviction levels. Not "this is broken, fix it." Instead: *"This is a hunch based on what I've seen so far — I want to validate, but my initial read is X."* You're not claiming to have all the answers. You're showing you're *thinking*, not just absorbing.

The deeper rationale: at startups, *"there's little to no historical precedent about what to do — you don't have a baseline from last quarter because you might have shifted your strategy, and you don't have explicit signposts to tell if you're doing a good job."* The team needs people who can move forward without certainty. Developing a POV — rooted in your instincts, data points, and logic — is what unlocks forward motion.

### Move 3: Review information with an active stance

Your new team will dump pages of documents, links, and decks on you. The default mode is "read for context." That's the wrong mode.

> 🚫 Reading docs as background information.
>
> ✅ Reading docs as you're going to use them in the next 30 days.

Ask, as you read each doc:

- **How will I use this information?**
- **Is this a nice-to-know one-time piece, or will I refer to this on a weekly basis?**
- **What questions are emerging? What patterns am I noticing?**

The trap: most new hires assume the first weeks are about basics — 1:1s with the team, learning customers, learning the product, industry context. *At startups, those are table stakes.* Every team assumes you're doing those. They have no stakes. *"If you only do these things, your manager will likely wonder why you haven't done more in the first few weeks. The sooner you show even a small win, the more you'll confirm why it was a great decision to hire you."*

For 1:1s and cross-functional meetings: come with a specific question and a clear idea of what you want out of the meeting. If you're remote and want to socialize as humans, that's important — *do it on Friday afternoons when work tends to be calmer*. Don't burn productive blocks on relationship-building you could time better.

### Move 4: Pick up on the unspoken rules

The spoken rules are easy. The unspoken ones are harder, mainly because people in the company might not even know the rules are rules — those approaches are just normalized.

Wes's list of dimensions to read on:

- **How does the team debate?** Are debates happening in public Slack channels and live meetings? People debating across levels? Or is the default more indirect — backchannel-heavy, hierarchical?
- **How territorial is the team?** Some cultures are siloed; some aren't. This affects how much you'll need to backchannel and get individual buy-in.
- **How much praise does the team give?** Some teams say "great job" for every small thing. Others are reserved. (One CEO told his team: *"I expect perfection. If you're perfect, don't expect kudos. If you go beyond perfect, you can expect praise from me."*) There's no right or wrong — equally successful cultures sit in opposite places. Knowing where yours is helps you stop wondering "is it just me?"
- **How does the team give and receive feedback?** Frequency, specificity, type, format, and norms for the receiver's reaction.

Reading these dimensions early prevents the most common new-hire mistake: applying the unspoken rules from your *last* job to your *new* one and getting subtle penalty for it without knowing why.

### Move 5: Gather what you need to make recommendations

There's a difference between **passive and active observation.** You should take time to absorb because you're new. At the same time, **assume you'll be asked to make recommendations in the near future.**

Ask yourself:

- **What are the biggest gaps and how can you fill them?**
- **What should your team start, stop, or continue?**
- **What do you want to prioritize given finite resources?**
- **How do you want to lead in the next phase?**
- **What's the proposed sequence of what you want to do?**

You won't answer these in week one. *Be patient with yourself.* The goal is to start pondering — because the moment you do, your information consumption changes. Suddenly the docs aren't "for context" — they're inputs to a decision you're going to have to make. You read differently. You retain differently. You ask sharper questions in 1:1s.

### Move 6: Identify the types of decisions you'll make

The first weeks of any job are characterized by information overload. Identifying the decisions you're expected to make helps you mentally bucket which information is worth keeping top of mind.

> 🚫 All information feels equally hypothetical, abstract, "for context."
>
> ✅ Information becomes tangible, concrete, real, and time-bound — because you can map it to an upcoming decision.

The questions:

- What decisions are mine vs. my manager's vs. shared?
- When are those decisions due?
- What do I need to know to make each one well?

You're triaging. You want to make a smart, well-informed decision you can stand behind. So you seek information that's relevant to the decisions actually coming.

### Move 7: Assume you'll lead the meeting

You behave differently when you're a participant vs. the meeting leader. So adopt the leader stance, even when no one told you to.

> 🚫 "I'm just here to observe."
>
> ✅ "If I were driving this, how would I run it?"

Wes's first-week-on-the-job story at Bare Escentuals: she was 24, expected to "observe" the task force. Fifteen minutes before the meeting, her manager called her in: *"I can't make it to the task force meeting anymore. The task force is 12 people from 7 cross-functional teams. Folks from legal, compliance, finance, product development, sales, customer education, and supply chain will be there. Starting today, you'll head it. It starts at the top of the hour, so you might want to start heading over."*

Lesson: *"You may be asked to jump into the deep end of the pool from day one. And you may be asked to do so publicly, with leaders who don't know you and aren't sure if they like you, where you're expected to lead them without positional authority the first time you meet them."*

The play: arrive more prepared rather than less. *"If you end up only needing to listen, you can do that. And if you're unexpectedly asked to lead, you can rise to the occasion."*

## Examples

**Situation:** You start as a senior PM at a Series B startup. There's no onboarding doc. Your manager says, "Take a couple weeks to get the lay of the land."

**Application:** Don't take that literally. Translate it as: *"You have a couple of weeks before I expect to see your first POV."*

Week 1: Aggressive 1:1s. Specific questions in each: *"What's the biggest thing you'd want me to fix in 90 days?" "What's the political third rail I should know about?" "What's a small win I could ship in 30 days that would give the team confidence?"* Read product strategy docs, customer interview transcripts, PR/FAQs. Active stance: write down what you agree with, what surprises you, what feels off.

Week 2: Frame your point of view. Calibrated conviction: *"Hunch — based on what I've seen, I think we're under-investing in [X]. I want to validate by talking to [3 people] this week."* Run it past your manager. Iterate.

Week 3-4: Ship a small win. Document the onboarding so the next person can use it. (Bonus: you've now shown you make situations better than you found them.)

**Result:** By day 30, you have a POV your manager has signed off on, a small shipped win, four sharp questions you've asked of every senior person, and an onboarding doc the next hire will use. You've manufactured your edge.

---

**Situation:** You're starting at a remote company. The team is friendly but distributed. You can feel yourself drifting into "I'll figure it out as I go."

**Application:** Take charge. List the moves: 1:1s with every cross-functional partner in week 1-2 (specific questions, not "tell me about your team"). Read the last 6 months of decision docs with an active stance. Identify two upcoming decisions — even small ones — and start gathering what you need to weigh in. Pick up on the rules: is this a public-Slack-debate culture or a backchannel culture? Is feedback effusive or reserved? Adjust accordingly. Ship the first POV by week 3.

**Result:** You're not relying on the absent onboarding plan. You built one.

---

**Situation:** You've been at the new job 60 days. Your manager casually asks, "How's it going?" and you realize you've been mostly absorbing. You haven't asserted anything yet.

**Application:** Run a course-correct. *"Honestly, I've spent the first 60 days mostly in absorption mode and I think that's been the right move for the first half — but I want to flip into output mode. Here's my POV on where I think the highest-leverage things are: [3 hunches with calibrated conviction]. I'd love to validate one of these with you and make a recommendation by [date]."*

**Result:** You've named the gear shift, made an assertion (with appropriate conviction levels), and set a deadline for yourself. Your manager now has signal that you're moving — which was the missing signal.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Walk in on day three and start telling people what's broken.
**Why:** Beginner's mind is valuable when paired with humility about what you don't yet know. A POV with calibrated conviction is welcomed. A confident verdict from someone who hasn't earned context is the new-hire archetype your team will quietly write off.

**Don't:** Read documents as "background" without asking how you'll use them.
**Why:** Passive consumption produces no recall and no insight. Active stance — *how will I use this, what patterns am I seeing, what does this imply for the decisions I'll need to make* — produces both.

**Don't:** Skip 1:1s with cross-functional partners because "I'll meet them when I need them."
**Why:** You always need them. Allies determine whether you can ship. The 30-minute coffee in week 2 buys you a year of unblock-able favors.

**Don't:** Confuse "1:1s and learning the product" with "doing the job."
**Why:** Those are table stakes at most startups. If your output by week 4 is "I had a lot of 1:1s," your manager is going to wonder. Pair the learning with shipped (small) wins.

**Don't:** Wait for explicit permission to lead a meeting.
**Why:** You may not get that permission, and you may end up leading anyway when someone bows out. Walk in prepared as if you're leading. Worst case, you're over-prepared. Best case, you save the day.

**Don't:** Stay quiet because "I want to seem humble."
**Why:** Quiet humility is indistinguishable from disengagement. Calibrated POVs ("hunch — not 100%") are the move that signals both humility *and* engagement. Pick that.

**Don't:** Wait until the formal 90-day review to share a POV.
**Why:** By 90 days, the team has already formed opinions about you. The window where "fresh perspective" is your asset closes by week 6 or so. Use it before it disappears.

**Don't:** Document your onboarding only for yourself.
**Why:** Document it as a deliverable. Hand it to your manager. Now you've made a process improvement *and* manufactured visible early output. *"I've done this and it's a great way to make a situation better than when you found it."*

**Don't:** Skip the unspoken rules check.
**Why:** Importing the unspoken rules from your last company is the silent killer. Different debate styles, feedback norms, and territorial dynamics get you penalized in ways no one will explicitly tell you about. Read the room first; act second.
