# Minimum Viable Backstory

## When to Use
- The user is about to share a story, pitch, presentation, or any explanation in a business context — and they have an instinct to "set the scene" first.
- The user has a draft email, doc, or Slack message that opens with throat-clearing.
- The user just wrapped a meeting and realized they spent the entire call on context with no time left for the actual ask.

## Fails When
- The user is a great storyteller and the audience came specifically for the slow burn (bedtime stories, a memoir reading, stand-up comedy).
- The situation calls for building trust through ritual context — e.g., a delicate personal conversation, a grief situation, a cultural setting where preamble is expected.

## Core Concept

A lot of stories, presentations, and pitches take too long to get to the point. **Start right before you get eaten by the bear.** That means: cut non-essential backstory so you can spend time on the juicy part.

Backstory scope creep is real. You start with the intent of sharing the basics, but once you start talking, you keep going. Before you know it, you've explained the history of your product, covered your career journey, gone on three tangents, and there are only a few minutes left for your recipient to actually engage. This is a losing situation for both sides — the speaker doesn't get advice, the listener zones out.

Aim for the **minimum viable backstory**:

> What's the minimum backstory necessary to set the context, so we can spend time on the important stuff?

You'll have to fight your urge to over-explain. You'd be surprised at how little backstory is needed to dive in. Once you start the conversation, you can always add information later.

To be clear: context is important. **Useful context is different from useless backstory.** Backstory is irrelevant sidebars, rabbit holes, tangents, preamble, and background information that's barely tied to your main point. If what you're sharing is relevant and what you actually want to talk about, that's not backstory — that's part of the actual story.

As a rule of thumb, aim for backstory to be roughly 10–20% of the conversation. That leaves 80–90% for what you actually wanted to talk about.

- 30 minute call: 5 minutes of initial backstory, 25 for the real topic
- 60 minute call: 10 minutes of initial backstory, 50 for the real topic

You are not Faulkner. You are not Steinbeck or Tolstoy. Your readers aren't 11th graders with required reading — they're coworkers, customers, investors, direct reports, your manager. They do not want anything resembling Grapes of Wrath on a Monday morning.

Skilled storytellers already do this. The Dark Knight opens with the Joker robbing a bank. The Matrix opens with Trinity high-kicking agents. Jurassic Park opens with zoo keepers failing to transport a velociraptor — and only then earns the room to explain the science. Action doesn't mean a Porsche has to blow up in 30 seconds. It means giving the audience a reason to keep watching.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the bear.** What's the actual juicy part — the moment of tension, the surprising claim, the ask, the punchline? Write it down.
2. **Cut everything before the bear.** Camping for three months, the Patagonia half-zip, the five-hour drive, the tent setup — gone. "On the last night, Steve left beef jerky out. The next thing we know, we're almost getting mauled by a bear."
3. **Keep cutting.** When you think there's nothing left to cut, cut some more. It will feel painful. Your words will become more efficient and impactful for it.
4. **Add context AFTER, not before.** If your recipient needs the setup, they'll ask. You can always layer it in. Most of the time they won't ask, because the action carried them.
5. **For STAR-method interview answers, watch the "Situation."** It's where backstory accidentally devours your time. Allocate so you have room for Task, Action, and Results — those are the parts that actually demonstrate you.
6. **For Slack/email, use Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF).** The 5 W's — who, what, where, when, why — at the top. Background below.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is asking Wes for advice on a 30-minute call.

**Application:** Instead of: "So three years ago, we started the company. We pivoted twice. Our first investor was X and they suggested Y. Then we hired Z…" — try: "We have 2 weeks until our launch. Conversion is 40% below where we need it. I think the issue is the pricing page, but my co-founder thinks it's the email sequence. Here's the data… What would you do?" Five sentences of minimum viable backstory. Twenty-five minutes left for the real conversation.

**Result:** The advice-giver can actually engage. The founder leaves with something useful instead of having narrated the entire history of their company.

---

**Situation:** A reader emails Wes after applying her advice. Before BLUF: "Hi Mike, based on our last discussion about the budget and at your recommendation Angela, Mike, John, and I had a meeting today at 9:15 AM to discuss the challenges we're facing in regard to the proposed budget."

**Application:** "Hi Mike, we've updated the proposed budget and need your sign-off by 3 PM. This will help us present it during the board meeting. Background info below. Let me know if we need to meet."

**Result:** Mike sees the ask in two seconds, knows the deadline, can decide whether to read the rest. Sign-off arrives on time.

---

**Situation:** A panelist at a conference spends 7 of their 20 allocated minutes on the "About me" slide and personal background.

**Application:** "I wish the panelists spent more time talking about their backgrounds," said no one ever. Cut the About-Me slide to one line of credibility ("I ran growth at X for 5 years"). Get into the substance.

**Result:** The audience remembers the substance, not the bio.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open a pitch with your founding-story origin myth before you've earned the right to it.
**Why:** Your audience didn't sign up for a hero's journey. They want to know what you do, why it matters to them, and what you want from them. The origin story is something you earn the right to tell — usually after the audience already cares.

**Don't:** Confuse "concise" with "short." Cutting backstory doesn't mean shrinking everything to 30 seconds.
**Why:** Concise means sharing enough to get your idea across with an economy of words. Some ideas need space — high-stakes business cases, complex strategic write-ups. The point isn't brevity for its own sake; it's removing the parts that aren't your actual story.

**Don't:** Treat backstory as the place where you "warm up" your thinking.
**Why:** When you don't know what you want to say, you tend to think out loud — and it's hard to think, analyze, and edit in real time. Spend 5–10 minutes BEFORE the meeting figuring out what you actually want to say. Then the backstory shrinks to its real size.

**Don't:** Give backstory on your backstory.
**Why:** That's the moment the listener checks out. If you find yourself adding "but first, some context about the context," stop. Find the bear and start there.

**Don't:** Apologize for being brief.
**Why:** Don't say "sorry to be so direct" or "I know this is short." That undoes the work of cutting. Just send the short message.
