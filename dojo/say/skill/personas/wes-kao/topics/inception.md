# Inception

## When to Use
- The user is writing or speaking in a high-stakes context: a pitch, a feedback conversation, a negotiation, a customer email, a co-founder argument.
- The user is about to "preempt an objection" by naming a negative idea out loud.
- The user is asking for something and instinctively wants to apologize or downplay first.

## Fails When
- The user is trying to deceive or manipulate someone against their own interest. Inception only works ethically when you're a good person trying to come across as one.
- The user gets paranoid and over-edits every word until the writing is stilted and emotionless. Use this lens at the editing stage, not as a real-time anxiety filter.

## Core Concept

In the movie Inception, characters debate whether you can plant ideas in people's heads. Anyone who has ever worked in an office knows the answer is YES. All those times your manager agreed to your idea because they thought it was their idea? That was inception.

The principle that follows: **be mindful of the ideas you're planting.** The issues you shine attention on will influence what your audience thinks about. **What you spend time on gets aggrandized — which means you might accidentally turn a non-issue into an issue.**

The classic example: 1973. Nixon on TV says, "I'm not a crook." Everyone watching immediately thinks, "Omg he's a crook. I didn't have the right words for this vague feeling, but now I do. You are totally a crook." Not only did Nixon incept the idea that he was complicit — he gave America the language to describe his actions.

Scott Adams calls this a "linguistic kill shot." A phrase that's visual, sticky, memorable, and once you hear it, you can't unsee it. **Don't give people vocabulary to use against you.**

This is constantly happening in business. Founders pitching: "We are only a team of 6 people right now, but we're adding 5 new features to our platform every month and respond to all customer support emails within 24 hours." You think you're proactively setting expectations. The buyer hears "only a team of 6" and starts questioning your ability to deliver. The pitch is stronger when you drop the caveat: "We have a product that addresses your biggest pain point, and we're adding 5 new features every month. On top of that, we respond to all customer support emails within 24 hours." If you can handle their needs, who cares if you're 6 people? You don't have to hide your team size — but you don't have to lead with it either.

**Ideas are fuzzy until you're able to put them into words. Then they become concrete and real.** If you want an idea to remain in the background, don't give it air time.

This works in two directions:

🚫 **Don't incept negative ideas.** "I'm not your enemy" makes the other person think you might be. "Concerns" makes a question feel like a problem. "Lowball" plants the idea that the offer might be one. "Hesitations" tells the customer they should have some.

✅ **Do incept positive ideas.** Frame around upside. Take control of the long-term frame. Explain why your idea aligns with their goal. End on a positive note — the last thing they read shapes the decision.

> 🚫 "This is late and the deadline was months ago, and this probably throws a wrench in your planning process… But can you still review my application?"
>
> ✅ "If you have room and want an additional student to maximize the quarter, I'm ready to go. I can send the payment, paperwork, and logistics tonight."

It's not "just" about words. **Words shape how your recipient thinks.** They can build or destroy goodwill, save or cost you money, and determine whether people trust you. And the cost is tiny — being mindful of your messaging takes very little effort, prevents misunderstandings you'd otherwise spend hours addressing, and makes you someone people like and trust. High ROI.

## How to Apply

1. **Read your draft and circle every negative word, frame, or "I'm not trying to X" phrase.**
2. **For each one, ask: did I just plant the idea I'm trying to negate?** "I'm not trying to lowball" plants "lowball." "I won't put your needs second" plants "second." Yes? Cut it.
3. **Replace with a positive frame.** Instead of negating the bad idea, state the good one directly. "I'll put your needs and wants on the same level as my own." "We are in this together."
4. **Drop preemptive caveats about your shortcomings.** You don't have to hide them, but don't lead with them. The customer will form their impression based on what you give them. Give them strengths.
5. **End on a positive word.** Your reader's eyes see the first and last words more than the middle. "At your leisure" is better than "no big deal." "Looking forward to it" is better than "let me know if you change your mind."
6. **When a customer raises an objection, summarize lightly — don't aggrandize.** Show you listened, then reframe. Don't repeat their painful framing back to them with extra emotional weight.
7. **Don't tell them about your hesitations, concerns, or things you "won't" do.** Tell them about your commitments, capabilities, and what you'll deliver.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is closing a customer who said "I'm too busy right now to take this on." The founder wants to nurture the lead.

**Application:**

> 🚫 "Hey, totally understand that now might not be the right time to teach. I'll have our head of partnerships reach out via email to share more."
>
> ✅ "Hey, totally hear you re: timing. I'll have our head of partnerships reach out via email to share more. This way you'll have the info whenever you're ready."

**Result:** The "before" version is illogical (you say you understand it's not the time, then immediately schedule follow-up) AND it incepts the customer's "no." The "after" treats timing as soft, not as a closed door.

---

**Situation:** A hiring manager is delivering an offer they know is fair but the candidate might find low.

**Application:**

> 🚫 "I'm not trying to lowball your offer or get you for cheap. The way we came up with the offer was by looking at Carta comp data for…"
>
> ✅ "The way we came up with the offer was by looking at Carta comp data for…"

**Result:** The "before" plants two kill-shot words — "lowball" and "cheap." The candidate's mind is now hunting for evidence the offer is one. The "after" leads with credibility (Carta data) and lets the candidate evaluate without the loaded vocabulary.

---

**Situation:** A leader is giving a direct report hard news.

**Application:**

> 🚫 "I don't say this to discourage you and be a jerk, I'm trying to empower you by sharing that…"
>
> ✅ "I'm sharing this because [sell why the idea benefits your direct report]"

**Result:** "Discourage" and "jerk" both plant ideas the report didn't have until you said them. Now they're filtering the rest of the conversation through that lens. The "after" opens with rationale and benefit.

---

**Situation:** A customer follows up after a customer churn risk has been raised.

**Application:**

> 🚫 "Hi Tom, thanks for being transparent with your concerns."
>
> ✅ "Hi Tom, thanks for sharing what's been on your mind."

**Result:** "Concerns" sounds serious and concrete. "Transparent" usually prefaces bad news. Both make the issue feel heavier than it needs to. The "after" treats the conversation as collaborative information-sharing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Negate negative words by saying "not."
**Why:** The brain doesn't process "not" as efficiently as the noun it's attached to. "I'm not a crook" is heard as "crook." "Don't think of an elephant" makes you think of an elephant. Remove the word entirely instead of negating it.

**Don't:** Preempt objections by naming them.
**Why:** You think you're being honest and proactive. You're actually planting the objection. If your prospect didn't have the concern, you've just given it to them. If they did have it, you've made it worse by labeling it. Address concerns when raised — don't summon them.

**Don't:** Repeat the customer's objection back with emotional intensity.
**Why:** "You might have spent nights, weekends, all your free time on this course, and to only have so few students must have felt surprising, disappointing, demoralizing, and like a complete waste of time…" is a vivid painting of the objection. The customer now feels it more strongly than they did before you spoke. Acknowledge briefly, then reframe.

**Don't:** Apologize for things that aren't actually wrong.
**Why:** "Unfortunately" and "I'm sorry" subconsciously accept blame. If you offer two livestream dates and the customer wants others, you don't need to say "unfortunately." You're providing a service. State the offer in the affirmative.

**Don't:** End your message with the loaded word.
**Why:** Last word lingers. "Let me know if you want to make changes" plants the idea of changes. "Looking forward to launching this together" plants alignment. End where you want their thoughts to settle.

**Don't:** Use inception to manipulate.
**Why:** This works because words actually shape thought — which means the ethical bar is real. Use it to communicate clearly and accurately on behalf of work the customer/recipient genuinely benefits from. Use it to manipulate, and you become the kind of operator nobody trusts twice.
