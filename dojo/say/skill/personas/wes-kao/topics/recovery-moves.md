# Recovery Moves: In-the-Moment Communication

## When to Use
- A meeting is mid-flight and going sideways. Someone derailed the agenda. Someone's drilling you on something you weren't prepared for. The conversation is now offense vs. defense and you're losing.
- You have to walk into a conversation and deliver bad news — especially bad news that wasn't your fault — and you don't want a negative halo to stick to you.
- You messed up. Something on your watch went wrong. You're not sure if you should apologize or just power through.
- The dynamic of a conversation has shifted from problem-solving into something more emotional, and you can feel both of you running on a hamster wheel — lots of activity, no progress.

## Fails When
- **You use the "buy time" move to never come back.** "Let me think about this and get back to you" is a power move *if* you actually come back better-prepared. It's a credibility tax if you don't.
- **You substitute scripts for substance.** These are scaffolding for the moment. They don't excuse you from doing the actual work behind the scenes.
- **The relationship is structurally broken.** If your manager is acting in bad faith or the customer has already decided to leave, no recovery move will save the conversation — you're in a different problem.

## Core Concept

Hard conversations don't reward fluency. They reward composure. The skill is not having the perfect spontaneous answer — it's having a small set of grounded, reusable moves that **buy you time, reset the frame, or transfer agency** in the moment, so that you can do the actual thinking and follow-up offline.

> 🚫 Spinning your wheels trying to think of an answer on the spot.
>
> ✅ Knowing what to say when you're surprised, so you can be present and elegant about it.

These moves aren't about being slick. They're about making sure your **perceived level of competence reflects your actual level of competence.** When you're caught off guard, your panic is louder than your thinking. The moves below let your thinking catch up.

There are four canonical situations. For each, there's a routing move:

1. **Caught off guard** → buy time + suggest a next step.
2. **Conversation has turned into offense vs. defense** → break the frame, do a reset.
3. **Meeting is being derailed** → acknowledge, redirect, mention a third party, or surface the cost of going off-track.
4. **You messed up or need to deliver bad news** → apologize when warranted, deliver bad news without inviting blame.

The unifying principle across all four: **don't keep playing the game you're losing.** Recognize the shape of what's happening, name it, and transfer the conversation onto better ground.

## How to Apply

### Move 1: Caught off guard? Buy time gracefully.

The hardest moment: someone asks you something specific, in front of people, that you don't have a great answer to. The instinct is to scramble. The scramble looks bad — and the more you answer, the more questions they ask, because your half-answers create new openings.

The elegant move is to acknowledge, take ownership, and propose a next step.

**Scripts:**

> *"I'm coming out of another meeting, so I'll need some time to context switch and make sure I give this the attention it deserves. Let's set up a meeting for later this week so we can carve out time and go deep on this issue. How does that sound?"*

> *"This sounds important and I want to make the best use of everyone's time. Let's set up a time later this week and go deep on this. I'll present the updates at our next meeting."*

These work because they accomplish three things:

1. **The person feels seen and heard** — you're acknowledging the issue is important.
2. **You take responsibility** — you're not hiding, squirming, or making excuses. You're saying *I'll do my part offline.*
3. **You suggest a next step** — it's harder to push back when you've offered an alternative.

The result: you appear reasonable and thoughtful — because you *are* being reasonable and thoughtful. You've transferred the conversation from "answer right now or look bad" to "let me come prepared and answer well."

The catch: **you have to actually come back.** The move is a credibility builder if you follow through. It's a credibility-killer if you don't.

### Move 2: Conversation gone offense-vs-defense? Break the frame.

There's a specific dynamic that derails high-stakes conversations: one person is asking aggressive questions because they're frustrated, and the other person is reactively answering each one in good faith. **You're playing different games.** Person A is venting through interrogation. Person B thinks "if I just answer the questions, they'll be satisfied." They never are.

> *"More importantly, it means both parties are running on a hamster wheel: there's lots of activity, but no forward progress."*

The move is to **break the frame and reset the power dynamics.** Both people should come into the conversation as equals. A reset doesn't call anyone out — it pauses, addresses the underlying emotion, and offers a path forward. It's based on mutual respect and lets everyone save face.

**Reset scripts:**

> *"Let me step back for a second. I want to make sure we're solving the right thing. What I'm hearing is that you're concerned about [X] — is that right? If so, here's how I think about it: [reframe]. I'd love to hear what you'd want to see happen."*

> *"Hmm. Let's take a step back. I think we might be going down a rabbit hole. The big picture I want us to land on is [Y]. Can we agree on that, then come back to the details?"*

The "let's step back" / "let's take a step back" phrasing makes you sound like the person watching the forest. It signals to others in the room that you're tracking the goal — not just defending yourself.

When to do a reset: **whenever you feel like incremental gains aren't enough, and when you sense you and the other person are having two different conversations.** That's the diagnostic. If you keep answering and they keep escalating, it's not a question-answering problem. It's a frame problem.

### Move 3: Meeting being derailed? Three scripts.

Bold ideas pull groups of people in different directions. When you have more than one person in a room, the conversation can get hijacked. Most "how to control a meeting" advice is too abrasive — for collaborative leaders, you need scripts you can realistically picture saying.

**Script A — Tangent:** *"That's a good point. It's a bit of a different topic though, so let's park that for now. To follow up with what Jane said earlier, I think we should…"*

When to use: someone goes on a tangent.
Why it works: "That's a good point… let's park that" makes the person feel acknowledged. Then you redirect *immediately* — timing matters, you don't want to give people a chance to object. Naming Jane uses subtle peer pressure; you're showing the room is focused on the main point.

**Script B — In the weeds:** *"Hmm. Let's take a step back for a second."*

When to use: people get bogged down in nitty-gritty details.
Why it works: It makes you sound like you see the forest *and* the trees. Watching out for the team. Eyes on the prize.

**Script C — Mutual accountability:** *"Love that! I want to make sure we cover X because of the Y deadline though. If we don't figure this out, we'll have to carve out more time to meet again to discuss this. Are you cool if we continue for now?"*

When to use: when you want others to be mutually accountable for keeping the meeting on track.
Why it works: explaining with "because" is psychologically compliant — but the deeper move is **making the cost of the tangent visible.** *"By mentioning the cost of going on a tangent, you remind them that the tangent is not free."* The time always cost something; now the cost is explicit. Most people will gladly stick to the topic if it means ending on time.

### Move 4: Delivering bad news that wasn't your fault.

The trap: you feel guilty (even when it's not your fault), so you over-explain, use heavy words, and accidentally accept blame. Your audience absorbs the negative halo and starts associating the bad news with *you*.

Five rules for delivering bad news cleanly:

**1. Avoid negative words — especially "however."** *"However was great for high school essays. There's rarely a need for it in your toolkit now. It makes everything sound heavier and more dramatic than it needs to be. The goal here is to minimize drama. If you really need to, use 'but.'"*

**2. Don't give too many details — save some information if they ask.** When you feel guilty, you want to explain everything, secretly hoping the explanation will absolve you. Don't. It complicates the message, and recipients can't tell where the facts end and your narrative begins. Respect them enough to give the key info; if they have questions, they'll ask.

**3. Don't accidentally accept blame.** There's a difference between empathizing (*"I acknowledge this is frustrating"*) and accepting blame (*"I should have done X and Y"*). Scan your phrasing: *"Might they think I'm accidentally accepting blame because of my word choice?"* When upset, people project anger as a way to cope. Accepting blame legitimizes that projection — even if it was misplaced.

**4. Get to the point quickly.** Adding too much preface makes things sound *worse*. The tension builds and they imagine the worst possible thing. Then when you finally deliver the news, they've already spent five minutes catastrophizing. Preface as little as you can.

**5. Remind them of their own agency.** When people get bad news, they want to blame someone — don't let it be you. Remind them of the role they played. *"You both knew the result might not work. You're on the same team and working toward the same goal. You're going out of your way to help them."*

Wrap it softly. Don't say *"we both knew this might not work, don't act so surprised."* Try:

> *"Last month, we talked about how we had a one in 10 chance of winning this bid. We decided to pursue it given the high payoff. At this point, if we choose to continue in the process, we could submit another proposal. I know it takes a lot of effort on your part, so it might or might not be worth the time. I can discuss with my team, too. Thanks for working on this together. Let's circle back mid next week — let me know what you prefer to do."*

The move: you delivered the news cleanly, didn't catastrophize, didn't take blame, reminded them they're part of the decision, and proposed a next step.

### Move 5: When you should actually apologize.

A lot of people were taught "never apologize at work." Wes's reframe: that advice is outdated, and the reverse is usually right.

> *"Businesses are run by people. And people — even your bosses — want to feel seen, heard, and recognized. We're not machines."*

When you cause your friend an inconvenience, you acknowledge it because that's what decent people do. Why not extend the same courtesy to people you work with?

The reframe: it's not "show remorse OR explain how you'll prevent this." It's **AND.**

> 🚫 "I will explain how we'll prevent this in the future." (Stoic, machine-like.)
>
> 🚫 "I'm so sorry I'm so sorry I'm so sorry." (Groveling, no path forward.)
>
> ✅ *"I will show remorse AND I will share how we'll prevent this in the future."*

Both. Show you care, then show you've thought about it. The test: when something goes wrong on your watch, your manager wants to know two things — *did you feel bad about this?* and *what's going to be different next time?* If you only answer the second, it lands as cold and entitled. If you only answer the first, it lands as performative.

A script Wes has actually used (as a manager, to a direct report who was scared to apologize):

> *"This was pretty bad and I know this isn't reflective of who you are or your quality of work. I don't know if you feel bad about this, but if you do, it's okay to say sorry. I know we were all taught not to apologize at work. But when you apologize and share that you feel bad, it makes me feel like you care enough to feel bad. And caring counts for a lot."*

The takeaway: don't grovel, but don't suppress the human response either. *"Caring counts for a lot."*

## Examples

**Situation:** You're in a quarterly review. Your CEO unexpectedly pivots to a metric you weren't tracking. The room is watching.

**Application:** *"That's a fair question and I want to make sure I give you a real answer instead of guessing. I'm coming out of another meeting and don't have those numbers in front of me. Let me pull the data this afternoon and send a 1-pager by EOD — would that work? In the meantime, the headline I can speak to is [the thing you do know]."*

**Result:** You've acknowledged, taken ownership, proposed a next step, and given them something concrete in the moment. You haven't squirmed. You haven't faked an answer that will be embarrassing later.

---

**Situation:** A peer is hammering you in a planning meeting with rapid-fire questions about a launch you led. The room is starting to feel like a courtroom.

**Application:** *"Let me step back for a second. I want to make sure we're solving the right thing. It sounds like the underlying concern is [X] — is that right? Because if so, here's the frame I'd want us to look at this through: [reframe]. The questions you're asking are valid, and I'd love to work through them — but I want to make sure we're aligned on the bigger picture first."*

**Result:** You've named the dynamic without calling them out. You've offered a frame. You've moved the conversation off the hamster wheel.

---

**Situation:** You have to tell a partner organization that the joint proposal lost the bid.

**Application (negative-halo version):** *"Hi — unfortunately I have some bad news. We didn't win. I'm so sorry, I know you put a lot of work into this. I should have probably set better expectations earlier on. The client said another firm came in lower. Let me know how you want to handle it."*

This message accidentally accepts blame ("I should have set better expectations") and over-explains.

**Application (Wes's way):** *"Quick update — the client went with a different firm; they came in at a lower price point. When we kicked this off, we'd talked about the bid being competitive given the field — and the team you put together still produced our strongest proposal yet. A few options from here: (1) re-bid at a different price point, (2) wait for the next opportunity from this client, (3) take what we built and pitch a similar Fortune 1000. I lean toward (3). Let me know what you'd want to do — happy to discuss this week."*

**Result:** Bad news delivered cleanly. No accidental blame acceptance. Reminded them of the original frame (we both knew it was competitive). Proposed concrete next steps. Forward-looking.

---

**Situation:** You're a junior person and you sent an external email with a typo in the client's name. The team noticed. Your manager pinged you.

**Application:** *"You're right — that was sloppy and I'm sorry. I'm sending a corrected version now with a brief apology to [client]. To prevent this going forward, I'm adding a 'read aloud' check before any external send and copying [colleague] on the first batch this quarter. Want to flag anything else I'm missing?"*

**Result:** Remorse + plan. Concrete prevention. Invitation for further input. You don't grovel; you also don't pretend it didn't happen.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "let me get back to you" as a permanent escape hatch.
**Why:** The move only builds credibility if you actually come back, prepared, in the timeframe you committed to. Otherwise you're just deferring credibility damage by 48 hours.

**Don't:** Try to do a "reset" by literally announcing "I'm doing a reset on this conversation."
**Why:** It's heavy-handed and makes the other person feel called out. The point is to do it gracefully — *"let me step back for a second"* — without naming the maneuver.

**Don't:** Use "however" when delivering bad news.
**Why:** It's heavier than "but" and signals "the bad part is coming." Adds drama you don't want. Cut it.

**Don't:** Over-explain when you feel guilty.
**Why:** Excessive explanation is selfish — you're trying to make yourself feel better, not serve the recipient. Respect them enough to give them the information they need to make a smart decision. They'll ask if they want more.

**Don't:** Accept blame for things that aren't yours.
**Why:** Empathy ≠ accepting blame. *"I acknowledge this is frustrating"* is empathy. *"I should have done X but didn't"* is blame. Don't legitimize a frustrated party's projection — it tarnishes your credibility and erodes trust in working relationships.

**Don't:** Apologize without a plan, *or* deliver a plan without acknowledging the human cost.
**Why:** Either alone falls flat. The pairing is what works: AND, not OR. Care and competence in the same breath.

**Don't:** Confuse "regaining control of the meeting" with steamrolling.
**Why:** *"Most recommendations are too abrasive to say to people you actually want to continue working with."* The collaborative scripts work because they make the other person feel acknowledged before redirecting. Skip the acknowledgment and you sound like a jerk — and they remember.

**Don't:** Treat these as scripts you read off a card.
**Why:** The phrasing matters less than the underlying move. Internalize the shape — *acknowledge, take ownership, suggest a next step* — and the words come out in your voice.
