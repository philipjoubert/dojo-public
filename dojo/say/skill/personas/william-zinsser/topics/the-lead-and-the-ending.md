# The Lead and the Ending

## When to Use

- A founder shows you a board update where the actual news is in paragraph four.
- A cold outbound email opens with "Hope you're doing well" and three sentences about who the founder is before getting to the ask.
- A launch post opens with "Today, we are excited to announce..." — five words spent before any information.
- A pitch deck opens with the founder's biography rather than the company's proposition.
- A blog post ends with "In conclusion, we believe..." and then re-states what was already said.
- A founder is writing a fundraising email and asks: "What's a good opener?"
- A draft has a strong middle and forgettable bookends — the parts the reader actually sees.

## Fails When

- **The genre demands a slow opener.** A long-form magazine essay, a memoir, a slow-burn investigative piece — these can earn the right to take a paragraph before the lead bites. Most founder writing cannot.
- **You write a clever lead instead of a useful one.** "What if I told you that..." Cleverness ages quickly and signals you are performing. The right lead is fresh, not coy.
- **You truncate a piece that needs more setup.** Some technical posts, some memoir essays, some serious investigative writing genuinely need 200 words to set up the question. Don't strip context from a piece that lives by its context.
- **The first sentence is mandated.** A court filing or earnings release has its own conventions. Don't fight them.

## Core Concept

*The most important sentence in any article is the first one. If it doesn't induce the reader to proceed to the second sentence, your article is dead. And if the second sentence doesn't induce him to continue to the third sentence, it's equally dead. Of such a progression of sentences, each tugging the reader forward until he is hooked, a writer constructs that fateful unit, the "lead."*

*— On Writing Well, "The Lead and the Ending"*

For a founder, the lead is the most consequential paragraph they ever write. The investor scanning the cold email will read the first sentence and either reply or delete. The board member opening the update on a phone in an Uber will read the first paragraph and either keep going or skim. The candidate considering the role will read the founder's blog post and decide in 15 seconds whether this is a person they want to work for. The lead is doing all of this work, and most founders give it the least attention.

The lead has two jobs. The first is to get the reader to the next sentence. *Therefore your lead must capture the reader immediately and force him to keep reading. It must cajole him with freshness, or novelty, or paradox, or humor, or surprise, or with an unusual idea, or an interesting fact, or a question. Anything will do, as long as it nudges his curiosity and tugs at his sleeve.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 11*) Notice what is not on this list: announcing yourself, expressing gratitude, summarising what you are about to say, or affirming that you are excited about something. None of these tug a sleeve. They are the writing equivalent of a presenter clearing their throat.

The second job is to get real work done. *Next the lead must do some real work. It must provide hard details that tell the reader why the piece was written and why he ought to read it. But don't dwell on the reason. Coax the reader a little more; keep him inquisitive.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 11*) "I'm writing because I have an unusual offer to fund Series A companies in your stage" — that is a lead doing real work. "I hope this email finds you well" — that is a lead doing no work and using up the only attention you had.

The cardinal sin of founder writing is the throat-clearing opening. It is so universal that founders no longer recognise it as a problem. *It's amazing how often an editor can throw away the first three or four paragraphs of an article, or even the first few pages, and start with the paragraph where the writer begins to sound like himself or herself. Not only are those first paragraphs impersonal and ornate; they don't say anything — they are a self-conscious attempt at a fancy prologue.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*) Apply this to your last five board updates. The actual lead — the moment the founder began saying something specific — almost always lives somewhere on page two.

The fix is mechanical. Read the draft. Find the first sentence that is doing real work — that says something a reader could not have written themselves, that commits to a fact, that frames a stake. That is your real lead. Cut everything above it. Most documents survive the cut. Most readers prefer the cut version.

The same principle applies in reverse to the ending. Founders, trained by school essays, want to end with a summary. *We can still visualize the outline, with its Roman numerals, which staked out the road we would faithfully trudge, and its subnumerals denoting lesser paths down which we would briefly poke. But we always promised to get back to III and summarize our journey. That's all right for elementary and high school students uncertain of their ground. But if you're going to write good nonfiction you must wriggle out of III's dread grip. You'll know you have arrived at III when you see emerging on your screen a sentence that begins, "In sum, it can be noted that…"* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 11*) When you find yourself writing a closing paragraph that re-states what you've already said, you have ended already and are now padding.

The right ending arrives slightly before the reader expects it. *The perfect ending should take your readers slightly by surprise and yet seem exactly right. They didn't expect the article to end so soon, or so abruptly, or to say what it said. But they know it when they see it.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 11*) For a founder, this means: when you have made the point, stop. Resist the temptation to soften, to summarise, to "round it out." Trust the reader to retain what you said five paragraphs ago without you re-staging it.

A specific founder discipline: the last sentence is the one the reader carries out of the room. Choose it deliberately. *You should give as much thought to choosing your last sentence as you did to your first.* The last sentence of a board update is the one the board member will say to their spouse at dinner when asked how the meeting went. The last sentence of the fundraising email is the one the investor will quote when forwarding to a partner. The last sentence of the all-hands email is the one the team will internalise as the takeaway. Founders often spend an hour on the lead and forty seconds on the close. Reverse the ratio at least once.

The simplest rule, often the right one: *when you're ready to stop, stop.* The professional writer's instinct is to look for the nearest exit once the work is done. The amateur's instinct is to keep talking. A board update that has made its point in eight paragraphs should be eight paragraphs long, not twelve.

## How to Apply

1. **Find your real lead by deletion.** Read the draft. Identify the first sentence that does real work. Cut everything above it. About 70% of founder drafts have a buried lead two to four paragraphs in. The remaining 30% have a buried lead in the second half of the first paragraph; cut to that sentence.

2. **Test the lead alone.** Read the first sentence in isolation. Would you, as a stranger, read the second? If not, rewrite. The lead should produce a small forward push of attention — curiosity, or surprise, or a stake — sufficient to carry the reader into the next sentence.

3. **Front-load the news.** Investors, board members, and busy employees read the lead and decide whether to read the rest. Put the most consequential fact in the first or second sentence. "We missed Q3." "I'm leaving as CEO." "We just closed Series B." "We're laying off 15% of the team." Do not bury this. Do not dress it up. State it.

4. **Refuse the throat-clearing opener.** "I hope this finds you well." "I wanted to take a moment to share..." "It's been a while since our last update, but..." Cut every variant. The reader does not need warming up. They are already reading; you have their attention; do not waste it on civilities they will skim past.

5. **Make every paragraph in the lead amplify the one above it.** The lead is not one sentence; it is a small structure that tightens around the reader. *Every paragraph should amplify the one that preceded it. Give more thought to adding solid detail and less to entertaining the reader.* Each paragraph adds a fact, a stake, or a complication.

6. **Find a snapper at the end of each paragraph.** *Take special care with the last sentence of each paragraph — it's the crucial springboard to the next paragraph. Try to give that sentence an extra twist of humor or surprise, like the periodic "snapper" in the routine of a stand-up comic. Make the reader smile and you've got him for at least one more paragraph.* Found in many great essays; underused in founder writing. The last sentence of each paragraph should earn the next one.

7. **End where the work ends.** Do not summarise. Do not say "in conclusion." Do not "round it out." When the last useful thing has been said, stop. If the ending feels too abrupt, sit with the discomfort for a day. By tomorrow you will have stopped wanting to add the closing flourish.

8. **Choose the last sentence with care.** Often the last sentence is a quotation, a specific image, a question, or a small surprise that crystallises what came before. *Surprise is the most refreshing element in nonfiction writing. If something surprises you it will also surprise — and delight — the people you are writing for, especially as you conclude your story and send them on their way.* Read the last sentence aloud. If it doesn't ring, rewrite.

9. **Consider bringing the piece full circle.** A common move: end with an echo of an image, fact, or phrase from the lead. *Something I often do in my writing is to bring the story full circle — to strike at the end an echo of a note that was sounded at the beginning. It gratifies my sense of symmetry, and it also pleases the reader, completing with its resonance the journey we set out on together.* Useful for founder essays, anniversary letters, and any piece where the reader needs to feel the arc.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder writes a Series B fundraising email. The current opening: "Hi Sarah, I hope this email finds you well. I'm reaching out because we're starting to think about our Series B and I wanted to give you the chance to consider participating. As you may recall, we last spoke six months ago when we were preparing for our Series A, which we ultimately led with [Fund X]. A lot has happened since then..."

**Application:** Five sentences in and there is no information. Sarah, who gets a hundred of these emails, has already moved on. Find the real lead. Buried somewhere on page two: "We grew from $4M to $14M in the last twelve months and we're raising $30M in the next four weeks." That is the email. Open with it. Add one supporting sentence. Make the ask. Stop.

**Result:** "Hi Sarah — short version: we tripled to $14M ARR this year, retention is 130%, and we're raising a $30M Series B in the next four weeks. You passed at Series A; the company looks different now. Worth a 30-min call?" The investor opens this and either replies or doesn't, in seconds. The original email died at "I hope this finds you well."

**Situation:** A founder writes a quarterly board update. Current opening paragraph: "Q3 was a quarter of significant developments and meaningful progress for the company. As we reflect on the past three months, we are proud of what the team has accomplished and grateful for your continued support. Across our key strategic priorities, we made notable strides while also confronting several important challenges that we'll discuss in the sections below."

**Application:** This paragraph contains zero information. Every adjective ("significant," "meaningful," "notable") is a placeholder. The board member reading this on a Sunday night already knows you are proud and grateful — they assume that. They want to know what happened. The real lead is on page three: "We hit $3.8M against a $4.2M plan, missed mainly because two enterprise deals slipped to Q4. Net new ARR was up 40% YoY despite the miss; gross margin held at 78%."

**Result:** Open with the actual numbers. The whole document compresses by 30% because the redundant context paragraphs become unnecessary. The board reads the update in five minutes instead of pretending to read it for fifteen. The questions that come back are the substantive ones, not "wait, what was the actual ARR number?"

**Situation:** A founder writes a launch blog post. Current opening: "Today, we are thrilled to announce a major milestone in our journey to revolutionize how teams collaborate. After two years of relentless work and customer obsession, we are launching [Product], the next evolution of our platform."

**Application:** "Thrilled," "major milestone," "journey," "revolutionize," "relentless work," "customer obsession," "next evolution" — every word is corporate boilerplate. None describe the actual product. None hook the reader. The real lead, twenty paragraphs in: "When we started, we had one customer who was using six different tools to plan a quarter. After today, that's one tool." That sentence hooks. It promises specificity and stakes.

**Result:** "Two years ago, our first customer was using six tools to plan a quarter. Today, we're launching the one tool that replaces all six. Here's what's in it." Three sentences. The reader is now committed to paragraph two. The original opening would have lost half of them by sentence two.

**Situation:** A founder writes the all-hands email after a layoff. Their draft ends with: "I want to close by saying that I have full confidence in the strength and resilience of this team. While today is hard, I believe our best days are ahead. Together, we will emerge stronger and more focused than ever. Thank you for your continued dedication to our mission."

**Application:** Every closing sentence is a cliché. The reader does not believe in "best days ahead" right after losing colleagues; they read this as performative. Go back to what is actually true. The strongest possible last paragraph: "I'm sorry. To the people leaving — I will write each of you personally tomorrow. To the people staying — there will be an all-hands at 4pm where I'll answer any question you ask. I'll see you there."

**Result:** The team registers the apology and the commitment to the 4pm meeting, both of which are concrete and verifiable. The original ending would have been forwarded to The Verge under the headline "Founder closes layoff email with motivational rhetoric." The rewrite cannot be ridiculed because it does not perform — it commits.

**Situation:** A founder writes a long essay on the company's tenth anniversary. The lead currently runs eight paragraphs of company history before reaching the actual subject — what the founder has learned about the relationship between founders and customers.

**Application:** The history is fine material; it is not the lead. Find the moment the founder said something only she could have said: "Six years ago, a customer told me 'I trust your product more than I trust my own engineers.' I have been thinking about that sentence ever since." That is the lead. The company history can come later, woven through the essay as illustration of the central thesis.

**Result:** The reader, hooked by the unusual claim, reads on. The history, when it arrives, is in service of an idea rather than blocking access to one. The piece gets shared because it is now an essay rather than a corporate retrospective.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the trick lead — the future archaeologist, the visitor from Mars, the "what do X, Y, and Z have in common?"
**Why:** *I'm tired of him already and he's not even here. I'm tired of the cute event that just happened to happen "one day not long ago" or on a conveniently recent Saturday afternoon.* These leads were tired in 1976. They are still tired. The founder using them is signalling that they have been reading too much business writing and not enough good writing.

**Don't:** Use a question as the lead unless you actually answer it.
**Why:** "What is the future of work?" "Why is enterprise software so painful?" Questions as leads almost always promise more than the piece delivers. They also place the reader in a posture of being asked rather than told. Most of the time, a declarative sentence does better work than the rhetorical question that "frames" it.

**Don't:** Confuse a strong lead with a long lead.
**Why:** Some founders, having heard the lead is important, write a 400-word opener crammed with hooks, stakes, and dramatic build. The lead is the engine, not the cargo. Two clean sentences usually beat eight ornate ones.

**Don't:** Recapitulate at the end.
**Why:** "In conclusion, as I have outlined above..." If you have to remind the reader what you said, the body of the piece failed. The closing should resolve, surprise, or commit — not summarise.

**Don't:** Tack on a sentimental closing because the body was technical.
**Why:** Founders writing operational updates often feel the document is "too cold" and graft on a closing paragraph of warmth. The graft is visible. If the body deserves warmth, weave it in throughout. If it doesn't, end where the work ends.

**Don't:** Mistake "stop" for "snap shut."
**Why:** Ending well is not ending abruptly. The right ending feels inevitable, even if the reader didn't see it coming. Endings that snap shut leave the reader stranded; endings that arrive feel earned.

**Don't:** Apologise for the length in the closing.
**Why:** "Thanks for reading this far!" "Sorry for the long email!" Either the piece earned its length or it did not. Apologising for it announces to the reader that you suspect it didn't, which makes them retroactively suspect it didn't.
