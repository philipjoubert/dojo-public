# Unity and Finding Your Subject

## When to Use

- A founder shows you a draft that has three good ideas in it and is therefore hard to follow.
- A founder is writing a strategy memo and cannot reduce the company's plan to one sentence.
- A pitch deck has eighteen "core" things the company does and no spine.
- A board update mixes financial commentary, product updates, hiring news, and competitive positioning with no organising principle.
- A founder is writing a "definitive" document — the manifesto, the whitepaper, the long-form launch post — and is paralysed because every fact seems too important to leave out.
- A founder asks "what should this piece really be about?" — that is the right question and they should be helped to answer it.
- A draft slips between tones — corporate paragraph, then folksy, then philosophical — without committing to any of them.

## Fails When

- **The genre is a digest.** A weekly all-hands recap or an investor monthly is supposed to cover several threads. Forcing artificial unity onto material that is genuinely diverse produces a worse document.
- **You're in early discovery.** When a founder is still figuring out what the company is about, premature unity is fake. Let the draft sprawl. You can only find the unifying thread by writing past it.
- **The form is dictated externally.** A 10-K has its sections. A YC application has its prompts. A regulatory filing has its structure. Don't pretend you have the latitude you don't.
- **You enforce unity at the cost of important nuance.** Reducing a complicated quarter to one sentence is helpful for the lead. Insisting the entire memo say only one thing erases information the reader needs.

## Core Concept

Most nonfiction is wrecked at the structural level, not the sentence level. The sentences are clean. The grammar is fine. The piece still does not work because the writer never decided what it was about. They had material they wanted to use; they used it. They had several good points to make; they made all of them. They had multiple tones available; they tried each one. The result is the document that every founder has produced and every reader has abandoned: a piece of writing that is too long to follow and too thin to remember.

*Unity is the anchor of good writing. So, first, get your unities straight. Unity not only keeps the reader from straggling off in all directions; it satisfies your readers' subconscious need for order and reassures them that all is well at the helm.*

*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*

The founder's version of this problem is the strategy memo that wants to be three different memos, the mission statement that tries to please six audiences, the board letter that careens between bragging and apologising, the launch post that explains the product, the company history, the team, and the market in twelve hundred words. None of these documents are bad because their parts are bad. They are bad because they have no spine. The reader, halfway through, no longer knows what the writer is asking them to think.

Unity is a stack of decisions made before you start writing. *Therefore ask yourself some basic questions before you start. For example: "In what capacity am I going to address the reader?" "What pronoun and tense am I going to use?" "What style?" "What attitude am I going to take toward the material?" "How much do I want to cover?" "What one point do I want to make?"* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*) The last two questions are the founder's most-skipped ones. Most founders, faced with a writing task, decide on tone almost by accident — whatever they happen to be feeling that morning — and never decide what one point the piece is supposed to make. So the piece makes none.

The hardest decision is reduction. *Most nonfiction writers have a definitiveness complex. They feel that they are under some obligation — to the subject, to their honor, to the gods of writing — to make their article the last word. It's a commendable impulse, but there is no last word. Nobody can write a book or an article "about" something. Tolstoy couldn't write a book about war and peace, or Melville a book about whaling. They made certain reductive decisions about time and place and about individual characters in that time and place — one man pursuing one whale. Every writing project must be reduced before you start to write.* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*) This is the piece of advice founders most need and most resist. They have a $30M Series B to raise, and they want the deck to be about the whole company. They are writing the manifesto, and they want it to say everything. The discipline is the opposite: pick the one whale.

The reduction has a specific test. *Every successful piece of nonfiction should leave the reader with one provocative thought that he or she didn't have before. Not two thoughts, or five — just one. So decide what single point you want to leave in the reader's mind. It will not only give you a better idea of what route you should follow and what destination you hope to reach; it will affect your decision about tone and attitude.* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*) For a founder, this means: at the end of this board update, what is the one thought I want every board member walking out of the room with? At the end of this memo, what is the one new fact lodged in the reader's head? If you cannot name it, the document does not yet have unity. It has material.

Tone is the second axis of unity. *Another choice is unity of mood. You might want to talk to the reader in the casual voice that* The New Yorker *has strenuously refined. Or you might want to approach the reader with a certain formality to describe a serious event or to present a set of important facts. Both tones are acceptable. In fact,* any *tone is acceptable. But don't mix two or three.* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*) The classic founder failure is the all-hands email that opens with a joke, slides into a sober paragraph about a lost deal, then closes with motivational rhetoric. Each section, in isolation, is fine. Together, they signal a writer who has not decided how seriously to take the moment. Readers can tell.

There is one more move. The unity you choose at the start may not be the unity the piece wants. *Now it often happens that you'll make these prior decisions and then discover that they weren't the right ones. The material begins to lead you in an unexpected direction, where you are more comfortable writing in a different tone. That's normal — the act of writing generates some cluster of thoughts or memories that you didn't anticipate. Don't fight such a current if it feels right. Trust your material if it's taking you into terrain you didn't intend to enter but where the vibrations are good. Adjust your style accordingly and proceed to whatever destination you reach. Don't become the prisoner of a preconceived plan.* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*) When this happens, you do not abandon unity. You go back to the beginning and rewrite the early section so that the piece is unified around the new direction. Unity is not the plan you started with. It is the consistency the finished piece holds.

For founders specifically, the test of "what is this really about" usually surfaces a deeper question. The strategy memo that won't reduce is usually a strategy that hasn't been decided. The mission statement that pleases six audiences is usually a company that hasn't chosen one. The board update that wants to explain everything is usually a CEO who hasn't yet committed to a story about the quarter. Writing-level unity is downstream of decision-level unity. When you cannot reduce the piece, the right move is sometimes not to fix the writing — it is to stop writing and finish thinking.

## How to Apply

1. **Reduce the piece to a single sentence before you start.** Not the topic. The point. "We are growing fast but burning fast and need to choose between the two by Q3." "Enterprise has become our real business and consumer is now an experiment." "I am leaving as CEO." If you cannot write that sentence, you are not ready to write the piece — you are ready to think more.

2. **Pick a tone and stay in it.** Decide whether this is a sober document, a confident one, an analytical one, a personal one, or a celebratory one. Then make every sentence in the piece consistent with that choice. If a sentence belongs to a different tone, it does not belong in this piece.

3. **Pick a pronoun and stay in it.** "I." "We." "The company." Each implies a different posture. Mixing them is the surest sign of a writer who is hiding from one of them. Consistency is felt by the reader as control; switching is felt as evasion.

4. **Pick a tense and stay in it.** Most founder writing should be in the present tense for current state and the simple past for what happened. Switching ("we have been seeing... we then experienced... we are observing...") is one of the small clutter-tells of corporate prose. Pick one and let the verbs do less work.

5. **Pick the corner of the subject. Then narrow further.** A piece about "growth" — too big. About "growth in Q3" — still too big. About "the two enterprise deals that defined Q3" — that is a piece. The constraint forces depth. *Decide what corner of your subject you're going to bite off, and be content to cover it well and stop.* (*— On Writing Well, "Unity"*)

6. **Cut the material that doesn't serve the spine.** *Fondness for material you've gone to a lot of trouble to gather isn't a good enough reason to include it if it's not central to the story you've chosen to tell. Self-discipline bordering on masochism is required.* (*— On Writing Well, "A Writer's Decisions"*) The founder has run a survey, has the customer-interview transcripts, has the competitor analysis. None of it earns its place by virtue of having been gathered. It earns its place by serving the one point the piece is making. *Readers should always feel that you know more about your subject than you've put in writing.*

7. **Write the lead last, or rewrite it twice.** The opening usually changes once you've discovered what the piece is actually about. Founders typically write a generic opener, then write the piece, then never go back. Go back. The first paragraph should be the one that announces the spine. If it doesn't, rewrite it.

8. **Test by asking: what one thought will the reader leave with?** Imagine the board member walking to the parking lot. The investor closing the email. The employee finishing the all-hands. What single sentence is in their head? If it is the sentence you wanted, the piece is unified. If it is something else — or nothing — go back.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is writing the Series B narrative deck. The team has assembled material across product, market, growth, hiring, technology, partnerships, and competition. The current draft has 47 slides and the founder cannot say in one sentence what the company is.

**Application:** The fix is upstream of the deck. Sit with the founder and ask: what is the one sentence an investor should walk out with? After two hours, the founder lands on: "We are the tax-compliance system for global remote teams, and we are the only product that handles all 50 US states and the 30 countries our customers actually employ in." That is the unity. Now everything in the deck either supports that sentence or comes out. Slides on adjacent verticals — out. The growth narrative — kept, framed against that claim. The hiring slide — only kept if it shows the people hired to deliver that promise. The deck shrinks to 19 slides. The pitch becomes legible.

**Result:** Investors who would have nodded politely through the 47-slide version now have a clear question to evaluate: do we believe this is the right wedge, and are these the right people to win it? Decisions get made faster, in either direction. A clear "no" from one fund is more valuable than three weeks of polite "we're looking forward to following along" from five.

**Situation:** A founder writes the company's mission statement. The current version is: "Our mission is to empower enterprises through cutting-edge AI, transforming how organizations operate while delivering value to customers, employees, and shareholders alike."

**Application:** No unity. Three audiences (customers, employees, shareholders) are being addressed in one sentence. Two tones (visionary "transforming," operational "delivering value") are being mixed. The verbs are abstractions. Reduce. Ask: what one thing does this company do, for whom? Answer: "We help insurance companies process claims twice as fast." Now the mission has unity. It says one thing. It commits to one audience. It is testable against actual behaviour. The other constituencies — employees, shareholders — are served by *also* having a mission they can act on.

**Result:** Employees can use the mission to settle a roadmap argument. Investors can evaluate whether the company is on-mission. Customers can decide whether they want what is being offered. The original mission did none of this because it was about everything, which means it was about nothing.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a long-form post on the company blog about the launch of a major new product line. The draft mixes product features, the engineering journey, customer testimonials, market analysis, and a personal story about why the founder cares about this problem.

**Application:** Five pieces are fighting for the same 1,800 words. Each one is good and none are doing their job because of the others. Ask the founder: what is the one thing the reader should take away? They eventually say: "That this product solves a problem I personally lived with for ten years and never saw anyone else solve, and now we have." That's the spine. The personal story becomes the lead. The product features get folded in around the moments where they fix what was broken in the founder's own experience. The market analysis goes into a footer. The testimonials get cut entirely (they undermine the personal voice). The post drops to 1,100 words. It feels twice as long because it now has a centre.

**Result:** Readers share the post because it is now an essay rather than a press release. The product gets remembered because it is attached to a specific person, with a specific problem, with a specific resolution. The other material that got cut — the testimonials, the market analysis — survives in adjacent formats (case studies, market notes) where it can do its own work without diluting the launch piece.

**Situation:** A founder writes a board update that opens with operational results, then segues into a strategic question about the next year, then into a hiring update, then into a competitor's recent move, then back to a financial forecast. Each section is well-written. The board comes out of the meeting confused.

**Application:** The update has no thesis. Each section is a separate document trying to share one envelope. Pick the spine: what is the one decision or one signal the board most needs from this update? In this case it turns out to be "the company is at a crossroads about whether to move upmarket, and I want the board's input by next month." Restructure: open with that. Frame the operational results as evidence for the choice. Frame the competitor's move as evidence for the choice. Frame the hiring update as a hedge depending on which choice gets made. The financial forecast becomes two scenarios, one per direction.

**Result:** The board has a clear question to answer. Their attention focuses. The follow-up email after the meeting is short because the meeting was unified. The next update can pick up the thread and report on the decision; it does not have to start over.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to write the "definitive" document.
**Why:** *What you think is definitive today will turn undefinitive by tonight, and writers who doggedly pursue every last fact will find themselves pursuing the rainbow and never settling down to write.* The founder who is waiting until they "know enough" to write the strategy memo will never write it. Reduce. Pick the corner you can write today. Write it. Iterate.

**Don't:** Resolve unity by adding more sections.
**Why:** When a draft is incoherent, the temptation is to add a "framing paragraph" or a "summary section" to make the whole thing hang together. Almost always, the right move is to remove material, not add it. The framing paragraph that was needed is a sign that the spine isn't there.

**Don't:** Let the unity slip in the last paragraph.
**Why:** Founders write a tightly-unified piece, then panic in the closing paragraph and try to "round it out" with material from the second-best version of the document. The reader's last impression is the loss of unity, not the unity that came before. End in the same key you played in.

**Don't:** Confuse "things I want to say" with "what the piece is about."
**Why:** Founders are often writing because something has been on their mind. Several somethings, usually. The piece is not about all of them. It can only be about one. Make a list of the things you want to say. Pick one as the subject. The others are notes for future pieces or for follow-up conversations. They are not paragraphs in this one.

**Don't:** Use sub-headings to paper over a missing spine.
**Why:** "1. Product. 2. Growth. 3. Team. 4. Outlook." A document organised by topic-headers is usually one that has not decided what it argues. Sub-headings are fine; they should be the visible joints of an argument that already exists. If the argument is missing, the headings are scaffolding around an empty building.

**Don't:** Switch tones because a section "felt heavy."
**Why:** When a sober document gets a sudden joke or a celebratory document gets a sudden dark passage, it is usually because the writer became uncomfortable with the tone they had committed to. Sit with the discomfort. The reader's discomfort with a tonal switch is much greater than yours with sustaining a tone.
