# Business and Bureaucratic Prose

## When to Use

- A founder shows you a draft memo, report, mission statement, or annual letter and asks if it sounds right.
- A founder is writing on behalf of the company — annual report, public statement, customer communication, partnership announcement.
- A founder is editing copy from marketing, PR, or HR that has acquired the corporate dialect.
- A founder's writing is full of MBA-deck language: "leverage," "alignment," "value-add," "north star," "go-to-market motion," "operating cadence."
- A founder is writing a customer-facing communication (price increase, policy change, terms-of-service update, apology after an incident).
- A founder is writing internal documentation — process docs, RFCs, runbooks — that nobody reads because the prose is dead.
- A founder is producing recruiting copy that sounds like every other recruiting copy.
- A founder asks "how do we make the company sound less corporate?" — almost always answerable.

## Fails When

- **The document is genuinely formal.** A legal contract, a regulatory filing, a 10-K, a SOC2 attestation — these have their own conventions for good reason. Don't apply "humanise it" to a document where the institutional voice is doing real work.
- **The user mistakes "warm up the institution" for "make every memo a personal essay."** A weekly engineering update does not need a memoir-style opening. Calibrate.
- **The user strips out structure that the genre needs.** Some documents — meeting minutes, change logs, security disclosures — have a specific structure for navigability. Don't reinvent the form to make it readable; the form is the readability.
- **The user replaces corporate dialect with breezy slang.** "Hey team! So real talk..." is not the cure for "Per the alignment of our strategic priorities..." Both are wrong.

## Core Concept

Most institutional writing is bad. Almost all of it. Annual reports, mission statements, customer policy emails, internal memos, RFC docs, careers pages, partnership announcements, all-hands communications, board updates, investor letters — the dominant register across all of these is a kind of homogenised corporate dialect that no human being would speak in any other setting and that everyone produces because everyone produces it. *Most people work for institutions: businesses, banks, insurance firms, law firms, government agencies, school systems, non-profit organizations and other entities. Whoever they are, they tend to be so afraid of writing that their sentences lack all humanity — and so do their institutions. It's hard to imagine that these are real places where real men and women come to work every morning.*

*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*

The founder's situation is specific: they are signing some of the worst-written documents in their company, and many of them are documents the founder either wrote or approved. The annual letter to investors is theirs. The mission statement on the website is theirs. The all-hands script is theirs. The customer apology after the outage is theirs. The job description for the VP role is theirs (or at least approved by them). And every one of these documents is a place where the dialect of the institution has eaten the voice of the company. The cure is the founder noticing — and refusing.

The first move is to recognise that institutional voice is a choice, not a requirement. *Just because people work for an institution, they don't have to write like one. Institutions can be warmed up. Administrators can be turned into human beings. Information can be imparted clearly and without pomposity. You only have to remember that readers identify with people, not with abstractions like "profitability," or with Latinate nouns like "utilization" and "implementation," or with inert constructions in which nobody can be visualized doing something: "pre-feasibility studies are in the paperwork stage."* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) The dialect is comfortable for the writer because it allows them to hide. It is bad for the reader because it transmits no actual person and no actual claim.

The second move is to identify the specific dialect. Zinsser, in 1980s American business prose, called out: "facilitate" instead of "ease," "implement" instead of "do," "utilize" instead of "use," "enhance" instead of "improve," "objective" instead of "goal," and the deadly category of empty motion verbs: "going forward," "moving forward," "in the process of." Every era's bad business writing has its own version. The 2026 founder version includes: "leverage," "align," "operationalise," "stand up," "run point on," "drive value," "north star," "first principles" (used in MBA decks where no first principles are visible), "scale" as a noun, "AI-native," "agentic." None of these words are bad in themselves. They are bad when they have replaced the specific verb or specific noun that would have done real work.

The third move is to find the human inside the institution. *I urge them to be natural. How we write and how we talk is how we define ourselves. The institution sounds cold because it acquiesces in the process of being pumped up and dried out. Preoccupied with their high technology, they forget that some of the most powerful tools they possess — for good and for bad — are words.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) The way to warm up an institution is to locate the missing "I." The annual letter has a CEO. The customer apology has a person who is responsible. The recruiting copy is for a team led by a specific human. Find them. Use their words. Let them sign.

A specific founder example: most company values are written in the corporate dialect. "We are customer-obsessed." "We move fast." "We disagree and commit." Each phrase is admirable and tells the team nothing. The values that actually shape behaviour are specific, often awkward, written in a voice that sounds like a person: "When in doubt, send it." "Document the decision, not the discussion." "If you're going to argue with me, send me the memo first." These commit to behaviour. They are testable. They sound like the company they came from. The corporate-dialect values sound like every other company's values.

The fourth move applies most strongly to operational and administrative writing. The school principal's letter to parents that says "We have established a special phone communication system to provide additional opportunities for parent input" is the same letter as the founder's email that says "We have implemented a new feedback mechanism to enhance customer engagement touchpoints." Both are institutions hiding behind nominalisations. *The way to warm up any institution is to locate the missing "I." Remember: "I" is the most interesting element in any story.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) Cut "implemented." Cut "enhance." Cut "engagement." Cut "touchpoints." Replace with: "I'm setting up a new way to give us feedback. Reply to this email or call my mobile."

The fifth move is to refuse the manager's defence. Founders, when challenged on bad business prose, often defend it as required by the genre. "This is just how board updates are written." "Investors expect this language." "Marketing has to sound polished." Most of this is wrong. *Plain talk will not be easily achieved in corporate America. Too much vanity is on the line. Managers at every level are prisoners of the notion that a simple style reflects a simple mind. Actually a simple style is the result of hard work and hard thinking; a muddled style reflects a muddled thinker or a person too arrogant, or too dumb, or too lazy to organize his thoughts.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) Investors do not actually want corporate dialect; they want clarity. Customers do not want polished apologies; they want specific accountability. Employees do not want an aspirational mission statement; they want guidance for how to make the call when the founder is not in the room. The "expectations" that justify bad prose are usually the writer's own.

There is a specific founder discipline: the moment a memo gets edited by committee, plain language starts to die. The founder writes a clear draft. Marketing softens it. Legal hedges it. PR adjusts the tone. HR adds a paragraph. The final document, two weeks later, is corporate dialect. The fix is to draw a line at the founder's voice. Edits for accuracy and risk are welcome. Edits that strip voice — that turn "I" into "we," that replace specific verbs with abstract ones, that add throat-clearing preambles — are rejected. The founder's job is not to approve the document; their job is to defend the voice in it.

A particular pathology: the executive who doesn't write their own communications. *I realized that most executives in America don't write what appears over their signature or what they say in their speeches. They have surrendered the qualities that make them unique. If they and their institutions seem cold, it's because they acquiesce in the process of being pumped up and dried out. Preoccupied with their high technology, they forget that some of the most powerful tools they possess — for good and for bad — are words. If you work for an institution, whatever your job, whatever your level, be yourself when you write. You will stand out as a real person among the robots, and your example might even persuade Thomas Bell to write his own stuff.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) The founder who delegates all writing has surrendered the most important leverage they have. Each piece of written communication is a chance to make the company sound like a specific, peculiar, identifiable entity rather than a category of company.

The deepest claim under all of this: institutional writing is a market signal. Companies that produce bad institutional prose are signalling something about themselves that customers, investors, employees, and recruits all register, even if they don't articulate it. *Any organization that won't take the trouble to be both clear and personal in its writing will lose friends, customers and money.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) Conversely, companies whose every public document has a recognisable voice are signalling something equally specific. The signal is competence and confidence and care. It compounds.

## How to Apply

1. **Read every institutional document for "I" and the absence of it.** Annual letter, mission statement, customer apology, all-hands email, recruiting copy. Where is the human responsible for the words? If the answer is "no one in particular," that is the problem to fix.

2. **Audit for the dialect of your industry.** List the words that show up in your sector's decks but in no human conversation. "Leverage." "Drive value." "Scale up." "Align around." "North star." "Operating cadence." Notice each one in your drafts and replace with what you actually mean.

3. **Replace process-nouns with people doing things.** "There has been a reorganization of the engineering team" is a sentence in which no one acts. "I moved Priya to platform and asked Marcus to lead infra" is the same fact, alive. The most common bureaucratic pattern is the switch from active sentences to passive ones to nominalisations; the cure is to invert.

4. **Resist committee-driven softening.** When a draft is being passed around for edits, watch what happens. The first edit usually softens a strong sentence. The second hedges a commitment. The third adds a preamble. By the fifth, the document is dead. The founder's job is to push back at each stage with the question: "What information have we lost?"

5. **Use specific verbs and specific nouns.** "We will improve customer success outcomes" is empty. "We will respond to every support ticket within four hours and refund anyone who has waited longer" is action. The first is a prose-shaped commitment; the second is an actual one.

6. **Sign your work and own it.** Letters that go out under the founder's name should sound like the founder. Letters that go out under the company's name should still have a recognisable voice. The "we" that everyone uses is the "we" no one believes.

7. **Treat every public document as a recruiting and culture artifact.** The careers page, the all-hands email, the partnership announcement — each is read by current and prospective employees, customers, and investors. Each is a chance to communicate what the company is actually like. The corporate-dialect version communicates that you are like every other company. Don't be like every other company.

8. **Use plain language in the moments that matter most.** The layoff announcement. The pricing change. The product retirement. The major mistake. These are precisely the moments where founders reach for institutional dialect because it feels safer. It is not safer. It is more dangerous. The plain-language version of the hard message lands; the corporate version produces lasting trust damage.

9. **Make rules, not aspirations.** Mission statements that say "we are customer-obsessed" tell the team nothing. Mission statements that say "we will not ship anything we wouldn't use ourselves" or "we will not lie to customers, including by omission, even when it costs us a deal" are actionable. The first is a pose. The second is a commitment.

10. **Read your competitors' worst documents.** Their annual letters, their all-hands transcripts, their customer apologies. Notice the dialect. Notice that they all sound the same. Resolve to sound different — and then watch which of your specific writing choices produce the difference. Most of them are subtractions.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is finalising the company's annual letter to investors. The current opening: "Fiscal year 2025 was a year of significant transformation for [Company], characterised by strategic execution against our core priorities, meaningful progress on key operational metrics, and substantial investment in long-term capabilities that will position us for continued growth."

**Application:** No information. Every adjective is a placeholder. Replace with: "We grew from $14M to $34M ARR in fiscal 2025. The growth came almost entirely from existing customers expanding their usage; new logo acquisition was flat. Two things drove the expansion: the platform feature we shipped in March, and a 60-person sales hiring spree in Q2 that I am still uncertain about." Now the investor knows the numbers, the source of the growth, and the founder's actual epistemic state. The original opening would have been skimmed; the rewrite is read with attention.

**Result:** Investors who read the original would have signed off on the company's progress without engaging. Investors who read the rewrite engage with the actual situation — including the founder's uncertainty, which produces useful follow-up conversations. The annual letter becomes a working document instead of a ceremonial one.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the company's first customer-policy update — a price increase. The current draft says: "We are reaching out today to inform you of upcoming changes to our pricing structure, effective January 1st, designed to better align our pricing with the value we deliver and to support continued investment in product innovation."

**Application:** "Reaching out today to inform" — a phrase no one says. "Designed to better align" — corporate cliché. "Support continued investment" — the customer doesn't care about your investment. Replace with: "We are raising prices on January 1st. The increase is 22% for the Pro tier and 35% for Enterprise. We have not raised prices in three years; we should have done it 18 months ago. Existing customers on annual contracts get a 6-month grace period at current pricing. Here's what changes for you specifically." Plain. Specific. Honest about the company's own delay.

**Result:** Customers register the price increase as fair (because the explanation is honest about why) and as bounded (because the specific terms are clear). Original draft would have produced confused inbound about what the increase actually was; rewrite is self-explanatory.

**Situation:** A founder is approving the company's careers page. The current copy: "We are a mission-driven team of passionate, talented individuals dedicated to transforming [industry]. Our culture is built on the values of innovation, collaboration, and customer obsession. We're looking for ambitious, smart people who want to make a meaningful impact while growing alongside an exceptional team."

**Application:** Indistinguishable from every other careers page. Replace with: "We are 38 people working on automated tax compliance for global remote teams. We hire for two things: technical taste and the willingness to talk to customers. Most weeks, every engineer joins at least one customer call. Most quarters, we ship something we are slightly ashamed of, then fix it. We are not for everyone — read this honest internal doc on our tradeoffs before applying." The version is unmistakably about this specific company.

**Result:** Applications drop in volume and rise dramatically in quality. The candidates who apply have read the page and self-selected for specific traits. The original page attracted 200 applications a month, of which 5 were qualified. The rewrite attracts 60, of which 25 are qualified.

**Situation:** A founder approves an internal RFC document on a major architectural decision. The doc is well-structured but written in pure engineer-corporate dialect: "This RFC proposes the migration of our authentication subsystem to a centralized identity service, motivated by the need to enable cross-product integration, support advanced authorization patterns, and reduce the long-term maintenance burden of our current implementation."

**Application:** RFCs need structure, but they don't need dialect. Rewrite: "We currently have three different login systems, one per product. They have drifted apart and are starting to fight each other. Anything that requires single-sign-on across products is now four engineering quarters of work. This RFC proposes that we replace all three with one. The cost is a six-month migration. The benefit is that we stop paying the SSO tax forever." The same content, with the actual problem visible and the actual tradeoff stated.

**Result:** Engineers can argue about the proposal because they understand it. The original would have been signed off without scrutiny; the rewrite produces useful disagreement and a better final design. The architecture-by-corporate-dialect failure mode is averted.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the layoff email. HR has produced a draft full of "right-sizing," "operational optimisation," and "transitioning impacted teammates." The founder wants to send it as-is because "this is the kind of language people expect for these announcements."

**Application:** This is exactly when the dialect must be refused. The layoff is the highest-stakes communication of the year. Corporate language is read as evasion at the moment when honesty is most needed. Rewrite: "Today I am laying off 47 people, about 15% of the company. I'll explain why, who is affected, what severance and benefits look like, and what we owe the people leaving. I'm sorry. I should have made this decision three months ago." The email is half the length and twice as effective.

**Result:** Internal trust holds. Departing employees, while still angry, register the founder's accountability and recommend the company to others later. The "right-sizing" version would have produced a Glassdoor review titled "lying coward" within 48 hours; the rewrite produces "hard but handled with respect."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the corporate "we" when you mean "I."
**Why:** "We have decided to sunset the product" is what the company says when no one is responsible. "I have decided to shut the product down" is what a founder says when they are taking accountability. Readers can always tell the difference; "we" is read as evasion in any document where one person is making the call.

**Don't:** Replace corporate dialect with breezy slang.
**Why:** "Per our alignment around the value-add" is one failure mode. "Hey folks! So real talk on the layoff..." is the other. Both are dialects, both are protective, neither is voice. The founder's task is to find their actual voice, which is neither.

**Don't:** Approve communications you wouldn't send under your own name.
**Why:** Many founders sign documents — careers pages, customer FAQs, partnership announcements — that they would never write themselves. The signature is the endorsement. If you wouldn't write it, don't approve it. Send the draft back. The marketing team's third version is almost always better than their first.

**Don't:** Defend bad business prose by appeal to genre expectations.
**Why:** "This is just how investor letters are written." "All careers pages sound this way." The genre conventions are mostly fictional; the small set of companies whose institutional writing is recognisable have produced the conventions of *their* writing, and those conventions are the ones worth following — they've been chosen, not inherited.

**Don't:** Add a "softening" paragraph at the start of every hard memo.
**Why:** The temptation, when about to deliver bad news, is to lead with appreciation, gratitude, or context. "I want to start by thanking everyone for their hard work this quarter..." Cut it. The reader knows you appreciate them. They are reading the memo to find out what changed. Get to the change.

**Don't:** Use a thesaurus to replace simple words with complex ones.
**Why:** The instinct to "elevate" prose with longer synonyms — "utilise" for "use," "implement" for "do," "facilitate" for "ease" — is one of the great vandalisms of business writing. It does not elevate. It muddies. The simple word is almost always the right word.

**Don't:** Mistake "professional" for "voiceless."
**Why:** Professionals write with voice; only middle managers write voicelessly. Surgical case notes have voice. Judicial opinions have voice. Trial-balloon investor memos have voice. The "professional tone" that is voiceless is the tone of the writer who is afraid to be seen.

**Don't:** Strip out the specific in service of "broad applicability."
**Why:** Founders sometimes write generic mission statements and policies because they want the language to "apply broadly." It will never apply broadly because no one will read it. Specific commitments — to specific customer types, specific behaviours, specific tradeoffs — are the only ones with traction. Apparent specificity is the writer's most underused tool.
