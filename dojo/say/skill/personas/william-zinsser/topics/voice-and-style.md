# Voice and Style

## When to Use

- A founder is writing anything that goes out over their own name — investor update, all-hands email, blog post, customer apology, hiring letter — and the draft sounds like nobody in particular.
- The founder has scrubbed all "I" out of the document and replaced it with "we" or with passive constructions, hiding behind the collective.
- The draft is technically correct, full of accurate information, and somehow lifeless. You can't picture anyone saying these sentences.
- The founder has tried to sound like Jeff Bezos / Warren Buffett / Patrick Collison and produced a parody.
- The writing is "professional" in the worst sense — a defensive, neutered version of how the founder actually talks.
- A founder asks: "Should this sound more authoritative?" — almost always, the answer is no; it should sound more human.

## Fails When

- **The genre genuinely is institutional.** A SOC2 attestation, a privacy policy, a regulatory filing — these are not places to find your voice. They have rules of their own and you should follow them.
- **You mistake voice for breeziness.** Voice is not "Hey guys! Buckle up!" It is not exclamation points. It is not folksy slang. The breezy style is harder to read than good English, and the writer who reaches for it has confused informality with humanity.
- **You try to import someone else's voice wholesale.** Reading Bezos to learn structure is one thing; copying his cadence is another. Voice that has been borrowed sounds borrowed. Style is organic to the person doing the writing, as much a part of him as his hair.
- **The fact in the room is too painful for "I" to bear.** In some moments — a death, a fire, a serious safety issue — a leader speaks plainly and almost institutionally because the gravity of the event demands restraint. Voice can yield to occasion.

## Core Concept

A founder's writing problem is rarely a writing problem. It is almost always a problem of who-am-I-when-I-write. They sit down to write a board update, or a pitch email, or a launch announcement, and the person who shows up on the page is not the person who walked into the office that morning. The voice is more cautious. The vocabulary is borrowed from McKinsey decks. The sentences have been pre-vetted by an imaginary committee of investors, employees, and journalists. What gets produced is competent and dead.

*Style is organic to the person doing the writing, as much a part of him as his hair, or, if he is bald, his lack of it. Trying to add style is like adding a toupee. At first glance the formerly bald man looks young and even handsome. But at second glance — and with a toupee there's always a second glance — he doesn't look quite right. The point is that he doesn't look like himself.*

*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*

The first move is therefore subtractive. You do not learn to write with voice by acquiring style. You learn by removing the things you put on top of yourself when you sat down to write. The corporate "we." The defensive jargon. The professorial "one." The throat-clearing preamble. The sentence that signals to the reader, "I am about to say something important." All of these are toupees. The sentence underneath them — the one that says what you actually believe in the words you would actually use over coffee with your co-founder — is your voice.

The second move is positive: write in the first person, even when you have been told not to. *Writers are obviously at their most natural when they write in the first person. Writing is an intimate transaction between two people, conducted on paper, and it will go well to the extent that it retains its humanity.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*) Founders are taught — by lawyers, by PR people, by HR, by conventional wisdom — to use "we" instead of "I." We are excited. We are committed. We have decided. The "we" feels safer. It distributes responsibility. It also drains the writing of the one quality that gives a founder's letter any value: a single human being is responsible for what comes next, and you are reading what they think.

Even when "I" is not strictly available — a press release, a regulatory filing — the discipline holds. *Even when "I" isn't permitted, it's still possible to convey a sense of I-ness. Good writers are visible just behind their words. If you aren't allowed to use "I," at least think "I" while you write, or write the first draft in the first person and then take the "I"s out. It will warm up your impersonal style.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*) This is the writer's version of method acting. The voice on the page comes from the posture in the chair. If the founder sat down composed of the worry that fifty people will misread their words, the worry will be on the page. If they sat down as themselves talking to one specific person they trust, that will be on the page too.

There is a discipline beneath all of this called taste, and taste is finally what separates a voice readers love from a voice readers tolerate. Two writers can be equally direct, equally specific, equally informed, and one will be a pleasure to read while the other is a chore. The difference is taste. *For writers and other creative artists, knowing what* not *to do is a major component of taste.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Sound of Your Voice"*) The painter without taste gives us a landscape that is too pretty, or too cluttered, or too gaudy. The writer without taste reaches for the cliché instead of the fresh phrase, the corny synonym instead of the right word, the gilded adjective instead of the strong noun. They cannot help it; their ear is uncalibrated. The writer with taste is the one who left out the words you would have left in.

The good news is that taste can partly be acquired. The way to acquire it is the way every craft is learned: imitation followed by abandonment. *Never hesitate to imitate another writer. Imitation is part of the creative process for anyone learning an art or a craft. Bach and Picasso didn't spring full-blown as Bach and Picasso; they needed models. Find the best writers in the fields that interest you and read their work aloud. Get their voice and their taste into your ear — their attitude toward language. Don't worry that by imitating them you'll lose your own voice and your own identity. Soon enough you will shed those skins and become who you are supposed to become.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Sound of Your Voice"*) For founders, this means reading good writing — Joan Didion on Howard Hughes, A.J. Liebling on boxing, Janet Malcolm on anyone — and listening for what their ear catches that yours misses. Read aloud. Listen.

The breezy style is a special trap. It is what inexperienced writers reach for when they are told to "write like you talk." They produce something that sounds like a bad podcast — folksy, slangy, full of "you guys" and "spoiler alert" and aggressive parentheticals. *Inexperienced writers miss this point. They think that all they have to do to achieve a casual effect is to be "just folks" — good old Betty or Bob chatting over the back fence. They want to be a pal to the reader. They're so eager not to appear formal that they don't even try to write good English. What they write is the breezy style.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Sound of Your Voice"*) The casual masters — White, Thurber, Pritchett — sound effortless because they are not. The grammar is in place, the words are precise, the cadences have been polished. The breezy writer has fewer rules in place, not more, and the result is harder to read, not easier.

The deepest point about voice is one founders systematically resist: the audience for the writing is yourself. *You are writing for yourself. Don't try to visualize the great mass audience. There is no such audience — every reader is a different person. Don't try to guess what sort of thing editors want to publish or what you think the country is in a mood to read.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Audience"*) This sounds like solipsism. It is the opposite. The writer who tries to please everybody pleases nobody and is mistrusted by both. The writer who writes for themselves — to amuse themselves, to make themselves think clearly, to say what they actually believe — produces work that other people, recognising a real voice on the page, decide to read.

For a founder, this means: write the email you would want to receive if you were one of your investors. Not the email you imagine your investors want to receive. Those are not the same email. The first one is yours. The second one is a forgery you have produced of someone else's idea of a founder.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the first draft in the first person, even if you'll strip it later.** "I" forces you to commit. "We" lets you hide. Even if the final document needs to be in the corporate voice, the draft underneath should have been written by a person. The "I"-ness will leak through into the final version and warm it up.

2. **Read the draft aloud, alone, in your office.** If you would not say this sentence to a colleague over coffee, do not write it. The mouth is a better editor than the eye. Founders who do not read their own writing aloud are missing 80% of the available signal.

3. **Cut the throat-clearing.** Most founder letters open with a paragraph that is not about anything. "I want to take a moment to reflect on the journey we've been on as a team and share some thoughts about where we're headed." Delete it. Start with the sentence where you start sounding like yourself. *It's amazing how often an editor can throw away the first three or four paragraphs of an article, or even the first few pages, and start with the paragraph where the writer begins to sound like himself or herself.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*)

4. **Refuse the dialect of your role.** "As CEO, I am committed to ensuring that we continue to drive..." is the dialect of an actor playing a CEO. Real CEOs talk about their company the way real fathers talk about their kids — specifically, with affection, with concrete recent details. Replace role-language with self-language.

5. **When in doubt, sound like a person.** *Never say anything in writing that you wouldn't comfortably say in conversation. If you're not a person who says "indeed" or "moreover," or who calls someone an individual ("he's a fine individual"), please don't write it.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Audience"*) Make a small list of words you would never use out loud. Then notice how often you let them onto the page when you write professionally. Cut all of them.

6. **Develop a single voice across all your writing.** *I wrote one book about baseball and one about jazz. But it never occurred to me to write one of them in sports English and the other in jazz English. Don't alter your voice to fit your subject. Develop one voice that readers will recognize when they hear it on the page.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Sound of Your Voice"*) Your board update, your hiring email, your customer apology, your launch blog post — all of them should sound like the same person wrote them. The reader is supposed to learn what your voice is by encountering it repeatedly. If every piece sounds different, you have no voice; you have a wardrobe of costumes.

7. **Imitate, then shed.** Pick three founders or writers whose voice you admire — not for their fame but for the specific way their sentences move. Read them aloud. Try writing in their cadence for a week. You will produce embarrassing imitations. Keep going. After a month, the cadences will start to inflect your own writing without dominating it. This is how voice is acquired — not by introspection, but by ear.

8. **Stop curating yourself for the audience you're afraid of.** Most founders have an internal audience — usually a category of investor or journalist or employee — they are silently writing toward, defending themselves against in advance. The voice that results is hedged, defensive, anaesthetic. Identify your imagined hostile reader. Then write the email as if they were not in the room. Edit later for what they need to hear; do not draft for them.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is writing the annual letter to investors. The opening reads: "It has been a transformational year for the company, marked by significant progress against our strategic objectives and notable milestones across our core product portfolio."

**Application:** No person could have written this. There is no "I" anywhere in it; every noun is generic; "transformational," "significant," "notable" are pre-judging adjectives that do the reader's thinking for them. Ask: what would the founder say if a friend asked over a beer "how was the year?" Probably something like: "We almost died in February. By June we had figured out the enterprise motion and shipped the new platform. We grew 3x and we have a real business now." That is the founder's voice. Now the letter has somewhere to start.

**Result:** Investors read with attention rather than skim. They feel they know what happened, in what order, with what stakes. They trust the next paragraph more because the first one was written by a human being who was prepared to commit to specific facts.

**Situation:** A founder posts a product launch blog post that opens: "Today, we're thrilled to announce a major new chapter for [Company], one that will fundamentally reshape how teams collaborate at scale."

**Application:** "Thrilled" is the dialect of marketing. "Major new chapter" is a phrase nobody has ever said out loud. "Fundamentally reshape" is a cliché. "How teams collaborate at scale" describes about 2,000 SaaS products. The whole sentence is what a writer produces when they have been told to "be excited" and have responded by performing excitement. The reader feels the performance and discounts it. Replace with: "We just shipped the thing we've been promising for two years. It lets a team plan a quarter in an afternoon. Here's what's in it and why we made the choices we made."

**Result:** Customers and prospects can tell whether the founder is writing or whether marketing is writing. The blog post that sounds like a person gets shared. The blog post that sounds like marketing gets ignored. Voice converts.

**Situation:** A founder needs to send a sober update to the board after a senior departure. The lawyer-vetted draft uses the third person throughout: "The Company announces the departure of [Name] from the role of Chief Revenue Officer. The Company thanks [Name] for their contributions and wishes them well in their future endeavors. The Company has commenced a search for a successor."

**Application:** This is institutional voice doing the work of pretending no human being is involved. It will land badly with the board, who know the founder personally and will read this as evasion. Rewrite in the founder's voice while keeping the facts: "I'm writing to let you know that Sara is leaving as CRO at the end of the month. I asked her to leave. The short version is that we needed someone with more enterprise experience and the gap had become a problem for closing late-stage deals. I think highly of Sara — she built our SMB motion from zero — and we parted on good terms. Search starts next week; I have a shortlist of three I'll share at the next meeting."

**Result:** The board gets the facts, the founder's view, the specific reason, the action plan, and a sense of judgment. The original draft would have triggered a flurry of follow-up calls trying to figure out what really happened. The rewrite ends the conversation by being the conversation.

**Situation:** A founder writes an all-hands email apologising for an outage. The draft says: "We sincerely apologise for any inconvenience caused. Please be assured that we are taking all necessary steps to prevent recurrence of this issue. Our team is working tirelessly to restore service and we appreciate your patience during this time."

**Application:** This is the dialect of any company sending the same letter. There is no "I." There is no information. "Sincerely apologise," "inconvenience caused," "all necessary steps," "working tirelessly," "appreciate your patience" — every phrase has been used a million times by a million companies and now reads as wallpaper. Customers do not believe wallpaper. They believe a person who tells them what happened, what they think, and what is being done.

**Result:** Rewrite: "We were down for 47 minutes today. The cause was a bad config push that I personally approved on Friday afternoon — a process failure on my part that we'll be talking about internally for some time. Service is restored. The specific fix is below; the longer post-mortem is by Tuesday. I'm sorry. If you depend on us and this hurt you, please reply to this email — I read every one." Voice converts an apology into an act of accountability rather than a press release.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "casual" with "voice."
**Why:** The breezy style — "Hey folks! So real talk: this quarter was wild!" — is not voice. It is a costume of informality, and it reads as condescending the moment the reader notices it. White's pig essay sounds easy because the grammar is iron and the words are precise. Casual prose without that scaffolding is not casual; it is sloppy.

**Don't:** Borrow a famous founder's cadence and call it your style.
**Why:** Bezos's three-paragraph memos work because Bezos thinks in three-paragraph memos and his organisation was structured around them. If you imitate the form without having earned the underlying habits of mind, you produce a parody. Use other writers as ear-training. Do not try to wear them.

**Don't:** Strip every "I" out of a piece because it feels egotistical.
**Why:** "Who am I to say what I think?" is the question every founder asks themselves on the way to writing nothing memorable. The answer is: who are you not to? You started a company. You are asking people to give you money or work for you or buy from you. Of course you have to say what you think. Hiding behind "we" is the literary equivalent of hiding behind your CFO at a press conference.

**Don't:** Write to please everybody.
**Why:** *If you lose the dullards back in the dust, you don't want them anyway.* The founder who tries to please all readers — bullish investors and bearish ones, optimistic employees and worried ones, retail customers and enterprise ones — produces a piece of writing that is no use to any of them. Pick the reader who matters most for the piece. Write to them. The others will either come along or they will not, and either is fine.

**Don't:** Confuse "professional tone" with quality.
**Why:** "Professional tone" almost always means voiceless, hedged, and over-edited. Real professionals — surgeons writing case notes, generals writing after-action reports, judges writing decisions — use plain, specific language because the stakes are too high for ornament. The founder who hides behind "professional tone" is doing the opposite of being professional.

**Don't:** Make grand stylistic gestures in the first paragraph.
**Why:** Almost every founder draft has too much in the opening: a Big Statement, a Sweeping Frame, a paragraph of throat-clearing rhetoric before any information. Throw it away. Start with the simplest, most concrete sentence you can write about what actually happened. The reader will trust you more after that than they would after a thousand words of ceremony.
