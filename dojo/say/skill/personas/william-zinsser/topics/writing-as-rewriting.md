# Writing as Rewriting

## When to Use

- A founder shows you a "draft" they intend to send in twenty minutes and asks for feedback.
- A founder is frustrated that their first draft is not good and concludes from this that they are not a writer.
- A draft contains a paragraph or sentence the founder is precious about even though it does not serve the piece.
- A founder asks how long it should take to write a board update / fundraising memo / launch post.
- A founder is paralysed in front of a blank document, trying to write the whole thing in their head before typing.
- A founder says "I'm not a writer" — usually they mean "my first drafts are bad," which is not the same thing.
- The piece is technically grammatical but lifeless; the founder doesn't know that lifelessness is a sign the piece needs another pass, not that they should give up.

## Fails When

- **The reply is genuinely urgent.** A customer is on fire, the press is asking, the board needs an answer in two hours. Send the ugly version. Don't waste time perfecting an email that needed to leave thirty minutes ago.
- **The user is mid-generation.** If the founder is still trying to find what they think, asking them to revise will freeze them. Generate first, edit second; never simultaneously.
- **The writing is throwaway.** A Slack reply, a quick scheduling email, a friend's birthday note. These do not warrant ceremony. Save the rewriting discipline for the writing that matters.
- **You revise so much you lose the original energy.** Some pieces lose voice in the polish. The first draft had a current; the seventh draft has metric correctness and no current. Stop two drafts before that.

## Core Concept

The single most useful thing a founder can learn about writing is that the first draft is not the writing. The first draft is the discovery of what they want to say. The actual writing — the version that gets sent — happens in revision. Founders who don't know this stay amateur their whole careers, no matter how many words they produce, because they keep mistaking the moment of typing for the moment of writing.

*Rewriting is the essence of writing well: it's where the game is won or lost. That idea is hard to accept. We all have an emotional equity in our first draft; we can't believe that it wasn't born perfect. But the odds are close to 100 percent that it wasn't. Most writers don't initially say what they want to say, or say it as well as they could. The newly hatched sentence almost always has something wrong with it. It's not clear. It's not logical. It's verbose. It's klunky. It's pretentious. It's boring. It's full of clutter. It's full of clichés. It lacks rhythm. It can be read in several different ways. It doesn't lead out of the previous sentence. It doesn't… The point is that clear writing is the result of a lot of tinkering.*

*— On Writing Well, "Bits & Pieces"*

The "tinkering" framing is important. Rewriting does not mean starting over. *I don't mean writing one draft and then writing a different second version, and then a third. Most rewriting consists of reshaping and tightening and refining the raw material you wrote on your first try.* (*— On Writing Well, "Bits & Pieces"*) The first draft is rarely thrown out wholesale. It is the lump of clay. Revision is shaping. The piece you finally send is mostly the words from the first draft, but the order has been changed, the connective tissue has been tightened, the redundant sentences have been cut, the placeholder verbs have been replaced with specific ones, the long-windedness in paragraph three has been compressed to a sentence, and the ending has been moved to where the actual ending was hiding in paragraph six.

There is a deeper claim under this. *You won't write well until you understand that writing is an evolving process, not a finished product.* (*— On Writing Well, "Bits & Pieces"*) Founders, more than most professionals, are trained to ship and move. Writing rewards a different metabolism. The piece is not done when you wrote the last sentence. It is done when you have read it three times, cut 30%, found the actual lead, fixed the rhythm where it limped, replaced the abstract nouns with concrete ones, and read it aloud once. The founder who skips this is not saving time. They are sending a worse version of the email and absorbing whatever cost the worse version produces — a lost meeting, a confused board, a customer who unsubscribes, a journalist who quotes the awkward sentence.

The deeper trap is the fixation on the finished product. Founders, like most American writers, want to picture the document done. *This fixation on the finished article causes writers a lot of trouble, deflecting them from all the earlier decisions that have to be made to determine its shape and voice and content. It's a very American kind of trouble. We are a culture that worships the winning result: the league championship, the high test score. Less glamorous gains made along the way — learning, wisdom, growth, confidence, dealing with failure — aren't given the same respect because they can't be given a grade. For writers the winning grade is the check.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Tyranny of the Final Product"*) Translated to a founder: the winning grade is the closed round, the won customer, the favourable press piece. The product becomes the only thing that gets attention. The process — the actual sequence of decisions that produced a good or bad document — is invisible.

This produces a specific founder pathology: writing-as-stress, where the piece is started too late, written in one sitting under pressure, sent because it must go, and never reflected on. The next piece is approached the same way. The founder gets no better at writing across hundreds of attempts because each attempt is an emergency, never a craft exercise. By contrast, the founder who treats each board update as an iteration in a long practice — who notices what worked last quarter, who keeps a folder of openings that landed well, who reads their old letters with detachment — gets meaningfully better at writing inside two years.

The mechanics of rewriting are unromantic and learnable. Read the draft aloud. Mark the places where you stumbled — those are the places the reader will stumble. Cut every sentence that does not earn its place. Look for transitions where one paragraph does not lead from the one before it; either fix the transition or fix the order. Find the buried lead and move it up. Find the closing summary you tacked on and delete it. Replace abstract nouns with specific ones. Replace passive constructions with active ones unless the passive is doing real work. Read it aloud again.

There is a personal note Zinsser keeps coming back to. *Learn to enjoy this tidying process. I don't like to write; I like to have written. But I love to rewrite. I especially like to cut: to press the DELETE key and see an unnecessary word or phrase or sentence vanish into the electricity. I like to replace a humdrum word with one that has more precision or color. I like to strengthen the transition between one sentence and another. I like to rephrase a drab sentence to give it a more pleasing rhythm or a more graceful musical line. With every small refinement I feel that I'm coming nearer to where I would like to arrive, and when I finally get there I know it was the rewriting, not the writing, that won the game.* (*— On Writing Well, "Bits & Pieces"*) The founder's parallel: writing the first draft is making the deck. Rewriting is the difference between the deck that gets the meeting and the deck that sits in the inbox.

One more discipline: trust your material. *The longer I work at the craft of writing, the more I realize that there's nothing more interesting than the truth. What people do — and what people say — continues to take me by surprise with its wonderfulness, or its quirkiness, or its drama, or its humor, or its pain.* (*— On Writing Well, "Bits & Pieces"*) Founders, in revision, often soften specifics into generalities. The customer who said "I trust your product more than I trust my own engineers" gets revised to "Customer feedback has been very positive." The Tuesday at 4am where everything almost broke gets revised to "We worked through some early operational challenges." Always go the other way in revision. Push toward the specific. The interesting fact is more interesting than the interesting framing of the fact.

## How to Apply

1. **Always write a first draft. Always revise it.** Set the rule once and apply it without exception for any piece longer than a paragraph that is going out to people whose attention matters. The first draft is for you. The revised version is for them.

2. **Wait between drafts.** Sleep, walk, eat. The eye that wrote the draft cannot see what is wrong with it. Twelve hours later you can. Twenty-four hours later you can see more. If the piece must go today, leave it for an hour at minimum.

3. **Read it aloud.** This is the highest-leverage edit available. The mouth catches what the eye does not — the rhythm that limps, the sentence that runs out of breath, the phrase that sounds like a press release. Founders systematically skip this and produce documents 20% worse than they could.

4. **Cut by at least 20%.** Most first drafts can be cut by 50% without losing information; for a founder, aim for a minimum of 20% on the second pass. The cut version is almost always better than the long version. Trust this.

5. **Look for the buried lead.** Read the first three paragraphs. The actual lead is usually somewhere in there. Move it to the top. Cut what came before.

6. **Look for the buried ending.** Read the last three paragraphs. The actual ending is usually somewhere in there too — and is followed by a paragraph or two of "wrapping up" that should be deleted.

7. **Sharpen the verbs and the nouns.** Pass through the draft hunting for "is," "are," "has been," "are seeing." Each is a candidate to be replaced by a verb that moves. Pass again hunting for abstract nouns — "alignment," "engagement," "implementation," "utilization." Replace each with a person doing something specific.

8. **Don't be precious about good sentences that don't fit.** Founders, having written one nice paragraph, will distort the rest of the document to keep it. *Self-discipline bordering on masochism is required.* If the sentence does not serve the piece, save it in a notes file for later. Cut it.

9. **Read it once more after you think you're done.** *Then I go over it once more, reading it aloud, and am always amazed at how much clutter can still be cut.* You will catch one more pass of clutter every time. There is no draft so polished that another reading does not improve it.

10. **Stop before you over-polish.** A piece can be revised to death. The seventh draft is sometimes worse than the third because the energy has been buffed out. Notice when you are revising for revising's sake. The right number of drafts for most founder writing is between three and five.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is preparing the Series B fundraising memo. They write the first draft on Sunday night, two weeks before the planned send date. The draft is 12 pages, mostly competent, with a buried lead and three weak sections.

**Application:** Do nothing on Monday. On Tuesday, read it aloud — find the buried lead in section 3, find that section 2 repeats section 4, find that the section on competitors is throat-clearing. Cut to 9 pages. Send to a trusted operator on Wednesday for outside eyes; they spot two arguments that don't follow. Revise on Thursday. Cut to 7 pages. Read aloud again Friday. Replace the abstract paragraph about market dynamics with a single concrete customer story. Send the next Monday. The memo that goes out is not the memo that was written; it is the memo that was rewritten.

**Result:** Investors who would have skimmed the 12-page Sunday draft read the 7-page final version closely. The lead, now in front, sets the right frame. The customer story, kept specific, sticks. The first-call meeting starts with the investor referring to the customer story by name; the founder knows the memo did its job because the investor remembers the right thing.

**Situation:** A founder writes a launch blog post. The draft is fine — coherent, accurate, no obvious errors. The founder is ready to ship. You read it; it is dead.

**Application:** The post needs another two passes. Pass one: find the buried lead — paragraph six is the actual opening; cut paragraphs one through five and start there. Pass two: read aloud — half the sentences begin "we are excited" / "we believe" / "we have built." Replace each with a verb that moves. Pass three: cut every sentence that summarises what the post is about; the post itself should do that work, not its summary.

**Result:** The post drops from 1,400 words to 900. It opens with a specific sentence the reader hasn't read in any other launch post. It gets shared. The original draft would have been forgotten by the next afternoon.

**Situation:** A founder writes a customer apology email after a major outage. They send it the moment they finish writing it because "speed matters."

**Application:** Speed matters more than perfection — but the difference between sending it now and sending it forty-five minutes later is almost zero in customer impact, and the difference in quality is enormous. The first draft contains: a buried lead (the actual fact of the outage and what happened comes in paragraph two), a defensive paragraph that reads as excuse-making, and an awkward closing about commitment. Forty-five minutes of rewriting produces an email that opens with the apology, states the cause without excuse, names the action being taken, and closes with a direct invitation to reply.

**Result:** Customers respond to the second version. The original would have generated three angry tweets. The rewritten version generates two, plus several "thank you for the transparency" replies. The 45 minutes paid for itself many times over.

**Situation:** A founder writes a sober internal memo about a strategic pivot. They love a particular paragraph in the middle that includes an extended metaphor about chess. The metaphor is clever. The paragraph is a digression.

**Application:** Cut the chess metaphor. Save it in the notes file. The rest of the memo, freed of the digression, reads as a straight-line argument. The chess metaphor will get used three months later in an investor letter where it actually serves the argument.

**Result:** The pivot memo lands. The team understands what is changing and why. The founder learns the most useful editing lesson of their career: a sentence you love is no defence against the sentence's failure to serve the piece.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Skip the second pass because "it's already pretty good."
**Why:** "Pretty good" is the danger zone. Most founder writing is "pretty good" and almost none of it is good. The gap between pretty good and good is the second and third pass. The founder who stops at pretty good never finds out what their writing could be.

**Don't:** Polish a draft you should have thrown away.
**Why:** Sometimes the first draft has the wrong shape entirely — wrong audience, wrong unity, wrong tone. Revising it sentence-by-sentence will not save it. The right move is to set it aside, identify what the piece actually wants to be, and start the second draft from a different premise. Some pieces are rescued by polishing. Some need to be re-conceived.

**Don't:** Revise during generation.
**Why:** Editing while writing the first draft is the single most common cause of paralysis. The voice that judges interrupts the voice that produces. Write a bad first draft fast. Edit later. Founders who try to write the perfect sentence on the first try produce three sentences in two hours.

**Don't:** Confuse "more polish" with "more value."
**Why:** A 12-pass document is sometimes worse than a 4-pass document because the energy has been smoothed out. Notice diminishing returns. When the changes you are making are no longer affecting comprehension, stop.

**Don't:** Treat draft length as a cost.
**Why:** Founders sometimes refuse to write long first drafts because "it'll just get cut." That is the wrong frame. The cuts cannot be made if the material was not generated. Generate generously. Cut ruthlessly. The two are sequential, not opposed.

**Don't:** Show the first draft to other people.
**Why:** Outside readers, given a first draft, will mostly comment on first-draft problems — clutter, awkward transitions, sentences you would have caught yourself in revision. Their attention is wasted on noise. Show them a third or fourth draft so they can comment on signal.

**Don't:** Write under maximum pressure if you can avoid it.
**Why:** The board update written in 90 minutes the night before the meeting is almost always worse than the same update written in three sessions across a week. The same is true of fundraising memos, launch posts, and major emails. Writing wants intervals. Plan around them.
