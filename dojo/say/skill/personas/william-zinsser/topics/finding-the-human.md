# Finding the Human

## When to Use

- A founder is writing about a category, market, or trend in pure abstractions and the writing has no pulse.
- A pitch deck describes the addressable market in dollars and percentages without naming a single customer or use case.
- A launch post describes technical architecture without showing one human task it changes.
- A case study has been written about "Customer X" rather than about a specific person at Customer X who used the product on a Tuesday at 4am.
- A founder has done thirty customer interviews and the resulting strategy memo paraphrases them all into "users want simplicity."
- A press release for a new partnership says "the partnership will enable mutual customers to..." without saying which customers will do what.
- A board update describes "headwinds in the enterprise segment" instead of telling the story of the two deals that actually slipped.
- The writing is competent but forgettable, and the founder cannot tell why.

## Fails When

- **The piece is a genuine aggregate.** Quarterly cohort analysis, a survey summary, a regulatory filing — these are about populations, not people. Forcing a single human into the lead would mislead.
- **You don't have permission.** The customer quote, the employee story, the prospect's words — none of these can be used without consent. Don't manufacture humanity at the cost of trust.
- **You confuse "anecdote" with "argument."** A heart-warming customer story is not, by itself, evidence of a strategic claim. Some founders use a single human story to do the work of analysis. The story should illustrate; the analysis should argue.
- **The genre forbids it.** A 10-K is not the place to find the human. Some forms have institutional voice for good reasons.

## Core Concept

Most founder writing fails not because the founder lacks information but because they have abstracted the information so thoroughly that the human who lived it has been removed from the page. The "enterprise segment" had two specific deals slip; the "customer base" includes a head of operations at a 200-person manufacturing firm who emails the founder at 11pm on Sundays; the "team" includes a particular engineer who shipped the rebuild on a particular weekend. None of these specific people make it into the writing. What makes it in is the category they belong to. The result is prose that contains no living thing.

*Get people talking. Learn to ask questions that will elicit answers about what is most interesting or vivid in their lives. Nothing so animates writing as someone telling what he thinks or what he does — in his own words.*

*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*

The principle is broader than interviewing. It is that the strongest unit of nonfiction is a specific human being doing something specific. Even when the founder is writing about an institution, a market, or a strategic shift, the load-bearing material is people. *Take heart. You'll find the solution if you look for the human element. Somewhere in every drab institution are men and women who have a fierce attachment to what they are doing and are rich repositories of lore. Somewhere behind every storm sewer is a politician whose future hangs on getting it installed and a widow who has always lived on the block and is outraged that some damn-fool legislator thinks it will wash away. Find these people to tell your story and it won't be drab.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing About People"*)

For a founder, this translates with almost no adjustment. The board update about the missed quarter is drab. The board update about a specific procurement officer who told you "I love your product but my CISO won't approve until you have SOC2 Type 2" is alive. Same fact. Different unit. The first is an aggregate; the second is a person, and the strategic implication (we need to accelerate SOC2) follows naturally from the human detail in a way that no abstract framing can match.

The discipline has two parts. The first is to find the human. *Somewhere behind every storm sewer is a politician...* For the founder this means: in every market analysis, find the operator whose job depends on this. In every product launch, find the user whose Tuesday morning changes. In every strategy memo, find the customer whose call last week reframed your view. In every hiring decision, find the existing team member who will be affected. The human is always there; founders trained in business-school abstraction have been taught not to see them.

The second part is to render the human in their own words. The paraphrased version of what a customer said is always weaker than the verbatim version. *His own words will always be better than your words, even if you are the most elegant stylist in the land. They carry the inflection of his speaking voice and the idiosyncrasies of how he puts a sentence together. They contain the regionalisms of his conversation and the lingo of his trade. They convey his enthusiasms. This is a person talking to the reader directly, not through the filter of a writer. As soon as a writer steps in, everyone else's experience becomes secondhand.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Interview"*) For a founder, this means: when you have a customer quote that is exactly right, use it verbatim. Do not "polish" it. Do not "make it sound more professional." The exact words — including the slightly awkward phrasing, the specific verb, the brand name they used — are the value.

The deeper claim is about how readers process information. Abstractions are skimmed. Specifics stick. *Readers identify with people, not with abstractions like "profitability," or with Latinate nouns like "utilization" and "implementation," or with inert constructions in which nobody can be visualized doing something.* (*— On Writing Well, "Business Writing"*) An investor who read your "we are seeing strong adoption in mid-market" cannot picture anything; they have read this sentence in a hundred decks. An investor who reads "Sara, head of operations at a 180-person logistics company in Memphis, told me last week 'this is the first software we've bought in five years that I haven't had to fight my team to use'" can picture Sara, the logistics company, the moment of being told. The picture is what they remember. The picture does the persuasive work.

There is a structural principle here too. *Look for the human element* is not just a stylistic adjustment; it is a reordering of how you think about your subject. The founder writing about "the future of work" is going to produce one of fifty thousand essays about the future of work, and theirs will be indistinguishable from the others. The founder writing about a specific moment in a specific 12-person team where one decision changed the texture of how that team operated is writing something only they can write. The market-trend essay is generic by definition. The human-anchored essay is unique by construction.

A specific founder version of this: case studies. Most B2B case studies are dead because they have been written by marketing about an abstract entity called "the customer." The customer in the case study has no name (or has a token name like "John, IT Director"), no specific moment of decision, no specific resistance, no specific moment of relief. They are a placeholder. *Find the men and women who have a fierce attachment to what they are doing and are rich repositories of lore.* The good case study has a specific person, on a specific Tuesday, who said a specific sentence. It is read because it is unmistakably about a real situation rather than a templated narrative.

Another founder version: the all-hands. The all-hands talk that opens with "I want to share some context on where we are as a company" is dead. The all-hands talk that opens with "On Friday, a customer named Marcus called me and said something I haven't been able to stop thinking about" is alive. The same strategic content can be delivered. The difference is whether anyone in the room remembers it on Monday.

There is one further point. The human element you find is often not the obvious one. *The whole building turned out to be just such a mixture of scholars and searchers and crackpots, and my story, though ostensibly the chronicle of an institution, was really a story about people.* (*— On Writing Well, "Writing About People"*) The founder writing the company-history essay should not lead with themselves; they should lead with whichever specific person — early customer, first hire, weird-but-formative advisor, employee who quit and came back — encapsulates what is true about the company that nothing else does. The institution is the backdrop. The person is the thing.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit any draft for people.** Read through. Count the named, specific human beings. If the count is zero, that is your problem. If the count is two, ask whether either of them is doing real work in the piece or whether they are decorative.

2. **In every strategic memo, find a customer.** Before you write about "the enterprise segment," find one specific enterprise customer whose situation makes the abstract case real. Use them. Quote them. Their experience is more persuasive than five sentences of generality.

3. **Use verbatim quotes, not paraphrases.** When a customer says something useful, write it down exactly. *His own words will always be better than your words.* If they said "this is the first software in five years I haven't had to fight my team to use," do not paraphrase to "customers report low organizational friction with our product."

4. **Get permission, then use names.** "John, IT Director at a Fortune 500 company" is dead. "Anthony Reyes, IT Director at Mass General" is alive. Founders systematically under-ask for permission to use names. Most customers, asked properly, will agree to be named — especially in B2B, where being publicly associated with an interesting product reflects well on them.

5. **Tell stories about specific moments, not patterns.** "Customers love the new feature" is a pattern claim. "On launch day, three customers replied within the first hour to say they had been waiting for this for years; one of them, the head of ops at [company], sent a video of his team applauding" is a specific moment. The specific moment, properly chosen, makes the pattern claim believable.

6. **Find the human inside your own institution too.** A founder writing about their company should treat it as a place where specific people work, not as an abstract entity called "we." When the engineering team did something hard, name the engineer. When a customer interaction taught the company something, name the salesperson. The reader registers a company that contains real people.

7. **Resist the urge to elevate the language around the human.** Once you have found the human, the temptation is to surround them with rhetoric. Don't. The human's specific situation is the value. Decorating it with adjectives ("incredible," "passionate," "tireless") cheapens it. Let the facts do the work.

8. **When you have multiple humans, choose ruthlessly.** A piece can hold one or two specific people well. Three becomes confusing; five becomes a list. Pick the most representative, most vivid, or most surprising specific case and use that one. The others can wait for other pieces.

9. **Use the human at the lead.** *Look for the human element.* Often the right opening is not the company's claim, the strategic frame, or the market context. It is the moment a specific person said something specific. The opening is the place where finding-the-human pays back the most.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is writing a Series B fundraising memo. The "market" section currently reads: "The total addressable market for compliance automation in financial services exceeds $12 billion globally, with mid-market and enterprise segments showing accelerating digitisation in response to expanding regulatory complexity."

**Application:** Every word is generic. Replace with: "Our highest-paying customer is a 320-person broker-dealer in Charlotte. Their head of compliance, Linda Brooks, told me last quarter that before us she had four full-time analysts manually copying data between Excel spreadsheets to file quarterly with the SEC. Three of those four are now doing actual compliance work. We charge her firm $180k a year. There are 4,800 broker-dealers like hers in the US." Now the market section makes the same case (the market is large; the value-per-customer is high; the alternative is painful) but through a person.

**Result:** Investors who would have skimmed the original section now ask follow-up questions about Linda Brooks. The conversation moves to specifics. The founder ends up with sharper questions, more meeting time, and at least one investor who will reference Linda by name in their internal memo.

**Situation:** A founder writes a launch blog post for a new product. The current draft describes the technical architecture in detail and the product's capabilities exhaustively. There is no person in the piece.

**Application:** Open with a person. "Tom Wei is the head of platform at a 60-person startup in Austin. Last summer he ran out of patience with our old workflow and emailed me one sentence: 'Either fix the planning view or we're switching to Linear.' We have been trying to fix the planning view for fourteen months. Today's launch is what we shipped." The technical architecture section can stay (interested readers will read it), but it now has a frame.

**Result:** The post gets read by people who would have skipped the technical-architecture version. Tom Wei's company gets credited as a power user (good for them, good for the founder). The product features land harder because they have a problem to be the answer to. The post is shared with the framing "this is how a launch announcement should be written" — because the founder remembered to start with a human.

**Situation:** A founder writes a board update about a missed quarter. The "go-to-market" section currently reads: "Sales velocity has slowed in the enterprise segment, with deal cycle times extending and several anticipated Q3 closes shifting into Q4. We attribute this primarily to elongated procurement cycles and increased scrutiny in the current macro environment."

**Application:** Replace with: "Two enterprise deals slipped from Q3 to Q4 — Mercer Insurance and Alpine Health, together worth $480k of our $5M plan. The Mercer slip is procurement: their CFO froze new vendor commitments for sixty days; we expect close in the first week of November. The Alpine slip is more concerning: our champion, the VP of Engineering, left for another company; we are rebuilding the relationship with her successor, who has not committed to our solution. Of these two, only Mercer is at high probability of closing in Q4." Now the board has facts. They can ask useful questions.

**Result:** The board meeting moves from generic concern about "macro headwinds" to a specific conversation about the Alpine Health relationship and what reinforcement the founder needs from the board's network. One board member knows the new VP of Engineering at Alpine and offers an introduction. The original framing would have produced an unhelpful meeting; the human framing produces an action.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a hiring blog post. The current draft describes the company's mission, values, and the specific role being hired for, in that order. It is well-organised and forgettable.

**Application:** Replace the opening with a specific employee story. "Three years ago I hired Priya Kapoor as our second engineer. She had been a backend lead at a consumer company; she had never built infrastructure before. Twelve weeks in, she rewrote the platform layer that I had built and I was relieved when she did. Today she runs our infrastructure team. I'm writing this because I want to hire someone like her — someone who joins a company before its second platform rewrite and who builds the version that lasts." The role description follows. The mission and values are now load-bearing because they are anchored to a person who lived them.

**Result:** The post attracts engineers who want to be like Priya. The applications self-select for ambition and willingness to operate at a specific career stage. The original generic post would have attracted noise; the human-led post attracts signal.

**Situation:** A founder writes a customer apology email after an outage. The draft says: "We deeply regret the disruption to your operations and are committed to preventing this from recurring through enhanced monitoring and process improvements."

**Application:** Add a person. "On Tuesday at 11:14 AM ET we shipped a config change that took the platform down for 47 minutes. The change was approved by me, and the post-mortem is below. I want to call out our SRE on call, Marcus Olu, who diagnosed the issue in eleven minutes and rolled back the change before our automated systems would have. He saved us another half hour of downtime." Now the apology has both accountability (the founder approved the change) and recognition (a specific human did good work in the response). The customer reads it as truthful rather than templated.

**Result:** Customers reply with appreciation rather than frustration. Marcus reads the email and registers that the founder noticed; team morale rises. The apology, which would have been a routine corporate communication, becomes something the company is remembered for.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use a customer story as a fig leaf for missing analysis.
**Why:** The discipline is to find the human and let them illustrate a real argument. If the analysis is absent, the human becomes a substitute for thinking, not a vehicle for it. "Linda at Mass General loves us, therefore enterprise health care is our market" is not an argument. It is a single data point.

**Don't:** Manufacture humanity by inventing detail.
**Why:** The temptation, given the principle that specific is better than abstract, is to invent specifics where you don't have them. "Sarah, a busy mom in Cincinnati..." in marketing copy is the dead version. Real humans, named, are different from invented composites. Readers, especially sophisticated ones, can tell the difference.

**Don't:** Replace every abstraction with a story.
**Why:** Sometimes the abstract is the right unit. A cohort analysis is about cohorts. A regulatory disclosure is about facts. The discipline is "find the human where finding the human is appropriate" — not "always lead with a person." Misapplied, this principle produces case-study-as-narrative writing where the reader cannot extract any general claim.

**Don't:** Polish customer quotes into corporate-speak.
**Why:** "I love your product more than I love my engineers" is the quote you wanted. "The customer reported high satisfaction with the product relative to alternatives" is what marketing will turn it into. Resist. The roughness, the specific verb, the slightly inappropriate hyperbole are exactly what gives the quote weight.

**Don't:** Use "John, a customer" without a real name when you could have asked.
**Why:** Anonymous quotes carry less weight than named ones. Many founders use placeholder names because they didn't ask permission, or they assumed the customer wouldn't agree. Most customers, asked clearly ("Can I quote you by name in our investor materials?"), will say yes. Asking is free. The named version of every quote is several times more persuasive than the anonymised version.

**Don't:** Hide behind "we" when the human is actually you.
**Why:** "We made a decision to..." is sometimes the right framing. "I made a decision to..." is sometimes the right framing. The founder who always uses "we" is hiding from accountability — which the reader registers, even if they don't articulate it. Find the human; sometimes the human is the founder.

**Don't:** Confuse "specific person" with "celebrity."
**Why:** Founders sometimes assume the human element requires a famous customer. It does not. The most powerful specific people in business writing are the unfamous operators — the head of compliance at a mid-market company, the SRE on call at 4am, the receptionist who asked the question that reframed a strategy. Their unfamousness is part of what makes them believable.
