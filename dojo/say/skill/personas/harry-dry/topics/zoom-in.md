# The Zoom-In

## When to Use
- Any time a line has survived test 2 (falsifiable) and test 3 (unique) but failed test 1 (visualise). You need to get it concrete.
- Any time you're staring at a sentence like "We help businesses grow" and don't know what replaces it.
- Training yourself or a team to stop writing at the abstract level. Even a few rounds of deliberate zoom-in practice retrains the instinct.
- When a founder insists "the copy is fine" but you can't picture what the product actually does after reading the homepage.

## Fails When
- **The abstraction is hiding a genuine product hole.** You zoom in and find nothing concrete to say. That's not a writing problem — that's a product or positioning problem. The zoom-in is diagnostic; if it lands on void, go back to the iceberg.
- **You zoom in on the wrong word.** "Premium experience" has two abstracts. You have to zoom in on both to land somewhere specific — premium against what, experience of what. Pick the nouns and verbs, not the modifiers.
- **You stop one level too early.** The first concrete answer is often still too generic. Keep zooming until the answer is something you could photograph.

## Core Concept

The zoom-in is the mechanical fix for abstraction. Get a sheet of paper. Write "abstract" at the top. "Concrete" at the bottom. Draw a line between them.

Start with your vague sentence at the top. Ask yourself: "What do I actually mean?" Write the answer lower down. Ask again. Keep going until you hit something you can see.

A fitness app. Their tagline: "Regain your fitness." Zoom in. "Regain" — what do you mean? Someone who stopped running. Sat on the couch for six months. "Fitness" — what kind? Running. "Running" — how far? Five kilometres. Bottom of the page: "Couch to 5K." A person on a couch. Same person crossing a finish line. That's visual. Funny enough, it's the name of the most downloaded fitness app ever made.

Sony made a portable cassette player. They could have sold "portable music." Two abstract words. Instead, they showed two kids roller-skating through the park, sharing headphones, wind in their hair, listening to music. Don't sell a Walkman. Sell skating through the park with your boyfriend listening to it. The product disappears. The picture stays.

Apple made a portable media player. 5GB of storage. Nobody knows what 5GB means. "1,000 songs in your pocket." Now you see it. A thousand songs. Your pocket. You feel the weight of your entire music library sitting next to your keys. Steve Jobs understood. Don't sell the spec. Sell the picture the spec creates.

Old Spice. They could say "long-lasting freshness." Every deodorant brand says that. Two words: "SMELL CONFIDENT." Don't sell deodorant. Sell first-date confidence. Sell walking into a room and knowing you belong there. The deodorant is a means. The confidence is the picture.

Patek Philippe. "You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation." Close your eyes. You see a grandfather handing a watch to his son. That son, decades later, handing it to his daughter. They're not selling a watch. They're selling a family tradition.

Here's the pattern. Bad marketers describe features. Good marketers describe outcomes. Great marketers paint pictures of outcomes. "5GB storage" is a feature. "1,000 songs" is an outcome. "1,000 songs in your pocket" is a picture. Each level gets more visual. Each level sticks harder.

When you force yourself to zoom in, something uncomfortable happens. You discover you don't know what you're talking about. A startup's homepage: "We help businesses grow." What does "grow" look like? More revenue? More customers? More employees? Growing from what to what? Long pause. The abstract language was hiding the fact that they hadn't figured out who they were for. The zoom-in is a diagnostic as much as a rewrite technique. It reveals whether you actually know what you're selling.

## How to Apply

1. **Take the abstract line. Write it at the top of a page.**

2. **Identify the abstract noun or verb.** Not the adjectives — the nouns and verbs. "Premium experience" — the noun is experience. "Drive growth" — the verb is drive, the noun is growth. "Innovative solutions" — both are abstract. Circle them.

3. **Ask "what do I actually mean?" for each abstract.** Write the answer lower down the page. Not as a sentence yet — as a fact or image.
   - "Grow" → More customers? More revenue? More repeat purchases? Pick the specific one you actually help with.
   - "Experience" → What do they see, touch, feel, hear, receive? A handwritten note in every box. A confirmation email that arrives in two seconds.
   - "Locally sourced" → Eggs from a specific farm. Milk from a specific dairy.

4. **Ask "what do I actually mean?" again on your answer.** Zoom deeper. Almost every first answer is still too generic.
   - "More customers" → from what source? in what timeframe? with what behaviour?
   - "Handwritten note" → saying what? from whom? on what kind of paper?

5. **Keep going until you hit something you could photograph or drop on your foot.** Most abstractions go three to five levels deep before landing. Don't stop at level one.

6. **Rewrite the line using the concrete bottom of the page.** The line doesn't have to contain every detail — just carry the specificity into the copy. "Eggs from Trevean Farm. Milk from the dairy across the bay. Bread baked today." That's what "locally sourced" actually means. The specifics do the selling.

7. **If you hit void during the zoom-in — no concrete answer exists — stop and diagnose.** The abstraction was hiding a hole. Either the product has nothing concrete to stand on yet (fix the product), or the positioning is generic (fix the positioning), or the team doesn't understand the customer (go talk to customers). Don't force a concrete line where none honestly exists.

## Examples

**Situation:** A coffee shop in St. Ives writes "Locally sourced produce" on the menu.
**Application:** Zoom in. What does "locally sourced" mean here? Eggs from a farm two miles down the road. Milk from a dairy you can see from the window. Bread baked that morning. Rewrite the menu: "Eggs from Trevean Farm. Milk from the dairy across the bay. Bread baked today."
**Result:** The good stuff was hidden behind abstraction the whole time. The zoom-in didn't change the product — it surfaced what the product already was. Customers can now picture the supply chain; that's the brand.

**Situation:** A startup's homepage says "We help businesses grow."
**Application:** Abstract nouns: businesses, grow. Zoom in on "businesses." Which businesses? E-commerce brands doing £100K–£1M a year. Zoom in on "grow." Grow how? Specifically: increase repeat purchase rate. Result: "We help mid-size e-commerce brands turn one-time buyers into repeat customers." Deeper zoom: "We help mid-size e-commerce brands send the right email at the right time — so a customer who bought in April comes back in May."
**Result:** The abstract line had hidden three specific choices: segment, outcome, and mechanism. The zoom-in forced the founder to decide. And the decisions are a real position.

**Situation:** A SaaS homepage reads "Increase operational efficiency."
**Application:** Close eyes — nothing. Abstract nouns: operational efficiency. Zoom in. What operation? Running weekly payroll. Efficiency how? Reducing the time from six hours to thirty minutes. Rewrite: "Weekly payroll in 30 minutes, not 6 hours." Zoom deeper for a sub-head: "All tax codes updated automatically. Approved in one click by the partner. Files directly with HMRC."
**Result:** Generic B2B copy becomes a photograph of a specific workflow a real bookkeeper would recognise. The SaaS homepage now passes all three tests because the zoom-in did the work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Zoom in on adjectives instead of nouns and verbs.
**Why:** "Premium seamless innovative experience" has four abstract modifiers stacked on a core abstract noun (experience). Deleting the adjectives gives you "experience," which is still abstract. The real zoom targets are nouns and verbs. Modifiers rarely survive a zoom-in — they just get cut.

**Don't:** Stop zooming when the answer is only mildly concrete.
**Why:** "We save time" is not concrete. It's mid-abstract. "Weekly payroll in 30 minutes, not 6 hours" is concrete. Go deeper until you could photograph what you're describing. The first concrete answer is usually still too vague.

**Don't:** Invent details during the zoom-in that aren't true.
**Why:** The zoom-in is finding what's real, not inventing what's specific. If you've written "eggs from Trevean Farm" and the eggs are actually from three different farms, the concrete line is a lie. Go find the real specifics — or if there aren't any, the abstraction was hiding a weakness the customer will feel anyway.

**Don't:** Use the zoom-in to produce a list of features.
**Why:** Zoom-in produces pictures and specifics, not bullet points. "We help businesses grow" doesn't zoom to "We offer a CRM, a marketing tool, an analytics dashboard, a reporting engine, and customer support." That's a feature list. Zoom to the one picture that matters for the specific customer you serve.

**Don't:** Confuse zoom-in with making things longer.
**Why:** "1,000 songs in your pocket" is shorter than "portable music player with significant storage." Zoom-in usually makes copy shorter, not longer, because abstraction is padding. The concrete version of three paragraphs of generic benefit is often a single image-rich sentence.
