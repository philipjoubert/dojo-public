# Readability

## When to Use
- Any page designed to be scanned rather than read start-to-finish: landing pages, blog posts, sales decks, newsletters, ads.
- When you've got the substance right (iceberg, three tests, juxtaposition) but the page still feels like work to read.
- When bounce rates are high but the copy is actually good — often a formatting problem, not a writing problem.

## Fails When
- **The content is wrong and you're optimising the layout.** Packing wallpaper into shorter paragraphs gives you shorter wallpaper. Fix the positioning first.
- **The medium expects density.** Literary essays, academic writing, some prestige long-copy ads — the reader has signed up for density. Formatting them like a Twitter thread is a category error.
- **You use formatting tricks to fake clarity you don't have.** Bullet points don't make confused thinking clearer. They just list the confusion.

## Core Concept

If it looks hard to read, nobody reads it. You can have the best copy in the world — perfect positioning, concrete language, falsifiable claims, three yeses across the board — but if it looks like a wall of text, nobody's getting past the first line. Spend 70% of your time on visuals, not copy.

**Short paragraphs feel easy.** The paragraph break is a gift to your reader. Every time you hit enter, you're saying: "You made it. Catch your breath. Now keep going." Long paragraphs say the opposite. "Strap in. This is going to take a while." Most readers aren't strapping in. They're scrolling. They're distracted. They're deciding whether to care. Short paragraphs lower the barrier. One idea per paragraph. 1-3 sentences. That's it. "It looks readable and it is readable." The two are connected.

**Conjunctions as bridges.** But. And. Now. So. These four words carry your reader between paragraphs. Starting a paragraph with a conjunction is fine. More than fine. It's useful. It creates momentum. Each paragraph connects to the next. The reader feels pulled forward. If every paragraph stands alone — island, island, island — the reader can stop anywhere. Nothing's pulling them to the next bit. HEY's website does this brilliantly. "Email started as a treasure." / "But somewhere along the way, it all changed." / "And that's why we built HEY." Each "but" and "and" carries you forward. One paragraph demands the next.

**People scan. They don't read.** Nobody reads your page word for word. They scan. They skim. They jump between headings. They read the first sentence of each section. Maybe the last. Your formatting has to reward that behaviour. Clear headings that tell a story on their own. Bold text on the key phrases. Short paragraphs that can be absorbed in a glance. If someone scans your page in five seconds and gets the gist, your formatting is working. If they scan and come away confused, no amount of clever copy saves you.

**The squint test.** Take your landing page. Don't read the words. Just squint at it. Look at the shape. Big blocks? Problem. Your reader sees the same thing and thinks "this looks hard." Short chunks with space between them? Good. The page looks approachable. "I can do this." That snap judgment happens before anyone reads a word.

**70% visuals.** Most effort goes into images, not text. This sounds backwards — writing about copywriting, words are the thing. But the image-to-text ratio in the best work often exceeds 50%. Visuals aren't decoration. They're the argument. A before/after screenshot proves the transformation. A side-by-side comparison makes the contrast undeniable. A diagram explains what three paragraphs couldn't. The Volkswagen winter checklist (see juxtaposition.md) IS the argument — two columns of checkboxes, the copy just labels them. When Marketing Examples performs best, it's because people can see the example. You don't have to imagine what good copy looks like. Show them. Ask yourself: what can a picture say that words can't? Can you show a screenshot instead of describing a feature? Show a graph instead of listing statistics? Show a before/after instead of explaining the transformation? Design and writing work together. One without the other fails.

**Short paragraphs convert into Twitter threads.** Side benefit. Each paragraph stands alone. Each idea is complete in one or two sentences. That means your newsletter naturally converts into a Twitter thread — each paragraph becomes a tweet. If the ideas don't survive being separated, they're too tangled. If each paragraph works on its own, the structure is right. This isn't just a distribution trick. It's a quality check. Short paragraphs force economy. There's no room for padding. Every word has to earn its place.

**White space is content.** What you leave out matters as much as what you put in. White space isn't wasted space. It's breathing room. It lets ideas land. It gives the eye a place to rest. Crowded pages create confusion — every element fights for attention, nothing wins. Clean pages create focus. One thing at a time. Apple puts a single sentence in massive font surrounded by white space. The formatting says: "This matters." A corporate site crams seventeen bullet points into one section. The formatting says: "We don't know what matters."

**The read-aloud test.** Read your copy out loud. Where do you stumble? Where do you run out of breath? Those are the problems. If you can't say it smoothly, they can't read it smoothly. Writing is designing in words.

## How to Apply

1. **Take your longest paragraph. Break it into three.** Any paragraph longer than 3 sentences almost always contains 3 ideas. Separate them. Each gets air.

2. **Start paragraphs with conjunctions where the flow demands.** "But." "And." "So." "Now." Don't force it everywhere, but wherever one paragraph's meaning depends on the previous one's, make the connection explicit.

3. **Squint at the page.** Literally. Look at the shape, not the words. Blocks? Problem. Chunks? Good.

4. **Count your text-to-image ratio.** If it's 95% text, push toward 50/50. Screenshots, photos, before/after, comparison tables, diagrams. If you can't find a visual for a section, the section might be describing something that should be shown (see show-dont-tell.md).

5. **Use headings as the narrative.** Skim-readers read headings and the first line of each section. Make sure the headings alone tell the story. If someone read only your headings, would they get the argument? If not, rewrite the headings.

6. **Bold the key phrase per paragraph.** Not the whole sentence. Not three words in each sentence. One phrase that carries the paragraph's meaning. Scan-readers catch those.

7. **Read the whole page aloud.** Where you stumble or run out of breath, cut. Your tongue knows when the rhythm is off faster than your eye does.

8. **White space is a design choice, not a default.** Put serious space between distinct sections. Make the headings bigger than you think. A single sentence floating in space on a hero section communicates "this matters" more than three paragraphs in a hero section communicate "lots of good stuff here."

## Examples

**Situation:** A landing page has a 12-sentence hero paragraph explaining the product.
**Application:** Squint test — big block of text. Problem. Break into four short paragraphs, each with one idea. First paragraph: the headline (one line). Second: the differentiator (one line with a conjunction bridge — "But we do X differently"). Third: the proof (one line, a specific number). Fourth: the CTA invitation (one line). Add a product screenshot right of the text.
**Result:** Same substance. Completely different read. Scan-test now takes three seconds to understand. The single 12-sentence paragraph was unread. The four one-liners get read.

**Situation:** A newsletter written in long, literary paragraphs gets low engagement.
**Application:** Check whether the medium demands density. If it's a marketing newsletter being scanned on mobile, no. Break every paragraph to 1-3 sentences. Add "But" and "And" openers between connected thoughts. Add one screenshot or photo per major section. Bold the key phrase per section.
**Result:** Engagement climbs. The substance was fine — the formatting was costing the reader. The rhythm of short paragraphs + conjunctions pulls readers through even long pieces.

**Situation:** A SaaS page describes "integrations" with a list of 12 logos and a paragraph of copy.
**Application:** Replace the paragraph with a screenshot of the integrations actually working — the Slack channel with notifications, the Jira ticket auto-created, the Stripe dashboard synced. A single image shows what the paragraph described. Keep the 12 logos beneath as proof of breadth.
**Result:** The page dropped 200 words and gained visible proof. Integration pages tend to be where copy fills for lack of imagination; a single demonstration is usually enough.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Break paragraphs for the sake of breaking paragraphs.
**Why:** Some thoughts need two connected sentences to land. Single-sentence paragraphs stacked indefinitely read as staccato and artificial. Rhythm alternates short and slightly longer. The rule is "one idea per paragraph," not "one sentence per paragraph."

**Don't:** Add visuals that don't earn their place.
**Why:** Stock photos of "team meetings" or "diverse smiling faces" push the text-to-image ratio toward 50/50 without doing any work. A good visual argues. A bad visual fills space. If the visual doesn't replace copy or add proof, cut it — empty space is better than decoration.

**Don't:** Bold everything.
**Why:** If five words per paragraph are bold, nothing is emphasised. The scan-reader's eye skips bold as "wallpaper" once there's too much of it. One bolded phrase per paragraph is the ceiling.

**Don't:** Use formatting to mask thin thinking.
**Why:** A thin argument reformatted into bullets is still thin. "We're fast, easy, and reliable" as three bullets is still "We're fast, easy, and reliable." Formatting amplifies content. Good formatting on weak content amplifies the weakness.

**Don't:** Assume one layout works everywhere.
**Why:** A landing page, a newsletter, a Twitter thread, a sales deck, a mobile ad — same substance, different containers. Each medium has its own reading pattern. Design for the medium, not the copy. A paragraph that works in a newsletter will feel too long in an ad and too short in a long-copy sales letter.
