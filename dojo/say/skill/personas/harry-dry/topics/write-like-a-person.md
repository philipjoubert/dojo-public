# Write Like a Person

## When to Use
- Any copy meant to sound like a human wrote it. Which is almost all marketing copy, including yours.
- Rewriting corporate sludge back into something you'd actually say.
- Establishing a founder's voice on the homepage, in emails, in social posts — the voice that makes readers feel they're in a conversation with a person, not a brand.
- Fixing copy that's been through a committee and come out flat.

## Fails When
- **The context genuinely demands formality.** Terms of service. Regulatory filings. Academic writing. Medical claims. The rules here bend for a reason. Still — most "we need formal copy" claims are fake. Test before accepting.
- **You confuse casual with sloppy.** "Yeah so we do stuff and it's pretty good I guess" sounds conversational. It's also doing no work. Human ≠ lazy. A real salesperson is focused and specific, not rambling (see doorstep-test.md).
- **The register clashes with the customer.** Writing breezy and casual to a procurement officer buying a £1.2M enterprise contract might not land. Match the dog (see talk-to-the-dog.md). Human doesn't always mean breezy.

## Core Concept

Writing and speaking use the same words. But most companies write like no one would ever speak. Passive voice. Polysyllabic Latinate verbs. Sentences that run for three lines without breathing. Third-person corporate voice: "The company's commitment to excellence is reflected in our unwavering focus on customer-centric innovation."

Nobody talks like that. Not in a pub. Not at a dinner table. Not at a doorstep. The words on a page should sound like the words in a person's mouth.

You are alive in inverse proportion to the density of clichés in your writing. That's Nassim Taleb. Substitute "clichés" with "corporate tics" and you've got the rule. Every contraction you strip out, every active verb you replace with a nominalisation, every conjunction you refuse to start a sentence with — each one drains a little life out of the page.

**Here's how to make copy sound like a person wrote it.**

**1. Load up on personal pronouns.** "You" and "your" make people lean in. "You're not going to believe what happens next" is a sentence you read. "Customers will be surprised by the following development" is a sentence you skip. Every sentence without a "you" in it is a missed chance. Not literally every sentence — but the ratio matters. Look at your homepage. Count the "you"s. Count the "we"s. If "we" is winning, you're writing about yourself, not to the reader.

**2. Use contractions.** "You're" not "you are." "We've" not "we have." "Don't" not "do not." Only academics write "you are." Lawyers write "do not." The rest of us speak in contractions, every single day. Writing without them sounds artificial the moment anyone reads it aloud. "We are committed to delivering value" vs "We're committed to delivering value." Two letters, one apostrophe — completely different feel.

**3. Start sentences with conjunctions.** "And." "But." "So." "Now." Your primary-school teacher told you not to. Your primary-school teacher was wrong. Starting a sentence with a conjunction is how real speech flows. It connects ideas. It creates momentum. "We built this. But then we realised the users wanted something different. So we rebuilt it." Three short sentences, chained by conjunctions, feel like someone talking to you.

**4. Use parentheses for personality.** Like the asides you'd add in a conversation. (You know the kind — the thing you'd slip in with a slight smile.) Parentheses let you do two voices on the page. The main line does the argument. The aside does the human-being-behind-the-words. Use them sparingly. Over-used they become tics.

**5. Don't worry about grammar.** If breaking the rules makes you sound human, break them. Sentence fragments. Like this. They work because they mimic speech. Nobody speaks in perfect sentences with main clauses. Nobody needs to read them either. The rule is readability and voice. Everything else is optional. (Though spelling still matters — typos still look like mistakes, not personality.)

**6. Ditch the thesaurus.** You're not impressing anyone. "Utilise" doesn't sound smarter than "use." It sounds insecure. Long words don't confer authority — they confer distance. The best copywriters use the smallest words that do the job. "Because" beats "due to the fact that." "Now" beats "at this juncture." "Help" beats "facilitate." Every three-syllable Latin-root verb is a tell. You swapped a real word for a posh one because you didn't trust the real word.

**7. Use your customers' words.** The easiest way to get the tone right. Grab phrases from support tickets. From sales call transcripts. From the review section. From Slack. Whatever your customers actually say, put that on the page. If three customers described the problem as "my inbox is an absolute mess," write "your inbox is an absolute mess." You don't need to invent a voice. Your customer already speaks in one — borrow it.

**8. Read it aloud at the kitchen table.** If your partner cringes, rewrite it. This is the test. Not the squint test. Not the SEO test. The kitchen-table test. Because writing that doesn't sound like speech never sounds like anything. You wrote words that live on a page. Fine — but they'll be read by a person with an internal voice that is still a voice. If the prose doesn't sound like it could ever exit a mouth, it doesn't sound like anything worth listening to.

**The Troy McClure test.** Read your copy in Troy McClure's voice. The washed-up actor from The Simpsons: "Hi, I'm Troy McClure. You may remember me from such self-help videos as 'Smoke Yourself Thin.'" If your line works in his voice — if it sounds perfectly natural coming out of his mouth — it's too phony. "We're passionate about delivering innovative solutions that empower your team." That's a Troy McClure line. It sells. "We answer support tickets in 4 hours." Troy McClure can't say that. It's too specific. Too real. Too human. If Troy can sell it, rewrite it.

**Your enemy is the passive voice.** "Value is delivered by our team to our customers." Nobody talks like that. Write it active: "Our team delivers value to customers." Better — cut the Latin: "Our team does the work." Better still — make it concrete: "We answer tickets in four hours." Every passive construction is a place where you took the human out. Put them back in.

**The real-person test.** For every sentence you write, ask: would a real person say this? Not to a customer. Not in an ad. To their mate. Over a pint. If no, cut. If yes, keep. "Stop drowning in emails" — a real person says that. "Achieve email efficiency" — nobody says that, ever, in the history of speech. The difference is the life signal. One sentence has a pulse. The other's been embalmed.

**Why corporate copy exists in the first place.** Not laziness. Fear. Specific copy can be argued with. "We ship orders same-day before 2pm" is a fact someone has to verify. Legal steps in. Operations weighs in. "Fast shipping" sails through review. Every round of corporate review pushes writing toward the blandest, safest, most unfalsifiable version. The writer didn't fail. The approval process did. The fix isn't better writers. It's braver reviewers — or a smaller review process that doesn't sand off every edge.

**Basecamp's tone is one person.** Read any Basecamp page. Any email. Any blog post. It sounds like Jason Fried wrote it, because Jason Fried wrote it. (Or David. Or someone who speaks in their register.) One person. One voice. One point of view. Not "our brand believes that…" — "I think that…" or "We think that…" It's easier to write like a person when you let a person write.

**Compare two lines.**

*Corporate:* "Our platform leverages advanced machine learning to deliver personalised recommendations that enhance user engagement."

*Human:* "We learn what you like. Then we show you more of it."

Same claim. Same technology. One was written for an investor pitch deck, then leaked onto the homepage. The other was written for a person who might actually use the thing. Read both out loud. One feels like a press release. The other feels like a sentence.

**You don't need to persuade. Let the reader be persuaded.** Over-selling kills voice fastest of all. "Groundbreaking, revolutionary, game-changing, industry-leading." Stacking adjectives is what you do when you don't trust the product to speak for itself. Real voice doesn't beg. It states. "We cut your tax prep from six hours to forty minutes." That's persuasion. Because it's a fact. The reader persuades themselves.

**The opposite of a voice is a brand.** A voice sounds like one person. A "brand voice" document, written by committee, ends up sounding like no one. You don't have to choose: the voice IS the brand. Marketing Examples sounds like Harry because Harry wrote it. HEY sounds like Jason. Innocent drinks sound like Richard and Adam. The move isn't to manufacture a voice. It's to let a human show up on the page.

## How to Apply

1. **Read every line aloud.** Seriously, out loud. Where you stumble — that's a problem. Where you run out of breath — that's a sentence to cut in half. Where you cringe — rewrite. Your tongue knows more about rhythm and tone than your eye does.

2. **Find every "you are" and change to "you're."** Do a find-replace. Every one. Same with "we have," "it is," "cannot," "do not," "I am." The contractions are cheap wins — they do immediate work.

3. **Count the "we"s and the "you"s on your page.** If "we" is winning, the page is about you. Flip it. "Our platform delivers X" → "You get X." "We've built Y" → "Your team does Y without Z." The grammatical subject shifts from your company to the reader's world.

4. **Underline every Latin-root polysyllable and replace with a plain word.** "Utilise" → "use." "Facilitate" → "help." "Commence" → "start." "Demonstrate" → "show." "Implement" → "do" (or cut). The Latin ones are the ones that sound impressive and read dead.

5. **Kill the passive voice.** Find every "is … by," "was … by," "being … by" construction. Rewrite so the human is doing the verb. Active voice is how people talk.

6. **Steal five customer phrases.** Go to your CRM / reviews / support transcripts. Grab five phrases customers use when they describe the problem or the outcome. Put those phrases on the page, word for word. Your voice matches your customer's voice.

7. **Cut hedge words.** "In most cases." "Generally speaking." "It's worth noting that." "Arguably." "Perhaps." Every hedge is fear on the page. If the claim is real, state it. If it's not real, delete it. (See shorter-is-better.md.)

8. **Start one paragraph in three with a conjunction.** And. But. So. Now. Don't force it — let the meaning demand it. Connected paragraphs pull the reader forward. Isolated ones let them stop (see readability.md).

9. **Sign your work.** Put the founder's name on the page. The photo. The first-person "I." "I'm Philip. I built this because I was tired of X." People buy from people, not logos (see individual-over-institution.md). The voice of a person on a page beats the voice of an institution every time.

10. **Run the Troy McClure test.** Imagine Troy reading your copy. If it works in his voice, it's too phony. Rewrite. Troy can sell any generic line. He can't sell a specific one.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS about page: "Founded in 2019, our organisation is committed to revolutionising the way modern enterprises leverage data to drive strategic outcomes through our innovative, cloud-native platform."
**Application:** Thirty-word Latin bomb. Count the sins. Passive voice. "Our organisation" instead of "we." "Revolutionising" (Troy McClure loves it). "Leverage." "Innovative." "Cloud-native." Rewrite, human voice: "We built this in 2019. Our customers were losing three days a month fighting their own data. Now they're not." Twenty-three words, three short sentences, real customer outcome, first person, contractions, zero Latin.
**Result:** The first version was approved by a committee and read by nobody. The second version sounds like a founder. Funnel metric changes, because the page starts getting read to the bottom.

**Situation:** A welcome email: "Thank you for joining our community. We are thrilled to have you on board. At [Company], we are committed to delivering exceptional value. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions."
**Application:** Four Troy McClure lines in a row. "Thrilled to have you" is what you write when you have nothing to say. Nobody is thrilled. Nobody is really "on board." Rewrite, human: "Hey — you just signed up. Thanks. Here's what happens next: I'll send you one email a week with one specific thing to try. If you hate it, reply 'stop' and I'll delete you from the list. If you like it, tell your friends. That's the whole deal. — Philip"
**Result:** Same information, completely different voice. Contractions (you're / I'll). First-person signed. Concrete offer (one email, one tip). An escape hatch ("reply stop"). Signature with a real name. The original sounds like an auto-responder. The rewrite sounds like a person.

**Situation:** An agency's services page: "We provide end-to-end marketing solutions designed to help ambitious brands achieve sustainable growth through data-driven strategies and creative excellence."
**Application:** Every word is abstract or hedge. "End-to-end." "Solutions." "Ambitious brands." "Sustainable growth." "Data-driven." "Creative excellence." Troy McClure's reading it at full volume. Ask: what do you actually do for customers? If the honest answer is "we run their paid search and write their landing pages," write that. Rewrite, human: "We run your Google ads and we rewrite your landing pages. That's it. No retainer. You pay per project. Our last five clients doubled signups inside a quarter."
**Result:** A real offer, a real price-shape, real proof. Specific enough to be refused. Specific enough to be bought. The abstract version attracts nobody because it means nothing.

**Situation:** A founder's investor-update email, written in the corporate "we" voice, gets no replies.
**Application:** The "we" voice kills personal updates. Rewrite first person. From: "Q3 was a strong quarter for the company. We achieved key milestones including the launch of Feature X." To: "Q3 felt hard from the inside, but the numbers are good. I shipped Feature X in August. Three customers switched plans because of it. I'm attaching a graph of the MRR jump — it's bigger than I expected."
**Result:** Investors respond to updates that sound like a founder talking. The "we" version sounds like a press release. The "I" version invites replies. Investors want to know what the founder actually thinks, not what the PR department approved them to say.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "passionate" as your voice strategy.
**Why:** "We're passionate about X" is the purest Troy McClure line in the language. Nobody knows what it means. Everyone uses it. It signals the writer had nothing else to say. Delete every "passionate about" from your site. The page gets better instantly.

**Don't:** Replace "corporate" with "quirky."
**Why:** Swapping buzzwords for emoji and silly jokes isn't voice — it's a different kind of performance. Real voice is quieter. Calm. Specific. A single well-placed aside beats ten forced jokes. If the "personality" feels bolted on, it is. The fix isn't quirky. The fix is honest.

**Don't:** Demand that every writer adopt the same house voice.
**Why:** A house voice that flattens individual writers produces copy that sounds like no one. Let writers keep their rhythm. Unify the positioning and the claims — let the sentences breathe. (See individual-over-institution.md.) One strong personal voice per page beats one committee-approved brand voice across everything.

**Don't:** Mistake "professional" for "good."
**Why:** "Professional" is usually a warning sign. Teams use it to justify cutting personality. "We need to sound more professional" almost always means "we need to sound more like the faceless competitors the customer is trying to escape." Professional is a trap. Human is the move.

**Don't:** Write for the review cycle.
**Why:** When you know legal and comms and product marketing all have to sign off, you pre-sand the copy before they even see it. The result is "in most cases, our platform may contribute to improved outcomes." Write the honest version first. Defend it through the cycle. Expect to lose some specificity. Don't pre-sand.

**Don't:** Use "I" and "we" interchangeably in the same piece.
**Why:** If it's a founder's note, first-person singular — "I built this because…" If it's a company page, first-person plural — "we built this because…" Mixing the two mid-paragraph reads as confusion about whose voice is on the page. Pick. Stay there.

**Don't:** Fake a British voice, a founder voice, or anyone else's voice because it sounds good.
**Why:** Voice is taste plus honesty, not accent. Harry's British directness works because Harry is British and direct. Imitating it produces pastiche. Find your own register — the words you'd actually use, the rhythm you actually have. Your voice is the residue of what you've read and who you are. Let it be yours, not a copy.

**Don't:** Confuse reading aloud with reading for grammar.
**Why:** Some human writing is technically ungrammatical. "You. Me. A pizza. Tuesday?" is four sentence fragments and it works because it sounds like a text to a friend. If your editor "fixes" it to "Would you be available to meet for pizza on Tuesday?" the voice is dead. Read aloud for rhythm and realness, not for grammar correctness. (Keep spelling tight, though.)
