# Shorter Is Better

## When to Use
- Any headline, tagline, subhead, CTA, ad, or anything else that has to land in under two seconds. Billboards, social ads, and mobile heroes get half a Mississippi.
- Diagnostic cutting: "Can I remove this word? Does the meaning survive? Cut it." Applied ruthlessly, line by line.
- When you catch yourself hedging — "in most cases," "generally speaking," "it's worth noting." Every hedge is a tax on the reader's attention and a tell that you're scared of the claim.

## Fails When
- **High-consideration long-form genuinely needs the length.** HEY's landing page is long because they're selling a philosophy. Patek Philippe ads run long because the reader wants reassurance on a watch that costs more than most cars. The rule isn't "always be short." The rule is "earn every word."
- **You cut to a pithy line and lose the claim.** Shorter isn't always better if shorter means vaguer. "Fast accounting software" is shorter than "Accounting for freelance photographers — finally, invoicing that makes sense" and worse. Short only wins when the meaning survives.
- **The reason the copy is long is the thinking is unclear.** Cutting won't fix it. Writing "we do X and also Y and also Z" and cutting to "we do X, Y, Z" just makes the list shorter. Pick one and kill the and's.

## Core Concept

Sometimes the best copy to sell a horse is "Horse for sale."

Current favourite example of copywriting: "The VPN that just works." Five words. No clever wordplay. No creative twist. It just says exactly what matters. Most VPNs are confusing. This one works. That's the whole pitch.

People overcomplicate copy constantly. They think good writing means impressive writing. Long words. Complex sentences. Sophisticated-sounding phrases. It doesn't. Good writing means clear writing. And clear usually means short.

**When "and" signals failure.** Look at your headline. Is "and" in it? If so, you probably haven't decided what you're selling. "And" on a landing page is almost never good. It signals hedging. Trying to appeal to everyone. Afraid to pick one thing. Walking through Tesco once, spotted this on a chocolate bar: "Dairy Milk and more." And more. That's the tagline. Because nothing screams conviction quite like "and more." Every benefit you add divides your reader's attention. One strong claim beats three weak ones. Every time. Apple could have said the iPod was "a portable music player with large storage capacity and intuitive navigation and sleek design and long battery life." They said: "1,000 songs in your pocket." One claim. One image. One thing to remember.

**The one-Mississippi test.** Show someone your ad. One Mississippi, two Mississippi. Do they get it? If yes, you're fine. If they're still squinting after two Mississippis, cut it down. "The VPN that just works." One Mississippi. Done. "The comprehensive VPN solution that provides seamless connectivity and enhanced security for modern professionals." Eight Mississippis. Lost them. Matters even more for billboards, social ads, and anything passing in the corner of your eye. You don't get one Mississippi. You get half.

**Three words on a poster.** "Labour Isn't Working." Three words. A queue of unemployed people stretching across the poster. It helped win a general election. Three words and one image told you everything. John Hegarty: "If the French can inspire a revolution with three words, why should you need any more to sell soap powder?" Liberté. Égalité. Fraternité. Three words. Changed a country. Your landing page headline doesn't need 14 words. It probably needs five.

**Light. Years ahead.** Apple wrote an ad for the MacBook. Two words, split by a full stop. "Light. Years ahead." Two words. Two ideas. Light — as in weight. The laptop is light. Light years ahead — as in the competition isn't close. One headline. Two benefits. Three words. The full stop does the heavy lifting. It splits the meaning in half, and your brain reads both. When every word is earning its place, each one carries more meaning.

**Why we write long.** Fear. Fear that we haven't explained enough. Fear someone will misunderstand. So we add qualifiers. "In most cases." "Generally speaking." "It's worth noting that." All of it padding. All of it fear. Your reader knows "double your conversions" doesn't mean literally every person. They know "4-hour response" is an average. You don't have to explain everything. Just say the thing. Confident copy is short. Confident people don't hedge. "We have pretty good customer service." Pretty good? So not great. "We respond within 4 hours." Now I believe you. The hedge words don't protect you. They undermine you.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the long version first.** Every first draft is too long. That's fine. That's how writing works. Don't try to write short on the first pass. You'll cramp.

2. **Go through sentence by sentence. Can I remove this word? Does the meaning survive? Cut it.** Standard cuts:
   - "In order to" → "to."
   - "Due to the fact that" → "because."
   - "At this point in time" → "now."
   - "It's worth noting that" → [delete].
   - "Generally speaking" → [delete].
   - "In most cases" → [delete].
   - "The company that…" → [relative clause becomes an aside or cut].

3. **Can I remove this sentence? Does the paragraph still work? Cut it.** Most paragraphs have one sentence doing the work and two repeating. Find the working one. Keep it.

4. **Can I remove this paragraph? Does the piece still make sense? Cut it.** First paragraphs on websites are usually throat-clearing — a generic intro before the real claim. Delete them.

5. **Kill the and's.** Every "and" in a headline is a decision you didn't make. Pick one side. If you truly need both, use a full stop — "Light. Years ahead" — which treats them as two ideas rather than one bloated one.

6. **Run the one-Mississippi test.** Show someone your headline. Count. If they haven't got it by two Mississippis, cut or rewrite. For billboards and ads, half a Mississippi.

7. **Kill hedge words.** "Pretty good" → "good" or cut. "Reasonably fast" → "fast" or a specific number. "Fairly simple" → "simple" or a three-step demonstration. Every qualifier is fear. Confidence is a voice, not a disclaimer.

8. **Count words on the headline.** More than ten? Cut. More than seven? Probably still cut. More than five? Only if every word is earning its keep.

9. **Long is fine when it's earned.** The test isn't length. The test is: if I removed this sentence, would anyone notice? If yes, it earns its place. If no, cut it. HEY and Patek Philippe and Basecamp's manifesto can be long because every line works. A five-line homepage can be rubbish if every line is padding.

## Examples

**Situation:** Homepage hero: "We help businesses improve their conversion rates through data-driven optimisation and testing."
**Application:** Ten words doing the work of three. Kill "help," "their," "through," "data-driven." Land on: "Double your conversions." Three words. Concrete outcome. Falsifiable if you have the data. Still passes test 3 if the number is real for your customers.
**Result:** Ten words to three, meaning intact. Mid-fund hedge language ("help" softens; "data-driven" adds no info) replaced with a direct promise. If you can back "double" with proof, the short version is strictly better.

**Situation:** A SaaS tagline: "Empowering teams to collaborate more effectively and build better products faster."
**Application:** Count the and's — two. Count the hedge-ish verbs — "empowering," "effectively," "better," "faster." All generic modifiers. Pick ONE thing the product is actually best at. Cut everything else. If it's speed of shipping: "Ship features in hours, not sprints." If it's collaboration quality: "Your whole team reviews every line before it ships." Pick one, commit.
**Result:** The long version was trying to own collaboration AND velocity AND quality. Picking one makes the message memorable; keeping all three made it invisible.

**Situation:** An ad CTA: "Click here to learn more about our service and sign up for a free trial to see for yourself."
**Application:** Cut. "Click here to learn more" is throat-clearing. "See for yourself" is filler. Land on: "Start your free trial." Four words, one action. Or even shorter: "Try it free." Three words.
**Result:** Shorter CTAs often convert better because they reduce cognitive load. The reader already knows what clicking means. You don't have to explain the mechanics of clicking.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Cut words until the meaning breaks.
**Why:** "Accounting for freelance photographers" is specific enough to position a product. "Accounting" is too short — the specificity is the positioning. Shorter isn't an end in itself. Meaning survives or you've cut too much.

**Don't:** Cut the proof in the name of brevity.
**Why:** Numbers, customer quotes, screenshots, specific claims — these take words. That's fine. A short line followed by a short concrete proof is stronger than a short line alone. HEY's manifesto isn't long because HEY is wordy; it's long because selling a philosophy against Gmail requires arguments, not soundbites.

**Don't:** Replace all long sentences with staccato.
**Why:** Short for its own sake gets punchy fast and tiring faster. A well-placed long sentence lands — especially when it lives next to short ones. Rhythm matters. All-short copy reads like a children's book. Vary length; trim padding.

**Don't:** Delete the "and" without making the decision the "and" was hiding.
**Why:** "Project management and team communication and file sharing" cut to "Project management" changes the product's positioning — maybe correctly, maybe not. Kill the "and" only after you've picked which side to commit to. Don't cut the words without deciding.

**Don't:** Confuse "short" with "clever."
**Why:** "Horse for sale" is short because it's clear, not because it's clever. Clever short (wordplay, pun, cryptic reference) often takes longer to parse than long-but-clear copy. The goal is clarity in the fewest words, not minimum words regardless of meaning.
