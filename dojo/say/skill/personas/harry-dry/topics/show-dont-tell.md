# Show, Don't Tell

## When to Use
- Any claim that could be replaced with a demonstration. "Great customer service" → "We respond to every support ticket within 4 hours." "Fast delivery" → "Ordered at noon, arrives by five."
- Moments in a landing page or ad where one image or one stat would do what three paragraphs of adjectives currently attempt.
- When the product has a natural demonstration moment — the Jobs-manila-envelope moment — that you haven't found yet.

## Fails When
- **The demonstration needs explanation to land.** A demonstration that requires a paragraph of setup isn't a demonstration anymore, it's an argument with a picture attached. If it needs explaining, keep looking for a better one.
- **There's no honest evidence.** Show-don't-tell is a copywriting move only when the proof exists. If you don't have it, go get it — run the survey, measure the metric, ship to the customer — before writing the line. You can't show what isn't there.
- **The demonstration is visual but not undeniable.** A stock-photo of a "happy customer" isn't a demonstration. A screenshot of a real Slack message from a named customer is. The distinction is whether the image stands up on its own.

## Core Concept

Don't talk. Only point.

Let's say you're trying to sell gold. You could tell someone it's a great investment. Explain the fundamentals. Quote the analysts. Talk about inflation hedging and portfolio diversification. Or you could point at the graph of gold for the last 50 years going one way. Point at what happened during the last financial crash — gold went up while everything else went down. Point at Warren Buffett's portfolio and ask: "How much gold does Warren Buffett hold?" Zero. Same argument. One version is you talking. The other is you pointing at evidence and shutting up. Which one does the reader trust more?

**The manila envelope.** Steve Jobs could have said the MacBook Air was thin. Put up a spec slide. "40 millimetres." Instead, he pulled a manila envelope from his jacket. Opened it. Slid the laptop out. That's not a presentation. That's a demonstration. 40 millimetres means nothing. A laptop sliding out of an envelope means everything. He didn't describe thinness. He proved it. In front of a live audience. With a paper envelope.

Every product has a demonstration moment. The one action that makes your value undeniable. For Jobs, it was the envelope. For a fast-loading website, it's the stopwatch. For a weight-loss programme, it's the before-and-after photo. For IKEA, it's the Allen key, the flat pack, and the finished bookshelf — three images, no words. For Blendtec, it's dropping an iPhone in and pressing blend ("Will It Blend?" got 300 million views). The demonstration IS the copy.

**Run your tongue across your teeth.** Pepsodent ran an ad in the early 1900s. "Just run your tongue across your teeth. You'll feel a film — that's what makes your teeth look 'off colour' and invites decay." Read that again. They didn't describe the problem. They made you discover it yourself. Right now, reading that sentence, you ran your tongue across your teeth. You couldn't help it. And you felt something. That slightly gritty texture. Pepsodent didn't tell you that you had a problem. They pointed at your own mouth and let you find it. Telling says "plaque is bad." Showing says "feel your teeth right now." One is information. The other is experience. Experience wins every time.

**Adjectives are claims. Evidence is proof.** Every adjective is a claim. Every piece of evidence is a proof. Claims need defending. Proofs stand on their own. "We have great customer service" — that's a claim. "We respond to every support ticket within 4 hours" — that's a proof. It stands on its own.

**Leaving a gap.** Sometimes showing is about leaving room. BIC's lighter ad: Martha Stewart and Snoop Dogg. Side by side. Copy: "Perfect for candles... and more!" That's the whole ad. BIC never says what Snoop Dogg uses the lighter for. They don't have to. The implication does the work. The reader's imagination does the last 20%. That participation creates memory. The best jokes don't explain the punchline. The best ads don't either.

**Evidence requires confidence.** Here's why most companies don't do this. Pointing at evidence is scary. It puts you on the hook. If you say "0.4% unsubscribes" and your unsubscribes go up to 2%, you look foolish. If you say "New York City chef" and she moves to Jersey, you have to update your copy. Adjectives are safe. "Premium" can mean whatever you want. "Leading" is unfalsifiable. You can't be proven wrong because you haven't said anything. But that safety is exactly what makes adjectives weak. Readers sense the hedge. They know you're not committing. When you point at evidence — real numbers, real demonstrations, real proof — you're communicating confidence.

## How to Apply

1. **Circle every adjective on the page.** For each one, ask: what evidence could replace this?
   - "Great customer service" → response time metric.
   - "Fast results" → specific timeframe.
   - "Easy to use" → screenshot of someone using it in three clicks.

2. **Find the manila envelope moment.** What's the one demonstration that makes your value undeniable in one action? A screenshot. A number. A before/after. A live demo. A physical demonstration. Keep hunting until you find it. Every product has one.

3. **Test the demonstration for three things.**
   - **Simple:** If it needs explanation, keep looking.
   - **Visual:** If you can't show it, it doesn't count.
   - **Undeniable:** When people see it, they get it immediately.

4. **Show instead of stating in three moves.**
   - **Replace adjectives with numbers.** "Tasty" → "All ingredients you would eat." "Low unsubscribes" → "0.4% unsubscribe rate." "Fast" → "Ships before noon, arrives same day."
   - **Replace descriptions with screenshots or photos.** A customer Slack message. A response-time graph. A before/after photo. A real dashboard.
   - **Replace arguments with evidence.** Warren Buffett's portfolio. The Rolls-Royce electric clock. The manila envelope.

5. **Leave a gap where you can.** Don't explain the punchline. "Perfect for candles... and more!" Two Snoop Dogg words and the reader does the rest. Trust the reader.

6. **If the evidence doesn't exist, go get it before writing the line.** Run the deployment survey. Measure the response times. Ask a customer to send a real quote. Photograph the thing. Don't write a demonstration that isn't there.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS homepage claims "We have great customer service."
**Application:** That's a claim. Find the evidence. Their actual support response time: median 4 hours, 95th percentile under 9 hours. Rewrite: "We respond to every support ticket within 4 hours. Median. Not average." Below it, a screenshot of their Intercom dashboard showing the live queue.
**Result:** "Great customer service" was unverifiable. The new line is a proof. A prospect who cares can check. The dashboard screenshot is the manila envelope — it doesn't need explaining.

**Situation:** An eco-friendly laundry product claims "Powerful cleaning that's gentle on the planet."
**Application:** Two adjectives stacked. Zoom to evidence. Their internal stain-removal testing vs a market leader. A side-by-side photo: identical wine stains, 30-minute soak, both products. One stain visibly out, one visibly in. Line under the photo: "Same wine. Same 30 minutes. Different result." The planet-friendly claim gets its own proof: ingredient list next to a competitor's — fewer chemicals, named substances you recognise, nothing you don't.
**Result:** Two claims, two demonstrations, zero adjectives. Each proof can be scrutinised. The confidence of pointing is the brand.

**Situation:** A recruitment agency's homepage: "We place top talent with leading companies."
**Application:** Full sentence of wallpaper. Evidence-first rewrite. "In the last year we placed 47 senior engineers at YC-backed startups that went on to raise Series A within 12 months. Average time from intro to signed offer: 19 days." The demonstration is the numbers: 47, Series A, 19 days. All falsifiable. All specific. All unsignable by a generic recruiter.
**Result:** The agency went from indistinguishable to specific in one rewrite. The evidence was always there — buried under platitudes. Showing surfaces it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use stock photos as "show."
**Why:** A stock photo of people shaking hands or laughing around a laptop isn't a demonstration. It's decoration. Real demonstrations are real — a screenshot of real software, a photo of a real customer's real result, a video of the real product working. Stock photos signal "we have nothing to show," which is worse than not trying.

**Don't:** Overload one demonstration with too much to prove.
**Why:** One demonstration, one claim. The manila envelope proved "thin." It didn't also try to prove "fast" and "beautiful" and "affordable." If you're trying to prove five things with one demonstration, you'll prove nothing. Separate demonstrations for separate claims.

**Don't:** Replace adjectives with invented numbers.
**Why:** Numbers only work when they're real. "3x faster" with nothing behind it is worse than "faster" because now you've lied specifically, not vaguely. If the number isn't measured, don't use it. Go measure it. Then use it.

**Don't:** Explain the demonstration after you've shown it.
**Why:** A good demonstration doesn't need a caption that says "this proves the product is fast." The demonstration IS the proof. Explaining kills the moment — like explaining a joke. Trust the visual. If you feel you must explain, the demonstration isn't strong enough; find a better one.

**Don't:** Skip the demonstration because you think it makes the page "too busy."
**Why:** A page full of adjectives is busier than a page full of proofs because the reader has to do twice the work to decide whether to believe you. Well-designed demonstrations are less cognitive load, not more. Trade paragraphs for screenshots and the page often becomes cleaner.
