# Reframing

## When to Use
- Taking a fact the customer won't care about as stated and finding the frame that makes them care.
- Pricing copy. Costs don't exist in a vacuum — the frame around them determines how they feel.
- Translating specs into life. "24g protein" means nothing. "More than four eggs" means something.
- Reframing the category name, the pain, the objection — anywhere the reader's default framing fights you, change the framing rather than the argument.

## Fails When
- **The reframe is dishonest.** Comparing your price to "what most people spend on lattes in a year" when the comparison population is wildly different is a cheat that customers notice once they think about it.
- **The reframe is further from reality than the original fact.** "Enough carbon savings to cover Manhattan with trees" is visual but if the original fact is "we saved 100 tonnes of CO2," the stretch may feel fabricated.
- **You reframe without the original fact visible.** The reframe works because the reader can check — "450,000 cars" means something because they can double-check. Hiding the original makes the reframe marketing, not proof.

## Core Concept

Same fact, presented differently, can be invisible or unforgettable. Your job is finding the right frame. The information doesn't change. The presentation does. And suddenly it matters.

**Translate the stat.** Big numbers are meaningless. Your brain can't picture 177 billion pounds of plastic — it's just noise. Liquid Death reframed it: "Plastic pollution will outweigh fish in the sea pound for pound by 2050." Now it's a race. Plastic versus fish. And plastic is winning. The image sticks because it replaces a number with a picture. "10 million trees planted" means nothing. "Enough trees to cover Manhattan" means something. "Carbon savings" means nothing. "Carbon savings equal to 450,000 fewer cars on the road per year" means something. Find the comparison that makes your number visual. Quick exercise — take any stat. Finish this sentence: "That's the same as ___." Whatever fills the blank is your reframe. If nothing comes to mind, the stat might not be worth sharing.

**Reframe the cost.** A diamond engagement ring costs thousands. De Beers knew they couldn't argue it was cheap. So they reframed the cost: "How can you make two months' salary last forever?" The price isn't gone. It's recontextualised. Two months' salary for something that lasts forever. Suddenly the money feels proportionate. Blinkist: "4,500+ nonfiction books for the price of 3 lattes a month." The subscription costs whatever it costs. But compared to three coffees? Nothing. People don't evaluate prices in a vacuum — they compare. Your job is to choose what they compare to. Compare to something expensive and your product feels like a bargain. Compare to something trivial and the price disappears.

**Feature to outcome.** Nobody cares about your feature. They care about what it does for them. Huel has 24 grams of protein per serving. Fine. So what? Reframe: "More protein than 4 eggs." Now I understand. The protein number became something I can picture at my breakfast table. The "so what?" test. State the feature. Ask "so what?" Keep asking until you land on something a human being cares about. "Matcha." So what? "Gives you energy." So what? "You skip the afternoon crash." Now you're talking about my life, not your ingredient.

**Product to lifestyle.** Old Spice doesn't sell deodorant. "SMELL CONFIDENT." Two words. No aluminium-free formulas. Just the feeling. STIHL sells chainsaws. Their ad shows a backyard swing set: "THE ORIGINAL PLAYSTATION. GET REAL. GET OUTSIDE." They're not selling a chainsaw. They're selling childhood memories. Don't sell a mattress — sell waking up without back pain. Don't sell running shoes — sell the 6am runner's high. The product is the bridge. The destination is what matters.

**Reframe the category.** Channel 4 was promoting the Paralympic Games. Instead of "Watch the Paralympic Games on Channel 4" — "MEET THE SUPERHUMANS." One word changed everything. "Paralympians" became "Superhumans." The frame shifted from disability to extraordinary ability. Recycled toilet paper. Who Gives a Crap: "Stop wiping with trees!" Regular toilet paper became "wiping with trees." The boring option sounds absurd. The recycled option sounds like common sense. Question to ask: if I had to rename my category to make it exciting, what would I call it?

**Reframe the pain.** Shelf Engine sells AI-powered ordering for grocery stores. Technical. Boring. Their copy: "Over 40% of deli sandwiches go to waste." They didn't lead with the solution. They led with a shocking pain. Forty percent. Nearly half. Every other sandwich, in the bin. Now you want to know who fixes this. The pain is the pitch. Lead with the wound. Offer the bandage second.

**Reframe the objection.** Wild Turkey bourbon is expensive. Instead of denying the price: "There are less expensive bourbons. There are also thinner steaks and smaller cars." They didn't defend. They reframed what "less expensive" means. Cheaper bourbon is in the same category as thin steaks and small cars. Things you settle for. The objection didn't disappear. It became a selling point.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the boring version first.** The version a spreadsheet would produce. "10 million trees planted." "£29/month." "24g of protein." "We reduced emissions."

2. **Pick the reframe type that fits.**
   - Abstract number → visual comparison (stat translation).
   - Price → familiar-purchase anchor (cost reframe).
   - Feature → outcome the customer lives (feature-to-outcome).
   - Product → lifestyle moment (product-to-lifestyle).
   - Category → more exciting label (category reframe).
   - Dry problem → vivid pain (pain reframe).
   - Defensive price/quality/timing objection → analogy that makes the objection a positive (objection reframe).

3. **Fill the blank: "That's the same as ___."** For stats and prices, this is the core move. If nothing fills the blank, the reframe won't land — the fact may not be worth using at all.

4. **Keep the reframe fact-checkable.** Show the original underneath the reframe. "450,000 fewer cars on the road per year (equivalent to 2.1 million tonnes of CO2)." The headline is the reframe; the proof sits next to it.

5. **Apply the "so what?" loop for features.** State feature. Ask "so what?" Write the answer. Ask again. Keep going for three or four rounds. The answer that lands at the business goal or personal goal is your headline. Anything short of that is a benefit, not value (see Dunford's differentiated-value.md for the parallel move).

6. **Test the reframe with a stranger.** If they instantly get it, you've found the frame. If they need explanation, keep reframing. The reframe should be the explanation.

## Examples

**Situation:** A fintech startup says "Our software processes transactions in under 250 milliseconds."
**Application:** Zoom the feature into outcome. So what? 250ms is faster than you can blink. So what? Checkout doesn't feel like checkout — it feels instant. So what? Abandoned carts drop. Reframe: "Checkout so fast it doesn't feel like checkout." Or for B2B CFOs: "Processes £10M of orders while you read this sentence."
**Result:** "Under 250ms" is a spec. Nobody buys specs. The reframe translates speed into what speed creates — either feeling (consumer) or scale (B2B).

**Situation:** A £49/month B2B SaaS tool priced against £9/month competitors.
**Application:** Cost reframe against the non-tool alternative, not against the cheaper tool. "Hiring a junior ops person to do this manually: £2,800/month. Our tool: £49/month." Or anchor against failure cost: "One missed invoice costs you more than a year of us."
**Result:** Anchoring against a cheap competitor makes you look expensive. Anchoring against the real alternative (headcount or error cost) makes you look cheap. Same price, completely different perception.

**Situation:** An eco-friendly delivery service wants to highlight carbon savings. Their stat: "We've prevented 4,000 tonnes of CO2 emissions."
**Application:** 4,000 tonnes is a spreadsheet number. Translate: "That's 2,000 cars taken off the road for a year" or "the carbon footprint of flying 3,500 people from London to New York." Choose the comparison that matches the customer's frame of reference.
**Result:** The stat moves from invisible to memorable. Customers can re-tell the comparison at a dinner party — which is free distribution for the brand.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reframe in a direction that's technically accurate but psychologically misleading.
**Why:** "£29/month — that's less than £1 a day" is fine. "£29/month — that's less than a Tesla" is technically true and insulting. Stretch too far and the customer spots the con. Reframes should recontextualise, not distort.

**Don't:** Hide the original fact behind the reframe.
**Why:** "Equivalent to 450,000 cars" works because the reader can check. "Massive carbon impact" is a reframe that deletes the fact. Show both. The reframe is the headline, the fact is the proof.

**Don't:** Reframe the category just to sound clever.
**Why:** "MEET THE SUPERHUMANS" works because Paralympic athletes are genuinely extraordinary. "Meet the luxury experience" for a budget hotel is a cheat the customer will resent once they arrive. Reframes must be honest — they illuminate what's true, not invent what isn't.

**Don't:** Stack three reframes on one claim.
**Why:** Pick one. "Four eggs worth of protein, twice the iron of spinach, sustainably sourced" is three reframes competing. Each dilutes the others. Lead with the one that matters most to the customer. Move the others to supporting copy if they earn their place.

**Don't:** Reframe when you could show instead.
**Why:** A reframe explains; a demonstration proves. "Faster than most CRMs" is a reframe. A screenshot of a five-second deploy is a demonstration. Demonstrations beat reframes when you have them. Reserve reframes for claims that don't have a clean visual proof (see show-dont-tell.md).
