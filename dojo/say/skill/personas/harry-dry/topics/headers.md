# Headers That Work

## When to Use
- Writing the hero headline on any page. This is where 80% of the work lives — five out of six people read the headline and nothing else.
- Newsletter sign-up pages, lead magnet pages, founder's notes. Each has a specific header pattern.
- Reviewing existing headlines. Most generic headlines fail because they're using no pattern at all — or the wrong one.

## Fails When
- **The positioning underneath is generic.** No header pattern fixes "nothing to say." The Iceberg questions must be answered first.
- **You force the wrong pattern.** A misdirection header on a boring product reads as confused, not clever. Match pattern to content.
- **You stack all the patterns in one header.** Four-layer plus misdirection plus founder story in one line produces soup. Pick one structure per place on the page.

## Core Concept

David Ogilvy said it decades ago. Five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. That ratio hasn't changed. If anything, it's got worse. Your header is your one shot. If it doesn't work, nothing below it matters.

Here are the patterns that keep showing up on the best landing pages.

**The Four-Layer Header.** Most powerful pattern. Four layers stacked:
1. Irresistible outcome.
2. Made believable.
3. Made frictionless.
4. De-risked.

MainStreet does all four:
- **Outcome:** "Get $50K+ back from the IRS in 20 minutes."
- **Believable:** "$95,425 credits found" (with a real founder's photo and name).
- **Frictionless:** "We do all the paperwork."
- **De-risked:** "If we can't help you, you pay $0."

Each layer removes a reason to say no. Layer one hooks. Layer two proves. Layer three removes effort. Layer four removes risk. Most headers do layer one and stop. "Save money on taxes." Fine. But I don't believe you. And it sounds like work. And what if it doesn't work out? Four layers. Four objections killed.

**The Two-Sentence Header.** Simple. What you do. Why you're different.
- "Virtual cooking classes. Taught by Michelin-Star chefs."
- "Online whiteboard. Built for teams that actually ship."
- "Podcast hosting. With analytics that aren't rubbish."

First sentence: what. Second sentence: why I should care. The pattern works because it answers two questions instantly. Most headers try to be clever with one sentence. Two sentences let you be clear AND compelling. You can flip it too — lead with the differentiator: "Taught by Michelin-Star chefs. Virtual cooking classes." Same info, different emphasis.

**The Three-Part Header.** What, who, why. Three pieces of information in one line. FigJam: "An online whiteboard for teams to build and brainstorm."
- **What:** An online whiteboard.
- **Who:** For teams.
- **Why:** To build and brainstorm.

Every word earns its place. No adjectives. No fluff. This pattern works especially well for tools and SaaS — when someone lands on your page, they need to know what is it, who is it for, what does it do. Answer all three in one line and you've done 80% of the job.

**The Misdirection Header.** Setup and punchline. Lead the reader one way. Then flip it. Shapr3D: "Clunky. Expensive. Archaic." You read that and think — wait, why are they insulting themselves? Then: "These shouldn't be the words you use to describe your CAD program." The flip. They're describing your current CAD, not theirs. The negativity belongs to the competition. Creates a moment of confusion. Confusion demands resolution. You keep reading. The trick is the speed of the flip. The longer the gap between setup and punchline, the weaker the payoff. "Clunky. Expensive. Archaic." is three words. Then the redirect hits immediately.

**The Founder Story Header.** Not a headline — a mini-narrative. Put yourself in the customer's shoes, explain their problem, take ownership, show the happy ending. Privy's founder:
- **Customer's shoes:** "Out of the 12 million stores online, only 8% will sell more than $1,000 in products this year."
- **The problem:** "Most ecommerce brands have two challenges with growth: getting people to buy once, and getting them to come back and buy again."
- **Ownership:** "That's why we built Privy."
- **Happy ending:** "In the last ten years we've helped over 600k stores generate $5 billion in sales."

This pattern works for landing-page bottom sections — the founder's note, where you stop being a brand and start being a person. Notice the structure: he doesn't start with "We built Privy because..." He starts with a surprising stat about the customer's world, then names their problem, then introduces himself as the solution. The customer is the hero. You're the guide.

**Lead Magnet Headers.** Four elements. Design Academy:
- **Clear outcome:** "Learn the basics of design."
- **Timeframe:** "In just 6 days."
- **Call-to-value:** "Send me Lesson 1."
- **Social proof:** "Wow, uhmmmm... I've learned more from this one lesson than I have from paid courses from well-known designers!"

"Send me Lesson 1" works harder than "Subscribe." It tells you exactly what happens when you click. The timeframe matters — "Learn design" is infinite, "Learn design in 6 days" is a commitment I can picture. Cover your eyes and ask someone to read it. Can they tell you what they'll get, how long it takes, and why it's worth it? If not, rewrite.

**Newsletter Headers.** Four things: name it, whet my appetite, tell me when, tell me what. James Clear's 3-2-1 Newsletter:
- **Name:** "3-2-1 Newsletter"
- **Appetite:** "The most wisdom per word of any newsletter on the web."
- **When:** "Every Thursday."
- **What:** "3 ideas from me, 2 quotes from others, 1 question for you."

That last line is brilliant. You know exactly what you're getting. Specificity creates expectation. Expectation creates habit. Habit creates loyalty. Same pattern for Marketing Examples: "Harry's Newsletter. Every Monday. 3 short examples, 2 copywriting tips, 1 favourite tweet. Marketers voted it the #1 marketing newsletter. 85k+ people enjoy it." Give people the format. Let them picture their inbox with your newsletter in it.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the pattern that matches the page.**
   - Landing page hero with a big claim to make → Four-Layer.
   - SaaS tool page → Three-Part or Two-Sentence.
   - Product page where competitors sound identical → Misdirection (if product genuinely is different).
   - Landing page founder's note section → Founder Story.
   - Lead magnet page → Lead Magnet four-element.
   - Newsletter page → Newsletter four-thing.

2. **Write each layer/element as a single sentence.** Don't let any one layer sprawl. The layers are beats.

3. **Run the draft through the three tests.** Even with a great pattern, each layer still needs to visualise, falsify, be unique.

4. **For CTAs within the header, use call-to-value language.** "Send me Lesson 1" beats "Subscribe." "Find my credits" beats "Get started." "See my results" beats "Sign up." The CTA describes what the user gets — not what they do.

5. **Test the header with one question: "Could a competitor sign this?"** Especially for four-layer and two-sentence headers. If any line could be signed by a competitor, rewrite it until only you could say it.

6. **For founder story headers, start with a surprising stat about the customer's world — not about you.** "Out of 12 million stores, only 8% will sell over $1,000 this year" is about the customer's world. "We built this in 2020 because…" is about you. Customer first, you second.

## Examples

**Situation:** A bookkeeping SaaS homepage says "Bookkeeping made easy."
**Application:** Four platitudes in three words. Swap to Two-Sentence or Three-Part. "Bookkeeping software for freelance creatives. Monthly invoices in ten clicks, tax-ready by year end." What (bookkeeping software for creatives). Who (freelance creatives). Why (specific outcome).
**Result:** The generic tagline becomes a position. A bookkeeper serving enterprise can't sign it. A broad "everyone" bookkeeper can't sign it. Only this product can.

**Situation:** A B2B SaaS page for a new tax-credit service: "Find tax credits you didn't know you had."
**Application:** Upgrade to Four-Layer. Outcome: "Get £50K+ back from HMRC in 30 days." Believable: "£2.7M found for clients this year" + logo of a real founder. Frictionless: "We do all the paperwork — you sign one form." De-risked: "No win, no fee. Ever."
**Result:** Generic benefit becomes a four-layer pitch that kills four objections (incredulity, effort, commitment, risk) before the prospect scrolls.

**Situation:** A newsletter sign-up saying "Subscribe to our weekly newsletter."
**Application:** Newsletter four-thing. Name it. Pitch it. Tell them when. Tell them what.
"Field Notes. Every Sunday. 1 case study, 2 tactical moves, 1 question for founders in the trenches. 8,200+ operators already read it."
**Result:** The anonymous "weekly newsletter" becomes a specific, named, predictable product the subscriber can picture in their inbox. Sign-ups climb because the reader knows what they're committing to.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use misdirection on a product that isn't actually different.
**Why:** "Clunky. Expensive. Archaic." only works if your CAD is actually simple, affordable, and modern. If the reader reads the misdirection, then reads your product page, and can't see the differentiation, you've set up a letdown bigger than the initial hook. Misdirection without substance reads as bait-and-switch.

**Don't:** Skip the de-risked layer in a Four-Layer header.
**Why:** That layer disproportionately closes deals. "If we can't help you, you pay $0" is often the highest-leverage line on the page — it reframes the decision from "should I buy?" to "why wouldn't I try?" Skipping the de-risk layer leaves conversion on the table.

**Don't:** Write a founder's note that's about you.
**Why:** "Hi, I'm Harry. I've been obsessed with copywriting for ten years..." starts with you. Nobody cares yet. Start with the customer's world ("12 million stores online, only 8% will sell…"). Then their problem. Then your ownership. Then the ending. Customer is the hero; you are the guide.

**Don't:** Use the Newsletter four-thing pattern with vague "what."
**Why:** "Every Monday. Great content for marketers." That's not a "what" — that's a promise to provide wallpaper. "3 short examples, 2 copywriting tips, 1 favourite tweet" is a "what." Specific, countable, predictable.

**Don't:** Treat the header as a standalone decision.
**Why:** The header is the top of the landing-page blueprint (see landing-page-blueprint.md). The sub-head, visual, social proof, and CTA below must all line up with the claim in the header. A four-layer header followed by generic body copy reads as bait. The header is the promise; the rest of the page is the proof.
