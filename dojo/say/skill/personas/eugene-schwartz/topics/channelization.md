# Channelization

## When to Use

- You have located the mass desire in the market and you now have to aim it — not enlarge it, not prove it, not dramatize it; *aim* it — at one specific performance of your product.
- A product appeals to several mass desires at once, and you have to pick which one the headline carries and which ones get demoted to body copy.
- The market is still at the stage where a diffuse want ("I want to feel better," "I want more money," "I want to look younger") has not yet been crystallized by any competitor into a specific, specific, specific product-promise.
- A product has run for years on one "does" and the market has tired of it; the product contains other "does" you have not yet exploited, and you need to swap in a fresh one.
- The prospect's State of Awareness is 3, 4 or 5. The less aware he is, the more channelization work the lead has to do to narrow the field down to *this* product.

## Fails When

- You try to channelize a desire that isn't there. No amount of narrowing focuses nothing. If the desire underneath is absent or weak, channelization turns into invention — and invention is education, and no single advertiser can afford to educate the American public.
- You channel onto what the product *is* instead of what it *does*. The reader does not buy the steel in a car, the glass in a vase, the tobacco in a cigarette, or the paper in a book. He buys what those things *do* for him. Channelize onto the steel and you are selling a commodity no one wants.
- You stack three "does" in one headline because the product team loves all of them. Every product offers dozens of keys; only one fits the lock at a time. Stacking blunts each of them.
- You channelize onto a "does" the market is exhausted on. The diet field cannot be re-channelized by promising weight loss in a market where every competitor has promised weight loss. You either switch to a *different* "does" the same product delivers (protecting the face, destroying fat cells, eating to lose) or you move to Identification.

## Core Concept

The mass desire comes from the market. You do not make it. You cannot fight it. But between that desire — broad, public, permanent, shared by millions — and the product on your desk, there is a gap. The reader wants to be healthier. He does not, by wanting that, want *your book*. He wants money. He does not, by wanting that, want *Ty Hicks's book on borrowing*. He wants to lose weight. He does not, by wanting that, want *Dr. Reinish's eat-to-get-thin plan*. Between the field of desire and the point of purchase there is a corridor, and the copywriter's second job — after picking the desire — is to build that corridor. That is Channelization.

Channelization is the act of narrowing the desire's target from a field to a point. The field is the mass desire as it exists in the market — diffuse, enormous, shared. The point is the one thing the reader is going to write a check for. You do not change the desire in the process. You *focus* it. You take the already-existing urge and guide it, sentence by sentence, onto a particular performance of a particular product. The creativity is not in you. The creativity is in the market and in the product, and all you are doing is joining the two together — you are the electrician running the wire from the power source to the bulb that happens to be yours.

And this is where the Schwartz cornerstone lives. Every product is actually two products. There is the physical product — the steel, paper, glass, tobacco, chemistry, pages, circuitry. And there is the functional product — what it *does* for the consumer, the series of benefits, the performance. The physical product does not sell. "People do not buy the steel in a car, the glass in a vase, the tobacco in a cigarette, or the paper in a book." They pay for what it does. That means channelization runs entirely on the functional axis. When you narrow the desire down onto your product, you narrow it onto a verb, not a noun. "Lose 20 pounds without hunger." "Turn back your clock 20 years." "Stop paying a penny more for gas than you did last year." "Remove your fear of death forever." Every one of those is a verb applied to the reader. None of them is a material. That is channelization.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the mass desire in one sentence.** Before you channel anything, know what you are channelling. "Readers in the 1973 gas crisis are furious at Detroit and the oil companies and want to beat them at the pump." "Overweight women have tried every diet and are afraid the next one will age their face." "Middle-aged men want to feel young again and are bitter that science has doubled crop yields but barely lifted human life expectancy." That is the field. Write it.

2. **Inventory every "does" the product contains.** Not what it *is*. What it *does*. For an automobile: it transports, it protects, it impresses, it saves money, it signals status, it accelerates, it lasts. For a book on nutrition: it diagnoses hypoglycemia, it restores energy, it undoes "food-as-perpetrator" anxiety, it lets the reader skip the psychiatrist, it arms the reader against their own doctor. List ten. If you only find three, you have not read the product closely enough. Schwartz read every product four times and took forty pages of notes; that is how the list gets long enough.

3. **Match each "does" against the three dimensions of the mass desire.** Urgency, staying power, scope. One of the "does" will score highest across all three. That is your channel. If two are close, pick the one the market has not yet been pitched on. Freshness of channel beats strength of channel by a nose, inside a sophisticated market.

4. **State the channel as the headline's promise.** The headline names the desire being satisfied, and names the specific performance that satisfies it. Not the noun of the product. The verb of the result. "Feel 20 years younger, live 20 years longer." "Doesn't just shrink fat cells — destroys them." "Don't pay 1¢ more for gas this year than you did last." Each of those is a channel and nothing else.

5. **Let the body copy demonstrate that this specific product is the inevitable deliverer of that specific channel.** This is where the physical product finally enters — not as content, but as *proof*. The weight of steel in the car door only matters once the headline has promised safety. The chemistry of the face cream only matters once the headline has promised the face. Channelization gets the reader to the door; physical specifics then carry the last mile into belief.

6. **When the market tires of one "does," switch to another.** This is the single most important move inside Channelization. The product has dozens of "does." The one you are running on today is not the last one available. When sales go flat on "lose weight," the same product can run on "lose weight without a haggard face." When "horsepower" exhausts the automotive market, the same cars can run on "gas economy," then on "safety," then on "status." The product has not changed. The channel has. This is how one product survives three markets.

7. **Refuse to stack.** Every product appeals to two, three, four mass desires. The temptation is to get them all into the headline. Don't. Only one key fits the lock at a time. Pick the strongest, and pour the others into body copy as reinforcement. A headline that tries to channel three desires channels none of them.

## Examples

**Channelizing the 1973 gas crisis onto a single book — `dont-pay-one-cent-more-for-gas-this-year`.**
The mass desire in 1973 was thrift stacked on top of indignation: every driver in America was bleeding at the pump and furious at Detroit and the oil companies. That is the field — millions wide, urgent, permanent as long as gas prices keep rising. The product is Ed Almquist's *How To Save Gas & Repairs*. Schwartz did not channelize onto "a great book about cars." He channelized onto one specific, measurable performance: *"DON'T PAY 1¢ MORE FOR GAS THIS YEAR THAN YOU DID LAST!"* The verb is *pay*. The measurable is *1¢*. The timeframe is *this year vs. last*. The entire diffuse national fury has been narrowed to a single point the reader can picture at the next fill-up. Notice what is not in the headline — nothing about the book, nothing about the paper, nothing about the author's credentials, nothing about the 320 pages. All that is physical. The channel is functional.

**Channelizing wealth-desire through a counter-intuitive verb — `How-To-Buy-Money-Cheap`.**
The field: readers want money and are afraid of debt, a permanent mass instinct with unlimited staying power and scope. Rival books all channelled onto some version of "make money" or "get rich." Schwartz channelized *How to Shake the Money Tree* onto a verb no competitor had touched: *"How to Buy Money... Cheap!"* He treated money as a commodity you purchase at a discount — a single functional inversion. The product does many things, but Schwartz picked one channel: you can *buy* money, cheap. The 60-second test in the lead reinforces it: *"Can you name the one loan source that will give you up to $3,000 in five minutes—with NO inquiry made as to your financial ability, place of employment, or purpose of loan?"*

**Channelizing the reducing field's exhausted desire through a new "does" — `Doesnt-Just-Shrink-Fat-Cells-But-Destroys-Them`.**
The reducing field is Stage 5 sophistication — every adult American has heard every weight-loss claim. Mass desire remains at maximum (urgent, permanent, enormous scope) but every standard channel has been used. Schwartz's move: channelize onto a *new* "does" the product could credibly claim. Not lose weight. Not lose weight fast. Not lose weight without hunger. *Destroy the fat cells themselves.* The headline: *"Doesn't Just Shrink Fat Cells, But Destroys Them!"* And the body: *"here at last is a simple diet that doesn't just empty out the contents of those ugly fat cells, distributed all over your body...but can actually destroy, in case after case, the fat-cells themselves!"* Same mass desire — lose weight and keep it off. Same category. Same product type. Different channel. The "does" has been swapped, and the ad pulls in a market where the old "does" had died.

**Channelizing health-desire onto the digestive furnace — `Turns-Up-Your-Digestive-Furnace`.**
Dr. Reinish's book could be channelled a dozen ways — lose weight, eat more, gain energy, reduce fluid, detoxify. Schwartz picked one functional image and drove everything toward it: *"Turns up your 'Digestive Furnace' and burns flab right off your body!"* The channel is industrial-metaphor physiology — the body as a furnace, the flab as fuel, the diet as the throttle. Notice the verbs: *turns up, burns off.* Notice the specificity: *20...40...60...80...even 100 or 120 ugly pounds.* Same permanent instinct; new channel.

**Channelizing the fear of aging onto a physical clock — `Feel-20-Years-Younger-Live-20-Years-Longer`.**
The mass desire is the most universal on earth: fear of aging and death. Channelization narrows the entire terror down to two numbers: *"FEEL 20 YEARS YOUNGER LIVE 20 YEARS LONGER!"* Same number, two outcomes, one rhythm. The field is terror of the biological clock; the channel is the literal clock, turned back twenty and stretched forward twenty. The pre-headline pushes it further: *"A baby born in the year 2100 will outlive you by 20 years!"* The same permanent instinct could have been channelled as *stop dying*, as *keep your hair*, as *don't be sick* — Schwartz picked the dial-the-clock channel because it is measurable, dramatic, and fresh against competing anti-aging copy.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a headline about what the product is made of. "Contains 1,200 mg of Omega-3." "Over 300 pages, hardcover, 900 illustrations." Those are physical facts, and the physical product does not sell. They may end up in body copy as proof — they do not belong in the headline.
**Why:** Schwartz is immovable on this: people do not buy the steel in a car, the glass in a vase, the tobacco in a cigarette, or the paper in a book. Channelization runs on the functional axis — what the product *does* for the reader. Put the material in the headline and you have channelled the mass desire onto nothing the reader wants.

**Don't:** Stack three or four "does" into one headline because the product does all of them and the client loves all of them.
**Why:** Only one key fits the lock at a time. The reader has one glance of attention and will admit one thought during that glance. Three promises in one headline read as zero. Pick the single dominant performance that satisfies the single dominant desire, and demote the rest to body copy where they can reinforce.

**Don't:** Channelize onto a "does" the market is exhausted on. If every diet ad in the reducing field has already promised weight loss, writing another headline that promises weight loss will not pull — even if your copy is brilliant and your mass desire is correctly identified.
**Why:** The mass desire is still there, but the channel has been saturated. The correct move is to find a different "does" the same product delivers — destroy fat cells, protect the face, eat to lose — or to move up the sophistication ladder to new mechanism, or to switch to Identification entirely. More volume on the same channel will not revive it.

**Don't:** Channelize onto a general desire when you could channelize onto the specific symptom. "I want to be healthier" does not buy a book. "I want to stop waking up tired after eight hours of sleep" buys *How to Eat Your Way Out of Fatigue*. The specific symptom is already *half* the channel — the reader has narrowed the desire himself by the time he has named the symptom.
**Why:** The whole point of channelization is to move from field to point. Starting at "general health" when you could have started at "chronic fatigue" means you spend the first several paragraphs doing narrowing the reader has already done for you. Take the handoff where the reader stopped.

**Don't:** Confuse channelization with identification. Channelization narrows the desire's *target* — from field to point, from category to product, from "health" to "your eyes heal themselves." Identification narrows the reader's *self-image* — from "people like me" to "I am specifically this person." They are parallel techniques, not the same one, and they fail when you substitute one for the other.
**Why:** A perfectly channelized ad that never lets the reader see himself in the copy will not convert at the low end of the awareness scale; a perfectly identified ad that never focuses the desire onto this one product will not convert even if the reader feels seen. You need both — channelization gets the desire pointed at the product, identification gets the reader to step into the copy. Running one without the other produces an ad with one wing.

**Don't:** Try to channelize before the mass desire is locked. If you haven't nailed the desire — in one sentence, with all three dimensions scored — then channelization is premature. You will end up narrowing the wrong beam.
**Why:** Channelization has no content of its own. It focuses whatever desire you feed it. Feed it a weak or wrong desire and you will focus it beautifully onto a product the market does not care about. Nail the desire first. Then channel.

**Don't:** Fall in love with a clever channel that is not the strongest channel. The most elegant inversion, the most counter-intuitive verb, the most memorable metaphor — none of these beat the channel with the most urgency, staying power, and scope.
**Why:** The literary school of copywriting treats the ad as a thing to be admired. The market does not admire. The market buys. If the clever channel and the powerful channel are the same, good. If not, take the powerful one.
