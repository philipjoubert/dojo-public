# Mass Desire

## When to Use
- Before you write a word of copy for any product, for any market, ever.
- When you're given a product and asked "what's the headline" — stop and ask "what's the mass desire" first.
- When a brief tries to tell you what the reader should want — push back and find what the reader already wants.
- When an ad has failed and you suspect the wrong dimension of desire is being pulled.
- When you have a product that satisfies two, three or four desires and you must pick the one that predominates.

## Fails When
- **You try to create the desire.** If the want is not already there in the market, copy cannot install it. No advertiser can afford to educate the American public. Try it and you are no longer writing advertising — you are writing tuition.
- **You try to fight the desire.** Running against the tide is not courage, it is suicide. Chrysler 1948 is the proof. Edsel is the proof.
- **You pick a desire that exists but is weak in scope, urgency, or staying power.** All three dimensions must register. Two out of three is not enough.
- **You stack every desire the product touches into one headline.** The ad goes fuzzy. Only one desire can predominate; only one can be the key.

## Core Concept

The power, the force, the overwhelming urge to own that makes advertising work comes from the market itself, and not from the copy. Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product. This is the copywriter's task: not invention, but *connection*.

Mass desire is the public spread of a private want. It is the moment when a private desire is shared by a statistically significant number of people, large enough to profitably repay selling to them, that a market is born. These desires take years to develop. They are built by social, economic, and technological forces far greater than any advertising budget. Your job is to detect them, inventory them, chart their force and direction — and then harness your product onto their backs. This is a study that will occupy part of every working day for the rest of your life.

You cannot create this desire, and you cannot fight it. But you can — and must — direct it, channel it, focus it onto your particular product. The copywriter performs three jobs in sequence: choose the most powerful desire that can be applied to the product; acknowledge that desire and promise its satisfaction in a single statement — the headline; then show, through the product's performance, that the satisfaction is inevitable. Step one is the only one that matters. Every later step is execution of that first choice. If the choice of desire is wrong, nothing else in the ad will save it.

## The Three Dimensions

Every mass desire has three vital dimensions. Measure each one before committing.

1. **Urgency / intensity.** The degree of demand to be satisfied. Constant arthritic pain demands to be satisfied now. A mild wish for a gourmet meal can wait a week. The intensity of the feeling is the force behind the pull. Headlines written against urgent desires can afford to be blunt; headlines written against languid desires have to do more work.

2. **Staying power / inability to be satiated / degree of repetition.** Raw hunger is satiated in twenty minutes; the craving for a gourmet food is a slow-burning once-in-a-while. The desire to lose weight, to stay young, to make more money — these never fade. They are permanent mass instincts that renew themselves continuously, and that is why the reducing field and the longevity field and the wealth field are insatiable forever. The desire for one specific style of furniture, by contrast, dies out in six years. Weigh the permanence.

3. **Scope.** The number of people who share this desire. How many men will pay $10 for a gas-saving accessory? How many will pay $10 for a repair-prevention accessory? The first number is larger than the second by a factor of ten. Scope is the size of the lake; urgency and staying power are the depth of it. You need both.

You are trying to choose the mass desire that gives you the most power across all three dimensions simultaneously. Urgent, enduring, and broad. A single overwhelming desire existing today in the hearts and minds of millions of people who are actively seeking to satisfy it at this very moment. That is the one you channel. That is the one the headline carries.

## Channel, Don't Create

This is the load-bearing principle of the entire craft. When advertising exploits already-existing desire, it produces what may be called the Amplification Effect — one dollar of ad spend can produce fifty or a hundred dollars in sales, because you are riding on top of a mass desire that was built by years of social and technological momentum. When advertising tries to create this desire — to convince people they ought to want something they don't — it stops being advertising and becomes education, and education produces at best one dollar in sales per dollar of spend. No advertiser has that kind of money.

Run against the tide and you die. In 1948, the American public decided they wanted a longer, lower, wider car — to display their rising standard of living. Chrysler chose to buck the trend, and offered a fine, functional car with more head, leg, and shoulder room than its competitors. The advertising copy was beautifully executed. Sales collapsed. The desire was for *length*, not *roominess*, and no amount of copy about roominess could override the desire for length.

By 1954, cars had become universally long, and the new mass desire was for *horsepower*. The Hudson Hornet, the twin-exhaust Cadillac, the Chrysler 300 — all exploited this trend and rode it to millions of dollars in sales. The Ford Company decided to play horsepower down, and devoted millions of advertising dollars to selling safety. The advertising ran into a wall of disinterest; results were non-existent. The next year Ford produced, and advertised, the highest-horsepower engines in their history. They had stopped fighting the tide.

And then the most painful proof of all: the Edsel. Here was a good car, backed by a deluge of fine advertising, that died trying to fight the overwhelming switch in demand to a cheap, simple, inexpensive-to-run compact car. The car was not the problem. The copy was not the problem. The copy was excellent. It ran against the desire, and so it died.

Learn from this. Never, ever, try to convince a market to want what it does not already want. Find what it does want, and connect your product to that want.

## How to Apply

1. **Inventory the desires in the market before you look at the product.** List every mass desire a product of this type *could* plausibly channel — greed, fear of aging, vanity, fear of illness, pride, social status, sexual magnetism, thrift, protection of family. Do this as a taxonomy before the product influences your thinking.

2. **Measure each candidate on all three dimensions.** Urgency: how much does this hurt or burn right now? Staying power: is this a permanent mass instinct or a passing style? Scope: how many people share it at the price you will charge?

3. **Pick the single desire that scores highest across all three, and for which this product has a credible claim.** Not the one the product team finds most interesting. Not the one you find most elegant. The one with the most power.

4. **Acknowledge that desire, intensify it, and promise its satisfaction in the headline.** The headline does not mention the product. The headline names the desire and its fulfilment. The body copy then takes the performance of the product — what it *does*, not what it *is* — and shows the satisfaction is inevitable.

5. **Refuse to stack.** Every product appeals to two, three, or four mass desires. But only one can predominate. Only one can reach out through the headline to the customer. If you put three desires in one headline, none of them pulls. Pick the key that unlocks maximum economic power at this moment, and hold to it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Ed Almquist's *How To Save Gas & Repairs*, written in the middle of the 1970s oil crisis. The obvious desire was thrift. Schwartz identified a deeper, more urgent desire beneath it — indignation at being cheated by oil companies and Detroit.
**Application:** The headline channelled indignation *first*, with thrift as the promise: "DON'T PAY 1¢ MORE FOR GAS THIS YEAR THAN YOU DID LAST!" The command frame pulled the reader's resentment and aimed it at a specific antagonist; the penny-specificity turned a vague complaint into a measurable outcome.
**Result:** The ad converted. The mass desire was pre-existing — every American driver in 1973 was bleeding at the pump and furious. Schwartz did not create that anger. He channelled it onto a book that promised to fight back.

**Situation:** Dr. Robert Franklyn's reducing book, sold into a market already glutted with diet claims. The obvious desire was weight loss. Schwartz found the knot no competitor had addressed: women feared that dieting would ravage their faces — "diet-lines," "diet-sag."
**Application:** He channelled a conflict rather than a single desire. The headline: *"At Last! A Plastic Surgeon's Diet!"* — a diet that protects the face at the same moment it melts the body. Two mass desires (be thin / stay beautiful) wired together in one promise, with authority borrowed from an adjacent discipline.
**Result:** A fresh hook in an exhausted field. The vanity-plus-thinness stack worked because they are both permanent mass instincts at maximum urgency in the female reducing market. If either had been weaker the ad would have failed.

**Situation:** Judge Tom Blaine's *Mental Health Through Nutrition*. The product promised food-as-cure for nervous complaints. The weakest possible desire to target would have been "general wellness."
**Application:** Schwartz targeted the most urgent and permanent instinct available — parental fear of mental decline in oneself or a loved one. The headline: *"Are The Foods You Are Eating Today STARVING YOUR BRAIN?"* Present-tense accusation, negative-polarity framing (food as perpetrator, not remedy), and a mass-scale fear. The desire was urgent (anxiety every day), enduring (permanent instinct), and broad (millions of readers with some version of the symptom).
**Result:** The question-as-accusation forced the reader into the ad. The desire was already there — fear of losing your mind, fear of a child slipping — Schwartz did nothing more than name it and connect it to a book.

**Situation:** Alan Watts's philosophy book, directed at a narrow market of readers confronting existential anxiety. The desire was real but not mass.
**Application:** Schwartz channelled the deepest possible permanent instinct — fear of death — with a conditional promise: *"This book could remove your fear of death forever."* The conditional "could" softened the claim to believability; the stakes (death, forever) gave the urgency maximum weight.
**Result:** The desire was there. Fear of death is the most universal permanent mass instinct that exists. Schwartz did not try to manufacture curiosity about Eastern philosophy; he pointed Eastern philosophy at the dread the reader already carried.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Stack multiple mass desires in one headline because the product has multiple benefits.
**Why:** Every product appeals to two, three, or four desires, but only one can predominate. When you stack, none of them pulls with force. You get a headline that reads like a product spec sheet. Pick the dominant one — the one with the most power across urgency, staying power, and scope — and hold it. Stack the others into the body copy as supporting proof.

**Don't:** Choose the desire that most flatters the product or the client.
**Why:** Clients fall in love with mechanisms and features. They want the ad to celebrate the thing they built. The mass desire is in the market, not in the product. If the client's preferred desire is not the one the market is burning for, the ad fails and the client blames you, not the product. Do the research, name the desire, defend the choice with the three dimensions.

**Don't:** Try to write a headline for a market that doesn't have a pre-existing desire of sufficient magnitude.
**Why:** You will fail. No headline, no matter how clever, channels a desire that isn't there. If the three-dimensional scan shows low urgency, weak staying power, and narrow scope — refuse the brief, reposition the product into a different market where a mass desire does exist, or tell the client the product will not sell until a desire builds up around it.

**Don't:** Confuse a style with a permanent mass instinct.
**Why:** Styles fade. The mass desire for bell-bottom jeans is not the mass desire for reducing. Permanent instincts — health, wealth, love, fear of aging, protection of family, sexual magnetism, vanity, greed — renew themselves continuously and never die. Style desires (this year's colour, this season's silhouette) are fragile and timed. If you build a long-running campaign on a style, you have built on sand.

**Don't:** Dismiss a desire as "irrational" or "embarrassing" to target.
**Why:** Readers do not buy what copywriters find dignified. Readers buy what satisfies greed, fear, vanity, pride, and lust. The copywriter's personal discomfort with these desires is a professional handicap. The mass desires that move markets are overwhelmingly primal. Name them plainly. Channel them. Anyone who edits out the desire because it offends their literary taste is writing poetry, not advertising.

**Don't:** Mistake a product feature for a desire.
**Why:** "Forty percent lighter" is not a desire. "Thirty-inch screen" is not a desire. Thrift, vanity, convenience, safety, ease — *those* are desires. Features become the mechanism that delivers on the desire (see `states-of-sophistication.md`). Never put the mechanism in the headline when the market is still at First or Second Stage, and never put the desire out of the headline entirely. The headline names the want. The body proves the product delivers.

**Don't:** Assume the mass desire that worked last year still works this year.
**Why:** Mass desires shift. The horsepower desire of the mid-Fifties collapsed into the gas-economy desire of the late-Sixties. An ad still channelling horsepower in 1970 was an ad running against the tide. Recheck the three dimensions before every new campaign. The copywriter's job is a study that will occupy part of every working day for the rest of his life.
