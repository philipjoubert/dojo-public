# States of Awareness

## When to Use
- Before writing any headline, ever. Diagnose where the prospect stands on the awareness scale first — everything in the headline follows from that single answer.
- When a headline that worked for one product is being ported to another and you need to check whether the two markets are at the same point on the scale.
- When an ad is failing and you suspect the copy is pitched above or below the prospect's current state of knowledge.
- When introducing a brand-new product and you must decide whether to lead with the product, the need, or the reader's own identity.
- When reviving a tired product whose old promise no longer pulls — the market has slid down the awareness scale and a different headline job is now required.

## Fails When
- **You diagnose the scale wrong and write Level 1 copy to a Level 4 market.** A direct price-and-product headline means nothing to a reader who does not yet know the product exists. A headline that will work wonders in the first stage will fail completely when addressed to a third-stage market where your prospect doesn't even realize that soaps can be made with built-in deodorants. Diagnose before you write.
- **You treat one product-market pair as if the answer is permanent.** Markets *move*. A market can slide up (more aware) as competitors educate it, or slide down (less aware) as the desire dries up and the product becomes old-fashioned. A headline that sold a million copies in 1954 will die in 1973 if the market has shifted underneath it.
- **You try to do the full selling job in the headline of a Level 4 or 5 market.** At those levels the headline sells nothing but the *next sentence*. When you try to sell the product in the first ten words to a market that doesn't even know the desire exists, you shoot craps with your client's money.
- **You mention price in a Level 5 headline.** Price means nothing to a person who does not know your product or want it. Name in a Level 5 headline damages the ad; direct claim of function damages the ad. The only thing left is the market itself.

## Core Concept

Before you write a single word of copy, answer this: how much does my prospect *already know* about the way my product satisfies his desire? Is he aware the problem exists? Does he know that products exist which solve it? Does he know that *my* product exists, and what it does, and what it costs? The answer to that question — and not your cleverness, and not the product's superiority — determines every word in the headline.

Every market's awareness passes through five stages, from the Most Aware at the top of the scale to the Completely Unaware at the bottom. Each stage is separated from the others by a psychological wall. On one side of that wall is indifference; on the other, intense interest. The headline that vaults one wall will not touch the next. The more aware your market, the less you need to say. The less aware, the more work the ad must do — not to sell harder, but to *lead the prospect step by step up the scale* until he is standing where your product can close him.

Your headline's real job — at every level — is to stop the prospect and compel him to read the second sentence. The ad is a chain of acceptances, and the headline is the first link. What goes *in* that first link changes entirely by level. At Level 1 the headline is a price tag. At Level 5 it is a mirror.

## The Five Stages

### Level 1 — Most Aware
The customer knows the product, knows what it does, knows he wants it. He just hasn't gotten around to buying it yet. The headline's job is mechanical: name the product, give the price, and add whatever news justifies action now — a sale, a free gift, instant delivery.

*Schwartz on this stage:* "The remainder of the advertisement can summarize quickly the most desirable selling points. Then add the name of a store, or a coupon, and close… There is nothing creative about his job, and he should receive the lowest possible scale of pay."

**Headline form:** Product name + price (or other news). Example from the book:

> "Revere Zomar Lens, Electric Eye Camera — Formerly $149.50 — Now Only $119.95."

Most of Schwartz's corpus sits further down the scale. Level 1 is the department-store, discount-catalog, mail-order-bargain register — Schwartz rarely worked there. The index does not contain a clean Level 1 headline of his own because the job did not need him. If you find yourself writing at Level 1, take a price, take a product name, and stop being clever.

### Level 2 — Product-Aware
The prospect knows your product. He isn't yet convinced of everything it does. He may not yet want it, or not want it enough, or not want it right now. Here lives the great bulk of all advertising. The product has a brand name; the name has been linked to an acknowledged public desire; the desire has been satisfied before. The headline's job is to reinforce the existing image, sharpen it, extend it, or add fresh proof — or to announce a new mechanism that lets the product satisfy the desire better than before.

**Headline form:** Name the product (headline or large logo) and use the rest of the headline to enlarge or extend its claim. Sharpen, extend, or prove.

**Example from the corpus — `From-4-Packs-A-Day-To-Zero-In-4-Hours`:**

> "FROM 4 PACKS A DAY TO ZERO, IN 4 HOURS!"

The reader already knows cigarettes are killing him. He already knows that quit-smoking products exist. He has tried several. The ad does not need to sell desire — it needs to sell *believability* of this specific method by enlarging the claim (a four-pack-a-day smoker, quit in four hours) and pinning a time-bound guarantee behind it. Classic product-aware stage: the market knows, the headline intensifies.

### Level 3 — Solution-Aware
The prospect knows, or recognizes immediately, that he wants what the product does. But he doesn't yet know that there is a product — your product — that will do it for him. His desire exists but is still amorphous. He feels it; he would recognize its fulfillment if he saw it; he has no specific brand name or solution-category in mind. This is the problem of introducing a new product into a desire-aware market.

*Schwartz on this stage:* "Here the problem is two-fold. First, to pinpoint the ill-defined, as-yet-uncrystallized desire that is slowly spreading through great masses of people all over America. And second, to crystallize that desire, and its solution, so sharply and so dramatically that each and every prospect will recognize it at a glance."

**Headline form:** Name the desire and/or its solution in the headline. Prove that the solution can be accomplished. Show that the mechanism of that accomplishment is contained in your product.

**Example from the corpus — `37-Ingenious-Spare-Time-Businesses`:**

> "37 ingenious SPARE-TIME BUSINESSES that require almost no investment, and net you up to $200 EXTRA INCOME every week!"

The reader already wants more money and already suspects "side businesses" exist. He has no product in mind. The headline crystallizes the amorphous desire — *extra income* — into a specific count (37), a specific risk elimination (almost no investment), and a specific weekly dollar figure. The desire was there. Schwartz named it and pinned it to a book.

### Level 4 — Problem-Aware (Need-Aware)
The prospect has not a desire but a *need*. He recognizes the need — he feels it, he suffers it. But he doesn't yet realize the connection between the fulfillment of that need and your product. Sometimes he hasn't even named the need aloud to himself.

*Schwartz on this stage:* "Here you start by naming the need and/or its solution in your headline. Then dramatize the need so vividly that the prospect realizes just how badly he needs the solution. And then present your product as the inevitable solution."

**Headline form:** Name the problem vividly, then promise (or hint at) the solution. A bare statement of the problem — "Corns?" — can be enough when the pain is specific and personal. More often the headline must drag the problem to the surface with a question, a list of symptoms, or a vivid scene that forces the reader to recognize himself inside it.

**Example from the corpus — `are-the-foods-you-are-eating-today-starving-your-brain`:**

> "Are The Foods You Are Eating Today STARVING YOUR BRAIN?"

The reader suffers the symptoms — the anxiety, the fading memory, the fatigue, the nervous wreck of a spouse — but has no idea those symptoms might be food-related. The need exists, unnamed. The headline forces the reader to confront a need he did not yet know he had, and the question-as-accusation structure pulls him into the ad before he can look away.

**A second example — `does-your-childs-behavior-worry-you-sick`:**

> "Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?"

The parent knows something is wrong — the tics, the bed-wetting, the poor grades, the night terrors. He doesn't yet know that structured expert help exists cheaply in the form of a book. The headline names the feeling ("worry you sick") in the exact phrase parents use about themselves. The pre-headline then stacks ten blockquoted parental laments so the reader sees his own private panic in a stranger's words. The need was there; Schwartz dragged it to the surface.

### Level 5 — Completely Unaware
The prospect is either not aware of his desire or his need; or he won't honestly admit it to himself without being led into it by your ad; or the need is so general and amorphous that it resists being summed up in a single headline; or it's a secret that just can't be verbalized. This is the outer reach of the awareness scale. The prospect is, in his own mind, hundreds of miles away from your product. It is your job to bridge that gap.

*Schwartz on this stage:* "What you are doing essentially in this fifth stage is calling your market together in the headline of your ad. You are writing an *identification* headline. You are selling nothing, promising nothing, satisfying nothing. Instead, you are echoing an emotion, an attitude, a dissatisfaction that picks people out from the crowd and binds them together in a single statement."

**Headline form:** Rule out what cannot appear. Price means nothing to a person who doesn't want the product — cut it. Product name damages the ad if the prospect has no context for it — cut it. A direct statement of function fails — cut it. What is left? The market. The headline speaks to *who the reader is* — echoes an emotion, jars them into awareness, offers a dream or a hidden fear, and gives them a name for something they have been carrying silently.

**Example from the corpus — `Now-Run-Your-Car-Without-Spark-Plugs`:**

> "Now — Run Your Car Without Spark Plugs — Get Up to 31 More Horse-Power, 8 More Miles per Gallon USING ONLY REGULAR GAS!"

The reader has never imagined a car running without spark plugs. The category does not exist in his head. The headline jars him into awareness with a shock-reversal of how he understands his own car — and only *after* the hook is set does the body copy teach him the science (air gaps, carbon buildup) that builds the new belief system.

**A second example — `this-book-could-remove-your-fear-of-death-forever`:**

> "This book could remove your fear of death forever."

The reader has anxiety he may never have named as *fear of death*. The headline surfaces it with maximum stakes and a softened conditional ("could") so the claim reads as thoughtful rather than hyped. The body copy's first move is push-away: "If you are satisfied with your life as it is now… then, please turn away from this page now." Pure Level-5 identification — the reader self-selects into the ad.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the scale first.** Before you write a word, answer: Does my prospect know my product exists? Does he know the category exists? Does he know the problem exists? Does he know the desire exists? The first "no" in that chain marks your level. Everything in the headline flows from that.

2. **Write the headline job the level actually calls for, not the one you wish it called for.** Level 1 = price and product. Level 2 = reinforce, extend, or announce a new mechanism. Level 3 = name and crystallize the desire. Level 4 = name and dramatize the need. Level 5 = identification — echo the reader back to himself.

3. **At Levels 3, 4 and 5, keep the product *out* of the headline.** The headline's job at these levels is to connect to the market, not to announce the product. The product comes in later — after the desire is crystallized, the need dramatized, or the reader identified. Put the product logo in a Level 5 headline and you break the mood of the ad before identification has landed.

4. **Build the chain of acceptances from the level you're at up to the product.** Paragraph by paragraph, the copy walks the prospect from identification → awareness of the problem → desire for a solution → belief that the solution is possible → belief that *your* product delivers it. The lower the starting level, the longer the walk. Longer copy is not a vice at Level 5; it is the job.

5. **Recheck the scale before every campaign.** Markets move. What was a Level 3 market last year is a Level 2 market this year after three competitors have educated it, or a Level 5 market five years from now when the category has collapsed under its own fatigue. Never assume last year's diagnosis still holds.

6. **Match the awareness diagnosis with the sophistication diagnosis (see `states-of-sophistication.md`).** The two scales work together. Awareness says *what does the prospect know*. Sophistication says *how many competing claims has he already heard*. A Level 3 / Stage 4 market gets a different headline from a Level 3 / Stage 1 market, even though the awareness number is the same.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Put the product name in a Level 5 headline.
**Why:** The prospect has no context for that name. He has never seen the product, doesn't want it, doesn't yet know the desire. A prominent logo at Level 5 breaks the mood of the identification headline — the reader sees an ad instead of seeing himself. Keep the product out of the headline entirely; bring it in later, after the chain of acceptances has carried the reader to where the product can close him. Save the logo for when it has meaning.

**Don't:** Try to sell the full product story in a Level 4 headline.
**Why:** The reader does not yet believe the solution exists. Cramming the product, the mechanism, the proof and the guarantee into one line short-circuits the dramatization step that Level 4 absolutely requires. The headline names the need and hints at the solution; the body copy dramatizes the need so vividly the reader *feels* the urgency; only then does the product appear as inevitable. Skipping dramatization is the number one failure mode at this level.

**Don't:** Reuse a Level 1 or 2 headline in a Level 4 or 5 market because it "worked before."
**Why:** The headline did not work because it was clever. It worked because the market it addressed already knew the product, already wanted it, and needed only a nudge. Drop that same headline into a market that has never heard of the product or the desire, and you are speaking a foreign language. The failure won't be of execution; it will be of diagnosis.

**Don't:** Mistake Level 5 (unaware) for Level 1 (aware) because "they haven't bought it yet."
**Why:** Both markets contain prospects who haven't bought. The Level 1 prospect hasn't bought because he hasn't gotten around to it; the Level 5 prospect hasn't bought because he does not yet know the desire exists. Copying Level 1 shortcuts — price, coupon, close — onto a Level 5 market is the classic suicidal move. The test: ask "does the prospect, right now, want what my product does?" If the answer is "not yet," you are not at Level 1.

**Don't:** Assume your diagnosis is permanent.
**Why:** The cigarette market moved from Stage-1 taste claims to Stage-3 mechanism claims to Stage-5 identification (the Marlboro cowboy) across fifty years with a product that hadn't physically changed. The copywriter who writes to last year's level is the one whose ad fails for reasons the client will never understand. Recheck before every new campaign.
