# Print Layout Discipline

## When to Use
- Laying out a magazine or newspaper advertisement, a direct-mail piece, or a long-form landing page where readership is the goal.
- Briefing an art director who is reaching for reverse type, sans-serif body, or a two-page spread for no functional reason.
- Reviewing a draft layout and asking whether a reader, at the normal 20-inch reading distance, can actually enter the copy.
- Translating Ogilvy's magazine rules to a web article format — column width, serif body, captions under images, cross-heads every few paragraphs.

## Fails When
- **The rules are followed but the promise is empty.** Serif type and tidy captions do not rescue an ad with nothing to say. Layout is the last step, not the first.
- **The medium is outdoor, subway, or packaging.** These have their own readership physics; the magazine rules don't transfer.
- **The art director insists the layout is "the creative."** Layout is a delivery mechanism for the idea, not the idea.
- **The layout is approved at a distance of 15 feet from a bulletin board.** "This leads to setting headlines in 72-point, which is impossible to read at the normal distance of 20 inches." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## Core Concept

"Readers look first at the illustration, then at the headline, then at the copy. So put these elements in that order – illustration at the top, headline under the illustration, copy under the headline. This follows the normal order of scanning, which is from top to bottom." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The layout is not a canvas; it is an instrument. It exists to move a tired reader, at a 20-inch viewing distance, from the illustration through the headline into the copy and out to the coupon. Every convention that obstructs that traverse is a cost. Every convention that matches the reader's eye-habit is a gain. "Advertising suffers from sporadic epidemics of Artdirectoritis. Those afflicted with this disease speak in hushed voices of 'cool grey bands of type,' as if the copy in advertisements was a mere *design* element. I tell them KISS – an acronym for Keep It Simple, Stupid." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The governing insight: six times as many people read the average article as the average advertisement. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Readers have been trained by a century of magazines and newspapers to expect a particular set of graphic conventions. News magazines — *Time*, *Newsweek*, *L'Express*, *Der Spiegel* — share the same format: copy has priority over illustration; the copy is set in serif type; three columns of type, 35 to 45 characters wide; every photograph has a caption; the copy starts with drop-initials; the type is black on white. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Now look at the ads in the same magazines: sans-serif body, a single 120-character column, no captions, reverse type, gadgetry. The ads break every convention the reader's eye has been trained to accept — and then the advertisers complain that nobody reads their ads. Pretend you are an editor. You will get more readership. Far more.

The specific rules are old and well-measured. Headline below the illustration, not above — ten per cent more readership, which on a 20-million-reader magazine is two million extra readers. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Caption under every photograph — four times as many people read captions as read body copy, so the caption is a second headline and should carry the brand name and the promise. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Body copy in serif type (Century, Caslon, Baskerville, Jenson) because "the eye is a creature of habit" and sans-serif "repels readership as wax paper repels water" (John Updike). — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Black on white, not reverse — I once counted 47 advertisements set in reverse in a single German magazine, long after research had shown reverse is hard to read. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Columns of about 40 characters wide, because that is what newspapers have taught the eye to expect. Headlines in lowercase, not capitals — "Professor Tinker of Stanford has established that capitals retard reading. They have no ascenders or descenders to help you recognize *words*, and tend to be read *letter by letter.*" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* No period at the end of the headline — a full stop is literally a full stop, and stops the reader dead in his tracks.

For long copy, the subhead-and-cross-head discipline is mandatory. A subhead of two lines between headline and body heightens the reader's appetite. A drop-initial at the start of the body lifts readership by 13 per cent. Limit the first paragraph to eleven words. Insert a cross-head every two or three inches and throughout — some of them interrogative, to pull the reader forward. Widows — short lines — increase readership, so do not square up the paragraphs. Leading between paragraphs lifts readership by an average of 12 per cent. Bold or italic on key paragraphs, arrowheads or bullets on lists, numbered points when you have unrelated facts. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* These are not style choices. They are the scaffolding on which long copy can actually be read.

## How to Apply

1. **Picture, then headline, then body — in that order, top to bottom.** Readers scan in this order; the layout must follow the eye. Headlines above the illustration cost you ten per cent of readership; headlines under the copy are self-indulgence. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

2. **Caption every photograph. Put the brand name and the promise in it.** Four times as many people read captions as body copy. If the caption merely describes the picture, you have wasted a second headline. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

3. **Serif, black on white, 35–45 characters per column.** Century, Caslon, Baskerville, Jenson — the faces the reader's eye already knows. Sans-serif on a page-size ad reads like wax paper; reverse type has been known to reduce readership for fifty years; 120-character columns are beyond what the eye will track. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

4. **Lowercase headlines, no period.** Capitals retard reading. The period stops the reader. Neither move earns a sale.

5. **Open the body with a grabber in eleven words or fewer.** "Going on vacation is a pleasure to which everyone looks forward" has already lost the reader. "Cesare Borgia murdered his brother-in-law for the love of his sister, who was the mistress of their father — the Pope" has not. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

6. **Subhead, drop-initial, cross-heads every two or three inches.** Long copy only gets read if the reader's eye is given somewhere to land every few paragraphs. Make some cross-heads interrogative — they pull the reader into the next block.

7. **Leading between paragraphs, widows allowed, key paragraphs in bold or italic.** Each of these moves adds readership at no cost in paper or ink. Numbered points when the facts are unrelated; arrowheads or bullets when a list is natural.

8. **Test the layout at 20 inches, not at 15 feet.** Hold the proof at the distance a reader will actually hold the magazine. 72-point headlines set for bulletin-board approval are unreadable in the reader's hand.

9. **Pretend you are an editor.** If the ad looks like an article, it will be read like one. If it looks like an ad, 95 per cent of readers will skip it.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** The Ogilvy house layout, used for every magazine ad in the early years of Ogilvy & Mather.
**Application:** A single repeating template: large photograph at the top, headline of up to nine words under the photograph, 240 words of copy set in serif, three columns of around 40 characters, captions under the photograph carrying the brand and the promise. "I challenge you to invent a better layout than these." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A second variant — wide shallow photograph, headline up to twenty words, subhead up to twenty-eight words, four or five cross-heads, 600 words of body copy — was built when the copy needed more room.
**Result:** The templates ran for years across disparate accounts — Rolls-Royce, Dove, KLM, Shell. Demonstrates that a fixed, well-reasoned layout is not a constraint on craft; it is the craft.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958). 719-word body.
**Application:** Photograph of the car at top. Headline under the photo, in lowercase, no period. Body copy in serif, three columns, set black on white. Seventeen numbered facts broken with cross-heads. Caption under the car carrying the brand and a proof point. Coupon at the bottom for a dealer booklet. Every rule on the list, applied.
**Result:** 50 per cent sales lift year-on-year. Demonstrates that the layout discipline is not separate from the copy's effect — it is the channel through which the copy reaches the reader.

**Ad / Situation:** The Guinness Guide to Oysters (1950).
**Application:** Designed explicitly to look like editorial content. Nine varieties of oyster, each with a photograph, each with a caption carrying the oyster's name and a fact. Serif body, three columns, readable line length, no advertising gadgetry. Readers wrote in for framable reprints. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Readers wrote in for copies. Spawned a series on game birds and cheeses. Demonstrates that "pretend you are an editor" is not a metaphor; it is the layout instruction that turns a Guinness ad into something a reader will paste on the wall.

**Ad / Situation:** KLM front-page newspaper launch for Guinness-style editorial format.
**Application:** "Editorial graphics also work well in newspapers. I made this launch ad for Guinness look like the front page of a newspaper." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The front-page format is particularly appropriate for the announcement of a new product.
**Result:** Cited by Ogilvy as a superior readership format for launches. Demonstrates that the editorial-style layout is not a magazine-only move; it travels.

**Ad / Situation:** The 47 reverse-type advertisements in one German magazine (counted by Ogilvy).
**Application:** A reverse case. Ogilvy counted forty-seven ads set in reverse — white on black — in a single issue of a German magazine, long after research had shown reverse reduces readership. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* "When I suggested that they test black type on a white background, they raised twice as much money." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* (referring to a charity that ran reverse-type fundraising ads)
**Result:** Demonstrates that the layout rules are ignored even by the professionals whose jobs depend on them. The client that tests the rule instead of arguing it wins.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Set body copy in sans-serif.
**Why:** "The eye is a creature of habit. People are accustomed to reading books, magazines and newspapers in lowercase" serif type. "Sanserif in page-size sheets repels readership as wax paper repels water" (John Updike, quoted in *Ogilvy on Advertising*). Use serif for body. Reserve sans-serif for captions and the odd mandatory.

**Don't:** Set copy in reverse (white on black).
**Why:** I have counted 47 reverse ads in one magazine long after readership research killed the technique. A charity that ran reverse-type fundraising ads raised twice as much money when the copy was set black on white. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Reverse is a self-inflicted wound.

**Don't:** Set the headline in all capitals.
**Why:** Capitals retard reading. They have no ascenders or descenders to help you recognize words, and tend to be read letter by letter. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Lowercase headlines match reading habit and are read faster.

**Don't:** Put a period at the end of the headline.
**Why:** A full stop is a full stop. It stops the reader dead. You will find no full stops at the end of headlines in newspapers, which have been tested against readership for a century. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Put the headline above the illustration.
**Why:** Readers scan top to bottom; their eye lands first on the picture. Headlines below the illustration are read by 10 per cent more people. On a 20-million-reader magazine, that is two million additional readers — "not to be sneezed at." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Yet 59 per cent of magazine ads still put the headline above.

**Don't:** Skip the caption on a photograph.
**Why:** Four times as many people read captions as read body copy. A photograph with no caption is a wasted second headline. Every caption should carry the brand name and the promise. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Set copy in one wide column of 120 characters.
**Why:** "People are accustomed to reading newspapers which are set about 40 characters wide." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A 120-character line is beyond the eye's tracking distance; the reader loses his place and turns the page.

**Don't:** Approve the layout at 15 feet from a bulletin board.
**Why:** That is how ads end up set in 72-point headlines which are unreadable at the 20-inch distance the magazine is actually held at. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Review the layout at reading distance, on paper, at actual size.

**Don't:** Use two-page spreads when a single page would do.
**Why:** Spreads cost twice as much as single pages but rarely get twice the readership or pull twice as many coupons. "Nine times out of ten, double-spreads are no more than self-indulgence by an art director who wants his advertisements to look big and juicy. If you swear off double-spreads, you will be able to run twice as many advertisements for the same money." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Use a page cluttered with trademarks, slogans, and corporate gadgetry.
**Why:** They "add to the gadgetry which clutters up layouts, and proclaim 'this is only an advertisement'. Readership of the advertisement is reduced." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Every mandatory at the foot of the page is a tax on readership.
