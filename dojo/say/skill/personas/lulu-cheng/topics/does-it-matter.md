# Does It Actually Matter? (Master Diagnostic)

## When to Use
- Something negative just happened — a post, an article, a tweet, a comment — and you're deciding whether to respond
- Your team is in reactive mode, responding to everything, burning hours
- You're about to write a response and haven't actually asked whether this warrants one
- You want a filter to apply before engaging deterrence or crisis mode

## Fails When
- You're in an actual crisis (injury, illegality, major trust breach) — run PATSLF instead
- The attack is a slow burn that will compound — "reaching people that matter" has a time component
- You apply it to legitimate criticism and miss real product feedback
- You use the diagnostic to justify silence when the answer is actually to respond

## Core Concept

Before you spiral into response mode, ask: does this actually matter?

Don't waste your one wild and precious life responding to every single thing if it doesn't matter. Most things don't matter. Most criticisms are noise. Most attacks are from people your audience doesn't care about. Negative things will always exist somewhere on the internet. That's not the test. The test is whether those negative things are reaching people whose opinions you actually need to care about — and whether the accusation, if believed, would meaningfully affect how people perceive your integrity, capability, or trustworthiness.

Two criteria, both must be yes.

**Criterion 1: Is it reaching people that matter?** Customers. Employees. Investors. Partners. The media that shapes their perceptions. If the negative thing is contained to a dark corner of the internet with some crank and your actual audience is not there, that's fine. Let it go. Counter-case — sometimes being attacked helps you. If your audience distrusts the attacker, an attack from them is a credential. Republican politician attacked by AOC? Congratulations — that's good for you, you might even turn it into an ad. Know your audience; know the attacker.

**Criterion 2: Is it something material?** Non-material things don't deserve responses. Someone says your office snacks were bad? Non-material. Someone says you lied about what a partnership would involve? Material. The test: does this accusation, if believed, meaningfully affect how people perceive your company's integrity, capability, or trustworthiness?

If both are yes, respond immediately and aggressively. You can't do the "oh, I don't know, it feels so bad, maybe it'll go away" thing. It doesn't go away. It ossifies. If either criterion is no, ignore it. This discipline is hard because ignoring things feels dangerous. It feels like inaction. But responding to things that don't meet both criteria is also dangerous — you validate attacks by acknowledging them, you amplify noise by engaging with it, you spend resources on fights that didn't matter. Save your ammunition.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the audience that matters.** Write it down. Customers? Employees? Enterprise buyers? Regulators? If you can't name them specifically, you're not ready to judge whether the attack reaches them.

2. **Check reach.** Is the attack actually landing in front of them? Is it in a channel they use? Are allies forwarding it to them? Or is it isolated to a venue they'll never visit?

3. **Check the attacker.** Is the attacker someone your audience trusts or distrusts? If distrusts — the attack might be a credential rather than damage.

4. **Test materiality.** Imagine your core audience fully believes the claim. Does their perception of your integrity, capability, or trustworthiness change meaningfully? If yes, material. If not really, not material.

5. **If both yes — respond now.** See `hit-pieces.md`. Broken nose principle applies. Don't wait.

6. **If either no — ignore.** Genuinely ignore. Don't post a "look, we're above this" subtweet. Don't have employees quietly respond. Let it die in the corner it's already in.

7. **Recheck in 24-48 hours.** A thing that didn't meet the criteria can migrate. A post in a niche subreddit can get picked up by a journalist. The diagnostic is not one-and-done; revisit if the reach changes.

## Examples

**Situation:** A crank blog with 500 readers writes a hit post about your company, accusing you of vague wrongdoing. Your customers are enterprise buyers who don't read that blog.
**Application:** Criterion 1: not reaching people that matter. Criterion 2: vague, not specific or provable. Both no.
**Result:** Ignore. Spend zero minutes on it. Responding publicly would actually elevate the crank to your customers for the first time.

**Situation:** A journalist at a trade publication your enterprise customers read claims you misled a partner on a specific deal. They've named facts. The piece is circulating in buyer circles.
**Application:** Criterion 1: yes, your audience reads this. Criterion 2: yes, a lying-to-partners claim hits trustworthiness directly.
**Result:** Respond immediately. Go direct. Broken nose principle. Apply the narrow-offense version of the pressure equation — specific response, specific facts, specific counter-evidence.

**Situation:** Republican politician. AOC attacks on Twitter.
**Application:** Criterion 1: yes, reaching political Twitter. But the attacker is someone your audience distrusts. The attack is a credential, not damage.
**Result:** Lean in. Turn it into an ad. "Thanks for the endorsement, @AOC." The framework inverts: attacks from the right enemies are better marketing than any campaign.

**Situation:** Someone on a small podcast says your product is overpriced and the founder is pretentious. No specific accusations. Small audience outside your enterprise buyer base.
**Application:** Criterion 1: not reaching people that matter. Criterion 2: subjective complaint, not material. Both no.
**Result:** Ignore. A response would send your customers to the podcast. Silence is correct.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Use the diagnostic to rationalize silence when you're actually in an attack that matters. If an attack is reaching your customers and makes a material claim, "does it actually matter" is a yes. Don't hide from the broken nose principle by pretending you're being disciplined.
**Why:** The cost of ignoring a material attack that's landing is permanent. Narratives ossify. Waiting is losing. Discipline means ignoring the noise — not the signal.

**Don't:** Treat every piece of criticism as an attack requiring the diagnostic. Real customer feedback isn't an attack — it's information. Apply this diagnostic to malicious or reputational attacks, not to your customer complaining their onboarding flow is broken.
**Why:** Mis-classifying legitimate criticism as a malicious attack alienates people acting in good faith and draws more attention to real product issues.

**Don't:** Run the diagnostic in your own head and skip writing down the audience. The discipline of specifying "who exactly matters here" is the entire point. Vague answers to criterion 1 produce vague conclusions.
**Why:** "Our customers" is not specific enough. "Enterprise buyers at companies over $100M ARR who are currently evaluating us" is. The more specific the audience, the clearer the reach check.

**Don't:** Respond to something that doesn't meet the criteria just because it emotionally bothers you. Founders spiral. An attack that hurts personally isn't automatically material or reaching.
**Why:** Over-response amplifies the attack. It tells the world this attack really bothered you. It makes the attacker look more important than they are. If it stings but doesn't matter, take a walk. Don't post.

**Don't:** Conclude "doesn't matter" without rechecking. A post in a corner today can be a news article next week.
**Why:** The diagnostic has a time dimension. Reach can expand. Recheck when the situation moves.
