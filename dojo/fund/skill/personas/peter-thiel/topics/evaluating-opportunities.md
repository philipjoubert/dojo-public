# Evaluating Opportunities — the Diagnostic Toolkit

## When to Use
- Running diligence on a startup, a pitch, or a business plan.
- Evaluating a founder you have just met.
- Reviewing a strategy document that feels plausible but thin.
- A deal is moving fast and you need shortcuts that are better than vibes.
- Diagnosing why a pitch "feels off" despite no single obvious flaw.

## Fails When
- The diagnostic is used as a substitute for thinking about the substance. These are adjuncts that sharpen judgment, not replacements for it.
- The company is mature and the relevant evaluation is operational (efficiency, execution) rather than foundational.
- The framework is applied mechanically, scoring without reflection.

## Core Concept

Everything else in the Thiel body of work describes how to build. This chapter is about how to evaluate — how to look at a business, a founder, or a strategy and determine whether it is real or fake, durable or fragile. Each diagnostic below is self-contained. Each one reveals something most evaluators miss.

The general rule: *"You always want to get the team, the technology, and the business strategy. If people want to talk about the technology, talk about the people and the business strategy. Go to the topics they don't want to talk about."*

### The Contrarian Truth Test

The contrarian question — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — becomes a diagnostic tool when applied to business evaluation. It is not just an interview trick. It separates founders who have genuine insight from founders who are executing on consensus.

When evaluating a startup, ask: what does this founder believe that the rest of the market does not? If the answer is "nothing" — there is no secret, no edge, no monopoly potential. If everyone understands it is a great company and you understand it too, "then you're just in a crazy auction where whoever wins the auction may really lose because you will overpay."

Good answers are specific and uncomfortable. Bad answers are either consensus disguised as contrarianism ("our education system is broken") or abstract positions with no business implications.

### The Market Definition Diagnostic

How a founder defines their market tells you whether they understand monopoly dynamics.

**Red flags:**
- Huge TAM. "We're in the $X-trillion energy market." A giant market means brutal competition and near-zero probability of dominance.
- "We just need 1% of a $100 billion market." You will never reach even 1% of a genuinely large market. The pitch sounds modest; the math is delusional.
- Category intersections to create a fictional niche. "We're the only British Nepalese fusion restaurant in downtown Palo Alto." Intersecting two or three narrow categories does not create a monopoly. It creates an illusion of one.

**Green flags:**
- Tiny specific niche they can dominate immediately — not "eventually," now.
- 60%+ market share in the initial market, with evidence. PayPal hit 25-35% of eBay PowerSellers in three to four months. Facebook hit 60% of Harvard in ten days.
- Clear plan for concentric expansion. Amazon: books → CDs → videos → everything. Facebook: Harvard → Ivy League → all colleges → everyone. The initial market was tiny; the expansion path was deliberate.

**Underlying logic:** monopolists lie by defining their market broadly to appear as small players (Google calls itself "a technology company" rather than "a search engine"). Competitors lie by defining their market narrowly to appear as monopolists (every restaurant in Palo Alto claims to be unique). The diagnostic move is to ask which way the founder is lying to themselves — and correct for it.

### The Founder Assessment

Evaluate character, conviction, and capacity for original thought — not credentials.

**Prehistory of the team.** How long have the founders known each other? If the answer is "we just met at a networking event," that is a bad prehistory. If deep and generative, good. Founder relationships under crisis depend on what was there before the crisis.

**Conviction vs. consensus-seeking.** Does the founder have strong specific views about the future? Or do they defer to whatever the market, advisors, or investors say? The tell: how they respond to pushback. A founder with conviction will tell you why you are wrong. A consensus-seeker will ask what you think and adjust accordingly.

**The "Will They Sell?" test.** If a founder would sell the company for a reasonable large number, they probably do not have conviction. Companies with definite plans do not sell, because the founders know what they are building and value it more than any acquirer's cash. Every company that was offered $1B and refused — eBay, Google, Facebook — was right.

**The suit test.** Never invest in a tech CEO who wears a suit. The suit signals the founder is playing a role rather than building something. Real technologists wear T-shirts and jeans. This is a crude proxy but remarkably accurate in practice; it is really a proxy for independence of mind. The founder too socially calibrated to skip the suit is usually too socially calibrated to hold an original view.

**Calibration from experience.** People from very successful companies learn the wrong lesson — "it's easy." People from failed companies learn the opposite wrong lesson — "it's impossible." The correct calibration is "hard but doable." PayPal alumni learned this, which is why so many went on to build billion-dollar companies.

**Starting point.** "I don't want to be an entrepreneur" is a better starting point than "I want to be an entrepreneur." The latter is in the same category as "I want to be rich." Good founders start with a specific problem they are obsessed with solving. The entrepreneurship is a byproduct of the obsession.

### The Buzzword Detector

The more buzzwords in the pitch, the less there is underneath.

Buzzwords function as tells in poker. "If I gave you a startup that was a concatenation of buzzwords — 'we're building a mobile platform for SaaS businesses to bring big data to the cloud' — my pattern recognition is that the buzzwords are a tell, like in poker, that you're bluffing."

During the cleantech bubble, every pitch sounded the same — "sustainable," "green," "clean," "innovative." The presentations were interchangeable because there were no real secrets underneath. When everyone uses the same words, no one is saying anything.

**Buzzwords that signal danger:** "Disruption." "Big data." "Cloud computing." "Platform." "Social entrepreneurship." "AI" when used without specifics about what the system actually does. "Sustainable." "Impact." Any term that has become a category rather than a description.

**How to apply it:** Count buzzwords in a pitch deck. High density = low insight density. Then ask the founder to explain the company without using any industry jargon. If they cannot, they may not understand their own business.

### The Secret Plan Assessment

A real company has a "secret plan" — a long-term vision not yet visible to the market.

**What a secret plan looks like:** Apple under Jobs — multi-year product roadmaps executed against, specific products designed years in advance. Facebook under Zuckerberg — a specific roadmap of products Yahoo could not value because they did not yet exist. SpaceX under Musk — a clear trajectory from reusable rockets to Mars, each step building on the previous one.

**What absence looks like:** Companies optimizing for free cash flow because they have no ideas for investment. Companies described by process rather than products — "we're lean," "we're agile." Founders who would accept acquisition offers.

**The diagnostic question:** "Where will this company be in ten years?" If specific and detailed, there is a plan. If hedged, generalized, or deferred to market conditions, there is no plan — only hope.

### The Timing Diagnostic

"Why hasn't anyone done this before?"

**Good answers:**
- "The technology didn't exist until now."
- "Market conditions have just shifted."

**Answers that require investigation:**
- "People have tried and failed." Could mean the timing was wrong before and is right now, or the idea is simply bad. Dig into the specific failures.
- "Nobody thought of it." Almost never true. Others thought of it and dismissed it for reasons you need to understand.

The first-mover trap: being first is not the same as being right on timing. First movers without durable advantages are just infrastructure for companies that come after.

### The Value Capture Diagnostic (X × Y%)

A business creates X dollars of value and captures Y% of it. These are independent variables.

Most founders think only about X. The question is always: can you capture Y?

Airlines create hundreds of billions of dollars of value; they capture 37 cents per passenger trip. Google creates less total value and captures 21% margin. A business with large X and tiny Y is a charity. A business with modest X but large Y is a monopoly. A business with large X and large Y is Google.

When evaluating a business, separately assess: how much value does this create? What percentage can it capture? What structural factors determine Y (monopoly characteristics: proprietary technology, network effects, scale, brand)?

### The Imitation vs. Innovation Diagnostic

Is this 0-to-1 or 1-to-n?

**1-to-n tells:**
- "I want to be the Google for X" or "the Amazon for Y." Template-plus-slot is imitation.
- Following the HBS herd into whatever sector is currently hot.
- Defining the company by what it is disrupting rather than creating.

**0-to-1 tells:**
- The founder describes a future that does not yet exist and has a specific plan to build it.
- The company creates a new category rather than entering an existing one.
- No obvious competitors, because the thing being built has not been built before.
- When asked "who are your competitors?" the founder struggles because the question does not map.

"The next Bill Gates will not build an operating system. The next Larry Page won't start a search engine. If you are copying these guys, you aren't learning from them."

### The Bubble Detection Framework

**Preconditions for a bubble:**
- Public involvement is nearly necessary. If the general public is not participating, it is probably not a bubble. In 1999, ~300 tech companies IPO'd per year (bubble). In 2014, ~30 (not a bubble).
- Psycho-social herd phenomenon. People copying each other's excitement.
- Consensus enthusiasm without independent analysis. When everyone agrees something is great and no one can articulate specific differentiated reasons, the enthusiasm is mimetic, not analytical.

### The Team / Technology / Strategy Triangle

Three vectors: team, technology, business strategy. Whatever the founder wants to talk about is usually where the strength is. Whatever they avoid is usually where the problem is.

**Diagnostic move:** Talk about the other two. If they are excited about technology, probe team dynamics and business model. If excited about team, probe technology and strategy.

**A specific trap:** People from very successful companies often do not understand what made the company work. An early Google employee may think the key was "hiring smart people." The actual key was the monopoly business model built on dominant search market share. The lesson extracted is wrong, and it will be applied incorrectly to the next venture.

## How to Apply

1. **Run multiple diagnostics in parallel, not sequentially.** The contrarian test, the market-definition test, the suit test, the buzzword test, the team/tech/strategy triangle — all are doing different work. Use them together; a pitch that fails any one deserves investigation; a pitch that fails several is almost always what it appears.

2. **Investigate where the founder will not go.** The team/tech/strategy triangle applies to every meeting. Push into the dimension the founder is quietest about. If they love talking about technology, spend the meeting on distribution and market definition. If they love talking about their team, press on the secret.

3. **Use the buzzword count as a filter, not a verdict.** A heavily buzzworded pitch is a red flag, but some founders are bad communicators and have real insight underneath. Force them to explain the company without jargon. If they cannot, the buzzwords were the substance — pass.

4. **Test the will-they-sell question even if no offer is on the table.** "What would it take for you to sell the company?" is a useful hypothetical. The answer reveals whether the founder has a plan worth more than any reasonable number.

5. **Correct for monopolist-vs-competitor market-definition lies automatically.** Assume both sides are lying in opposite directions. Google-class monopolists broaden the market to look small. Commodity competitors narrow the market to look unique. Reframe the founder's TAM claim before accepting it.

6. **Apply the "hard but doable" calibration test.** Ask the founder how hard this will be. The answer reveals their calibration. "It's easy, the technology is great" is the miscalibrated successful-company alumnus. "It's impossible, the market is brutal" is the miscalibrated failed-company alumnus. "It will be difficult and will take ten years and we have specific plans for how" is the PayPal-alum calibration.

7. **Prefer founders who cannot not be founders.** "I don't want to be an entrepreneur but I have to solve this problem" over "I want to be a founder." The first is vocation; the second is aspiration. Aspirations pivot; vocations keep building.

## Examples

**Situation:** An investor meets a Series A founder. The pitch is polished. The founder wears a blazer. The deck contains eighteen uses of "AI-powered," seven of "platform," and five of "disruption." Market size is given as $1.2 trillion; the company claims "we just need 2%." The team is ex-FAANG with impressive credentials.

**Application:** Nearly every diagnostic flashes red. Suit test fails (blazer, performance-of-role over building). Buzzword density is extreme. Market definition is the 1%-of-a-huge-market red flag. The team pitch is ex-FAANG credentials without specific prehistory of working together on this problem. If the contrarian question produces a conventional answer ("the industry is underserved" or similar), the full picture is a well-dressed commodity with no secret. Pass unless the investigation into why-hasn't-anyone-done-this-before produces a specific surprising answer.

**Result:** A remarkably high fraction of pitches fail on this many dimensions at once. The filter is the point — it lets you spend time on the rare pitch that passes.

---

**Situation:** A founder is slightly rumpled, gives technical answers that are detailed and hard to follow, struggles to answer "what's your TAM" because she wants to talk about the specific 2,000-customer starting market, and explicitly says she is not interested in an acquisition offer "because the product roadmap is what the company is, and selling means giving it up."

**Application:** Nearly every diagnostic flashes green. The scruffy appearance passes the suit test; the technical depth suggests obsession; the refusal to inflate TAM suggests she understands monopoly dynamics; the will-not-sell answer suggests conviction about a definite plan. The remaining work is to probe the technology (is the 10x claim real?), the distribution plan (is there a channel for her product?), and the secret (does her answer to the contrarian question hold up?). But the structural markers point strongly positive.

**Result:** Founders who pass the diagnostics are rare and worth fighting for — the competitive market for deals is mostly among the ones that fail most diagnostics, which is why those deals are overpriced and the diagnostic-passing deals are often accessible at reasonable valuations.

---

**Situation:** A senior partner is pushing the firm to invest in a "generative AI for legal contracts" company. The pitch is a slick deck. The market is "the $500B legal services industry." The founders are two ex-Goldman analysts with no legal or technical background who met six months ago at a startup accelerator.

**Application:** Run the diagnostics. Buzzwords: generative AI, platform, disruption. Market definition: $500B is the huge-TAM red flag. Founder prehistory: six months, bad. Team/tech/strategy: the team is finance, the technology is a third-party LLM, the strategy is "sell to law firms" — none of the three vectors is a strength. Contrarian question: almost certainly produces a convention ("law is underserved by technology"). Suit test: ex-Goldman, probably fails. If the senior partner is pushing despite all of this, the pressure is social, not analytical — the mimetic pull of the AI sector, not a read on the company. The right move is to name each failed diagnostic specifically and ask which of them the partner thinks is wrong.

**Result:** Partner discussions about specific deals often devolve into vibes. The diagnostics force a specific factual conversation — "the team has no legal background and no technical background; which is the source of edge?" — that usually dissolves the vibes-based enthusiasm.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Score a pitch on a 1-10 rubric and call it diligence.
**Why:** The diagnostics are not additive scores. One catastrophic failure (no secret, no distribution plan, cofounders who met last week) is enough to kill the deal, regardless of how many green flags exist elsewhere. Treat each failure as potentially fatal, not as one tenth of an average.

**Don't:** Confuse polish with substance.
**Why:** Polished pitches are often polished because the underlying substance is weak and needs to be compensated for with presentation. Rough pitches are often rough because the founder is spending time on the product rather than on the deck. The correlation between presentation quality and company quality is weakly negative in technology, not positive.

**Don't:** Take founder charisma as evidence of ability to build.
**Why:** Charisma is a distinct skill that sometimes co-occurs with ability to build and sometimes does not. The suit test is really a charisma-skepticism test; the point is to separate "can perform the CEO role" from "can build the company." Many great founders have been mediocre public performers. Many terrible founders have been magnetic.

**Don't:** Use the diagnostics as a check-the-box substitute for thinking.
**Why:** The diagnostics are sharpening tools, not answers. They tell you where to investigate, not what to conclude. A founder who fails the suit test but passes the contrarian test deeply may still be a great bet. The diagnostics direct attention; judgment still has to be exercised.

**Don't:** Ignore a failed diagnostic because "everything else is strong."
**Why:** Power-law outcomes require nearly every dimension to be present. One fatally weak dimension — no distribution plan, no secret, no prehistory — is often exactly the dimension the company dies on. Do not paper over specific weak answers with general strong impressions.
