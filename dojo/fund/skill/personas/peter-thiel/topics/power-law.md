# The Power Law

## When to Use
- Structuring a venture fund or an angel portfolio.
- A founder is spreading engineering effort across three markets, two products, and five channels.
- A career decision framed as "hedging" when one move is obviously dominant.
- Someone treating startup outcomes as if they fell on a bell curve.
- Evaluating whether a portfolio strategy reflects conviction or the absence of conviction.

## Fails When
- The domain is genuinely low-variance — ordinary employment, utility investments, operational payroll — where concentrating produces no reward proportional to the risk.
- "Diversification" means genuinely uncorrelated risks rather than multiple shots at the same tail outcome (e.g., diversifying across asset classes that move differently in a recession is different from diversifying across many venture bets).
- Early in a discovery process where the right play is deliberate exploration, not concentration.

## Core Concept

The biggest secret in venture capital is that the best investment in a successful fund equals or outperforms the entire rest of the fund combined. This is not a metaphor. It is not an approximation. It is the literal structure of venture returns.

At Founders Fund, Facebook returned more than all our other investments combined. Palantir, the second-best, returned more than every remaining investment after Facebook. The pattern continued down the line. This is not an anomaly of our fund. It is the fundamental law governing outcomes in technology, in business, and in life.

Most people do not understand the power law. They think about the world in terms of averages, normal distributions, and bell curves. They plan for the median outcome. They diversify to smooth out variation. They treat life as a portfolio of roughly equivalent bets. This is the single most costly error in thinking.

### The Power Law in Venture Capital

The returns of a venture fund do not follow a normal distribution. They follow a power law. A tiny number of investments generate nearly all the returns. Everything else is a rounding error.

This produces two rules for venture investors:

**Rule One:** Only invest in companies that have the potential to return the value of the entire fund. If a fund is $100 million, every investment must have a plausible path to returning $100 million or more to the fund. This sounds extreme. It is the minimum rational standard.

**Rule Two:** Because rule one is so restrictive, there cannot be any other rules.

Most venture capitalists do not follow these rules. They invest in forty, sixty, a hundred companies per fund. They call this "spray and pray" or "portfolio diversification." What it actually means is that they do not understand the power law — they are treating returns as though they followed a bell curve, as though the twentieth-best investment might contribute meaningfully. It will not. In a power-law distribution, the twentieth-best investment is noise.

At Founders Fund, we focus on five to seven companies per fund, each of which we believe could become a multibillion-dollar business based on its unique fundamentals. This is not temperament or style. It is the only rational response to a power-law distribution.

The spray-and-pray approach has a psychological explanation its practitioners rarely admit. They diversify not because they understand portfolio theory, but because they lack conviction. They cannot identify which company will be the outlier, so they invest in everything and hope. Once you accept a small probability of a big payoff, small number times big number normally equals a small number. You have already psyched yourself into writing checks without thinking.

### The Power Law Inside Companies

The power law does not stop at the boundary of a venture fund. It operates inside every company.

One market will matter more than all others. One distribution channel will be worth more than every other channel put together. The same concentration applies to products, strategic decisions, even individual hires. The most important thing a founder does is identify which market, which product, and which channel sits at the top of the power curve — and concentrate everything on that single point.

Most founders do not operate this way. They spread attention across multiple products, multiple markets, multiple channels. They optimize for average performance rather than finding the one thing that actually matters. They spend equal time on their tenth-best initiative and their best initiative. This is the organizational equivalent of spray-and-pray: it feels prudent, but it guarantees mediocrity.

The search space of all businesses is much bigger than the search space of great businesses. If you are iterating randomly, you are exploring a vast landscape that is mostly barren. The power law tells you that the fertile ground is concentrated in a tiny area. Iteration finds local maxima. Conviction, informed by deep analysis, finds the one thing that matters.

### The Power Law in Life

The power law applies to careers and life decisions with the same force it applies to venture capital. One career move will matter more than all others combined. One relationship, one insight, one commitment will outweigh everything else.

*Life is not a portfolio.*

This is the most important sentence in the chapter. The portfolio approach — try many things, keep options open, don't commit too deeply — is the application of indefinite thinking to human existence. It sounds wise. It is catastrophically wrong.

Our schools teach the opposite of power-law thinking. Institutionalized education traffics in homogenized, generic knowledge. Every university believes in "excellence," and hundred-page course catalogs seem designed to reassure you that "it doesn't matter what you do, as long as you do it well." That is completely false. It does matter what you do. Some paths generate ten or a hundred or a thousand times more value than others. Choosing the right path matters more than executing any particular path well.

The education tournament selects for people who are good at competing, not good at choosing. Students learn to win at whatever game is placed in front of them. What they never learn is how to evaluate whether the structure itself is worth their time. Elite students climb confidently until they reach a level of competition sufficiently intense to beat their dreams out of them. Higher education becomes the place where people who had big plans in high school get stuck in fierce rivalries with equally smart peers over conventional careers like management consulting and investment banking. The power law tells you that the differences between companies dwarf the differences in roles inside companies. Joining the right company matters far more than negotiating a better title at the wrong one.

### Why People Don't See the Power Law

The power law is counterintuitive because human intuition is calibrated for normal distributions. Most daily experience feels roughly average. But the things that determine life outcomes — which company you join, which technology you back, which market you enter — follow a power law. The mismatch between intuition and reality means people consistently misallocate attention, time, and capital.

This is why the power law is the biggest secret in venture capital. It is not a secret because it is hidden — the math is simple, the data is available. It is a secret because people cannot make themselves believe it. They know the power law intellectually but act as though returns are normally distributed. They diversify when they should concentrate. They spend equal time on every initiative when they should spend all their time on one. They keep their options open when they should commit.

### Concentration Is Not Recklessness

The power law creates a temptation to take the most extreme bet possible. Swing for the fences. Maximum risk, maximum reward. This is wrong for a specific reason: extreme optimism and extreme pessimism converge on the same outcome — inaction.

Extreme optimism says: it will happen anyway. Progress is inevitable. You don't need to work. Extreme pessimism says: it's impossible. The odds are against you. Nothing you do will matter. Both positions lead to not working hard. Both arrive at the same destination from opposite directions.

The productive position is neither. It is hard but doable. The future is not guaranteed. It is not impossible. It is contingent on specific people making specific decisions. The power law makes the stakes enormous — the difference between finding the right path and the wrong path is 1000x, not 2x. But the existence of the power law does not tell you *which* path is right. It tells you only that the choice matters enormously.

This is why the power law demands conviction, not recklessness. You must concentrate your efforts, but you must concentrate them on the right thing. Random concentration is worse than thoughtful diversification. The power law raises the stakes of every decision, which means every decision deserves more thought, not less.

## How to Apply

1. **For every investment, ask: can this return the fund?** If the answer is no, do not invest. This eliminates the vast majority of opportunities — which is the point. The power law means most opportunities are not worth pursuing. Your job is to find the rare outlier, not to harvest every plausible deal.

2. **For founders, identify the one of everything.** The one market that matters most. The one product that matters most. The one distribution channel that matters most. Concentrate there. Do not spread across markets, products, or channels until you have dominated the first one. The temptation to expand prematurely is indefinite thinking — it spreads resources because you lack conviction about where the maximum value lies.

3. **For career decisions, prefer the right company over the right role.** The differences between companies dwarf the differences in roles inside companies. A good role at a power-law-tail company beats a great title at an average one. Do not agonize over negotiating a better title at a company that is not on the tail.

4. **For time allocation, ruthlessly prioritize.** In a power-law world, you cannot afford not to think hard about where your actions will fall on the curve. The one thing that matters most deserves the majority of your time. Everything else is a distraction. The refusal to prioritize — the desire to be "well-rounded" or to "keep options open" — is the most expensive mistake you can make.

5. **Check whether "diversification" is really "absence of conviction."** Every time you diversify a decision (across investments, projects, time allocation, career moves), ask: am I spreading because I have a theory about why these risks are uncorrelated, or because I cannot commit to any single one? The first is portfolio theory. The second is epistemic cowardice dressed up as prudence.

6. **Calibrate between extremes.** The power law does not mean "take the most extreme bet." It means "concentrate on the right bet, and accept that most bets are not worth making." Hard but doable. Neither "it will happen anyway" nor "it's impossible."

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series B founder runs a company with three products in three different markets. He claims each is growing well. Revenue is evenly split across them. His team is stretched. His engineering roadmap is a triage.

**Application:** The even split across three products is the tell. In a power-law world, one of these three products is massively more important than the other two combined, and the founder has not identified which one — which is why his attention is split equally. The question to force: if you had to kill two of these products tomorrow, which would you keep? The answer is almost always clearer than the founder admits. Then the second question: what would change in the company if 100% of engineering went into the one you kept? The answer is usually that the kept product would grow twice as fast, compete with a different class of company, and start building something that is not merely "a product in a crowded space" but a category leader. The reason the founder resists this exercise is that committing feels like giving up optionality — but optionality in a power-law world is the slow decay of all three products into average outcomes.

**Result:** The hardest but most valuable exercise for founders at this stage. The ones who concentrate usually report six months later that the discarded products were never going to be winners and the freed-up focus was the single most important move they made.

---

**Situation:** A large university endowment is deciding whether to allocate $200M across 40 venture funds as a "diversified VC exposure" or to allocate the same $200M to eight concentrated funds.

**Application:** The 40-fund approach is equivalent to buying an index of venture capital — which is precisely what the performance will converge to. Venture as an asset class underperforms the S&P when measured across the median. The concentrated eight-fund approach forces the endowment to do the work of picking managers with genuine edge, which is the only way to earn the power-law returns that justify the liquidity cost of private investment. If the endowment does not have the capacity to pick good managers, the honest move is not to allocate to venture at all — not to buy the index. Allocating broadly is the indefinite move; it produces nothing except the appearance of exposure.

**Result:** Endowments that concentrate on manager selection usually report dramatically better returns than those that diversify broadly — and the ones that diversify broadly usually later move to concentration after a decade of mediocre results.

---

**Situation:** A 32-year-old product leader at a strong company has been offered two roles: VP Product at a Series A startup with a founder who has a compelling specific vision, or Senior Director at a FAANG company with a well-known name and higher total compensation.

**Application:** The power law says the difference between companies dwarfs the difference between titles. The Series A role at a company with a real thesis has a plausible path to a power-law outcome — the product leader could be part of a $10B company ten years out. The FAANG role is a roughly predictable above-average career with no upside tail. The question is not "which role is safer" — it is "which outcome does he want, and which company could produce it?" The FAANG role is the indefinite move (high floor, low ceiling, optionality). The startup role is the definite move (lower floor, tail outcome possible). If the founder's thesis is real and specific, the startup role is the correct power-law choice.

**Result:** Many people choose the FAANG role because the decision is "safer." The power law predicts their career outcome will be above average but not tail; that is exactly what happens. The ones who take the startup bet on a real thesis have a distribution of outcomes that is much more bimodal — some lose, some end up substantially ahead of where FAANG would have taken them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Diversify because you cannot decide.
**Why:** Diversification driven by indecision is not portfolio theory; it is a confession. Real portfolio theory diversifies across truly uncorrelated risks. Venture-style diversification across many similar bets is just many shots at the same bet, which does not reduce risk but does guarantee median returns.

**Don't:** Treat "risk-adjusted returns" as a strategic frame for tail-distributed outcomes.
**Why:** Risk-adjusted returns are a sensible metric for normally-distributed outcomes. For power-law outcomes, the distribution has no meaningful variance — the tail is so fat that standard deviations are uninformative. Optimizing for "risk-adjusted" in a tail-distributed world produces the appearance of prudence with none of the upside.

**Don't:** Spread engineering effort evenly across multiple products "until we learn which one wins."
**Why:** Even spreading produces even mediocrity. You will not learn which one wins, because none of them is getting enough concentrated effort to produce a tail outcome. One of the three is almost certainly the right bet. The fact that you can't tell which one means you need more investigation, not more spreading.

**Don't:** Mistake "well-roundedness" for "excellence."
**Why:** Well-roundedness is optimized for the middle of a distribution. Power-law outcomes live at the tail. The best founders, best investors, best operators are usually jagged — extraordinary at a few things, mediocre at many others. The educational system's preference for well-roundedness is a calibration error in exactly the domain where it matters most.

**Don't:** Apply power-law logic outside its domain.
**Why:** Not every distribution is a power law. Mortality rates, commuting times, most physical measurements, most employment income — roughly normal. Concentrating a retirement account because "the power law applies" is wrong. The discipline is to recognize the domain. Venture capital, technology companies, cultural influence, career outcomes at the top of a profession, investment returns in innovation — power law. Most other things — normal.
