# Hiring Great People — Drive, Curiosity, Ethics, and How to Actually Run the Process

## When to Use
- Founder is putting together an early team and wants to know what to value.
- Founder is about to hire a first executive — VP Eng, VP Sales, CFO — and is nervous.
- Founder is considering a candidate from a hugely successful incumbent (IBM in the 80s, Microsoft in the 90s, Google today) and wondering if the halo is real.
- Someone is avoiding firing a bad hire and needs the frame for why they should do it now.

## Fails When
- A role has a genuine, specialized, hard-won domain skill where experience *does* dominate drive (deeply technical scientific roles, certain regulated functions). The framework still works but weights need adjusting.
- It's used as a checklist rather than as a set of priors. The point is not a scoring rubric. The point is which signals to *trust* and which to *discount*.

## Core Concept

Lots of people will tell you to hire for intelligence. Especially in this industry. "Hire the smartest people and your company's success is all but guaranteed." **I think intelligence, per se, is highly overrated.** I'm unaware of any actual data correlating raw intelligence — on any standard metric — with company success. Clearly you don't want to hire dumb people, and clearly you'd like to work with smart people. But the lore comes from two stratospherically successful companies — Microsoft with its logic puzzles, Google with its educational-achievement hierarchy — and I'm not aware of *another* Microsoft built by hiring on logic puzzles, or *another* Google built by hiring PhDs. Maybe their success had to do with other factors — huge markets, extreme aggressiveness, right time/right place, key distribution deals, and at least in one case, great products.

So maybe there are hiring criteria that are equally, or more, important. I think there are three.

**First, drive.** Drive is self-motivation — people who will walk right through brick walls, on their own power, without having to be asked, to achieve whatever goal is in front of them. People with drive push and push and push until they succeed. Some people have it; some don't. Where it comes from doesn't really matter — guilt, family pressure, a burning desire to make it, raw Type-A — go with it. You can see it in a candidate's eyes and in their background. I look for something someone has *done*. Not been involved in, not been part of, not watched happen, not was hanging around when it happened. *Done*. The business they started and ran in high school. The nonprofit they started and ran in college. The open-source project they created or made major contributions to. Something. If you can't find anything — if a candidate has just followed the rules their whole lives, showed up for the right classes and the right tests and the right career opportunities without achieving something distinct and notable, relative to their starting point — then they probably aren't driven. And you're not going to change them. **Motivating people who are fundamentally unmotivated is not easy. Motivating people who are self-motivated is wind at your back.**

**Second, curiosity.** Curiosity is a proxy for: do you love what you do? Anyone who loves what they do is inherently intensely curious about their field, their profession, their craft. They read about it, study it, talk to other people about it — immerse themselves in it, continuously — not because they have to, but because they love to. Sit a programmer candidate down and ask about the ten most interesting things happening in software right now. If they love it, they'll have informed opinions. Same test works for marketers, salespeople, designers, operators. Curiosity and drive tend to coincide; the easiest way to *be* driven is to be in a field you love.

**Third, ethics.** Ethics are hard to test for. But **watch for *any* whiff of less than stellar ethics in any candidate's background or references. And avoid, avoid, avoid.** Unethical people are unethical by nature, and the odds of a metaphorical jailhouse conversion are quite low. Priests, rabbis, and ministers should give second chances on ethics. Hiring managers at startups should not. One honesty test: pick a topic you know intimately and ask increasingly esoteric questions until the candidate hits their limit. They'll either say "I don't know" or try to bullshit you. **If they bullshit you during the interview, they'll bullshit you on the job.**

Notice what's *not* on the list: raw IQ, pedigree, career success at a famous company. Especially that last one. **Beware people who have been at highly successful companies.** An awful lot of them were just along for the ride. Career success is great to look for, but you have to verify candidates out of hugely successful companies actually did what they claim in the roles they claim, and that they really get it — that the real world is tougher than being IBM in the 80s, Microsoft in the 90s, or Google today. Old IBM rule: never hire someone straight out of IBM. First let them go somewhere else and fail. *Then* hire them.

Process is the other half of hiring. Most startups have a random hiring process and hire apparently randomly. **Have a written hiring process.** Do basic skills tests (it's amazing how many candidates can't do the thing they claim to do — programmers who can't code a linked list on a whiteboard, "sales" people who can't close you on your own product). Plan and write down interview questions ahead of time — even if you've run interviews a hundred times, don't count on coming up with questions on the fly. Pay attention to the little things: person never laughs, probably hard to get along with; person constantly interrupts, egomaniac; person claims to be friends with someone you know but doesn't know what they're up to, bullshitter; person gives nonlinear answers to simple questions, disorganized on the job; person drones, get ready for hell. Pay attention to reference-call softballs: "sometimes wasn't that motivated" means they're a slug you'll have to kick every morning; "could sometimes be hard to get along with" means hugely unpleasant; "had an easier time working with men than women" means raging sexist; "was sometimes a little moody" means clinical depression, unmedicated.

Even if you do all this, **you'll still have something like a 70% success rate on individual contributors, and about 50% on executives. That's life.** Anyone who tells you otherwise is hiring poorly and doesn't realize it. So the last piece of the framework is: **fix your mistakes, fast — but not too fast.** Most startups are undisciplined about firing. You're going to hate doing it, and you're going to feel *way* better after. Your great people will be happy you did — they knew the person wasn't working out, they want to work with other great people, they want the average high. You're usually doing the person a favor, giving them a shot at a role where they might actually be a star. You're almost never pushing anyone onto the street; the industry is always hiring. *You're not that important in their lives.*

Executives are their own species. **The output of an executive is the output of her organization.** But in a startup, a successful executive has to do three more things at the same time: **build the organization** (often from scratch — big-company executives often never have to do this), **be a primary individual contributor** ("roll up her sleeves" — there's no shortage of things to do, and an executive who can't produce while building and running doesn't last), and **be a team player** (big companies tolerate internal rivalries; startups can't). The evaluation takes longer — you have to wait for the organization to produce output — but the damage from a bad executive is far worse. Andy Grove told me once: *you always fire a bad executive too late. If you're really good, you'll fire her about three months too late.* If you did it fast enough that it wasn't too late, you wouldn't have enough data and you'd look arbitrary.

## How to Apply

### Criteria — what to screen for:

1. **Drive.** Look for real *done*-things in someone's history. Favor candidates from challenging backgrounds who are nevertheless on par with their more fortunate peers — that usually implies drive. Favor candidates for whom this job is their big chance. I actually like hiring people who haven't done the specific job before but are determined to ace it.
2. **Curiosity.** Test it directly in the interview. "What are the most interesting things happening in your field right now?" If they shrug or regurgitate stale stuff, pass. The corollary: if someone in a long-tenured job isn't current, the job isn't keeping them current, and the kind of person you want wouldn't have tolerated that job for long anyway.
3. **Ethics.** Any whiff, pass. The "I don't know" test separates candidates who are confident and honest from candidates who will bullshit you. If they bullshit you once on something minor, you've seen the whole movie.

### Process — how to run it:

1. **Write the hiring process down.** Make sure everyone who interviews has a copy. This is the single cheapest way to go from random to consistent.
2. **Run basic skills tests.** Programmers code on a whiteboard (even pseudocode — they should be fluent with the basics). Salespeople sell you on your product all the way to a closed deal. Marketers whiteboard the launch of your next product. If they flail on fundamentals, it doesn't matter how great the resume is.
3. **Plan interview questions ahead and assign them across interviewers.** Iterate them as you interview more candidates. You're refining your criteria and your screens at the same time.
4. **Watch the little things.** The micro-behaviors in an hour of interview are the macro-behaviors on the job. Every odd thing you see becomes a bigger thing onboard.
5. **Do the reference calls. Translate the softballs.** Every euphemism maps to a stronger thing. Write down the translation dictionary and use it.

### Executives specifically:

1. **Don't hire one if you don't need one yet.** Especially pre-PMF, a highly motivated manager or director running a function beats an expensive executive sitting in a chair doing very little. You'll burn money, burn the executive's morale, and burn your own bandwidth managing up to her when she should be pulling her weight.
2. **Hire for the next nine months, not the next three years.** The single most common exec-hire mistake: hiring someone from a 400-person org because you'll need 400 people "someday," when you need them to get you from 4 to 30. Best case they're uninterested in the current scale; worst case they don't know how to operate at it. Hire for now — they can always scale.
3. **Promote from within when you can.** You develop great people, you keep them longer, they know your culture and ethics, and you minimize the "devil you don't know" risk. The biggest way to hold onto great people long-term is to give them a shot at executive roles.
4. **Look for hungry and driven — someone who wants a shot at "their thing."** Up-and-comers from a mid-sized company who want to be a primary executive at a startup are often your catch of the year.
5. **Beware people who have "done it before."** The VP Eng at four companies who loves it and wants a fifth might be your person. More often, you're dealing with someone who is no longer hungry. That's very hard to recover from.
6. **Beware the incredibly-successful-big-company executive.** The IBM/Microsoft/Google fairy-dust hire is tempting for obvious reasons, and most of the time it's a disaster. They often cannot function in a normal, real-world, competitive situation where they don't start the day with 80% market share.
7. **Don't hire weak on purpose into the function that used to be yours.** The "Michael Eisner Memorial Weak Executive Problem": CEOs who were once in sales/marketing/product hire weak executives in those functions so they can keep being "the man." Make sure the person you hire into that role is way better than you used to be.
8. **Use executive recruiters for sourcing, not evaluation.** You're the one who has to fire the executive if it doesn't work out. Don't outsource that judgment.
9. **Watch the compensation conversation for red flags.** The candidate you want is focused on the option package. The candidate you don't want is focused on cash, bonuses, restricted stock, vacation days, perks — or worst, guaranteed severance. Someone negotiating hard on those up front isn't ready to do a startup.
10. **Manage your executives.** Weekly 1:1s, performance reviews, written objectives, career development plans — the whole nine yards. "I just hired this really experienced VP of X, I should let him do his thing" is one of the most common and damaging instincts in first-time founders.
11. **Give them latitude, but ruthlessly violate the chain of command to gather data.** Ask questions at every level of the organization, continually. You never want the bulk of your information about a function coming from the executive running it. A great executive doesn't mind; she loves it, because she knows you'll hear great things about her. An executive who doesn't want you talking to her people is a bad executive.
12. **If you're reluctant to give an executive full latitude, fire her.** That gut reluctance is a surefire signal she isn't working out. Follow it.

### Firing:

1. **Expect a 30% miss rate on ICs, 50% on executives.** Budget for it. It isn't a character flaw; it's the job.
2. **When you have a bad feeling, start gathering data.** Unless you're paranoid, that feeling is probably right. Talk to everyone. Coach the executive in the meantime. If it works out, great. If not, you're ready.
3. **Fire crisply.** No long transition periods — confusing, demoralizing, weird. No "we'll hire her a boss" demotions — great people don't take demotion well; it poisons the relationship. Make a clean break, put a new person in charge (even acting), move on.
4. **Fire a few months early by Andy Grove's measure, not a year late by yours.** You'll always fire late; the question is how late. If you're a disciplined CEO, aim for three months late. If you're a typical first-time founder, assume your instinct is at least six months later than it should be.
5. **Don't feel guilty.** You're not beheading anyone. Anyone capable of getting hired as a startup executive will be employed again soon. More often than not, you're doing them a favor by releasing them from a role where they won't succeed. If they can't find the next role, they had a deeper problem and you just dodged a bullet.
6. **Value the hell out of the great people who are working.** Given the base rates above, they're incredibly special.

## Examples

**Situation:** A first-time founder is two months into a $400k/year VP Sales hire who came from a hot SaaS company. Pipeline isn't moving. The VP is hiring slowly. The team under her is confused.
**Application:** Gather data. Violate the chain of command — talk to every salesperson, every SDR, the marketing lead who interacts with the function. The signals to check: is she hiring? Are the hires any good? Is she training and developing the team? What do the other executives think? Is it painful for you to interact with her? If four out of five answers are bad, the gut feeling is right. Coach for one more cycle, then fire crisply. Don't demote. Don't bring in a "chief of staff" to prop her up.
**Result:** You take the pain now — one conversation, one disruption, a short acting-VP window. You don't take the pain a year later when the sales org is irreparably underperforming and your Series B is off track because of it.

**Situation:** A founder has a candidate from a hugely successful big tech company — legitimate title, real tenure, warm references from inside. Asking $450k cash, no flexibility on that, wants accelerated vesting on change of control.
**Application:** Two red flags. First, the "done it before at famous company" profile is high-risk in a startup — they often cannot function without 80% market share at their back. Second, the compensation discussion: the candidate you want is focused on the option package. A candidate whose hard line is cash and change-of-control severance is not ready to do a startup. Deep reference the specific work they did (not just "did they work there"), specifically with peers and direct reports, not just their bosses. If the specifics don't check out — pass. If the specifics are stellar, counter on a package heavily weighted to options, and see what happens.
**Result:** Either the candidate leans in and becomes a real hire, or they don't, and you avoid a $450k mistake with a painful unwind.

**Situation:** An engineer on the founding team has become increasingly checked out over six months. Good work history, was core to getting to PMF. But now: slipping deadlines, avoiding 1:1s, combative in meetings.
**Application:** This is the hardest category of firing because of the founding-team history. Gather the data — talk to the team, check recent output, check whether the work has structurally gotten harder (scale, architecture) or the person has changed. If it's the person: have the direct conversation. If it doesn't move, fire crisply. The team knows. Waiting too long costs you their trust, not his. You're not that important in his life; he'll find a role, often a better fit, somewhere else.
**Result:** Short-term pain, morale restored over a quarter. The team sees you're willing to uphold the bar even with people you're close to. That signal compounds in every future hire and every future retention conversation.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire on pedigree as a shortcut for drive. **Why:** Pedigree filters are a crude proxy and they miss the thing that actually matters. A 4.0 GPA signals drive at predefined tests with clear criteria and a grader — often with parents paying for the privilege. That may or may not transfer to the real world. Look at what someone has *done* outside the rails, not how cleanly they rode the rails.

**Don't:** Hire "the smartest person in the room" if they show any whiff of bullshitting or ethical shortcuts. **Why:** Unethical people are unethical by nature. You will not reform them. Every ethical compromise in an interview is a rehearsal for a bigger compromise on the job. The cost of avoid-avoid-avoid is low; the cost of tolerating a charming bullshitter on your exec team is extremely high.

**Don't:** Hire an executive because you're tired, and rationalize that this expensive senior person will "fix it." **Why:** It's often the opposite. Expensive senior hires arrive into the role with their own assumptions about how things should work and their own organizational expectations. Your exhaustion gets replaced with a different exhaustion — managing up to a senior person who doesn't yet know what your company needs. If you can't articulate the next nine months of what this executive needs to accomplish, don't hire them yet.

**Don't:** Let a new exec fire half the team and rebuild in their image before they've produced anything. **Why:** New executives who come in and immediately want to blow up and restaff are often importing their old playbook rather than understanding your actual problem. Give them a few months to produce with the team you have. Real executives will. The ones who insist on a clean sweep before they've generated output usually can't generate output.

**Don't:** Do reference calls only with the candidate's provided list. **Why:** The list is the friendly list. Back-channel for real signal — the person's peers, people who reported to them, anyone who worked closely with them on the specific outcomes they're claiming credit for. The softball language in a structured reference call is almost always hiding something; the informal back-channel conversation is where the real information shows up.

**Don't:** Delay firing because you "want to give it one more quarter." **Why:** You're going to fire them too late regardless — Grove's law. Every quarter you delay, the damage to the function compounds. The team starts leaving, the hires they bring in are misaligned, the culture drifts. Budget for an intentional three-months-too-late, not an unintentional twelve-months-too-late.

**Don't:** Use demotion or "hiring a boss" as alternatives to firing. **Why:** Great people don't take demotion well. It poisons the working relationship, and it tells the whole org that the bar is flexible. Rare exceptions exist; assume you're not one of them.

**Don't:** Hire weak into the function you used to run. **Why:** The "Michael Eisner Memorial Weak Executive Problem" is real and usually subconscious. Test for it by asking: is the person I'm hiring into this role clearly better at this function than I ever was? If your honest answer is no, keep looking.

**Don't:** Outsource exec evaluation to recruiters. **Why:** Recruiters' incentive is to close a placement. Their evaluation judgment isn't always good, and even when it is, betting the company on it is a bad risk. You're the one who has to fire the person. Own the decision.

**Don't:** Treat hiring as solo reps. **Why:** Written process, prepared questions, consistent interviewers — the structure is what moves you from random to reliable. If you don't have a written process, your hiring is random even if you feel smart about each individual call.
