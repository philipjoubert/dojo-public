# The Truth About VCs — How the Asset Class Actually Works

## When to Use
- Founder is trying to model the incentives of the person across the table in a pitch.
- Someone is trying to understand why VCs behave as a herd, or why "brand" VCs actually matter.
- Someone is debating whether VC is a rational asset class at all, given how many funds produce terrible returns.
- Founder wants to know why a specific VC passed, beyond the surface reason given.

## Fails When
- The user is looking for a single-villain story about their own bad experience. This framework is about the structure — why the system produces the behaviors it does — not about any specific partner who behaved badly.
- The business isn't venture-scale. Then VCs' decisions aren't irrational, they're just responding correctly to a bad fit.

## Core Concept

Start with what a VC actually *is*. A venture capitalist raises a large amount of money — often $100 million or more — today, in order to invest in a series of high-risk startups over the next three to four years. The legal lifespan of the fund is ten years — that's the outer limit on their horizon. They generally intend, and their investors expect, returns to flow back within four to six years. Within that structure, they operate on the baseball model: **out of ten swings at the bat, you get maybe seven strikeouts, two base hits, and if you're lucky, one home run. The base hits and home runs pay for all the strikeouts.**

They don't get seven strikeouts because they're stupid. They get seven strikeouts because most startups fail, most startups have always failed, and most startups will always fail. That is the ground truth of the asset class. Everything downstream of that ground truth is a consequence.

**The math, not the personality, dictates their investment criterion.** To make the winners pay for the losers in the time their own investors expect, they need a credible *potential* of a 10x gain within four to six years on any individual investment. Startups with that leverage — a product you make once and sell to 10 million people, or to 1,000 enterprise customers at high price — should seriously consider venture capital. Startups without that leverage — good, possibly profitable, possibly very satisfying businesses — shouldn't. Not because they aren't good, but because VCs literally can't fund them without their own investors firing them.

This is the first truth: **VCs don't pass because they're dumb, and they don't invest because they like you. They're running a portfolio to a power-law outcome constraint.**

The second truth is about who VCs actually work for. Their investors — "limited partners" or LPs — are overwhelmingly institutional: large university endowments, philanthropic foundations, pension funds. The largest investors in many top-tier firms are nonprofits. **The best VCs' primary job is often helping very worthwhile nonprofits build larger endowments to make the world a better place.** Read the Sequoia disclosures from the Google/YouTube deal and you'll see Amherst, Brown, Colby, Columbia, Dartmouth — just into the D's — plus the Ford Foundation, Moore, Irvine, Rockefeller, Hewlett. This isn't unusual. It's structural. It also means VCs have *fiduciary* constraints, not just preference constraints. An LP that thinks a fund drifted off-strategy can decline the next fund.

The third truth is what happened to the industry. Traditionally the venture industry followed a "seven fat years, seven lean years" cycle — boom, bust, capital flight, a smaller base, explosive returns in the next cycle. That cycle broke in the early '90s when David Swensen at Yale and his peers crunched the numbers and realized that long-horizon endowments could generate higher returns by allocating to alternative asset classes — hedge funds, real estate, commodities, LBO firms, and venture. The institutions that adopted the Swensen playbook *kicked the ass* of the institutions that didn't. So the late '90s saw massive institutional capital pour into venture. And here's the surprising part: even after the 2000–2002 crash, **that capital didn't leave.** Venture capital became a permanent "asset class" of institutional investors. I call this the **"asset-classization" of venture capital**. 3.5% of a ginormous endowment is still a *lot* of money. The result: 200+ venture firms within 20 miles of Sand Hill Road, $20B+ at their disposal, chasing a small number of real opportunities, despite terrible *average* returns for the asset class over long stretches. That's a structural feature, not a bug.

The fourth truth is what this does to the inside of the industry. **Most of the positive returns in venture go to the top 10% of firms — maybe the top 10 firms.** Most of the dollars going into the asset class are therefore going into the other 90% of firms that, as a cohort, deliver poor returns. This is the brand VC effect. It's not snobbery. It's power law. The top firms see more deals (referral density), win more contested deals (brand), deploy more capital behind winners (reserves), and therefore compound those returns into the next fund. The bottom 90% are selecting from a picked-over opportunity set. "Signaling" exists because of this: taking money from a top-tier firm and then having that firm *not* follow on in your next round is a negative signal precisely because their information is known to be good.

The fifth truth is what a VC's week actually looks like. **A VC's ideal investment is the one that would be a huge success without her.** So where does her time go? Counterintuitively, her winners are succeeding — they don't need much help. The best VCs spend most of their time either sourcing the next home run or **trying to jumpstart or otherwise fix fatally flawed startups**. Life in the VC trenches consists disproportionately of working with failing companies. It's not fun. It also means: when you ask a VC for help, you're competing for time against her worst-performing portfolio companies, which are screaming the loudest. **Never expect the help to just happen unsolicited. If you want it, ask for it proactively.** Assume her primary value-add is the cash she invested; then you'll always be surprised on the upside.

The sixth truth is why this whole structure is hard to disrupt. Tyler Cowen calls the underlying activity "project evaluation" — sorting through many possible configurations of people and ideas and picking a few to back with money and effort. This pattern traces back, literally, to the whaling industry: independent financiers funding captains and ships. "Carried interest" originally meant the share of the whale carried by the ship and kept by the captain and crew. The same pattern has played out for centuries, across many kinds of large-scale, risky projects — colonial settlements like the Plymouth colony, music/film/television, the world's largest private equity transactions. Maybe crypto and DAOs eventually change the shape of it; I'm genuinely open to that. But there's at least so far **no substitute** for someone doing the work of sorting and filtering all the potential projects and making big, risky bets. Call it Robert Michels' Iron Law of Oligarchy for risk capital if you want. The shell keeps getting rebuilt.

And the seventh truth, which gets lost in the cynicism: imagine living in a world in which professional venture capital didn't exist. Fewer new high-potential companies would be funded, fewer new technologies would be brought to market, fewer medical cures would be invented. Professional venture has only existed in its modern form for about 40 years. In that time the world has seen its most amazing flowering of technological and medical progress, ever. That is not a coincidence. You can yell at your VC in a board meeting. You should still be glad she exists.

## How to Apply

1. **Model the person across the table as a portfolio manager, not an individual.** When you're pitching, don't ask yourself "does she like me?" Ask "can she tell her partners this is one of the ten shots out of ten that could return the fund?" Everything a VC says and doesn't say is constrained by that question.
2. **Understand why "no" is the default.** Not because the idea is bad — because the asset-class constraint requires that the idea *could credibly* produce a 10x exit in four to six years. If she can't see that path, she can't invest, even if she finds you compelling. When she says "too early" or "unproven", she's often saying "I can't see the path to 10x yet."
3. **Read the firm-vs-partner distinction correctly.** Pick the partner, not the firm. Firm brand matters — for referrals, for follow-on capital, for signaling — but the partner is the person in your board meetings. Even great firms have weak partners. Reference the partner specifically by talking to other founders she's backed.
4. **Separate the "real value add" from the pitched value add.** The real value add is cash plus, in order, recruiting help, occasional strategy input, partner/customer introductions, and help raising the next round. "The firm has an operating platform" and "we have a network" and "we'll open doors" is almost always sold heavier than it delivers. Assume cash; anything else is upside.
5. **Understand why help shows up for struggling companies more than for winners.** If you're winning, your VC will not spend much time with you. That's not neglect. It's their correct allocation. If you want help, you have to ask for it specifically and repeatedly.
6. **Internalize the power law at every level.** Top 10% of firms capture most of the return. Top 10% of deals inside a given fund capture most of *that* return. Most companies in a VC's portfolio will, from her perspective, be irrelevant to her fund's outcome — either dead or modestly successful. This isn't cruelty; it's statistics. Build your relationship knowing you need to be in the small number she's actually paying attention to.
7. **Understand signaling.** Taking a round from a top firm whose follow-on signal is read carefully by the market is a two-edged sword. If they keep doubling down, great. If they don't, later investors will notice and read the non-participation as information. Factor this into whose money you take.
8. **Know that the cash spigot is basically permanent.** The asset-classization means that even after terrible vintage years, the money keeps coming. 200 firms within 20 miles. $20B+ at their disposal. That's why the environment feels competitive on the VC side — more dollars chasing fewer good deals than you'd think the returns would justify. Use that structurally: more firms, more competition for your round if you're credible, more ability to select the right partner rather than grab any check.
9. **Don't confuse asset-class structure with individual character.** Some VCs are extraordinarily helpful human beings. Some are awful. Neither tells you much about the firm's returns or the industry's mechanics. Evaluate the person as a person; evaluate the firm as a portfolio engine; evaluate the industry as a 40-year-old structural adaptation to the power law of early-stage technology outcomes.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder is furious that a top-tier partner spent three meetings with her, seemed enthusiastic, then passed with a bland email.
**Application:** Structurally likely explanation: the partner loved it but couldn't get partnership conviction. That's why she won't give a real reason — she wants the door open if facts change. Thank her for her honesty, ask if you can update her on material new facts in three to six months, and take the next meeting. Don't model this as betrayal; model it as her optimizing within her partnership constraints.
**Result:** You either come back in three months with new facts and convert to yes, or you don't and the relationship stays useful because you didn't torch it. Either way, you reallocated your emotional energy to the next pitch instead of rehashing the last one.

**Situation:** Founder is deciding between a mid-tier firm offering $12M at a $60M pre and a top-tier firm offering $8M at a $40M pre.
**Application:** The headline price difference disappears inside five years if either option leads to a real company. The meaningful question is reserves and signaling. The top-tier firm's follow-on capability in a Series B is usually the better asset than $4M of additional first-round capital, and the brand attracts hiring and customer conversations you otherwise pay for in ways you don't notice. The power-law structure of the industry pushes toward the top-tier, absent strong reasons otherwise. Go top-tier unless the partner fit is clearly worse.
**Result:** You optimize for the follow-on and signaling math rather than the headline dilution, which is almost always the right call when you're planning to build a big company.

**Situation:** A founder is asking why the industry isn't being disrupted by crypto-native or DAO-based funding models, given that "VC is broken."
**Application:** The activity itself — project evaluation, selection, backing, guiding — traces back to whaling financiers centuries ago. It reappears in new shells — music labels, film studios, private equity — but the shell keeps reforming because somebody has to do the work. Crypto may reshape the shell over decades. It won't eliminate the underlying function. Don't bet on VC disappearing; bet on watching whose project-evaluation decisions compound over multiple cycles.
**Result:** You stop waiting for the industry to be disrupted away and start choosing which specific humans inside the existing structure are most likely to make good bets over the next decade.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume a VC's "no" is personal. **Why:** Ninety percent of the time it's the 10x constraint — she can't see the path. Reading it as personal leads to sour relationships you could have kept useful. Thank her and move on.

**Don't:** Model VCs as a monolith. **Why:** There's a massive spread in partner quality within any firm, including the best firms. "Firm X is great" is a bad proxy. Pick the partner. Reference the partner.

**Don't:** Expect your VC to be your therapist, co-founder, or operator. **Why:** She sits on a dozen boards, manages her LPs, sources new investments, spends disproportionate time on her failing companies. Asking for occasional specific help with specific things — a recruit, an intro, a strategy conversation — gets results. Expecting constant hands-on engagement will disappoint you and annoy her.

**Don't:** Take a lower-tier firm's check purely on headline valuation. **Why:** Power law means top-tier capital compounds into better referrals, better subsequent rounds, better signaling. You're trading a small amount of dilution for a large amount of downstream optionality. Only reverse that trade if there's a specific, concrete reason — a terrible partner at the top-tier firm, a unique domain expert at the lower-tier.

**Don't:** Assume the brand VC effect is unearned snobbery. **Why:** It's a feedback loop, not a cabal. Top firms see more deals, win more contested deals, win more of the winners, and therefore produce better returns, which reinforces their referral density. Treating it as snobbery leads you to under-invest in earning access to those firms, which is a real, measurable value loss to the company.

**Don't:** Assume cultural contempt for VCs scales to your individual partner. **Why:** The industry earns plenty of criticism — it's an opaque power structure optimizing for big wins. But individual VCs are extremely varied, and your board dynamics will depend overwhelmingly on the specific human. Form your view of your partner from her track record with founders, not from tech Twitter consensus.

**Don't:** Expect your "yes" to be permanent before the wire clears. **Why:** Partnership meetings, LP dynamics, macro shocks — any of them can still kill a deal after a handshake. The constraint under which the partner operates can change the week of closing. Keep other conversations live until the funds are irrevocable.

**Don't:** Ignore the LP layer. **Why:** The fund's LPs are why your partner can't make certain trades — why she can't put $50M into a company that plausibly can't do 10x, why she can't hold forever, why her time horizon is what it is. Understanding the LP layer is understanding the constraints above your partner's head. It's why some funds have recently been pushed into more evergreen or registered-investment-advisor structures: they're trying to renegotiate the LP-level constraints so they can hold longer and do more. That's real; it's also slow.
