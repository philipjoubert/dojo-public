# How to Do Great Work

## When to Use
- Founder is at a career crossroads — deciding what to spend the next decade on.
- Founder is tempted by prestige (a senior role, a famous program, a well-paid job) and sensing they should follow a different pull.
- Founder is choosing between two projects, one more impressive-sounding and one more genuinely interesting.
- Founder is stuck and wondering if they're on the wrong problem entirely.
- Founder is procrastinating on important work and feels guilty about it.

## Fails When
- The question is tactical — how to get users this week, how to close a deal, how to hire — route to the specific operational topic.
- The founder is asking in a survival context (runway, layoffs, crisis) — route to how-not-to-die.

## Core Concept

Great work requires three things in the work itself: **natural aptitude, deep interest, and scope**. You need something you have a natural ability for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers room to do work that shifts the boundaries of what people think is possible.

In practice you don't have to worry much about scope. Ambitious people are if anything too conservative about it. So what you actually need is to find something you have aptitude for and great interest in. That sounds straightforward. It's often quite difficult, because when you're young you don't know what you're good at or what different kinds of work are like. Some of the work you'll eventually do may not even exist yet. The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not sure, guess. Pick something and get going. You'll probably guess wrong some of the time, but that's fine. It won't be wasted — you'll learn things in each field that turn out to be useful in others.

The most important correction to the usual career advice: **curiosity is a better guide than ambition**. Ambitious people work on what they think will impress others. Curious people work on what's genuinely interesting. The difference matters because ambition is externally directed — it points you toward what the world currently rewards — while curiosity is internally directed — it points you toward what actually matters to you specifically. The best ideas are surprising; you're much more likely to find them in a domain you're genuinely curious about, because you'll have spent more time exploring it — not strategically, but because you couldn't help yourself.

This is also why prestige is dangerous. **Prestige is fossilized inspiration.** It's a lagging indicator of what was once exciting and has since hardened into mere status. If you admire two kinds of work equally but one is more prestigious, pick the less prestigious one — your estimate was probably distorted upward by the prestige anyway. *If it didn't suck, they wouldn't have had to make it prestigious.*

Returns in life are **superlinear**. Performance compounds rather than accumulating linearly. If your product is only half as good as your competitor's, you don't get half as many customers — you get no customers, and go out of business. This is a feature of the world, not an artifact of rules we invented. The same pattern in fame, power, knowledge, benefit to humanity — the rich get richer. Two sources reduce to: exponential growth (success breeds success) and thresholds (winner-take-all). The right strategy in a superlinear world is always to seek the most ambitious thing you can do, because the returns to getting it right are disproportionately large.

## How to Apply

1. **Aptitude + deep interest + scope.** Three criteria. The first two are what you work on. The third is usually not worth worrying about if you're ambitious — you overcorrect on the side of small.

2. **Curiosity over ambition.** When you have to choose between two projects, and one is more impressive but less interesting, choose the more interesting one. You'll work harder on it. You'll stick with it when things get difficult. And you'll end up in more interesting territory because you'll be following the actual gradient of ideas rather than the social gradient of prestige.

3. **The bus ticket test.** Genius requires three things: natural ability, determination, and obsessive interest in a specific topic. The critical ingredient is *disinterested obsession* — the kind a bus ticket collector has. Not motivated by external rewards or prestige, but by genuine fascination. Would you still work on this if you got nothing for it? If the answer is "no, I'd drop it," you're doing it for the wrong reason. If the answer is "yes, even without the money, even without the praise," that's the signal you're where you should be.

4. **What doesn't seem like work to you?** If you find yourself naturally drawn to something others find tedious — reading academic papers on some obscure topic, debugging a weird piece of code, analyzing historical data — that's evidence of where you should work. The stranger your taste, the stronger the signal. You don't have to identify genius-level obsession; just notice the asymmetry between your experience and everyone else's. That's where you have the natural advantage.

5. **Stay upwind.** Upwind choices give you more options; downwind choices give you fewer. Working on hard things is upwind. Working on easy things that pay well is downwind. Math is upwind of economics. Programming is upwind of most things. If you can't decide between two options, choose the harder one — not because suffering is virtuous, but because it maximizes optionality.

6. **Always produce.** If you subject yourself to that constraint, it will automatically push you away from things you think you're supposed to work on and toward things you actually like. *Always produce* will discover your life's work the way water, with the aid of gravity, finds the hole in your roof. Production is honest in a way that intentions aren't. If you're producing, you'll converge on what you should be doing. If you're not, you're dreaming.

7. **The three types of procrastination.** (a) Doing nothing. (b) Doing less important things. (c) Doing more important things. Only (c) is good procrastination. Hamming's three questions: What are the most important problems in your field? Are you working on one of them? Why not? These feel accusatory but they're navigational — they pull you toward the most important work you could be doing. Busywork that feels urgent but doesn't matter is fake work; noticing it is the first step to escaping it. The alarm that goes off when you spend money doesn't go off when you waste time. You have to build that alarm yourself.

8. **Start small; frame as experiment.** One of the biggest obstacles to great work is the difficulty of starting. People get paralyzed by the gap between where they are and where they want to be. Treat new ideas as experiments. Frame ambitious projects as "just a sketch" or "just a quick hack." This removes the fear of failure — experiments are supposed to fail most of the time. Once rolling, you can sneakily convert it to something more ambitious.

9. **Optimize in problem-space, not only in solution-space.** When something is hard, the default response is to work harder. But often the better approach is to work on a different problem. Rabin: "You need a big prime number? Those are expensive. How about a big number with a 10⁻¹⁰⁰ chance of not being prime?" That's redefining the problem. The people who do great work are often the ones who found a way to make the problem easier, not the ones who brute-forced the hard version.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 25-year-old is choosing between two offers. One: a senior role at a prestigious consulting firm, six-figure salary, brand-name clients, fast-track career. Two: a technical role at an obscure startup working on something the person is privately fascinated by, no name recognition, one-third the salary, uncertain future. They're leaning toward the consulting firm because it's "the responsible choice."

**Application:** Take the startup. The consulting firm is prestige and money — two of the three sirens that lead people astray. The startup is aptitude (if you have it) plus deep interest (which you admit you have privately). The "responsible" framing is the fossilized inspiration of a career path that was exciting a generation ago and is now mere status. Apply the bus ticket test: which of those two jobs would you still do if you got nothing for it? If it's the startup, that's the signal. The compounding over the next decade of working on something you're genuinely curious about will dwarf the compounding of a prestigious résumé, because superlinear returns favor the person who goes deep on what actually interests them. And you can always go back and join a prestigious firm at 30; you usually can't go back and be curious again at 30 if you've spent five years learning not to be.

**Result:** Takes the startup. Works harder than they ever would have at the consulting firm, because it doesn't feel like work. Five years later, is at the frontier of the startup's domain with unique expertise. The consulting firm would have been a safer local maximum; the startup was the path to a much higher global one.

---

**Situation:** Founder keeps procrastinating on the hard thing on their list — a redesign of the core product. Instead they answer email, take meetings, ship small features. They feel guilty and self-critical.

**Application:** Identify which type of procrastination. If you're doing Type A (nothing) or Type B (less important things), there's a problem. Email and small features are probably Type B — they feel productive, they're visible, they don't require facing the hard problem. The hard problem is the real problem, and fake work is cheap. The fix is not to "try harder" on the hard thing. The fix is to notice what you're doing. When you catch yourself reaching for the small feature instead of the core redesign, stop and ask: what are the most important problems in my company right now? Am I working on one of them? Why not? Those three questions, honestly answered, are navigational. Also: try the experiment frame. "Let me just sketch what the redesign could look like" is a different psychological task than "let me solve the redesign." The sketch often becomes the thing. Or: try optimizing in problem-space. Maybe the hard redesign is hard because you're solving the wrong version of the problem. Is there a different problem that would be just as useful and that you could actually solve? Sometimes the escape is not through the wall but around it.

**Result:** Founder reframes the redesign as "a sketch of what v2 could feel like," spends a week on it, discovers the real redesign is smaller than feared, ships it the following month.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Follow prestige.
**Why:** Prestige is fossilized inspiration. By the time something is prestigious, it has stopped being exciting. Chasing prestige pulls you away from the gradient of actually-interesting and toward the gradient of already-respected. You'll end up doing work others approve of that doesn't get you anywhere you want to be.

**Don't:** Compensate for boring work with high pay.
**Why:** Money is not the problem by itself — telemarketing pays well and nobody's tempted. The dangerous combinations are money + prestige (corporate law, banking, medicine). A comparatively safe and prosperous career with automatic baseline prestige is precisely what traps ambitious young people. The test: would you do this work for free if you had to support yourself another way? If not, you have a hobby that pays, not a calling.

**Don't:** Let rate of progress feel too slow to continue.
**Why:** Early rate of change matters more than absolute position. Bad early work is fine if it's improving fast. In fact bad early work is *necessary* — you can't learn to do great work without doing a lot of mediocre work first. Hardy (on professor-level optimism): "It is one of the first duties of a professor to exaggerate a little both the importance of his subject and his importance in it." Without a little compensating optimism, the natural harshness of early results kills too many promising projects in the cradle.

**Don't:** Demand the work pay off on a short horizon.
**Why:** Superlinear returns compound. Early returns look pathetically small, which is exactly why most people underinvest. Late returns are extraordinary. Learning compounds. Audience compounds. Network effects compound. If you're the kind of person who will give up after eighteen months because returns look small, you are structurally mismatched with the work that has the best long-term payoff.

**Don't:** Confuse obstinacy with persistence.
**Why:** Persistent people vary the approach and hold the goal. Obstinate people hold both and push harder. The difference is imagination. If you're grinding the same problem the same way for months with no progress, you're obstinate, not persistent. Look for a different problem, a different angle, or a different framing — that's what great workers actually do.

**Don't:** Ignore the asymmetry signal.
**Why:** What doesn't feel like work to you — while others find it tedious — is where you have a natural advantage. Ignoring that asymmetry because you "should" work on something else is throwing away the rarest information about what you personally should do. Follow it, even when the thing it points at seems small or unserious.
