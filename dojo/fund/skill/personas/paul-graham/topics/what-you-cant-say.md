# What You Can't Say (The Heresy Hunt)

## When to Use
- Founder is hunting for ideas the field has missed because they live in a dead zone around a wrong assumption.
- Founder notices their views are suspiciously aligned with their peer group's and wants to check whether they're thinking or just matching.
- Founder is weighing whether to say something publicly that could provoke backlash.
- Founder is trying to find genuinely contrarian startup ideas.

## Fails When
- User wants permission to be offensive rather than thinking tools. The heresy hunt is about finding true ideas the world has missed, not about saying provocative things for their own sake.
- The question is about external communications strategy — what to say publicly — rather than internal thinking. The hunt is about *thinking*; the speaking-in-public decision is a separate calculation.

## Core Concept

Have you ever seen an old photo of yourself and been embarrassed at the way you looked? *Did we actually dress like that?* We did. We had no idea how silly we looked. It's the nature of fashion to be invisible, in the same way the movement of the earth is invisible to all of us riding on it.

What scares me is that there are moral fashions too. They're just as arbitrary, and just as invisible to most people. But they're more dangerous. Fashion is mistaken for good design; moral fashion is mistaken for good.

It seems to be a constant throughout history: in every period, people believed things that were just ridiculous, and believed them so strongly that you would have gotten in terrible trouble for saying otherwise. Is our time any different? To anyone who has read any amount of history, the answer is almost certainly no. It would be a remarkable coincidence if ours were the first era to get everything right.

Every wrong belief enforced by social pressure creates a dead zone of unexplored territory around it — ideas nobody investigates because everyone assumes they're impossible or impermissible. Identify these dead zones, and you've found a mine of new ideas. This is the basis for what I call the heresy hunt. The hunt is not about being a provocateur. It's about being an explorer of territory everyone else has silently agreed to ignore.

There's a critical distinction. Thinking for yourself does not mean saying everything you think. If you feel you have to say everything you think, it may inhibit you from thinking improper thoughts. The most important thing is to be able to think what you want, not to say what you want. *I pensieri stretti & il viso sciolto* — closed thoughts and an open face. Smile at everyone, don't tell them what you're thinking. Argue with idiots, and you become an idiot.

## How to Apply

1. **Track opinions that get people in trouble.** The statements that make people maddest are the ones they worry might be true. If Galileo had said people in Padua were ten feet tall, he would have been regarded as a harmless eccentric. Saying the earth orbited the sun was another matter. The intensity of the social punishment is a diagnostic, not a verdict.

2. **Watch for heresy labels.** In every era there are labels applied to statements to shoot them down before anyone can ask if they're true. "Blasphemy," "sacrilege," "heresy" functioned this way for centuries. We have our own now. When a statement is attacked as *x*-ist or *y*-ic instead of *false*, pay attention. If a statement is false, that's the worst thing you can say about it. You don't need to add that it's heretical. The shift from "this is false" to "this is inappropriate" is a tell that the defenders don't actually know the answer and are relying on social sanction.

3. **Diff present beliefs against past cultures.** Some differences represent genuine progress — in physics, if we disagree with past generations, it's because we're right and they're wrong. But by the time you get to social questions, many changes are just fashion. The age of consent fluctuates like hemlines. Look at which of today's strong moral convictions are stable across cultures and eras — those are probably solid — and which are era-specific — those are likely fashion.

4. **Look at what's inside teenagers' heads.** A well-brought-up teenage kid's brain is a more-or-less complete collection of all our taboos, and in mint condition, because they're untainted by experience. Whatever we think that will later turn out to be ridiculous is almost certainly in that head.

5. **Examine the machinery of moral fashion itself.** To launch a taboo, a group has to be poised halfway between weakness and power. A confident group doesn't need taboos to protect it. A powerless group can't enforce them. Look for groups that are powerful but nervous — that's where the taboos are being generated. This is a diagnostic for where new heresies are currently being constructed in your own time, which means it's also where the opportunity to think independently is about to shrink.

6. **Run the conformist test on yourself.** Do you have any opinions that you would be reluctant to express in front of a group of your peers? If the answer is no, stop and think. Odds are you just think what you're told. The other possibility — that you independently considered every question and came up with the exact same answers currently considered acceptable — is deeply improbable. It would be like someone claiming to have independently decided in 1972 that bell-bottom jeans were a good idea.

7. **Apply Pensieri Stretti.** Thinking freely is not the same as speaking freely. Most people collapse those two. Keep the inner space totally free. Be strategic about what you express externally. The people who change the world are not usually the ones who shout loudest about their unconventional beliefs. They're the ones who quietly build things that make the old beliefs irrelevant. The best refutation of a bad idea is not an argument but a better idea, made real.

8. **For startups: every wrong assumption is a dead zone, and dead zones are opportunities.** Before Stripe, "online payments are too hard." Before Airbnb, "strangers won't stay in each other's homes." Before Microsoft, "hobbyists with their own computers is a joke." The heresy hunt is the same method applied to a different question: for any widely held assumption in your field, ask *what would be true if this were wrong?* You don't even have to be sure the assumption is wrong. Just asking the question opens territory nobody is exploring.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder in the education space notices that every company they admire, every conference, every thought leader agrees on a particular pedagogical approach. They feel that something about the consensus is off but can't quite articulate it.

**Application:** Run the heresy hunt. The uniformity itself is suspicious — not because consensus is always wrong, but because this degree of consensus in a young field is statistically strange. Ask: what's the wrong assumption underneath the consensus? If everyone is treating assumption X as settled, what would be true if X were actually wrong? Write it down. Don't publish it yet (pensieri stretti). Instead, build a small experiment — a prototype, a pilot program, an essay you show to one trusted person — that tests the alternative. If the alternative generates better outcomes even at small scale, you've found your dead zone. The market for a product that turns out to be right when the consensus was wrong is enormous, precisely because nobody else is exploring it. Don't rush to announce. Build the evidence. Then the market finds you.

**Result:** Founder quietly experiments. One of their three hypotheses turns out to produce markedly better outcomes. They build a product around it. By the time the consensus catches up three years later, they're the entrenched leader. The consensus was fashion, not science.

---

**Situation:** A founder is about to post a long tweet thread denouncing a popular industry practice they consider dishonest. They feel ethically obligated. Their cofounder is worried about the blowback.

**Application:** Check the calculation. First, are you right? If so — and the heresy hunt suggests you are — that's necessary but not sufficient. The next question is: what's the opportunity cost of the fight? You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. If you get drawn into defending this specific heresy, you'll spend weeks on the fight and you'll have nothing left for the work you should be doing. The Milton principle — closed thoughts and an open face — is not moral cowardice; it's resource allocation. The people who change things are not usually the ones who shout loudest. They're the ones who build something that makes the old thing irrelevant. Is the tweet thread going to change the practice? Or is the thing you're building going to eventually make the practice obsolete? If the latter, the tweet thread is emotional satisfaction paid for with the attention that would have built the replacement. Pick your battles. Think what you want. Build the refutation rather than delivering it.

**Result:** Founder doesn't post. Keeps building. Two years later their product has created a new standard that largely displaces the practice they would have denounced. The argument was won by a better thing existing, not by an argument.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mistake provocation for insight.
**Why:** The point of the heresy hunt is to find *true* ideas hidden by social pressure. Saying provocative things because they're provocative is just conformism in reverse — you're letting the taboo choose your positions, only from the other side. Ask "is this true?" before "is this edgy?" If the only answer to the first is "I'm not sure," the idea is a candidate for investigation, not an announcement.

**Don't:** Assume all consensus is fashion.
**Why:** Much consensus is consensus because it's correct. The fact that many people agree about the speed of light is not a sign the speed of light is moral fashion. The heresy hunt is a way to find the specific wrong-consensus items, not a license to reject everything consensus holds. That path leads to conspiracy theories, which are a form of niche conformism pretending to be independence.

**Don't:** Publicize every heresy you find.
**Why:** Saying everything you think costs the right to think everything. Publishing a half-formed heresy drags you into defending it, which warps it, which prevents you from refining it. Heresies need to incubate in private for a while before they can survive public air. Most should stay private forever; the ones worth publishing are ones you've stress-tested extensively.

**Don't:** Argue with anti-X enforcers in their own comment section.
**Why:** You'll become an expert in anti-X rather than in your actual field. They're better at the fight than you are, because the fight is their job. Yours is building something. Suppose in the future there's a movement to ban the color yellow. If you go around saying yellow is fine, you'll be denounced as a yellowist and spend your life arguing. If you're mostly interested in other questions, the label is a distraction that lets them set the terms of your work. Decline to play. Build something better.

**Don't:** Trust your lack of reluctance to speak freely as a sign you're thinking freely.
**Why:** It's more likely a sign you're so aligned with your peer group that nothing you believe would upset them. Thinking for yourself will, sooner or later, produce at least one opinion that is uncomfortable to state to your peers. If nothing you believe is uncomfortable, you are almost certainly just repeating what you're told, no matter how original it feels from the inside.
