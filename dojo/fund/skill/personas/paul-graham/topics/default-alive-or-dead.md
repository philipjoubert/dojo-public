# Default Alive or Default Dead

## When to Use
- Founder is about to raise, has just raised, or is thinking about raising.
- Founder describes runway in months rather than as a function of growth and burn.
- Founder says "we'll probably need to raise again in six months" without having done the math.
- Founder is considering cutting headcount under pressure and doesn't know whether cutting is enough.
- Founder is in the fatal pinch — growth too slow to attract investors, cash too short to reach profitability on its own.

## Fails When
- Founder just closed a large round and has obvious runway — the question is then about what to build with the money, not about survival arithmetic.
- The question is really about product quality or positioning and "default dead" is being used as a proxy for "I feel anxious."

## Core Concept

There's a simple calculation every startup should do, and surprisingly few founders have done: at your current revenue growth rate and your current expense growth rate, will you become profitable before you run out of money?

If yes, you're default alive. If no, you're default dead.

I'm surprised how often founders don't know the answer. They're optimistic by nature, so they assume things will work out. But default dead is not a position that works out by itself. You have to actively escape it. The defaultness is the whole point — you're asking what happens if nothing changes. If the answer is "we die," then you'd better change something.

Most founders who'd be classified as default dead would say, if you asked them, "well, revenue's growing, so we'll be fine." They haven't looked at the numbers. Revenue is growing and expenses are growing too, often faster. Worse, expenses don't just grow linearly with hiring — they compound, because each new hire needs a manager and a desk and benefits and onboarding, and meanwhile revenue is fighting a much harder battle. If you haven't drawn the two lines on the same chart, you don't know whether they cross before the money runs out.

The worst position is what I call the fatal pinch. The pinch occurs when you're default dead, too far from profitability to close the gap with your own revenue, and also too slow-growing to raise money on reasonable terms because investors can look at the trajectory and see what you see. Not enough growth to be investable; not enough money to reach profitability. It's self-reinforcing: desperation makes you worse at fundraising and worse at building. The escape is stark — act as if this is the last money you'll ever get. Either become profitable on existing funds, or grow fast enough to be obviously fundable. There is no third option.

## How to Apply

1. **Do the calculation. Literally draw the chart.** Plot your monthly revenue (not signups, not users — actual recognized revenue). Plot your monthly expenses. Extrapolate at your current growth rates. Do the two lines cross before your cash hits zero? Yes: default alive. No: default dead.

2. **Use a realistic revenue growth rate.** Not your best month. Not what you hope. The trailing three-month average. Not your theoretical TAM-capture rate. The actual number people have actually been paying you.

3. **Use a realistic expense growth rate.** Include the hires you're planning. Include the tooling, office, legal, accounting, benefits, taxes, and other things you're about to sign for. Do not model yourself as frozen in place.

4. **If you're default dead, change something now, not when the money is gone.** The worst move is to continue at the current burn, assume you'll raise, and find out at month 7 of 9 that the market is cold. By then you have no leverage. Options at month 7 are terrible. Options at month 12 out are excellent.

5. **If you're default alive but barely, behave as if you're default dead.** Optimism is correctly distributed when you're paranoid about cash and ambitious about users. The reverse (paranoid about users, optimistic about cash) kills.

6. **Fatal pinch: escape both walls or pick one.** You can't stay where you are. Either get aggressive enough on growth that investors overlook the profitability gap (hard), or get aggressive enough on costs that you reach profitability on current cash (also hard, but more within your control). The illusion is that you can quietly raise a bridge. In a cold market, bridges are only available to founders who don't need them.

7. **Don't raise reactively.** The time to raise money is not when you need it. It's when you can convince investors, and not before. Raising from a position of desperation gives you terrible terms and distorted incentives. Better to get ramen profitable and raise from strength than raise from weakness and spend the round extinguishing fires.

8. **Ramen profitable changes the game completely.** Crossing from a dollar of loss to a dollar of profit, even at $3,000 a month, is a qualitative change — the stars turning into lines when the Enterprise accelerates to warp speed. Your runway becomes infinite. You're no longer asking permission to exist. You're negotiating from a position of strength, which is where investors prefer to lend money anyway.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder tells you: "We have nine months of runway. Growing 8% month over month. Team of twelve. We'll raise in six months." They haven't run the chart.

**Application:** Run it. Monthly revenue today: $80k. Growing 8% MoM. Monthly burn: $250k. At that growth, revenue in nine months is $160k/month — still $90k short of burn, and burn will have grown with headcount. You're default dead. Not catastrophically, but clearly. Now your choice: (a) become default alive by getting aggressive on costs — freeze hiring, trim two positions, re-examine every SaaS bill — and you can be profitable in 12-14 months on current cash. Or (b) push growth hard, target 15%+ MoM through relentless user-love, and raise in four months off a compelling chart, not in six months off a concerning one. What you cannot do is continue current burn and hope the round closes on schedule. That's the fatal pinch pattern. Pick a path this week.

**Result:** Founder freezes hiring, trims two positions, redirects the freed energy to user-love and retention. Growth rate ticks up to 12% because the team is more focused. They raise from strength four months later at a valuation they wouldn't have gotten if they'd waited.

---

**Situation:** A founder is in the fatal pinch. Five months of cash. Growth rate 3% MoM. Three rejected investor meetings. Two employees already know something is wrong. They're asking whether to cut aggressively now or try one more round of pitches.

**Application:** Cut. Immediately. Today. The three rejections are data — at this growth rate investors will not back this. The ambient market knows what you know. If you try one more round of pitches, you'll burn six more weeks of cash and morale to confirm the data you already have. Here's the stark version: you cannot raise, which means you must become profitable on current cash. Model what that looks like. It will require cutting to the bone. Do it all at once — small cuts bleed morale; a single honest reset is survivable. Tell the team the truth about the pinch — they'd rather hear it from you than read it in the room. Then: relentlessly resourceful on revenue and ruthless on cost. If the math still doesn't get you to profitability, you have a different question — is this business actually viable? But don't answer that question through the fog of "one more investor meeting." Answer it in daylight.

**Result:** Founder cuts 40% of team honestly and quickly. Burn drops from $250k to $95k. Revenue at $70k is suddenly close to breakeven. Morale, counterintuitively, recovers because the survivors now know what game they're playing. Four months later they're ramen profitable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Measure runway in months while ignoring growth and burn curves.
**Why:** "Nine months of runway" is meaningless without the trajectory. If burn is growing and revenue isn't keeping up, you have less than nine months because expenses are compounding. If revenue is growing faster than burn, you have effectively more than nine. The scalar number lies.

**Don't:** Assume the round will close on schedule.
**Why:** Deals fall through. Markets change. Your numbers may stop being compelling between today and the close. The last 30 days of a six-month fundraising process is where most deaths happen. Always have a plan B that doesn't require the round.

**Don't:** Raise to avoid cutting.
**Why:** If you raise to avoid cuts you needed to make, you've bought six months and imported the problem. The round makes you look funded, but the structural math hasn't changed. When the money's gone, the same cuts will be even harder because you'll have built infrastructure around the fiction.

**Don't:** Model yourself as frozen.
**Why:** You're not going to stop hiring, not going to stop upgrading infrastructure, not going to stop paying more for tooling next year than this year. If your model assumes no expense growth, it's already wrong.

**Don't:** Let "I'll figure it out" substitute for running the numbers.
**Why:** The alarm that goes off when you spend money doesn't go off when you waste runway. Optimism is not a plan. The calculation is not optional. It takes twenty minutes and tells you whether you're default alive or default dead, which is the single most important operational fact about your company.
