# Fundraising (Survival Guide)

## When to Use
- Founder is preparing for a first round, a bridge, or a priced round.
- Founder has been in market for weeks and is demoralized by rejections.
- Founder is considering terms that feel wrong but they're desperate.
- Founder is being approached by corp dev at a large company and doesn't know how to handle it.
- Founder is about to trade equity for something (employee, advisor, partnership) and hasn't done the math.

## Fails When
- Founder is default alive with strong growth and the right answer is "don't raise" — route to default-alive-or-dead.
- Founder has no real shot at raising and the honest answer is get profitable or shut down — route to default-alive-or-dead.

## Core Concept

The default state of any deal is that it will fall through. Treat every deal as if it will die unless you actively push it forward. That's the most important thing to understand about fundraising, and if you internalize it, you'll navigate the process far better than most founders do.

Raising money is the second hardest part of starting a startup. The hardest is making something people want — most startups that die, die because they didn't do that. But the second biggest cause of death is the difficulty of raising money. Fundraising is brutal, and the reason it's brutal is not just that it's hard, but that it's so much harder than founders expect.

One reason fundraising is so broken is the structure of the market. Markets are less forgiving than schools or big companies. Customers and investors evaluate startups the same way, and both are unsentimental. But raising from investors is harder than selling to customers because there are so few of them. You're unlikely to have more than 10 who are interested. There's nothing like an efficient market; the randomness of any single investor's behavior can really affect you. And investors are random. By ordinary standards, most investors are incompetent. They constantly make big decisions about things they don't understand, so they're skittish. One day enthusiastic, next day not returning calls. They're not playing games. They just can't make up their minds.

Those wildly fluctuating nodes are all linked. Investors know each other, and the biggest factor in their opinion of you is the opinion of other investors. The result is a system like a primitive multi-celled sea creature — you irritate one extremity and the whole thing contracts violently. This is why fundraising has a tipping point: before momentum, it's grinding; after, it's a flood. The first commitment is by far the hardest to get. Don't be demoralized by how hard it is. Much of the difficulty comes from this external structure, not from your startup.

## How to Apply

1. **Have low expectations.** Founders are optimistic by nature. That works in technology. It's the wrong way to approach raising money. Assume investors will let you down. Assume deals will fall through. Tie yourself to the mast — the tendency as a deal progresses is to start believing it will happen and then to depend on it happening. Resist that.

2. **Keep working on the startup.** This sounds obvious. It's actually hard. Raising money has a mysterious capacity to suck up all your attention. Even one meeting a day with investors will burn your whole day — the prep, the meeting, the commute, the post-mortem. The fix: partition the company. Pick one founder to deal with investors while the others keep the company going. Put the startup first. Fit meetings into the spare moments in your development schedule, rather than doing development in the spare moments between meetings. If you keep the company moving — releasing features, increasing traffic, doing deals — those investor meetings get more productive because the story keeps improving.

3. **Be conservative with reasonable offers.** If someone reputable offers you funding on reasonable terms, take it. There have been startups that ignored this advice and got away with it. But in the same position I'd give the same advice again. Who knows how many bullets were in the gun they were playing Russian roulette with?

4. **Be independent. Get to ramen profitable if you can.** Once you cross ramen profitable, everything changes. You may still need investment to go big, but you don't need it this month. There is nothing investors like more than a startup that seems like it's going to succeed even without them.

5. **The recipe for impressing investors when you're not already impressive:**
   a. Make something worth investing in.
   b. Understand why it's worth investing in.
   c. Explain that clearly to investors.
   
   If you're saying something true, you'll seem confident saying it. Conversely, never let pitching draw you into bullshitting. The moment you leave the territory of truth, you're weak. Make the truth good, then just tell it.

6. **Seem formidable, which just means being justifiably confident.** A formidable person is one who seems like they'll get what they want regardless of obstacles. Formidable is close to confident, except someone could be confident and mistaken. Formidable is *roughly justifiably confident*. The way for an inexperienced founder to seem formidable is to stick to the truth. Convince yourself your startup is worth investing in by genuinely evaluating it. If it isn't, don't try to raise. If it is, you'll be telling the truth when you explain it, and investors will sense that.

7. **Have a one-line "why now?"** Every company that gets really big is "lucky" in the sense that their growth is due mostly to some external wave they're riding. If your idea is so great, why hasn't someone already done it? Ideally the answer is: it only recently became a good idea, because something changed, and no one else has noticed yet. If you can't answer "why now?" convincingly, maybe now isn't the time.

8. **The soundbite.** Create a phrase that makes you memorable. At Viaweb ours was "the Microsoft Word of ecommerce." The format is usually "the X of Y" or "X meets Y." Drop it casually early in the pitch. A sticky formulation is worth its weight when investors are trying to remember you after hearing 30 pitches.

9. **The equity equation.** Whenever you trade equity for something — money, an employee, a partnership — the deal is good only if what you get makes the company worth more than 1/(1-n) relative to before. Give up 7% and you need at least a 7.5% improvement in expected outcome (1/0.93 ≈ 1.075). You should always feel richer after trading equity. If you don't, you made a bad trade.

10. **Pick good investors over the best valuation.** The investor who picks up the phone on a Sunday when you're in crisis is worth far more than the one who got you an extra million on your cap. A high valuation with a bad investor is almost always worse than a lower valuation with a great one.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder is three weeks into a raise. Thirty meetings. Two soft yeses, eight maybes, twenty no's. They haven't shipped code in three weeks. They're exhausted and starting to question whether the company is even a good idea.

**Application:** Stop. Partition the company today. One of you goes back to building — because the company is dying from neglect while you chase money. The other one owns the raise. The twenty no's are not a verdict on your company; they're the base rate. The two soft yeses are your leverage — get them to a hard yes this week by showing them progress in the product (which is why the other cofounder needs to be shipping). Force a close by creating a deadline: one of the soft yeses becomes the wedge, and you tell everyone else they have until Friday. When one commits, the sea creature starts contracting, and the maybes will firm up or drop out. The worst mistake right now is to let the grinding phase go on for another month while your product stagnates. Make something that gets measurably better this week. Show it to the soft yeses. Close. If nobody closes in two weeks, that's information — you may need to cut and get to profitability instead.

**Result:** Founders partition, ship two meaningful updates in a week, one soft yes turns into a hard commit on Friday, other maybes follow within 10 days. Round closes. Founder who was shipping is happier than founder who was raising, which also tells them something about who should run fundraising in future.

---

**Situation:** Corp dev from a large tech company reached out. "They want to chat. It might be an acquisition offer."

**Application:** Don't take the first meeting. Seriously. Corp dev's job is not to buy your company. Their job is to gather information, understand competitive threats, and occasionally execute acquisitions when the timing is right for them, not you. The risk is not that they'll talk you into something. The risk is that they'll distract you for six weeks with meetings and diligence that go nowhere, during which your growth rate drops, your morale drops, and your team notices the founder's attention has left the building. Treat corp dev like alcoholism: never take the first drink. If you are genuinely open to being acquired on good terms right now, tell your investors or a banker to run a real process. If you're not, reply politely that you're heads-down building and aren't the right fit for exploratory conversations. You will not miss an offer by declining the meeting; if they want to buy you, they'll escalate. Coffee with corp dev is almost never the beginning of an acquisition. It's the beginning of a three-month distraction.

**Result:** Founder declines the meeting, keeps building, continues growing, and a year later gets a real offer through a real process at a much higher number.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Raise when you don't need to.
**Why:** The time to raise is not when you need it or when you hit an arbitrary deadline. It's when you can convince investors, and not before. Raising reactively gives you terrible terms and distorts incentives. Raising from strength is the only good kind of raise.

**Don't:** Let fundraising become the top idea in your mind.
**Why:** Your best thinking time is the top-idea slot (shower, walk, falling asleep). If fundraising occupies it, your product stops getting that time, which is exactly when your product most needs it. Protect the slot. Get ramen profitable so money isn't constantly resident in your head.

**Don't:** Be passive with investors.
**Why:** Investors resist committing except insofar as you push them to. It's in their interest to collect maximum information while making minimum decisions. The best way to force them is competing investors. The second best is to ask what specific questions they need answered and then answer them. Open-ended "let's chat again in two weeks" meetings are a slow way to die.

**Don't:** Take rejection personally.
**Why:** Statistically the average VC rejects 500–800 pitches per year and does one to two deals. Getting rejected is not data about you; it's the base rate. Use the content of rejections — what they said was wrong and whether they're right — but don't let the pile become an undifferentiated depressive mass. Sort them. Analyze them. Most are noise.

**Don't:** Chase the highest valuation.
**Why:** A high valuation with a bad investor is usually worse than a lower valuation with a great one. You want the person who picks up the phone on Sunday when you're in crisis, not the one who gives you an extra $2M on the cap and disappears for a year. Also: very high valuations set bars you later have to hit, which imports future problems for present bragging rights.

**Don't:** Pad your pitch with defensive hedging.
**Why:** Inexperienced founders try to convince with the pitch. The right move is to let the startup do the work. Every sentence of fluff or salesmanship reads as "something's not real here." Stick to the truth crisply. Make the truth good, then just tell it.

**Don't:** Treat a close as a close until the money is wired.
**Why:** Deals fall through. Term sheets get revoked. Diligence turns up surprises. Markets shift in a week. Keep other options warm until the money hits your account, not until the term sheet is signed.
