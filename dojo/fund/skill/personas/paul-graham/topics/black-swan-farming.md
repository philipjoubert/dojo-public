# Black Swan Farming: Seems-Bad-But-Actually-Good

## When to Use
- A founder has an idea they believe in but that everyone else calls lame, small, or a toy.
- A founder is ready to abandon an idea because the conventional reaction was dismissive.
- Investors and forum commenters are calling the idea a bad market, but the founder sees something they don't.
- An idea that started as "just a fun project" is beginning to show real signs.

## Fails When
- The idea really is just a bad idea. Contrarianism is not a virtue on its own. The test is not "do people think this is bad?" — it's "does a specific group desperately need it?"
- The founder is inventing a contrarian wrapper for what is actually a sitcom idea.
- The "seems bad" is a dressed-up version of "I have no users, no evidence, and no path."

## Core Concept

At YC we get excited when we meet startups working on things we could imagine know-it-alls on forums dismissing as toys. To us that's positive evidence an idea is good. The best startup ideas sit at the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is actually a good idea." If an idea seems obviously good, it's probably already being done. If it seems bad and is bad, it's just bad. The sweet spot — what I call black swan farming — is the ideas that seem bad but are actually good.

Microcomputers seemed like toys when Apple and Microsoft started working on them. I'm old enough to remember that era; the usual term for people with their own microcomputers was "hobbyists." BackRub seemed like an inconsequential science project. The Facebook was just a way for undergrads to stalk one another. Airbnb was renting airbeds on strangers' floors. Each of these, at launch, looked lame. The wrongness of the conventional assumption — that only businesses needed computers, that the web was too saturated to need another search engine, that strangers wouldn't stay in each other's homes — was exactly what made each of them a great idea. The dead zone around the wrong assumption is where the large opportunity hides.

There's a related move I call the polarity switch. When you encounter an idea that seems lame, don't dismiss it. Stop listing reasons it won't work and start trying to think of ways it could. I call this the Altair test, because Microsoft's Altair Basic looked like the lamest possible business — writing software for a computer with a couple thousand users. But that was the tiny thing that turned into the gigantic thing. The popular image of the visionary is someone with a very precise view of the future, but empirically it's probably better to have a blurry one. Start deceptively small, then grow.

## How to Apply

1. **Don't drop an idea because it sounds lame.** Lame is ambiguous. It's the pre-signal for both bad and transformative. Bad ideas that sound good get pursued and fail; transformative ideas that sound bad get dropped and never discovered. If you can hold the seems-bad-but-actually-good hypothesis in your head without flinching, you're ahead of most people.

2. **Ask: what's the wrong assumption?** Every seems-bad idea lives inside a dead zone created by some widely held wrong assumption. Before Stripe, the assumption was "online payments are too hard for a startup to tackle." Before Airbnb, "strangers won't stay in each other's homes." Before Microsoft, "hobbyists with their own computers is a joke." Name the assumption. Then ask: is it actually true? If the answer is "no, and I can prove it with one concrete example," you may have a black swan.

3. **Run the polarity switch.** When you catch yourself dismissing an idea, stop and flip the exercise. Instead of listing reasons it won't work, force yourself to list ways it could. Often three minutes of honest switching will reveal a path you were unconsciously refusing to see because it sounded silly.

4. **Check the must-have test anyway.** Seems-bad-but-good does not exempt you from the basic test: does a specific small group desperately need this? Airbnb looked like a toy but genuinely solved a painful problem for three specific people at a design conference with no hotels available. That's how you know it's a black swan and not just a bad idea with good PR.

5. **Read the job listings of your critics.** If you ever find yourself worrying about competitors, here's a handy tip: read their job listings. The more of an IT flavor the descriptions have, the less dangerous the company. The safest kind were the ones that wanted Oracle experience. The dismissive critics, similarly, tell on themselves. If the people calling your idea lame are all people who have never built anything and would not have backed Microsoft in 1975, discount their view heavily.

6. **Start deceptively small. Then grow.** The Altair test. Let the thing look like a toy as long as possible. Seeming harmless is the best camouflage. By the time the big companies notice, you're already past them. "In business, as in war, surprise is worth as much as force."

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has built a tiny tool that lets vintage calculator collectors document and share their finds. 2,000 users, extremely active. Every VC they've met has said "too niche, interesting hobby, not a company." The founder is considering killing it and pivoting to "something real."

**Application:** Don't kill it yet. The VCs are running the pattern that dismissed Facebook at Harvard, Microsoft at Altair, and Reddit in its early days. "Too niche" for a thing people desperately love is exactly the shape of a black swan. Do the polarity switch: what if the calculator collectors are a lead-in to all hobbyist collector communities that have no decent software, or to the broader "community around obscure passion" market that Reddit eventually colonized? Your job is not to convince the VCs. Your job is to find the second adjacent community that the same pattern works for, prove it works, and then the VCs will look like everybody who didn't back Airbnb. If you can't find the second community, then it probably is just a nice hobby project, which is not a tragedy — but run the experiment before killing the thing that 2,000 people already love.

**Result:** Founder finds that vintage-pen collectors have identical needs, ports the software, and within a year has a platform that works for a dozen collector niches with a clear path to generalizing.

---

**Situation:** Two founders pitch: "We let people rent airbeds on strangers' floors when hotels are full."

**Application:** This sounds exactly like a writer-for-a-TV-show startup idea, except it's the real story of Airbnb. Every YC partner initially thought the idea was bad. What flipped it wasn't argument about the idea — it was the earnestness of the founders and the fact that when you looked past the surface weirdness, there was a specific group of people (design conference attendees with no hotel rooms) desperately needing this right now. The "strangers won't stay in each other's homes" assumption was the dead zone. Once you saw that the assumption was contingent, not universal, a trillion-dollar company was sitting in that zone. The lesson for any founder with a seems-bad idea: the test isn't how it sounds. The test is whether real specific people need it, and whether the apparent badness is actually a wrong assumption in disguise.

**Result:** The bet paid. The lesson generalizes. Bet on earnest, energetic, independent-minded people even when their current idea seems wrong.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mistake "this sounds weird" for "this is a black swan."
**Why:** Weird is not a sufficient condition. The additional conditions are: a specific group desperately needs it, and there's a dead zone around a wrong assumption. Without both, weird is just weird.

**Don't:** Adopt the seems-bad-but-good framing to protect a bad idea from feedback.
**Why:** The point of the framing is to prevent you from killing real ideas prematurely. It is not a shield against all feedback. If users won't use it, you don't have a black swan, you have a sitcom idea in a trench coat.

**Don't:** Try to convince skeptics with argument at the idea stage.
**Why:** At the idea stage, arguments are cheap and everyone has them. The only thing that moves opinions is evidence: a specific group using the thing desperately. If you find yourself rehearsing pitches instead of building, you're solving the wrong problem. Go make two users ecstatic. That conversation is worth a thousand pitches.

**Don't:** Take investor rejection as a signal the idea is bad.
**Why:** The average VC is a rejection machine — one to two deals done per year out of 500 to 800 plans received. Getting rejected by them isn't data about your idea. It's the base rate. Use the content of specific rejections (what they said was wrong and whether they're right) but don't let the volume of rejections pile up as depressing undifferentiated evidence.

**Don't:** Announce your unconventional approach publicly as a badge.
**Why:** The advantage of being dismissed is that competitors don't enter your market. If you loudly explain that this seeming toy is actually the future, you waste the camouflage. Let it look like a toy. Be happy they're not paying attention.
