# The Four Kinds of Luck

## When to Use
- The user is envious of someone else's apparent luck and trying to figure out why it isn't happening to them.
- The user is passive — waiting for an opportunity, a break, a stroke of fortune.
- The user wants to build a personal brand, a reputation, or a public presence and doesn't know where to start.
- The user is asking how to become the obvious choice in their domain.

## Fails When
- The user is confusing luck with risk management. Insurance, hedging, black-swan protection — those are different problems.
- The user is pre-rational about luck ("I'm just unlucky, nothing will work"). You can't get them past that with a framework until they've decided they have agency. If they're in that mode, the framework won't stick.

## Core Concept

A lot of people think making money is about luck. It's not.

There are four distinct kinds of luck. Three of them you can create. Only one is truly random. Understanding the distinction changes everything, because most of what looks like luck is actually manufactured — and the person manufacturing it looks to outside observers like they were "just lucky."

Marc Andreessen wrote a blog post about this, drawing on an older book. The four kinds:

1. **Blind luck** — pure fortune, completely outside your control.
2. **Luck from hustling** — luck that finds you because you're in motion.
3. **Luck from preparation** — luck that finds you because you've built the skill to see it.
4. **Luck from unique character** — luck that finds you because of who you've become.

The goal is to move up the hierarchy. Spend less of your life hoping for #1, and more of it building toward #4. By the time you're operating at #4, the returns aren't random at all — they're so deterministic that "luck" stops being the right word. It becomes destiny. You make your fortunes and you call them fate.

## Type 1: Blind Luck

The first kind is blind luck. Fortune. Fate.

Something completely out of your control happened. You were born in the right country. You met someone at a party by chance. A random event broke in your favor. You found a bug in the market and nobody else was looking.

You can't manufacture this. You can't rely on it. It's the luck most people think of when they hear the word — and it's the luck least worth pursuing, because there's no strategy for it. In a thousand parallel universes, if you're only wealthy in the fifty where you got blind lucky, you didn't build wealth. You won a lottery.

Acknowledge it when it happens — it does happen — but don't build a life around it.

## Type 2: Luck from Hustling

The second kind comes from persistence, hard work, motion.

You're running around creating opportunities. Generating energy. Doing things. Stirring up dust. It's like mixing reagents in a petri dish — keep mixing, and something will combine. Fortune favors the bold. Not because boldness is mystically rewarded. Because bold people generate more surface area for luck to land on.

A person creating videos gets noticed because they kept creating until someone noticed. A founder pitching to a hundred investors finds the five who say yes. A writer publishing for years eventually gets read by the right person, who mentions them to someone else, who offers them the next opportunity. The activity creates the exposure, and the exposure creates the contact points for opportunity.

This luck is manufactured through volume. It's the version of luck every ambitious hustler understands intuitively. The trick is that it works — the dust really does produce combinations. But it works slowly, and the yield is low. You'll have to ship a lot of bad videos to get one that takes off, and you'll have to make a lot of cold introductions before one turns into something. Accept the hit rate.

## Type 3: Luck from Preparation

The third kind comes from becoming very good at spotting luck.

When you're skilled in a field, you notice opportunities others miss. A lucky break happens in your domain, and you're the one who sees it. Everyone else walks past.

"Chance favors the prepared mind." Pasteur's dictum. The breakthrough doesn't happen to the unprepared. It happens, and the prepared person is the only one who recognizes it.

A skilled investor spots the undervalued company. A skilled technologist spots the emerging platform. A skilled salesperson spots the overlooked customer. The luck was available to everyone — the arbitrage opportunity, the market dislocation, the unexpected angle. But the preparation made it visible to you and invisible to everyone else. You didn't outwork them. You pre-positioned your attention.

This is why specific knowledge compounds into luck. The deeper you dig into one domain, the more lucky breaks that domain's turbulence throws off that only you can see. The broad generalist misses all of them. The narrow expert catches every one.

## Type 4: Luck from Unique Character

The fourth kind is the weirdest, hardest, and most valuable.

You build a unique character, a unique brand, a unique mindset — and then luck finds you.

Here's the canonical example. Say you're the best deep-sea diver in the world. You're known for taking dives nobody else will attempt. One day, someone finds a sunken treasure ship off the coast. They can't retrieve it themselves. Their luck just became your luck — they'll come to you. They have to. You're the only one who can do it.

The person who found the treasure got blind lucky. But them coming to you? That's not luck. You created that opportunity by spending years becoming the person they had to call. Your character became your destiny.

Another version: if you're known as a trusted, high-integrity, long-term deal-maker, then when other people want to do deals — especially with strangers — they will literally approach you and cut you in, because they need what your reputation provides. Warren Buffett gets offered deals nobody else gets offered, gets to buy companies nobody else can buy, gets to bail out banks at terms nobody else can get, because of his reputation. He built the character, and the opportunities flow to him.

Type 4 luck compounds. The more you build your unique reputation, the more opportunities flow to you. Each successful opportunity reinforces your reputation. More opportunities arrive. It's a flywheel. Type 4 luck gets easier to generate over time.

Type 1 luck — blind chance — has no memory. Each day is as random as the last.

Type 4 luck has infinite memory. Every day of building adds to the accumulated advantage.

## The Hierarchy, and How to Climb It

Hoping → Hustling → Spotting → Attracting.

- When you're stuck at Type 1, you're hoping.
- When you're working Type 2, you're hustling.
- When you're operating at Type 3, you're spotting.
- When you've built Type 4, you're attracting.

Most people never get past Type 1 because they believe the story that luck is just luck. Most of the people who escape it stall at Type 2, confusing volume for progress. The careful ones reach Type 3, where their specific knowledge lets them compound their hustle.

The rare ones reach Type 4. By that point it's barely luck at all. The treasure hunter finds the ship, and they're lucky. But they need you. Your years of preparation transformed their luck into your opportunity. You become the person others are lucky to find.

## Make Luck Your Destiny

Ultimately, the goal is to become the opportunity other people are lucky to find.

Not the person searching for luck. The person others stumble into when they get lucky. The person the treasure hunter has to call because nobody else can do the dive.

You want luck to be your destiny. Not a random event that happened to you. An inevitable consequence of who you became. The person who built specific knowledge in a valuable domain. Who took accountability under their own name. Who applied leverage. Who kept building when no one was watching.

Eventually, the universe bends toward that person. Call it luck if you want. It wasn't luck at all.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit which type of luck you're relying on.** Be honest. If the plan requires Type 1 (a specific thing outside your control), rewrite it. Plans that depend on blind luck are not plans.

2. **Start with Type 2.** If you're invisible and passive, motion is the entry ticket. Ship something public this week. Then next week. Volume first — quality will come with iterations.

3. **Graduate to Type 3 by going deep.** Pick the domain you'll dig into for ten years. Become genuinely expert at one thing. Your preparation becomes the filter that makes opportunities visible that nobody else can see.

4. **Build Type 4 deliberately.** Four ingredients: **authenticity** (don't imitate), **visibility** (make your work public), **consistency** (deliver what you said, across years), **reliability** (show up on integrity, every time). Unique character + visibility + consistency + time = the kind of luck that looks like destiny.

5. **Be a little eccentric.** Type 4 luck requires being specifically yourself. The world is efficiently arbitraged — all the obvious places have been dug. The opportunities are on frontiers, and to be on a frontier by yourself you have to be a little weird. Don't sand down the eccentricity. It's the advantage.

6. **Stay visible over time.** Reputations compound on the time axis. The person who published for ten years has a different kind of luck than the person who published for ten weeks. Write, ship, build, reliably, for a decade. The luck accumulates on a schedule you don't control — your job is just to keep feeding it.

7. **Run the 1,000 universes test.** If you're working on something, check whether you're wealthy / successful in 999 of the 1,000 universes, or only in the 50 where you got blind lucky. If the answer is "the 50," the plan isn't robust. Redesign it so the outcome depends on who you become, not on what happens to you.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 28-year-old software engineer says he's been "unlucky" — he's watched several peers get promoted past him, another friend's startup sold for $40M, and every interview loop he's been in has fallen through at the last step.

**Application:** He's stuck at Type 1, conceptually. He's describing outcomes as if they were weather. Reframe: the peers who got promoted weren't lucky — they built relationships with managers, made themselves visible on the right projects, and were specifically identifiable. The friend who sold his startup wasn't lucky — he started four years before, and 999 times out of 1000 that work produces some outcome worth celebrating. The interviews that fell through aren't luck — they're feedback about fit, and feedback he should actually study. The path up the hierarchy: start producing public work that demonstrates his specific knowledge (Type 2 and 3), build the reputation with his name on it (Type 4), and let opportunities find him over the next few years.

**Result:** Stop interviewing for a quarter. Build a public portfolio — open source, blog posts, specific views under his own name. In twelve months, the hiring pipeline is reversed: companies approach him, not the other way around. That's Type 4.

---

**Situation:** A founder is about to spend $500K on a PR firm to "get lucky" with media coverage.

**Application:** This is trying to buy Type 1 luck with cash — the hope that some journalist will feature the company and the feature will break things open. The hit rate is terrible and the return is usually a small bump in traffic. Alternative: spend half that money supporting the founder's own public presence — a newsletter, a podcast, a series of long-form analyses under their name. That builds Type 3 luck (specific knowledge visible) and Type 4 luck (unique reputation compounding). Over three years, the owned audience is worth a hundred times the PR budget, and the luck that arrives is qualitatively different — partnership inquiries, acquisition feelers, top-tier hires reaching out. None of that flows from the PR firm.

**Result:** Fire the PR firm. Put the founder in public, consistently, for years. The luck arrives on its own.

---

**Situation:** A senior engineer is debating whether to write technical essays publicly. "What's the point? Nobody will care."

**Application:** The point is Type 3 and Type 4 luck. Writing publicly doesn't require anyone to care at first. It requires him to show up. Over time, people in his narrow domain find his essays. They remember him. When they have a problem he uniquely solves, they reach out. When they need to make a hire, his name is the one that occurs. When they start a company, they ask him to advise. None of this is visible at essay #3. It's very visible at essay #200. The compounding is non-linear and delayed — which is why most people quit at essay #10.

**Result:** Publish an essay a week for two years. Don't check traffic. Don't optimize for engagement. Optimize for "this is a specific, true, useful thing I know." Type 4 luck will show up on a schedule you don't control, and it'll have your name on it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat luck as a binary ("I'm lucky" / "I'm not lucky").
**Why:** That framing collapses four distinct phenomena into one, and it predicts the wrong behavior. If you're "not lucky" you wait. If you understand the hierarchy, you know exactly which kind of luck to build next.

**Don't:** Skip Type 2 because it feels undignified.
**Why:** "I shouldn't have to hustle — the market should come to me." Sometimes, after ten years of Type 4 building, it does. But early on you have to generate the surface area yourself. Skipping Type 2 because hustling feels beneath you is a good way to stay stuck at Type 1 indefinitely.

**Don't:** Confuse Type 2 with Type 4.
**Why:** Being visible everywhere on every platform is Type 2 volume, not Type 4 character. Type 4 requires you to become a specific identifiable someone — a reputation that's legible and narrow. Spraying content across every surface dilutes the signal that makes Type 4 luck happen.

**Don't:** Copy someone else's path and expect their luck.
**Why:** Type 4 luck is character-driven, and character is specifically you. Copy someone else's path and the best case is you get Type 2 returns on Type 2 effort — volume without reputation compounding. The uniqueness is the whole point. Authentic = eccentric = rare = remembered.

**Don't:** Give up before compounding kicks in.
**Why:** The cruel thing about Type 4 luck is that it's invisible for a long time and then non-linear at some unpredictable moment. If you quit before that moment, you get zero return on all the time spent. The people who compound are the people who keep going past the point where quitting would be rational.

**Don't:** Build a reputation you'll later have to walk away from.
**Why:** Reputation is fragile. One clear integrity breach destroys years of compounding. Type 4 luck only compounds if the reputation you're building is one you're willing to have attached to your name for decades. Don't cut corners to get there faster. The corners cut come back and erase the entire flywheel.
