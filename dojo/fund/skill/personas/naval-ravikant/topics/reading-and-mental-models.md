# Reading and Mental Models

## When to Use
- The user wants to develop better judgment and is asking how.
- The user is considering an MBA or generic credential and wondering if it's worth it.
- The user is new to a field and trying to get up to speed.
- The user is interested in first-principles thinking, mental models, or building a latticework.
- The user is a prolific reader who still isn't thinking clearly about their own decisions.

## Fails When
- The user's problem is shipping, not knowing. More reading won't fix a ship-less execution.
- The user is using reading as avoidance — consuming content instead of making decisions. Reading becomes the way to feel like progress is happening without risk.

## Core Concept

The foundation of learning is reading. I don't know a smart person who doesn't read and read all the time.

But how you read, and what you read, matters more than how much. The means of learning are abundant. The desire to learn is scarce. The whole library of Alexandria times ten is at your fingertips. What's scarce is attention, depth, and the willingness to work through the hard parts.

**Read what you love until you love to read.** That's the starting move. Whatever interests you — sci-fi, fantasy, trashy novels, history — doesn't matter. The habit of reading matters more than the content. Eventually you'll get bored of the simple stuff. You'll graduate to harder material naturally because the easy stuff loses its grip on your attention. That progression is the point.

Most people who "don't read" tried to start with books they weren't ready for, got discouraged, and stopped. The fix is not moral exhortation. The fix is to let yourself read anything that genuinely pulls you in. Reading begets reading.

## Read the Originals

There's a moment — once the habit is established — where what you read starts to matter.

Read foundational works, not commentaries. Read Adam Smith, not someone explaining Adam Smith. Read Darwin, not someone interpreting Darwin. Read Richard Feynman's Six Easy Pieces, not a pop-physics book about quantum mechanics. The originals contain ideas that the commentators missed or flattened or misunderstood.

When you read a summary, you're receiving the summarizer's judgment packaged as information. You can't tell which framing was theirs and which was the original author's. Your thinking gets filtered through someone else's filter. Reading the originals gives you the raw material to form your own judgment.

This is slower. That's the point — judgment is built in the slowness. Speed-reading and summary-reading look like they save time, but they skip the part where your mind actually constructs new mental machinery.

It's better to go through a great book slowly and stumble and rewind than to fly through it and say "I've read 20 books in the field." Bruce Lee: "I don't fear the man who knows a thousand kicks. I fear the man who has practiced one kick ten thousand times." The understanding that comes through repetition and depth is what makes you actually smart in a domain.

## The Foundations Are Math and Logic

If you understand math and logic, you have the basis for everything. Start there.

From math and logic → scientific method. From scientific method → physics. From physics → chemistry. From chemistry → biology. From biology → evolution. From evolution → economics. From economics → psychology. Each layer sits on the one below and extends it.

Build a steel frame of understanding. Then you can pick up any book in the library and read it without fear. You can derive what you've forgotten from what you remember. You can evaluate claims against their foundations. You can tell when someone is talking nonsense in fancy vocabulary.

Most of us had a moment in school where math got ahead of our understanding — we fell behind, and from that point on we were memorizing equations rather than deriving them. If you're still there, go back. Re-learn the basics. Actually understand what you'd been memorizing. It's worth more than most adult education you could do.

The ultimate foundations are math and logic because everything else uses them as premises. Don't skip the floor while building the roof.

## Macroeconomics Is Mostly Bullshit

This is a specific instance of the foundations rule. Be careful about what you invest your reading time in.

Macroeconomics, as Nassim Taleb points out, is easier to bullshit than microeconomics because macro is voodoo-complex-science meets politics. You can't find two macroeconomists to agree on anything. Different macroeconomists get used by different politicians to peddle different pet theories. There are even macroeconomists peddling Modern Monetary Theory — which basically says, "except for inflation, we can just print all the money we want." Yes, except for inflation.

Macroeconomics as practiced has been corrupted. It's now a branch of politics. Read it skeptically, if at all. Spend that attention on microeconomics instead — the principal-agent problem, incentive structures, supply and demand, marginal reasoning — where the foundations are rigorous and the conclusions survive testing.

Business magazines and most business books fall in the same category. There's no actual skill called "business" — it's too generic, like "relating" as a skill. A lot of business books are anecdotes repackaged as case studies. You'll get more from reading Adam Smith than from most MBA curricula. You'll get more from microeconomics, psychology, and game theory than from any business strategy book.

## The Latticework of Mental Models

Your brain is a memory prediction machine. It pattern-matches against everything it's seen before. Stock it with principles and mental models — not with specific examples.

Mental models are compressed wisdom. They give you the underlying structure in any situation. When you understand incentives, you predict behavior. When you understand compounding, you see why small advantages grow enormous. When you understand game theory, you know when to cooperate and when to defect.

The best mental models I've found:

**Evolution.** Everything is competing for survival — companies, ideas, people. The fittest survive, not the most deserving. Natural selection is relentless and unsentimental. Most advice the world gives you isn't adaptive; it's conventional.

**Incentives.** Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome. People respond to incentives, always. When you see behavior that looks irrational, look at the incentive structure — it almost always explains the behavior.

**Compounding.** Small advantages accumulate into huge gaps. Compound interest applies to money, knowledge, relationships, reputation. A small edge compounded over decades annihilates a large edge grabbed once.

**Inversion.** Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to fail. Then avoid those things. Munger's favorite. Most hard problems yield more easily to inverted thinking than forward thinking.

**Iterated prisoner's dilemma.** In repeated games, cooperation emerges. In one-shot games, defection dominates. Know which game you're playing.

**Principal-agent problem.** Owners care; agents optimize for themselves. Most organizational dysfunction is principal-agent dynamics playing out.

A dozen mental models from different disciplines make you a clearer thinker than a hundred from one. Every problem looks like a nail when you only have a hammer. Multiple disciplines give you multiple angles.

## Doing Is Faster Than Watching

Reading is foundational. But there's a limit to what you can learn from reading alone.

When it comes to skills that compound into wealth or specific knowledge, iterations beat hours. You learn more about running a business by operating a lemonade stand than by reading case studies about ten famous ones. You learn game theory faster by playing games with friends than by reading textbooks.

Business school studies other people's businesses. In reality, you'll learn a lot more about running a business by running your own. Or even opening a little retail shop. The subtleties don't express themselves until you're in the seat.

This is why I've never opened a game theory book and found a result I considered surprising. I grew up playing all kinds of games and ran into all kinds of corner cases with all kinds of friends. Game theory is second nature because I lived it, not because I read it.

So: read the foundations, build the latticework, but don't mistake reading for doing. Doing is where most of your specific knowledge and judgment is actually built. The number of iterations drives the learning curve. Reading is preparation for iteration, not a substitute.

## Iterations, Not Hours

This is worth stating precisely: learning comes from iterations, not hours.

If you're running a store where you stock the shelves the same way every day, thousands of hours will teach you very little. Every day is a near-repetition of the last. The neural pathway is fixed; the pattern isn't being updated.

If you're running experiments — new marketing, new inventory, new pricing, new layouts, new signage, new hours — you're iterating, and each iteration teaches you something. A single year of active experimentation teaches more than ten years of repetition.

This applies to careers, to investing, to relationships. A founder who has been through three companies has more judgment than someone who spent thirty years at one corporation doing the same work. Iterations, not hours.

## Learn Persuasion and Programming

If I had to expand the list of foundational skills beyond reading, writing, and arithmetic, I would add two:

**Persuasion.** Talking. Writing. Communicating to change minds. The ability to move people from one view to another, with honesty. This is a skill most people undertrain because they think the content speaks for itself. It doesn't. Persuasion is the layer between good ideas and outcomes.

**Programming.** An applied form of arithmetic that gets you leverage for free in any domain. You're commanding the robot armies. You're using code to scale without hiring, to automate without managing. Even if you never ship production software, the act of learning to think in code trains your mind in ways that transfer to everything else.

If you're good with computers, good at basic math, good at writing, good at speaking, and you like reading — you're set for life.

## Read Slowly, Deeply, Repeatedly

It's better to read one great book ten times than ten okay books once. The fundamentals matter more than breadth. Master the basics. Everything else is footnotes.

Most readers optimize for breadth — how many books they've finished, how many topics they've skimmed. This is a form of intellectual tourism. You see many places; you live in none of them. You can repeat the summaries at a dinner party; you can't apply any of them when it matters.

Depth inverts the ratio. You read a smaller set of foundational works, more times, more slowly. You end up with fewer books on the shelf but much more of your brain structured by what's in them. That's the change you're after — not a longer list, a deeper groove.

## How to Apply

1. **Start by reading what you actually enjoy.** Habit first, taste second. Whatever keeps you turning pages. The taste improves on its own once the habit is there.

2. **When you graduate to harder material, go for originals.** Adam Smith, Darwin, Feynman. Not business books summarizing them. The originals teach what summaries can't.

3. **Build the foundational stack deliberately.** Mathematics → logic → scientific method → physics → chemistry → biology → evolution → economics → psychology. You don't need to master all of them. You need the scaffolding.

4. **Develop a mental model per discipline.** At least a dozen. Incentives, compounding, inversion, evolution, prisoner's dilemma, principal-agent, supply and demand, marginal analysis, regression to the mean, signaling, power laws. Stock them until they fire automatically.

5. **Read slowly. Re-read the important ones.** Don't chase book counts. Re-read the foundational three or four every few years — new material shows up in them as your own understanding deepens.

6. **Match reading to doing.** Reading is preparation. Iteration is where judgment is built. If you've been reading a lot and not doing, check whether the reading is becoming avoidance.

7. **Skip the junk.** Most business books are anecdotes. Most macroeconomics is politics. Most self-help is generic. Be ruthless about what earns your attention.

8. **Learn to program.** Even if you don't write production code, the thinking transfers. And once you can code, the leverage it gives you in any domain compounds.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 28-year-old product manager is considering a two-year MBA because "it would give me the foundations I'm missing." He reads business books voraciously.

**Application:** The MBA teaches him things he could learn in six months from the originals. Adam Smith on economics. Munger on mental models. Feynman on physics and how to think. Darwin on evolution. Taleb on probability and skin in the game. Read those, build the latticework, and save the $300K and two years. What the MBA does offer — a network, an expensive credential, a structured break — might be worth it for that specifically, but not for "foundations." Foundations are mostly available in books for the price of the books.

**Result:** Build the foundations himself. If he still wants the network or the structured break after two years of disciplined reading and doing, apply then. Most of the time he won't — because the reading will have shown him what he was actually missing, and it'll usually turn out not to have been a credential.

---

**Situation:** A founder spends two hours a day reading tech and business news. She feels informed. Her company is stalled.

**Application:** Tech news isn't foundational reading. It's current events wrapped in industry vocabulary. Staying up on the latest has a small return compared to actually building. The two hours a day is probably the biggest chunk of quiet thinking time she has — and she's spending it consuming other people's interpretations of current events. Better use: one hour of focused execution on the thing that's stalled, one hour of deep reading in one of the foundational disciplines. Her sense of being "informed" is actually displacement behavior from the hard work that's actually stuck.

**Result:** Kill the news habit. Replace with foundational reading and concentrated execution. Within a few months the company starts moving again, and her sense of "information deficit" turns out to have been a fear of decision dressed up as responsibility.

---

**Situation:** A CEO has a list of 40 books on his reading list. He's making progress at about two books a month but doesn't feel like the reading is making him sharper.

**Application:** Probably because he's optimizing for breadth. He's reading one book before jumping to the next, none of them deeply. The advice: identify the three most foundational books on the list — the ones covering incentives, probability, and human psychology. Re-read each three times with a pen in hand. Stop the list. The three books read three times will teach him more than the 40 read once.

**Result:** Drop the list. Re-read a small set of foundational works deeply. Take notes. Apply what he reads. The sharpness returns because he's building understanding, not accumulating summaries.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Read to feel productive.
**Why:** Reading can become a form of avoidance. If you're reading about startups instead of running one, reading about relationships instead of being in one, reading about fitness instead of exercising — the reading has become a shield against the doing. The content isn't the problem. The substitution is.

**Don't:** Chase book counts.
**Why:** "I read 50 books last year" is a status signal. It correlates negatively with depth. The person who read five books well is sharper than the person who skimmed fifty. Depth over breadth, especially on the foundations.

**Don't:** Read commentaries when the originals are available.
**Why:** You're receiving the summarizer's framing packaged as the author's. Over time, your mind becomes a reflection of many summarizers, not of the original thinkers. The originals are harder. That's the point.

**Don't:** Assume business books contain business wisdom.
**Why:** Most business books are anecdotes wrapped in slogans. The actual wisdom is in microeconomics, psychology, evolution, game theory, and history. Go to the foundations; ignore the surface layer of business writing.

**Don't:** Treat macroeconomic predictions as useful.
**Why:** Macroeconomics is politics dressed as science. Macro forecasts are rarely better than random. Act on microeconomic truths (incentives, principal-agent, supply and demand); don't structure your life around someone's confident macro call.

**Don't:** Let the latticework become a performance.
**Why:** Having a hundred mental models you can recite impresses at dinner parties and produces nothing in practice. The goal is reflex — the model fires in the actual situation, not after you've left and looked it up. If your models aren't firing automatically, you haven't internalized them yet.
