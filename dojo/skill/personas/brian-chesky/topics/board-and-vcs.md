# Board and VCs

## When to Use
- You're choosing which investor to take money from and you need to think about the board seat, not just the check.
- You're disagreeing with a board member on a major decision and you're not sure how to handle it.
- A VC is pushing you toward an acquisition or a strategic move and your gut says not yet.
- You're being offered a junior partner as your board representative and you're weighing whether to push back.
- You took a piece of board advice, it didn't work, and you're trying to figure out what to do differently next time.

## Fails When
- You use this to dismiss all board input. Some board members — usually the ones who've actually built companies — add real value.
- You treat the board as an adversary by default. The good ones want you to succeed; they're partners, not opponents.
- You apply the "courtside seats" frame to the rare board member who's genuinely been in the chair.

## Core Concept

Here's the analogy I keep coming back to. Just because I had a front row seat at championship games doesn't mean I can coach an NBA team. And that's true of venture capitalists — just because you had a front row seat at a bunch of companies doesn't mean you know how to run a company. Watching a company from the board isn't the same as running one. The VC watched twenty companies from the outside. You've run one from the inside. The asymmetry of information isn't in their favor. It's in yours. That doesn't mean their input is worthless. It means you have to calibrate it for what it actually is: an outside view, sometimes pattern-matched well, often pattern-matched to situations that aren't yours.

Board members can't make companies, but they can destroy companies. I saw that with OpenAI — or almost destroyed companies. The average board member is kind of like a zero or a 0.1. They don't move the needle up. But they can move it down. So the job is twofold: pick board members who lean toward the 0.1 and away from the negative, and know how to manage them when they disagree with you.

Bias toward investors who have actually started or run companies. They know what it's like to be in your chair. And be very wary of junior partners. Junior partners tend to be the most risk-averse and the least likely to have your back when times go bad. The senior partners will just say "get rid of them." If you're going to take money from a fund, fight to get the operator-senior-partner who knows how ugly the inside of a real company looks, not the junior partner who's going to panic at the first bad quarter.

When you disagree with a board member, my approach is to take them through the details. Walk them through the journey. Show them what you see. Eighty percent of the time, they'll back off. Because most of the time, they're disagreeing from the outside view and they haven't done the work to earn a strong position on the inside view. If they stay entrenched after you've walked them through it, find other competent board members to weigh in. And here's the last thing you have to internalize: even if you take a VC's advice and it doesn't work, you're still responsible. The only thing that matters is whether you're successful, not whether you listened to them. You own the outcome no matter what. So it is a deeply strange move to follow advice you disagree with just because it came from an authority figure. They don't carry the consequences. You do.

One more lens I use, borrowed from Jeff Bezos. Perishable versus non-perishable opportunities. Most of the strategic moves your board will push you toward — acquisitions, market entries, big platform bets — are not perishable. They'll still be there in six months. The things that are perishable are the boring ones: product quality eroding, a key talent leaving, a cultural drift that's actually a one-way door. So when the board is pushing you to do the shiny thing, ask whether it's perishable. If not, default to getting the core right first. Most board-level urgency is manufactured.

## How to Apply

1. **Choose the partner, not the fund.** When you take money, fight for the senior partner who has actually run a company. Don't get stuck with a junior partner just because that's who the fund assigned.
2. **Map every board member into "makes" or "destroys."** Most are a zero or 0.1. Some tilt to the destroy side. Know which is which before you hit a hard decision.
3. **When you disagree, walk them through the details.** Don't argue at the level of conclusions. Take them through the journey you've been on. Show them the evidence. Eighty percent back off. You built that evidence by being in the details — this is when it pays off.
4. **Find the other competent board members.** If one entrenched board member is pushing in the wrong direction, don't go head-to-head alone. Get the operator-experienced members to weigh in.
5. **Remember you own the outcome.** Any advice you take and execute on is now yours. The VC doesn't carry the result. You do. So don't defer to authority when you disagree on substance.
6. **Apply the perishable filter.** When the board pushes for a move, ask: is this opportunity perishable or non-perishable? Non-perishable means it'll be there next year. Most strategic moves are non-perishable. Get the core right first.
7. **Keep the board informed. Don't surprise them.** The rule is you argue over substance, not over communication. A surprised board is a defensive board. The more context they have, the more 80% back off when you disagree.

## Examples

**Situation:** During the pandemic, advisers and board members told us to be cautious. Hold capital. Don't make dramatic moves. Wait for the dust to settle.
**Application:** A consensus decision in a moment of crisis is very often the middle of the road, and those are usually the worst decisions. Usually in a crisis you have to go left or right. I went hard. I made what felt like five years of decisions in three months. Not consensus. Decisive. Some board members didn't love it at the time. But I took them through the details — what the core product needed, what the org chart had to look like, what I'd learned from Jony Ive and Hiroki about how Apple actually ran. Most of them came around.
**Result:** We went from a company people said was going to go out of business to an IPO at a hundred-billion-dollar market cap. Had I deferred to the cautious consensus, we'd have survived — but we wouldn't have been defined by the crisis. Bad companies are destroyed by a crisis. Good companies survive. Great companies are defined.

**Situation:** A founder is being offered $30 million from a top fund, but the partner on the board will be a 28-year-old junior partner who has never run a company.
**Application:** The check is great. The board seat is a liability. Junior partners are the most risk-averse, the least likely to have your back when times go bad, and if you hit a rough patch, the senior partners will just say "get rid of them" rather than back you. Push hard to get the senior partner on the board, or take slightly less money from a fund where you can get a board member who's actually been in your chair.
**Result:** The founder negotiates for the senior partner. Two years later, in a down round, that senior partner is the one who carries the board through the hard conversation instead of panicking.

**Situation:** A VC is pushing a founder to make a large acquisition while the founder's core product still has NPS problems and hosts are complaining on social media.
**Application:** Perishable vs. non-perishable. The acquisition isn't perishable — that target will still exist in six or twelve months, and there will be other similar targets. What's perishable is the trust of the existing customers. They're telling you every day that the core isn't good enough. Absorbing a big company while your core is leaking trust is the worst possible use of attention. You don't have permission to expand until people love the core service.
**Result:** The founder passes on the acquisition, spends two quarters fixing the core, earns permission to expand, and does a better acquisition twelve months later from a position of strength.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Take a VC's advice just because they have pattern-matched from outside your company.
**Why:** Having a front row seat at twenty companies isn't the same as having run one. They're looking at your company from the outside. You're in the details. The asymmetry of understanding is in your favor — don't give it away. And you own the outcome regardless of whose advice you followed.

**Don't:** Let a junior partner take your board seat because that's "who the fund assigned."
**Why:** Junior partners are typically the most risk-averse and the least likely to have your back when things get hard. When you need a board member to absorb a bad quarter and defend you to the senior team at the fund, the junior partner is the wrong person for that job.

**Don't:** Argue conclusions with the board. Walk them through the details.
**Why:** Board members disagree at the level of conclusions because that's the only level they have access to. If you walk them through the journey you've been on — the details, the evidence, the customer voices, the operational reality — 80% of the time they back off. Because they're smart enough to know that the inside view usually beats the outside view when the inside view is actually informed.
