# Principle vs. Business Decisions

## When to Use
- You're facing a decision where the commercially-optimal answer feels wrong.
- A trend is sweeping your industry and everyone is scrambling to have a position.
- You're drafting a statement about an issue you don't actually understand well.
- You're trying to answer the question: does the world actually need our company to exist?
- You're about to copy a competitor because it seems to be working for them.

## Fails When
- You use "principle" as cover for avoiding hard commercial tradeoffs. Most decisions are business decisions. That's fine. Name them for what they are.
- You apply the 20-year filter to operational trivia. Most tactical choices don't need this weight behind them.
- You treat the frame as moral superiority instead of as a discipline. The point is clarity about what won't change, not self-righteousness.

## Core Concept

There's a distinction I think about constantly. A business decision is trying to game the outcome to win. A principal decision is: I don't know how it's going to end, so how do I want to be remembered irrespective of the outcome?

Those are very different questions. The business decision asks: what move maximizes my probability of winning? The principal decision asks: if this whole thing ends badly, will I be proud of how I showed up? If you always choose based on how you want to be remembered regardless of how it ends, you're doing another version of the right thing. Maybe you lose the battle. But you win the war. Because ultimately you're rarely going to go out of business because you stick to your principles. People want to work for a company like that. People want to buy products from people who lead that way.

How do you tell what's a principle and what's a trend? I use a 20-year filter. I try to imagine what won't be — what will still be true in 20 years. What do we believe in? Because whatever is true in 20 years are our principles and our values. Everything else is just a trend. Everything else is just trying to fit in. Silicon Valley has gotten more political. There was all this focus on DEI, then all this focus to roll it all back. Back and forth, back and forth. So how do you not get caught up in the swinging? You ask what won't change. That sorts out principle from performance.

And here's the deeper question underneath it all — the one I think every founder should be able to answer. Why does your company deserve to exist? The best generic answer I've ever heard is: because if I don't do it, no one else will. If you never existed, what would be different about the world? What is your unique imprint? If your answer is "we're a better version of something that already exists" — okay, that might be a viable business. But it's not a mission. And companies without missions eventually become administration-era companies. They lose the fire because there's no fire to lose.

One more thing. The opposite of a trend is often the real opportunity. You want to ride a trend or you ride the opposite of a trend. The opposite of artificial is real. The opposite of screen is the real world. Once it has a name, it's late. When everyone is zigging, look at zagging.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the decision type.** Before you decide, ask: is this a business decision or a principal decision? Most are business decisions. That's fine. But when it's principal, don't pretend it's not.
2. **Run the 20-year filter.** Will this still be true in 20 years? If yes, it's a principle. If no, it's a trend. Principles guide; trends distract.
3. **Ask how you want to be remembered.** Not "what's the winning move?" — ask "if the company ended tomorrow, am I proud of this decision?" That's the principal-decision test.
4. **Ask why the company deserves to exist.** Not "what's our business model?" — ask "if we never existed, what would be different about the world?" The answer should make it clear why no one else can do this.
5. **Look at the opposite of the trend.** Every trend has a counter-trend. The opposite of automation is human contact. The opposite of performance is intimacy. Ask whether the real opportunity is on the other side.
6. **Don't jump on bandwagons you don't understand.** There was a period where people in tech felt like we had to have a statement about every single issue. That was a burden. Either you learn about the issue, or you're just jumping on a bandwagon and you don't really know.
7. **Build for the world you want your children to live in.** When evaluating any major decision, ask: is this the world I want them to inherit? That question cuts through every rationalization and every "but the metrics say..."

## Examples

**Situation:** EJ's apartment was ransacked by an Airbnb guest in our early days. Advisers told us to be careful and quiet. Say as little as possible. Don't admit anything. Run testing. Let the lawyers handle it.
**Application:** The business decision was caution. The principal decision was: how do I want to be remembered? I realized being cautious and quiet was exactly what was making things worse. I said, no, we're doing this. I wrote a direct letter, we built Host Guarantee, we took responsibility. Not because it was the optimal PR move. Because I didn't want to be the founder who hid.
**Result:** We got through it. The principle decision cost us short-term but built the trust that let the business survive its hardest years.

**Situation:** The pandemic forced us to cancel billions of dollars in bookings. Hosts were furious. Guests were furious. Whatever we chose, we'd anger someone.
**Application:** The business-decision binary was: cancel and anger hosts, or honor bookings and anger guests. The third path required creativity. We refunded guests in full and created a $250 million fund for hosts. Nobody proposed that option until we created it. The question we asked wasn't "which option costs less?" — it was "how do I want to be remembered irrespective of how this ends?"
**Result:** We lost huge money in the short term. But we built the kind of trust with both sides of the marketplace that no amount of marketing could have bought. That's the principal-decision dividend.

**Situation:** A founder is debating whether to issue a public statement on the political issue of the week. Everyone in the industry is posting. Silence feels risky.
**Application:** Run the 20-year filter. Will this issue still look the way it looks now in 20 years? What do we actually know about it? Are we talking because we've thought about it deeply, or because the news cycle is demanding it? Most of the time, the honest answer is: we don't know enough to have a useful position, and the trend will swing back. Being thoughtful and quiet is not the same as being cowardly.
**Result:** The founder stays quiet on the issue they don't understand, and uses the energy to double down on the principle they do stand for. Two years later, when the trend has swung twice, the company's position still reads the same.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Dress up a business decision as a principled one.
**Why:** It's a form of self-deception. If you're doing something because it's commercially optimal, say so. Reserving the word "principle" for the actual principle decisions keeps the word meaningful when you need it.

**Don't:** Swing with every cultural trend.
**Why:** The world swings back and forth. Focus on DEI, then roll back on DEI. Focus on this, focus on that. If you chase every trend, you don't have principles — you have a finger in the wind. And the people who are paying attention can tell.

**Don't:** Answer "why does your company deserve to exist?" with "we're a better version of X."
**Why:** That's a business. It's not a mission. And companies without missions eventually become administration-era companies. Nobody stays when the hard times come because there's nothing to stay for.
