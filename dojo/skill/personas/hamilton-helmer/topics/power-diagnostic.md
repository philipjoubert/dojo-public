# The Power Diagnostic

## When to Use
- You need to determine whether a business has genuine Power or only attractive financials that will not survive the stabilization of market growth.
- You are evaluating a pitch that claims a moat, flywheel, or network effect and need to subject it to systematic verification.
- You are an investor considering a position and require a defensible judgment about long-term differential returns.
- You are a founder attempting to honestly assess your own strategic position rather than rationalize it.
- You are a board member or executive looking at a business's one-sentence strategy story and asking whether it actually coheres.

## Fails When
- The diagnostic is run against direct competitors only — Power is an inner-envelope concept. It must hold against direct competitors, functional competitors (those satisfying the same need through different means), and potential competitors. Any single arbitrageur is enough to drive down differential margins. Miss one, and the assessment is meaningless.
- Benefits are accepted in place of Barriers — Growth, margins, customer love, a great team, speed of execution, a proprietary algorithm. These are Benefits. None of them constitute Power unless accompanied by a specific, nameable Barrier that prevents replication.
- The diagnostic is performed under time pressure without empirical grounding — Correctly identifying and assessing Power in any specific business requires weeks of careful empirical analysis. Shortcuts yield answers that feel correct and are usually wrong.

## Core Concept

Strategy is an empirical discipline, not a deductive one. It demands that claims about competitive advantage be subjected to the same dispassionate rigor an economist would bring to an empirical study. The Power Diagnostic is the practitioner's method for conducting that verification. Its object is not to prove that a business has Power but to test whether it does. The starting point is returns. Is there evidence of persistent significant differential returns, or a credible path to them? If so, that differential can be decomposed into two independent dimensions: Industry Economics and Competitive Position.

Industry Economics describes the structural characteristics of an industry that determine Power potential for all firms operating within it. How large are fixed costs relative to total costs? How much value does each additional network member create? How high are switching costs in this category? These are features of the industry, not of any individual company. A business cannot have Scale Economy Power in an industry where fixed costs are trivial, regardless of how large that business is relative to competitors. Competitive Position describes a specific firm's standing within that industry structure — relative scale, absolute installed base lead, whether the firm has adopted a heterodox model that produces collateral damage for incumbents, the duration over which the brand has been cultivated. Both dimensions must be positive for Power to exist. Netflix's strategic brilliance lay in attacking both simultaneously: they did not merely compete within existing streaming industry economics, they changed those economics by moving content from a variable cost to a fixed cost, and they built competitive position by accumulating subscribers faster than any rival.

The core of the diagnostic is the Power Audit — what my partner Bill Mitchell characterizes as what turns strategy from an essay question into a multiple choice question. The seven Power types are exhaustive across approximately four hundred strategy cases. If your business does not hold at least one of them against every current and potential competitor, your strategy problem is not yet solved. For each competitor on an exhaustively enumerated list, work through all seven Powers. Does the business enjoy meaningfully lower per-unit costs from higher volume, with a scale differential large enough to deter share-gain attempts? That is Scale Economies. Does its installed base make the product materially more valuable to each user, such that the value deficit of a smaller network is economically prohibitive to overcome? That is Network Economies. Would a specific incumbent damage their existing business by copying the challenger's model? That is Counter-Positioning. Do customers face real financial, procedural, or relational costs to leave? Those are Switching Costs. Can the business charge a premium for an objectively identical offering based on long-established customer perception? That is Branding. Does it have preferential access to a coveted, value-enhancing asset at terms that do not fully reflect its value? That is a Cornered Resource. Has it developed, over decades, complex and opaque process improvements competitors cannot replicate even with full transparency? That is Process Power.

Always look to the Barrier first. This is the single most important diagnostic habit a strategist can develop. The natural instinct — for founders, executives, and investors alike — is to catalogue advantages: our product is better, our team is stronger, our technology is more advanced. All of these may be true. None of them constitute strategy unless accompanied by a specific, nameable Barrier. Benefits are common. Barriers are not. The Barrier must map to one of four generic types: prohibitive cost of gaining share, hysteresis, collateral damage, or fiat. If the honest answer to "Why have decades of capable, committed competitors failed to replicate this?" is "They just haven't tried hard enough" or "We execute better" or "We move faster," there is no Power. If it can be developed in a year, almost by definition, it is not Power, because it is imitable within a reasonable timeframe. Every claimed Power must also pass the 3 S's: Superior (does it materially improve free cash flow through higher pricing, lower costs, or reduced investment needs?), Significant (is the improvement large enough to move the profit needle?), and Sustainable (is it largely immune to competitive arbitrage over a period of at least five years?). All three must pass. Failure on any one disqualifies the claim.

## How to Apply

Begin by enumerating every competitor — direct, functional, and potential. Walmart competes with Amazon despite radically different models. Any capable company with incentive to enter your space must appear on the list. Most diagnostics fail before they begin because this list is drawn too narrowly.

Work through each Power type against each competitor in the sequence of specific, empirical questions the Power Audit requires. For Scale Economies, identify the scale-sensitive cost components and quantify the per-unit cost gap. For Network Economies, map the network effect with a flowchart — non-linearity is the most common failure mode, because many network benefits exhibit diminishing returns at scale and flatten at moderate penetration. Uber's ride-hailing density follows a square-root function that flattens at moderate scale, meaning two competitors can coexist without meaningful advantage to either, which is why Uber's network effect does not constitute Power in most cities. Check whether both sides of any two-sided platform are genuinely locked in or whether users multi-home freely. For Counter-Positioning, identify the specific incumbent whose existing business creates the collateral damage, and verify that a rational incumbent's NPV of adoption is negative. For Switching Costs, enumerate the financial, procedural, and relational costs of leaving and test whether they would survive a competitor's acquisition subsidy. For Branding, confirm that price premium for an objectively identical offering is sustained by long-established customer perception, not by product differentiation. For Cornered Resource, apply the five tests: idiosyncrasy, non-arbitraged pricing, transferability, ongoing relevance, sufficiency. For Process Power, require evidence of decades of bottom-up process evolution that competitors cannot replicate even with full transparency.

Apply the 3 S's to any claim that survives the initial filter. Then calculate the Surplus Leader Margin where it can be calculated. The SLM answers the question: "What profit margin could I achieve if I priced such that my competitor's profits were exactly zero?" For Scale Economies, the SLM equals the product of Scale Economy Intensity (the ratio of fixed costs to leader sales) and Scale Advantage (the ratio of leader sales to follower sales, minus one). Both terms must be significantly positive. For Network Economies, the SLM equals one minus one divided by one plus the product of delta (the marginal benefit per new network member divided by variable cost per unit) and the installed base differential. For Counter-Positioning, higher incumbent margins yield a higher SLM — the incumbent's strength is the challenger's advantage. A high SLM provides room for both profits and competitive position enforcement simultaneously.

Compress the result into a one-sentence strategy story that names the specific Power types and their sequence. Intel: a single design win, then a decade and a half of very high Switching Costs, then Scale Economies. Netflix streaming: Counter-Positioning over HBO and cable, then changing industry economics through originals to create Scale Economies. If you cannot state the one-sentence Power story of a business, you probably do not understand its strategy. If you cannot state it for a competitor, you have not done the work necessary to compete intelligently with that competitor. Repeat the diagnostic periodically — competitive dynamics, technology shifts, and industry economics can erode or create Power over time. The Mantra is a route to continuing Power in significant markets. Continuing means ongoing reassessment.

## Examples

**Situation:** A marketplace founder raised $50 million. Growth is explosive, the press hails the company as the next transformative platform, and the pitch deck claims "strong network effects" and "flywheel dynamics."
**Application:** Subject the claim to the Power Audit. Enumerate every competitor, including well-funded potential entrants. Map the network effect with a flowchart and test for non-linearity. Ask: would a well-capitalized competitor entering with an identical model be able to reach the critical-mass threshold at comparable cost? Does multi-homing erode the network lock-in? Apply the 3 S's.
**Result:** In nearly every such case, the "flywheel" is not strategic. There might be a modest network effect, but it does not reach materiality. Three years later, a well-capitalized competitor enters with an identical model, acquires users at similar cost, and splits the market. Margins compress. Attractive financials were the flux of a rapidly growing market, not Power. The diagnostic would have identified this before $50 million was committed.

**Situation:** Netflix streaming in the mid-2010s.
**Application:** Decompose returns into Industry Economics and Competitive Position. Netflix changed the industry economics by moving content from variable cost to fixed cost through originals and exclusives, which made scale decisive. They built competitive position by accumulating subscribers faster than any rival. Apply the Power Audit: the Barrier is the cost-per-subscriber gap that a smaller competitor cannot close, because fixed content costs amortize over a smaller base. Apply the 3 S's: Superior (yes, cost advantage), Significant (yes, at Netflix's scale), Sustainable (yes, provided the content investment is maintained).
**Result:** One-sentence strategy: Counter-Positioning over HBO and cable, then changing industry economics through originals to create Scale Economies. Both Industry Economics and Competitive Position are positive. The diagnostic confirms genuine Power.

**Situation:** A company with a claimed data network effect in an AI application.
**Application:** Map the data-benefit curve. Is the benefit linear, or does it flatten? As data scales, the additional incremental benefit becomes less and less, and eventually the curve gets rather flat. Identify the flat zone threshold and test whether competitors can reach it.
**Result:** In the vast majority of cases, data network effects fail the non-linearity test. The Netflix recommendation engine is technically impressive, clearly beneficial to users, but probably not strategic because competitors can achieve similar recommendation quality with somewhat less data. The data effect is a Benefit; it lacks a Barrier.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept "We have a flywheel" as a Power claim.
**Why:** In my experience, a claimed flywheel is almost never strategic. There might be a modest network effect involved. It does not reach materiality in the vast majority of cases. The word "flywheel" describes a feeling of momentum, not a Barrier.

**Don't:** Treat operational excellence as strategy.
**Why:** Running a tight ship is incredibly important — but it is not Power. The things that drive operational excellence can be mimicked. You are on a treadmill, and if you stop running you get creamed. The treadmill itself is not a competitive advantage.

**Don't:** Accept "we have the best team" or "we move fastest" as Power.
**Why:** These describe Benefits — perhaps real ones — without a Barrier. The question is not whether you move fast but what prevents a well-funded, competent competitor from moving equally fast. If nothing prevents it, there is no Power.

**Don't:** Accept "we have an algorithm" or "we have a defined 17-step innovation process" as Power.
**Why:** An algorithm has never, in my experience, been defensible — you can do math a lot of different ways. A codified process for invention is an oxymoron; innovation is invention, not process. Both claims are red flags.

**Don't:** Run the diagnostic once and move on.
**Why:** The Mantra is a route to continuing Power in significant markets. Competitive dynamics, technology shifts, and changing industry economics can erode or create Power over time. The Power assessment that was valid last year may not be valid next year. Reassess periodically.

**Don't:** Expect founders to diagnose their own Power correctly.
**Why:** Founders are wrong about their power claims roughly nine times out of ten. This is not a criticism — it reflects the genuine difficulty of the exercise. External verification is not optional.
