# The 3 S's Test

## When to Use
- A founder, executive, or analyst has asserted that a particular source of advantage is a moat, and you need a fast, rigorous screen before committing to a fuller diagnosis.
- You are evaluating a claim of Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, or Process Power against a specific competitor and want to verify it qualifies as Power at all.
- You are reviewing investor decks, board materials, or strategy documents and need to cut through the catalogue of benefits to reach the question of durability.

## Fails When
- The claim is too vague to test. "We have a strong brand" is not testable until it has been decomposed into a specific price premium or cost advantage against a specific competitor. First specify the mechanism; then apply the 3 S's.
- You treat Superior or Significant as sufficient on their own. Many businesses pass the first two and fail the third. A superior, material advantage that competitors can replicate within a short horizon is not Power — it is a temporary differential that competitive arbitrage will erode.
- The horizon for Sustainable is set too short. Power is about persistent differential returns. A five-year horizon is a reasonable lower bound. Advantages that decay meaningfully inside that window do not pass.

## Core Concept

Power is a configuration that creates the potential for persistent significant differential returns, even in the face of fully committed and competent competition. The 3 S's test is the practical screen that tells you whether any specific claim of advantage satisfies that definition. Every claimed business advantage must simultaneously pass three conditions:

**Superior.** Does it improve free cash flow? What specifically gives you higher prices or lower costs versus every relevant competitor? "Better" in the abstract does not qualify. The test requires a concrete, measurable delta — a price premium that competitors cannot match, a cost position competitors cannot reach, or a reduction in capital intensity competitors cannot replicate. If the claim cannot be reduced to a specific economic differential, it has not yet cleared this first hurdle.

**Significant.** Is the improvement material? Is it large enough to meaningfully move the profit needle? The Significant test is especially useful for exposing false positives. Businesses routinely tout network effects or brand recognition that, when measured carefully, are not material enough to drive meaningful differential returns. A mild network effect that produces a one-percent price premium is technically Superior but fails Significance. A modest switching cost that is dwarfed by the cost of the underlying product fails in the same way. The question is not whether the advantage exists. The question is whether it is big enough to shift free cash flow in a way that shows up in long-term differential margin (m) in the Fundamental Equation.

**Sustainable.** Is the improvement largely immune to competitive arbitrage? What specifically prevents a motivated, capable competitor from replicating it within a horizon of five or more years? This is the hardest test of all, and the one most claims fail. Superior and Significant together constitute the Benefit. Sustainable constitutes the Barrier. Without the Barrier, the Benefit is eroded by arbitrage and differential returns converge to zero. There are four generic Barriers that produce Sustainability: prohibitive cost of gaining share, hysteresis (a structural time constant that prevents replication regardless of investment), collateral damage (where mimicking the Power holder would damage the competitor's existing business), and fiat (patents, property rights, or the personal choice of key people). If you cannot map the claim to one of these Barrier types, it is not Sustainable, regardless of how impressive the underlying Benefit sounds.

All three must pass. One failure disqualifies. A Superior and Significant advantage that fails Sustainability is the most common false positive in strategic analysis — it looks like Power and yields returns for a period, but arbitrage will grind it down. A Superior and Sustainable advantage that fails Significance produces real but small differential returns — directionally correct but strategically uninteresting. A Significant and Sustainable advantage that fails Superiority is usually a category error: the claim was not actually about economic differential in the first place. The test is designed to catch all three failure modes before they become strategic commitments.

## How to Apply

1. **Specify the claim as a mechanism against a specific competitor.** "We have Scale Economies" is not a testable claim until you say "We have a cost advantage of X percent versus Competitor Y, derived from fixed-cost spreading over a volume base Y cannot match." Power is relative, not absolute. Each competitor relationship must be evaluated independently.

2. **Test Superior.** Quantify the free-cash-flow delta. Higher price, lower cost, or reduced investment requirement? How much? Versus which competitor? If you cannot produce a specific economic differential, stop — the claim has not yet been made rigorously enough to test.

3. **Test Significant.** Is the delta material in the context of the business? A one-percent margin advantage rarely is. A ten-point cost advantage often is. The standard is whether the advantage is large enough to meaningfully drive long-term differential margin. If the claim survives Superior but shrinks under Significant, the advantage is real but strategically unimportant.

4. **Test Sustainable.** Name the specific Barrier. Hysteresis? Prohibitive cost of gaining share? Collateral damage? Fiat? If you cannot name it — and describe concretely why a fully committed, competent competitor cannot replicate the advantage within five or more years — the claim fails.

5. **Repeat against every relevant competitor.** A business may hold Scale Economies over a small rival, Counter-Positioning over a large incumbent, and no Power whatsoever against a peer of equal size and similar model. The 3 S's test is a competitor-by-competitor discipline, not a portfolio-level adjective.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS founder claims Switching Costs as the company's moat. Customers rarely churn, and the product has extensive integrations.
**Application:** Run the 3 S's. Superior: does the lock-in produce a price premium or cost advantage? The claim is that customers accept annual price increases because the cost of ripping out the integrations exceeds the savings from switching. Quantify. If the true cost of switching — procedural retraining, integration rebuild, data migration — is meaningfully larger than one year of the price premium, Superior passes. Significant: is the premium material to long-term m? If the founder can only extract a modest annual increase before customers begin serious evaluation of alternatives, Significance is doubtful. Sustainable: what is the Barrier? Switching Costs are their own Barrier only insofar as they are embedded in procedural, financial, or relational dimensions that competitors cannot eliminate by offering migration services or financial incentives. If a competent competitor can neutralize the integration cost with a few engineering weeks and a discount, Sustainability fails.
**Result:** Many SaaS claims of Switching Costs pass Superior, wobble on Significant, and fail Sustainable. The diagnosis is often "real friction, not Power." The implication is that long-term m will be closer to commodity SaaS margins than the founder's plan assumes.

**Situation:** An investor argues that a consumer marketplace has Network Economies because more supply attracts more demand and vice versa.
**Application:** Superior: is the experience materially better at higher scale? If so, quantify. Significant: does the scale advantage translate into pricing power or lower customer acquisition cost versus the next-largest competitor? If the second-largest competitor has reached a liquidity threshold beyond which marginal network scale no longer matters, Significance may fail even though Superiority nominally holds. Sustainable: what prevents a smaller competitor from reaching that same threshold, particularly if the category is still in takeoff? The Barrier in Network Economies is typically prohibitive cost of gaining share — the cost for a late entrant to subsidize both sides of the network to liquidity is usually uneconomic. Test that specifically.
**Result:** If the network is large enough to produce durable liquidity advantages and the category is past takeoff, all three pass and Network Economies qualifies as Power. If the category is still open or the leading network has not reached a scale that would cost a competitor more to replicate than the total profit pool justifies, the advantage is real but not yet Power.

**Situation:** An executive asserts Branding as a source of Power because customer surveys show high brand awareness.
**Application:** Superior requires a price premium over functionally equivalent alternatives. Awareness is not a price premium. Does the brand produce willingness to pay more for an identical product, in a way competitors cannot match? If so, quantify. Significant asks whether that premium is material to long-term m. Sustainable asks whether the brand has been built over a long enough period, with enough consistency, that a well-funded competitor cannot replicate it within five or more years — the hysteresis test specific to Branding.
**Result:** Most claims of Branding as Power fail on Superior (awareness conflated with willingness-to-pay) or on Sustainable (insufficient hysteresis, often because the category is still young). Where they pass — Hermès, Tiffany, a small number of luxury and affective-category brands — the 3 S's clear cleanly and the Power is real. The test's virtue is that it disqualifies the first, frequent case rather than accepting it on the basis of survey data.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Stop at Superior.
**Why:** A Superior advantage that fails Significance is strategically immaterial. A Superior and Significant advantage that fails Sustainability is the most common false positive in strategy, producing the appearance of Power for a period before arbitrage erodes it. All three tests must pass.

**Don't:** Apply the test portfolio-wide rather than competitor-by-competitor.
**Why:** Power is relative. The same advantage may pass the 3 S's against one competitor and fail against another. A portfolio-wide verdict obscures exactly the relationship-specific diagnosis the test is designed to produce. Any single arbitrageur is enough to drive down differential margins.

**Don't:** Confuse customer satisfaction, brand awareness, or product superiority with Superior in the 3 S's sense.
**Why:** Superior in this test means a specific, quantifiable economic differential — price, cost, or capital intensity — versus a specific competitor. Satisfaction and awareness are inputs to that differential, not the differential itself. A loved product that cannot command a premium or produce a cost advantage does not clear the first hurdle.

**Don't:** Accept "we've been at this for years" as evidence of Sustainable.
**Why:** Duration is not a Barrier. Many long-established advantages are fully arbitrageable the moment a competitor decides to mobilize against them. Sustainable requires a named Barrier — hysteresis, prohibitive cost of gaining share, collateral damage, or fiat — that explains specifically why arbitrage will fail, not merely why it has not yet occurred.
