# Switching Costs

## When to Use
- The business has a repeated economic interaction with its customers — follow-on products, modules, subscriptions, maintenance, adjacent services.
- Customers face material costs to change vendors, across some combination of financial, procedural, and relational dimensions.
- You are in a takeoff-stage market where switching costs can be designed into the product and relationship from the start, before customers become sophisticated about lock-in.
- You are assessing whether an incumbent's retention reflects genuine Power or merely the absence of a credible competitive push.

## Fails When
- There is no repeated economic interaction. Expensive industrial equipment bought once with nothing to sell the customer afterwards produces switching costs that are real but economically inert.
- High retention is driven by customer satisfaction rather than by the cost of departure. If customers would leave at the first meaningful dissatisfaction, the retention is operational excellence, not a Barrier.
- The installed base was acquired at fully arbitraged prices in a mature market; the switching-cost premium has already been competed away on the front end.
- A tectonic shift — cloud replacing on-premise, open standards replacing proprietary formats — structurally lowers the cost of switching itself.
- The lock-in relies on contractual coercion, termination fees, or proprietary data formats rather than on embedded value. Resentment grows, regulators notice, and competitors who are willing to subsidize the exit dismantle the position.

## Core Concept

Switching Costs, as a Power type, is defined as follows: a company possesses Switching Cost Power when the value it delivers to customers embeds itself so deeply that departure entails costs customers are unwilling to bear, enabling the company to charge higher prices for follow-on products and services to its existing installed base. Let me parse this carefully, because each element carries weight.

The **Benefit** is higher prices charged to existing customers for follow-on products. This is not a cost advantage. It is a pricing advantage, and — a critical qualifier — it applies only to customers you have already acquired. A company with Switching Cost Power enjoys no advantage whatsoever in competing for new customers. The Benefit is entirely retrospective; it accrues to the installed base, not to prospects.

The **Barrier** is the prohibitive cost of share gains. A competitor attempting to wrest away an incumbent's customers must compensate those customers for the full cost of switching, through dramatically lower prices, migration subsidies, extended free trials, or contractual guarantees. The economics of this compensation are frequently so unattractive that rational competitors conclude the attempt is value-destroying. After several bouts of failed competitive assault, followers learn to focus elsewhere. This is a Barrier rooted in economic rationality: competitors can see the switching costs, calculate the required investment to overcome them, and choose not to try.

Switching costs come in three distinct varieties, and the taxonomy is worth naming precisely because the most powerful positions combine all three. **Financial switching costs** are the most transparent: directly monetary expenditures to change vendors — replacement software, complementary licenses, data migration, consultants, productivity losses during changeover. They are the easiest for a competitor to target with a compensating offer. **Procedural switching costs** are subtler and often more durable. They arise from accumulated familiarity: employees across divisions have learned a system's idiosyncrasies, and entire workflows have been designed around its capabilities and limitations. Equipment gets embedded in how people do things; they not only get used to it, but there is a whole process designed around it. The deeper cost is uncertainty — will the new system actually work in our specific context? **Relational switching costs** are the most difficult to quantify and the most underestimated. These involve the breaking of emotional bonds — with account managers, support teams, user communities, even the identity of being a user. Enterprise customers develop multi-year relationships with implementation teams. Consumer technology users form attachments to ecosystems that express something about who they are.

A structural feature worth emphasizing: Switching Costs is **non-exclusive**. Multiple competitors can simultaneously enjoy Switching Cost Power over their respective installed bases. SAP, Oracle, and IBM all benefit from high ERP switching costs. Each firm charges premium prices to its own customers while finding it prohibitively expensive to steal the others' customers. The industry reaches an equilibrium in which all major players enjoy elevated margins over their existing bases, and the fiercest competition occurs only over genuinely new customers.

Two implications follow, and both are strategically decisive. First, **the potential is realized only through a repeated economic interaction**. You can only monetize switching costs if the customer buys more stuff — there must be some kind of razor-blade aspect to it. A company selling an expensive industrial machine with enormous switching costs but no follow-on products has lock-in that is economically inert. The customer is locked in, but there is nothing to lock them in to. Switching Cost Power demands a product-line extension strategy. Each new module, integration, or service layer provides another revenue stream that the customer cannot easily redirect to a competitor. Second, **switching costs compound with scale in a very particular way**: existing customers become more valuable than new customers. The Benefit applies only to the installed base, so the lifetime value of an acquired customer grows as the product portfolio deepens, while the cost of acquiring a new customer in a mature market tends to rise to reflect that lifetime value. Once that acquisition cost fully arbitrages out the lifetime value, the economic surplus has been competed away on the front end. This is why timing is not optional.

Switching Cost Power is a **takeoff-stage** Power. The window for establishing it opens during rapid market growth and closes as the market matures. During takeoff, customers are flooding into the market and their primary concern is acquisition — can they get the product? — not optimization. They are not carefully comparing total cost of ownership or demanding contractual protections against lock-in. In this environment, the customer acquisition cost does not fully arbitrage out the profit stream that customer will eventually generate. As the market matures, customers become smart. The value of an acquired customer becomes transparent to all players; customer acquisition costs rise to reflect true lifetime value; and customers begin demanding interoperability, open standards, and migration assistance. It is very, very rare, once a business is up and running in a mature market, to introduce switching costs. The switching costs must be designed into the product and the customer relationship from the beginning.

## How to Apply

Work from the Barrier back to the Benefit. The diagnostic question is whether a competitor who fully understood your installed base could calculate the compensation required to extract those customers — and would rationally decline to try. If the answer is yes, you have a Barrier. If the answer is maybe, you have customer satisfaction. If the answer is no, you have nothing.

Then run the test:

1. **Materiality.** Are the switching costs large enough relative to the price of the product that a competitor cannot simply compensate the customer for them? Many products create modest inconvenience when abandoned. These are real costs but rarely sufficient to prevent switching when a competitor offers meaningfully better value. This is the Significant leg of the 3 S's — Superior, Significant, Sustainable.
2. **Repeated economic interaction.** Is there a revenue model that involves follow-on purchases, subscriptions, maintenance fees, or adjacent products? If the customer buys once and never returns, lock-in is strategically irrelevant regardless of its magnitude.
3. **Takeoff-stage establishment.** Were the switching costs established during market takeoff, or did the business pay full freight in a mature market where acquisition costs already reflect lifetime value?
4. **Resilience to technology shifts.** Is the switching-cost structure robust to the kind of tectonic change that can sweep it away? Cloud computing has fundamentally threatened on-premise ERP switching costs by enabling lighter-weight integrations and reducing migration complexity. A position that was impregnable for decades can be undone by a change in the underlying cost structure of switching itself.
5. **Value-first framing.** Are the switching costs a natural consequence of deep integration — the customer staying because leaving means abandoning real capabilities — or are they artificial barriers designed to trap customers against their will? The wrong framing is "I'm going to lock in my customers." The right framing is "What can I do for my customers that creates value that then has a lock-in characteristic?" The value-first approach produces genuine Power; the adversarial approach produces resentment, regulatory scrutiny, and a Barrier that erodes the moment a credible alternative offers to subsidize the exit.

For builders: the strategic imperative is bifurcated. During takeoff, the priority is acquiring customers at attractive economics and embedding the product deeply enough to create material departure costs. During stability, the priority is extending the product portfolio to monetize the installed base. Adjacent products, modules, and integrations are not merely growth tactics; they are the mechanism by which latent switching costs become realized revenue.

## Examples

**Situation:** In 2008, Hewlett-Packard attempts to migrate its ERP system away from SAP. The transition produces a catastrophic $160 million financial hit when roughly 20 percent of HP's orders freeze during the switchover. Survey data from the period reveals that 89 percent of dissatisfied SAP customers still expect to continue paying maintenance fees.
**Application:** Parse what is actually happening. SAP's software has been customized — sometimes over decades — to reflect each customer's specific business processes, organizational structures, and reporting requirements. These customizations represent not just investment but institutional knowledge: the system has become the repository for how the company actually operates, as distinct from how it says it operates. The financial switching costs are large, but the procedural and relational costs are arguably more decisive. SAP has then systematically monetized this position through an enormous acquisition program and ever-expanding product portfolio — supply chain, HCM, CRM, business intelligence, cloud. Each extension creates new revenue streams accessible only through the existing installed base, and each new integration deepens the switching costs further.
**Result:** A self-reinforcing position: deeper integration creates higher switching costs, which create more opportunity for follow-on sales, which justifies further deepening of integration. Customers stay — even when dissatisfied — because the value embedded in their operations is genuinely irreplaceable in any reasonable timeframe. The Barrier is the prohibitive cost of share gains, made concrete in HP's $160 million migration loss. Competitors can see this, calculate the compensation required, and rationally decline to pursue SAP's installed base.

**Situation:** Intel wins the IBM PC microprocessor design contract, and software written for Intel's x86 architecture cannot run on competing chips without costly rewriting. For roughly a decade and a half, Intel sells successive generations of processors to the installed base.
**Application:** The initial switching costs arise from chip-specific programming — a procedural and financial lock-in specific to the x86 architecture. Crucially, this lock-in generates value because Intel has follow-on products to sell. Each new generation of processor is a monetization event for the installed base. The one-sentence story of Intel is instructive: a single design win, then a decade and a half of very high switching costs, then Scale Economies. The Power types layered on each other in sequence, each establishing protection appropriate to its phase of the business.
**Result:** Extraordinary differential returns over an extended period, produced not merely by the switching costs themselves but by the repeated economic interaction through which those switching costs were monetized. When Intel's Switching Cost Power began to weaken as software abstractions reduced architecture-specific lock-in, Scale Economies had already been layered in to sustain the position.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse customer satisfaction with switching costs.
**Why:** A company may have high retention rates simply because customers are happy. This is operational excellence, not Power. The test is whether customers would stay if they were mildly dissatisfied. If the answer is no, the retention is a Benefit without a Barrier and will be arbitraged away by the next competitor offering a better deal.

**Don't:** Claim switching costs for products that have no repeated economic interaction.
**Why:** Switching costs without follow-on purchases are economically inert. The customer is locked in, but there is nothing to lock them in to. Power requires that the installed base generates ongoing revenue the customer cannot easily redirect.

**Don't:** Rely on contractual lock-in, early-termination fees, or proprietary data formats as the core mechanism.
**Why:** Artificial lock-in is fragile. It generates resentment, invites regulatory scrutiny, and provides no Barrier against a competitor willing to subsidize the exit. Genuine Switching Cost Power arises from embedded value — customers stay because leaving means abandoning real capabilities — not from contractual coercion.

**Don't:** Ignore the acquisition-cost arbitrage in mature markets.
**Why:** A business may have enormous switching costs over its installed base and still earn no economic profit if the cost of acquiring those customers was equally enormous. During late-stage competition for new customers, the switching-cost premium is fully priced into the acquisition cost. The result is a business with locked-in customers and zero economic surplus.

**Don't:** Frame the work as "trapping customers."
**Why:** The adversarial framing produces the wrong product decisions — mandatory accounts, proprietary data formats, contractual exit fees — that create resentment without creating value. The value-first framing asks: what can we do for our customers that creates value that then has a lock-in characteristic? The distinction is not merely rhetorical; it determines whether you end up with a durable Power or a liability waiting to be regulated.

**Don't:** Assume switching costs established on one technology platform carry forward through a platform shift.
**Why:** A Switching Cost position that was impregnable for decades can be swept away by a tectonic shift that changes the underlying cost structure of switching itself. On-premise ERP lock-in is not the same position after cloud-native alternatives lower migration complexity. The position must be reassessed whenever the platform shifts.
