# The Moby Dick Theory of Big Companies

## When to Use
- A startup is about to enter (or is already inside) a partnership, distribution, investment, or acquisition conversation with a major incumbent.
- A founder keeps being told by a big company that the deal is "two weeks away" — and has been for six months.
- A business plan has, written into it, a critical dependency on a specific big-company deal to work.
- A founder is wondering whether to keep chasing a big-company conversation or let it go.

## Fails When
- The startup is in a market that genuinely has a shape where one specific deal is a necessary condition — e.g., a mobile startup that must have a carrier deal. The framework says: don't do that business. If you're already in it, the framework is diagnostic more than prescriptive.
- The user wants a recipe for "winning" at a big company. The whole point is that you can't reliably win at that game and shouldn't stake your company on it.

## Core Concept

There are times in the life of a startup when you have to deal with big companies. Maybe you're looking for a partnership or distribution deal. Perhaps you want an investment. Sometimes you want a marketing or sales alliance. From time to time you need a big company's permission to do something. Or maybe a big company has approached you and says it wants to buy your startup.

The most important thing to know going into any discussion or interaction with a big company is that **you're Captain Ahab, and the big company is Moby Dick.**

When Captain Ahab went in search of the great white whale, he had absolutely no idea whether he would find Moby Dick, whether Moby Dick would allow himself to be found, whether Moby Dick would try to immediately capsize the ship or instead play cat and mouse, or whether Moby Dick was off mating with his giant whale girlfriend. What happened was entirely up to Moby Dick. Ahab would never be able to explain to himself or anyone else why Moby Dick would do whatever it was he'd do.

That's because **the behavior of any big company is largely inexplicable when viewed from the outside.** I always laugh when someone says "Microsoft is going to do X" or "Google is going to do Y" or "Yahoo is going to do Z." Odds are, nobody *inside* those companies knows what they're going to do in any given circumstance. The CEO might, if the issue is really big. But you're probably not dealing at the CEO level, so that doesn't matter.

The inside of any big company is a very, very complex system consisting of many thousands of people, of whom hundreds and probably thousands are executives who think they have some level of decision-making authority. On any given issue, eight people, maybe ten, fifteen, twenty, sometimes many more are going to get some kind of vote. When I was at IBM in the early '90s, they had a formal decision-making process called "concurrence" — on any given issue, a written list of fifty-or-so executives from all over the company would be assembled, and any one of them could "nonconcur" and veto. That's extreme, but every big company has something like it. They have to.

Now layer on all the other impinging forces: the consensus building, trade-offs, quids pro quo, politics, rivalries, arguments, mentorships, revenge for past wrongs, turf-building, engineering groups, product managers, product marketers, sales, corporate marketing, finance, HR, legal, channels, business development, the strategy team, the international divisions, investors, Wall Street analysts, industry analysts, good press, bad press, articles being written you don't know about, customers, prospects, lost sales, prospects on the fence, partners, this quarter's sales numbers, this quarter's margins, the bond rating, last week's planning meeting, this week's cancelled planning meeting, bonus programs, who joined, who left, who got fired, who got promoted, who got sidelined, who's sleeping with whom, which dinner party the CEO went to last night, the guy who prepares the staff-meeting PowerPoint accidentally putting your startup's name in too small a font to be read from the back of the conference room. You can't possibly even identify all the factors, much less try to understand them, much less try to influence them.

Moby Dick might stalk you for three months, jump out of the water and raise a huge ruckus, vanish for six months, come back and beach your whole boat, or alternately give you the clear shot you need to harpoon his giant butt. A big company might study you for three months, approach you and tell you they want to invest or partner or buy you, vanish for six months, come out with a directly competitive product that kills you, or alternately acquire you and make your whole team rich. **And you're never going to know why.**

**The upside of dealing with a big company** is that there's potentially a ton of whale meat in it for you. The right deal with the right big company can have a huge impact on a startup's success. **The downside** is that he can capsize you — maybe by stepping on you in one way or another, but more likely by wrapping you up in a bad partnership that holds you back, or just by making you waste a huge amount of time in meetings and get distracted from your core mission.

Related to all this: a startup's relationship to *big markets* themselves. Startups should aim at markets with massive potential — market pulls product out of you (see the PMF doctrine) — which often means markets with massive incumbents. The Moby Dick theory is how you handle the specific counterparties inside those markets when you have to interact with them. The two doctrines work together: go after big markets, but do not build a plan that requires a specific whale to play ball. Hunt where the whales live without staking your life on a single whale.

## How to Apply

These are the rules I'd write on the inside of the ship:

1. **Don't do startups that require deals with big companies to make them successful.** The risk of never getting those deals is way too high, no matter how hard you are willing to work. And even if you get the deals, they probably won't work out the way you hoped. The mobile startup whose whole plan hinges on one carrier, the infrastructure startup whose plan hinges on being embedded in a specific OS, the media startup whose plan requires one specific network as a distribution partner — these are all structurally bad bets. Change the plan.
2. **Never assume a deal with a big company is closed until the ink hits the paper and/or the cash hits the company bank account.** There is always something that can cause a deal that looks closed to suddenly get blown to smithereens — or vanish without a trace. I've seen it in every direction: the internal advocate gets promoted away, a new SVP takes over and re-examines the pipeline, legal surfaces a concern three weeks before signing, a reorg absorbs the acquiring team. Count zero deals before the wire.
3. **Be extremely patient.** Big companies play "hurry up and wait" all the time. I've dealt with one big East Coast technology company that played "hurry up and wait" with me at least four separate times — including a mandatory immediate cross-country flight just to have dinner with the \#2 executive — and never followed through on anything. If you want a deal with a big company, it is probably going to take a lot longer to put together than you think.
4. **Beware bad deals.** A high-profile Internet startup in San Francisco that I'm thinking of did two big deals early with high-profile big-company partners and has become completely hamstrung in its ability to execute on its core business as a result. A deal you regret is worse than a deal you never got. Exclusivity clauses, tight technical integrations, non-competes, anything that constrains your future optionality — assume any of those will bind exactly when you most need to pivot.
5. **Never, ever assume a big company will do the obvious thing.** What is obvious to you — or any outsider — is probably not obvious on the inside, once all the other factors are taken into account. The "obvious" deal that would benefit both sides often loses to the politically obvious deal, or to the "deal we always do," or to the deal that doesn't require any internal advocate to spend political capital.
6. **Be aware that big companies care a lot more about what other big companies are doing than what any startup is doing.** Big companies often care more about other big companies than about their own customers. Moby Dick cared more about other giant white whales than about the annoying little people in the flimsy boat. Your strategic significance to them is rarely what you think it is.
7. **If doing deals with big companies is going to be a key part of your strategy, hire a real pro who has done it before.** Only the best and most experienced whalers had a chance at taking down Moby Dick. This is why senior sales and business development people get paid a lot of money. They're worth it. The 26-year-old first-time founder without the pattern library is at a profound disadvantage in a room full of 20-year enterprise BD veterans who have seen every negotiating gambit.
8. **Don't get obsessed. Don't turn into Captain Ahab.** By all means talk to big companies about all kinds of things, but always be ready to have the conversation just drop and to return to your core business. Rare is the startup where a deal with a big company leads to success, or lack thereof leads to huge failure. (The exception that proves the rule: Microsoft and Digital Research circa 1981. That was a huge whale. There's roughly one of those a decade.)

Corollary rules I've learned from watching founders miss them:

9. **Run the core business as if the deal will never happen.** Every founder who lets the roadmap drift "so we don't conflict with the partner conversation" ends up with both a broken roadmap and a dead deal. Your default plan should assume zero big-company deals materialize. Any that do are a bonus.
10. **Cap the attention a big-company conversation consumes.** I've watched founders spend fifty percent of their calendar on a partnership conversation worth, in expected value, maybe five percent of the company's outcome. Put a number on it. "No more than X hours per week until there's a signed term sheet." Hold the line.
11. **Keep multiple conversations live.** Exclusivity before the deal exists is almost always a bad trade. Multiple parallel conversations keep each counterparty honest, give you optionality, and — critically — give you a cleaner negotiating position.
12. **Watch for the "warm meeting that doesn't close" pattern.** Big-company personnel often host genuinely friendly meetings that produce no deal and no follow-up. That's not bad faith; that's just how big companies consume information. If you've had three friendly meetings and no one is assigned to move the thing, the thing is not moving. Treat it as intelligence, not progress.
13. **If you're being acquired, keep running the company.** Acquisition conversations take months and fail routinely. The ones that don't fail usually take longer than announced. Letting the operations slip during the process will either kill the deal or produce a hollow company on the other side of the close.

## Examples

**Situation:** An infrastructure startup has been in conversations with Google's platform team for four months. Every meeting is enthusiastic. Senior people on the Google side are "aligned." No contract has been drafted. The founder is spending 40% of her time on the relationship. The core business is stalling because engineering keeps doing "just-in-case" integration work.
**Application:** Classic Moby Dick. The enthusiasm means approximately nothing about whether a deal closes. No draft contract at month four is a strong signal that no single Google executive has assigned internal capacity to close it. Stop the integration work. Tell the Google team you'd love to keep talking, but the roadmap is returning to its default. Cap conversation time at two hours a week. Take other meetings. When Google is ready to close, they'll be ready. Don't be the one leaning in.
**Result:** Either Google re-commits (at which point the deal actually moves), or the conversation stays warm and meaningless (in which case you lost nothing by deprioritizing it), or it goes quiet for six months (in which case you just saved the quarter).

**Situation:** A founder has been told privately that a Fortune 500 may want to acquire her company. No term sheet, but "very serious" signals. She stops running a competitive process on a fundraise because the acquisition "might happen soon."
**Application:** Never assume a deal is closed until the money hits the bank. "Very serious" language without a term sheet is worth zero. Keep the fundraise running. Keep shipping product. Assume acquisition probability is meaningfully below 50% regardless of how warm the talk is. Let the acquirer race to put an offer on the table in parallel with the real round closing; that's the only dynamic that produces a serious bid on a serious timeline.
**Result:** Either the acquirer offers real money on a real timeline because they see the round close approaching, or they let the conversation fade (saving you from a deal that was never real). Both are fine. What's not fine is stalling the round for a deal that evaporates.

**Situation:** A mobile gaming startup's entire plan rests on a deal with one specific app-store partner. They have no B-plan if that deal doesn't happen.
**Application:** This is the category Marc's first rule addresses directly: *don't do startups that require deals with big companies to make them successful*. The plan needs to change before the deal ever happens. Either find a model that works via independent distribution, or shelve it. Otherwise you'll spend two years trying to get the deal, you won't get it, or you will and it'll be so extractive you can't build a business out of it.
**Result:** A hard reset of the plan is worth more than any heroic negotiation effort on the original plan.

**Situation:** A big-company partner offers a deal with tight exclusivity on a segment the startup hasn't even entered yet, plus a restriction on working with their named competitor.
**Application:** Bad deal. Exclusivity before you've tested the segment means you're binding future options for a near-term advantage. The named-competitor restriction makes you a pawn in someone else's strategic chessboard. Walk or negotiate the exclusivity way down. If the counterpart won't move on those terms, the deal isn't yours anyway.
**Result:** Either the deal gets restructured into something that preserves your optionality, or you walk and keep your strategic flexibility. Either is better than binding yourself into a hamstrung-for-life partnership.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build your business plan around "when [big company] signs with us." **Why:** You've made your whole startup a dependent variable on an institution you can't predict or steer. Every rule in the Moby Dick theory says this is the worst trade in the book.

**Don't:** Accept exclusivity before the deal is signed. **Why:** Exclusivity is the one thing the big company *always* asks for and the one thing you should almost never grant pre-deal. It locks out the alternatives that are keeping the counterparty honest. Granting it is a form of unilateral disarmament in a negotiation that isn't over yet.

**Don't:** Treat friendly meetings as progress. **Why:** Big-company employees host friendly meetings for all sorts of reasons — information-gathering, pipeline-padding, personal curiosity, training a new hire. None of those correlate to a closed deal. Track real commitments: signed NDAs, drafted term sheets, assigned internal counsel, executive sponsors who have put their name on it. If none of those exist, it's coffee.

**Don't:** Let your core product roadmap bend to accommodate a possible big-company deal. **Why:** You will end up with a frankenstein roadmap that serves neither the deal (because the deal doesn't close) nor your actual customers (because their needs didn't drive the design). Keep the roadmap committed to your customers and layer deal-specific work as a separate, explicitly-scoped optional module.

**Don't:** Send a junior team member to do senior BD with a big company. **Why:** The other side is a 20-year enterprise BD veteran with a pattern library you don't have. Match their seniority or hire someone who has the patterns. The deal lifecycle is driven by the more experienced party, and that party will be the big-company side if you don't staff to it.

**Don't:** Fall in love with the deal. **Why:** Ahab's problem was obsession. Every week you spend convinced this deal is "the deal" is a week you're not iterating the core business. The correct emotional stance toward any big-company conversation is mild, detached interest. If it closes on reasonable terms, great. If not, you didn't bet anything on it.

**Don't:** Assume the champion inside the big company can carry the deal alone. **Why:** Your internal champion is one vote in the concurrence process. She can get the meeting, she can socialize the idea, but she cannot close. The moment she gets reorged, promoted, poached, or loses a political battle, the deal dies. Build multiple relationships inside the counterparty so the deal doesn't depend on any one human.

**Don't:** Let acquisition conversations slow your operations. **Why:** Acquisitions routinely take longer than announced, routinely fall through, and often fail because the acquirer got spooked by performance wobble during diligence. The best way to close the acquisition is to keep running the business as if it won't happen. The best way to be fine if it doesn't happen is the same thing.

**Don't:** Generalize "some big-company deals do work" into "therefore I should invest heavily in this one." **Why:** A handful of startups got made by big-company deals (Microsoft and Digital Research, Apple and AT&T with the iPhone, the occasional transformative acquisition). That's a tiny fraction of all attempted big-company deals. Survivorship bias dominates the public narrative. The base rate is bad. Plan around the base rate, not the highlight reel.

**Don't:** Confuse "big markets" with "single big counterparty." **Why:** Marc's PMF doctrine pushes you toward huge markets with real customer pull. That's right. But "huge market" does not mean "one giant incumbent gatekeeper." Go play in markets measured in hundreds of millions of customers or tens of thousands of enterprises, not markets where one or two whales control the gate. The former is where startups win; the latter is where startups die politely.
