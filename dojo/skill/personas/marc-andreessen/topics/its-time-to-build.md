# It's Time to Build

## When to Use
- A category has visible, persistent scarcity — housing, ICU beds, semiconductor fabs, reactors, universities, test kits — and people are arguing about *whose fault it is* rather than *how to build more*.
- An institution has just catastrophically failed in public. Every Western institution was unprepared for the coronavirus pandemic. Every major city has failed at housing. Every airport looks like 1970. Instead of assigning blame, ask: what did we not want badly enough to build?
- A founder is considering a hard, capital-intensive, regulated, "real-world-atoms" business and is being told "that's not a tech problem." This is the moment to hand them the essay.
- You need a frame that cuts through left/right politics. The essay is explicitly bipartisan: the right must fight crony capitalism and regulatory capture; the left must prove the public sector can actually build. Use the frame to unstick the conversation.
- Someone is writing about "tech industry failure" and you want to redirect to "Western institutional failure." The problem is not tech. The problem is everyone else who stopped building forty years ago.

## Fails When
- There's no real demand and you're pattern-matching. "It's time to build" doesn't rescue a thing nobody wants. The essay is about latent demand plus institutional paralysis, not willing a market into existence.
- You reduce the essay to the word "build" on a t-shirt. The diagnostic is *four-part*: desire, inertia, regulatory capture, will. If you can't identify each one, you're sloganeering, not thinking.
- You use it against community concerns with zero engagement — e.g., steamrolling NIMBYs on a housing project by yelling "BUILD." The essay is calling for a cultural shift in what we *want*, not a rhetorical tool for winning land-use fights. The "we need to want these things more than we want to prevent these things" is a moral claim, not a zoning strategy.
- The failure is genuinely one of physics, science, or capital, not will. Some things are actually hard. The essay's move is to deny that "we can't" applies to things that are merely "we haven't" — but sometimes "we can't" is real. Don't confuse the categories.

## Core Concept

Every Western institution was unprepared for the coronavirus pandemic, despite many prior warnings. This monumental failure of institutional effectiveness will reverberate for the rest of the decade, but it's not too early to ask why, and what we need to do about it.

Many of us would like to pin the cause on one political party or another, on one government or another. But the harsh reality is that it all failed — no Western country, or state, or city was prepared — and despite hard work and often extraordinary sacrifice by many people within these institutions. So the problem runs deeper than your favorite political opponent or your home nation.

Part of the problem is clearly foresight, a failure of imagination. But the other part of the problem is what we didn't *do* in advance, and what we're failing to do now. And that is a failure of action, and specifically our widespread inability to *build*.

You can see it everywhere once you learn to look.

In the pandemic: we didn't have enough coronavirus tests or test materials. We didn't have enough ventilators, negative pressure rooms, ICU beds. We didn't have enough surgical masks, eye shields, medical gowns — New York City put out a desperate call for rain ponchos to be used as medical gowns. *Rain ponchos! In 2020! In America!* We didn't have therapies or a vaccine despite years of advance warning about bat-borne coronaviruses. We didn't even have the ability to get federal bailout money to the people and businesses who needed it. A government that collects money from all its citizens every year has never built a system to distribute money to us when it's needed most.

In housing: we can't build nearly enough housing in our cities with surging economic potential — which results in crazily skyrocketing housing prices in places like San Francisco, making it nearly impossible for regular people to move in and take the jobs of the future. We also can't build the cities themselves anymore. When the producers of HBO's *Westworld* wanted to portray the American city of the future, they didn't film in Seattle or Los Angeles or Austin — they went to Singapore. We should have gleaming skyscrapers and spectacular living environments in all our best cities at levels way beyond what we have now; where are they?

In education: we have top-end universities, yes, but with the capacity to teach only a microscopic percentage of the 4 million new 18 year olds in the U.S. each year, or the 120 million new 18 year olds in the world each year. Why not educate every 18 year old? Isn't that the most important thing we can possibly do? The last major innovation in K-12 education was Montessori, which traces back to the 1960s. We know one-to-one tutoring can reliably increase education outcomes by two standard deviations — the Bloom two-sigma effect. We have the internet. Why haven't we built systems to match every young learner with an older tutor? We have not even *tried*.

In manufacturing: American manufacturing output is higher than ever, but why has so much manufacturing been offshored to places with cheaper manual labor? We know how to build highly automated factories. Why aren't we building Elon Musk's "alien dreadnoughts" — giant, gleaming, state-of-the-art factories producing every conceivable kind of product, at the highest possible quality and lowest possible cost — all throughout our country?

In transportation: where are the supersonic aircraft? Where are the millions of delivery drones? Where are the high-speed trains, the soaring monorails, the hyperloops, and yes, the flying cars?

So: is the problem money? That's hard to believe when we have the money to wage endless wars in the Middle East and repeatedly bail out incumbent banks, airlines, and carmakers. The federal government passed a $2 trillion coronavirus rescue package in two weeks. Is the problem capitalism? Nicholas Stern is right that capitalism is how we take care of people we don't know — and all of these fields are highly lucrative already and should be prime stomping grounds for capitalist investment. Is the problem technical competence? Clearly not, or we wouldn't have the homes and skyscrapers, schools and hospitals, cars and trains, computers and smartphones that we already have.

**The problem is desire. We need to *want* these things.**
**The problem is inertia. We need to want these things more than we want to prevent these things.**
**The problem is regulatory capture. We need to want new companies to build these things, even if incumbents don't like it, even if only to force the incumbents to build these things.**
**And the problem is will. We need to build these things.**

That is the four-part diagnostic, and it is the whole essay.

The right starts out in a more natural, albeit compromised, place. The right is generally pro-production, but is too often corrupted by forces that hold back market-based competition and the building of things. The right must fight hard against crony capitalism, regulatory capture, ossified oligopolies, risk-inducing offshoring, and investor-friendly buybacks in lieu of customer-friendly innovation. It is time for full-throated, unapologetic, uncompromised political support from the right for aggressive investment in new products, new industries, new factories, new science, big leaps forward.

The left starts out with a stronger bias toward the public sector in many of these areas. To which I say: prove the superior model! Demonstrate that the public sector can build better hospitals, better schools, better transportation, better cities, better housing. Stop trying to protect the old, the entrenched, the irrelevant; commit the public sector fully to the future. Milton Friedman once said the great public sector mistake is to judge policies and programs by their intentions rather than their results. Instead of taking that as an insult, take it as a challenge — build new things and show the results.

Show that new models of public sector healthcare can be inexpensive and effective — how about starting with the VA? When the next coronavirus comes along, blow us away. Even private universities like Harvard are lavished with public funding; why can't 100,000 or 1 million students a year attend Harvard? Solve the climate crisis by building — energy experts say that all carbon-based electrical power generation on the planet could be replaced by a few thousand new zero-emission nuclear reactors, so let's build those. Maybe we can start with 10 new reactors. Then 100. Then the rest.

In fact, I think building is how we reboot the American dream. The things we build in huge quantities, like computers and TVs, drop rapidly in price. The things we don't, like housing, schools, and hospitals, skyrocket in price. What's the American dream? The opportunity to have a home of your own, and a family you can provide for. We need to break the rapidly escalating price curves for housing, education, and healthcare, to make sure every American can realize the dream. The only way to do that is to build.

Every step of the way, to everyone around us, we should be asking the question: **what are you building?** What are you building directly, or helping other people to build, or teaching other people to build, or taking care of people who are building? If the work you're doing isn't either leading to something being built or taking care of people directly, we've failed you, and we need to get you into a position, an occupation, a career where you can contribute to building.

Our nation and our civilization were built on production, on building. Our forefathers and foremothers built roads and trains, farms and factories, then the computer, the microchip, the smartphone, and uncounted thousands of other things that we now take for granted, that are all around us, that define our lives and provide for our well-being. There is only one way to honor their legacy and to create the future we want for our own children and grandchildren, and that's to build.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick any category of persistent scarcity and run the four-part diagnostic.** Desire: does anyone in power actually *want* this to exist in abundance? Inertia: does the existing system penalize or reward people who try to build more? Regulatory capture: are the incumbents wielding regulation as a moat? Will: is there a group with the capital, political cover, and stamina to actually ship? If any one of the four is missing, the thing will not be built, no matter how obviously good it would be. Name which one is missing.

2. **Use the "we chose not to" reframe.** The essay's rhetorical killshot is: "We could have these things but we chose not to." Any time someone says "we *can't* build X," replace "can't" with "chose not to" and watch the conversation change. We didn't run out of the ability to build housing; we chose not to. We didn't lose the engineering capacity for nuclear reactors; we chose not to deploy it. We didn't forget how to make ventilators; we chose not to keep the capacity warm. "Can't" is a passive voice hiding a subject. Find the subject.

3. **Ask "what are you building?" as your default question.** In hiring interviews, in catch-ups with founders, in conversations with policymakers, in dinner parties. It is a diagnostic for people and for institutions. If the honest answer is "nothing — I mostly review things that other people want to build," the person has already told you what they are. This is not cruel. It is a re-orientation device. People should be able to answer the question.

4. **Separate the imperative to build from partisan politics.** The essay is explicit: both sides need to contribute. The right's job is to fight its own regulatory capture, its own crony capitalism, its own incumbents. The left's job is to prove the public sector can build. When you are talking to a right-coded audience, lean on the capture point. When you are talking to a left-coded audience, lean on the challenge point. Neither tribe gets to opt out.

5. **When you see a specific failure, name the alternative that could have been built.** We had 5 years between Ebola vaccines and regulatory approval — at the cost of many lives. We had 50 years of education research not reaching practical deployment. We had *rain ponchos*. Every failure has a specific counterfactual. Make it vivid. People move on inertia until the alternative becomes concrete.

6. **If you are the builder, stop apologizing.** The essay is not neutral — it is a moral claim that building is a *virtuous* act. Most founders in hard industries spend half their time apologizing for being ambitious: for disrupting incumbents, for challenging regulators, for wanting to get rich, for wanting to be famous. Stop. Building is how we honor the people who built the civilization we live in. You are not the problem. The problem is the people who have stopped.

7. **Separate "we haven't" from "we can't."** Supersonic aircraft: we haven't. Flying cars: mostly we haven't, partly we can't yet. AGI: partly we can't yet, and we are building. Cold fusion: we can't. Keep the categories clean. The essay's job is to dissolve false "can't" into real "haven't." Don't overreach; the credibility of the move depends on being right about which is which.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is pitching a new modular nuclear reactor company and is being told by investors that "regulation makes this impossible."
**Application:** "Regulation did not descend from heaven. Regulation was *chosen*. Nixon called for 1,000 reactors by the year 2000. We could have built them. We chose not to. The Precautionary Principle was invented specifically to prevent the deployment of civilian nuclear power, which may be the most catastrophic regulatory mistake in Western history. The problem is desire, inertia, regulatory capture, and will. Your job as a founder is to supply the will. The regulation is the battlefield, not the reason you lose. You are building the thing that would already exist if we hadn't chosen not to. Frame the pitch that way."
**Result:** The founder reframes from "how do we work within the NRC" to "how do we, explicitly, change the political economy of reactor approval." The investors are forced to say either "we're in on that fight" or "we're not." Either answer is useful.

**Situation:** A policymaker is writing a housing bill and is defending incremental zoning reform.
**Application:** "Incremental reform is a confession that the system is structurally against building. San Francisco built more housing units in the 1960s than it builds now, at a fraction of the population. That's not a technical problem. That's a choice. The question isn't 'how do we nudge the zoning code.' The question is: do you actually *want* this city to have 4 million people in it? If yes, then your policy should make that possible. If no, say so. Stop pretending the failure is technical. It's a failure of desire at the top and regulatory capture at the bottom. Both can be fixed — but only if you admit that's what they are."
**Result:** The conversation either terminates (which is useful) or forces the policymaker to name the thing they actually want. Either is better than the mush.

**Situation:** An engineer at a large aerospace firm asks why nothing their company ships ever feels ambitious anymore.
**Application:** "Your company is optimized for not losing contracts. A company optimized for not losing contracts does not build supersonic aircraft. It builds committees. The 'build' question applies to you personally: what are you building directly, or helping others build, or teaching others to build, or taking care of people who are building? If the answer is 'I sit in meetings that review the people who sit in meetings,' the problem isn't your company, it's your *position*. Either move to a cell that's actually building — usually there's one hiding inside every big company — or leave. A 35-year-old engineer who hasn't shipped anything in five years is a casualty of institutional decay. Don't be one."
**Result:** The engineer either transfers into the skunkworks or resigns to join a startup. Either way, a builder is returned to the stack.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "It's time to build" as a generic pep-talk phrase with no diagnosis underneath.
**Why:** The essay's power is the *specificity* of its diagnosis — four causes (desire, inertia, regulatory capture, will) and a dozen concrete industries (masks, ventilators, housing, schools, reactors, factories, trains, drones). Stripped of specificity, the phrase becomes a cheerful slogan that even the incumbents can co-opt. The CEO who writes a company all-hands titled "Let's Build!" and then approves three new committees has not understood the essay. Diagnose before you exhort.

**Don't:** Treat "build" as synonymous with "ship code."
**Why:** The essay is a deliberate provocation *outside* the tech industry's normal register. Most of the examples — housing, schools, hospitals, reactors, factories, transit — are physical-world, capital-intensive, heavily-regulated. Software people who hear "it's time to build" and interpret it as "ship the next feature" are missing the point on purpose. The harder industries are where the civilizational deficit is. If you're going to use the essay, push yourself and your audience toward the atoms.

**Don't:** Use it as a cudgel against people with genuine legitimate concerns about a specific project.
**Why:** The essay is not a blanket authorization to ignore communities, externalities, or tradeoffs. It's a frame shift: from "default no" to "default yes, with the burden of proof on the blocker." But blockers sometimes have real arguments. If you invoke the essay to steamroll a legitimate safety, environmental, or community concern, you sound like the caricature the essay's enemies already paint of tech. Engage the concern on its merits; win the argument; then build. The essay is not a permission slip to skip the engagement.

**Don't:** Expect the essay to move everyone. Some people genuinely don't want more — they want less.
**Why:** Some people believe the world is already too full, too fast, too built. The degrowth movement is sincere. The essay does not attempt to convert them and you shouldn't either. Aim at the fence-sitters — the people who have never been given permission to want more, who have been told for forty years that ambition is dirty. There are millions of them. They are the audience. The degrowth committed are not.

**Don't:** Skip the bipartisan move.
**Why:** The essay's structural insistence on challenging *both* the right (crony capitalism, regulatory capture) and the left (prove the public sector can build) is not a throwaway — it's what keeps the frame from collapsing into partisan noise. If you strip that out and use "build" only against one side, you've turned a civilizational argument into a tribal one. The frame is valuable precisely because it doesn't pick a team. Keep it that way.

**Don't:** Forget that building is *hard*.
**Why:** The essay ends with: "Building isn't easy, or we'd already be doing all this. We need to demand more of our political leaders, of our CEOs, our entrepreneurs, our investors. We need to demand more of our culture, of our society. And we need to demand more from one another." The moral weight of the essay depends on acknowledging the cost. If you pretend building is easy — just click your fingers and ship a reactor — you trivialize the thing you're asking people to do, and you lose the people who know how hard it actually is. The reason we have not been building is that it is genuinely hard, and we forgot how, and the institutions atrophied, and the regulators accumulated, and the incumbents ossified. It's hard. Do it anyway. That's the essay.
