# Product/Market Fit — The Only Thing That Matters

## When to Use
- A founder is trying to figure out what to focus on, and everything looks important.
- A startup is doing a lot of impressive activity — hiring, PR, process — but usage isn't growing.
- A founder is arguing that their team, or their product, will carry them through a weak market.
- Anyone pre-PMF asks what they should be spending their time on.

## Fails When
- The underlying market truly doesn't exist and no iteration will conjure it. PMF is a law, not a lifeboat.
- "PMF" is used as an excuse for years of indefinite drift with no customer signal. The discipline is to *feel* whether it's happening; if you can't feel it either way after real swings, the answer is usually no.
- Someone mistakes early-adopter enthusiasm for full PMF. Early adopters are a tiny sliver. Confusing them with "the market" is the first thing that kills you after you *think* you've won.

## Core Concept

Look at thirty or forty startups and two facts jump out. First, the divergence in outcomes is insane — some are rocket ships, most are dead. Second, the divergence in quality is just as insane — the team ranges from outstanding to remarkably flawed, the product from masterpiece to barely functional, the market from booming to comatose. So the question is: which of those three — team, product, market — correlates most with success? What's the **\#1 company-killer**?

I'll take the third position. **Market is the most important factor in a startup's success or failure.**

In a great market — a market with lots of real potential customers — **the market *pulls* product out of the startup**. The market needs to be fulfilled, and the market *will* be fulfilled, by the first viable product that comes along. The product doesn't need to be great; it just has to basically work. The team doesn't need to be great; it just has to produce that viable product. Customers knock down your door. The main goal is to answer the phone and respond to all the emails from people who want to buy. And when the market is that strong, the team is remarkably easy to upgrade on the fly.

Conversely, in a terrible market, you can have the best product in the world and an absolutely killer team, and it doesn't matter — **you're going to fail**. You'll break your pick for years trying to find customers who don't exist for your marvelous product, your wonderful team will get demoralized and quit, and your startup will die. This is the story of videoconferencing, and workflow software, and micropayments. Markets that don't exist don't care how smart you are.

This is *Rachleff's Law of Startup Success* — credit Andy Rachleff, formerly of Benchmark, who crystallized it for me:

- When a great team meets a lousy market, market wins.
- When a lousy team meets a great market, market wins.
- When a great team meets a great market, something special happens.

Which gives us the corollary, and the only rule that really matters: **The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit.** Product/market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market.

And here's the crucial diagnostic. **You can always feel when product/market fit isn't happening.** The customers aren't quite getting value out of the product, word of mouth isn't spreading, usage isn't growing that fast, press reviews are kind of "blah", the sales cycle takes too long, and lots of deals never close. **And you can always feel product/market fit when it's happening.** The customers are buying the product just as fast as you can make it — or usage is growing just as fast as you can add more servers. Money from customers is piling up in your company checking account. You're hiring sales and customer support staff as fast as you can. Reporters are calling because they've heard about your hot new thing. Investment bankers are staking out your house. You could eat free for a year at Buck's.

The life of any startup divides into two parts: **before product/market fit** — BPMF — and **after product/market fit** — APMF. These are different companies. They need different strategies, different hires, different cultures, different funding plans.

## How to Apply

### When you are BPMF:

1. **Focus obsessively on getting to product/market fit.** Nothing else. Not your org chart, not your design system, not your PR strategy, not your Series B deck. PMF.
2. **Do whatever is required to get to product/market fit.** Including changing out people, rewriting the product, moving into a different market, telling customers no when you don't want to, telling customers yes when you don't want to, raising that fourth round of highly dilutive venture capital — whatever is required.
3. **Ignore almost everything else.** I'm not saying you *must* ignore everything else. I'm saying that judging from what I've seen in successful startups, you *can*. Successful startups usually screw up plenty of other things along the way — channel model, pipeline, marketing plan, press relations, comp policies, the CEO sleeping with the VC — and still succeed. Because they have PMF.
4. **Don't hire executives too early.** Particularly before you've achieved PMF, it's often better to have a highly motivated manager or director running a function than an expensive executive sitting there in the middle of the room, doing very little. Too much structure pre-PMF is a tell that you're avoiding the actual work.
5. **Read the feel constantly.** Are customers pulling the product, or are you pushing it at them? Is usage growing or is it flat with a lot of visits? Do your users say "I couldn't live without this" or do they say "this is pretty nice"? If you can't tell the difference, you don't have it yet.
6. **Raise enough money to get to PMF with insurance on top.** BPMF is when external shocks kill you. Raise more than you think you need. You have no idea how long this will take.

### When you are APMF:

1. **Switch modes completely.** The main thing becomes *taking the market* — figuring out how to get the product to the entire market, how to get dominant market share. Most tech markets tend to end up with one company taking most of the share, and that company captures most of the value in the sector.
2. **Build a distribution engine.** Contrary to myth and legend, the model for successful tech companies is that they become **distribution-centric rather than product-centric**. They become a distribution channel, and then put many new products through that channel. A better product routinely gets beaten by a better distribution channel. This is the pattern behind IBM, Microsoft, Cisco, and most of the other giants.
3. **Don't confuse early adopters with the whole market.** Early adopters sought you out. The other 95% won't. Consumer or enterprise — doesn't matter — CIOs don't wake up looking for the next hot thing, and neither does the median consumer. If you don't go get that 95%, someone else will, and then they'll acquire you for a rounding error of their equity.
4. **Start on the next product.** Every product in tech becomes obsolete, and fast. If you only ride the first one, you go stale. The good news: distribution + cash gives you both R&D capacity and M&A currency for the second act.
5. **Layer in "everything else."** HR, legal, finance, recruiting. You can put this aside for a while if you've got a killer product and a great sales engine, but the longer you defer it, the more you expose yourself to catastrophic self-inflicted wounds. Once you're between 50 and 150 people — Dunbar territory — you need HR or something breaks.
6. **Raise more money.** Enough to fully exploit the opportunity, then to get to profitability while still fully exploiting it. In an APMF scenario this is usually easier, not harder, but don't get complacent.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has a well-run company — HR in place, 30" monitors for the programmers, top-tier VCs on the board, a beautifully thought-through marketing plan — but customers aren't really paying, usage is plateau'd, and deals don't close.
**Application:** You have a BPMF company that has wrongly concluded it is APMF and is behaving like an operating business. The entire org has to collapse back to "get to PMF or die." Everything that isn't in the direct service of reaching PMF — most of the executive team, most of the process, most of the marketing plan — gets scaled back or delayed. The product team rewrites. The market gets re-examined. The founders do whatever is required.
**Result:** Either you find PMF and the "everything else" you deferred looks silly in retrospect (fine, you can rebuild it fast), or you don't find PMF and you die — but at least you die without having lit the entire war chest on PR and ping-pong tables.

**Situation:** A technical founder with a viral consumer product has enthusiastic early adopters and assumes the rest of the market will find them the same way. They pour all energy into v2 of the product and none into distribution.
**Application:** You have PMF with 5% of the market — early adopters — and the other 95% doesn't work that way. The 95% needs to be sold to, marketed to, acquired. Build the distribution function now, before a better-distributed competitor shows up and takes the 95%. Raise prices to fund it.
**Result:** If you build distribution fast enough, you take the market and the whole sector's value flows to you. If you don't, within six months twenty venture-backed competitors will exist, one of them will outrun you on distribution, and they will eventually buy you for a small percentage of their equity.

**Situation:** A founder is in a market that genuinely doesn't exist yet — too early — and their team is strong, their product is clever, but customers just don't care.
**Application:** Market wins. A great team executing brilliantly against a terrible market fails. Either change what the market is — move to an adjacent segment where customers actually exist — or shelve the plan. Don't spend three more years on it because the team is good.
**Result:** The teams that change the market survive (frequently as totally different companies than they started as). The ones that don't, die, and the post-mortem always blames "go-to-market" when the real answer was "there was no market."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build out a full executive bench — VP Sales, VP Marketing, CFO, Head of People — before PMF. **Why:** You're paying senior-person salaries to do caretaker work in an org that doesn't exist yet. Each of those hires drags in their own overhead — hiring plans, quarterly planning, recruiting budgets — and none of it substitutes for the actual thing you need, which is customers pulling product out of you. Pre-PMF, you want hungry doers, not experienced administrators.

**Don't:** Convince yourself the market is great because you have a handful of enthusiastic users. **Why:** Early adopters mean the *fit with early adopters* is good. That's a necessary condition, not a sufficient one. The discipline is to look for word-of-mouth growth beyond the enthusiasts, real repeat usage, and a sales motion that works on people who didn't find you first. If the only customers you can name by heart are the ones who cold-emailed you, you haven't taken the market yet.

**Don't:** Stay in a "great product, bad market" combination because the product is clever. **Why:** Markets that don't exist don't care how smart you are. You'll break your pick for years and your best people will quit. If five to eight qualified investors pass, and your product is clearly great, the signal is almost always "market" — either too small, too early, or too late. Re-market the product or shelve it.

**Don't:** Skip the "feel" diagnostic and rely on vanity metrics. **Why:** You can assemble any number of charts that make a non-PMF company look like a PMF company. The actual tell is whether you're drowning — in inbound, in demand, in hiring urgency — or whether you have to force each next step. If your week is full of outbound selling to prospects who only kind-of care, you're BPMF. Admit it, and act accordingly.

**Don't:** Raise the absolute minimum because it flatters your valuation. **Why:** BPMF is when the unanticipated setbacks kill you — a product slip, a customer going bankrupt, a patent suit, the funding window closing. You have no idea how long PMF will take. Raise enough to get there plus substantial insurance. The entrepreneurs who raised less in 1999 and died had a better valuation on paper; the ones who raised a ton and survived have companies worth a billion dollars today.

**Don't:** Treat "we're pre-PMF" as a permanent, self-excusing state. **Why:** The whole point of the framework is that you *can feel it* — BPMF and APMF are distinct, and the job is to force yourself into APMF by whatever means necessary. If you've been BPMF for four years with no meaningful change in the customer pull, the honest diagnosis is usually "this market isn't there, or this team can't build the product it needs," and pretending otherwise just burns the clock.

**Don't:** Assume PMF is permanent. **Why:** Markets change, products go stale, distribution channels evolve. APMF is not a laurel you rest on; it's the starting gun for the next race — taking the market, then building the next product. Most companies that fall apart at scale do so because they mistook one moment of PMF for a steady state and stopped iterating.
