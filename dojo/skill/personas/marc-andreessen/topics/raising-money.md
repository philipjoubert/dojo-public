# Raising Money — How to Get It, How Much, and How to Handle No

## When to Use
- Founder is preparing to raise and wants to know how to structure the approach.
- Founder has been told "no" and is trying to figure out whether the problem is the pitch, the company, the environment, or the VC.
- Founder doesn't know any VCs and is asking how to get in the door.
- Founder is debating how much to raise — too little, too much, enough to not think about this for 18 months.

## Fails When
- The business isn't fundable by venture capital at all — it's a good business but doesn't have the leverage for a 10x return in four to six years. Pushing harder on VCs won't change that. Wrong tool.
- The founder wants to stay small, stay independent, stay private long-term. VC is then exactly the wrong funding source regardless of tactics.
- The founder personalizes each "no" and uses it as evidence the system is rigged, rather than as the data point it is. At that point the advice below can't cut through.

## Core Concept

Raising venture capital is a process, not a charm offensive. VCs are not evaluating whether they like you. They are running an asset class. They raise $100 million today to invest in high-risk startups over the next three to four years, they expect returns back in four to six years, they operate on the baseball model — seven strikeouts, two base hits, one home run out of every ten swings — and therefore they require a **credible potential of a 10x gain in four to six years** on every single investment. If your startup can't plausibly clear that bar, they *can't* fund you, even if they love you. Their own investors wouldn't stand for it.

That one constraint — 10x in four to six years — explains almost everything about how VCs behave. It explains who they fund, why they pass, why they say "maybe" instead of "no", and why the best investment for a VC is the one that would be a huge success without her.

If you think about raising from that vantage point, three facts become obvious:

**One, VCs work mostly through referrals.** Any individual VC can only fund a few companies per year. For every one she funds, she probably meets 15 or 20, and there are hundreds more she doesn't possibly have time to meet. She has to rely on her network to screen the hundreds down to the 15 or 20. **Submitting a business plan "over the transom" is the startup equivalent of submitting an unsolicited screenplay to a Hollywood talent agency** — that is, precisely nothing.

**Two, the "no" is almost never personal and almost never stupid.** When a VC turns you down, it's usually because she can't see the 10x leverage, or she thinks you're too early or unproven, or she isn't convinced you've assembled the right team. Maybe all three. Occasionally she loves it and can't talk her partners into it — that happens too. But the frustrating part is she won't always tell you the real reason, because she wants to keep the door open if things change. So when a VC passes and explains why, no matter how mean or unfair it sounds, **the best response is to thank them for their honesty**.

**Three, you are not trying to raise money. You are trying to take risk off the table.** The whole theory of venture capital is that VCs invest in risk — another term for it is "risk capital" — but the reality is that VCs will only take on *so much* risk. **The best thing you can do to optimize your chances of raising money is to take out risk.** Peel the onion.

## How to Apply

### If you don't know any VCs:

1. **First, have your stuff together.** Before you even think about pitching, work on your plan, pitch, and supporting materials until you're not embarrassing yourself. Most of the hundreds of startups VCs *could* meet with come across as amateurish and uninformed. Cut through the noise by **making a great first impression** on the first meeting — which requires thinking through every objection ahead of time and doing all the hard work up front.
2. **Walk in with a working product if at all possible.** Or at least a beta, a prototype, a web site that works but hasn't launched, a software mockup with partial functionality, *something*. Even better: existing traction, real users, paying customers. When in doubt, **work on the product**.
3. **Don't bring a long detailed written business plan.** Most VCs either fund based on a fleshed-out **PowerPoint of about 20 slides** or they don't fund at all. Corollary: any VC who requires a long detailed written business plan is probably not the right VC to be working with.
4. **Qualify, qualify, qualify.** Do extensive research. Find the VCs who focus on your sector. Pitching a health care VC on a consumer Internet startup is counterproductive for everyone. Individual partners are quite focused; screening out the wrong ones is absolutely key.
5. **Develop referral paths.** The best way: work at a venture-backed startup, kick butt, get promoted, and network the whole way. Failing that, work at a well-regarded big tech company (Google, Apple) and network there. Failing that, go to a major research university from which those companies recruit. This sounds like a joke. It isn't. This is the path many VC-backed founders I know actually walked.
6. **Shortcuts that work:** Y Combinator or similar programs. Being a Stanford/MIT grad student (the VCs are literally prowling the engineering campus). Creating or contributing meaningfully to a well-known open-source project — "I'm the creator of program X with 50,000 users worldwide" is dramatically more effective than a cold pitch. Reading VC blogs carefully and engaging with individual partners on the platforms they're actively using.
7. **Last resort funding alternatives, in reverse order of preference:** angel funding (best, because good angels know good VCs), bootstrapping off consulting contracts or early customers, keeping your day job and working on it in your spare time, and — the one I don't recommend but some people have made work — credit card debt.

### How to pitch:

1. **Pick the partner, not the firm.** Firm quality matters, but the partner is the person you're actually going to work with. The other people at the firm you'll see maybe twice in the whole lifespan of your company. Best of both worlds is a strong partner at a strong firm; be aware that even strong firms have weak partners.
2. **Frame around the 10x.** A VC's ideal investment is one that could produce a 10x gain in four to six years *without her*. Structure the pitch around: the leverage in the business model, the size of the market, why the team can execute, what milestones the money hits. If a thoughtful listener can't trace the path from "this round" to "10x exit in five years" by the end of the deck, the deck is wrong.
3. **Never say you have no competitors.** That signals naivete. Great markets draw competitors, so if you really have no competition, you must not be in a great market. Even if you believe it, build a competitive landscape slide with adjacent companies and be ready to talk crisply about how you're like and unlike them.
4. **Never say your projections assume 2% of a large market.** Also signals naivete. If you're going after 2% of a large market, the companies taking the other 98% are going to kill you. You need a theory for meaningfully higher share.
5. **Keep "yes" fragile.** "Yes" can turn to "no" at any point up until the cash hits your bank account. Keep your options open, keep other conversations live, don't exclusive-negotiate early.

### When they say no:

1. **One "no" is noise.** VC could be having a bad day, bad experience with another company in your category, another founder with a similar name, Mercedes engine blew up. Go meet more VCs.
2. **Three "no"s could be a coincidence.** Go meet more VCs.
3. **Five, six, eight "no"s is not a coincidence.** There is something wrong with your plan. Or if there isn't, there may as well be — you're still not getting funded. Meeting more VCs after that is usually wasted time. **Retool the plan.**
4. **Lay the groundwork to come back.** VCs rarely say outright "no" — they say "maybe", "not right now", "my partners aren't sure". That's exactly what you want: a door to come back through with new facts. Thank them, ask (without expecting honest answers) for feedback, ask if you can call them again if things change.
5. **Run the onion theory of risk.** Investors see risk in layers: founder, market, competition, timing, financing, marketing, distribution, technology, product, hiring, location. Look hard at each. Which layers are you unconsciously asking the VC to absorb? Peel them away one at a time.
6. **Top risk-reduction moves:**
   - *Market risk:* get customers. Paying customers, ideally. Or at least credible prospects willing to talk to VCs and validate the hypothesis.
   - *Competition risk:* sharpen the differentiation. From the ground up. "Differentiated enough to win" is the bar, not "differentiated enough to describe."
   - *Team risk:* swap out or add founders. The hardest one, because nobody will be fully honest with you about it, but it's often the real answer.
   - *Technology/product risk:* build the thing or get it to beta, then raise.
   - *Distribution risk:* if your plan requires a specific distribution deal to work, shelve it. Otherwise you're doing the deal to raise the money to do the deal. Almost impossible.
   - *Location risk:* the one founders hate. If you're not in Silicon Valley, New York, or one of a handful of places, and you're having trouble raising, the honest answer may be you need to move.
7. **Re-pitch with new facts.** Then go back. The "maybe" becomes a "yes" precisely when the facts have changed enough that the VC can see the 10x without squinting.

### How much to raise:

1. **BPMF: raise at least enough to get to PMF.** With a substantial cushion beyond your default plan, as insurance against bad surprises. You have no real idea how long PMF will take.
2. **APMF: raise at least enough to fully exploit the opportunity, then enough more to get to profitability while still fully exploiting it.**
3. **In general, raise as much as you can.** Without giving away control, and without being insane. Founders who hold back to "raise it later at a higher valuation" are gambling their whole company on top of all the normal startup risks. Suppose you raise a lot and do well — you'll be happy, even if you made slightly less than you would've if you'd rolled the dice. Suppose you hold back and it backfires — you lose the company. Is that risk worth the dilution you saved? **Raising money is buying yourself insurance.**
4. **Know the consequences of not raising enough:** unanticipated internal setbacks (product slips, key engineer quits, customer goes bankrupt, patent suit); the funding window closing when you need more money ("the window is shut"); genuinely unexpected external shocks (2001–2003, 2008, pandemics). The *big* difference between late-'90s startups that exist today and ones that don't is that the survivors raised a ton of money when they could, and the dead didn't. Opsware would be bankrupt if we hadn't raised when we could.
5. **The one thing to watch: liquidation preference.** More money raised → higher floor an acquisition has to clear before founders and employees make anything. If you *know* you're going to sell low, raising less is correct for your personal outcome. But if you're optimizing for "try to build a real company," raising more is right.
6. **Don't raise so much it corrodes the culture.** Raising a ton of money *feels* really good, but **raising money is never an accomplishment in and of itself** — it just raises the stakes for all the hard work you'd have to do anyway. Signs of cultural corrosion from too much cash: hiring too many people (the single easiest way to lose control of spending), a lazy management culture where managers' only job is hiring, engineering team bloat and the Mythical-Man-Month effect, loss of focus on product and customers, salespeople out too early selling an unfinished product, schedule slippage because urgency evaporated.
7. **If you do raise a lot: act like you didn't.** As Jim Barksdale said, *the main thing is to keep the main thing the main thing* — stay as obsessed with product and customers as if you were still scraping. Run lean. Bank as much as possible. Save for a rainy day or a nuclear winter. Tell your team, over and over until they can't stand it, that **raising money does not count as an accomplishment**. Ikea furniture, big monitors, ergonomic chairs, that's it. Pay close attention to deadlines — the first thing to slip after a big round is urgency.

## Examples

**Situation:** Technical founding team with a live beta and a dozen delighted early users. They've pitched eight Silicon Valley VCs. Every one has passed. They're starting to wonder if the whole category is cursed.
**Application:** Eight passes is not noise. Run the onion. Which layers are they asking VCs to absorb? Market — they have delighted users but can't articulate a path from dozen to million. Team — one technical co-founder, nobody who's run a business. Financing risk — no clear plan for how much it'll cost to get to the next milestone. Retool: add a business co-founder or at least a real CEO-track hire, go get ten paying customers (even tiny ones) to validate the market, model the financing path out three rounds. Then go back to the two VCs who were most intrigued with "new facts."
**Result:** Either one of them now sees the 10x and writes the check, or the passes continue — at which point the honest question is whether this is actually a venture-scale business or whether it's something better run profitably.

**Situation:** Founder has a term sheet in hand, is debating whether to take $8M on terms that feel generous or push for $15M at the same valuation.
**Application:** Default to more. Raise enough to get to PMF or fully exploit it, plus insurance. $8M assumes zero surprises. $15M covers a product slip, a key departure, and a closed funding window eighteen months from now. The only reasons to stay at $8M: cultural corrosion risk (you don't trust yourself to act scrappy with more) or a very specific view that a low-priced acquisition is likely and liquidation preference matters more than insurance. Default is more.
**Result:** Take more, bank it, run like you raised less. Your future self thanks you the next time a customer goes bankrupt or Google ships a competitor.

**Situation:** Founder hears a VC say "we love this but our partners aren't sure — let's talk in three months."
**Application:** That's not a no. That's a door. Thank them, walk out, and in three months come back with new facts — a couple of signed customers, a key hire, a cleaner pricing story, whatever risk layer they implicitly flagged. Don't try to argue the current facts into a yes. Change the facts.
**Result:** The follow-up meeting goes very differently. Or, if the "maybe" was actually a polite "no," you still learn that faster than you would by going back in unchanged.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Over-transom. **Why:** Sending unsolicited cold pitches to info@firm.com is, numerically, a non-strategy. VCs fund through their referral networks because they have to. Spend your energy building the referral path, not polishing cold emails that nobody will open.

**Don't:** Claim you have no competitors. **Why:** It signals you haven't thought about the space. Every interesting market has competitors — or if it truly doesn't, that's a strong hint it isn't an interesting market. Build the landscape slide, pick the two or three closest analogs, and crisply articulate the delta.

**Don't:** Take the "our projections assume 2% of a $50B market" path. **Why:** It's a red-flag cliché. A thoughtful investor reads "2% of a large market" as "I haven't thought about the other 98% or who's going to take it from me." If your market really is that big, have a theory for winning 30%+ of a meaningful segment of it first.

**Don't:** Personalize the "no". **Why:** One no is noise. Three is still mostly noise. Only at five-to-eight should you update your priors on the plan itself. Spending a week licking wounds after a single "no" is wasted time you should spend peeling the onion or taking the next meeting.

**Don't:** Hold back on round size to minimize dilution. **Why:** You're buying a tiny amount of incremental ownership on paper in exchange for catastrophic downside risk if anything unexpected goes wrong — and something unexpected *always* goes wrong. The tradeoff is asymmetric and almost always favors raising more.

**Don't:** Let raising money become the internal celebration event. **Why:** It tells your team the wrong thing about what counts as progress. Ship the product, close the customer, hit the milestone — those are accomplishments. Raising is a stakes-raising event, not an achievement. If you let the team treat the announcement like the finish line, the company's momentum dies the week the check clears.

**Don't:** Take money from a partner you don't want to be in a room with for the next decade. **Why:** You're picking a board member, not a check. Firm brand fades next to the reality of monthly board meetings, quarterly partner dynamics, and whoever signs the pass on your Series B. Reference the partner the way you'd reference a senior hire — talk to founders she's backed, especially the ones whose companies didn't go well, because that's where character actually shows.

**Don't:** Require a big-company distribution deal to make the plan work, and then try to raise around it. **Why:** You'll be told "come back when you have the deal," and the deal is almost impossible to get without the money. Either eliminate the dependency from the plan or plan to bootstrap until the deal exists.

**Don't:** Treat "yes" as done before the money is in the bank. **Why:** Yes can turn into no at any point before the wire clears — diligence issues, partner meetings, macro shifts. Keep competing conversations live until the funds are irrevocable. Celebrate after the cash arrives, not after the handshake.
