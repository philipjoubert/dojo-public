# The Ackerman Model

## When to Use
- Buying or selling a vehicle, a house, equipment, or anything where the price is negotiable.
- Vendor contracts, consulting rates, project fees, retainers.
- Salary counter-offers, once they've anchored and you're responding with a number.
- Any one-shot price negotiation where the counterpart expects to haggle.
- High-stakes cases where the "price" is a hostage or a ransom. Yes, this was built for that, and it works.

## Fails When
- You use round numbers. $30,000 reads as "picked from the air." $29,847 reads as "calculated."
- You move in equal steps: 20k, 25k, 30k, 35k. You've just told them you have unlimited room.
- You skip empathy between offers. Now you're haggling, not negotiating, and every move looks like caving.
- You lose nerve and fold before your target, treating the first three steps as the whole game.
- You use it with someone you need an ongoing relationship with. Aggressive anchoring damages trust.

## Core Concept

The Ackerman Model is a six-step bargaining system. Mike Ackerman, a former CIA officer who ran kidnap-for-ransom consulting, developed it. The FBI refined it. I've used it on car deals and on ransoms where the price was a human life. The arithmetic is simple. The psychology is the whole point.

You pick a target. Your opening offer is 65% of target. Then you raise in diminishing increments: 85%, 95%, 100%. Each raise is smaller than the last, and that's the point. The shrinking steps broadcast a signal without you having to say a word: I'm approaching my limit. Each concession is costing me more than the last. By the time you hit 100%, the pattern tells the story for you.

Between every offer, you use tactical empathy, labels, and calibrated questions. That's what separates this from haggling. Your final number is precise and non-round ($37,893, not $38,000), so it sounds calculated, like you did the work. And when you land on your target, you add a non-monetary sweetener. That's the final tell, the thing that says, "if I had more cash, I wouldn't be throwing in extras."

## How to Apply
1. Set your target. Not your walk-away, not your "I'd be happy with this." Your actual goal. Write it down.
2. Open at 65% of target. Deliver it wrapped in empathy. Something like, "Look, I understand this probably seems low. You've got a quality product and I know you need to make a profit. I'm not wasting your time, I'm trying to find a number that works for both of us." Then give the number.
3. Between offers, use "how am I supposed to do that?", labels, and tactical empathy. Make them earn each move. Every dollar you give up should feel to them like a real concession, because it is.
4. Raise in diminishing increments: 65 to 85 (twenty points), 85 to 95 (ten points), 95 to 100 (five points). The shrinking pattern is doing the psychological work.
5. Use precise, non-round numbers. Not $28,000, use $28,457. Not $5,000, use $4,751. Odd numbers signal calculation. Round numbers signal guessing.
6. When you land on your target, add a non-monetary item: free maintenance, faster delivery, a personal favor, an introduction, extended warranty. It sweetens the deal and, more importantly, it signals you have no cash left. If you did, why would you be adding extras?

## Examples

**Situation:** Buying my Toyota 4Runner. Dealer asking $36,000. My target $30,000. Most people would have split the difference around $34,500.
**Application:** Opened at $19,500 (65% of $30,000). Salesman almost choked. Empathy first, then the calibrated question: "How am I supposed to pay $35,000 when the research I've done shows similar vehicles selling for much less?" Worked up through $25,500 (85%) and $28,500 (95%). Landed at $30,000 with a non-monetary kicker: "I'll agree to do the financing through your dealership, which I know gets you some credit."
**Result:** He took the deal. I got my target price.

**Situation:** An American kidnapped in Haiti. Kidnappers demanded $150,000. These people would kill the hostage if things went sideways.
**Application:** Ackerman Model, but working toward a much lower target. Between every offer, massive tactical empathy. We labeled their frustrations. We asked calibrated questions. Offers were always precise: not $5,000, but $4,751. Not $3,000, but $3,127. The odd numbers made them believe we'd scraped together every last dollar.
**Result:** Ransom paid: $4,751. Less than 4% of the original demand. Hostage home safely.

**Situation:** Freelance consultant negotiating her rate. Client wants $75/hour. Her target is $120/hour.
**Application:** She countered with a non-round opener around 65% of her target movement, then worked up in diminishing increments. Empathy between each move: "I understand budgets are tight right now, and I want to make this work." Final number $117/hour, plus a non-monetary add: a free strategy session in month one.
**Result:** Client agreed. She got effectively her target without the relationship turning adversarial.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with a "reasonable" number because you're embarrassed by the 65% anchor.
**Why:** The low anchor is the whole engine. It gives you room to make real concessions, it resets their expectations, and it makes your later moves feel significant. Open at 90% and you've squeezed all the leverage out of the process before it starts. You end up splitting a tiny difference and paying more.

**Don't:** Use clean round numbers ($25,000, $30,000) anywhere in the sequence.
**Why:** Round numbers sound arbitrary. They telegraph "I made this up." Precise numbers ($24,763, $29,847) sound calculated, which means they sound defensible, which means they're harder to push against. In the Haiti case, $4,751 got paid. $5,000 would have kept the kidnappers hunting for more.

**Don't:** Raise your offer without running empathy and calibrated questions in between.
**Why:** If you just rattle off $19,500 then $25,500 then $28,500 on demand, you look like a kid haggling at a market. The empathy is what makes each concession feel expensive to give. Without it, your raises signal there's endless room above. The model only works inside a relationship; the numbers alone are a skeleton.

**Don't:** Forget the non-monetary sweetener at the final offer.
**Why:** That add-on is the signal that closes the deal. It tells them "if I had any more money, I wouldn't be doing this." Without it, your final number just looks like one more step in the same ladder, and they keep pushing. With it, they see the limit and take the deal.
