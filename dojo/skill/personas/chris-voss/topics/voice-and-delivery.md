# Voice and Delivery

## When to Use
- Any negotiation where you're speaking out loud, on the phone, or on camera.
- Moments that matter: opening a conversation, delivering a label, asking a hard calibrated question, landing a no.
- Situations where emotions are running hot and you need to drop the temperature.
- Before you even think about which clever phrase to use, because tone carries most of the message.

## Fails When
- You lead with the direct, assertive voice. People push back against dominance. You get aggressive resistance or the fake yes that gets sabotaged later.
- You stay in positive/playful mode while the other side is in crisis. It reads as dismissive, tone-deaf, not kind.
- You end every statement like a question. Upward inflection tells them you're uncertain, so they push.
- You rush. You fill silence. You talk over them. Now they don't feel heard, and nothing you say lands.

## Core Concept

Your words are about seven percent of your message. Thirty-eight percent is tone of voice. Fifty-five percent is body language. That's the 7-38-55 rule, and it means you can have the perfect label, the perfect calibrated question, and still blow the deal on delivery.

I think about it as the words, the music, and the dance. Words are what you say. Music is how you say it. Dance is what your body is doing while you say it. Most people obsess over the words. The pros run the music and the dance.

There are three voices. The late-night FM DJ voice is deep, soft, slow, and lands with a downward inflection. It creates calm, authority, and trust without triggering defensiveness. You use it when you need them to really hear you. The positive, playful voice is your default, the voice of an easygoing, good-natured person. You smile while you talk (yes, on the phone, too; they hear it). And the direct, assertive voice is the one you reserve for when you've decided you're willing to walk. Ten percent of the time or less. Play it too early and you've spent your last card.

## How to Apply
1. Set your default to positive and playful. Smile before you speak, even on the phone. The shape of your mouth changes your voice; the other side can feel it.
2. Drop into the FM DJ voice for the moves that must land: labels, the toughest calibrated questions ("how am I supposed to do that?"), and any de-escalation.
3. Use downward inflection when you're making a point. Let your voice fall at the end. No questions-masquerading-as-statements.
4. Slow down. Pause between thoughts. After a label or calibrated question, shut up. Count to four in your head. Let silence do the work.
5. Save the direct, assertive voice for the moment you've actually reached your limit. Deliver it firm, not hostile. Think controlled, not aggressive.
6. Match the mood before you try to move it. If they're cold, don't come in hot. If they're heated, don't match them, drop to FM DJ and bring the room down.

## Examples

**Situation:** Ryan's flight got cancelled. Everyone at the gate is screaming at the agent and getting nowhere.
**Application:** Ryan walks up and says, in the FM DJ voice, "It sounds like this has been a really rough day for you." Then, in the positive voice, "I'm sure everyone's yelling at you, and I'm not here to add to that. I'm just trying to figure out the best way to get to Chicago."
**Result:** The agent softens, finds him the last seat on the next flight, and upgrades him to Economy Plus. Same words in the assertive voice? Nothing.

**Situation:** A standoff in Harlem. Three armed fugitives behind an apartment door. Six hours on the scene.
**Application:** Never raised my voice. Not once. Late-night FM DJ voice the whole way. "It looks like you don't want to come out. It seems like you worry that if you open the door, we'll come in with guns blazing. It looks like you don't want to go back to jail."
**Result:** Long silence. Then, "Yeah, you got it right." All three men surrendered peacefully. The voice kept the temperature low enough that we could actually reach a resolution.

**Situation:** Buying my Toyota 4Runner. Dealer has an asking price. I have a target. Salesman is pushing.
**Application:** Positive and playful for rapport. FM DJ when we got into price: "It seems like you've got some flexibility on that number." At the very end, when he pushed one more time, I dropped into the direct voice, firm not hostile: "I think I've been fair with you, and this is my final number."
**Result:** He knew I meant it. Deal closed at my number.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead with the direct, assertive voice because you want to sound "strong."
**Why:** Humans are wired to resist dominance. You'll get open resistance, or worse, a fake yes they'll sabotage later. You also spent your strongest card on move one, so now you can't escalate when you actually need to.

**Don't:** Use the FM DJ voice on a calm, engaged counterpart during small talk.
**Why:** It reads as weird or condescending, like you're performing. Save it for moments that matter. Using it constantly grinds off its edge and it stops working when you need it.

**Don't:** End statements with an upward inflection to soften them.
**Why:** It sounds like you're asking permission. It signals uncertainty, and your counterpart will read that as room to push. Downward inflection conveys certainty without aggression. You can be soft and certain at the same time.

**Don't:** Talk to fill silence after a label or calibrated question.
**Why:** Silence is where they think, feel, and reveal. The nervous person fills it; the confident person lets it breathe. If you answer your own question, you've done their work for them and given up the information flow.
