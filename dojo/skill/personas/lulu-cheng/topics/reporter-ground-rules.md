# Reporter Ground Rules

## When to Use
- You're about to talk to a journalist — any journalist, any time
- A reporter has reached out for comment on a story
- You're building a relationship with a journalist who covers your space
- You're deciding whether to participate in a story

## Fails When
- You assume a conversation is off the record because you've talked before
- You divulge actual secrets "off the record"
- You forget anyone with a phone can tweet anything you said at dinner
- You participate in a story that's going to be negative regardless of your input
- You skip confirmation because it feels paranoid

## Core Concept

Before any conversation with a journalist, establish the ground rules. These terms have specific meanings, and misunderstanding them creates problems.

**On the Record.** Everything is fair game. Anything you say can be quoted, attributed to you by name, and published. Use when making announcements, defending yourself publicly, saying things you want on the record. Caution: assume EVERYTHING is on the record unless explicitly stated otherwise. Even "casual" comments before or after the "official" interview.

**Off the Record.** The information you provide cannot be quoted, paraphrased, or attributed. The journalist agrees not to use it directly. Use when building relationship, providing context you don't want published, helping a journalist understand something they won't write about. Caution: "off the record" is a gentleman's agreement, not a legal contract. Some journalists honor it religiously; others don't. Never say anything truly sensitive off the record.

**On Background.** The information is usable, but you're not identified. The journalist can say "a source close to the company" or "according to a person familiar with the matter." Use when wanting to shape a story without being quoted, providing information that helps coverage without being the spokesperson. Caution: "background" has variations — not for attribution vs. deep background. Confirm exactly what the journalist understands the term to mean.

The one rule above all: ask AND get confirmation before EVERY conversation about which rules apply. Don't assume. Don't rely on past conversations. Every single time, clarify: "Just to be clear, this is on the record?" or "This is off the record, right?" And don't say anything until you've gotten confirmation. This sounds paranoid. It's not. Misunderstandings about record status have destroyed relationships and careers. The two seconds it takes to clarify is always worth it. Also remember: anyone can tweet anything. "Off the record" with a journalist doesn't stop someone at the dinner table from posting what you said. If you don't want something public, don't say it anywhere.

## How to Apply

1. **Confirm the ground rules before speaking.** Every conversation, every time. "To confirm, this is on background, right? You can attribute to 'a person familiar with' but not to me by name?" Get the confirmation. Then speak.

2. **Assume on-the-record by default.** Unless explicitly confirmed otherwise. Comments before the official interview starts, comments after it ends, comments in the hallway — on the record.

3. **Don't switch modes mid-conversation.** "Okay, now this is off the record..." is dangerous. Did the journalist catch that? Did they agree? If you need to switch, stop completely, confirm the new terms, then proceed.

4. **Don't say secrets off the record.** Off the record reduces risk; it doesn't eliminate it. Anything catastrophic-if-leaked shouldn't be said anywhere.

5. **Triage incoming requests.** When a journalist reaches out, don't respond immediately. Take a beat. Find out: what's the story about? What angle? Who else have they talked to? What's the deadline? What specifically do they want? Then decide if it's worth participating.

6. **Decline when appropriate.** "We're not able to participate in this story." "We don't have anything to add beyond our public statements." You don't owe journalists access. They don't owe you coverage. It's okay for either side to say no.

7. **Be a useful source, not a pitcher.** Journalists hate PR pitches. They tweet about bad ones and dunk on PR people. The healthier dynamic is mutual utility: you provide access, information, quotable comments, story ideas. They provide coverage, reach, third-party credibility. Neither side is doing the other a favor.

8. **Train employees on media policy.** Reporters will reach out to random employees on LinkedIn trying to get information or quotes outside official channels. Employees should know to route requests and not feel obligated to respond to random outreach.

## Examples

**Situation:** You have a regular relationship with a journalist. You've talked many times. She calls with a question. You start answering.
**Application:** Wrong. Even with a known journalist, confirm: "Is this on background, or are we on the record?" Prior conversations don't carry over. Each conversation has its own terms.
**Result:** The two seconds of confirmation prevents the 10-year career consequence of getting surprised by a quote.

**Situation:** Journalist reaches out for comment on a story clearly designed to be a hit piece. They've already talked to your critics. The angle is hostile.
**Application:** You don't have to participate. Declining isn't admission of guilt. "We don't have anything to add beyond our public statements." Or go direct yourself — if they're going to write it anyway, publish your version on your own Rostra before their piece lands.
**Result:** You've refused to legitimize the story and you've provided your own version for the record. Journalist runs their piece, you have your counter-narrative already in the world.

**Situation:** You're at a dinner with a journalist. You say something indiscreet. You think "this isn't an interview."
**Application:** Wrong assumption. It's either on the record (default) or you confirmed otherwise. Also — anyone at that dinner with a phone can tweet what you said. Off-the-record doesn't cover citizen journalism.
**Result:** If you don't want it public, don't say it anywhere. "Off the record" is not a force field.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Assume terms carry over between conversations. Every conversation is its own ground-rules check.
**Why:** Past familiarity doesn't bind future conduct. Journalists' editors change. Journalists' incentives change. A confirmed-off-the-record conversation last year doesn't mean today is off the record.

**Don't:** Divulge actual secrets off the record. Off the record reduces risk; it does not eliminate it.
**Why:** Some journalists honor it religiously; others don't. Even honest journalists have editors, colleagues, and notes that leak. If it would be catastrophic public, don't say it anywhere.

**Don't:** Treat media engagement as obligatory. You can decline. They don't owe you coverage; you don't owe them access.
**Why:** Not every story is worth being in. Some stories are going to be negative no matter what you say. Some journalists are fishing for a quote to hang you with. Declining is a valid move.

**Don't:** Switch ground rules mid-conversation without pausing. "Now this is off the record" while still talking is ambiguous and dangerous.
**Why:** Did the journalist actually agree? Did they pause? Did they keep recording? Ambiguity here destroys you later. Stop fully, confirm fully, then proceed.

**Don't:** Pitch through agencies running a numbers game. The PR pitch template that goes to 100 journalists at once gets deleted.
**Why:** Journalists pattern-match it instantly. A direct relationship where you're a useful source beats a thousand cold pitches. Better to have three journalists who take your call than three hundred on a mass-email list.

**Don't:** Think going direct means ignoring traditional media entirely. Media still matters — reaching audiences you don't have, third-party credibility, narrative influence. Media is a tool in the toolkit, not the primary channel.
**Why:** Going direct is primary. But when stories are written about your space, you want to be a source, or others frame the narrative. Selective engagement is the move.
