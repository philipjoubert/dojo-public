# The Master Narrative

## When to Use
- You're building a company and don't have a coherent story yet
- Your current mission statement is something no reasonable person would disagree with
- You're repositioning after a strategic pivot
- You're raising a round and need investors to buy a narrative, not just a roadmap
- You're recruiting mission-driven people and need a flag for them to rally around

## Fails When
- Your narrative doesn't match what your company actually does — the dissonance gets exposed
- Your antagonist is vague ("the status quo," "friction," "inefficiency") — vague enemies don't generate loyalty
- Your founder can't deliver it with conviction — written-on-paper narratives die when spoken
- You force conflict where none exists — audiences sense manufactured enemies
- Everyone agrees with your mission — if no one opts out, your filter is broken

## Core Concept

Every company needs a story. Not a mission statement. Not a tagline. Not a value proposition designed by committee. A story — with a protagonist, an antagonist, and stakes that matter.

Most company missions are nothing that nobody would possibly disagree with. "We exist to elevate human potential." "Our mission is to make the world a better place." "We're committed to excellence and innovation." Who would disagree? Nobody. And that's exactly the problem. If your mission is something everyone would nod along to, it isn't doing any work. It's not filtering anyone in or out. It's not creating loyalty. It's not giving people a flag to rally around. The purpose of a mission isn't to sound nice. It's to filter — to attract the right people and repel the wrong ones. If nobody hears your mission and thinks "I fundamentally disagree," your mission is pablum.

The master narrative is the canonical version of why your company exists, what it's fighting against, and why anyone should care. Everything else in your communications flows from this. Get it right and every piece of content, every interview, every tweet becomes an extension of a coherent worldview. Get it wrong — or never define it at all — and you're just making noise. Your mission, if it means anything at all, should make some people uncomfortable. That discomfort is proof of life.

## How to Apply

A master narrative has six parts. Miss any of them and the story doesn't land.

1. **The Ideal State.** What the world should look like. Not utopia — a specific vision of how your corner of the world could work better. It sets the standard.

2. **The Fall.** What went wrong. Who or what is responsible. This is where the antagonist enters. Not a vague enemy — a specific force that created the problem you exist to solve.

3. **The Consequences.** What happens if nothing changes. The stakes. If the stakes are low, why should anyone care?

4. **The Turn.** Your mission. The line in the sand. The moment where you said "not on my watch." Not a wish — a commitment to action.

5. **The Method.** How you're going to win. The approach that makes your mission plausible. Anyone can declare a grand mission. The method is what makes it believable.

6. **The Bright Future.** What the world looks like if you succeed. Not just the absence of the problem — an affirmative picture of something worth building.

## Examples

**Situation:** Boom Supersonic. "We make fast planes" is a product description, not a narrative.
**Application:**
- *Ideal state:* Air travel should connect the world, making distant places accessible.
- *The fall:* Fifty years ago, bureaucrats imposed a speed limit in the sky. The Concorde was retired. For half a century, planes haven't gotten meaningfully faster.
- *Consequences:* Billions of hours lost. Business trips that could be day trips require overnight stays. Families separated by distance stay separated.
- *The turn:* Boom exists to break the arbitrary speed limit and restart progress in aviation.
- *The method:* New materials, new engines, new economics that make supersonic flight viable.
- *Bright future:* New York to London as a morning commute.
**Result:** A mission people will work 80-hour weeks for. An antagonist that is specific enough to be identifiable (a regulation from 1973) and inhuman enough that nobody gets personally hurt by the fight.

**Situation:** Coinbase during the SEC war. "We do crypto" isn't a narrative.
**Application:** Brian Armstrong named a specific regulatory apparatus strangling innovation. He specified who was doing what wrong and why. He didn't gesture vaguely at "regulatory challenges."
**Result:** The specificity is what made the narrative stick. Crypto users, employees, and allied companies could see who the enemy was and join the fight.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Name a generic enemy like "the establishment" or "outdated thinking." Those aren't antagonists — they're mood lighting.
**Why:** Audiences can't picture a generic enemy, can't hold one accountable, and can't tell when you've won. "The assumption that air travel can't be faster than it was in 1973" is specific. "Resistance to change" is not.

**Don't:** Write the narrative in a Google Doc and expect it to do the work. A narrative only works if the founder can deliver it with conviction. If they're reciting it robotically, rewrite it in their actual voice until they mean it.
**Why:** The master narrative is the script for everything else. If the person delivering it doesn't believe it, audiences will feel the hedge.

**Don't:** Pick an enemy you don't actually oppose because it polls well. Manufactured conflict feels fake. People can sense when a company is inventing enemies for marketing purposes rather than fighting genuine battles.
**Why:** Audiences tolerate a narrow or specific enemy. They punish a fake one. And once you've been caught manufacturing, your narrative is dead — every future claim gets discounted.

**Don't:** Write a narrative that would be cheering at your own quarterly earnings. "We are disrupting the industry" is a press release, not a story.
**Why:** Narratives are about stakes beyond your balance sheet. If the story ends with "and then we became a unicorn," nobody outside your cap table cares.
