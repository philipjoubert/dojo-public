# Comms Tactics Formula (Work Backwards from Business)

## When to Use
- You're planning a comms campaign and want to know if it's worth the effort
- Someone is pitching "we should go viral" or "we should be on TikTok"
- You have a budget and need to decide where to spend it
- You're evaluating whether an existing comms activity is actually doing work

## Fails When
- You start with the tactic and back-rationalize the reason
- Your audience is defined as "everyone"
- The belief you're targeting doesn't match what your audience would actually need to believe to act
- You're pursuing a business objective that isn't actually yours (chasing industry awards, influencer engagement, vanity-metric "reach")

## Core Concept

**COMMS PLAN X** will help instill **BELIEF A** in **AUDIENCE B**, to make them want to take **ACTIONS C**, which will help achieve **BUSINESS OBJECTIVE D**.

Most communications plans fail because they optimize for the wrong goal. Wrong goals: "go viral," "get TV hits," "increase social followers," "get media coverage." These are vanity metrics. They feel good but don't connect to business outcomes. Right goals: "increase qualified job applicants by 20%," "move 10,000 units of the new product," "get meetings with 50 target enterprise customers," "raise the Series B from specific investor profiles."

You work backwards from D. Never start with a comms goal. Start with what the business actually needs. Then figure out what actions people need to take for that to happen. Then figure out specifically who those people are — not "everyone," but a defined audience. Then figure out what they need to believe to take those actions. Only then do you figure out the comms plan that instills that belief. Work in any other direction and you produce theater — activity that feels like progress but produces nothing.

If your comms plan doesn't trace back to a business objective, it's not a plan. It's vanity. The test is the reverse walk: if the tactic succeeds wildly, does the business outcome follow? If yes, it's a plan. If no — if you can go viral and nothing happens — it's entertainment.

## How to Apply

Work backwards through the chain:

1. **D = Business Objective.** What does the business actually need? "Hire 30 senior engineers by Q3." "Close a Series B from deep-tech-aligned investors." "Convert 10,000 signups into paid users." Specific, measurable, and actually yours — not a vanity goal disguised as strategy.

2. **C = Actions.** What do people need to DO to make D happen? "Apply for jobs." "Take pitch meetings." "Convert free-to-paid." Every piece of content should eventually be evaluable against whether someone took the action.

3. **B = Audience.** Who specifically are the people who need to take those actions? Not "everyone." Not "developers." Specific: "senior engineers who are currently frustrated at a specific tier of BigCo." "Partners at funds that have led rounds in [category] in the last 18 months." The more specific, the better the message.

4. **A = Belief.** What do they need to believe to take that action? This is the hard one. Most comms plans fail here. "They need to believe we're the best place to solve [specific kind of problem]." "They need to believe the category is real and our team is the one to execute it." Get the belief wrong and everything downstream is misaimed.

5. **X = Tactics.** NOW you can figure out the tactics. What content, in what format, on what platform, with what hook, will instill belief A in audience B? The tactic is now bounded and strategic. You're not picking platforms for vanity — you're picking platforms because that's where audience B is.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder walks in with "we need to go viral on LinkedIn."
**Application:** Work backwards. D = "hire 30 senior backend engineers by Q3." C = "apply for jobs here." B = "senior backend engineers with distributed-systems experience." A = "this is where I can work on the hardest problems in my field with the best colleagues." X = likely NOT viral LinkedIn posts. More likely: deep technical blog posts by engineers on the team, a podcast appearance by the CTO discussing the specific systems challenge, and a hiring page that actually shows the work.
**Result:** Not viral. But measurable increase in qualified applicants. That's the point.

**Situation:** A team pitches "let's get a TechCrunch feature for the launch."
**Application:** Work backwards. D = "1000 enterprise demos booked in Q1." C = "book a demo." B = "heads of engineering at mid-market SaaS companies." A = "this tool solves a problem I'm spending budget on right now." X = TechCrunch is unlikely to reach heads of engineering at mid-market SaaS, and even if it does, it doesn't convert them to demo requests. Better: focused podcast appearance on a show those heads of engineering actually listen to, paired with direct outreach, paired with a conversion-optimized landing page.
**Result:** Might get zero tech press. Books demos. That's the business outcome, not the press hit.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Start with the tactic. "We should be on TikTok" is a tactic in search of a reason.
**Why:** Starting with tactics means the plan gets reverse-engineered to justify the tactic. You end up measuring the tactic's success (engagement rate, views) instead of the business outcome.

**Don't:** Define audience as "everyone" or "people interested in [category]." "Everyone" isn't an audience — it's an abdication.
**Why:** You can't write for everyone. You write for someone specific, and everyone else is a bonus. Broad audience definitions produce pablum because you hedge to avoid offending anyone.

**Don't:** Target a belief the audience wouldn't hold even if they wanted to. "They need to believe we're the safest choice" — but your audience actually cares about innovation, not safety. Tone-deaf messaging is almost always a mis-specification of belief A.
**Why:** The belief has to be a step they're willing to take given who they are. If it's not, you're shouting at them, not persuading.

**Don't:** Accept "go viral" as a business objective. It isn't. Virality is sometimes a tactic; it's never a goal.
**Why:** People who were hugged enough as kids don't need to go viral for its own sake. Attention is a means to an end. If it doesn't convert to the business outcome, you built a cool moment and nothing else.

**Don't:** Hand the formula to an agency and accept "we'll figure out D for you." If you outsource the definition of the business objective, you outsourced the strategy.
**Why:** The founder knows D. The agency doesn't. If the plan doesn't start with a D you own, the plan isn't actually yours.
