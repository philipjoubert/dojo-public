# Betting on Change

## When to Use
- You are setting strategy on a five-to-ten-year horizon and you notice your plan is really just "today, but a bit better."
- You are looking at a market whose current size is zero or near-zero and everyone's analysis turns on that current size.
- You are deciding whether to pivot or hold direction, and the argument for pivoting is that the current trajectory "is not extraordinary."
- You are evaluating competitive threats on the assumption that the current competitive landscape will persist.

## Fails When
- **The trend is plausible rather than directionally robust.** "AI will be big" is robust. "Consumers will prefer this specific UI metaphor in 2030" is not. If the direction is arguable, treat the bet as speculative rather than structural.
- **Used to justify ignoring operational reality.** You still have to survive today. Betting on change is about allocation at the margin, not about abandoning current execution.
- **Used to rationalize constant pivoting.** Changing direction every year is the opposite of betting on change. It is interrupting compounding. Real bets on change are expressed through positioning that stays stable for a decade.
- **Used to dress up a prediction as a structural view.** If the real claim is "this specific thing will happen on this specific timeline," that is forecasting. Forecasting is usually wrong. The framework is about directions, not events.

## Core Concept

Most business strategy implicitly assumes the world will remain roughly as it is. Competitive moats get analyzed as if market structures are static. Plans project current trends forward with minor adjustments. Investments get evaluated against the existing landscape. But the world changes, often in predictable directions even when the timing is uncertain. The right strategic question is not "what is true now?" but "what will be true in five to ten years that isn't true today?" Positioning for the changed world, rather than the current one, is the essence of ambitious strategy. This is both obvious and underappreciated. Everyone will tell you the world is changing. Very few people actually position for specific changes. The default is to optimize for today and hope tomorrow is similar enough that the optimization holds. That is betting on stasis, even if nobody calls it that.

Betting on stasis feels safe because it is betting on what you already understand. You know the current market, the current competitors, how things work today. Betting on change requires imagining a world that does not exist yet and positioning for it before it arrives. That feels like speculation. But stasis is also a bet, and it is usually the wrong one. Market structures do change. Technologies do transform industries. Advantages do erode. The company positioned perfectly for today may be poorly positioned for a decade from now, and by then it may be too late to reposition. A small detail from early Stripe illustrates this. Our Series A deck included a list of companies that would "never use Stripe." Amazon was on that list. The reasoning made sense at the time — Amazon was building its own payment infrastructure, had no reason to use an external provider, operated at a completely different scale. Years later, Amazon became a major partner. We were not prescient about Amazon specifically. We were positioned for a world where internet commerce would be dramatically larger and more sophisticated than it was. In that world, many things that seemed impossible would become possible. We bet on the direction, not on the specifics.

The right horizon is roughly five to ten years — long enough for significant change, short enough to keep planning coherent. A useful exercise is to project forward to a specific year, say 2032, and look back. What would need to be true for us to have succeeded? What capabilities? What positioning? What relationships? Working backward from an imagined future is different from working forward from the present. It forces you to articulate what you believe about how the world will change. This is not forecasting. I am not predicting specific events or timing. I am identifying directions that seem robust. The internet will continue to grow. More commerce will move online. Emerging markets will become larger portions of the global economy. Software will eat more industries. These are not risky predictions; they are almost certain to be directionally true. The question is whether you are positioning for them. Most competitors operate quarter to quarter. If you are operating on a five-year horizon and they are operating on a ninety-day one, strategies are open to you that are not open to them. You can make investments that take years to pay off, hire people and wait for them to develop expertise, enter markets before they are economically interesting because you are positioning for what they will become.

There is a lesson from Henry Singleton at Teledyne: adapt to prevailing conditions, but do not interrupt compounding without good reason. His tactical moves changed dramatically over his tenure — aggressive acquisition, share repurchases, diversification, focus — but he never reset the fundamental trajectory of value creation without cause. That applies to the relationship between betting on change and operational stability. You can believe the world is changing while still maintaining consistency in execution. The bet is about positioning, not about constant pivoting. Constant pivoting undermines compounding, and compounding is the actual secret. 1.3 to the N ends up being a large number. Thirty percent growth compounded for a long time transforms into something enormous — but only if you do not interrupt it. Every discontinuity resets the clock. The internet never grew in any given year at an insane rate. It was incredibly durable: roughly 30% a year, compounding. At any given moment it did not feel transformative. Each year looked similar to the last. Over decades, the cumulative effect was revolutionary. Betting on the internet in 1995 did not require believing in sudden explosive growth. It required believing in sustained moderate growth over a long period, without interruption. That bet turned out to be correct. Stripe is the same shape. We are not a business of discontinuities. Everything starts very small and compounds for a long time. The trajectory looks like a clean exponential on a chart, not because any given period is extraordinary but because we are not interrupting the compounding.

This connects to the idea of zero billion dollar markets. Jensen Huang has said Nvidia much prefers zero billion dollar markets to ten billion dollar markets. When they introduced CUDA, they entered a market that did not exist. There was no measurable TAM for GPU computing. Now it is one of the most valuable technologies in the world. The logic is the same logic. A ten billion dollar market is optimized for current conditions. It has incumbents who understand it. The rules of competition are established. A zero billion dollar market has no incumbents because it does not yet exist. The rules are not set because the game has not been played. That is betting on change in its purest form. When Stripe started, e-commerce was perhaps 2% of consumer spending. The market for "making internet commerce dramatically easier" had no measurable size because the concept barely existed. We were not competing for existing payment processing business; we were enabling businesses that could not easily have existed before. The market grew as we enabled more of it.

## How to Apply

1. **State, out loud, what your plan assumes will stay the same.** Make the implicit bet on stasis explicit. Every strategy is betting on something; write down what. If the list of assumed-stable things is long, you are betting heavily on stasis, whether you intended to or not.
2. **Work backward from a specific future year.** Pick a year five to ten out. Write down what would need to be true for you to have won. Derive today's decisions from that, not from this quarter.
3. **Distinguish directional bets from timing bets.** Directions — internet penetration rising, emerging markets growing, talent decentralizing, software eating more industries — are robust. Timing is not. Bet on directions; do not anchor on specific years for specific events.
4. **Do not interrupt compounding without a real reason.** Every pivot resets the clock. Tactical adaptation is fine; strategic discontinuity is expensive. Most "new direction" announcements are worse than sticking with the old direction and adapting execution.
5. **Be willing to look wrong in the short term.** If you are positioning for a world that has not arrived, your current position may look suboptimal relative to competitors who are optimizing for today. That is fine. Strategy is about being positioned for the world as it will be.
6. **Remember that stasis is also a bet.** Failing to position for change is not neutral; it is an active wager that nothing important will happen. That wager is usually wrong. The only question is when.

## Examples

**Situation:** A payments founder in 2010 is told by investors that certain large companies — Amazon among them — will never be customers. The current analysis is airtight.
**Application:** The current analysis is correct about the current world. It is wrong about the world a decade later, because internet commerce is going to grow, sophistication is going to increase, and constraints that look permanent now will erode. Build for the world where the "impossible" customer becomes plausible, without betting the company on when specifically that happens.
**Result:** Years later, the impossible customer becomes a major partner, and the company benefits from infrastructure it built for that future rather than having to retrofit.

**Situation:** A team is evaluating whether to make their product work equally well in emerging-market geographies where current spending is much smaller than in the US.
**Application:** Current market size is the wrong question. Internet penetration in emerging markets will continue to rise. Mobile-first commerce will dominate in more countries. Payment infrastructure will become more sophisticated. Build for the internet as it will be, not the internet as it is. The investment looks suboptimal against today's revenue map; it is correctly sized for the revenue map a decade from now.
**Result:** When the change arrives, the product is already positioned. Competitors who optimized for current geography distribution have to rebuild from scratch.

**Situation:** A board is pushing for a strategic pivot because growth, while durable, is "not extraordinary" quarter to quarter.
**Application:** 30% a year compounding for a long time is extraordinary. It just does not look extraordinary in any single period. The right question is not "is this quarter impressive?" but "are we interrupting the compounding for a good reason?" If there is no good reason, holding direction is the aggressive move, not the cautious one.
**Result:** The team resists the pivot, continues to compound, and the cumulative outcome over five years dwarfs anything the proposed pivot would have produced.

**Situation:** A founder is looking at a market with zero current revenue — there is no TAM analysis, no comparables, no established incumbents — and the team is nervous.
**Application:** That is the feature, not the bug. A zero billion dollar market has no incumbents because it does not yet exist. The rules are not set because the game has not been played. The question is not "how big is this market today?" but "what trends make this market inevitable, and how long will it take?" If the directional bet is robust, the absence of a current TAM is an invitation, not a disqualification.
**Result:** The team enters, gets to define the category, and benefits from years of compounding inside a market they helped create — the market grows as they enable more of it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "betting on change" with "pivoting constantly."
**Why:** Real bets on change are expressed through stable positioning that holds for a decade. If you are rewriting strategy every year, you are not betting on change — you are interrupting compounding. 1.3 to the N is an enormous number, but only if N is allowed to grow.

**Don't:** Bet on specific events at specific times.
**Why:** Timing predictions are usually wrong. Directional predictions — that penetration will rise, that more industries will get eaten by software, that talent will decentralize — are robust. If your plan depends on a specific event landing in a specific quarter, it is a forecast, not a strategy, and it will probably break.

**Don't:** Treat zero TAM as a red flag.
**Why:** A zero billion dollar market has no incumbents because it does not yet exist. That is where the largest opportunities are. The markets with visible profit pools are the ones optimized for current conditions, with established rules of competition. The opportunities with the most headroom are invisible in today's data — where enabling infrastructure does not exist, where the technology is not mature, where the regulatory environment has not caught up. Those are the zero billion dollar markets, and they will not show up in any TAM analysis.

**Don't:** Let the present-day competitive landscape define what is possible.
**Why:** The world of a decade from now will not look like today. Things that seem impossible now will become possible. Markets that do not exist will emerge. Constraints that seem permanent will evaporate. The companies that succeed are the ones positioned for that world before it arrived — not through prediction of specifics, but through understanding of direction. Stasis is also a bet. It is usually the wrong one.
