# What Remains Undone

## When to Use
- You are asking what is actually worth working on, and the answer keeps defaulting to whatever the people around you are already working on.
- You are staring at an obvious gap in the world and telling yourself that surely someone must be handling it.
- You are deciding whether the marginal return to your talent, applied to your current problem, is really higher than the marginal return to that talent applied elsewhere.
- You are trying to understand why important problems — disease eradication, housing in productive cities, fundamental scientific infrastructure — remain persistently under-resourced.

## Fails When
- The gap has a real structural reason that defeated competent predecessors. If you skip the investigation step and charge in, you will hit the same walls they did.
- The framework becomes vague moralism — a way of feeling virtuous about other people's choices without doing the hard, specific work of building anything.
- It is used to shame people working on legitimate technology problems. Not everyone has to be a forcing function on the world's largest absences. Plenty of good work happens inside well-resourced fields.

## Core Concept

There is a thing I keep noticing about the world that took me a long time to accept, and it runs against almost every assumption I inherited from growing up in rural Ireland. The assumption was that in the important places — MIT, Silicon Valley, the governments, the great laboratories — there must be really enlightened people making really enlightened decisions, quietly handling the important problems. The reality, which you learn slowly by approaching those places one at a time, is that there is nobody behind the curtain. The important problems are not being handled by enlightened authorities. They are, in a great many cases, not being handled at all. Things that are extraordinary and possible are simply not happening, not because the technical knowledge is missing, not because regulators have forbidden it, not because some hidden force stands in the way, but because no one has gotten around to doing them.

Before we started Stripe, I spent weeks googling for a solution that, by every reasonable assumption, should have existed. I was not trying to invent a self-driving car. I was trying to charge a credit card on the internet. This was 2009. The web was old. E-commerce was old. Payments were central to the internet. And yet the experience of actually accepting money online was absurdly difficult — merchant accounts, gateways, contracts, weeks of integration work for a problem that was supposed to have been solved years earlier. I kept assuming I must be missing something, that competent people surely had noticed this obvious gap and built the obvious solution. They had not. The solution did not exist. When I looked into why, the answer was not regulatory, not technical, not economic. It was just that nobody had done it. This was formative. It is not a story about payments. It is a story about how you calibrate the difference between what the world looks like and what it should look like, and how wide that gap actually is when you look closely.

Having seen the shape of it once, I started seeing it everywhere. Science funding is the glaring example. The grant system we have now — long applications, multi-month review cycles, peer committees optimising for incremental safety — was not handed down as the only possible structure. It was constructed by specific people after the Second World War, and it has since become, in significant ways, an impediment to the progress it was designed to enable. Fast Grants during COVID committed funds in 48 hours and produced important work that would not have happened otherwise. The question is not whether responsive funding is possible; we proved it is. The question is why it is not the norm, and the answer is structural: a small number of very large funders become risk-averse almost by necessity, because each decision is scrutinised and failure is visible and embarrassing. Pre-war science funding was far more diversified — many funders with many theories about what mattered — and we should return to something closer to that pattern.

The same shape repeats. Housing in productive cities: the technical problem is solved, we have been building housing for thousands of years; what is missing is political will against restrictive zoning, slow permitting, and structures that favour current residents over potential future ones. Diseases: millions of people die annually from conditions that, with serious research investment, could probably be addressed, but the incentives do not align — common conditions that are not fatal do not get funded because pharma ROI is weak, and neglected tropical diseases do not get funded because the addressable markets are poor. These are what you might call zero billion dollar markets — enormous in human terms, invisible in spreadsheet terms, and therefore under-attended. The problems are not intractable in the technical sense. We are simply choosing not to work on them.

This brings me to the misallocation of talent, which is the part of the argument that tends to land uncomfortably. Silicon Valley is one of the greatest concentrations of technical ability in human history — brilliant people, good resources, strong networks. I am not going to pretend the work happening there is unimportant; Stripe itself is part of it, and I believe what we do matters. But the marginal return to one more exceptional engineer at a FAANG company is, honestly, low. Those companies already have thousands of exceptional engineers. One more produces some value, but the increment is small. Meanwhile, there are problems that lack any champions at all — fields where a single talented person could make an enormous difference, because no one else is there. The marginal impact of talent applied to a well-resourced problem is lower, by orders of magnitude, than the marginal impact of that same talent applied to a problem that is obvious and neglected. The counterfactual GDP of you moving from one more AI research lab to one under-attended problem — disease eradication, a new scientific funding institution, serious infrastructure for a field that lacks it — is not close. It is a large, unflattering difference.

The Herb Boyer path is worth remembering in this context. Boyer spent decades building deep expertise in recombinant DNA, working in relative obscurity, before co-founding Genentech and transforming medicine. His route looks nothing like the dropout-founder mythology. He did not have a brilliant idea at twenty and execute on it. He accumulated domain depth and then applied it to a problem that actually mattered. That path produces more value over time than the celebrated alternative, and it is far more available to far more people than the mythology suggests.

I have come to think of this in terms of forcing functions. A forcing function is something that makes things happen that would not otherwise happen, or happen faster than they would otherwise. Stripe is a forcing function for internet commerce — much of what runs on it would eventually have existed through some mechanism, but by making it easier and faster to start businesses online we accelerated something that would have taken considerably longer. Fast Grants was a forcing function for COVID research. Arc Institute is trying to be a forcing function for biomedical research in a structure that is not the NIH. The question I would put to anyone reading this is simple: what is your forcing function? What are you going to make happen that would not otherwise happen? Not everyone has to answer this. But if you have the capacity and the inclination, the opportunities are everywhere, and the default — drifting along whatever career path is already laid out — is quietly a choice to let what is undone remain so.

I want to end with the optimism, because the reasoning above can read as bleak and it is not intended to be. I am fundamentally optimistic, and the optimism follows from the observation itself. If the reason important things are not happening is just that no one has done them yet, then the world is much more tractable than it looks. Individual action matters. Institutions can be built, or rebuilt. Funding mechanisms can be redesigned. Diseases can be tackled. Houses can be built. The alternatives to optimism — resignation, cynicism, despair — assume the current state is fixed, and that is empirically false. Things change when people make them change. There is no one behind the curtain. The important problems are waiting for someone to handle them. Maybe that someone is you.

## How to Apply
1. Notice the obvious absence. Look at the world not as it is but as it should be, and find one specific thing that should exist and does not. Not a general complaint — a specific missing thing, stated in one sentence.
2. Assume competent people have tried and investigate why they failed. Regulatory barriers, technical impossibilities, economic dynamics, political coalitions. Rule out the hidden forces before you act. If the barriers are fundamental, move on; if they are contingent, you have a real opportunity.
3. Estimate the counterfactual, not the absolute. The right question is not "is this problem important?" but "how much would the world differ if I specifically worked on this rather than on my current path?" Well-resourced problems have low counterfactual returns almost by definition. Zero billion dollar markets have high ones.
4. Consider the Herb Boyer path seriously. Deep domain expertise applied to a neglected problem is an under-celebrated, over-performing career shape. It does not look like the dropout mythology. It works.
5. Define your forcing function in concrete terms. Not "I care about X" — that is not a forcing function. A forcing function is "I will make this specific thing exist, or exist faster, by this specific action, starting now." If you cannot phrase it that way, you do not yet have one.

## Examples

**Situation:** A talented engineer is deciding between staying at a well-resourced AI lab and joining a small team trying to build a new kind of research funding institution. The AI lab pays more, has prestige, and works on fascinating problems. The research funding work pays less and looks, from the outside, less interesting.

**Application:** Ask the counterfactual question. The AI lab has thousands of excellent engineers; your absence would be approximately invisible. The research funding institution has roughly zero other people with your particular combination of skills. The absolute importance of AI is not the right frame; the marginal impact of you on AI is. Zero billion dollar markets — the under-resourced corners of the world — are precisely where one person's work goes furthest, because there is no surrounding machinery doing the work already.

**Result:** The engineer joins the funding institution. Five years later, the structure they helped design has redirected meaningful capital into research that the NIH-NSF pattern would have filtered out. Some of that research produces real outcomes. No one ever runs the controlled experiment, but the counterfactual GDP is large and legible to anyone who looks.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Conclude that an obvious gap must be unfillable because otherwise someone would already have filled it. Do not skip the investigation step.

**Why:** The whole argument is that "surely someone would have done it" is empirically false in a great many cases. There is nobody behind the curtain. Using the persistence of the gap as evidence for its impossibility converts an actionable observation into a reason for inaction. Investigate the real barriers specifically. If they are genuinely fundamental, fine — walk away. If they are contingent, which they often are, then the gap is precisely the opportunity, and the fact that it has persisted is part of what makes it yours.

**Don't:** Treat "work on what remains undone" as a general moral exhortation rather than a specific research and career question.

**Why:** Moralising about other people's career choices produces nothing. It is not a forcing function; it is a way of feeling virtuous about other people's time. The useful form of this frame is always specific: one identified absence, one investigation of why it persists, one concrete move by one specific person. If it cannot be reduced to that, it has not landed. The point is not to make everyone leave their jobs. The point is to notice, honestly, that many of the most important problems lack any champion, and to let that observation do real work in the one life where you actually get to decide what to do next.
