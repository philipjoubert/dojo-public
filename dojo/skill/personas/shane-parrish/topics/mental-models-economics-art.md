# Mental Models — Economics and Art (Vol 4)

## How to Use This File

This is the catalog for Volume 4 of the latticework — the youngest, least time-tested layer Shane works with, but also the layer most directly relevant to almost every decision you'll make in a day. Economics gives you the models for choice under constraint: what's scarce, what's being exchanged, what people give up when they pick one thing over another, and how reward structures quietly shape behavior under stated intentions. Most behavior is downstream of incentives. Most pricing is downstream of supply and demand. Most strategic mistakes are over-optimizations of one variable that quietly degrade the system around it. If you can hold these six econ models in working memory, a large fraction of business and life decisions become legible.

Art adds two epistemic models that catch what the econ ones miss. Framing and Representation are not about aesthetics — they're about how the lens shapes the seen. Every communication, every report, every map of a market is a frame: it includes some things and leaves others out. Every representation — a chart, a pitch, a quarterly summary, a piece of public reporting — is a choice about what stands in for the territory. Shane treats these the way an intelligence analyst does: not "is the picture pretty," but "what does the choice of picture tell me about who made it and why." Use these eight models together. The econ models tell you what's happening. The art models tell you why what you're seeing of it looks the way it does.

## When to Reach for an Economics or Art Model

Reach for an econ model when the situation involves allocation, exchange, choice under constraint, or value formation — pricing a product, designing compensation, evaluating a deal, deciding what to do next, diagnosing why people in an organization keep doing the thing nobody asked them to do. Reach for an art model when you're consuming or producing a representation — a pitch, a report, a news story, a competitor's annual letter — and you want to see the choices behind the surface. Don't reach for econ models when the situation is fundamentally about meaning, identity, or relationship. People do not optimize their marriages. Treating love as a utility function is exactly the kind of category error that gives the econ-brained a bad name. Use the model where its assumptions hold; switch buckets when they don't.

## Scarcity

**In one line:** Limited supply changes behavior — and perceived scarcity changes valuation as much as actual scarcity does.

**Core concept:** All economic problems are allocation problems. Resources are finite, wants are infinite, and every allocation of a scarce resource is a non-allocation everywhere else. That's the textbook version. Shane's extension matters more: what's actually scarce in a given situation is rarely what you first reach for. Money is the obvious one. Time, attention, trust, status, and reliable information are scarcer than money for most people who already have any. Naming the scarce resource correctly is most of the work — it tells you what's actually binding, and therefore what's actually valuable. Perceived scarcity is its own engine. Hermès could make more Birkin bags. They choose not to. The whole luxury sector runs on manufactured scarcity because the more rare something is, the more we want it. The same logic runs in reverse: experiencing scarcity in childhood — of money, of food, of safety — leaves behavioral fingerprints decades after the actual scarcity is gone. People who grew up in the Depression kept saving foil long after they could afford to throw it out. Scarcity also narrows attention. When the constraint is binding, the binding constraint is all you can think about, and important things outside the scarcity get neglected.

**When to use:** When someone says "we need more resources." When evaluating a market — what's actually rare and desirable here? When designing pricing. When you notice you've been making decisions that all run through one constraint that may not actually be the binding one anymore.

**How to apply:** Name the scarce resources explicitly. Which one is binding right now? What happens to the system if that constraint relaxes? What happens if it tightens? What would be most valuable to make less scarce — and what's the second-order effect of that abundance?

**Example:** Information was scarce before the printing press, and that scarcity shaped everything from medieval scholarship to the structure of the Church. The printing press collapsed information scarcity and the entire downstream — Reformation, scientific revolution, modern publishing — followed. Same playbook today: any time someone reduces information scarcity in a domain that's been hoarding it, the value capture is enormous.

**Fails when:** You assume the scarce resource is the obvious one (usually money) when it's actually attention, trust, or time. You treat artificial scarcity as if it's natural — and miss that someone is choosing it. You over-extend "everything is scarce" into a worldview where nothing has slack and every decision feels emergency-grade.

**Shane quote:** "Where there is scarcity of something, its price goes up. For a resource to have economic value, it must be both scarce and desirable."

## Supply and Demand

**In one line:** Price is the signal that balances how much people want something against how much of it exists — and both sides can be manipulated.

**Core concept:** The foundational mechanism of market allocation. When demand exceeds supply, price rises until quantity demanded falls or supply increases. When supply exceeds demand, price falls. The dance is dynamic, never really at equilibrium — supply and demand are actively stable, not statically so. The textbook curves assume no single agent can move the price; the real world routinely violates that assumption. Most people think about demand-side manipulation — marketing, advertising, status signals — because that's what they see. Supply-side manipulation is often more powerful and far less remarked-on: OPEC controlling oil supply, professional licensing controlling the supply of doctors and lawyers, luxury brands like Hermès deliberately under-producing to maintain price. A second insight Shane draws out: it's much easier to regulate supply than demand. You can ban the sale of a thing; you can't legislate that someone no longer want it. Where demand persists and supply is restricted, an underground market forms — every prohibition in history has demonstrated this. The third insight: demand is not always given. GM's invention of the model year created demand for new cars that didn't perform any better than the old ones. The frame "does it still work?" was replaced with "does it still look new?" — and a whole industry was built on that reframe.

**When to use:** Pricing strategy. Evaluating a market's attractiveness. Understanding why a price is what it is. Diagnosing why a regulation isn't working (usually: it's targeting the wrong side). Spotting opportunities where current supply or demand is being artificially held in place.

**How to apply:** Ask what the current supply-demand balance looks like and what would tip it. Can you influence supply, demand, or both? Where's the elasticity — how much does demand actually shift when price moves? If you're trying to change a behavior in a market, are you working the side you can actually move?

**Example:** Luxury goods: Hermès makes fewer than 100,000 Birkins a year and the buying process is famously difficult. The scarcity is the product. A version available at any quantity at the prevailing market price would not be a Birkin in any meaningful sense — the bag is the supply restriction.

**Fails when:** You treat supply or demand as fixed when both can move. You assume the curves are clean — in reality, substitutes, brand effects, and information asymmetries make most markets messier than the diagram. You under-weight how strongly demand can be created where it didn't exist (faster horses vs. cars) or how stubborn demand can be where you wish it weren't.

**Shane quote:** "Supply and demand are the push and pull determining availability and price. Their dance is never-ending."

## Optimization

**In one line:** Find the best allocation given your constraints — but know that what's optimal in one environment becomes a liability when the environment changes.

**Core concept:** Optimization is the process of finding the best solution within a defined set of constraints. Maximize utility subject to budget. Maximize profit subject to capacity. Maximize strength subject to weight. The first trap: most situations involve optimizing along multiple dimensions at once, and the trade-offs between dimensions are usually subjective. A company optimizes for profit, but also for employee satisfaction, brand, optionality, founder happiness — and there's no objective function that combines them. The second trap, which Shane treats as the more dangerous one: optimization is environment-specific. The species perfectly adapted to its niche is the one most vulnerable when the niche disappears. The company perfectly optimized for its current market is the one that can't pivot when the market shifts. There's a constant tension between executing for today and adapting for tomorrow — slack and optionality cost something now and pay off only sometimes, but without them you're an over-fitted local optimum waiting for the environment to move. The third trap: local vs. global maxima. A solution that's the best in its neighborhood may not be globally best, but reaching the global optimum often requires getting worse before you get better. The fourth, and the one Shane is most insistent on: just because you can measure something doesn't mean you should optimize it. We tend to optimize what we measure. Clock time made productivity legible — and gave us the concept of wasted time as a moral failing. Some of the most valuable things in life are intangible and resist optimization. Optimizing them anyway tends to hollow them out.

**When to use:** Resource allocation problems. When choosing between competing strategies. When a setup that used to work isn't working anymore (the environment moved). When you notice a local maximum and want to ask whether the global one is worth the dip.

**How to apply:** Define the objective function and the constraints explicitly. Notice if you're optimizing for one variable while quietly degrading three others. Ask periodically: have my constraints changed? Am I stuck on a local peak? Is what I'm optimizing what I actually want, or just what's easy to measure?

**Example:** Nintendo's foundation is gaming, and every attempt to optimize for adjacent markets — educational software, non-game uses of the NES — has run into the wall of what the customer base will accept the brand doing. The foundation is both the source of optimization power and the constraint on it.

**Fails when:** You over-optimize one variable and degrade the system around it. You optimize for the current environment at the cost of the optionality you'll need when it changes. You measure what's measurable and call it the goal. You apply optimization logic to relationships, identity, or meaning — domains where the framing is itself wrong.

**Shane quote:** "Optimization often works for you until it doesn't. It's like the student who writes the answer but doesn't show their work. Knowing when to use it, when to let it go, and when to avoid it entirely can give you a key advantage."

## Trade-offs and Opportunity Cost

**In one line:** Every yes is a no to something else — and the value of the best alternative you didn't pick is the real cost of any decision.

**Core concept:** Trade-offs are the mechanism: because resources are finite, choosing one thing forecloses others. Opportunity cost is the unit: the value of the best forgone alternative. The two are inseparable, but most people only see the trade-off they consciously made and miss the opportunity costs entirely. We see what we picked. We don't see what we lost by picking it. That asymmetry is why opportunity costs are systematically underweighted. The pattern is sharper than just money. Time is the form of opportunity cost most adults under-account for, because time on one thing is time not on every other thing — including sleep, exercise, family, learning, the relationships that compound, the health that compounds. Shane's specific extension: as your time becomes more valuable, the opportunity cost of any given use of it grows. Decisions that were efficient at 25 — assembling cheap furniture, taking the long commute to save rent, doing your own taxes — quietly stop being efficient at 45. The same hour costs more because the alternative uses of it are now worth more. Most mid-life "I have no time" complaints are unrepriced opportunity costs from a younger life. Trade-offs also hide in long timeframes: the chocolate bar today doesn't make you unhealthy, the chocolate bar every day does, and the trade-off only shows up after the consequences are already locked in. And trade-offs are what give the world its variety — if every house could be optimized along every dimension at once, all houses would be the same. The fact that they're not is evidence that real trade-offs were made.

**When to use:** Before any significant commitment of money, time, or strategic attention. When someone calls a plan "free" or "no-cost." When evaluating whether to keep doing something vs. start something new. Whenever a decision keeps feeling unsatisfying without you being able to name why — usually the opportunity cost is sitting just outside the frame.

**How to apply:** Name the best forgone alternative explicitly. Don't compare a choice to "doing nothing"; compare it to the next-best use of the same resources. Include time, attention, and relational costs alongside the financial ones. For major decisions, build the opportunity cost into the analysis upfront rather than letting it surface as regret later.

**Example:** A free evening: you can work on the startup or go to the movie. If you work, you miss the fun. If you go to the movie, you miss the progress. There's no version where the choice is free — only versions where you're honest about what you're giving up. The same logic scales. A company choosing to build a new product isn't just trading against "not building it" — it's trading against the specific improvements to existing products that would have been made instead.

**Fails when:** You over-extend it into paralysis, evaluating opportunity costs on every trivial decision until you can't move. You only count what's measurable and miss the relational and meaning costs. You treat opportunity cost as if it stays constant when in fact it's growing as you grow.

**Shane quote:** "Every yes is also a no to something else. The opportunity cost of a decision is the potential benefit of the best forgone alternative choice."

## Comparative Advantage / Specialization

**In one line:** Trade with whoever you have a comparative advantage over — even if they're better than you at everything — because what matters is opportunity cost, not absolute skill.

**Core concept:** Ricardo's insight, and one of the few non-obvious results in economics that is both true and useful. Trade and specialization are mutually beneficial even when one party is better at everything than the other. What matters is not who can do something well in absolute terms, but who can do it at lower opportunity cost. The classic version: a surgeon who happens to also be the fastest typist in her practice should still hire a typist, because her opportunity cost of typing — surgeries not performed — is enormous, while the typist's opportunity cost of typing is low. Adam Smith's pin factory said the same thing at the macro level: ten specialized workers can produce 48,000 pins a day where one untrained worker would struggle to make twenty. The output difference is not a difference in talent — it's a difference in the structure of who does what. Specialization is upstream of essentially every productivity gain in human history. Shane extends the model into team and personal life. Every team should be designed so each member specializes in their highest comparative contribution and trusts others to specialize in theirs. Mediocre generalism in five domains is almost always worse than excellence in one plus trust in four. Specialization has costs — boredom, alienation from the product of your work, vulnerability to coordination failures, dependence on others not failing — and the model warns about each. But the alternative, do-everything generalism, is usually a recipe for being mediocre at all of it. Munger: "Warren and I avoid doing anything that someone else at Berkshire can do better."

**When to use:** Deciding what to delegate. Building or restructuring a team. Choosing what to focus on personally. Evaluating whether to outsource or insource. Diagnosing where your time is being spent on things that someone else has lower opportunity cost on.

**How to apply:** For each task or domain, estimate your opportunity cost — what else would you be doing with that time? Specialize where your opportunity cost of *not* doing it is highest. Delegate where your opportunity cost is low relative to the alternative. Build teams that allow mutual specialization rather than redundant generalism. Be wary of taking on a task just because you can do it well; the question is whether your highest-value alternative is even higher.

**Example:** Hayley Wickenheiser on hockey: "I focused on offense. I knew my defensive responsibilities, but I didn't focus on what was required of goalies and defensemen." She didn't try to be the best at everything on the ice. She specialized where her comparative advantage was greatest and trusted teammates to do the same.

**Fails when:** You over-specialize so narrowly that you lose adaptability when the environment changes. You become dependent on others whose failure can cascade through your own work. You confuse comparative advantage (relative opportunity cost) with absolute advantage (raw skill) and end up doing things you're best at when you should be doing things you're most uniquely useful at.

**Shane quote:** "We're usually better off doing what we do best and working with someone else for the rest. We just have to be mindful of the vulnerabilities that specialization creates."

## Incentives

**In one line:** Show me the incentives and I'll show you the outcome — behavior follows reward structures, not stated intentions.

**Core concept:** Munger: "Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." And later: "I think I've been in the top five percent of my age cohort all my life in understanding the power of incentives, and all my life I've underestimated it. And never a year passes but I get some surprise that pushes my limit a little farther." If one of the most incentive-aware humans alive keeps being surprised by how powerful incentives are, you should assume you're underestimating them too. Incentives are anything that changes the perceived cost or benefit of a behavior. Financial incentives are the obvious ones, but rarely the only ones in play and frequently not the dominant ones. Status, belonging, narrative self-consistency (the need to feel like a good person who made the right call), fear of social punishment, fear of looking stupid — these are all incentives, and all of them routinely override stated values. The asymmetry that Shane keeps returning to: short-term incentives systematically override long-term ones, even when people consciously believe they care about the long term. Quarterly earnings warp ten-year strategy. Use-it-or-lose-it budgets produce year-end waste. Large executive comp packages tilted to short-term stock price produce executives who optimize for short-term stock price. None of this is mysterious. It's incentives doing exactly what incentives do. The model also predicts the corollary: if you want to change behavior, change the incentive structure, not the rules. Exhortation, training, and culture posters are weak forces against badly aligned reward structures. And every new incentive structure has a second-order signature — what unintended behaviors will this new system produce? Munger's other line, on the same theme: design incentive structures with a paranoid eye for what people will do to optimize them, because someone, somewhere, will.

**When to use:** Any time you're trying to change behavior — your own or someone else's. Any time stated goals and actual behavior don't match. Before designing any system, policy, compensation plan, or ruleset. When something obviously bad keeps happening and "people are stupid" or "people are evil" feels like the explanation — usually neither is true and the incentives are the explanation.

**How to apply:** Map the incentives. For each major actor, ask: what do they actually gain from the current behavior, and what would they gain from the desired behavior? If the desired behavior pays less in the relevant currency (money, status, belonging, narrative consistency), the desired behavior won't happen no matter how loudly you ask for it. To shift behavior, shift the payoff structure. Then run a second-order check: how will this new structure be gamed?

**Example:** Thalidomide and the perverse incentives of drug development. Scientists, marketers, and executives all had financial and reputational incentives to support the drug's safety narrative. Even after evidence of harm accumulated, narrative self-consistency — "I'm a good person who supported this drug, therefore the drug cannot be harmful" — kept the story alive. Frances Kelsey at the FDA had a different incentive structure. Her career depended on scientific rigor, not sales. She held the line.

**Fails when:** You treat "incentives explain everything" as a complete answer when culture, habit, environment, and ignorance are doing equal work. You assume incentives are only financial. You design a clever incentive scheme and fail to game out how it will be gamed. You explain everyone else's behavior with incentives and your own with values.

**Shane quote:** "Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." (Munger, the line Shane returns to most)

## Framing (from Art)

**In one line:** What's inside the frame — and what's left outside it — shapes how the audience sees the thing as much as the thing itself does.

**Core concept:** Every work of art is a frame. It includes some things and excludes others, and the frame itself is never neutral — it reflects the creator's intent, perspective, and context. Shane treats this as an epistemic model, not a marketing one. Every communication, argument, data presentation, news story, pitch deck, quarterly report, and historical account is a frame. The frame shapes interpretation as much as the content inside it does. Three things follow. First, framing is active. You can reframe a setback as a learning, a weakness as a context, a stalled launch as a controlled rollout — without changing any underlying fact, you change what the audience perceives. This is power, and like any power it can illuminate or mislead. Second, every frame leaves something out, and what's left out is rarely random. Ask "who drew this frame, and what's outside it?" of any compelling argument and you find the load-bearing edits. Historical frames are the cleanest example: the way Nur Jahan's reign was framed as a love story by male chroniclers — every commentator since has had to reach past that frame to recover what she actually did as a sovereign. Third, frames are mostly invisible to the people inside them. We inherit frames, they accumulate over a lifetime, and they shape what feels intuitive without ever announcing themselves. Cronkite's framing of Vietnam as a war the Americans were essentially right to be fighting was invisible to him until on-the-ground reporting forced him to update it — and his updated frame, broadcast on the *CBS Evening News*, moved a country. Framing's power lies in its subtlety; unlike a logical argument, a frame doesn't need to be explicitly stated to have its effect. It works on the emotional, often subconscious level. That's exactly why surfacing frames — your own and others' — is one of the highest-leverage epistemic moves available.

**When to use:** When consuming any curated information — news, pitches, arguments, data, briefings. When communicating, especially with stakes. When in conflict and a reframe could dissolve the disagreement without changing any underlying fact. When you notice a story everyone agrees on and want to ask what's been edited out to produce that consensus.

**How to apply:** Ask what's been left outside this frame. Ask who drew the frame and what they gain by drawing it that way. Ask what a person with different interests would frame differently. Then, if you're doing the framing, do it deliberately — name what you're including, what you're leaving out, and why.

**Example:** Fs.blog's tagline: "Mastering the best of what other people have already figured out." That's a frame for an enormous amount of content. The frame shapes what kind of writing belongs and how readers engage with it. A different frame — "a productivity blog" — would attract different readers, generate different content, and change the perception of value. The site is the same; the frame is the lever.

**Fails when:** You use framing as a euphemism for spin and forget there's still a territory underneath the map. You become so meta about frames that you can no longer act inside any of them. You spot other people's frames sharply and never notice your own.

**Shane quote:** "When people frame something, they're making a statement not about the objective truth but about themselves."

## Representation (from Art)

**In one line:** Every representation is a choice — what gets represented and how reveals what the maker values, fears, and intends.

**Core concept:** All visual art is representation of something, and representation is never random or neutral. What gets represented, and how, reflects what the creator (and their patron, and their culture) values, fears, and is willing to acknowledge. Egyptian art shows figures from their "most characteristic angle" simultaneously rather than from a single viewpoint — that's not a failure of perspective, it's a different theory of what art is for. A thousand years of Western painting is dominated by the Virgin Mary because the Catholic Church was for a thousand years one of the only institutions wealthy enough to commission art and large enough to display it. The chess queen's evolution from a weak vizier piece to the most powerful figure on the board mirrors the rise of female rulers in medieval Europe. Representation reflects the world that produced it. Shane's extension is the move that makes this model operational outside of art history: open-source intelligence (OSINT) uses representation explicitly. What a government, company, or individual chooses to represent publicly *is* intelligence. Society pages revealed troop locations during World War II. Concert cancellations and last-minute opera changes in the *Deutsche Allgemeine Zeitung* let an Allied analyst trace bombing damage in Berlin. The surface content of these publications was secondary; what they represented — and what they conspicuously stopped representing — was the signal. The same logic applies to a competitor's annual report, a politician's choice of where to give a speech, a startup's homepage. The question is never just "what does this say?" — it's "what does the choice to say this in this way, and not other things, reveal about who's saying it?" Liking and understanding are two different activities. You don't have to like a representation to learn from it. You do have to look past the surface and ask what choice it's making and why.

**When to use:** Consuming any curated or produced content — annual reports, press releases, public communications, media coverage, your own historical narratives about yourself. Evaluating a competitor. Trying to understand an organization's actual priorities (which often diverge sharply from its stated ones). Designing your own external communications and wanting to know what you're actually signaling.

**How to apply:** Ask what is being represented here, and why in this way. What's the purpose behind the representation? What patron, audience, or context shaped these choices? What's conspicuously absent? What does the choice — to lead with this metric, to bury that one, to pose for this photo, to omit that quarter — tell you about what the maker actually cares about?

**Example:** A startup's "we're hiring" page. The roles listed, the order they're listed in, the language used to describe the work, what's said about compensation and what isn't, who's pictured on the team page — all of it is representation, all of it is a choice, and all of it tells you more about what the company actually is than the marketing copy on the homepage does. Read the recruiting pages of any company you're evaluating. The signal density is high.

**Fails when:** You over-read and find intentional signals in noise. You treat every representation as adversarial when most are just unconsidered. You become a connoisseur of others' representations and a sloppy maker of your own.

**Shane quote:** "Representation is not just about mirroring reality; it's also about shaping it. The representations we create and consume have the power to influence how we think and act, to change the very world they purport to describe."
