# Positioning and Promise

## When to Use
- Before any copy is written for a new account, a new product, or a relaunch.
- When a brand has been advertised for years and still has no clear answer to *what it is and for whom*.
- When the team is stuck choosing between three messages and nobody has tested any of them on consumers.
- When the client keeps broadening the brief to include every possible buyer.

## Fails When
- **The execution is weak even with a sound positioning.** "A lot of today's campaigns are based on optimum positioning but are totally ineffective – because they are dull, or badly constructed, or ineptly written. If nobody reads your advertisement or looks at your commercial, it doesn't do you much good to have the right positioning." — *Creative Council Reports*, reproduced in the Ogilvy quotations
- **The positioning is changed every two years.** A brand that is repositioned to suit each incoming marketing director ends up with no position at all.
- **The positioning is chosen by taste.** The CEO's preference, the creative director's hunch, the focus-group moderator's favorite — none of these is how to pick. Consumers pick.
- **The promise is vague.** "We deliver a superior experience" is not a promise. *Dove creams your skin while you wash* is a promise.

## Core Concept

"The most important decision is how to position your product." — *How to Create Advertising That Sells*

Before you write a word of copy, before you cast a model, before you commission a photographer, you have to decide *what to say*. You have to decide what benefit you will promise, and to which consumer. Everything downstream — the headline, the body copy, the illustration, the media, the tone — flows out of that decision. The results of your campaign depend less on how the advertising is written than on how the product is positioned. That is why I call it more than half the battle, and why at the agency it was the first slide in my Magic Lantern.

My own definition of positioning is plain. It is "what the product does, and who it is for." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* I could have positioned Dove as a detergent bar for men with dirty hands; instead I chose to position it as a toilet bar for women with dry skin. This is still working 25 years later. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Same product, same formula, same factory. A different answer to the two questions *what* and *for whom* produces a different brand. In Norway, SAAB had no measurable profile. We positioned it as a car for winter. Three years later it was voted the best car for Norwegian winters. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The car did not change. The answer to *for whom, and for what* changed.

Positioning is the strategic decision. The promise is the executional one. Two hundred years ago Dr. Johnson said, "Promise, large promise is the soul of an advertisement." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* When he auctioned off the contents of the Anchor Brewery he made the following promise: *We are not here to sell boilers and vats, but the potentiality of growing rich beyond the dreams of avarice.* — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* That is a promise. It tells the consumer what benefit she will receive if she buys. And it pays to boil down your strategy to one simple promise and go the whole hog in delivering that promise. — *How to Create Advertising That Sells*

Most manufacturers resist this discipline. "Most manufacturers are reluctant to accept any limitation on the image of their brand. They want it to be all things to all people. They want their brand to be a male brand and a female brand. An upper-class brand and a plebeian brand. They generally end up with a brand which has no personality of any kind, a wishy-washy neuter. No capon ever rules the roost." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The job of the advertising agent is to hold the line. Choose a buyer. Choose a benefit. Promise that benefit with conviction. Deliver it with the craft of the rest of the campaign. The brands that do this grow; the ones that hedge become capons.

Never decide the promise by guesswork. The selection of the right promise is so vitally important that you should never rely on guesswork to decide it. At Ogilvy, Benson & Mather we used five research techniques to find out which is the most powerful. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Run the candidate promises past real consumers. Put them on cards, or on the package, or inside matched ads. Count the orders. The one that pulls most becomes the fulcrum of every ad you write.

## How to Apply

1. **Answer two questions, in writing.** What does the product do? Who is it for? If you cannot write both answers in a sentence each, you do not have a positioning yet.

2. **Discover, do not invent.** The positioning comes out of the product and the consumer, not out of the copywriter's imagination. Study what the product actually does — its ingredients, its performance, its facts. Study who actually uses it and why. Then pick the intersection that is most distinctive and most saleable.

3. **Test candidate promises against consumers before you write an ad.** At the agency we distributed batches of product to matched samples with a different promise on each package, then compared repeat-order rates. We ran pairs of advertisements in the same position in the same issue of a newspaper with an offer of a sample buried in the copy, and counted which pulled more. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* For Dove, "Creams Your Skin While You Wash" pulled 63 per cent more orders than the next best promise, and it has been the fulcrum of every Dove advertisement that has ever run.

4. **Write the promise in one line.** The line should be specific, concrete, and performable. "Dove creams your skin while you bathe" is a promise. "Dove makes you feel fresh" is a wish.

5. **Decide, then defend.** Once positioning and promise are chosen, do not let the next incoming marketing director re-open the question. "It takes uncommon guts to stick to one style in the face of all the pressures to 'come up with something new' every six months. It is tragically easy to be stampeded into change." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Golden rewards await the advertiser who has the brains to hold the line over a long period.

6. **Reject audience-broadening that dilutes the promise.** When the client asks that the ad "also appeal to men" or "also work for older consumers," check whether the broader audience invalidates the original promise. Usually it does, and the answer is no. A capon brand is the only possible outcome of trying to appeal to everyone.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience… I'm head over heels in Dove" (1957).
**Application:** The client wanted Dove positioned as a detergent bar for men with dirty hands. Consumer research killed that angle. The ingredient list said Dove was one-quarter cleansing cream, and testing turned "Dove creams your skin while you wash" into the winning promise — 63 per cent more pull than its nearest rival. Positioning: a toilet bar for women with dry skin. Promise: it creams your skin while you wash. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*; *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Dove became the number-one cleansing brand in the world. The same promise, in the same words, ran for decades. Every Dove commercial since 1955 has promised that "Dove doesn't dry your skin the way soap can." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Positioning and promise, chosen once on evidence, carried the brand for thirty years.

**Ad / Situation:** SAAB in Norway.
**Application:** "In Norway, the SAAB car had no measurable profile. We positioned it as a car for *winter*." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* No change to the car. A change only to the answer to *for whom, and for what*.
**Result:** Three years later SAAB was voted the best car for Norwegian winters. The positioning created the image, the sales, and the category leadership.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958).
**Application:** The car was positioned not as the fastest, not as the prettiest, but as the best-engineered motorcar in the world — a position supported by fact-heavy long copy. The promise was silence and engineering specificity.
**Result:** Sales rose 50%. The positioning held through nine different headline angles and more than a decade of subsequent ads: the same 17-fact body copy was reused under headline after headline because the underlying positioning was stable.

**Ad / Situation:** Schweppes in America, featuring Commander Whitehead (1953 onward).
**Application:** A tonic water entering the U.S. with no awareness, on a peppercorn budget. The positioning was high-class quinine water — the drink of discrimination. The promise was authentic Britishness and curatorial quality, delivered through the face of the client, Commander Edward Whitehead, as the symbol of his own product.
**Result:** The campaign ran for eighteen years. Schweppes became the tonic of choice for people who wished to signal taste. Positioning chose the buyer; promise — Britishness delivered through a real commander with a real beard — carried the execution.

**Ad / Situation:** Volkswagen Beetle in the U.S. (Doyle Dane Bernbach, credited by Ogilvy as a masterpiece of positioning).
**Application:** "To advertise a car that looked like an orthopedic boot would have defeated me. But Bill Bernbach and his merry men positioned Volkswagen as a protest against the vulgarity of Detroit cars in those days, thereby making the Beetle a cult among those Americans who eschew conspicuous consumption." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** A physically unpromising car became a cult. The lesson is that positioning can turn a liability into the whole proposition — *for whom* does the consumer in question want a car that rejects Detroit?

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Position a brand as "premium and aspirational" and leave it there.
**Why:** That is not a positioning. It names a price tier and a feeling. A positioning answers *what does the product do* and *for whom*. Without both answers you have a mood board, not a strategy.

**Don't:** Try to be all things to all people.
**Why:** "They generally end up with a brand which has no personality of any kind, a wishy-washy neuter. No capon ever rules the roost." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Choose a buyer or forfeit the brand.

**Don't:** Let the CEO pick the promise based on taste.
**Why:** The promise is a bet with millions of dollars behind it. Guess wrong and the campaign fails no matter how well it is written. Test the candidate promises against real consumers and let the count of orders, or of sample requests, or of repeat purchases, decide.

**Don't:** Change the positioning every time a new marketing director arrives.
**Why:** Positioning compounds. A decade of consistent positioning builds a brand; a decade of quarterly repositioning builds nothing. Every new positioning forfeits the accumulated meaning of the old one.

**Don't:** Confuse the promise with the slogan.
**Why:** The promise is the substantive benefit ("creams your skin while you wash"). The slogan is how that benefit is summarized on the page. A brilliant slogan over a weak promise is lipstick on a capon.

**Don't:** Write the ad and then reverse-engineer the positioning.
**Why:** The positioning dictates the ad, not the other way around. When the order is reversed, the ad will be charming and off-strategy, and it will sell the wrong thing brilliantly.

**Don't:** Accept "we can't define the buyer narrowly — we'll lose volume."
**Why:** The Dove bar positioned for women with dry skin sells far more than it would if positioned for everyone with any skin. The narrow position is what gives the buyer a reason to choose you. Broadening the aim does not broaden the sales; it softens them.

**Don't:** Re-open the positioning question every meeting.
**Why:** Agencies and clients that argue strategy forever never produce advertising. Decide the positioning, write it down, get both sides to sign it, and protect it from being reopened by anyone who was not in the original decision.
