# Brand Image — The Long Investment

## When to Use
- At the beginning of a brand, to decide what image will be built; and at every decision thereafter, to decide whether the action adds a brick or kicks one out.
- When a working campaign is under pressure to change — from a new marketing director, a new agency, a bored client, or a fashionable trend.
- When weighing a tactical gain (this quarter's promotion, this month's stunt) against its cost to the ten-year image.
- When a brand has drifted and needs not a reinvention but a return to the original image.

## Fails When
- **The existing image is provably wrong.** If research shows the brand has acquired a bargain-basement image in a premium category, or a male image in a female category, stability is not a virtue. The image has to be changed — and the change is one of the most difficult operations the agency will ever perform.
- **The product has genuinely changed.** A new technology, a new formulation, a new use — if the brand image no longer reflects what the product does, the image is misrepresenting the product.
- **"Consistency" is defending dull work.** "A lot of today's campaigns are based on optimum positioning but are totally ineffective – because they are dull, or badly constructed, or ineptly written. If nobody reads your advertisement or looks at your commercial, it doesn't do you much good to have the right positioning." — *Creative Council Reports*, reproduced in the Ogilvy quotations Image without craft is image without readers.
- **There is no image yet.** A brand in its first year does not have an image to preserve. It has an image to build. The work is creation, not maintenance.

## Core Concept

"Every advertisement must contribute to the complex symbol which is the brand image." — *How to Create Advertising That Sells*

"Every advertisement should be thought of as a contribution to the complex symbol which is the brand image. If you take that long view, a great many day-to-day problems solve themselves." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* That is the decisive sentence. Almost every difficult argument inside an agency — should we run this stunt, should we chase this trend, should we do this tactical promotion, should we hire this spokeswoman — resolves itself the moment you ask whether the action contributes to the image of the brand over ten years. If it does not, the answer is no, however tempting it looks in the week.

Image means personality. Products, like people, have personalities, and they can make or break them in the market place. The personality of a product is an amalgam of many things — its name, its packaging, its price, the style of its advertising, and, above all, the nature of the product itself. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Consumers are not choosing between whiskies; they are choosing between images of whiskies. Give people a taste of Old Crow and tell them it's Old Crow. Then give them another taste of Old Crow but tell them it's Jack Daniel's. Ask them which they prefer. They'll think the two drinks are quite different. They are tasting images. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The brand image is 90 per cent of what the distiller has to sell.

The enemy of brand image is the impulse to change. Most manufacturers are reluctant to accept any limitation on the image of their brand. They want it to be all things to all people. They want their brand to be a male brand and a female brand. An upper-class brand and a plebeian brand. They generally end up with a brand which has no personality of any kind, a wishy-washy neuter. No capon ever rules the roost. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* And even when the brand has been given a clear personality, pressure arrives to scramble it. A new Marketing Director wants to make his mark. A new agency wants to bring a new look. A trend demands the brand participate. "It takes uncommon guts to stick to one style in the face of all the pressures to 'come up with something new' every six months. It is tragically easy to be stampeded into change. But golden rewards await the advertiser who has the brains to create a coherent image, and the stability to stick with it over a long period." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

The brands that grow rich in this discipline are the ones whose advertising in 1975 and 1965 and 1955 share tone, manner, and promise. Every Dove commercial since 1955 has promised that "Dove doesn't dry your skin the way soap can." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Leo Burnett's cowboy campaign for Marlboro has been running almost without change for twenty-five years — the image took the brand from obscurity to the biggest-selling cigarette in the world. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The Hathaway eye patch first appeared in 1951 and is still going strong. For eighteen years I used the face of my client Commander Whitehead as the symbol of his own product. It worked to beat the band on a peppercorn budget. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* These are the superstars — "the campaigns that go right on producing results through boom and recession, against shifting competitive pressures, and changes of personnel." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The long view also forbids the day-to-day mistake of cheap advertising. "It pays to give most products an image of quality — *a First Class ticket.* This is particularly true of products whose brand-name is visible to your friends, like beer, cigarettes and automobiles: products you 'wear.' If your advertising looks cheap or shoddy, it will rub off on your product. Who wants to be seen using shoddy products?" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* An ugly layout suggests an ugly product. Most products benefit from being given a first-class ticket through life, provided the taste is worn unobtrusively.

## How to Apply

1. **Decide what image to build — and write it down.** Personality, tone, buyer, manner. What this brand is, what it is not, how it speaks, how it looks, whom it is for. If the decision is only in the agency's head, it will not survive the next personnel change.

2. **Ask of every advertisement: does this add a brick to the image, or kick one out?** That is the test. Day-to-day problems — whether to chase a trend, whether to use a new visual style, whether to hire a new spokesperson — resolve themselves when the question is posed clearly.

3. **Choose consistency across every element, not only the copy.** "The personality of a product is an amalgam of many things – its name, its packaging, its price, the style of its advertising, and, above all, the nature of the product itself." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* If the ads are premium and the packaging is cheap, the consumer reads the packaging.

4. **Resist the pressures to reinvent.** New marketing directors, new agencies, and trend-chasers will arrive regularly. Have an answer ready: the campaign is still selling; the research says to run it; we are advertising to a moving parade, not a standing army. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The argument for change should have to clear a high bar.

5. **Repeat winners.** "If you are lucky enough to write a good advertisement, repeat it until it stops selling. Scores of good advertisements have been discarded before they lost their potency." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Measure the selling effectiveness at regular intervals; stop when research says stop, not when the client says he is tired of it.

6. **Use a First Class ticket — but wear it unobtrusively.** The image of quality should come from the layout, the paper, the typography, the photograph, the lack of clutter. Shoddy advertising gives a shoddy image however premium the actual product.

7. **When you genuinely must change the image, expect it to be hard.** Changing a faulty image is one of the most difficult operations advertising is called upon to perform, because the faulty image has been built up over many years by many factors — advertising, pricing, name, packaging, television shows sponsored, length of time on market. "In many cases it would be easier to start again, with a fresh new brand." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Do it only when the evidence is clear; do not redecorate for fashion.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway shirts, "The man in the Hathaway shirt" (first run 1951, continued for more than two decades).
**Application:** A single visual device — the model's black eye patch, chosen after testing eighteen story-appeal ideas — carried the image of quiet aristocracy. Later executions swapped the patch for a Purdey shotgun, the same move with a different prop; the tone, layout, and brand personality stayed the same. Every ad added a brick.
**Result:** A 116-year-old shirt company, unknown before the campaign, became a household brand. The Hathaway eye patch first appeared in 1951 and is still going strong. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The cumulative image was worth far more than any single ad in it.

**Ad / Situation:** Schweppes with Commander Whitehead (1953 onward, 18 years).
**Application:** "For 18 years I used the face of my client Commander Whitehead as the symbol of his own product. It worked to beat the band on a peppercorn budget." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Same face, same beard, same voice, same curatorial Britishness, year after year. No new spokesperson, no reinvention.
**Result:** Schweppes became the tonic of discrimination in the U.S. on a small budget. Commander Whitehead became a talk-show regular — "manna from heaven, but nobody knows how to do it on purpose." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The image compounded because nothing was changed for the sake of change.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove soap, one-quarter cleansing cream (1955 onward, decades).
**Application:** The positioning — a toilet bar for women with dry skin — and the promise — "Dove creams your skin while you wash" — were chosen on research once, and then kept. Every Dove commercial since 1955 has promised that "Dove doesn't dry your skin the way soap can." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Voice and claim, unchanged across decades of executions.
**Result:** Dove became the number-one cleansing brand in the world. The compounding was the whole strategy.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, the 1958–1962 campaign and its descendants.
**Application:** Nine different headlines across several years, but one tone (restrained, factual), one typography, one copy register (no adjectives, no "gracious living"), and largely the same seventeen-fact body copy recycled under new angles. The headline that repositioned the car as "designed to be owner-driven. No chauffeur required" extended the image without contradicting it; the $13,550 price-led ad sat inside the same register as the electric-clock headline.
**Result:** A coherent image of best-engineered, understated luxury, built ad by ad. The brand in 1962 was the 1958 brand, deepened.

**Ad / Situation:** Marlboro, the Leo Burnett cowboy (cited by Ogilvy as the canonical example).
**Application:** "Leo Burnett's campaign for Marlboro projects an image which has made it the biggest-selling cigarette in the world. It has been running, almost without change, for 25 years." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** From obscurity to global leadership. The lesson Ogilvy draws: "Next time an apostle of hard-sell questions the importance of brand images, ask him how Marlboro climbed from obscurity to become the biggest-selling cigarette in the world." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Ad / Situation:** Zippo, the "It Still Works" ad compilations (1961 and 1964).
**Application:** A rolling series of real durability stories — a lighter that worked after being recovered from the belly of a fish, lighters returned from wartime service — all resolving to the same refrain: *it still works*. The 1964 execution tightened the layout and added more examples per page, but did not reinvent the concept.
**Result:** A single concept refreshed indefinitely by new instances. The quiet discipline is in *not* abandoning a working idea — just tuning it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reinvent the brand every three years to look fresh.
**Why:** A decade of four reinventions is a decade of nothing. "Ninety-five per cent of all the campaigns now in circulation are being created without any reference to such long-term considerations. They are being created ad hoc. Hence the lack of any consistent image from one year to another." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Each reinvention forfeits what the previous image accumulated.

**Don't:** Let a new marketing director put her stamp on the work.
**Why:** "There are always forces at work to change the advertising – like a new agency, or a new Marketing Director who wants to make his mark." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Personnel change is not a reason to change the brand. The brand is older than the marketing director and will outlast her tenure.

**Don't:** Change agencies to get a new look.
**Why:** The new agency will bring its own voice, its own visual style, its own fashions — and the image that compounded under the old agency will start over from zero. Change agencies only when craft has failed, not when the brand is bored.

**Don't:** Chase trends that have nothing to do with the brand.
**Why:** Every off-brand stunt adds a brick that does not fit. Trend-chasing is how brands with strong images become brands with no image over five years of tactics.

**Don't:** Let committee-authored brand guidelines replace a defensible image.
**Why:** Committees can criticize, but they cannot create. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A brand book written by twelve people to offend no one is a brand book that no one will defend when the next marketing director arrives. The image has to be defensible in a single line by someone who cares about it.

**Don't:** Dress a premium product in shoddy advertising.
**Why:** "If your advertising looks cheap or shoddy, it will rub off on your product. Who wants to be seen using shoddy products?" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A first-class ticket is one of the cheapest investments in image available, and failing to pay it contradicts the entire brand strategy.

**Don't:** Confuse variety for life.
**Why:** Different spokespeople, different tones, different visual systems month by month do not signal a vital brand; they signal a directionless one. One face, one voice, one look, sustained — that is what the consumer remembers. "A lot of advertisers have the delusion that they can outsmart the law of averages by changing the personality of their product every year." The result is the wishy-washy neuter; no capon ever rules the roost. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Don't:** Stop running a campaign because you, the client, are tired of it.
**Why:** "You aren't advertising to a standing army; you are advertising to a moving parade." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The consumer is not tired. New consumers enter the market every year. Kill the ad only when research says it has stopped selling, not when the account team has watched it too many times.

**Don't:** Treat image and short-term response as opposites.
**Why:** They are the same long investment. Every direct-response ad that runs also contributes to the brand's image — tone, typography, manner — whether the agency intended it to or not. The promotions you run, the price-off deals you run, the stunts you approve all add or subtract bricks. Choose them with the long view in mind, or pay the image cost without meaning to.
