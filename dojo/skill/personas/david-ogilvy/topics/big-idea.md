# The Big Idea

## When to Use
- Drafting a new campaign for a product that will be advertised for years, not weeks.
- The positioning is settled but the advertising keeps passing like a ship in the night.
- The client has a parity product and every competitor has access to the same factory, the same research, the same claims.
- Reviewing a draft and trying to answer the honest question: is there a big idea here, or is there only a layout?

## Fails When
- **The strategy is wrong.** A big idea welded onto the wrong positioning will sell the wrong thing brilliantly. Fix the strategy first.
- **There is real news.** If the product is genuinely new or genuinely improved, announce it. News beats any cleverness you could invent. Do not dress up news as a riddle.
- **"Big idea" becomes a committee word.** Once everyone in the room is arguing about whether the idea is "big enough," the idea is usually dead. The tests are private, not parliamentary.
- **The idea is unique but irrelevant.** A gasp-worthy picture that has nothing to do with the product is worse than a boring ad; the reader remembers the gag and forgets the brand.

## Core Concept

"Unless your advertising contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

That is the whole business. You can spend three weeks on homework, you can test forty headlines, you can buy the best photographer in New York, and if there is no big idea at the center of it, your advertisement will not be seen, will not be remembered, and will not sell. I doubt that more than one campaign in a hundred contains a big idea. I am supposed to be one of the more fertile inventors of them, and in my long career as a copywriter I have not had more than twenty, if that. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

A big idea is not a clever line and it is not an original picture. It is the thing that lets one advertisement do the work of fifty. It is what makes Hathaway the shirt with the eye patch, Schweppes the drink of the bearded Commander, Marlboro the cigarette of the cowboy. It is a simple idea — "big ideas are usually simple ideas," as I wrote in *How to Create Advertising That Sells* — but a simple idea that is also strategic, also unique, also capable of carrying the brand for thirty years without being refreshed every time a new marketing director joins the client.

Big ideas come from the unconscious, but your unconscious has to be well informed or your idea will be irrelevant. Stuff your conscious mind with information — study the product, the research, the competition, the consumer — then unhook your rational thought process. Go for a long walk, take a hot bath, drink half a pint of claret. Suddenly, if the telephone line from your unconscious is open, a big idea wells up within you. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

And when it wells up, you must recognize it. That is the hard part. Albert Lasker, the most astute advertising man who ever lived, was asked what he thought was the best asset a man could have. He replied, "Humility in the presence of a good idea." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* I shudder to think how many good ideas I have rejected because they did not look like what I expected a good idea to look like. Research cannot help you here, because research cannot predict the cumulative value of an idea, and no idea is big unless it will work for thirty years.

The temptation, once you have a big idea, is to throw it away and come up with something new every six months. Resist it. "It takes uncommon guts to stick to one style in the face of all the pressures to 'come up with something new' every six months. It is tragically easy to be stampeded into change. But golden rewards await the advertiser who has the brains to create a coherent image, and the stability to stick with it over a long period." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The Hathaway eye patch ran for twenty-one years. Every Dove advertisement since 1955 has carried the same promise. Marlboro has been the cowboy for a quarter of a century. These are the superstars — the campaigns that go on producing results through boom and recession, against shifting competitive pressures, and changes of personnel.

## How to Apply

1. **Do the homework first.** Study the product. The more you know about it, the more likely you are to come up with a big idea for selling it. When I got the Rolls-Royce account, I spent three weeks reading about the car. When I got Mercedes, I sent a team to Stuttgart for three weeks of interviews with engineers. No homework, no idea.

2. **Stuff the conscious, then unhook it.** After the research, stop working. Walk. Bathe. Drink. Sleep on it. The unconscious does the final assembly, but only if the conscious mind has fed it the parts.

3. **Apply the five tests.** When a candidate idea arrives, ask yourself, in this order:
   - Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?
   - Do I wish I had thought of it myself?
   - Is it unique?
   - Does it fit the strategy to perfection?
   - Could it be used for thirty years? — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
4. **If it fails any test, keep looking.** An idea that gasps but doesn't fit the strategy is a joke. An idea that fits the strategy but doesn't gasp is a layout. An idea that is unique but exhausts itself in six months is a tactic, not a campaign.

5. **Once you have it, repeat it until it stops selling.** You are not advertising to a standing army; you are advertising to a moving parade. The advertisement that sold a refrigerator to couples who got married last year will sell one to couples who get married this year. "Get a good radar, and keep it sweeping." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

6. **Defend it against the client, the new agency, and the new marketing director.** The pressure to change a working idea is constant and comes from people who are bored with it. Clients are bored with their own advertising long before consumers are. Research the selling power of the campaign at intervals; run it until the research shows it has worn out — not until a man in a meeting has.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** The Hathaway shirt campaign, "The Man in the Hathaway Shirt" (1951).
**Application:** Hathaway was a 116-year-old shirt company nobody had heard of, with a $30,000 budget against Arrow's $2 million. I needed a miracle. Knowing from Harold Rudolph's research that photographs with "story appeal" draw attention far above average, I concocted eighteen different ways to inject this magic ingredient. The eighteenth was the eye patch. On the way to the studio I ducked into a drugstore and bought one for $1.50. — *Confessions* That was the big idea: an unexplained, aristocratic prop that made the reader ask *What's going on here?* and then read the copy to find out.
**Result:** Hathaway was put on the map after 116 years of obscurity. The campaign ran for twenty-one years. Scores of other manufacturers stole the device — I have seen five copies from Denmark alone. Passes all five tests: it made the room gasp, competitors wished they had thought of it, nobody had done it before, it fit the strategy of projecting quiet class, and it ran for two decades without tiring.

**Ad / Situation:** Schweppes tonic, featuring Commander Whitehead (1953 onward).
**Application:** Schweppes was entering the American market with no awareness. Instead of advertising the tonic, I used the bearded face of the client himself, Commander Edward Whitehead, as the symbol of his own product. "For 18 years I used the face of my client Commander Whitehead as the symbol of his own product. It worked to beat the band on a peppercorn budget." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The big idea was brand-as-personality — a real man with a real beard representing the Britishness and curatorial snobbery of the drink.
**Result:** Whitehead became a talk-show regular; the campaign ran for eighteen years on a small budget; Schweppes became the tonic of discrimination. This was the first brand-personality campaign of its kind, and most of what is now taught under "brand image" is a footnote to it.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove soap, "one-quarter cleansing cream" (1955 onward).
**Application:** The client arrived with a "neutral detergent" angle that bombed in consumer interviews. The big idea came from the product's own formula: *DOVE IS ONE-QUARTER CLEANSING CREAM — IT CREAMS YOUR SKIN WHILE YOU WASH.* The ingredient was always in the bar; nobody had thought to make it the headline. The execution — a woman in a bathtub on the telephone, purring "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience… I'm head over heels in Dove" — used the word *darling* because a psychologist had tested hundreds of words for emotional reaction and *darling* came out top. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Every Dove advertisement since 1955 has promised the same thing. Dove became the biggest-selling cleansing bar in the world. The ingredient-as-idea is the single most-copyable move in the corpus: find the fact the chemist takes for granted and make it the claim.

**Ad / Situation:** The Guinness Guide to Oysters (1950).
**Application:** First ad I wrote as head of my own agency. The idea: borrow interest for Guinness from the fascinating foods you drink it with. I immersed myself in a Yale biologist's book on shellfish, then structured the ad as a guide to nine varieties of oysters, each with its own caption. The ad looked like editorial content, not advertising.
**Result:** Readers wrote in asking for reprints and framed the ad on their walls. A whole series followed — game birds, cheeses — all built on the same borrowed-interest structure. The big idea here is that the most productive angle is sometimes not the product itself but the world the product lives in.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reinvent the campaign every six months because the client or the creative director is bored.
**Why:** The consumer is not bored; the consumer is a moving parade and is seeing your advertisement for the first time. Changing a working idea is one of the most expensive things in advertising. Scores of good advertisements have been discarded before they began to pay off.

**Don't:** Mistake an execution for an idea.
**Why:** A witty headline, a clever photograph, a catchy jingle — these can wrap a big idea and deliver it, but they are not the idea. If you strip the craft away and ask "what's underneath?" and there is nothing underneath, you have executions chasing each other. A big idea survives a change of illustrator.

**Don't:** Chase originality for its own sake.
**Why:** "Originality is the most dangerous word in advertising," said Rosser Reeves. Mozart said he never made the slightest effort to compose anything original. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* An idea is big because it sells for thirty years, not because nobody has done it before. The electric-clock headline was swiped from an old Pierce-Arrow ad; I have admitted this freely. The swipe worked because the fact was true of the Rolls-Royce.

**Don't:** Submit the candidate to a committee for approval.
**Why:** "Advertising seems to sell most when it is written by a solitary individual. He must study the product, the research, and the precedents. Then he must shut the door of his office and write the advertisements." — *Confessions* Committees can criticize; they cannot create. Run the five tests yourself, then show the idea only to people whose judgment you already trust.

**Don't:** Force a big idea on a product that genuinely has news to announce.
**Why:** News is the most reliable selling premise known. Ads with news are recalled by 22% more people than ads without. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* If your product actually is new, say so plainly. Save the big idea for the third campaign, when the news is old and you need a hook that will last.
