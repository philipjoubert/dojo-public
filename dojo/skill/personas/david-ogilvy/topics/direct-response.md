# Direct Response — The Secret Weapon

## When to Use
- Whenever the business has a measurable channel: coupon, mail order, telephone, inbound form. Every dollar spent can be counted against every order returned.
- As the training ground for every copywriter, before they are allowed to touch a general-advertising account.
- To settle arguments that would otherwise be won by the loudest opinion in the room — long copy versus short, one headline versus another, two-page spread versus single page.
- As the new-business weapon of an agency: the direct-mail shot that wins the account.

## Fails When
- **The business is unmeasurable.** If orders cannot be attributed to the ad, a response test is a guess dressed up as a number. Do not pretend.
- **Direct-response *tactics* are imported without the measurement *discipline*.** Long copy, coupons, specific numbers — none of these work in isolation. They work because they have been tested against alternatives, and only testing keeps the advertising improving.
- **The creative team treats direct response as tacky.** The work will lack the respect that makes good direct-response copy work. "I have been a voice crying in the wilderness, trying to persuade the advertising establishment to take direct mail more seriously and to stop treating its practitioners as noncommissioned officers." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
- **The test is run against too small a sample, too briefly, with too many variables changed at once.** You can only test one variable at a time. So you have to choose which to test. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## Core Concept

"Direct mail was my first love and secret weapon in the avalanche of new business acquisitions which made Ogilvy & Mather an instant success." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

Direct response is the only branch of advertising that tells the truth to the penny. "Advertisers who distribute their products in the normal way, through wholesalers and retailers, have great difficulty in isolating the results of their advertising from the other factors in their marketing mix, but direct-mail advertisers can measure the results of their mailings to the dollar. This makes it possible for them to test everything they do. *In direct mail, testing is the name of the game.*" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* That is the whole epistemology. A direct-response practitioner does not have opinions about what works. He has counts. And the counts, accumulated over decades by the mail-order fraternity — Scherman of the Book-of-the-Month Club, Vic Schwab, John Caples — are the most reliable body of knowledge in the advertising business.

This is why I say every copywriter should start his career by spending two years in direct response. "One glance at any campaign tells me whether its author has ever had that experience." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The discipline of the measured result calibrates the judgement for life. A writer who has been trained in direct response knows, in his bones, that long copy outpulls short copy when the stakes are high. He knows that the headline is the most important element. He knows that two-minute television commercials sell more than thirty-second ones. He knows these things not because they are fashionable but because the orders have been counted.

The chasm between the direct-response fraternity and the general-advertising establishment is wide, and it is mostly to the detriment of the latter. "General advertisers use *30-second* commercials. But the direct response fraternity have learned that it is more profitable to use *two-minute* commercials. Who, do you suppose, is more likely to be right? General advertisers broadcast their commercials in expensive *prime time*, when the audience is at its peak. But direct response advertisers have learned that they make more sales *late at night.* Who, do you suppose, is more likely to be right? In their magazine advertisements, general advertisers use *short* copy, but the direct response people invariably use *long* copy. Who, do you suppose, is more likely to be right?" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* "I am convinced that if all advertisers were to follow the example of their direct response brethren, they would get more sales per dollar." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The direct-response lessons are simple and durable. In print, the headline is the most important element; one headline can out-pull another by five times. Good photographs of the product outsell bad ones. Long copy sells more than short copy, particularly when you are asking the reader to spend a lot of money — only amateurs use short copy. Testimonials increase credibility and sales. Cross-heads give breathing space and carry the sales story to the skimmer. Coupons should be mini-ads, complete with brand name, promise, and a miniature photograph of the product. Readers often tell themselves they will mail the coupon later and never get around to it — so use "limited edition," "limited supply," "last time at this price," "special price for promptness" to keep the prospect on the hook. — *Ogilvy on Advertising, Chapter 12*

The first ad is only the start. "Once you have evolved a mailing which produces profitable results, treat it as the 'control' and start testing ways to beat it. Try adding a premium, or putting in an expiration date, or adding enclosures – like a personalized letter from your President." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The control-and-challenger discipline is what makes direct response compound over years: every winning test becomes the new floor, and the next round of tests tries to beat it.

## How to Apply

1. **Put a measurable response on every advertisement you can.** A coupon. A telephone number. A post office box. An offer that requires action. Without a response channel there is nothing to count, and the whole discipline collapses.

2. **Make the headline earn its keep.** "In print advertisements, your *headline* is the most important element. The other day I saw one headline produce five times as many orders as another. If your headline promises your strongest and most distinct benefit, you are on your way to success." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Name the benefit. Name the reader. Do not be clever at the expense of being clear.

3. **Write long, not short.** "Long copy sells more than short copy, particularly when you are asking the reader to spend a lot of money. Only amateurs use short copy." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* When Mercedes-Benz had 1,170 obsolete diesels to unload, a five-page letter sold them. For Cunard, an eight-page letter worked. The reader who is interested reads everything.

4. **Set up a control and beat it.** Every winning ad becomes the benchmark. Try one variable at a time against it: a different premium, a different price, a different offer, a different coupon design. Keep the loser. Rotate the winner in as the new control. "The more you test, the more profitable your direct mail will become." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

5. **Test pricing and terms — they matter more than the copy.** "A highbrow magazine tested three terms of payment for subscriptions. In one, the subscriber was asked to pay $65 for 56 issues. In another, $42.50 for 39 issues. In the third, $29.95 for 29 issues. Guess which won? Although it cut the price 40 per cent, the third generated 35 per cent more net revenue." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The shape of the offer is often the single biggest lever.

6. **Treat the coupon as a mini-ad.** Brand name, promise, miniature photograph of the product, everything legible. Readers skip from headline to coupon to find out the offer.

7. **Apprentice every new copywriter in direct response first.** Two years minimum. The writer who has had to live by the count of orders returns to general advertising incapable of mistaking a clever line for a selling one.

8. **Use direct mail as the agency's own weapon.** "It was my secret weapon in the avalanche of new business acquisitions which made Ogilvy & Mather an instant success." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* When I was trying to get clients I sent frequent progress reports to 600 people in every walk of life; when I solicited part of the Seagram account, Sam Bronfman played back to me the last two paragraphs of a sixteen-page speech I had sent him, and he hired us. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* If the agency will not use its own method on itself, it should not be paid to use it on clients.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Ogilvy's first direct-response campaign — a small country hotel in England, $500 budget.
**Application:** A man walked into a London agency and asked to see the boss. He had bought a country house and was about to open it as a hotel. Could the agency help him to get customers? He had $500 to spend. Not surprisingly, the head of the agency turned him over to the office boy, who happened to be the author of this book. I invested his money in penny postcards and mailed them to well-heeled people living in the neighborhood. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Six weeks later the hotel opened to a full house. "I had tasted blood." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* One budget, one list, one offer, one measurable result. The template of the whole career.

**Ad / Situation:** "How direct response advertising can increase your sales and profits" (Ogilvy & Mather agency ad, 1974).
**Application:** A long-copy print ad announcing the opening of a Direct Response office, stuffed with specific information. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* It practised the method on itself: a direct-response ad selling the direct-response practice.
**Result:** Served both as lead-generation and as proof that the agency's brand-minded clients should take direct response seriously. The self-demonstration is the argument.

**Ad / Situation:** Mercedes-Benz, 1,170 obsolete diesels.
**Application:** A five-page sales letter, mailed as direct mail, no other support. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** The surplus was unloaded. Five pages of copy, on one product, to one list. Long copy sells when the ask is large.

**Ad / Situation:** Cessna Citation business jet prospects.
**Application:** "Prospects for a new Cessna Citation business jet were surprised when we sent them live carrier pigeons, with an invitation to take a free ride in a Citation. The recipient was asked to release our carrier pigeon with his address tied to its leg. Some of the recipients *ate* the pigeons, but several returned alive, and at least one Citation was sold – for $600,000." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** One $600,000 sale from a list small enough to mail pigeons to. Direct response is not only postcards; it is any measurable invitation to act.

**Ad / Situation:** Subscription offer split-test (highbrow magazine).
**Application:** Three payment terms tested head-to-head: $65 / 56 issues; $42.50 / 39 issues; $29.95 / 29 issues. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** The cheapest option cut list price 40 per cent but generated 35 per cent *more* net revenue. The tested offer beat every opinion in the room about what the customer wanted.

**Ad / Situation:** Vic Schwab's book-selling mail-order ad (referenced in *Ogilvy on Advertising*).
**Application:** A promise-led long-copy ad selling a book by mail. "The promise in the headline and the content of the copy were irresistible." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Sold a million books in three years — by mail order. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Ogilvy cites this as the standard for what the discipline of a measured, promise-led headline can do.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Dismiss direct response as "tacky" or "not who we are as a brand."
**Why:** You are dismissing the only stream of evidence your agency has about what sells. The "tacky" word is what creative people who refuse to be measured say to defend themselves from accountability. Two minutes of long copy, a coupon, and a clear offer have built more brands than any beauty-prize campaign.

**Don't:** Run untested creative because "we like it."
**Why:** What you like and what pulls orders are not the same and usually not even related. Go through a magazine and pick out the advertisements you like best — you will pick ones with beautiful illustrations or clever copy, and you will forget to ask whether they would make you buy. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Test five variables at once.
**Why:** "You can only test one variable at a time. So you have to choose which to test." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A multivariable test that swings is a test that taught you nothing.

**Don't:** Stop testing once you have a winner.
**Why:** The winner is the new control, not the end of the work. "Once you have evolved a mailing which produces profitable results, treat it as the 'control' and start testing ways to beat it." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Agencies that stop testing regress.

**Don't:** Use reverse type (white on black) on response copy.
**Why:** It reduces readership, it has been proved to reduce readership for decades, and reduced readership is reduced orders. "Set your copy in black type on white paper." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Assume the biggest audience buys the most.
**Why:** "The better the program on which your commercials appear, the fewer sales you make. When viewers are bored by an old movie, they are more likely to pick up the telephone and order your product than when they are riveted by an episode of *Dallas.* … Remember, there is no correlation between the size of your audience and the number of orders you receive." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The CPM you pay for the glamorous slot does not translate into the orders you count.

**Don't:** Treat direct-mail practitioners as noncommissioned officers.
**Why:** They are the people who have actually measured what sells. The general-advertising creative director who refuses to learn from them is an officer who refuses to read the reconnaissance reports. The client pays the price.

**Don't:** Import the *look* of direct response (long copy, coupons, urgency) without the *measurement*.
**Why:** The form is nothing without the count. Long copy that has not been tested against short copy for this product in this market is just long copy. What made these tactics win elsewhere was the test, not the format.
