# Agency management — principles, not improvisation

## When to Use
- You are running an agency, studio, consultancy, or partnership, and realise you are improvising the management of it.
- The firm has grown past the size where one person can keep the whole operation in his head and is on the edge of becoming a job shop.
- You are writing — or should be writing — a set of principles your successors will actually inherit.
- A client, a department, or a senior partner is dragging the morale of everyone else down, and the question is whether to act.
- The firm is about to take on an RFP, a "strategic" merger, or a client whose ethos you cannot stomach.

## Fails When
- The firm is five people in a room. You do not need a written set of principles; you need to ship work.
- The crisis is specific — a client threatening to leave, a lawsuit — and demands improvisation now, principles later.
- The firm's product is not creative work but commodified service, where the right management style is standardization, not the cultivation of mavericks.

## Core Concept

Running an agency is not a job you can do by walking in at nine, leaving at five, and reviewing work by memo. "Running an agency requires midnight oil, salesmanship of the highest order, a deep keel, guts, thrust, and a genius for sustaining the morale of men and women who work in a continuous state of anxiety." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The firm is an institution; the question is whether you run it as an institution or as a personal appendage. Marvin Bower of McKinsey told Ogilvy that every company should have a written set of principles and purposes. Ogilvy wrote them, showed the draft to Bower, and was given holy hell — the first draft listed *Earn an increased profit every year* at the top. Bower said that any service business which put profit ahead of serving its clients deserved to fail. Profit moved to seventh. That ordering is the whole shape of the principles: client, standards, urgency, services, people, respect, profit. In that order.

The first operational principle is visibility. "Do not summon people to your office – it frightens them. Instead, go to see them in *their* offices. This makes you visible throughout the agency. An office head who never wanders about the agency becomes a hermit, out of touch with the staff." — *Principles of Management*. The corollary — "He who is absent is always wrong" — is why Ogilvy went to meetings instead of sending memos, and why the CEO who "reviews work by memo" is, in operational fact, reviewing no work at all. The second principle is scraping the barnacles. "To keep your ship moving through the water at maximum efficiency, you have to keep scraping the barnacles off its bottom. It is rare for a department head to recommend the abolition of a job, or even the elimination of a man; the pressure from below is always for *adding*. If the initiative for barnacle-scraping does not come from Management, barnacles will never be scraped." — *Principles of Management*. Every service the firm has ever added stays on the ship unless management cuts it. Every job that has outlived its purpose survives unless management ends it. The pressure from below is always to add. Scraping is a management duty, not an HR function.

The third is firing. "It is the inescapable duty of management to fire incompetent people." — *Principles of Management*. Ogilvy told on himself: "I have always funked firing people who needed to be fired." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. He called this his failure, not his mercy. The reason is that keeping an incompetent demoralizes everyone who is working around him. "I saw my old boss in the kitchens of the Hotel Majestic fire one of his chefs because the poor devil could not get his brioches to rise straight. I was shocked by this ruthlessness, but it made all the other chefs feel that they were working in the best kitchen in the world. Their morale would have done credit to the U.S. Marine Corps." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. Alongside firing comes its opposite — tolerating genius. Creative people are "nonconformists, dissenters and rebels." They are difficult. The firm that cannot tolerate them cannot hire them, and the work suffers. "Conan Doyle wrote that 'mediocrity knows nothing higher than itself.' My observation has been that mediocre men recognize genius, resent it, and feel compelled to destroy it." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. Part of the chairman's job is to protect the geniuses from the mediocre majority.

The fourth concerns clients. An agency cannot function without clients; an agency that will take any client cannot function for long. "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year. However, there are two circumstances in which resignation is the wisest choice: (1) When the agency would be more profitable without the account; this is uncommon. (2) When the client bullies the agency to such an extent that the morale of your staff is seriously impaired and starts hurting their performance on other accounts." — *Principles of Management*. Ogilvy resigned accounts five times as often as he was fired — Rolls-Royce when the cars stopped working, others when the client's executive vice-president was a "shit" who was demoralizing the agency's people. "Avoid clients whose ethos is incompatible with yours. I refused Charles Revson of Revlon and Lew Rosenstiel of Schenley." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. There is a dinner-table test: if you would be ashamed to tell your family what the account is doing, resign the account. "Never run an advertisement you would not want your own family to see" — the tenth of the *ex cathedra* sayings — extends, by the same logic, to the clients themselves.

The fifth and sixth are mood and morale. "Make it *fun* to work in your agency. Kill grimness with laughter. Encourage exuberance. Get rid of sad dogs who spread gloom." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. And: "I believe in the Scottish proverb: *Hard work never killed a man*. Men die of boredom, psychological conflict and disease. They do not die of hard work." — *Principles of Management*. These are the same claim: work that matters is hard, and people who are doing it well are happier than people who are doing it badly. Sibling rivalry, office politics, paper warfare, and favoritism are all enemies of morale. Treat subordinates as grown-ups — and they will grow up. Never play divide et impera; if you practice that fiendish art, your agency will go up in smoke.

## How to Apply

1. Write down the principles. Not in a conference deck — in a document every new office head will read, inherit, and be expected to amend. Ogilvy's own *Principles of Management* lists seven purposes in order: serve clients, earn an increased profit, maintain high ethical standards, run with competitive urgency, keep services up-to-date, make the agency the most exciting to work in, earn the respect of the community.
2. Never summon people to your office. Go to see them in their offices, unannounced. The chairman who never wanders becomes an invisible hermit.
3. Scrape the barnacles. Once a year, list every service, department, and senior person. Cut what has outlived its purpose. Nothing from below will recommend its own elimination.
4. Fire incompetents. It is unkind to keep them. "Great leaders… have the guts to make unpopular decisions – including the guts to fire non-performers. Gladstone once said that a great Prime Minister must be a good butcher." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
5. Tolerate genius. Protect the nonconformists from the mediocre majority who will try to destroy them.
6. Pay the best above market. If you find someone who is better than you are, hire him; if necessary, pay him more than you pay yourself.
7. Do not hire people you cannot fire. Never hire friends, your children, your partners' children, your clients' children, or your current clients.
8. Refuse RFPs that are theatre rather than selection. "The best way to get new accounts is to create for our *present* clients the kind of advertising that will attract *prospective* clients." — *Principles of Management*.
9. Resign an account only when it meets one of the two criteria: unprofitable, or bullying your staff. "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year."
10. Refuse clients whose ethos you cannot stomach. Ogilvy refused Revson and Rosenstiel; the revenue was never indispensable.
11. Apply the dinner-table test: if you would be embarrassed to tell your family what the account is doing, you have your answer.
12. Defend your people against clients. "The recommendations we make to clients are the recommendations we would make if we owned their companies, without regard to our own short-term interest. This earns their *respect*, which is the greatest asset an agency can have." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
13. Encourage free communication upward. Discourage poaching, paper warfare, politicians, secrecy, and favoritism.
14. Run training as continuous — not confined to trainees.
15. Be a good follower of your own partners. Partnership is a round table. "Only second-raters accept permanent subordination." Treat your lieutenants as equals or lose them.

## Examples

**Situation / Ad:** Ogilvy's own *Principles of Management*.
**Application:** After Marvin Bower of McKinsey insisted every company should have a written set of principles, Ogilvy drafted his. Bower made him reorder them — profit moved to seventh. The document covers Profits, Morale, Respect, Hiring, Partnership, Comers, Creative People, Researchers, New Business, When to Resign an Account, Remuneration by Fee.
**Result:** Demonstrates that management principles are a governance document, not a communication deck. The firm that has one outperforms the firm that improvises.

**Situation / Ad:** Refusing Charles Revson of Revlon and Lew Rosenstiel of Schenley.
**Application:** Both were enormous potential billings. Both had ethos incompatible with the firm. Ogilvy refused. "Avoid clients whose ethos is incompatible with yours. I refused Charles Revson of Revlon and Lew Rosenstiel of Schenley." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
**Result:** Demonstrates that the "refuse clients whose ethos you cannot stomach" rule has to survive a specific dollar amount in the room, or it is not a rule. Revson and Rosenstiel were not theoretical; they were signed contracts on the table that Ogilvy pushed back.

**Situation / Ad:** Resigning the Rolls-Royce account.
**Application:** When Rolls-Royce shipped six hundred cars to the United States and the automatic gearboxes did not work and the air conditioning fogged up the windows on the first drive around Central Park, Ogilvy wrote the client a letter headed "Lemons": "the last 600 cars you sent to the United States don't work. And I will no longer be a party to recommending that people buy them. I resign." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
**Result:** Demonstrates the second test for resigning an account — resign when the product is genuinely bad, because continuing to advertise it is dishonest. The head of Rolls-Royce, an engineer, wrote back and agreed.

**Situation / Ad:** Resigning the account whose executive vice-president was demoralizing the agency's staff.
**Application:** "I said because your Executive Vice President is a shit… he's treating *your* people atrociously and he's treating *my* people atrociously. Now what he does to your people – that's your business. But I'm not going to allow this man to go on demoralizing the people of Ogilvy & Mather. So goodbye." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
**Result:** Demonstrates the "morale of your staff" test. A client demoralizing your people inflicts costs that compound across every other account — resign.

**Situation / Ad:** The firing of the pastry cook at the Hotel Majestic.
**Application:** "I saw my old boss in the kitchens of the Hotel Majestic fire one of his chefs because the poor devil could not get his brioches to rise straight. I was shocked by this ruthlessness, but it made all the other chefs feel that they were working in the best kitchen in the world." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
**Result:** Demonstrates that firing the incompetent is the clearest signal a manager sends about standards.

**Situation / Ad:** The annual all-staff address at the Museum of Modern Art auditorium.
**Application:** Once a year, Ogilvy assembled the entire agency and gave a candid report — operations, profits and all — then told them what behavior he admired and what he expected of himself. "I admire people who work hard, who bite the bullet. I dislike passengers who don't pull their weight in the boat… I admire people who hire subordinates who are good enough to succeed them." — *Confessions*.
**Result:** Demonstrates that the chairman owes the firm a spoken, personal account of standards — not a memo from HR.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Review work by memo.
**Why:** You become an invisible hermit. The work degrades because nobody feels watched. "A boss who never wanders about his agency becomes an invisible hermit."

**Don't:** Summon people to your office.
**Why:** It frightens them. Go to see them in their offices, unannounced. You will learn more in one unannounced visit than in ten summoned meetings.

**Don't:** Promote by tenure.
**Why:** Ambitious people leave firms where promotion is a queue. Tenure is a trailing indicator of fit; it is not a qualification.

**Don't:** Accept every RFP and chase every pitch.
**Why:** "No first-class man will take the job; no second-class man can do it effectively." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. The best agencies do not have new-business departments. They do good work for present clients, and that attracts new ones.

**Don't:** Keep an incompetent because firing him would be unkind.
**Why:** Keeping him is unkinder — to him, because he is in a job he cannot do, and to the rest of the staff, who are working around him. Unkindness is the comfortable move. "It is the inescapable duty of management to fire incompetent people."

**Don't:** Take an account whose ethos you cannot stomach.
**Why:** You will resign it later at greater cost, or you will not resign it and the firm will smell of the account for a generation. Refuse it now while the refusal is cheap.

**Don't:** Resign an account in a moment of pique.
**Why:** "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year." The two legitimate reasons are unprofitable and demoralizing. Pique is neither.

**Don't:** Hire relatives, friends, clients, or clients' children.
**Why:** Each exception creates an untouchable inside the firm and alienates the ten people who resent the untouchable. "Ambitious people won't stay in outfits which practice nepotism."

**Don't:** Let office politics run unchecked.
**Why:** "Agencies have been *destroyed* by internal politics." Sack incurable politicians. Forbid paper warfare. The chairman's inattention is what lets politics metastasize.

**Don't:** Build the firm as a holding company for independent subsidiaries that compete with each other.
**Why:** "Megamergers are for megalomaniacs." Each office should be a partnership in a larger partnership, not an island competing with its siblings.

**Don't:** Run an agency from the chairman's office.
**Why:** Partnership is a round table. "Each of our offices has a *managing* partner. The total responsibility for the office rests on his shoulders. However, if he is wise, he will treat his lieutenants as equals." — *Principles of Management*. The chairman who runs the firm as a personal instrument is building a company that cannot survive him. The chairman who cultivates equal partners is building one that can.

**Don't:** Let the dinner-table test become optional.
**Why:** The test — would you be ashamed to tell your family what the agency is doing — is the only ethical backstop that will survive a profitable quarter. Apply it to every client, every campaign, and every hire.
