# Vertical Integration

## When to Use

- You are at the mercy of a supplier whose price you cannot predict and whose reliability you cannot trust — and the input is material enough to take down the firm if it fails.
- A competitor has locked up an input you depend on, or a cartel has formed around a process you need.
- You have reached operating scale, and the capital and the time horizon are there to build or acquire the capability upstream.
- The quality of the input — not merely its price — is limiting the quality of your finished product, and the supplier cannot or will not raise his standards.

## Fails When

- **The input is truly a commodity and the market is truly competitive.** Do not integrate for its own sake. I did not try to make my own sand at the Homestead Works. Pig iron was another matter.
- **Your scale of use is small.** If you will consume only one-twentieth of a modern blast furnace's output, building the furnace is folly. Integrate when your scale justifies it.
- **The domain is foreign and you cannot attract the talent to master it.** I knew iron. I did not pretend to know oil, though I invested in it briefly and withdrew. Integrate into what you can learn; do not integrate into what you will merely own.
- **You are using integration as a substitute for the harder question.** If your firm is unprofitable at scale, integrating backward will not save it — it will only spread the loss over a larger capital base.

## Core Concept

The vital lesson I learned in iron and steel — and I did not learn it in theory, I learned it in the bridge works when our pig iron supply failed us in 1870 — is the necessity for owning raw materials and finishing the completed article ready for its purpose.

Every firm I have seen that grew large while dependent on external suppliers paid a tax that was not on any ledger. The supplier's price rose in a panic. The supplier's quality fell when his own margins were squeezed. The supplier's allocation went to a bigger customer when material was short. The supplier raised his rate when he sensed our desperation. None of these things were catastrophes in any single instance; they were a drip, a drip, a drip that over a decade becomes the difference between a firm that survives a panic and a firm that does not.

The principle is simple. Any input that, if it fails, will take the firm down — you must own. Any input that is genuinely substitutable and genuinely commoditized — you may buy. The test is not the size of the bill but the structural dependence.

We began as a bridge-building firm, dependent on the market for iron. We became a firm that made its own iron. We became a firm that made its own steel by the Bessemer process. We secured our own coke through the Frick partnership — by 1897 the H.C. Frick Coke Company held two-thirds of the true Connellsville vein. We secured our ore through the Mesabi arrangement with Mr. Rockefeller in 1896 — twenty-five cents a ton on a fifty-year lease, when the market was paying six times that. We owned our own rail spurs, our own lake ships.

The picture at the end, the year before the sale to Mr. Morgan: one and a half tons of ore, one and a half tons of coal, one ton of limestone — all of it ours, from our ground through our coke ovens through our furnaces through our rolling mills onto our rails. When the steel business turned down, as it did in 1893, we were the last firm standing, and we took the orders our competitors could not afford to bid on. When it turned up again, we had the capacity to fill the orders our competitors no longer had the plant to meet.

It is clear to me that profit is to be made in steel manufacturing only by the concerns which do every step in the process themselves. I wrote that to my cousin on New Year's Day, 1898. I would write it again today of any manufacturing business of consequence.

## How to Apply

1. **Map every material input by dollar share and structural dependence.** For each input, write the annual bill and a single sentence about what happens to the firm if the supply fails for ninety days. If the sentence ends in "we close the plant," you have your candidates.

2. **For each candidate, cost the integration.** Not the vague "cost of building a plant" — the specific arithmetic. What is the capital cost? What is the operating cost at your scale? What would you save per unit? Over how many years does the saving pay the capital? If the payback is under five years, integration is almost certainly correct. If it is over ten, probably not.

3. **Ask who will run it.** Every integration is a new firm inside the firm. Who is your Frick for this input? If you cannot name him today, you must find him before you begin construction; building the plant without the man is how these projects die.

4. **Build or acquire — whichever is faster at comparable quality.** I acquired the Frick Coke Company because Mr. Frick had built a better coke operation than I could have built from scratch. I built the Lucy Furnace and the Edgar Thomson Works from the ground up because no plant then existing was the plant I wanted. Either path can be correct; what is never correct is waiting.

5. **When integrating, preserve the independence of the managing operator.** I did not try to manage Frick's coke company from Broadway. I gave him the autonomy the operation required. The integration is only a financial and strategic fact; the operating unit must still be run by a master of its own craft.

6. **Do not integrate beyond the basket.** Vertical extension into ore, coke, rails, ships — all in the service of steel. Horizontal diversification into railway bonds, oil wells, telegraph ventures — that is scattering. The basket is still the same. Watch it.

## Examples

**The Lucy Furnace (1870).** We were building bridges at the Keystone Works and depending on outside pig iron. The supply was uneven, the quality variable, the price rose whenever we were in a rush. I built the Lucy Furnace in Pittsburgh — named for Tom's wife, who drove the first rivet — and within two years it was the most productive blast furnace in America. The furnace did not merely give us pig iron; it gave us control of our input quality, which let us set standards at Keystone that no competitor could match. *Ours don't fall* became the Keystone motto. The bridges held because the iron was ours.

**The Frick Coke acquisition (1882).** We could not make pig iron without coke, and in the Connellsville region one man had built the finest coke operation in the country. Rather than fight him — which would have been expensive and uncertain — I partnered with him. I purchased a minority stake in the Frick Coke Company and gave him an interest in Carnegie Brothers. Within fifteen years we held majority ownership of Frick Coke and controlled two-thirds of the Connellsville vein. The arrangement was not without cost — Mr. Frick and I parted bitterly — but the coke was ours, and when the steel market turned, our fuel cost was known and fixed while our competitors' was not.

**The Mesabi ore lease (1896).** For years I resisted ore investments. "If there is any department of business which offers no inducement, it is ore," I wrote to Frick in 1892. Then Mr. Rockefeller, who had consolidated the Mesabi ranges, the Great Lakes ore carriers, and the connecting rail lines, approached us. The deal we struck: we would route all our ore shipments via his railroads and steamers; in exchange, we would have a fifty-year lease on the Mesabi output at twenty-five cents per ton — far below the market. It was the final piece. "We can now figure cost within a trifle and take contracts ahead without danger," I wrote to Frick the next year. An impregnable position; that is what vertical integration produces when it is complete.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Integrating to signal seriousness rather than to solve a problem.** I have known founders who built an upstream capability because they thought it looked strong. It did not. It diluted their attention, spread their capital, and failed to produce the input at competitive cost. Integrate only where the arithmetic demands it.

- **Integrating without the operator.** A plant without a master is a liability. If you cannot find the man who will run the upstream operation as if it were his own firm, you should buy the input from the supplier who has one.

- **Confusing vertical with horizontal.** Coke, ore, rails, ships — all vertical, all inside the basket of steel. Railway bonds, telegraph ventures, oil speculation — all horizontal, all outside the basket. Be ruthless about the distinction. The basket contains what serves the one domain you have chosen to master; nothing else.

- **Over-integrating.** I have seen firms so fully integrated that they cannot adapt when a piece of the chain becomes obsolete. Do not integrate into capabilities that will be overtaken by technology in a decade. Ask whether the input's economics will still favor owning it in ten years — and if not, rent it rather than buy it.
