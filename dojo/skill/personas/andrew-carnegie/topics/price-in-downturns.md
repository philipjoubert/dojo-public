# Price Into Downturns

## When to Use

- The market in your industry has turned down. Orders are fewer, prices are lower, competitors are pulling back.
- Your competitors are announcing capex cuts, hiring freezes, or layoffs. Capital equipment is available at steep discounts.
- You know your cost per unit and you have enough cash or credit to sustain an expanded operation through the trough.
- The downturn has the character of a cycle — one that has occurred before in the industry and that has historically recovered — rather than a structural decline.

## Fails When

- **The decline is structural.** Cyclical downturns recover; structural ones do not. A cyclical steel market recovered in 1895 and carried our expanded plant into record profits. A structural decline — say, a market in which the underlying demand is permanently falling — would have made our expansion into a graveyard. Distinguish the two before committing.
- **You are guessing at your costs.** The entire strategy depends on knowing your cost per unit with precision. If your cost data is annual, quarterly, or imprecise, you will not know whether the low prices are above or below your cost line. See `cost-mastery.md`.
- **You cannot sustain the position through the trough.** If the cycle lasts eighteen months and your cash runs out in twelve, you are not "pricing into the downturn" — you are gambling. Know your runway; do not take the strategy without it.
- **You are using it as bravado.** The counter-cyclical bet is not a matter of courage; it is a matter of arithmetic. If the arithmetic does not support it, courage will not rescue it. Do not adopt this strategy because it flatters your self-image as the bold operator.

## Core Concept

Every panic has been the making of Carnegie Steel.

I do not say this lightly. The panic of 1873 — when I had just opened the Edgar Thomson Works and the country had fallen into depression — was the most dangerous moment of my early business life. I was personally in debt; my brother was running the steel works while I managed the paper side of the operation in New York; and the market for rails fell by nearly half. What did I do? I did not retrench. I went to Wall Street, I met with every bank that held my notes, I secured extensions. I wrote to Edgar Thomson Steel's superintendent to build the plant out fully, on the cheap labor and cheap materials that the depression had made available. By 1878 we had finished the works at a cost the market would never have permitted in good times, and we entered the recovery as the lowest-cost producer in America.

The panic of 1893 was worse. The entire American industrial economy contracted. Rail prices dropped thirty per cent in ninety days. Our competitors pulled back from bidding on the assumption that the new prices must be below cost. They were below *their* cost. They were not below ours. We had the cost sheet. We knew the margin, even at the reduced prices. We bid aggressively — for every contract, on every rail job, on every structural order. We took orders our competitors would not touch. We kept the plants running near full capacity while our competitors ran at sixty per cent or less. By the time the market recovered in 1895, we had not merely held our market share; we had grown it by fifteen per cent. Our competitors had spent two years not producing; we had spent two years producing at a margin.

This is the central principle: **in a panic, the lowest-cost producer is never in a panic.** He is experiencing a temporary contraction of price, which he knows will end, during which he can do two things his competitors cannot: take market share from them at the margin, and buy capacity from them at distress prices.

The second half is as important as the first. The best time to build a new plant is during a depression. The best time to acquire a competitor's idle equipment is during a panic. The best time to hire a superior operator whose former firm has failed is in the aftermath of a collapse. The prices of labor, of capital equipment, of skilled men, of land, of everything the firm needs to expand — all are cheaper in the trough than they will ever be in the recovery.

What do your competitors do during the downturn? They cut capex. They defer expansions. They let the plant go unmaintained. They lay off skilled operators whom they will need to rehire at premium prices when demand returns. They cancel research programs. They do, in short, all the things that will leave them under-equipped when the market comes back. The man who watches his competitors retrench during a downturn is watching his competitors bequeath him their position.

The prerequisite — and it is an absolute prerequisite — is the cost sheet. Without it, you cannot price into the downturn because you do not know whether the low prices are above or below your breakeven. The firm that does not know its cost will either refuse orders that were profitable (leaving market share on the table) or take orders that were loss-making (bleeding capital in a panic). Only the firm with precise cost knowledge can bid with confidence at the market's new levels.

The second prerequisite is cash. Counter-cyclical strategy requires the ability to sustain expanded operations through the trough. You are pricing low to win orders; margins are thinner; you may be expanding while others contract. If you run out of cash at month twelve of an eighteen-month cycle, your advantage disappears and you may not survive. Before you commit, know your runway. Conservative financing through the boom — plowing profits back, avoiding outside debt, keeping reserves — is what makes the counter-cyclical position possible in the panic.

## How to Apply

1. **Classify the downturn.** Is this a cycle the industry has seen before, or is this a structural change in underlying demand? Read the history. Check whether the end-customers of your product still need it. A temporary reduction in steel orders during a financial panic is cyclical; the permanent decline of the whaling industry was structural. Treat them differently.

2. **Check your cost sheet.** What is your cost per unit at the current production level? What would it be at reduced volume? At expanded volume? If the market prices have fallen below your current cost but above your expanded-volume cost, the answer is to expand, not to contract. The arithmetic is specific; work it out.

3. **Check your cash runway.** Against your best estimate of the cycle's duration, do you have the capital to sustain expanded operations through the trough? If yes, proceed. If not, the cautious strategy is the correct one; you are not in a position to bet counter-cyclically.

4. **Bid aggressively at market.** Do not seek the premium price you would have charged in the boom. Take the market price; make your margin on the volume and the cost advantage. Your competitors will accuse you of irrational pricing; they are wrong, but the accusation will not hurt you as long as the orders are profitable at your cost structure.

5. **Accelerate capex during the trough.** New plant, new equipment, new technology — all cheaper now. Competitors defer; you build. By the time the market recovers, you have new capacity competitors have not built, and your plant is operating at a cost structure they cannot match because the equipment and labor they would need is no longer at trough prices.

6. **Hire superior operators whose firms have failed.** A downturn displaces capable men who would never have been available during the boom. Pick them up quickly; offer them the equity that wage employment elsewhere cannot match (see `partnership-model.md`). The downturn will end; the talent you acquired in it will compound for the next cycle.

7. **Resist the pressure to retrench.** Your investors, your partners, your bankers, your press will all urge caution. The conservative move feels virtuous; the counter-cyclical move feels foolhardy. The arithmetic is on your side; trust the arithmetic. Explain to the critics in plain language: we know our costs, we have the cash, we will take market share.

## Examples

**The 1873 depression and Edgar Thomson.** We were building Edgar Thomson Steel when the panic struck. Every banker with whom I spoke urged me to halt construction and conserve cash. I instead extended my notes, borrowed against receivables, and pushed the plant to completion at depression prices for equipment and labor. When the works opened in 1875 and the market began to recover, we had a modern Bessemer plant that had been built at perhaps sixty per cent of what it would have cost in the boom. The savings funded our margin advantage for a decade.

**The 1893 panic and counter-cyclical bidding.** Rail prices fell from about $40 per ton to under $28 in ninety days. Our competitors — Cambria, Pennsylvania Steel, Bethlehem — pulled back. I instructed Schwab and the sales organization to bid on everything, at market. Our cost per ton at full production was under $20; even at $28 we were making eight dollars a ton, at a scale that produced meaningful profit. We took orders our competitors had refused. We expanded our share of the American rail market from about twenty-five per cent to almost forty. When the recovery came in 1895, we had the orders, the customer relationships, and the production capacity; they had cut back.

**The Mesabi ore lease (1896).** The Mesabi range had been consolidated by Mr. Rockefeller during the 1890s, at prices enabled by the depression. By 1896 he held the mines, the lake carriers, and the connecting rail lines. He approached us seeking volume for his fleet. The arrangement we struck — twenty-five cents per ton on a fifty-year lease — was available only because of the depression. In a boom, he would have had other bidders; in the trough, we were the only firm with capital and appetite. Our secure ore supply for the next half-century was a direct product of acting when others were retrenching.

**The acquisition of Duquesne Steel (1890).** Duquesne Steel was a competitor in trouble. Allegheny Bessemer, its operator, had been mismanaged and was short of capital. We bought it during its distress at terms that would have been impossible during a boom; we then modernized it and ran it as part of the Carnegie system. A healthy competitor in a healthy market cannot be bought at distressed prices. Wait for the distress, then buy.

**The anti-example: the cautious competitor.** I will not name the Pittsburgh firms that retrenched during 1873 and 1893; many of them are gone, and the rest are diminished. Their pattern was consistent: they cut capex, they laid off skilled operators, they refused contracts at prices they believed were below cost (without actually knowing their cost). When the market recovered, they were under-equipped and under-manned. They had to rebuild from behind. They never caught up. The cautious course looked prudent at the moment; it was, in fact, the more dangerous course.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Pricing into a structural decline.** If the underlying demand for your product is permanently falling — the market for buggy whips after 1910, the market for typewriters after 1985 — expanding capacity is suicide. The strategy requires a cyclical downturn with eventual recovery; verify the nature of the decline before betting on it.

- **Counter-cyclical strategy without the cost sheet.** If you do not know your cost per unit, you will bid in the dark. Some of your bids will be profitable; others will be catastrophic. You cannot distinguish in real time. The cost sheet is the absolute prerequisite.

- **Counter-cyclical strategy without the cash.** Courage without runway is insolvency. Know exactly how long you can sustain expanded operations, and verify the duration exceeds your expectation of the cycle.

- **Taking on outside debt to fund the counter-cyclical position.** Tempting in a downturn, when lenders are scarce and your existing equity position is devalued. Usually wrong. The partners who came through the boom with you are more patient than outside creditors; the bank that extended you credit in the trough will call the loan at the worst possible moment in the recovery. Finance from retained earnings and existing partners, not from strangers.

- **Confusing counter-cyclical bidding with underpricing.** The strategy is to take profitable orders at the market's new, lower prices. It is not to bid below cost to gain share. Bidding below cost is a race to the bottom that the lowest-cost producer wins, but only after everyone involved has bled considerably. Take the orders that are profitable at your cost; let your competitors take the ones that are not.

- **Retrenching when your competitors are retrenching because you have not checked the arithmetic.** The herd instinct during a panic is powerful. The founder who retrenches because everyone else does is not making a decision; he is absorbing a mood. Check the numbers; decide accordingly; let the mood go.
