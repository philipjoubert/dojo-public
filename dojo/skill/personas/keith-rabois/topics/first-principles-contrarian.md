# First Principles and Contrarian Thinking

## When to Use
- You're forming an investment thesis or a company thesis and want to know if it's actually differentiated.
- You're picking a co-founder and need to know what to align on.
- You keep seeing the same startup advice everywhere and wondering if it's true.
- You're a junior investor learning how to develop your own judgment.
- You're a founder trying to figure out whether your idea has the secret that makes it non-obvious.
- You're deliberating whether to have a backup plan.

## Fails When
- Applied to operational questions where standard playbooks genuinely work. Payroll, legal structuring, benefits — go use the playbook. Being contrarian about 401(k) administration is just being annoying.
- Used to justify contrarian-for-contrarian's-sake. Peter's formulation is contrarian AND right. Contrarian and wrong is just wrong, and it's the more common outcome.
- Used to dismiss domain expertise. In biotech, payments compliance, or semiconductors, the first-principles derivation has to happen on top of deep knowledge, not in place of it.

## Core Concept

Life works out with a couple fundamental principles, and going back to first principles makes it a lot easier to get to the right answer. That's the philosophical piece. The practical piece is: I read books, because if you consume the same content and talk to the same people as everyone else, you're not going to derive new views. You're going to derive the same view as everyone else, which is by definition consensus, and consensus in a power-law business produces median outcomes, not outlier outcomes. If you want to know why most VCs underperform, it's this. They all read the same five newsletters, follow the same 40 accounts on Twitter, go to the same dinners. They're not doing original thinking. They're doing pattern-matching on each other's pattern-matching.

Peter Thiel's concept of the secret is the operational version of first principles. Every great startup has a secret — something the founder believes about the world that most smart people don't understand yet, or actively reject. PayPal's secret was that email money would work in an era when people thought the internet was for eyeballs, not transactions. YouTube's secret was that video bandwidth would collapse in price and everyone would want to upload. Opendoor's secret was that you could price houses algorithmically well enough to buy them with your own balance sheet. The secret is the load-bearing belief. If the belief is true and no one else sees it yet, you can build a cult around it and make it a reality before anyone shows up to compete. If you don't have a secret, you're running a business, not a startup. That's fine, but the economics are different.

Co-founder alignment on first principles is non-negotiable. Tactics you can disagree about every day — should we price at 9.99 or 12.99, should we hire this VP, should we launch in Austin or Seattle. Those arguments are healthy. What kills companies is disagreement on first principles. Does the internet change everything in this industry or just some things? Is design a nice-to-have or the entire competitive advantage? Is this a winner-take-all market or a fragmented one? If you and your co-founder have opposite answers on those questions, every tactical decision turns into a proxy war for a philosophical disagreement you're not having out loud. I'd rather have a co-founder I argue with all the time about tactics than a co-founder I silently disagree with on principles.

The acid test for being a good investor is this: do half your VC friends laugh at your investments? If yes, you're in the zone. If no, you're consensus, which in a power-law business means mediocre returns. Every time I've believed something that has turned into correct and consensus, I have to go back to the drawing board, because by the time a view is priced in by the market, the alpha is gone. And the related principle: life is path-dependent. No backup plans. Backup plans decrease your probability of success because they give you an exit. A movie can't be pivoted in the middle of production. You committed to the film, you cast it, you shot it, you finish it. Startups work the same way. Optionality sounds smart, but the CEOs who have backup plans end up executing them, which means they didn't fully commit to the primary plan, which means the primary plan failed.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify your secret and write it down in one sentence.** "We believe that [X is true] and most people think [Y is true]." If you can't complete that sentence crisply, you probably don't have a secret.
2. **Pressure-test the secret against smart people.** Tell it to five smart friends. If they all nod politely, the secret is already consensus. If half of them push back and think you're wrong, you're in the zone.
3. **Audit your sources.** How much of what you believe came from books, and how much came from Twitter threads and Substack? If it's mostly the latter, you are consensus-optimized without realizing it. Read books. Read primary sources. Talk to experts one-on-one, not at conferences.
4. **Before committing to a co-founder, align on first principles.** Have the long conversation. What do you believe about the industry, the market, the role of design, the culture you want, the trade-offs you'll make? Don't settle for tactical agreement. Probe the foundation.
5. **Kill your backup plan.** Literally. If you have a parallel track or an exit hatch, close it. Burn the boats. Commit. The commitment itself changes the probability of success, because you're all the way in on the one plan.
6. **For every consensus view you hold, ask: what's the non-consensus version?** If you can't articulate what the non-consensus version would be, you don't understand the topic deeply enough to have a view.
7. **Use the half-my-friends-laugh acid test on your investments and your strategic bets.** If everyone approves, it's not alpha. It's beta with a markup.
8. **Don't optimize for option value.** Pick a direction. Execute. Path dependency means the choice itself generates the outcome. Optionality is how ambivalent people lose.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder pitches me a consumer fintech idea. Her pitch is indistinguishable from five other fintech pitches I've heard this month. Clean UI, better UX, slightly lower fees.
**Application:** I ask: what's the secret? She says something about millennials. That's not a secret, that's a demographic. I press: what do you believe about this industry that no other smart person believes? She pauses, then says she believes embedded finance will cannibalize standalone banks within seven years and her wedge is to be the rails for companies that embed. That's a secret. That's a thesis with teeth. The first pitch was consensus. The second pitch is contrarian and, if she's right, a category of one.
**Result:** We invest on the second pitch. Two years in, the market has caught up to her view and the early moat she built during the consensus-laughs phase is exactly the compounding advantage.

**Situation:** Two co-founders are six months in and every decision turns into a drawn-out argument. They come to me thinking the problem is communication.
**Application:** It's not communication. I ask each of them separately: is this industry a winner-take-all market? One says yes, one says no. That's not a tactical disagreement. That's a first-principles fracture. Every tactical argument they have — hire fast vs hire slow, raise a lot vs raise a little, expand fast vs expand slow — is a proxy for the fact that they don't agree on the underlying nature of the market. They either resolve that in a weekend or they part ways before they burn 18 more months.
**Result:** They spend three days together, hash out the first principle, and discover they fundamentally disagree. One goes back to the company as CEO, the other leaves amicably to start a different company with a different thesis.

**Situation:** A junior investor on my team likes to back ideas that have five Twitter accounts already nodding about them.
**Application:** I do the acid test. Name three investments you've championed where half the room laughed when you said you were doing it. He can't. That's the problem. Every bet he's made is consensus. Consensus in venture cannot produce outlier returns because the price has already absorbed the view. I tell him: read three books on industries you don't currently invest in. Talk to ten operators in each. Come back with a thesis that would make half of our partners laugh.
**Result:** Six months later he brings a bet in a category nobody in the firm likes. Two partners explicitly argue against it. We do the investment. Three years later it's the single best-performing seed investment in the fund.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use Twitter as your source of truth.
**Why:** Twitter is consensus laundering. The same forty accounts retweet each other until a view looks like it emerged organically. It didn't. It's a closed loop of people trying to appear smart by agreeing with each other. Read the books those accounts claim to have read. Then you can form your own view.

**Don't:** Pick a co-founder because they're your friend and you haven't had the hard conversation yet.
**Why:** Every unhad conversation about first principles becomes a fight six months in. If you're going to argue, argue early, when the argument is cheap. Later, the argument has cap-table consequences.

**Don't:** Have a backup plan.
**Why:** Backup plans reduce your probability of success. You'll execute the backup plan, which means you didn't fully commit to the primary plan, which means the primary plan got less than 100%. The commitment itself is part of what makes it work. Life is path-dependent.

**Don't:** Be contrarian for its own sake.
**Why:** The formulation is contrarian AND right. Contrarian AND wrong is just wrong, and it's the more common outcome. The test isn't "do people disagree with me" — it's "do people disagree with me AND I have a reason I'm right that they'll understand in three years."

**Don't:** Pattern-match based on what's currently winning.
**Why:** By the time a pattern is visible, the alpha is gone. The next great company won't look like the last great company. It'll look weird for the first three years and then people will retroactively invent a narrative about why it was obvious.

**Don't:** Defer to experts on the core thesis.
**Why:** Experts are trained on the world that exists. First-principles thinking is about the world that could exist. Experts are useful for execution details and for knowing where the landmines are. They're poor at knowing whether the landmines have been moved.
