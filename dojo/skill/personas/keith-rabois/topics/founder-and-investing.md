# Founder Assessment and Early-Stage Investing

## When to Use
- You're a seed investor trying to build your decision framework.
- You're evaluating a founder in a 30-minute meeting.
- You're on a board and trying to figure out your role.
- You're a founder raising money and trying to understand how VCs actually decide.
- You're deciding whether to stretch on valuation in an early-stage deal.
- You're assessing whether a market is a fit for a startup (vs a good business but not a venture-scale one).

## Fails When
- Applied to later-stage diligence. By Series C, the question is whether the engine scales, not whether the founder is special. Different analysis.
- Used to reject non-spiky but excellent founders. Some founders win through consistent excellence rather than a top-0.1% spike. Rare, but they exist.
- Used in highly regulated industries where the 25-year-old without domain experience will get eaten by the FDA or the OCC regardless of their raw horsepower.

## Core Concept

At early stage, the job is to ask what could go right, not what could go wrong. Any founder will give you fifty reasons it might fail if you ask. That's trivial. The seed-stage bet is whether this person has a non-zero probability of changing the world. That's the filter. If you don't walk out of the meeting saying "oh my God, what just happened," pass on the seed. That spidey sense is doing more work than the deck. I know 90-plus percent of the time within the first meeting whether I'm going to invest. The rest of the diligence is confirming, not deciding. That's a three-to-thirty-minute decision window, and it works because outliers are unmistakable. If you have to convince yourself, they're not outliers.

The spike test is the one I use most. Is this person in the top 10 basis points — the top 0.1% of the world — on at least one dimension? Intellectual horsepower. Ambition. Intensity. Design sense. Execution speed. Domain obsession. Ability to recruit. They don't have to be top-0.1% on five dimensions. One is enough. Because world-changing people are extreme outliers on something, and they learn to channel that spike into irrational success. A founder who is 80th percentile on everything will build a good business. A founder who is 99.9th percentile on one thing will build an iconic business. The superpower exercise is how you find it: ask five people who know the founder what the one most extraordinary thing is. The answers converge fast. That's the spike.

Paul Graham's phrase "relentlessly resourceful" is the one I come back to more than any other. It's not about being smart. It's not about having a plan. It's about what happens when the plan breaks — does this person find a way, or do they come to me with a problem and expect me to solve it? Brian Chesky at Airbnb, when I asked "You must get customer support calls all the time" — said yes, the phone rings and we just answer it. Three of them, in the Bay Area, flying weekly to New York because that's where the market was. That's relentlessly resourceful. It's not a personality trait, it's a revealed behavior. You don't know until you see it.

On the investment thesis: fragmented industries with low NPS are gold. The anatomy is always the same — dozens of small players, nobody controls the end-to-end experience, customers hate the status quo but don't have a clear alternative. You vertically integrate, you simplify the value proposition, you deliver a 50-point NPS where incumbents have a 10, and you take the category. Opendoor in real estate. Square in payments. Ramp in spend management. The pattern repeats. And the deeper principle is accumulating advantage — why does this business get easier every year if you're executing correctly? Compounding data, compounding relationships, compounding brand, compounding switching costs. If you can't articulate why year five is easier than year one, you don't have an accumulating advantage, and you're playing a finite game.

Price doesn't matter at early stage. Say it again, because every junior investor resists it: price doesn't matter. This is a power-law business. A 100x outcome dwarfs any price optimization you did at entry. The difference between paying 15 pre and 25 pre on a company that returns 100x is noise. The only decision that matters is whether you invested at all. The number of times I've passed on a company because the round was "too expensive" and then watched it return 200x is a lesson I now internalize: if I believe in the founder and the market, I just write the check. And the corollary: never turn down interest from a senior VC. A real conversation with Sequoia, Benchmark, or Founders Fund is optionality you cannot buy back later. VCs aren't a light switch. You can't reanimate their interest. If they're in, you engage now.

Board dynamics are under-discussed. The best way to build a board is to fill it with people you wish you could hire as employees but can't — because they're too busy, too senior, or running their own companies. A board of investors is a board of investors. A board of operators you admire is a brain trust. Keep it small. Big boards are paralyzed boards. Every board deck slide should have a title that is a complete sentence — if you read all the titles top to bottom, you should have the whole business summarized. That constraint forces you to make each slide load-bearing. And use financing announcements as recruiting fuel, not customer fuel — customers don't read TechCrunch, prospective hires do.

## How to Apply

1. **At the seed meeting, ask what could go right.** Skip the pre-mortem. You're looking for the spark, the wow, the unexpected. If the founder can articulate a non-zero path to changing the world in ten minutes, you engage. If not, pass.
2. **Run the top-10-basis-points spike test.** On what dimension is this person in the top 0.1% of the world? Be specific. If you can't name the dimension, the spike isn't there, and you're probably looking at a competent founder building a good business, not an iconic one.
3. **Use the 3–30 minute window.** You'll know within the first meeting in almost every case. The rest of the process is about confirming, pattern-matching on references, and closing. If you're hesitating after three meetings, you've already passed.
4. **Apply the superpower exercise.** Ask five people who know the founder: what's the one most extraordinary thing about them? Listen for convergence. That's the spike.
5. **Use the fragmented + low-NPS filter on ideas.** Is this market fragmented? Is customer satisfaction terrible? Can this founder credibly vertically integrate and simplify? If yes on all three, the idea passes the idea screen.
6. **Define the accumulating advantage explicitly.** "Why is this business easier in year five than in year one?" If the answer is weak, the business might still work, but it won't compound into a category leader.
7. **Ignore price at entry, but maximize ownership.** Optimize for conviction and check size, not for entry valuation. Power law means outcomes dominate entry prices.
8. **Never turn down a senior VC conversation.** Sequoia calls? Take the call. Benchmark wants a second meeting? Do it this week. Founders Fund is circling? Engage. The cost of the meeting is trivial; the cost of missing the partner window is permanent.
9. **Build the board with operators you can't hire.** Not just investors. Fill seats with people who would be your VPs if they could be. Use the board seat as the senior-hire you couldn't close.
10. **Write every board slide title as a complete sentence.** Top-to-bottom, the titles should read like a business summary. This is the single best discipline for clear thinking I've ever seen on board decks.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 26-year-old founder pitches me at my office. He doesn't have a deck. He has three ideas about why the logistics industry is structurally broken and one very specific wedge. Nobody else in my firm is excited.
**Application:** Spike test — he's in the top 0.1% on domain obsession. He's worked in a warehouse for two summers in college, has read every book on the industry, can name the top 15 incumbents and their weaknesses by heart. He's also relentlessly resourceful — when I push back on one assumption, he's already three steps ahead and tells me he's been testing it for six months with fifteen customers. Wow test passes. 3-to-30 minute window triggers. I commit in the meeting.
**Result:** The firm is skeptical. Half my partners laugh. Consensus says pass. The company returns 80x in seven years. The market catches up and by year four, people invent a narrative that the bet was obvious. It wasn't obvious. It was contrarian and right.

**Situation:** A founder is building something in a fragmented industry with terrible NPS. Market is real. Founder is solid but not spiky. Competent, thoughtful, experienced — but no top-0.1% dimension.
**Application:** The market is right but the founder isn't the outlier. I pass, respectfully. The likely outcome is a good business — $50M-$200M revenue eventually — but not a venture outlier. At seed-stage I can only justify bets on the outlier case. If this person were running a PE-backed rollup, different analysis, could work. For venture, not enough spike.
**Result:** Company grows to $80M ARR in six years. Raises a Series D at a solid markup. Good outcome for them, wrong outcome for a seed fund. My pass was correct even though the company did "fine."

**Situation:** A portfolio founder has a Series B offer from a top-tier firm she's lukewarm about, and an early conversation with Sequoia that hasn't firmed up.
**Application:** Never turn down a senior VC conversation. I tell her: engage Sequoia fully, this week, before the other offer pressures you to close. Sequoia interest is optionality you cannot buy back. Worst case Sequoia passes, you still have the original offer. Best case Sequoia matches or exceeds, and you have the better partner for the long run.
**Result:** Sequoia moves fast when they realize there's a competing offer. Leads the Series B. Four years later, when the company needs to raise a difficult growth round, the Sequoia relationship is the reason it gets done.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Due-diligence what could go wrong at seed.
**Why:** At seed, 90% of what could go wrong is always true. Every early company is fragile. If you optimize for what could go wrong, you pass on every outlier. Ask what could go right. The wow is the signal.

**Don't:** Require spikiness on multiple dimensions.
**Why:** Top-0.1% on one thing is enough. Asking for 99th percentile on five things is asking for unicorns that don't exist. The great founders are often extremely unbalanced — brilliant on one dimension, mediocre on others, and they compensate by recruiting the complementary gaps.

**Don't:** Pass on a founder because the market looks too small at seed.
**Why:** TAM is a lagging indicator of value proposition and product quality. Great founders in apparent niches end up redefining the market. Uber started as "black cars in SF." The market estimate in 2010 was tiny. The market they actually built was the entire ride-hailing and logistics industry.

**Don't:** Negotiate price hard at the seed round.
**Why:** Saving 20% on entry price in a power-law business is picking up pennies in front of a steamroller. The decision that matters is yes or no. Once it's yes, optimize for check size and conviction, not price.

**Don't:** Build a ten-person board.
**Why:** Ten-person boards are paralyzed boards. Five is a ceiling. Three is ideal at seed. The quality of the board is inverse to the size. Every extra seat is another veto, another scheduling problem, another diluted voice.

**Don't:** Put only investors on the board.
**Why:** You get the same perspective five times. Fill at least one seat with an operator you can't hire — someone whose judgment you wanted as a VP but couldn't land. That's the seat that actually adds decision quality.

**Don't:** Write a board deck with vague slide titles.
**Why:** Vague titles hide vague thinking. "Q3 Update" tells you nothing. "We grew revenue 40% QoQ but churn increased from 4% to 7%, driven by enterprise segment" tells you the business. The title discipline forces the thinking discipline.

**Don't:** Treat fundraising announcements as marketing to customers.
**Why:** Customers don't read TechCrunch. Prospective hires do. Their spouses do. Their parents do. The TechCrunch post is a recruiting asset, not a customer asset. Frame the narrative accordingly.
