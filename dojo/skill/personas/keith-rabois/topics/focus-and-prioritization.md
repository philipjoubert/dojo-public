# Focus and Prioritization

## When to Use
- Your company has seven strategic initiatives and nothing is getting across the finish line.
- You have a decision to make and you're drafting a pros/cons list.
- Your #1 priority has been #1 for three months and hasn't moved.
- You're an exec and your team keeps surfacing B+ work while the A+ problem sits untouched.
- You're deliberating between two roles, two companies, two products, two deals, and you can't converge.

## Fails When
- You apply "one thing" to every role at every level without judgment. Junior ICs often need multiple tasks to stay productive. The mandate is most powerful at the exec and senior IC level where the A+ problem requires obsessive focus.
- You rank priorities once and then never revisit. Priorities shift. The rank order isn't a tattoo, it's a snapshot for this week or this quarter.
- You use the "one thing" mandate as cover for refusing to engage with real trade-offs. The point is focus, not obstinance.

## Core Concept

Peter had this mandate at PayPal about focus. Every single person in the organization was allowed to do exactly one thing. Literally one thing. Including execs. And Peter would absolutely refuse to talk to you about any topic that was not that one thing, period. We all rebelled. Every single person in the company hated it at first because it felt insulting. You're senior, you're supposed to do multiple things, you want to do multiple things. Peter wouldn't budge. When Peter would say to me, you need to fix this, and I literally won't talk to you for the next month or two until you fixed it — it would force me to bang my head against that damn wall every single day. Go home, sleep, think about it, go in the shower, dream about it. And once in a while it would lead to a holy-cow, there's an answer, we can do this. That's the breakthrough. You don't get that by solving five problems a little bit each. You get it by grinding one problem into dust.

Most people solve B+ problems instead of A+ problems. That's the failure mode of a smart team with a long to-do list. You wake up in the morning, the A+ problem is number one on the list, it's genuinely hard, you don't know the answer. So you knock out items two and three — they're easier, you know how to do them, they're progress. At the end of the day you've moved a lot of things and the A+ problem is still sitting there. Scale that across a hundred-person company for a year and you have a company that grew but didn't break through. No one spent 100% of their time banging their head against the wall on the hard problem. That's why the hard problem never got solved.

Reid taught me the decision model that replaces pros/cons lists. Reid was adamant that pros/cons is the worst possible way to make a decision. When you create pros and cons, you're creating an invisible false equivalence. Three things in one column, two in the other, your brain treats them as equivalent weights, and then you pick based on count or vibe, and you've decided nothing. The alternative is: rank your priorities strictly. First, second, third. Then make the decision solely on the first priority. Only if there's a tie — only — do you consult the second priority. If there's still a tie, the third. This collapses weeks of deliberation into thirty minutes. It also exposes when you actually don't know what your priorities are, which is useful information.

And the last piece: in any system there is a limiting step. The slowest step in a production line determines the throughput of the whole line. That's a manufacturing principle and it's the same principle for a company. Find the limiting step in your top priority. Put 20 to 80% of your time on that. Everywhere else is waste. A CEO is judged on roughly four decisions a year. Everything else is noise. The reason most CEOs lose is they spend 90% of their time on the noise.

## How to Apply

1. **Assign every exec exactly one thing this quarter.** Write it on a whiteboard. If they push back, hold the line. Let them hate you for a week. It's worth it.
2. **When you talk to them, only talk about that one thing.** Refuse to discuss anything else. If they bring up a different topic, redirect. This is the only way the one-thing mandate actually works.
3. **Identify the A+ problem and the limiting step inside it.** Not the strategy — the one specific constraint that if removed would unlock the whole thing. Allocate 20–80% of your calendar there.
4. **Kill pros/cons lists the moment you see them.** Replace with strict priority ranking. "What's your #1 priority?" Decide only on that. Then stop.
5. **Block sacred time on the #1 until it's solved.** Every single day, same block. Don't move it. If you move it, the #1 stays #1 forever and never resolves.
6. **Bypass filtered information.** Wander the office. Skip-level with your direct reports' reports. Read the weekly written report from each team — writing forces clarity that verbal updates hide. Bezos's six-page memo works for the same reason: the writer finds the gaps.
7. **Batch everything non-top-priority.** All 1:1s one day. Coffee in one block. Context-switching is a tax. You pay it in quality, every time.
8. **At the quarter, review. Did the one thing get done? If no, either the person is wrong or the problem is wrong.** Don't reset and pretend. Diagnose why.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series A CEO lists seven priorities for the next six months: hire VP Eng, ship the mobile app, close the enterprise round, expand to UK, rebuild onboarding, launch the API, recruit a head of sales.
**Application:** I ask him to rank those strictly. He stalls. Finally ranks: 1) hire VP Eng, 2) enterprise round, 3) rebuild onboarding. The other four are not priorities — they're wishes. He commits to one thing for each of his four execs this quarter, kills the UK expansion, puts the API launch on ice, defers the sales hire until after the VP Eng.
**Result:** VP Eng closes in eight weeks. Round closes in ten. Onboarding shipped. The other four initiatives were going to stall anyway — now they're stalled honestly, with the company's attention on what actually moves.

**Situation:** A founder is deliberating whether to raise a Series B now or wait six months. She has a pros/cons list with nine items per column.
**Application:** Reid Hoffman's model. What is her #1 priority? Hiring the best VP Product in the market. Decide solely on that. Which path gets her a better VP Product? Raising now — the higher valuation and announced round is itself recruiting fuel. Decision made in five minutes. She doesn't need to consult the other eight pros or cons.
**Result:** Raises. Hires the VP Product during the round announcement week. The other eight considerations were all real but irrelevant — none of them were the #1.

**Situation:** PayPal, 2001. Peter tells Keith to fix a specific operational problem. Won't discuss anything else with him.
**Application:** One thing. For weeks. Keith grinds on it — sleep, shower, dreams, walks. Eventually breaks through. That becomes the template Keith uses at LinkedIn, Square, and Khosla.
**Result:** The focus mandate moves with Keith from company to company and gets attributed as "Keith's obsession" — but it's Peter's system. It works because refusing to discuss the other topics is the thing that forces the grind. Without that refusal, the person substitutes a B+ problem and tells themselves they're making progress.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let priorities multiply silently. "We also need to do this" is how three priorities become seven in a month.
**Why:** The company's bandwidth is measured in barrels, and you don't have seven barrels. You have three or four. Every priority beyond your barrel count is a fantasy, and pretending otherwise just distributes the failure across seven initiatives instead of concentrating the success in three.

**Don't:** Let an exec tell you their team needs to work on three things in parallel.
**Why:** They're not wrong that the team has three real needs. They're wrong that parallel execution beats sequential. Each added step in the logical chain from "goal" to "business impact" costs 20–40% of team performance. Three-in-parallel is 40% of optimal. One-at-a-time at full intensity is 100%.

**Don't:** Make decisions by pros/cons lists. Ever. Even personally.
**Why:** Pros/cons creates false equivalence. You stare at the columns and your brain treats nine small pros as equivalent to one huge con. The mechanism that makes the method feel rigorous is precisely what makes it wrong.

**Don't:** Take filtered information as ground truth.
**Why:** Direct reports filter. They filter to make themselves look good, to not alarm you, to not dump on a peer, to not expose a project that's in trouble. A CEO who only hears filtered information makes decisions in a fog. Wander. Skip-level. Read the writing.

**Don't:** Reward people for the number of things they're working on.
**Why:** You're incentivizing exactly the wrong thing. The person doing five things is less valuable than the person who solved the one hard thing. Everyone knows this and pretends otherwise because the multi-tasker looks busy and the grinder looks stuck.

**Don't:** Skip the limiting-step diagnosis because you're "already working on the priority."
**Why:** Working on the priority and working on the constraint are different things. The priority is "ship the mobile app." The constraint might be one engineer on one specific bug. All the other work around that constraint is waste until the bug is fixed.
