# Barrels and Ammunition

## When to Use
- You're trying to figure out why great plans aren't turning into shipped products.
- You're deciding whether your company can take on another initiative.
- You're prioritizing your next hire and debating seniority vs. role fit.
- You're staring at an org chart and wondering why throughput is so low despite headcount.
- Someone impressive just became available and you don't have a role for them.

## Fails When
- You treat "barrel" as a title — it's not. It's a revealed behavior. People earn the designation by doing, not by interviewing well.
- You try to build an all-barrel company. Barrels need ammunition to shoot. A company of twelve barrels and no ammunition ships less than a company of three barrels and thirty good ammunition.
- You apply it to value-protection functions. A great compliance officer isn't a barrel. She's not supposed to be. That role needs experience and zero-defect execution, not end-to-end conviction-building.
- You mistake a loud, charismatic person for a barrel. Barrels are people who ship. Watch the output, not the performance.

## Core Concept

The company ships as many things as it has barrels. Period.

A barrel takes an idea from conception to live, almost perfectly. They pull people with them. They charge up the hill. They motivate. And critically, they edit themselves — you don't have to tell them what's wrong with their own work. Ammunition, by contrast, are talented people who need to be pointed in the right direction. They're valuable. You need them. But they don't expand the number of things your company can do.

If you have five barrels, you can do five things simultaneously. You add a sixth barrel, now you can do six. Add a seventh, now seven. That's the math. Adding ammunition past a certain point is adding firepower to guns that don't exist. At PayPal there were 254 people and probably 25 to 30 of them were truly exceptional. That's the critical density that produced the PayPal Mafia. Not the 254 — the 25 to 30. Square was similar. Most great people — even most genuinely great people — are ammunition. That's not an insult. Ammunition is how you win. But what you need to scale the company is more barrels.

Any one barrel you find, you hire instantly. Regardless of whether you have money. Regardless of whether you have a role. Regardless of whether the org chart has a box for them. If you wait until you have a role, they're gone. A friend tells you about someone they think is exceptional. You meet them. They have the pattern. You make them an offer this week. You figure out what they do after they've accepted. This sounds wrong to people running a tidy business. It's not wrong. It's the only strategy that compounds.

## How to Apply

1. **Count your barrels, honestly.** Not who has the title. Who has actually taken something from nothing to shipped and pulled others along? If the number is smaller than you thought, you've found your constraint.
2. **Match initiatives to barrels, not to strategy slides.** If you have three barrels and a strategy with seven bets, you have a fantasy, not a strategy. Kill four bets or hire four barrels. Those are the two options.
3. **Hire any barrel instantly.** When you find one, move the same week. Let the investors know you're adding someone great. Figure out comp later. You are always budget-constrained on barrels, never on headcount.
4. **Don't hire ammunition to solve a barrel shortage.** Ten more mid-level PMs will not make your stalled product ship. One barrel will.
5. **Watch for organic popularity in the building.** People seek out barrels even when they don't report to them. In an open office, watch whose desk gets crowded. That's a signal.
6. **Give barrels scope, not management.** A barrel doesn't need a manager. They need a scope of responsibility that's bigger than what they think they can handle. Keep expanding the scope until they break. That's the smoothie test applied to real work.
7. **Protect the ratio.** As you scale, the instinct is to hire for throughput — lots of ammunition. Resist it. The ratio of barrels to ammunition is what determines what the company can actually do, not the total headcount.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series B company has 80 people. The CEO complains that every important initiative — new product line, international expansion, enterprise motion — is stuck. Engineering shipped the V1 of each but nothing made it to v2.
**Application:** There are three barrels in the company, and all three are on the core product. There's no one to drive the other initiatives to completion. The ammunition on those teams is executing tasks but no one is charging up the hill. The fix isn't more headcount. It's either killing two of the three initiatives to match barrel capacity, or finding two more barrels and giving each one an initiative.
**Result:** Cut enterprise. Kill international for six months. Find one barrel for the new product line. Ship that. Then add the next initiative when a new barrel shows up.

**Situation:** A founder meets someone at a dinner who built and sold a company in an adjacent space. She has the unmistakable pattern — edits herself, has a view, already has ideas about the market. The founder has no open role for her.
**Application:** Hire her this week. Tell her you'll figure out the role together. Pay her. Put her on whatever is most stuck. A barrel without a job description will find a job to do within two weeks. If she can't, she wasn't a barrel.
**Result:** Within a month she's quietly running the most important initiative in the company because no one else was running it and she couldn't help herself.

**Situation:** PayPal, 2000. Max and Peter are hiring. Everyone in Silicon Valley thinks the right move is to pull senior operators from banks and credit card companies.
**Application:** Peter's rule is no one over 30. The 25-to-30 exceptional people out of the 254 were young, ambitious, undiscovered, and had something to prove. Two people in the whole company knew financial services — one was the general counsel, a value-protection role where experience was required. Everyone else was a value-creator with no industry experience.
**Result:** The PayPal Mafia.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire more ammunition when your problem is barrel capacity.
**Why:** You're adding people to a machine whose constraint is upstream. The plans won't get any more executed. You'll have spent the money and the problem will be worse, because now you have more people waiting to be pointed.

**Don't:** Pass on a barrel because "we don't have a role."
**Why:** Barrels don't wait. Google will pay them more than you anyway. Your only advantage is speed and the offer. If you wait for a role to open up, the barrel has already signed somewhere else and the role stays unfilled for six months.

**Don't:** Confuse title or pedigree with barrelness.
**Why:** A VP from a FAANG company who never shipped anything end-to-end isn't a barrel. They were a manager of ammunition. That's a different skill, and it's mostly useless in a startup where the barrel has to be the best at the actual craft, not the best at running one-on-ones.

**Don't:** Spread barrels thin by asking them to oversee three things.
**Why:** A barrel on one thing finishes it. A barrel on three things doesn't finish any of them. The focus mandate — one person, one thing — applies doubly to your scarcest resource.

**Don't:** Delegate the identification of barrels to a recruiter.
**Why:** Recruiters are mostly useless on this. The screens they apply are precisely the ones that filter out undiscovered barrels — resume pedigree, current title, years of experience. You have to do this yourself. This is the single highest-leverage thing a CEO does.
