# The Hiring Process

## When to Use
- You're about to start a search and want to run it correctly from day one.
- Your process currently runs seven interview rounds and great candidates keep dropping out.
- You're about to make an offer and want to close at a high rate.
- You're calling references and getting only glowing reviews.
- You're debating whether to "wait and see" on a candidate you're unsure about.

## Fails When
- You run the framework without first defining the profile. Process is downstream of profile. If you don't know whether the role is value-creation or value-protection, no process will save you.
- You delegate the process to recruiters or HR. This is a founder activity. Recruiters drive tempo. They do not pick winners.
- You use "speed" as an excuse to skip diligence. Speed means compressing the calendar, not skipping reference calls. You can do 20 references in five days if you hustle.

## Core Concept

The team you build is the company you build. That's the only quote from Vinod that matters for this topic. Everything else falls out of it.

Speed is your only structural advantage over Google. They have brand. They have comp. They have optionality. You have speed. If someone walks into your office on Monday and leaves with an offer by Friday, you will close them at a rate that big companies cannot match. The faster the process, the higher the close rate. I see candidates, I know within 30 minutes whether I want to hire them. The rest is closing, not assessing. Never run more than five interviews. Research backs this up — every interview past five has diminishing returns on signal and a linear cost on candidate experience. Make the decision in a day or two if you have to.

But speed without standards is just a fast way to hire mediocrity. The standards are: is this person a killer? Do they have a spike? Are they a barrel? What's their motivation? Can they think independently? Would they step up at the biggest moment? That last one is the Derek Jeter test. Jeter told me he knew in spring training whether a rookie would be an All-Star. How? Just their confidence. When you're actually great at something, you know it. Confidence and competence are the same signal seen from different angles.

Assessment techniques that work: ask "if you were CEO of your last company, what would you have done differently?" Works for anyone two-to-four years past college. Reveals ownership mentality, independent thinking, how much of the business they actually absorbed. Meet the five best people in the world doing the role before you hire for it — Brian Chesky's technique — so you develop taste. Borrow world-class friends to interview candidates in areas outside your expertise. Max can evaluate another technical person in three to thirty minutes.

Reference checks are for error avoidance, not enthusiasm. Keep calling until at least one person says something negative. If everyone raves, you have an incomplete picture, not a complete one. Ask the killer question: "Is this person a killer?" Watch their face. Real killers recognize each other instantly. If there's any pause, any hedging, pass. Tony at DoorDash does 20 references per key hire. That's a lot. It's also why his hires are so good.

First-day network extraction is the cheapest and best sourcing technique. Every new hire writes down the five-to-ten most impressive people they know, on day one. You reach out that week. The network gets filtered by company politics over time — you catch it before it filters.

Closing is a real skill. Ask Jack Dorsey's question: "Is there anything that would prevent you from accepting this offer?" Surfaces hidden objections you can address. Without it, you have a 10% save rate when they turn you down. Use your investors to close — they see many companies, they handle objections well, they help candidates' families feel the decision is safe. Announce your financings for recruiting, not for customers — your candidate's spouse needs to Google you and find something legitimate.

The most important principle: incomplete beats mediocre. Every time. Mediocrity spreads. Mediocrity compounds. Once you have a mediocre team, it is almost impossible to fix. So if the candidate isn't a clear yes, it's a no. Wait. Keep searching. An empty seat preserves optionality. An A-minus hire destroys it.

## How to Apply

1. **Know the profile before you open the role.** Value creation or value protection? Barrel or ammunition? What's the single superpower you need for this specific role? If you can't state it in one sentence, don't start interviewing.
2. **Cap the process at five interviews, full stop.** Candidate meets founder, hiring manager, two peers, and one wild card (skip-level, board member, or borrowed friend). That's it. Schedule within one week.
3. **Decide in a day if you have to.** Great candidates have multiple offers. Make the call fast. You can correct a bad hire. You cannot get a passed candidate back.
4. **Run a motivation-first interview.** First question: "What criteria are you using to decide what to do next with your life?" Let them talk. Watch what comes up. Money? Impact? Learning? Prestige? You learn more in three minutes of this than in an hour of structured questions.
5. **Ask the CEO question for mid-level candidates.** "If you were CEO of your last company, what would you have done differently?" Listen for "I" and "we" vs. "they." Listen for substance vs. deflection.
6. **Run the Derek Jeter test.** Does this person have quiet confidence? Not arrogance — assurance. Would they step up at the biggest moment? If you can't imagine them doing that, you have your answer.
7. **Do 20 references for key hires, or at least keep going until one is negative.** Focus on former direct reports, not just former bosses. Direct reports reveal leadership. Bosses reveal work product. Both matter; direct reports are underweighted.
8. **Ask the killer question on references.** "Is this person a killer?" Watch for instant positive reaction. Any pause is a no.
9. **Meet the five best in the world before hiring for a role you don't know.** Hiring a CFO and you've never worked with one? Get coffee with five great CFOs before you interview a single candidate. Developing taste is the prerequisite.
10. **Borrow friends for technical calls.** VP Engineering and you're not technical? Call Max Levchin. Three-to-thirty minutes. One friend, two finalists. Don't waste the favor on the early funnel.
11. **Extract the network on day one.** Every new hire, first day, writes down five-to-ten most impressive people they know. CEO emails each that week.
12. **Ask Jack Dorsey's closing question.** "Is there anything that would prevent you from accepting this offer?" Before extending. Surface and address the hesitation while you still can.
13. **Use investors to close.** Bring quality investors in on the closing call for senior hires. They see patterns. They handle objections. They make the opportunity feel real.
14. **Never compromise on early sales hires.** First two sales hires, zero tolerance for A-minus. Mediocrity in early sales kills the motion before it starts.
15. **Track false negatives.** Keep a file of candidates you passed on. Revisit annually. If three of them became amazing elsewhere, your screen is broken.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has a product lead candidate after four weeks of interviewing. Four interviews so far. The founder is on the fence — some signals good, some neutral, budget pressure to fill the seat.
**Application:** Don't run a fifth interview hoping for clarity. If you're on the fence at four interviews, the answer is no. Incomplete beats mediocre. Pass. Keep searching. In the meantime, ask three existing team members to extend scope on the product work.
**Result:** Six weeks later you find a candidate who is an immediate yes. The original one would have been a slow-burn disaster.

**Situation:** A candidate is about to get an offer. She has competing interest from two bigger companies. The founder has been assuming she'll just take the best offer.
**Application:** Before extending, ask Jack Dorsey's question: "Is there anything that would prevent you from accepting this offer?" She says she's worried her husband won't understand the unknown company. Founder sends her a one-pager with press coverage, investor names, a note from an investor. Extends the offer. Brings in a quality investor to do a 30-minute call with her.
**Result:** She accepts. Without the question, she would have turned down the offer and the founder would have scrambled to save it — 10% save rate. Surfacing the objection upfront took 30 seconds.

**Situation:** A founder is reference-checking a senior hire. Three references in, everyone is glowing. "Fantastic." "Best I've worked with." "Would hire again in a heartbeat."
**Application:** Keep going. Call five more. Ask specifically for former direct reports, not just former bosses. Ask the killer question. Ask for people who would have something critical to say. Keep calling until at least one person says something negative.
**Result:** Reference number seven, a former direct report, surfaces that the candidate can't handle peer conflict — walks away or escalates inappropriately. That's a real signal. Founder passes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run eight rounds of interviews.
**Why:** Diminishing returns on signal, linear cost on candidate experience. The best candidates have other offers. They drop out. You lose them to a company that moved faster. Cap at five. Always.

**Don't:** Filter candidates on passion for your industry.
**Why:** Patrick Collison is right — it's arrogant to assume they wake up caring about your company. Your job is to sell them on why the mission matters. If your strategy depends on finding candidates who already care, you are competing against every other company in that industry for a tiny pool. Expand the pool.

**Don't:** Screen out people who say they want to be founders.
**Why:** They have higher potential, not lower. If they leave in two years, that's your fault for not making the company compelling. Eric at Ramp: worry when that caliber of person stops applying.

**Don't:** Never compromise on early sales hires.
**Why:** Bad early salespeople teach the company the wrong motion, hire more bad salespeople in their image, and create sales processes that look busy but don't convert. First and second sales hires, zero tolerance for A-minus. I mean it.

**Don't:** Rely on recruiters to pick winners.
**Why:** Recruiters are mostly useless at identification. They drive tempo and inputs — that's their value. They will not find you the barrel. You have to pick. Don't outsource the single most important activity you do as a CEO.

**Don't:** Stop reference checking at three glowing references.
**Why:** Everyone curates references. Three glowing reviews is the default output, not signal. Keep calling until someone says something negative. No negative = incomplete picture = unknown risk.

**Don't:** Accept consensus references only from former bosses.
**Why:** Bosses tell you whether the candidate did the work product. Direct reports tell you whether the candidate can build a team, handle conflict, retain and develop people. For any leadership hire, direct-report references are more important than boss references.

**Don't:** Extend offers without asking the closing question.
**Why:** You're introducing a 10% save rate on candidates who turn you down with hidden objections. Asking "Is there anything that would prevent you from accepting?" costs 30 seconds and eliminates the problem. It's free.

**Don't:** Hire the A-minus because "we need to fill the seat."
**Why:** Mediocrity spreads. Mediocrity compounds. An empty seat preserves the possibility of excellence. An A-minus hire forecloses it and infects the rest of the team. The only exception is literal company-blow-up risk, and those cases are rarer than you think.

**Don't:** Treat hiring as something you can delegate once the company is past 50 people.
**Why:** Jack and I interviewed the first 400 at Square. Tony at DoorDash interviewed the first 1,500. If you think 50 is the handoff point, your bar is about to drop.
