# Leads

## When to Use
- The headline is settled and you face the paragraph (or two, or three) that follows it.
- You are writing long-form copy that must pull the reader line by line: direct mail, magalog, full-page newspaper, VSL, landing page.
- The hand has stopped — the next question is: *does the eye move to line two, and then to line three, and then to line eighteen?*

## Fails When
- **The lead congratulates the headline.** A paragraph that restates what the headline promised is a victory lap. Every sentence looking backward failed to reach forward.
- **The lead describes the product.** The prospect has not agreed to care about the product yet. Sell desire first.
- **The lead is a warm-up.** *"For many years I have been interested in…"* — the reader has already turned the page.
- **The lead names the price or offer.** Price belongs after desire is inflamed and mechanism is proved.
- **The lead breaks belief.** Violating existing convictions in paragraph one loses the reader for the whole ad.

## Core Concept

*"Your headline has only one job — to stop your prospect and compel him to read the second sentence of your ad. In exactly the same way, your second sentence has only one job — to force him to read the third sentence of your ad. And the third sentence — and every additional sentence in your ad — has exactly the same job."* The lead is the second sentence and the sentences immediately after it. The ad is a chain of acceptances; each link drags the reader to the next; every sentence that stops reaching breaks the chain, and a broken chain is a dead ad.

The lead is not a statement. It is a descent. The reader has granted you three seconds by not throwing the mail in the wastebasket. You spend those three seconds to buy another three, and those three to buy another three, until you have bought enough of his attention to make him *want* rather than merely *notice*. The lead is where the copywriter stops flagging the prospect down and starts *altering his vision of reality* — sharpening the desire the headline named, planting the first image of the life the product will produce, arranging the first acceptances of the chain. None of that happens if the lead admires itself.

The lead must be pitched at the same State of Awareness as the headline. A Level-5 headline that surfaces a hidden desire cannot be followed by a Level-1 product-and-price lead; the reader is not there yet. Awareness governs the pull of the whole chain, not only the top link.

## How to Apply

1. **Re-state the mass desire more sharply than the headline did.** The headline named it; the lead inflames it. Give it a picture, smell, dollar figure, or named symptom. Intensification before anything else.

2. **Promise the next sentence, not the product.** The closing word reaches. Every paragraph break is a cliff edge — the line before it must force the reader over.

3. **Match awareness level.** L1–2: you may name the product. L3: lead with the problem, solution hinted not stated. L4: crystallize the desire into a specific promise. L5: surface the desire itself before any promise lands.

4. **Work on the reader's ground, not yours.** Gradualization: start from a belief he already holds and walk him toward the claim he doesn't yet hold. The lead is the first two or three planks.

5. **Pick the corpus pattern that matches the diagnosis** — not the one you find clever. Fit, not flair.

6. **Test sentence by sentence.** Read aloud. The moment your voice drops — the moment the reader could stop without protest — that is the broken link.

## The Lead Patterns in the Corpus

Every pattern below is drawn from Schwartz's own ads. Each does a defined job at a defined awareness level.

### 1. Symptom-cascade with ellipsis rhythm

The signature Schwartz health-ad move. The lead opens with a vertical list of symptoms, each separated by ellipsis, that the reader privately checks off in his own body. By the end he has agreed — silently, without being asked — that he is afflicted. From *How To Correct Your Own Back Troubles*:

> "Chronic fatigue . . . headaches . . . colds . . . hardening of the arteries . . . stomach upset . . . stroke . . . sinus trouble . . . chronic gas . . . shortness of breath . . . migraines . . . loss of hearing . . . thinning hair . . . insomnia . . . stomach bulge troubles with elimination . . . even sexual inadequacy!"

And from *Your Nerves Can Cure Themselves*: *"constant nervousness and over-irritation…indecision…depression…loss of confidence in yourself and others…feelings of unreality…overwhelming obsession with one or two horrible thoughts…"* The ellipsis is not punctuation — it is pacing. Each dot-dot-dot is an invitation to nod or wince. Works at Level 3–4 when the reader owns the symptoms but has not connected them to the solution.

### 2. Imagined-consultation

The lead opens with *"How would you like to sit down with…"* and seats the reader, at no cost, in front of an expert. From *A Chiropractor's Treasury of Health Secrets*:

> "How would you like to sit down with licensed Chiropractor, Dr. Samuel Homola, and ask him such vital questions about your health as: 'Can I erase the wrinkles on my face?' 'Can I get a thicker and healthier head of hair?' 'Can I strengthen my eyesight and vision?' 'Can I rejuvenate my glands and organs?'"

Works at Level 3–4 awareness when the mechanism is a book or course and the author's authority is the main proof. The consultation-frame doubles as a question-cascade — each embedded question forces the reader to silently answer "yes, that one is me."

### 3. Pre-headline quoted complaint

A block of quoted laments, set before or beneath the main headline, in the reader's own voice. The reader scans it and sees his private panic in a stranger's mouth — not told he has the problem, but recognizing himself. From *Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?*:

> "'Why Doesn't Our Tommy Gain Weight?' / 'But, Mummy, I NEED a Friend!' / 'Why Is Our Boy a Fraidy Cat?' / 'Why Does My Husband Belittle Our Son, Always Favor Our Daughter?'"

Identification as the first beat. By the time the reader reaches the body copy, he has admitted — to himself, not to the writer — that the ad is about him. The Level-4/5 pattern of choice when the problem is socially embarrassing and the reader has never said it aloud.

### 4. Rotating-offer triplet

Three consecutive paragraphs, each opening with the same pull-verb, each offering a different incarnation of the same desire. From *37 Ingenious Spare-Time Businesses*:

> "How would you like to get a commission on almost every house sold in your neighborhood, without registering as a real estate agent, or even leaving your front porch."
> "To get perfect strangers to send you as much as $100 a year each, for ten years, just for reminding them of the price of certain crucial dates."
> "How ordinary iron ore-rocks (and an ingenious idea) into instant-money."

The triplet does two things at once: it demonstrates variety — *if one of these doesn't fit me, surely one of the next two will* — and it establishes the specificity of the promise through sheer particularity. Correct lead whenever the mechanization is a *set* of methods rather than a single move.

### 5. Case-history cold open

The lead drops the reader mid-story — no frame, no setup, no qualification. A specific person, a specific transformation. The reader's curiosity pulls him across paragraphs because he wants the outcome. From *From 4 Packs a Day to Zero, in 4 Hours*:

> "And he hasn't touched a cigarette again in 6 years! This is just one case history…"
> *"This book does not promise you, of course, that YOU will be able to duplicate this speed. But it does promise you this: YOU CAN QUIT SMOKING IN 14 DAYS!"*

The move-inside-the-move: the case history is extreme; the ad concedes the extremity in the same breath and drops to a believable guarantee. Works when the category is Stage 5 (exhausted by failure) and skepticism is the main barrier. The case gives him something to believe *against* the skepticism; the climb-down gives him something believable to climb onto.

### 6. Shocking statistic / identification

A number the reader does not expect, stated flat. *"Research indicates that the average American has a middle-aged body by 26!"* (*How to Say Goodbye to Your Middle-Aged Body*). The parenthetical *(Yes, 26 years of age!)* turns statistic into identification by forcing the reader to check the number against himself. Same in longevity: *"A baby born in the year 2100 will outlive you by 20 years!"* (*Feel 20 Years Younger, Live 20 Years Longer*). Each statistic is chosen to cause a flinch. The flinch is the pull-through.

### 7. Question-cascade

A ladder of questions, each of which the reader silently answers yes to at least one. The lead does not wait for the reader to commit — it stacks enough questions that commitment is statistically inevitable. From *At Last! Instant Relaxation*:

> "Are you vulnerable to other people's insults?… Do you have trouble falling asleep?… Are you one of those people who frets and procrastinates about spending money?… Do parties and other social gatherings make you wish you'd never received the invitation?"

Each question routes to a specific page number of the book — cascade *and* mechanism-demonstration in one. Rule: every interrogative in the lead must have a silent *yes* as its honest answer. A question the reader silently answers *no* is a door closed for good.

### 8. Binary taxonomy

From *How to Defend Yourself Against the Human Parasites Who Want to Rule Your Life*: *"There are two types of people in the world: the Givers, and the Takers. For every Giver there are a hundred Takers."* The reader instantly claims Giver; the product becomes his defensive weapon in the first two sentences. Binary taxonomy is the correct lead when the mass desire is indignation, resentment, or self-respect — anywhere the reader needs an antagonist to define himself against.

### 9. Pre-qualification / push-away

The lead tells the reader the ad is not for him unless he meets a condition. *"For lazy people only!"* (*How to Say Goodbye to Your Middle-Aged Body*). Or from *This Book Could Remove Your Fear of Death Forever*: *"If you are satisfied with your life as it is now… then, please turn away from this page now."* The push-away reverses ordinary sales logic: the reader who continues is no longer being sold to — he is proving he belongs. Works at Level 4–5 awareness where skepticism is the main obstacle, and for niche or premium products where qualification matters more than volume.

### 10. Teach-before-you-sell

Used when the mechanism is novel and the reader cannot be asked to believe the promise until he understands *how it works*. From *Now — Run Your Car Without Spark Plugs*: *"Your car runs because gasoline is fed into the cylinders where a spark causes it to fire."* That flat pedagogical opening is deliberate — Schwartz installs the baseline belief that the rest of the ad will overturn. The reader nods at the first sentence; the next sentence starts the overturn. By paragraph three, he has agreed with the mechanism on the ad's terms, not his own. Correct at Level 5 awareness and Stage 1–2 sophistication, when the product is so novel the reader has no frame for it.

## The Lead and the Rest of the Ad

The lead prepares the ground downstream claims will stand on. This is gradualization (see `gradualization.md`) — each later claim rests on a belief installed earlier. If paragraph three will claim fat cells can be *destroyed*, the lead must first install that the body *has* fat cells as a specific mechanism. The lead also opens the space for mechanization: the reader's curiosity focuses on *how it works* before the ad supplies the answer.

## Examples

### *Are the Foods You Are Eating Today Starving Your Brain?* — identification cascade at Level 4 awareness

Question-as-accusation headline. The lead runs three parallel paragraphs, each opening *"The man or woman who…"* and closing with the refrain *"may actually be a victim of his own starved brain!"* Each paragraph offers a different symptom-cluster; the reader who doesn't see himself in the first may see himself in the second or third. By the time the body copy names the mechanism (blood-sugar deficiency as the Great Masquerader), the reader has silently admitted he is the victim described. The lead makes the connection before the mechanism arrives.

### *Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?* — pre-headline quoted-complaint block

Before the main headline fires, a ten-item block of parental laments does the identification work: *"'Why Doesn't Our Tommy Gain Weight?' / 'But, Mummy, I NEED a Friend!' / 'Why Is Our Boy a Fraidy Cat?'"* The lead itself can then slow down and reassure: *"So don't push the panic button. Instead, let's face the facts. And the facts are that thousands of parents before you have had the very same type of child-behavior problems that bother you, and they've lived through them."* Identification, then reassurance, then authority — only available to a lead whose identification work has already been done in the pre-headline block. Converts shame into relief.

### *This Book Could Remove Your Fear of Death Forever* — push-away

> "It is, however, a completely subversive and dangerous book. It is difficult to read — shattering in its impact — full of strange consequences for you, your family, your job, and your responsibilities. If you are satisfied with your life as it is now… then, please turn away from this page now."

Counter-intuitive but verified: the ad pulled a small-market philosophy title by making every continuing reader a self-selected qualified buyer. Level 4–5 awareness; the push-away simultaneously raises the stakes (forbidden knowledge) and filters for the right reader.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with "As I was saying…" or any echo of the headline.
**Why:** An echo-lead repeats what the headline already got. The lead must introduce a new beat — deeper into the desire or further into the specifics — not rehearse.

**Don't:** Put the product name, the company, or the offer in the first paragraph (except at Level 1 awareness).
**Why:** The reader has not committed to caring about the product. Naming it too early asks for a commitment he has not offered. Product enters after desire is inflamed and (in Stage 3+) the mechanism is introduced.

**Don't:** Ask a question in the lead whose honest silent answer is "no."
**Why:** A "no" closes the door. *"Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?"* earns a silent *yes, every night*. *"Have you thought about buying steak knives?"* earns *no, go away*. Audit every interrogative before it goes to the page.

**Don't:** Use the lead to congratulate the writer, product, client, or category.
**Why:** The reader is not on the team yet. *"After years of research, our scientists have finally…"* spends his scarce attention on the writer's backstory. Every word before the desire re-fires is weight.

**Don't:** Write the lead at a different awareness level from the headline.
**Why:** Awareness governs pull. A Level-5 headline cannot be followed by a Level-1 price-and-offer lead. Keep both pitched at the same rung.

**Don't:** Bury the mass desire in a subordinate clause.
**Why:** The desire is the engine. If the reader has to hunt for it in paragraph three, he has stopped reading. The first sentence must touch the dominant mass desire in some form — intensified, imagined, symptomatized, or named.

**Don't:** Let any sentence in the lead sit back on its heels.
**Why:** Every sentence's one job: force the reader to the next. If a line could be the last one the reader reads without complaint, the lead has failed at that line. Cut, combine, or rewrite so it reaches.

**Don't:** Write a "literary" lead for a Stage-3-or-later market.
**Why:** Literary leads worked in Stage 1–2 when readers had time and claim-saturation was low. In Stage 3+, the reader has seen every mood-piece and learned to skim past it. Stage 3+ leads earn their pull with mechanism-teases, symptom identification, and specific case particulars — not prose for admiration.
