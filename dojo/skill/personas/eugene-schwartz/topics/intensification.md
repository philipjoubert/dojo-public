# Intensification

## When to Use

- You have the right desire and the right headline, and now you have to drag the reader one sentence deeper, then one more, then one more, for the length of the whole ad.
- The prospect is desire-aware or problem-aware, and the job now is to build that desire into a force powerful enough to close the sale inside this single piece of copy.
- The claim is credible but not yet *unavoidable* — you need to stretch the image across time, across actors, across numbers, until the reader cannot put the ad down.
- You are writing mail-order copy — long copy that must, in a single ad, say everything a campaign would have said across a year.

## Fails When

- The mass desire underneath is wrong. Intensification amplifies whatever is there — amplify nothing and you get loud nothing.
- You pile on adjectives without a fresh perspective. The book is explicit: "Every time the same basic promise is given a fresh setting for your prospect, it reinforces the descriptions that went before it. You cannot repeat, but you can reinforce." Repetition dressed up as hyperbole kills the ad faster than a flat claim.
- The specifics are invented. "Up to $1,000 a day" without a case-history behind it is no better than "a lot of money." Hyperbole must be grounded in a fact the reader can verify or at least picture.
- You are in a Stage 5 exhausted market. Intensification by itself made the market Stage 5 in the first place. At that point you do not turn the volume up again — you change instruments. Go to Mechanization, go to Identification.

## Core Concept

Intensification is the First Technique of Breakthrough Copy. Schwartz names it himself: *"The presenting of a series of fresh, new and different fulfillments for your prospect's dominant desire — our first mechanism of persuasion — is called Intensification."* It is not one trick. It is a whole kit of ways to keep a single dominant desire alive across hundreds or thousands of words, by showing the reader that desire being satisfied from angle after angle after angle, with no angle repeating the last.

You do not build desire by shouting. You build it by giving the prospect a new way to *see* the satisfaction every time his attention is about to drift. First you describe the product directly. Then you put the product in action. Then you put the reader inside that action. Then you hand him a test he can run himself. Then you stretch the benefit across weeks and months. Then you bring in an audience — neighbors gasping, experts approving, case histories stacking. Then you close with the guarantee turned into one more summary of the promise. Each of these is a different lens on the same single desire. The reader's resistance dies not from one heavy blow but from the *accumulation* of fresh perspectives he cannot shake off.

This is also where the signature Schwartz surface effects live — and they are *surface* effects, not the mechanism itself. The ellipsis-studded cascade ("$20,000… $50,000… $100,000"). The time-compression ladder ("30 minutes… 5 minutes… 3 minutes"). The verb choice that makes an abstract process physical ("turns up your digestive furnace," "burns flab right off your body," "destroys fat cells"). The enumerated catalog. The testimonial chain. These tricks are the observable surface of Intensification. They work because underneath them, each new line shows the *same* desire being satisfied in a way the reader has not yet seen. The moment the fresh perspective stops being fresh, the ad is over — whether there are ten more paragraphs or not.

## How to Apply

1. **Lock the single dominant desire first.** Intensification works on one desire at a time. If you have two competing desires in the copy, you are dividing your force. Pick the most powerful one by urgency, staying power and scope. Write that desire down in a single sentence. Everything after exists only to amplify that sentence.

2. **Describe the product or its result directly and physically first.** Before you get clever, put the thing on the table. Dimensions. Colors. Counts. What it looks like, what it smells like, what the reader will see the first morning he owns it. Schwartz's rose-ad opens with *"500 blooms in June . . . 1523 more blooms in July. . . 1616 more blooms in August. . . 437 more blooms in September . . . 4,076 roses all from one single plant."* That is not embellishment. That is ground.

3. **Put the product in action.** Show it doing the job. The fish lure doesn't just sit there — *"the nose of the lure will point downward, and it will begin its first descent! Slowly, jerkily, like a maimed minnow, it will swim noisily downward—buzzing and humming—traveling about nine feet every fifteen seconds!"*

4. **Put the reader inside the action.** Shift from third person to second. Walk him through what *he* will do, what *he* will see, what the neighbor at the stoplight will look like when he rolls past. This is the move from spectator to participant.

5. **Hand him a test.** Turn the demonstration into a self-proof. "Take this book and turn to page 39. Read eight short pages — no more." "Put your car in drive and let your engine idle. If your car stood still with spark plugs, it will now move forward at from 4 to 6 miles an hour." A test the reader can run dissolves skepticism faster than any adjective stack.

6. **Stretch the benefit across time.** Do not stop at the first day. Show the benefit at week one, week four, week twelve. The English plant-food ad builds in three waves: first burst of growth, then the second-stage nutrient, then the third-stage explosion of color. Time is free real estate for intensification.

7. **Bring in an audience.** Other actors — celebrities, case histories, experts, astonished neighbors — each provide a fresh perspective on the same product. *"They had lost as much as 17 and 25 and even 34 pounds . . . they had turned back their weight clock 5, 10 and even 15 years."*

8. **Cascade the numbers.** When you have a range, ladder it. The ladder must climb. *"$20,000… $50,000… $100,000."* The word *"even"* signals the top of the ladder and makes every earlier rung feel like the floor. Same for time compressions: *"30 minutes… 5 minutes… 3 minutes."*

9. **Stack verbs that physicalize the mechanism.** "Turns up your digestive furnace." "Burns flab right off your body." "Destroys fat cells." "Float fat right out." The verb does the work. Abstract verbs ("reduces," "improves") never intensify; physical verbs always do.

10. **Close with the guarantee as summary.** Do not let the guarantee be paperwork. Make it the last restatement of the promise. *"These plugs must give you up to 9 miles more per gallon instantly—or your full purchase price back! These plugs must give you up to 31 more horse-power instantly—or your full purchase price back!"* The guarantee now carries the claims one more time, each reinforced by the refund.

11. **Re-read the ad days later and cut what has stopped being fresh.** Schwartz: *"Perhaps the best way to measure the balance between the two—that critical turning point where reinforcement becomes mere repetition—is to re-read your ad, some days after you have first written it."* If a paragraph is not adding a genuinely new angle on the single dominant desire, it is dead weight pretending to be persuasion.

## Examples

**Dollar-cascade Intensification — `an-automatic-income-for-life`.**
The headline alone ladders three figures: *"An Automatic Income for Life of $20,000… $50,000… $100,000 a Year… Without Working…"* Then the lead reruns the same ladder at a different scale inside the copy: *"Did you ever stop to think how great it would be to have an automatic income for the rest of your life—an income that goes on whether you work or not? An income of $200 … $400 … $800 … $1,000 a week or more…"* Same desire — passive income. Two ladders. The reader climbs one, descends, and climbs the second before he has thought to object.

**Time-compression Intensification — `Dont-Pay-a-Penny-For-This-Book-Till-It-Doubles-Your-Power-To-Learn`.**
The promise is "doubled learning power." The intensification is a time ladder: *"You'll absorb the key ideas of most books in thirty minutes! Burn a magazine article into your memory in five minutes! Master the heart of a classic report in three minutes!"* Thirty, five, three. Each number is shorter than the last and the reader cannot help climbing the descent. Then a second ladder shows up in the "Tape-Recorder Memory" claim — scope intensification stacked on top of speed intensification, still on the single desire (learn faster).

**Verb-physicalization + stacked-mechanism Intensification — `Turns-Up-Your-Digestive-Furnace`.**
The headline verb does heavy lifting: *"Turns up your 'Digestive Furnace' and burns flab right off your body!"* Inside the body copy the numbers then ladder: *"you can lose 20…40…60…80…even 100 or 120 ugly pounds."* Notice the *"even"* — Schwartz uses it as a ceiling-flare. Everything under it now reads as conservative. Single desire — lose weight. Three intensification moves in one ad: physical-verb, numeric cascade, and the ceiling-marker.

**Adjective/symptom-stack Intensification — `How-To-Correct-Your-Back-Troubles`.**
After the back-pain promise, Schwartz runs a pure ellipsis cascade of *secondary* symptoms the single exercise allegedly fixes: *"Chronic fatigue . . . headaches . . . colds . . . hardening of the arteries . . . stomach upset . . . stroke . . . sinus trouble . . . chronic gas . . . shortness of breath . . . migraines . . . loss of hearing . . . thinning hair . . . insomnia . . . stomach bulge troubles with elimination . . . even sexual inadequacy!"* Each item is one more reason the reader should keep reading. The single desire — relief — is unchanged; the perspectives are sixteen deep.

**Case-history-as-Intensification — `From-4-Packs-A-Day-To-Zero-In-4-Hours`.**
Schwartz picks one extreme, dramatic case — four packs to zero in four hours — and puts it in the headline to anchor the single desire (quit smoking). Then he qualifies it openly (*"This book does not promise you, of course, that YOU will be able to duplicate this speed"*) before laddering back to the actual promise: *"14 days! 14 short days! 14 incredibly rewarding days! Two tiny weeks to the new freedom, the new health, the new vitality you have been dreaming about for years!"* Notice the repetition with transformation — "14 days" is repeated three ways (short, rewarding, tiny weeks), each fresh enough to intensify without becoming mere repetition.

**Absurdity-cascade Intensification — `Dont-Pay-a-Penny-Till-This-Course-Turns-Your-Mind-Into-a-Mental-Magnet`.**
Three paragraphs, each escalating past the last: *"you can attract a man or woman to your arms in a crowded room, by your appearance and bearing alone, before you even begin to speak to them… you can develop such invincible WILL POWER that you can simply crush anyone that dares oppose you, as though you were brushing a fly off your forehead… OVERNIGHT you have shattered habits, weaknesses and limitations that had crippled you for years—and replaced them with an irresistible drive… that makes enemies flee from your path like ants beneath your feet!"* The desire — personal magnetism — is one. The images are three, each a new angle, each larger than the last. The ad then pivots hard into authority (Harry Lorayne, Ed Sullivan) to catch the reader before disbelief sets in.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use more adjectives when the claim is already exhausted. Stacking "amazing, incredible, breathtaking" on a tired claim does not revive it. You are in the wrong stage of sophistication — escalate to Mechanization or Identification, not more volume.
**Why:** The market that is tired of your promise is not tired because you said it softly. It is tired because the promise itself has been heard too many times. Louder is the *cause* of Stage 5, not the cure.

**Don't:** Repeat the same perspective twice in a row. "This book will make you rich. This book will fill your bank account. This book will put money in your pocket." That is three sentences of the same perspective. Schwartz: *"You cannot repeat, but you can reinforce."* The second sentence must add an angle — a timeframe, an actor, a mechanism, a test.
**Why:** The reader's eye stops on the second identical line. Every sentence has one job: get the reader to read the next sentence. A paraphrase does not earn the next sentence; a fresh perspective does.

**Don't:** Invent specificity. "Lose up to 47 pounds" when you cannot point to a case history of 47 is worse than "lose a lot of weight." Hyperbole without a basis in product or proof is an unbelievable number, not an intensified promise.
**Why:** The reader's belief system is immutable. Violate it with a number you cannot defend and the whole ad — not just that line — collapses. Schwartz's rule: build on accepted belief, never past it.

**Don't:** Ladder numbers in the wrong direction. Descending dollars ($100,000… $50,000… $20,000) reads as disappointment, not intensification. Ascending dollars plus a ceiling-*even* intensify; descending dollars deflate.
**Why:** Intensification is accumulation of force. The reader's emotional vector has to track upward. Going the wrong way inverts the effect.

**Don't:** Put the guarantee in the fine print. Schwartz uses the guarantee as the final Intensification device — the last place the promises are restated, now backed by the refund. A guarantee buried at the bottom in small type is a missed amplifier.
**Why:** The guarantee is the one place where the reader has already given you permission to repeat the claims verbatim. Wasting that by making it legalese throws away a whole intensification pass.

**Don't:** Attempt Intensification on the wrong desire. If the underlying desire is weak or misidentified, every one of the eleven techniques above amplifies a dud. Schwartz is explicit: mass desire is the force; copy is only the channel. Channelization comes first, and Identification runs parallel. Intensification without them is a bellows blowing into an empty grate.
**Why:** Intensification has no content of its own. It amplifies content. Feed it the wrong content and it amplifies the wrong thing, faster.
