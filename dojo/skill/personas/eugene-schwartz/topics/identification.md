# Identification

## When to Use

- The mass desire is correctly located and the product performs — but the market has heard every promise and tried every mechanism, and the only thing left to say is *this ad is about you, specifically, and the people you want to be or are afraid of being*.
- You are writing into a Stage 5 market where further promises are disbelieved and further mechanisms are ignored, and the only remaining lever is the reader's need to recognize himself.
- The reader is problem-aware but unsure the product applies to him. You need him to answer YES to a private question before you ask him to buy anything.
- The category trades in roles — status, class, tribe, demographic — and the product functions as much as a badge as a tool. The fitness book is also a statement about the kind of body-owner the reader wants to be.
- The reader's symptoms are a cluster he has never named. The correct move is to name the cluster for him, watch him check the boxes, and let the recognition do the work the promise alone cannot.

## Fails When

- The market is still at Stage 1 or 2 and a direct, unadorned claim still pulls. Piling identification on top of a working raw promise dilutes the claim without adding force — save identification for when you need it.
- You assign a role the reader will not accept. If the ad offers him a self-image he finds embarrassing, unflattering, or untrue, he will reject the whole ad, not just the role. Identifications are as hard as rock and just as determinative — try to break past them and you hit the walls of a prison.
- You use identification *instead* of a performance claim, so the reader feels seen but is never told what the product does. The ad builds rapport and sells nothing.
- Your question cascade or symptom cascade is generic — items so broad that anyone reading would nod, which means no one feels specifically addressed. The cascade has to be specific enough that the reader thinks "how did they know about me?"

## Core Concept

Identification is the Second Mechanism of Persuasion. It lives parallel to desire and operates under its own rules. Desire asks *what do you want?* Identification asks *who do you want to be, who are you afraid you already are, who are the people like you?* Schwartz is explicit that identification, in his usage, is wider than the textbook sense of "identifying with a product personality." He stretches the word to mean *the active process by which the copywriter builds the reader's self-recognition into the product itself* — so the product becomes an instrument through which the reader acts out a role, claims a tribe, or admits a private condition he has been hiding from himself.

This is the technique you reach for when the claim is commoditized. In Stage 5 sophistication, every rival has promised the same thing, every new mechanism has been copied within a season, and the only territory not yet defended is the reader's sense of who he is. At that point the ad stops being about the product and starts being about the reader. The Marlboro cowboy is not selling tar-and-nicotine; he is selling the idea that the smoker is *this kind of man*. *How The Beautiful People Get Rid of Both Cellulite and Ordinary Fat Without Dieting* does not promise a better diet — it promises *membership in a class that does not diet*. The mechanism has vanished. The tribe has arrived.

Identification also runs at a smaller, tactical scale — inside leads, pre-headlines, body copy. The symptom-cascade in a health ad is not about the symptoms. It is a device for self-recognition: the reader scans seventeen ellipsis-separated ailments, finds four that match his private complaints, and now the ad is unmistakably about him. The question-cascade does the same with interrogatives; the case-history lead does it with other people. In every case the move is the same — the ad hands the reader a mirror at the right angle so he catches his own reflection, and once he has seen himself, the rest of the copy is about him by default.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the sophistication stage first.** Identification is the right tool for Stage 4–5 markets, for Stage 4–5 awareness, and for markets where the desire operates through a cluster of unnamed symptoms. At Stage 1 or 2, a direct claim still pulls — identification on top is wasted work. At Stage 3, mechanism still pulls — identification alone will not close.

2. **Decide which kind of identification you need.** Schwartz names two: *character roles* (adjectives the reader wants to claim — progressive, chic, well-read, youthful) and *achievement roles* (nouns the reader has earned — Executive, $20,000-a-year-Man, Home Owner, Wife). Character roles are flexible and easily persuaded into; achievement roles are rigid and must be documented in the product's symbols. Occasionally a third kind works — the *condition* role (the reader as sufferer, as parent-with-a-problem-child, as long-suffering patient), which operates through symptom-recognition rather than aspiration.

3. **Build a cascade that forces self-selection.** The strongest identification moves in the corpus are cascades — stacks of items, questions, complaints, or symptoms that make the reader check boxes he did not know he would check. The symptom-cascade of *How To Correct Your Back Troubles*: *"Chronic fatigue . . . headaches . . . colds . . . hardening of the arteries . . . stomach upset . . . stroke . . . sinus trouble . . . chronic gas . . . shortness of breath . . . migraines . . . loss of hearing . . . thinning hair . . . insomnia . . . even sexual inadequacy!"* Every item is one more reason the reader answers *yes, this is me*.

4. **Name the tribe or condition in the headline when the cascade will not fit.** *"For the man (or woman) who would like to break out of the salary-rut—for good…"* is a pre-headline entirely made of role-definition. *"The Beautiful People"* is a three-word class-assignment. *"For lazy people only!"* is reverse-snobbery pre-qualification in three words. The reader silently files himself into or out of the tribe before he reaches the promise.

5. **Use the quoted complaint as the strongest identification lead in parental or condition markets.** The ten-block quoted-complaint pre-headline of *Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick* is the masterwork: the reader scans ten real parental laments and finds at least one that mirrors his own private panic. He is reading his own thought in a stranger's words — identification complete before the product is mentioned.

6. **Put agency in the reader's body when the product runs against medical authority.** The body-part-as-agent headline — *"Your Eyes Can Heal Themselves"*, *"Your Nerves Can Cure Themselves"* — is a specific identification move for copy attacking medical establishment authority. The reader's *eyes* do the work. The reader's *nerves* do the work. No doctor stands between the reader and the result, which is the identification the anti-establishment buyer wants. The pattern recurs in the corpus — worth naming.

7. **Use the case history to let the reader see someone like him transformed.** A single extreme case — *"FROM 4 PACKS A DAY TO ZERO, IN 4 HOURS!"* — anchored early, qualified honestly, then laddered back to the achievable promise, lets the reader identify first with the extremity and second with the promise.

8. **Reject the wrong identification outright.** If the role the client wants the product associated with is one the market will not accept, cut it. Identifications are as hard as rock and just as determinative. Build on the ones the reader already grants the product; do not try to install one he refuses.

9. **In Stage 5, make identification the headline, not the body copy.** When the market is exhausted and the mechanism is exhausted, identification has to do the whole headline's work. In the reducing field at Stage 5, no new diet-mechanism would have pulled. The tribe was the only territory left.

## Examples

**Quoted-complaint lead as identification — `does-your-childs-behavior-worry-you-sick`.**
The pre-headline is a ten-item block of real quoted parental laments — not symptoms described but *voices* heard. *"'Why Doesn't Our Tommy Gain Weight?' 'But, Mummy, I NEED a Friend!' 'Why Is Our Boy a "Fraidy Cat"?' 'Why Does My Husband Belittle Our Son, Always Favor Our Daughter?'"* The parent scanning the page finds one matching the shape of their own private midnight worry. Identification is not persuasive here — it is forensic. The reader has been overheard. Then the headline lands: *"Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?"* — and *worry you sick* is a phrase parents actually use about themselves, one more identification layer.

**Symptom-cascade as identification — `your-nerves-can-cure-themselves`.**
The lead is a massive ellipsis-separated cascade of physical and emotional experience: *"constant nervousness and over-irritation…indecision…depression …loss of confidence in yourself and others…feelings of unreality…overwhelming obsession with one or two horrible thoughts…"* Then the physical stack: *"chronic fatigue... 'missed' the heart—'racing' heart—palpitations..."* Every item is an invitation for the anxious reader to nod. By item five, he has checked enough boxes that the ad is unmistakably about him. Only then does Schwartz name the reframe — *"Your nerves are not ill…they have not deteriorated…What has happened to them is that they have simply become OVER-SENSITIZED."* The identification opens the reader to the reframe. Had Schwartz led with the reframe, the anxious reader would have filed it as generic.

**Body-part-as-agent identification — `your-eyes-can-heal-themselves`.**
The headline grammatically puts agency inside the reader's anatomy: *"Your Eyes Can Heal Themselves"*. The subject of the verb is *your eyes*, not the book, not the method, not the exercises. That syntactic choice tells the reader what kind of person the product is for — someone who believes his own body can do the work, who mistrusts the optometry profession (*"for purely commercial reasons it is not generally accepted"*), who wants the doctor out of the loop. The pattern repeats in *"Your Nerves Can Cure Themselves"* and near-siblings. The body-part-as-agent construction assigns the reader the role of *self-reliant patient-who-doubts-medicine* — anyone who fills that role reads on, anyone who doesn't self-selects out. Identification doing the qualifying work for you.

**Demographic naming as identification — `37-Ingenious-Spare-Time-Businesses`.**
The pre-headline narrows the tribe before the main headline fires: *"For the man (or woman) who would like to break out of the salary-rut—for good…"* Every word is demographic self-selection. *The man (or woman)* — inclusive. *Would like to break out* — aspirational, active. *The salary-rut* — a condition the target reader has already named privately. *For good* — permanence. In eleven words Schwartz has assigned a tribe (wage-earners with a private ambition for escape), named their antagonist (the salary), and promised permanence. The reader who belongs reads on; the reader who doesn't, self-selects out — which is what a good pre-headline is supposed to do.

**Aspirational-class identification in a Stage 5 market — `How-The-Beautiful-People-Get-Rid-Of-Both-Cellulite-And-Ordinary-Fat-Without-Dieting`.**
The reducing field was fully exhausted — every mechanism tried, every promise copied, Stage 5. Schwartz made identification the whole headline. The reader is not told about a new diet; he is told about *The Beautiful People* — *"Those stunning, sleekly-slim couples who grace the pages of the fashion magazines and society columns…who spend each new Season in New York and Cannes and Saint Moritz and Saint Tropez…"* The mechanism (facial and body massage) is almost secondary. The product is a ticket into a tribe. *"I don't do ANYTHING: I don't diet!"* is not a mechanism claim — it is a class claim. Classic Stage 5 identification.

**Case-history identification anchoring a promise — `From-4-Packs-A-Day-To-Zero-In-4-Hours-Eugene-Schwartz`.**
One dramatic case sits in the headline: *"FROM 4 PACKS A DAY TO ZERO, IN 4 HOURS!"* The reader — a smoker who has tried and failed — sees someone *worse than him* succeed. The identification is with the *extremity*: if the extreme case cleared the bar, he can clear a lower one. Schwartz runs the honest qualifier (*"This book does not promise you, of course, that YOU will be able to duplicate this speed"*) and ladders back to the achievable promise: *"YOU CAN QUIT SMOKING IN 14 DAYS! 14 days! 14 short days! 14 incredibly rewarding days!"* The case-history identification has made the 14-day promise believable to a reader who has failed before.

**Pre-qualifier-as-identification — `How-To-Say-Goodbye-To-Your-Middle-Aged-Body`.**
Three words above the headline do all the work: *"For lazy people only!"* Reverse-snobbery. The reader who identifies as *not-the-disciplined-athletic-type* files in. The reader who objects files out. That silent voluntary confession makes the ad unmistakably written to him, and the rest of the headline — *"How to Say Goodbye to Your Middle-Aged Body!"* — lands on a reader who has already admitted he is the target.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a question or symptom cascade out of generalities. *"Do you ever feel tired? Do you ever worry?"* Every reader nods and none feels specifically addressed.
**Why:** Identification works by specificity, not generality. The reader's YES must be a *recognizing* YES, not a *polite* YES. The back-pain cascade works because *"hardening of the arteries... chronic gas... thinning hair... even sexual inadequacy"* is shockingly specific — the reader thinks *how did they know about me?*

**Don't:** Assign a role the market will not grant the product. A cheap product marketed through aspirational-class identification fails; a premium product marketed through mass-tribe identification fails for the reverse reason.
**Why:** Identifications are as hard as rock. The reader's beliefs about which roles are available for which products cannot be overwritten by copy alone. Work with the identifications the market already grants — do not try to install one it refuses.

**Don't:** Lead with identification in a Stage 1 or Stage 2 market. If a direct claim still pulls, starting with a tribe or cascade dilutes the raw claim.
**Why:** Identification is a tool for saturated markets and skeptical readers. In a young market, the mass desire plainly named does the work. Reaching for identification when you should be reaching for simplicity tells you you've misdiagnosed the sophistication stage.

**Don't:** Replace the performance claim with identification. An ad that builds kinship and never tells the reader what the product does will fail.
**Why:** Identification opens the door; the performance claim carries him through it. An ad without the claim is rapport without commerce.

**Don't:** Use identification to flatter the reader into a role he finds embarrassing or untrue. If he thinks *that's not me, and I don't want it to be me*, you have destroyed the credibility of the ad.
**Why:** False identification breaks the belief system. The reader rejects the role, and since the role was how the ad entered his attention, he rejects the ad with it. Better a smaller, true identification than a larger, false one.

**Don't:** Confuse identification with channelization. Channelization narrows *the desire* — from field to point, from category to product. Identification narrows *the reader's self-image* — from "people" to "people like me" to "I am specifically this person." They are parallel techniques; substituting one for the other leaves half the ad unbuilt.
**Why:** A well-channelized ad that never lets the reader see himself is sharp but cold. A well-identified ad that never points the desire at the product is warm but unsold. Channelization tells the reader *this product is aimed at you*. Identification tells the reader *you are the kind of person this product is for*. Both, or the ad limps.

**Don't:** Write the tribe vaguely. *"For people who want more out of life"* is a platitude. *"For the man (or woman) who would like to break out of the salary-rut—for good…"* is a tribe. *"For lazy people only!"* is a tribe.
**Why:** A tribe the reader cannot file himself into or out of is decoration, not identification. The move works only when it forces a decision: am I in, or am I out? Write the tribe with enough specificity that someone outside it recognizes they are outside it.

**Don't:** Stop at one identification when the product can carry two. The strongest ads in the corpus run identification at multiple scales simultaneously — a tribe in the pre-headline, a symptom cascade in the lead, a case-history in the body, a quoted testimonial in the guarantee.
**Why:** Identification compounds. A reader who has self-identified three times before the close has admitted his stake three times. One mirror can be dismissed as coincidence; three mirrors feel like a diagnosis.
