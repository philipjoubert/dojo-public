# No Recurring One-on-Ones

## When to Use
- Your directs are senior, expert in their craft, and have already reached the pinnacle of their careers. They don't need you for career coaching.
- You have many directs — 40, 60, 80 — and a standing 1:1 cadence would consume your entire week on repetition.
- You want information that currently lives in private conversations to instead travel through the whole organization at once.
- You are replacing the 1:1 with something richer — Top 5s, whiteboard sessions, staff meetings as town halls, problem-driven ad-hoc meetings.

## Fails When
- Your reports aren't at the pinnacle yet. New college grads need 1:1s. Early-career people need 1:1s. People going through a specific transition need 1:1s. Saying "no 1:1s" to those people is not flat-org sophistication, it's neglect.
- You remove the 1:1 and leave a vacuum. The T5s, the staff meeting as town hall, the whiteboard culture, the ad-hoc problem meetings — those are the replacement. Without them you've just removed the information channel.
- You use "no 1:1s" as a shield against being available. The whole point is that you're more reachable, not less. If a senior report needs ten minutes with you on a hard problem, they should get it same-day. Not "well, we don't do 1:1s."
- You let the category of "1:1s" disappear entirely, including the legitimate cases. Coaching a specific person through a specific transition is a different animal. Do it. Don't make a ritual of it, but do it.

## Core Concept

I have 60-something direct reports and no one-on-ones. No career coaching. No "what would you like to do with your life." Those are conversations you have with new college grads and early-career people, and we love those conversations, of course — we help them shape their careers, we mentor them, we give them access to new experiences. But at the executive staff level we're organized so we can pursue a whole lot of different things at the same time, and the people at that level are sophisticated, talented, experts in their field. They have already reached the pinnacle of their careers. They don't need me for career development. They need me for context, for strategy, for mission alignment, and for help solving the hardest problems — and none of that is well-served by a weekly 1:1.

Think about what a recurring 1:1 actually produces. The calendar hits. The person prepares a status update. They show up with a list of things they're working on. They deliver it. You nod. You share a couple of updates. You talk about a career question nobody really has. Forty-five minutes goes by. Now multiply that by 60 directs. That's 45 hours a week of my time on 1:1s, producing conversations I'd rather have on a whiteboard with seven other people in the room who need to hear the same thing. The 1:1 is not just a time tax — it's an information-hoarding device. Whatever you told me in our private conversation, everyone else in the company now has to learn from you second-hand. Why would I design it that way?

Instead: everybody tells everybody at the same time. If I have something important to share, or there's a change in direction, I tell everybody at Nvidia at the same time and I ask for feedback. Nobody has privileged information because they had their 1:1 with me on Tuesday. Everyone heard it in the staff meeting. The executive VP and the new college grad in the same room learned it at the same moment. That flattens power. It forces me to reason out loud in front of everybody — which is good for me, good for them, and good for the company. Intellectual honesty scales better in public than in private.

There are exceptions, and you should protect them. If someone on my staff is going through a specific transition — new job, new market, a personal crisis, a piece of the business in trouble — I will absolutely sit with them privately, as often as needed, for as long as the transition takes. That's not a "standing 1:1." That's a problem-driven meeting, bounded in time and specific in purpose. When the transition is over, the meeting cadence dissolves. That is very different from a weekly ritual that persists because it's on the calendar and has always been on the calendar.

## How to Apply
1. **Identify whether your directs actually need 1:1 management.** If they do, that's fine — they need it. Do not pretend to run a flat org on top of people who need coaching. Coach them. But if they are senior experts, they don't need it. Stop pretending they do.
2. **Kill the recurring cadence.** Delete the standing 1:1s from the calendar. Keep the ad-hoc door open. "Page me any time something is broken or a decision is needed. I will find time today."
3. **Put something in the vacuum.** Top 5 emails replace most of the "what are you up to" content. Staff meetings as town halls replace the "what should we do" content. Whiteboard problem meetings replace the "I'm stuck on X" content. Without these replacements, you've just gone dark on your team.
4. **Be reachable on demand.** The whole deal is "no ritual, more presence." You should be easier to get to when something actually matters, not harder. If a direct pings you about a real problem, they should get time the same day — on a whiteboard, usually with one or two more people who need to be there anyway.
5. **Keep 1:1s as a specific tool for specific situations.** Somebody new in a big role. Somebody going through a rough patch. Somebody stepping into a part of the business they don't yet know. Bounded time, specific purpose, dissolves when the purpose does.
6. **Use staff meetings to do what 1:1s used to do, but better.** Context-sharing. Strategy. Hard conversations about priorities. The people who would have heard it in their 1:1 hear it — and everyone else hears it too.

## Examples

**Situation:** A new board member at Nvidia, used to the standard playbook, asks Jensen about his management cadence. "How often do you meet 1:1 with your directs?" Expecting to hear "weekly" or "biweekly."

**Application:** Jensen's answer: "I have 40-some-odd direct reports and no one-on-ones, no career coaching." He explains: those conversations belong with new college grads and early-career people who are still shaping a path. At the e-staff level, the people reporting to him are already the pinnacle of their craft. He doesn't owe them a standing hour a week. He owes them context, mission, and help solving the hardest problems — and that happens on whiteboards and in staff meetings, not in private conference rooms.

**Result:** The board member initially doubts it. Then they sit in on a staff meeting and watch the information volume and quality that flows through it, and they realize: the 1:1 was the inefficient version. The flat-org staff meeting with 60 people in it is doing the work that 60 weekly 1:1s would have done, except everyone hears everything and nobody is hoarding information.

**Situation:** A mid-level engineering leader gets promoted to e-staff. On day one, they show up expecting a weekly 1:1 with Jensen. That's what every job before this one had.

**Application:** There isn't one scheduled. Instead, the leader is invited to the staff meeting that happens with all 60-some directs present. They write a Top 5 email. They join a whiteboard session when Jensen is working a problem adjacent to their area. The first week feels strange — where's my private line to the CEO? Then they realize: their private line is every staff meeting, every whiteboard, every Top 5 response, every ad-hoc problem meeting. It's just not pre-scheduled.

**Result:** The leader adapts. They learn to pull Jensen in when a real problem needs him, rather than banking questions for a weekly slot. They learn to read the Top 5 responses, the staff-meeting signals, the whiteboard commentary to understand what the CEO is thinking — which is a more honest and more complete picture than they'd have gotten in a private 45-minute conversation.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Kill 1:1s for people who still need them.
**Why:** Early-career people need 1:1s. First-time managers need 1:1s. Someone going through a personal crisis or a professional transition needs private time. The no-1:1s rule is a scaling pattern for the top of an expert pyramid, not a universal edict. Mis-applying it to a 25-year-old first-time manager is negligence dressed up as sophistication.

**Don't:** Use "no 1:1s" as an excuse to disappear.
**Why:** The whole model depends on you being more reachable than a CEO who does 1:1s. You are supposed to be in every staff meeting, on every whiteboard, reading every Top 5, available for page same-day on a real problem. If you're using the no-1:1 rule to protect your calendar from your team, you've inverted the purpose. Flat org is more CEO exposure, not less.

**Don't:** Remove 1:1s and not replace them with anything.
**Why:** The 1:1 was carrying information — context, strategy signals, relationship, trust. If you remove it and don't replace it with T5s, town-hall staff meetings, whiteboard sessions, and ad-hoc problem meetings, then your team goes dark. They won't know what you're thinking. They'll guess. Guessing is worse than a 1:1. Build the replacements before you kill the ritual.

**Don't:** Hold 1:1s in secret for your favorites.
**Why:** If "no 1:1s" is the stated rule but three people on your staff get private coffee with you every week, you've just created the worst version of the old system — with the added cost of hypocrisy. Either everybody gets a 1:1 or nobody gets a ritual 1:1. If a specific person needs bounded private time for a specific reason, say so openly and bound it.

**Don't:** Turn staff meetings into sixty sequential 1:1s in one room.
**Why:** That defeats the purpose entirely. The staff meeting should be working on problems that matter to everyone — strategy, priorities, live debugging of the business. Not "Alice, what are you up to? Bob, what are you up to?" That is status theater with a bigger audience. The T5s already cover "what are you up to." The staff meeting covers "what are we going to do together."

**Don't:** Treat "no 1:1s" as a culture export.
**Why:** It only works if you've also built the flat org, the Top 5 practice, the whiteboard culture, the staff meeting as town hall, and an intellectual-honesty norm that lets hard things be said in public. Remove any of those pieces and 1:1s are suddenly the right answer again. Don't copy the no-1:1 rule without the system it lives inside.
