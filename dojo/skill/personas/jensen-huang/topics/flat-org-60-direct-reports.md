# Flat Org with 60+ Direct Reports

## When to Use
- You're the CEO (or a functional leader) and deciding how many people report to you directly.
- You're tempted to hire a COO or insert a "chief of staff" layer to reduce your administrative burden.
- Your directs are senior, expert in their craft, and the last thing they need is career coaching.
- Information about what's actually happening in the business is arriving to you late, filtered, and cheerful.
- You want everyone in the company to hear the same thing at the same time so no one has power from hoarding information.

## Fails When
- The practices that make a flat org work aren't there — Top 5 emails, whiteboard meetings, staff meeting as town hall, intellectual honesty. The org chart without the practices is just a CEO who can't cope. It fails.
- Your directs still need conventional management — weekly 1:1s, career development, performative status reviews. A flat org assumes they don't. If they do, fix that first.
- The CEO can't tolerate embarrassment, can't do hard things in front of people, can't absorb a lot of information, or treats information as a source of personal power. The whole architecture depends on the opposite.
- You copy the structure from Nvidia the way people once copied "Amazon teams" without the memos, or "agile" without the practices. It looks the same. It doesn't work the same.

## Core Concept

Nvidia is not built like the military. Look at every company org chart in the world and they all look the same — somebody on top, then generals, then colonels, then lieutenants, then the people who do the work. And yet the number of direct reports of CEOs is very few, and the number of direct reports of the people who are just learning how to manage — first-level managers — is very large. That is exactly the opposite of how it should probably be architected. I have 60-something direct reports. The people who report to me are the most senior people in the company. They are sophisticated, they are incredibly talented, they are excellent leaders, they have great business acumen, they have vision. Every single one of them. They require the least amount of management, not the most. A CEO should have the most direct reports, not the fewest. By definition.

Most CEOs have six or seven directs because the knowledge and information of the CEO is supposedly so valuable, so secretive, that it can only be shared with a couple of people, and their information is so invaluable that they can only share with a couple more. I don't believe in that. I don't believe in a culture where the information you possess is the reason you have power. Your position in the company should have something to do with your ability to reason through complicated things, to lead other people to achieve greatness, to inspire them, to empower them, to support them. That is what a management team exists for — in service of everybody else in the company who volunteered to work for us instead of all the other amazing companies they could have gone to.

The architecture of a company ought to be the architecture of the machinery of building the product. That is what a company actually is. And yet everybody's company looks exactly the same, but they all build different things. How does that make any sense? You don't make fried chicken the way you flip burgers. You don't flip burgers the way you make fried rice. The machinery is different. The process is different. Nvidia is built like a computing stack — layers of architecture, chips, systems, software, modules — and people manage different parts of the stack. Your title has nothing to do with where you are in the stack. It just happens to be who is best at running that module on that function on that layer, and that person is the pilot in command. The whole thing is wired up more like a neural network than like an army. That is why we can have 60 people reporting to one person. The pressure on those leaders is high — I'll admit that's a real trade-off — but in exchange the company is flat, information travels quickly, and nobody is more powerful than anybody else because nobody has privileged information.

I can tolerate anything. I'm the senior-most person. That's the deal. The most senior person in the company takes the most discomfort because their resilience is supposed to be the highest. If you can't do hard things in front of people, you can't run a flat organization. The whole thing runs on the CEO being willing to absorb whatever the information throws at them — the ugly Top 5 emails, the uncomfortable staff meeting moment, the live-in-front-of-everyone debugging session — and still show up the next day with more energy than anybody else.

## How to Apply
1. **Count your directs and ask why that number.** If you have six directs it's probably because of a management book from the 1970s, not because of the architecture of what you're building. Work backward from the product. What are the natural layers of the machinery? Who runs each one? Those people are your directs. If that's 40, that's 40. If that's 60, that's 60.
2. **Make sure your directs don't need managing.** They are the best at what they do. They should require the least amount of your time on career coaching, on "what do you want to do with your life" conversations, on performance anxiety. If they need that, fix that first — or they don't belong reporting to you.
3. **Build the information architecture that makes it work.** Flat org without Top 5 emails is the CEO flying blind. Flat org without a staff meeting where everyone hears the same thing at the same time is 60 people playing telephone with you. Flat org without a whiteboard culture is slide theater. The org chart is the shell; the practices are the contents.
4. **Do not hire a COO to reduce your burden.** When board members push for it, say no, thanks. The "burden" is actually the job. If you delegate the information flow you've delegated the strategy.
5. **Tell everyone the same thing at the same time.** When there's an important piece of information or an impending change in direction, the new college grad learns it at exactly the same time as the executive staff member who has been here 20 years. Nobody is more powerful than anybody else because of access.

## Examples

**Situation:** A new board member at Nvidia observes the size of the e-staff — 40-something leaders reporting directly to Jensen, now more than 60 — and recommends he hire a COO to reduce the administrative load.

**Application:** Jensen says "No, thanks." He explains that the wide fan-out is not a burden he's suffering with; it's the point. "This is a great way to make sure everybody knows what's going on." He keeps direct communication with much of the rest of the company, not just the e-staff. He refuses to add a layer that would, on paper, free up his time but would, in reality, sanitize the information before it reaches him and give a small group structural power over the rest.

**Result:** The information stays unsanitized. The weak signals keep arriving. A new college grad in a robotics meeting hears the decision at the same moment the executive VP does. Nobody has power from privilege of information. Everybody is empowered because everybody understands the context. The company stays fast.

**Situation:** In an e-staff meeting, Jensen draws a whiteboard diagram of a typical competitor's org chart — he calls them "the upside-down V's." You become a manager and you build your little upside-down V. You defend it. Then you become a VP and you get more upside-down V's under you. The V's defend territory. Nobody talks across.

**Application:** He contrasts this with Nvidia's neural-network wiring. General managers at Nvidia talk to their direct manager once or twice a month but talk to Jensen two or three times a week. There is no frowning at skip-level conversations. There are no upside-down V's to defend.

**Result:** "The biggest difference was you only had to ask somebody once to get something done," one former executive said. "You never had to ask a second time." That is what a flat org buys you, if you pay for it with the practices underneath.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Copy the structure without the supporting practices.
**Why:** The 60 direct reports are the tip of the iceberg. Underneath sit the Top 5 emails, the no-1:1s rule, the staff meeting as town hall, the whiteboard, the tolerance for public embarrassment, the mission-is-the-boss culture, the pilot-in-command model. Remove any of those and the structure stops holding. You get 60 people sending you status reports you can't read, and a frustrated CEO who caves and hires a COO in year two.

**Don't:** Add the COO layer "just to handle the logistics."
**Why:** The logistics are the signal. Once somebody else schedules your time, decides which emails you see, and filters the staff meeting, they have more power than anybody else in the company — not because they're good at strategy, but because they control your information diet. The senior-most person in the company is supposed to eat that unfiltered.

**Don't:** Build this if your directs need weekly coaching.
**Why:** A flat org presumes the people reporting to the CEO are the pinnacle of their craft and need collective context, not individual attention. If they need individual attention, either they're not ready to be directs or they're a new hire in transition, in which case handle it on a project basis — not as a permanent 1:1.

**Don't:** Use it as an excuse to be absent.
**Why:** Flat org is not thin org. A CEO with 60 directs is actually more present than a CEO with six, not less. You are in every staff meeting. You are on every whiteboard. You read the Top 5's on Sunday night. The flat org costs the CEO more time, not less — but the time is spent on substance, not on private 1:1s where the same information gets repeated to one person at a time.

**Don't:** Forget that the pressure on your leaders goes up.
**Why:** Jensen says this plainly — the downside is the pressure on the leaders is high. In a command-and-control system, the person you report to has more power than you because they're closer to the information. In a flat org, everybody has the information, so leadership has to be earned on the basis of reasoning, judgment, and the ability to help others succeed. That is a harder job. Don't pretend it's easier. It's not. It's just better for the company.
