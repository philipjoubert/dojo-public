# Fundraising

## When to Use
- You're about to open a seed, Series A, or Series B process and you want to know if you're ready, how to run it, and what'll blow it up.
- Your runway is 9–12 months and you're debating whether to start talking to VCs now.
- You've got inbound from a VC who emailed you and you're trying to decide if you should engage.
- You're pre-writing a deck, a cold email, or a first-meeting pitch.
- You've got one or two term sheets in hand and you're trying to decide whether the valuation is right — or whether to take the money at all.
- You just raised and you want to understand what clock starts ticking the day the wire hits.
- You're a second-time founder and people are telling you you can raise at 3x your last company's seed price. You're wondering if it's real.

## Fails When
- Pre-revenue, pre-product, pre-team. There's nothing to pitch yet. Go build first. VCs at this stage are investing in the founder, not in any of this advice.
- You don't actually have a venture-scale business — low ACV, no expansion, flatlining below $1M ARR with no structural unlock in sight. No fundraising tactic fixes that. The right answer is: don't raise, or raise angel money and build.
- You're trying to close in a week with no relationships. Fundraising is a sales funnel with a 6–12 month sales cycle. You can't shortcut it by being hot for 5 days.
- You're at $100M+ ARR thinking about a growth round, crossover, or IPO prep. That's a different game — pre-IPO investors, PE, secondary. The rules I'm about to give you are seed / A / B.
- You're using fundraising to avoid selling. If you haven't closed customers and you think raising will fix it, you're going to raise and then blow the money figuring out what you should've figured out first.

## Core Concept

Look, fundraising is sales. Selling stock is sales. And most founders are getting a C+ to F grade at selling stock, even when they're a B+ at selling product. That's the whole thing. Once you accept that, everything else falls into place: you have a funnel, you have prospects, you nurture over time, you keep your existing customers (your existing investors) happy, and you treat every pitch like a first-call sales meeting. The founders who raise easy rounds aren't lucky. They put in the time. They meet 1 VC a week, 52 weeks a year, whether they need money or not. They send out a few emails when they're ready and three weeks later they have term sheets. It looks like magic. It isn't. It's pipeline.

Second thing to internalize: the initial yes / no / maybe is made within 20 minutes of the first meeting. Not 20 slides. Not 20 minutes of Q&A. Twenty minutes total. 95% of VCs know the odds they'll invest by the end of the first meeting. So your deck, your pitch, your first 5 minutes have to be exciting, data-rich, and to the point. Save slides 20 through 200 for back-up and Q&A. Lead with the best you've got. And at the end of the meeting, just ask: "Based on what you know now, what are the odds you'd invest?" I do this myself. Any seasoned VC will tell you. It saves everyone a month of ghosting.

Third: the CEO pitches. Full stop. Not the co-founder, not the COO, not the CFO, not an agent, not a banker. If anyone but the CEO is reaching out or driving the meeting, for most investors that's an immediate no, or close to it. Selling stock is the CEO's job. If you don't want it, get out of the CEO seat. That's the trade.

Fourth — and this is the one that breaks hearts — slow growth is death in fundraising. If you're not growing at a venture-scale rate, just wait. Don't go out. VCs will almost completely overlook a period of flat growth if they see 3–4 good months in a row right before you pitch. But one great month isn't a trend. Three great months in a row is. You need the pattern. If you don't have it, sit down, fix growth, and try again in 90 days. The worst thing you can say in a pitch is "we're not growing because we're fundraising." There are no excuses in fundraising.

Fifth: your existing investors are gold. Both as a signal and as a source of capital. They often reserve money for a second check — but only if you keep them in the loop. Most institutional investors save pro-rata or bridge capacity. Ignore them after the wire and they won't reserve it. They're also your #1 referral source into the next round — a warm intro from your existing seed investor to a top Series A firm is worth 100 cold emails. So invest in investor relations like you invest in customer success. Monthly updates. Real ones. Numbers. Asks. Wins and losses. It's how you compound a cap table into a raise engine.

Sixth: the clock. The day you raise, a clock starts ticking. Softly for the first 4–5 months. Louder by month 10–12. Much louder by month 15. The typical round funds 18–24 months of runway. Your existing investors expect the next round to be at 2x–3x the last valuation. That means you usually need to do a lot more than 2x revenue to clear the bar — closer to Triple-Triple-Double-Double. Going from $10K MRR to $20K MRR won't clear the hurdle on a seed. You often need to go from $10K MRR to $2M ARR by Series A. Know the game before you sign.

## How to Apply

1. **Send the CEO. Only the CEO.** No co-founder, no COO, no CFO, no banker, no agent, no VA scheduling your meetings. You — the CEO — pitch, you schedule, you follow up. Using a VA to schedule VC meetings says it's not a priority. 95% of the best founders I work with schedule VC meetings themselves.

2. **Pass the 20-minute test.** The initial yes / maybe / no is made in the first 20 minutes. Make it exciting, speak with data, and get to the point. Lead with your best 3 slides: what you do, how fast you're growing, why you win. Save slides 20–200 for Q&A and back-up. If you bury the metrics in slide 27, you've already lost.

3. **Send the full deck. Not a teaser.** Unless you are the hottest deal in the market this quarter, lead with your strength. Send every number a thoughtful VC would want. Customer count. ARR. Growth rate. Retention. Burn. Team. Hiding info behind a "teaser deck" turns many "maybes" into passes. Leave the games to folks who are already hot.

4. **Get 3 great months of growth in a row before you go out.** Not one. Three. Early-stage VCs will forgive 12–24 slow months if they see 3 months of clear acceleration right before the pitch. Growth-stage VCs need 3–4 quarters. Don't waste anyone's time — including your own — raising on one lucky month.

5. **Raise with 3–4 months of cash in the bank, minimum.** If you start raising with 2 months left, VCs smell weakness. Time is on their side. They'll drag out, and you'll take a worse deal or no deal. Add 6–12 months to your runway math — it's how long great VPs take to close, and it's also how long a real round takes end-to-end when you include nurture.

6. **Cold email works — if the email is actually great.** Every early-stage VC reads their cold email. I've funded Salesloft, Talkdesk, Pipedrive, Logikcull and more from cold emails. A great cold email beats a mediocre warm intro. But mediocre cold email is worse than useless. Keep it short, metrics-filled, personalized to that VC, and specific about why you win. Don't ask for coffee. Get to the point. One shot.

7. **A mediocre warm intro is worse than a great cold email.** VCs want warm intros where a trusted person says "this is the best of the best." A random "so-and-so said you might be interested" intro is actually a negative signal. If the person introing you won't call you top-tier, skip the intro — write a great cold email instead.

8. **Existing investors are your best referral source.** Before you blast the market, ask your seed investors: who should I be talking to for the A? They know the Series A firms, they know the partners, and their intro is worth 100x a cold. But only if you've kept them in the loop since the last check. If you've ghosted them for 12 months, don't expect them to move mountains.

9. **Ask at the end of every first meeting: "What are the odds you'd invest?"** I ask this myself. Any seasoned VC will answer honestly. "Too early for us, 20%." "If the diligence checks out, 80%+." You save a month of "ghosting" — which isn't ghosting, it's just you misreading the signal. And if the answer is 20%, you know to move on, not pine.

10. **Match your ask to the fund.** Most mid-sized funds want to write a check of ~2% of fund size for 10–20% ownership. A $150M fund is comfortable with a $3M check at a $20M valuation. Ask them for $15M and they pass — that's 10% of the fund in one deal, too much concentration. Know each firm's check size and fund size before you ask. Many deals die because the founder asked for 3x what the fund can write.

11. **Know your runway and the next-round hurdle before you sign the term sheet.** The day the wire hits, the clock starts. 18–24 months of runway on the money. Existing investors expect the next round at 2–3x last round's valuation. That usually means you need to Triple-Triple-Double-Double through the period. Back-solve: what ARR and growth rate do I need at month 16 to get the next round at 3x? If the math doesn't pencil, don't sign.

12. **Run the 1-and-30 rule. Fundraise 52 weeks a year.** Every week, 1 hour meeting a new investor (prospecting). 30 minutes updating existing investors (customer success). Every week. No excuses. When it's time to raise, you don't scramble — you already have 40+ warm VCs who've watched your numbers climb for 18 months and are ready to lean in.

13. **Don't raise if you don't need it at an unexceptional valuation.** Many founders regret taking capital they didn't need. More stakeholders, more board seats, less control, higher expectations, bigger next-round hurdle. The only cases where "raise even if you don't need it" wins: (a) balance sheet is weak — less than $1 cash for every $2 ARR, (b) the terms are exceptional and the round de-risks the next 24 months, (c) you can take meaningful founder secondary and de-stress your life. Otherwise — pass.

14. **Be careful of artificial urgency if you're not truly hot.** If you're the hottest deal of the quarter, a "tight process" with 48-hour decisions works. If you're interesting-but-not-obviously-yes, demanding a 48-hour decision on a multi-million-dollar investment gets you passed on. It's very hard to email back a week later saying "just kidding, you have more time." Run the process at the pace of the deal.

15. **Never tell a VC you're struggling.** Don't say "we're running out of money." Don't say "growth is slow because we're fundraising." Don't say "we've met 60 VCs and you're my last hope." Even if any of it is true. It's an instant delete. There are no excuses in fundraising.

16. **Be honest about everything that diligence will uncover.** Co-founder quitting, pilots that aren't signed deals, pieces of the product you don't actually own. 7 times out of 10 it's caught in diligence and the deal blows up. 3 times out of 10 you get away with it, and then trust is broken forever once the VC finds out post-close. Not worth it. Lead with the gaps — every VC knows every startup has them.

17. **Know your competition cold.** Every great CEO and every great salesperson does. "We don't really have competitors" is a near-instant no. You have competitors. Name them. Tell me why you win each one. If you can't, go spend a week on it before you take another meeting.

18. **Be yourself. 50% of fundraising is the VC getting to know the CEO.** Don't act like a CEO you saw on Twitter. Don't make the deck look like a Business Insider template. Don't pretend to be a sales person if you're a product person. Overindex on what you're passionate about. Rehearse. But be you. Fake CEOs get sniffed out in one meeting.

## Signature Numbers

- **20 minutes** — the window in which the initial yes / maybe / no is made. 95% of VCs know by the end of the first meeting.
- **3 months of strong growth in a row** — the minimum pattern before you open a process. Early-stage VCs will forgive 12–24 slow months before that.
- **3–4 months of cash in the bank** — the minimum you need on the balance sheet when you start raising. Less than that and VCs smell weakness.
- **18 months** — the typical runway a round buys you. Max 24.
- **2x–3x valuation** — the hurdle your existing investors expect at the next round. 2x minimum, 3x for seed investors. Less than 2x, you're in round-extension or down-round territory.
- **Triple-Triple-Double-Double** — the growth pace that clears the valuation hurdle. Going from $10K MRR to $20K MRR does not. Going from $10K MRR to $2M ARR in the window does.
- **2% of the fund** — the check size most mid-sized funds want to write for 10–20% ownership. A $150M fund is comfortable writing $3M at $20M. Not $15M.
- **$1 cash : $2 ARR** — the balance-sheet rule. Less than that on the balance sheet and you're underinvesting. One of the strongest arguments to raise even when you don't strictly need it.
- **Half of what you're raising now** — the rough upper bound on how much capital you should have already burned to get here. Burn more than $3–4M to $1M ARR and the venture math gets hard.
- **1-and-30** — 1 hour a week meeting a new investor, 30 minutes a week updating existing ones. 52 weeks a year. Never stop.
- **2x–3x at seed for second-time founders** — the valuation premium the market gives repeat founders. That's a data point, not a rule. Use it when it's offered; don't demand it to prove you're worthy.
- **7 of 10** — the rate at which lies about co-founders, pilots, or the product get caught in diligence and blow up the deal.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $1.8M ARR, growing 8% month over month for the last two months. First month was flat. Board wants to kick off the Series A next week.
**Application:** Don't start next week. You have 2 good months. You need 3 — and ideally 4. Push the kickoff 4–6 weeks. In the meantime, run the 1-and-30: meet 1 VC a week informally, keep nurturing. When you open, you'll have a 3-month pattern to point to, plus warm relationships with 4–6 partners who've seen your numbers climb in real time. Also — back-solve the clock. If the A closes in 5 months, you'll need to be at ~$5M ARR by Series B for a 2.5x markup. Is the team ready to Triple-Triple from here? If not, what breaks? Solve that before you raise.
**Result:** Founder waits 6 weeks. Opens with 3 strong months in a row. Three of the 1-and-30 nurtured VCs come in hot. Two term sheets in 3 weeks, closes at a clean 2.8x last round. Goes into the round knowing exactly what the 18-month hurdle looks like.

**Situation:** Founder at $8M ARR, growing 90%, profitable, has a ton of inbound VC interest. Asks whether to raise or not.
**Application:** If your balance sheet is strong (less than $1 cash per $2 ARR is the weak line — if you're above that, strong), if you don't know where you'd deploy the capital, and if you're already at the growth rate you want — don't raise just to raise. Most founders I know who took money they didn't need regret it. More stakeholders, more board seats, higher expectations on the next round. That said — if the terms are exceptional, and you can take meaningful founder secondary to de-stress, and you're 99% sure you're all-in for 10 more years to IPO, then yes, raise. But don't raise at an unexceptional valuation just because you can.
**Result:** Founder runs the test. Balance sheet is at $1 per $1.50 ARR — slightly weak. Terms are good but not extraordinary. Passes on this round. Grows another 4 quarters, raises 18 months later at 2x the valuation on the table today, with less dilution and more leverage. The right pass.

**Situation:** Founder sends a cold email to a VC. It's generic, mentions the VC by the wrong fund name, asks for "30 minutes to grab coffee and share the deck." No metrics. No deck attached. No specifics about why the VC is the right fit.
**Application:** This is an auto-archive for nearly every VC, including me. You've hit 4 of the 13 red flags in one email: (1) spray-and-pray generic outreach, (2) not knowing who you're emailing, (3) asking for coffee with no information, (4) putting too many gates in front of the key info. Rewrite. Make it short — 3 paragraphs, max. Lead with 4–5 metrics that would make the VC lean in: ARR, growth rate, retention, customer count. Name a specific investment the VC has made that's adjacent to yours and explain why you're a fit. Attach the full deck. Tell them what you're raising and why now. That's it. If the email is great, you get a meeting that week. If it's not, no warm intro will save it.
**Result:** Founder rewrites. Gets 8 replies out of 40 targeted emails. 5 first meetings. 2 second meetings. Closes the round from that batch. The delta isn't luck — it's that the email actually gave VCs enough info to decide to lean in within 30 seconds.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Send a 3-page teaser deck with no data.**
*Why:* Selling stock is sales. Lead with your best shot. A teaser deck says "I'm too good to share details" — which works only if you actually are. For the 99% who aren't, the VC politely passes because they can't see enough to lean in. I've passed on genuinely interesting founders just because their teaser deck told me nothing I couldn't get from their homepage. Lead with detail, not mystery.

**Don't: Fundraise after one great month.**
*Why:* One month isn't a trend. It's a blip. VCs know this. You'll burn your best prospects on a pitch the numbers can't back up, and now those VCs have you in their head as "slow growth" for the next 12 months. Wait for 3 in a row. VCs will almost completely overlook 12–24 previously-slow months if they see 3 strong ones right before the pitch. But one? No.

**Don't: Run a "tight process" with 48-hour decisions if you're not genuinely hot.**
*Why:* If you're the deal of the quarter, demand-side urgency works. If you're interesting-but-not-obvious, demanding a 48-hour decision on a multi-million-dollar commitment gets you passed on. VCs don't risk their fund on founders who won't give them time to get to know the company. A top-2 VC in the space said no to one of my portfolio companies because "we'd need time to dig in." When I promised them time, they funded the deal. The urgency play backfired them right out of a winner.

**Don't: Ask for way more capital than the fund wants to write.**
*Why:* Most mid-sized funds want to write 2% of fund size for 10–20% ownership. Ask a $150M fund for $15M and they pass — it's 10% of the fund in one deal, too concentrated. Ask them for $3M at $20M and they lean in. Many deals die because founders didn't do the math on fund size vs. check size. If you genuinely need $15M, target funds that are $500M+. Don't show up to the wrong fund and then get mad when they pass.

**Don't: Ask for coffee.**
*Why:* I don't have time for coffee. No VC does. A coffee ask with no info tells me you don't know enough to pitch, or you're not serious enough to lead with substance. Instead: tell me in the email why you rock, why you win, what the metrics are. If it's exciting, I'll want to meet that day. Coffee is how you meet friends. A pitch is how you raise money. Don't confuse them.

**Don't: Have a banker, agent, or contractor reach out on your behalf.**
*Why:* Close to an auto-delete for most VCs at seed / Series A. Selling your stock is your job as CEO. If you're hiring it out, it signals either that you don't have the chops or that you don't care enough. Later-stage — growth rounds, pre-IPO — bankers are fine, sometimes required. Seed and Series A: the CEO reaches out. Always.

**Don't: Tell VCs you're running low on money or struggling to find investors.**
*Why:* Instant delete. VCs have empathy — just not about writing checks. Scarcity signals from you tell them time is on their side, and you'll take a worse deal than you should. If you're 3 months from zero, don't say it. Raise at 6+ months runway from a position of strength, or don't raise — figure out a bridge with your existing investors instead.

**Don't: Raise at a unicorn valuation just because you can.**
*Why:* $1B+ valuations lock out many acquirers, they require you to actually exit well above $1B to make those investors whole, and they shorten the runway on your M&A options. Loom raised above $1B and sold for $1B. If you need the cash and you're truly all-in for a 10-year IPO path, a unicorn round makes sense. Otherwise, raising at $400M with clean terms often sets up a better ultimate outcome than raising at $1B with blocker terms. The valuation isn't the prize. The outcome is.

**Don't: Assume the clock is softer than it is.**
*Why:* The moment the wire hits, the countdown starts. Soft at first. Louder at month 10. Much louder at month 15. The round funds 18 months. Existing investors want a 2–3x markup at the next round. If you think you can cruise the first 6 months and then sprint, you're already 6 months behind. Pre-wire the plan: what ARR, what growth rate, what team — such that at month 15 you can open the next round from strength. If you can't pre-wire it, don't sign.
