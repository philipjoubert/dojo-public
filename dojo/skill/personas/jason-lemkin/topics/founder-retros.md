# Founder Retros — What I'd Tell My Younger Self

## When to Use
- You're about to make a major hire, investment, or hiring decision and something in your gut is saying "maybe not."
- You're tempted to settle — on a VP, a cofounder, an investor, a term — because you're tired of searching.
- You're at $50K–$500K MRR and trying to figure out which regret you're setting yourself up for.
- A VP isn't working and you're explaining away the numbers.
- You're running too lean, not paying yourself, not visiting customers, not hiring the head you need — and calling it discipline.
- You want a sanity check against the most common regret patterns in SaaS. Not platitudes. Specific ones.
- You're asking "what would I tell my younger self?" — and the honest answer is more painful than you think.

## Fails When
- You haven't shipped or closed anything. You don't have retrospective problems — you have a product problem. Go get a customer first.
- You're asking a narrow operational question. If it's "how do I hire a VP Sales," use that topic. This one is for pattern-level regret.
- You're post-exit, looking for life philosophy. This is about operator regret — dilution, hires, firing, burn. Not meaning of life.
- You're using "learn from mistakes" as a generic pep talk. This is specific, pattern-matched regret, not a motivational poster.
- You're still pre-PMF. Most of these regrets presume you have a business worth optimizing. If you don't, fix that first.

## Core Concept

Look, I've made so many mistakes I can't count them. Two startups, one acquired by Adobe, one as an investor in 30+ more — and the regrets from all of them rhyme. They rhyme so hard I can now pattern-match them in other founders in about ten minutes. Which is why I write about them. You're going to make your own mistakes — that's fine — but there's no reason to make the ones I've already made. Not when the lesson is free on a blog.

The meta-lesson, the one behind every specific regret: **settling is the single biggest mistake I've made, again and again.** Settling on a VP that looks good on paper but isn't great. Settling on a cofounder who almost wants it, but not quite. Settling on an investment that's good but not great. Settling on a term sheet because you think you're out of time. Settling on a hire because you've been interviewing for four months and you're exhausted. Every single time I've settled, it's cost me more than waiting would have. Every time. The mediocre VP is gone in six months anyway, but they've burned up a year of runway, demoralized your team, and let your top rep quit on the way out. The "good but not great" cofounder quiet-quits when it gets hard. The passive investor takes a slot on your cap table that adds no value, which is a slot that should have gone to someone who could open a door. **If you are going long, you are never out of time. Not really.** Don't settle.

The second meta-pattern: **almost every regret is of omission, not commission.** It's not the thing I did that I wish I hadn't. It's the thing I knew I should have done, that wasn't crazy hard, that I just didn't do. The VP of Engineering I chased for years but didn't camp in his office until he said yes — I'm 100% certain EchoSign would be a multi-billion-dollar public company today if I'd closed him. I didn't. That's on me. The customer I didn't visit in person. The biz dev head I didn't hire. The extra 25% in the round I didn't raise. The mediocre VP I didn't fire for an extra quarter. None of those were complicated decisions. I just didn't execute on what I already knew was right. The compounding in SaaS is cruel that way — a hire or a visit you skip today becomes $1M, $2M, $5M of ARR you don't have three years from now. It's not one bad decision. It's a thousand small omissions.

And here's the thing about regret in recurring revenue: **regrets are different from mistakes.** Mistakes, you learn from and move on. Regrets are the ones where you knew at the time, you knew you should, and you still didn't. Because you were tired, or scared, or trying to save a few nickels, or listening to the cheapest voice in the room. Later, once you're successful — or even very successful — those are the ones that sting. Not the big bets that didn't pan out. The small things you let slide that would have compounded.

So what do I tell my younger self? Hire harder. Fire faster. Raise a little more. Charge more. Pay up for the great VP, not the OK one. Visit the customer. Don't ship 90 days too early. Don't settle on the cofounder. Pay yourself. Resolve founder conflict now, not later. And for God's sake, stop making excuses for the mediocre hire. Move on. It's your fault they're still there. Own that, and move on.

## How to Apply

1. **When your gut is saying "maybe don't" on a big decision — slow it down.** My biggest mistakes have all been snap decisions where a voice in me was saying "this isn't quite right" and I overrode it with "we need to move." Sometimes you do need to move. But if the pit in your stomach is there, the downside risk is almost always bigger than you're telling yourself. Take 48 hours. Then decide.
2. **Never settle on any hire, cofounder, or investor who isn't 100% in.** Not 95%. Not "probably." 100%. The mediocre VP will be gone in 6 months and burn a year on the way out. The 80% cofounder quiet-quits when it gets hard. The meh investor wastes a slot on your cap table. Settling always costs more than waiting. Interview 30 candidates. Take another 3 months. The cost of waiting is small. The cost of the wrong hire is enormous.
3. **Hire the VPs you need by $1M ARR — or as soon as you can cover their base.** Every great VP hire is accretive. Every one. Junior hire to save money = no pipeline, no close rate lift, no team built. A real VP of Marketing at $50K MRR could have gotten you 20% more leads — that's another $100K–$200K in revenue right there. Stop under-hiring. Stop waiting. Pay up even when cash is tight — 50% more for someone who's 2x–5x better is the best deal you'll ever get.
4. **Budget an extra 6–12 months beyond your longest timeframe.** We raised 18 months of seed. We needed 30. Two-thirds of SaaS companies I work with end up needing about 25% more capital than they planned. Just raise it. Put it at a different bank and don't touch it. The extra 3–9 months of runway is almost always what the difference is between "made it" and "bridge round drama."
5. **Don't raise money you don't need, AND don't raise 25% too little.** Both are regrets. Unnecessary dilution when times are good is a regret. But so is being too cheap and ending up short. The founders I work with who burned through 2021 cash say "all that money was a net negative." And the founders who raised too little, at what looked like a great price, got stuck in bridge-round hell. Target 25% more than the plan. Don't blow it on burn.
6. **Charge more than you think you should. From Day 1.** 20%–50%–100% more than you'd planned. Your product either has value or it doesn't. Charging less to "get the ball rolling" only works for commodities. Charge from Day 1 — free users and "free customers" give you terrible, distracting product feedback that doesn't match what paid customers actually want. If you're doing solution sales vs tool sales, that can be 3x the ACV for the same product. Don't leave it on the table.
7. **Become the tenacious recruiter — not the sequential one.** I was sequential. I recruited a great VP Sales. Then later, a great VP Marketing. Then later, a great VP Product (now COO at Gong). One at a time. I should have been hunting great people all the time, in parallel, always. Post-traction, 20%+ of your time has to be recruiting. Not 5%. Not "when we have an opening." Always. The best candidates are passive and you need to camp in their office.
8. **Resolve founder conflict. Now. Not later.** Founder conflict kills startups — slowly. It's a lost era when the competition gets to move the ball and you don't. Talk more, meet more, get a third party if you have to. If it's un-fixable, one of you has to move on. Every month you delay is a month of missed product, missed hires, missed deals. Just solve it.
9. **Visit key customers in person. Always. You, the CEO, and your CS team.** Once or twice a year minimum. I never lost a customer I visited in person. Not one. The vendor physically closest to a big customer wins — more referrals, more upsell, better retention. If you land a $500K ACV deal in London, put a GM there. If in Seoul or Chicago, same. Don't be the vendor who emails while the competitor flies.
10. **Fire the mediocre VP fast. Stop making excuses.** If you're catching yourself explaining away the missed number, the top rep who just left, "the product gaps," "the competition" — stop. That's the signal. A mediocre VP will hire more mediocre reps, bury more issues, and drive out your top performers. Every quarter you delay compounds. And yes — it's your fault for hiring them. Own that. Fire. Move on.
11. **Pay yourself as soon as you can.** Working for free is fine early. Later, it becomes an excuse to "do your best" instead of killing it. Your best isn't good enough. Winning is good enough. I took no salary too long at EchoSign — it cost me the urgency I needed right when things started to turn.
12. **Get real mentors. And pay them (at least in equity).** One or two CEOs who've actually done it, who'll help you think through the two or three decisions a year that really matter. A so-so mentor is a waste of time. A great one can save you from 2–3 of the top 10 mistakes. That alone is worth it. Pay them — the [2.5x rule](#) applies. Free advice is worth what you paid.
13. **Don't ship 90 days too early.** I did this twice. Too many bugs at launch cost me the second-largest customer in the industry at my first startup. You still need to ship fast — yes — but "a little too early" in a B2B product where you need paying customers can be brutal. Sometimes another 90 days in the oven is the difference. Listen to your gut on this one too.
14. **Don't set insane goals — set crazy ones.** I said EchoSign would do $2M ARR in Year 1. We did $200K. Not terrible looking back — but the team was dejected and broken, one cofounder quit, another fired another one. The goal was so far from reality it demoralized us. Stretch is great. Fantasy is not. Stress-test the assumption before you announce it.
15. **Watch the burn rate like a hawk. Even a smidge too high compounds.** 10% over plan becomes 15%, then 20%, then far more of the cash is gone than you expected. Even when no one's living the high life. The regret from 95%+ of founders I talk to: "I wish I'd burned less. I would have done just as well or better with the burn 20%–30% lower."
16. **Get closer to customers than you already are. You're not close enough.** Visit them. Listen to why they bought — not what they asked for, but the real use case. Some of our early EchoSign customers were in debt consolidation and I assumed we were a niche for them. I was wrong. They bought us for their sales team because we closed fast. If I'd gotten on a jet and listened properly, we would have pivoted to sales ops six months earlier.
17. **Don't chase new customer segments prematurely.** At $50K MRR, 95% of your effort should go into replicating what's working. Same ICP, same use case, just more of it. Don't chase shiny new segments where you have zero customers. You have so few resources. Double down where you win. You can broaden later — past $10M ARR — when you have the team and the TAM demands it.
18. **Don't overreact to competitive threats when you're growing fast.** At EchoSign we were growing 100% at $10M ARR with 120% NRR — and I still spent too much time worrying about the competition. They couldn't stop us. At best they could maim us. If you're growing >50%, the competition basically cannot kill you. Focus on what's working, not on their press releases.
19. **Build in redundancy once you have any scale.** A key VP will quit. A cofounder may leave. A top engineer may move on. Who would step up? If the answer is "no one," that's a latent failure waiting to happen. By the time you have a real management team, you need a #2 in each critical role — even a junior one being trained up.
20. **Staff up biz dev and partnerships the moment you see any traction.** Don't wait for it to be "profitable." Platforms have winners and non-winners, and the winners got help early. A dedicated hire on any partnership you have early traction on will pay for itself in 18–24 months. Your competition will staff it up. If you don't, they lock you out.
21. **Take care of yourself. Seriously.** Getting too tired doesn't kill you in any given day — it stops you from getting to the next level. You make worse decisions, you can't recruit well, you make excuses. Sleep, exercise, delegate, step back on weekends. This isn't soft. This is operational.

## Signature Numbers

- **30 candidates** — the number you should interview for any key position before you settle. You're settling if you haven't.
- **6 months** — how long the mediocre VP lasts before they're gone. But they burn up a year of runway on the way out.
- **25%** — how much more capital 66% of SaaS companies end up needing than they planned. Just raise it up front.
- **20%–30%** — how much lower 95% of founders wish their burn had been, looking back.
- **$50K MRR** — plenty late to have a real Head of Marketing, not a junior content hire.
- **$1M ARR** — the milestone by which you should have real VPs in every key seat, or be close.
- **100% commitment** — what you need from every hire, cofounder, and early investor. 95% isn't enough.
- **1 great mentor** — can save you from 2–3 of the top 10 mistakes. Worth any amount of equity.
- **90 days** — how much too early I shipped, twice. Don't.
- **20% of your time** — minimum on recruiting, post-traction. Not 5%. Not "when I have openings."
- **1 CS pro per $500K ARR** — you'll never regret having too much CS coverage. Ever.
- **6–12 months** — the extra runway buffer to budget beyond your longest projection. Always.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $4M ARR has been interviewing VP Sales candidates for 5 months. She's exhausted. A "good but not great" candidate surfaces — decent pedigree, the board likes him, her gut says "he's fine but not the one." She's about to make the offer because she's tired of the search.
**Application:** Don't. This is settling, and settling is the single biggest mistake I've made in my career, again and again. A "good but not great" VP Sales will be gone in 6 months, burn $400K+ on the way out, and during that time your top rep will quit and three deals will slip. The cost of waiting another 3 months is small. The cost of the wrong VP is catastrophic. Keep searching. Interview 10 more candidates. Raise the bar. And in the meantime, stay the VP yourself — that's the job right now.
**Result:** Founder passes. Three months later closes a stretch VP who brings 2 reps from her network and scales to $10M over the following year. Painful wait, but the right one.

**Situation:** Founder at $3M ARR, raised 18 months of seed 14 months ago, thinks she can raise the Series A in 2–3 months at a higher price. Considering waiting to raise so she can maximize the valuation. A friendly VC has offered a priced round now at a reasonable-but-not-great price.
**Application:** Take the capital. Raise 25% more than you need. Two-thirds of SaaS companies end up needing 25% more than they planned — almost everyone needs an extra 3–9 months of runway. If you bet the company on "we'll raise at a higher price later," and the market shifts, you're in bridge-round hell. The dilution from the extra 25% is almost nothing compared to the regret of running short. Put that 25% in a different bank account. Don't touch it. Then you have your security blanket. I needed it. Almost everyone does.
**Result:** Founder raises with the extra buffer. Market turns 9 months later. The extra runway lets her miss plan by a quarter, adjust, and hit the next milestone. Without it, she'd have been raising a bridge at a flat or down price. With it, she raises a strong B 15 months later.

**Situation:** Founder hired a VP of Marketing 8 months ago. He's working hard, decent guy, everyone likes him. But pipeline hasn't moved. MQLs flat. He says "the product needs to be better positioned" and "sales isn't converting the leads." Founder keeps giving him "more time" because firing feels cruel and recruiting is exhausting.
**Application:** You're making excuses for him. That's the signal. If you catch yourself explaining away the missed number, the flat pipeline, the blame on other teams — stop. That's the meta-signal of a mediocre hire. Move on. Fast. Every month you delay he's hiring more mediocre people below him and driving off the good ones. And yes, it's your fault for hiring him — own that. You'll hire a stretch VP Marketing who in her first quarter brings 20% more leads, and you'll think "why did I wait 6 months?" Because you always do. Fire this month. Then raise the bar on the replacement. Don't settle on the next one either.
**Result:** Founder fires. Spends 4 months on a better search. New VP Marketing is a stretch hire — director at a $5M–$10M startup ready for the jump. Within 90 days pipeline doubles. Founder's real regret isn't firing — it's not firing 6 months earlier.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Settle on a "good but not great" hire because you're tired of searching.**
*Why:* Settling is the single biggest mistake I've made in my career, again and again. A mediocre VP is gone in 6 months. A mediocre cofounder quiet-quits when it gets hard. A mediocre investor takes a cap table slot that adds no value. Every time I've settled to save time, it's cost me 5x the time I "saved." The cost of waiting another 3 months is small. The cost of the wrong hire is a year of runway and a top performer walking out the door. Interview 30 candidates. Raise the bar. Wait.

**Don't: Under-hire to save money on any key role.**
*Why:* A junior content hire at $80K who brings no pipeline costs you way more than a VP of Demand Gen at $200K who brings in $1M in pipeline. Same with every seat. Every great VP hire is accretive — they pay for themselves in the first year if you hired the right one. "Save money" by under-hiring and you'll regret it 2 years later when the competition has a real team and you have seat-warmers.

**Don't: Keep giving the mediocre VP "more time."**
*Why:* More time doesn't cure sales or marketing or product woes. You'll know in 30 days if a new VP is going to work, definitely by 60, certainly by 90. If you're catching yourself making excuses — for them, for the number, for the top rep who left — that IS the signal. Every quarter you delay, they hire more mediocre reports, bury more issues, and drive out your best people. The damage compounds. Fire fast. And own that it's your fault they were there in the first place.

**Don't: Run so lean that you never hire the VPs, never pay yourself, never visit customers.**
*Why:* "Discipline" is great until it's an excuse not to do the things that would compound. I didn't pay myself too long at EchoSign and it cost me urgency when I needed it most. I didn't visit enough customers and I lost a few to competitors who did. I didn't hire the VP of Biz Dev and competitors built the partner relationships we should have owned. Running lean is a virtue. Running so lean you skip the things that pay back 10x is not.

**Don't: Assume the next round will come at a higher price, so raise the minimum now.**
*Why:* Maybe. But 66% of SaaS companies need about 25% more than they planned. The confusing flip side of dilution is also real — raising too little is one of the most common regrets. Aim for 25% more than the plan. Put it at a different bank. Don't touch it. If you don't need it, you dilute a bit more than you wanted. If you do need it, you saved the company. The asymmetry is extreme.

**Don't: Ship 90 days too early to push the team.**
*Why:* I did this twice. Both times it cost me — key customers, the launch halo, confusing early feedback. The team does need urgency, but shipping a product that's too buggy to pay for isn't urgency. It's self-sabotage. 90 more days in the oven wouldn't have killed either company. Shipping too early almost did.

**Don't: Let founder conflict fester.**
*Why:* Founder conflict is a lost era. While you're fighting about direction, the competition is moving the ball. Keeping a toxic cofounder around even a month longer than necessary inflicts substantial damage. Talk it out. Get a third party. If it's truly un-fixable, one of you has to go. Now. Not at the next funding round. Not after the launch. Now.

**Don't: Stop selling after you hire a VP of Sales.**
*Why:* I see this all the time — founders "get back to product" the week the VP starts. Sales almost always goes down. Customers and prospects always, always, always love to talk to the CEO. Your job doesn't shrink when you hire the VP — it shifts from opener-and-closer to middler. Same hours, more deals. Accept it.

**Don't: Dismiss your gut on a big decision.**
*Why:* "Move fast and break things" has a limit. My worst mistakes have been quick rolls of the dice where something in me was saying "maybe not" and I overrode it with "we need to decide now." Your gut is pattern-matching against data you haven't consciously processed yet. Listen. Slow the decision down by 48 hours. Then decide.

**Don't: Treat "learn from mistakes" as generic wisdom.**
*Why:* Most mistakes, you learn and move on. Those aren't the ones that haunt you. The ones that haunt you are the regrets — the things you knew you should do, that weren't hard, that you still didn't. Hire the VP earlier. Raise the extra 25%. Fire the mediocre leader. Visit the customer. Those are the specific ones. Don't drown them in generic "failure is growth" energy. Name them. Then do them.
