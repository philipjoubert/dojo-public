# Exits and M&A

## When to Use
- You've got a real, bona fide acquisition offer — signed LOI or close to it — and you're trying to decide whether to take it.
- You're at $5m–$50m ARR, growth is still decent, and someone has reached out about buying you.
- You're burned out, cash is tight, or you can feel the ceiling, and you're considering whether this is the moment.
- You're pre-$1m ARR and got an aqui-hire offer from a bigger company, and your VCs are telling you to decide.
- You're thinking about proactively shopping the company to a list of potential buyers.
- You just signed an LOI, the deal is closing in 60 days, and you want to know what to do before and after close.
- Your first startup sold, and you're now asking whether to go do another one.

## Fails When
- You don't have PMF yet and there's no real buyer. This is not an M&A conversation — it's a survival conversation. Don't waste cycles here.
- You're trying to use a fake acquisition threat to pressure your VCs into a bridge round. That's politics, not M&A. Different problem.
- The deal already closed, the wire already cleared, and you're 6 months in. You're past strategy. You're in integration. Most of this doesn't apply.
- It's a lifestyle business with no VCs, no preferences, no liquidation waterfall. The mechanics here assume a venture cap table. Back that out.
- You're asking whether to outbound-cold-email Google corp dev to ask them to buy you. Don't. It doesn't work that way — see below.

## Core Concept

I'll tell you what I've learned, and I'll be personal about it because this is the one topic in SaaS where the scar tissue is mine, not a client's. I sold EchoSign to Adobe at roughly $1m a month — call it $12m ARR — and I've had more than a decade to think about it. It was the biggest business mistake of my life. Not close. Not debatable. At the time it looked reasonable — cloud was smaller, the competitive set was more uncertain, there were personal reasons, the cap table had issues. All of that was true. And none of it changes the fact that if I had just gone long — held on another five, seven, ten years — the business would have compounded into something many, many multiples larger. I left that money on the table. I left the mission on the table. I left the team on the table. That's the regret I've carried, and it shapes every piece of advice I give founders about exits.

Here's what I tell founders now, and it's the opposite of what I used to say. I used to pretend to be neutral — "weigh both sides." I'm done with that. My actual position: if you're a real SaaS business with 100%+ NRR, with a real team, with a real product, and you're under $50m ARR — default to no. The odds you can 10x this thing if you just don't quit are higher than you think. SaaS compounds. Revenue compounds, brand compounds, customer base compounds, the team compounds. Every year you don't quit you get stronger. And most founders who sell under $50m ARR regret it within three years, once they see where the company would have been if they'd just kept going. I've watched this play out in my portfolio dozens of times. The regret is almost always "I sold too early," almost never "I held too long."

But — and this is the caveat — default to no is not the same as always no. There are three situations where you take the deal and don't look back. One: you're burned out in a way that sleep and a vacation won't fix, and the company won't survive you phoning it in. Two: you can see a real ceiling — market is shrinking, tech is being disrupted, a BigCo just launched a competitive product with ten times your distribution. Three: the strategic window is unusually good — one specific acquirer is uniquely motivated right now, there are only a handful of plausible buyers ever, and corporate priorities are fickle. Slack got $27B from Salesforce in 2020. Two years later Salesforce disbanded its M&A committee. That deal would never happen today, not at that price, maybe not at any price. If you say no to a good deal, mean it — because the same deal almost never comes back. The CEO moves on. The SVP moves on. The market moves on. Plan accordingly.

The other thing I'll say, because nobody else will say it plainly: **don't be a serial entrepreneur in SaaS.** Serial entrepreneurs, in B2B SaaS, are for suckers. Everything in SaaS compounds — if you have even a couple million in ARR and 100%+ NRR, the odds you have a *better* idea are tiny. You don't. You have this one. Don't quit. Don't sell and go start another thing. The single richest person in B2B tech is Larry Ellison, and he's never sold a share of Oracle. He just kept going. If you have momentum, if you grew even 40% last year, make your existing company better. Ship more product. Hire a better VP. Add a second product line. Don't tilt. A SaaS business with 120% NRR is almost impossible to kill if you keep feeding it — and founders who sell that business to go chase the next shiny idea almost always look back five years later at what their old company became and ask themselves what they were thinking. I know. I did.

## How to Apply

1. **Default to no under $50m ARR if growth is real.** If you're growing 60%+ at $10m, 40%+ at $30m, and NRR is north of 100%, don't sell. Almost any offer you're getting today, you will regret in three years. The compounding math is relentless — even at mediocre 50% growth past $10m ARR, you get to $100m in nine years. Most founders don't have something better to do with those nine years. I didn't.

2. **But default to yes if the offer is good and the answer isn't obviously no.** If you're not sure, take the offer. Great M&A offers are far rarer than the media suggests — only about 58 software deals of $50m+ happen per year in the US. A second good offer often just never comes. Don't hold out for perfection. If it's good and you're ambivalent, take it.

3. **Do the real math, not the headline math.** The founders I know who turned down a $50m offer at $1m ARR and later sold for $1B after eight years of grinding? Most of them walked away with roughly the same personal cash as they would have had in the original $50m deal — because of dilution, liquidation preferences, and time. Headline number lies. Run the waterfall. Model the dilution. Look at your actual take-home after prefs, carve-outs, and taxes.

4. **Recognize that acquisitions are bought, not sold.** 95 out of 100 proactive attempts to sell a startup fail. You can't email Google corp dev and make a deal happen. Real M&A comes from relationships built over years. In both my startups, we "met" the acquirer about five years before the deal closed. The actual transaction took 60 days — but the relationship took five years. Start building those relationships now, even if you're not selling. Meet the BigCos. Know the corp dev people. Let them see you grow.

5. **Understand what the buyer actually wants.** Three buckets: revenue (PE firms, usually above $20m ARR), tech (Big Tech filling a product gap they could build but don't have time to), or team (small deals, aqui-hires). Your pitch changes based on which one. Adobe didn't buy EchoSign for our revenue — we were $1m/month and they were $4B ARR. They bought us to spark a SaaS and Cloud transition in Document Cloud. The revenue was immaterial. Know what you're actually selling before you negotiate.

6. **Tell an aqui-hire from an acqui-kill.** An aqui-hire pays the team for 2–4 years of retention, kills the product, absorbs the talent. Fine outcome if you're pre-PMF or out of runway. An acqui-kill buys you to shut you down so you stop competing — look for: they don't want the product, they don't want the brand, the retention packages are short, no one's planning a post-close roadmap. Don't sell to an acqui-kill unless the cash is life-changing.

7. **Never run out of cash during a deal.** Keep 9+ months of runway. M&A teams can smell blood. If they know you're three months from payroll problems, every offer gets worse. Every term gets worse. Leverage is everything in this world and cash is leverage.

8. **Hire an M&A banker for any deal over $30m. 95% of the time.** They won't always get you a better headline number. They will save you from yourself — on tax structure, on deal terms, on earnout language, on working capital mechanics. And they're the buffer. You don't want to be the one pushing back on the low-ball, arguing the indemnity cap, fighting over the escrow. The one exception: a genuinely friendly deal from a CEO you know well who reaches out directly with a fair price. Don't break that trust by going banker-heavy. Ask for 20–50% more once, then make it easy.

9. **Get a second interested party, even a soft one.** You don't need a bidding war. You need one other credible interested buyer. When we got a second interested party during the Adobe deal, the term sheet showed up that week — Adobe had been telling us they needed 90 days. Competition compresses time and price. Even a soft competing interest is enough.

10. **Watch the deal structure, not just the topline.** Cash at close is real. Escrow is mostly real. Earnout is half real. Retention packages are not your money — they're future comp you have to work for. Stock in the acquirer is subject to their trading window, their lockup, their share price. If someone offers you "$200m" and it's $80m cash, $60m stock, $60m three-year earnout tied to hitting targets inside BigCo — that's not a $200m deal. That's an $80m deal with $120m of lottery tickets. Discount accordingly.

11. **If you say no, mean it.** Same deal almost never comes back. CEOs change. M&A strategy changes. Markets change. Salesforce wouldn't buy Slack today at $27B. Microsoft wouldn't buy Yammer today. If your gut says push on and you have something truly great, say no and don't look back. Just know what you're committing to.

12. **When you do sell, plan to stay 24 months, no matter what the comp package says.** Most founders want to bolt. Don't. Seeing the next chapter is valuable, and the thing you built mostly dies if you don't fight for it post-close. Identify your successor on day one — either on your team or theirs. Migrate yourself into a more strategic role. Give your product the best chance at surviving the 80% of acquisitions that fail.

13. **The rewarding part ends about 90 days after close.** No matter who the acquirer is. The initial high of the wire transfer and the press release fades fast. The post-close reality is BigCo meetings, integration friction, and watching priorities shift under you. Don't sell for prestige — don't sell because it'll be cool to tell people. Sell for dollars or don't sell. "Continue your vision inside of Google" is mostly a lie told during courtship.

14. **Don't be a serial entrepreneur.** Sell only if you have to. Then, if you do sell, think three times before starting another company. The compounding you walked away from is harder to recreate than you think. Nick Mehta sold his first company and lost five years of compounding before he got back to the same revenue base at Gainsight — and he's one of the best SaaS CEOs alive. If you've got a business with 100%+ NRR and real growth, the "next one" almost never beats the one you already have.

## Signature Numbers

- **$12m ARR** — where I sold EchoSign to Adobe. My biggest business mistake. The business went on to do hundreds of millions in ARR inside Adobe.
- **$50m ARR** — the line below which I tell founders to default to no on acquisition offers if growth is real.
- **~58 per year** — US reported software acquisitions of $50m+. Real M&A offers are rare. Don't assume another one is coming.
- **2.7%** — Carta's number for the odds of any real exit. Most startups don't get bought at any price.
- **95 out of 100** — proactive attempts to sell a startup that fail. Companies are bought, not sold.
- **5 years and 60 days** — typical timeline. 5 years to build the relationship; 60 days from "we're interested" to signed deal. If the second phase takes longer than 60 days, something is wrong.
- **9+ months runway** — what you need to preserve leverage in any M&A negotiation.
- **24 months** — how long to plan to stay post-acquisition, regardless of what the comp package says.
- **90 days** — how long after close the "rewarding" part lasts. Set expectations accordingly.
- **~2.5 years** — how long acquired founders typically stay at Google before leaving. Pattern is common across BigCos.
- **80 diligence people** — what Adobe put on our deal when we had fewer than 80 employees. Full diligence is brutal. Have a data room ready.
- **1x–2x** — the typical VC return on sub-$50m exits. Your VCs will say yes, but they won't be ecstatic. Know that going in.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is at $12m ARR, growing 80% YoY, 115% NRR. A big strategic has offered $150m cash. The founder is tired but the market is still growing. They ask whether to take the deal.

**Application:** Default to no. $12m ARR growing 80% with 115% NRR is exactly the business I had when I sold EchoSign, and I would not make that trade again. The compounding from here is brutal in your favor — at 80% growth with 115% NRR, you're at $30m+ in 18 months and $100m in 4 years, and the multiple expands as you scale. $150m today looks like a win; $1B+ in four years is plausible and probably your real trajectory. The "tired" part is real but separate — fix that by hiring a real COO and taking a real vacation, not by selling the company. The only reasons I'd take this deal: (1) a specific acquirer window that's closing and won't reopen, (2) a cap table problem where the waterfall means founders get a bigger slice now than later, or (3) genuine market disruption risk (AI is eating your category, a giant just launched a competitor with 100x your distribution). Absent one of those, push on.

**Result:** Founder pushes on. Eighteen months later they're at $30m ARR. Two years after that they get a $400m offer and take it. Founder's personal take-home, after dilution from a Series C, is roughly 2x what they would have gotten from the $150m deal. They don't regret passing.

**Situation:** A founder sold their first startup for $80m two years ago. They're now 18 months into a new SaaS company doing $1.5m ARR, growing slowly, and struggling to raise a Series A. They ask whether to keep pushing or wind it down and go do something else.

**Application:** This is the serial entrepreneur trap. You already know how hard the zero-to-one part is — you just did it. But the real money and the real impact came from the compounding, and you exited before you got the compounding from the first company. Now you're grinding up the mountain again from base camp. My honest advice: go work at your acquirer for two more years and run a big P&L there — or find a way to re-buy into a late-stage SaaS company with an existing compounding engine. The second time around, zero-to-one is not the best use of you. If you're committed to founding again, fine — but know that the instinct to "do another one" is usually nostalgia, not strategy. I've seen too many second-time founders give up three years of their life to get back to where the first startup already was.

**Result:** Founder doesn't love the answer but sits with it. Six months later winds down the new company, joins a $40m ARR SaaS as President with meaningful equity, rides it to $200m ARR, makes more money in that role than they did in the first exit. They're still frustrated they gave up the compounding on startup #1, but at least they stopped repeating the mistake.

**Situation:** A founder at $4m ARR has been approached by a BigCo that wants to "explore a strategic partnership." The founder shared their metrics in a deep-dive meeting. Two days later, the BigCo launched a competitive product. Founder is furious and asking whether to cut off communication.

**Application:** Don't. This is exactly what happened to me before Adobe bought EchoSign — literally the same thing. We shared metrics, a competitor launched inside the BigCo two days later, I was livid. That competitive product failed. Twelve months later, the same BigCo bought us for real money. In web services, almost nothing is that confidential — anyone with a credit card can figure out most of what they need to know about your business. What matters is the relationship. Most acquirers kick tires for years before they buy. A tire-kick today is often a real acquisition in 12–24 months. Keep the relationship warm. Don't share the truly secret stuff, but don't cut bridges either. Acquisitions take years to build and 60 days to execute. Stay in the game.

**Result:** Founder cools off, keeps the relationship warm, meets with the BigCo's corp dev team twice a year. Eighteen months later the BigCo's internal competitive product is killed, their SVP comes back asking to acquire, and a real deal happens at a much higher valuation. The "leak" turned out to be noise.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Sell under $50m ARR if you have a real business with growing revenue and 100%+ NRR.**
*Why:* SaaS compounds. Every year you don't sell, your revenue grows, your NRR compounds, your brand thickens, your team gets better. The founders I know who sold under $50m ARR and watched the industry go another five years almost always regret it. I sold at $12m. Don't. Default to no unless burnout, ceiling, or strategic window makes it obvious.

**Don't: Try to outbound sell your company.**
*Why:* 95 out of 100 proactive sale attempts fail. Companies are bought, not sold. Calling up Google corp dev and asking "want to buy us?" doesn't work — it signals desperation, kills your leverage, and almost never produces a deal. The real M&A comes from relationships you've built over years. If no one has approached you and you want to sell, the honest answer is you probably need to push on for another 18–24 months, or quietly take meetings with potential acquirers under the guise of partnership discussions and let them reach their own conclusion.

**Don't: Be a serial entrepreneur in B2B SaaS.**
*Why:* Everything in SaaS compounds. If you have a business with 100%+ NRR and any momentum at all, the odds you have a better idea elsewhere are tiny. I see founders sell at $10m, go start something new, and spend three-to-five years just getting back to the revenue base they already had. That's three-to-five years of compounding you gave up. Larry Ellison is the richest person in B2B tech. He never sold a share of Oracle. He just kept going. That's the model. Be a one-time winner, not a serial founder. Even if it's hard. Even if Q4 sucks. Even if Twitter says everyone else is growing 300%. Double down on what compounds.

**Don't: Confuse earnout with cash at close.**
*Why:* Earnouts are half-real. Retention packages are not your money — they're future comp you have to earn inside BigCo, subject to BigCo's politics, metrics, and decisions you no longer control. A $200m deal with $80m cash and $120m in 3-year earnouts is not a $200m deal. Stock in the acquirer is subject to their lockup and trading windows. When you model the deal, run it on cash-at-close plus a conservative probability-weighted estimate of the rest. That's your actual number. Everything else is a lottery ticket you're paying for with your next 2–3 years.

**Don't: Sell without 9+ months of cash in the bank.**
*Why:* M&A teams are professionals. They know exactly how much runway you have. If they can see you're three months from a down round or insolvency, every offer gets 20–40% worse and every deal term gets more punitive. Leverage in M&A is cash. No cash, no leverage. If you need to sell, start early — get to the table 9+ months before you'd run out of money, not 3 months before. Below 9 months, you're not negotiating, you're begging.

**Don't: Say no to a good deal without meaning it.**
*Why:* The same deal almost never comes back. Corporate priorities change every year. CEOs move on. M&A strategies get killed (Salesforce disbanded their M&A committee two years after the Slack deal). If you say no, you are committing to a multi-year push on a higher outcome — and if you don't have the gut for that push, if you waffle, if you're secretly hoping they come back in six months with the same number, you're going to be unhappy. Say no only if you're 95%+ sure you can build something 10x bigger. Otherwise default to yes.

**Don't: Skip the banker on any deal above $30m, except for genuinely friendly CEO-to-CEO deals.**
*Why:* Bankers don't always move the headline number meaningfully. But they'll save you from the rookie mistakes that bleed millions in post-close: bad tax structure, sloppy earnout definitions, weak working capital adjustments, insufficient escrow carve-outs, indemnity caps that blow up later. And they're the bad-cop buffer so you don't burn the founder-to-founder relationship with the acquirer. The one exception: if a CEO friend reaches out directly with a fair offer and a clean pitch, don't banker-up and play games. Ask for 20–50% more once, then close cleanly. Breaking trust in a friendly deal is how deals die.

**Don't: Plan to leave the day the wire clears.**
*Why:* Most acquisitions fail. 80%+ of the value gets destroyed in post-close integration. If you bolt on day 1, your product probably dies within 24 months. Plan to stay 24 months, mentally, regardless of what the comp package says. Identify your successor on your team or theirs. Migrate to a more strategic role. Fight to keep the product alive. The 90-day "rewarding" window fades fast — if you haven't set up the next chapter by then, you're checked out and coasting, and that's worse for you and worse for the product.
