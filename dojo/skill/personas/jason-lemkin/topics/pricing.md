# Pricing

## When to Use
- You're setting Day 1 pricing and the spread of "reasonable" numbers feels infinite — $20/seat, $200/seat, $20k/year, $200k/year all look plausible.
- You're sitting on 12 months of customer data and you can feel you're underpricing, but you can't prove it.
- You're designing a pricing page and trying to decide between one price, three tiers, or "Contact Us."
- A sales rep just closed a deal at 40% off list and your CRO is high-fiving them. You're wondering if you just got robbed.
- An AI-era buyer is pushing for a 1-year instead of a 3-year and your sales team is freaking out.
- Your ACV is $1,500 and you want to hire a field sales team. Someone needs to tell you no.
- You're about to raise prices on existing customers to make the quarter.

## Fails When
- You try to anchor price to cost. Software costs basically nothing to ship copy 10,001. There is no cost floor to hug. You're not a manufacturer.
- You price in a vacuum. Without comps, you're guessing. Without a "who else does the buyer pay?" anchor, you will underprice — every single time.
- You innovate on pricing the way you innovate on product. Buyers are confused by novel pricing. Use patterns they already know.
- You pick a pricing model that contradicts your ACV. $5k ACV with a field sales team is a slow-motion death. $200k ACV with a self-serve signup is leaving money on the floor.
- You raise prices 3-5x on existing customers overnight. You may make the month. You'll lose the reference calls, the case studies, and the referrals. That's worse than the quarter.
- You let sales over-discount to close. A 50%-off deal is structural damage — not a closed deal.

## Core Concept

Here's the thing most founders miss: there is no real reason any particular piece of software should cost anything in particular. Facebook and Google are free. Slack and Zoom are close to free. Dropbox is cheap. Salesforce is $300/seat. Workday is really expensive. ServiceNow closed 678 million-dollar deals in a single quarter. All of these apps cost roughly the same to build, host, and ship. The marginal cost is basically zero. So pricing isn't anchored to cost. It's anchored to comparables. That's the whole game.

The single most useful question in pricing is: who else does this buyer pay? That's your anchor. If you're selling to a Fortune 500 CFO and you know they pay Salesforce $12M a year, your ceiling isn't $50k — it's closer to $10M, depending on how your importance compares to revenue in that company. If you're selling to a VP of Support and they pay Zendesk $30/seat, your anchor is closer to $30, not $300. Pricing in software is almost entirely relative. Your job is to get the best comp and frame yourself against it — be the Cadillac of your space, or a few value layers up the stack from where your customers naively expect.

And the prior you should always start with is this: **you're almost certainly underpricing.** Most founders are scared of their own prices. They set them low because low feels safer, and then they never raise them because the existing customers scare them. The scar tissue from one bad early price-raise email stops them from raising prices on new customers for years. Don't be that founder. On your next big deal, ask for double. You may get a no. You may get a yes. You may get "yes, if you can do X." All three are useful data. You'll never learn this on your smallest customers. Learn it on your top 5–6 prospects.

The AI-era wrinkle on top of all this: contracts are getting shorter. Sub-1-year contracts for new logos went from 4% of deals in 2023 to 13% in 2026. Three-year deals dropped from 28% to 23%. Buyers aren't stalling — they're moving fast, but they won't lock in for three years when the market leader in their category might not exist in twelve months. Don't fight it with multi-year discounts. You'll win the deal and lose the renewal. Shorter contracts with stronger post-sales expansion is a completely viable business model. Arguably a healthier one.

## How to Apply

1. **Find your real comp before anything else.** Who else does your buyer pay? List the top 3 vendors in their stack. That's your anchor range. If you don't have direct competitors, anchor a few value layers up — if your customer success software is going to eat 70% of what Salesforce does for them, price at 70% of Salesforce. Not 10%. Not $20/seat because that "feels right."

2. **Pick one of the three Day 1 strategies. Don't invent a fourth.** (a) **Low End of Normal** — 80% of the market leader's price. Least friction, good for new apps building confidence. (b) **Identical Pricing** — same as the leader. Sends "we're the equal, pick on the merits." (c) **Anchor High** — priced above the leader, with a real enterprise story (security, audits, dashboards). Works only if it's true. Most founders should start at Low End of Normal and raise from there.

3. **Use three tiers. Not two. Not six.** Free or low / mid / enterprise. Buyers want a *few* choices — one cheaper, one premium, maybe an enterprise anchor. Tiers are well-understood (3/5/7 Series BMW). Buyers self-align — the F500 doesn't try to buy the single-seat plan, they grab the Enterprise tier without arguing. Tiers remove friction. Friction kills deals.

4. **Mark everything up ~20% so you can discount to target.** Enterprise buyers expect 10–15–20% off. Procurement is often *comped* on getting another cut. If you don't build the markup in, holding the line will feel cheap to them and will extend your cycle. Model the discount into the list price from day one. Even Salesforce does this.

5. **Match pricing to ACV, and match ACV to sales motion.** This is the part founders keep violating. $60–$1k ACV = word-of-mouth / PLG / AI self-serve. $1k–$5k ACV = inside sales, maybe AI-assisted. $5k–$20k ACV = blended inside + some field. $20k+ ACV = inside + field. $100k+ ACV = pure enterprise, suits, jets. Pick a pricing model that matches the motion you can afford. A $2k ACV with a $250k OTE field rep is a fake business.

6. **Raise prices regularly on new customers. Grandfather existing ones.** New customers pay the new rate. Existing customers keep the old rate — or get a small, equal, telegraphed bump (5–6%, 3–4 months' notice, applied to everyone). If you're growing 60%+ a year, don't bother touching existing prices at all. The $1M growing to $1.2M via a price-raise won't matter. The referrals and NPS you damage will.

7. **Never do the Qualcomm move.** Do not send an existing customer an invoice with a 3–5x price increase out of the blue. You'll make the month. You'll lose the reference, the case study, the intro calls to prospects. That customer becomes an anti-referral. If you must raise them, do it with a *new, truly optional* premium edition they can migrate to — or pair the price increase with a real value increase they can point to.

8. **On your next big deal, double your pricing.** Not on the Small Business tier. On your top 5–6 largest prospects. If your biggest customer is at $50k, ask the next one for $100k. Worst case you get a no. Best case you redefine your comp forever. Middle case you get "yes, if you can do X" — which tells you exactly what functionality is worth 2x. That's the most valuable pricing experiment you can run.

9. **Cap discounts with bright lines.** Reps will quote at list (max commission), then discount to the floor at end-of-quarter to hit quota. That's what a rational commissioned rep does. Your job is to give them a range, not eliminate the dance. 10–15% on a rep's own authority. 20% needs sales VP. 30% needs CEO. Above ~35–40% should almost never happen. Over-discounting past that point is structural damage to the brand and the renewal math — the rep doesn't know how to add value and is using price as the entire pitch.

10. **Accept shorter contracts in AI categories. Build a post-sales motion that earns the renewal.** Don't discount deeply to force a 3-year when the buyer wants a 1-year. You'll create a customer who resents the commitment by month 18. Instead, make your product so embedded in their workflow by month 3 that the renewal is a formality, not a negotiation. CS-sourced expansion wins at 52% — higher than sales (43%). Your comp plan should reward NDR and NRR, not just TCV.

11. **Never charge implementation fees in the early days.** A $2k implementation fee on a $299/month app sends "this is going to be hard." Kills more deals than the $2k is worth. Once you have a mini-brand and real enterprise customers? Fine, charge. Not before.

12. **Don't get cute on the first 100 customers.** Keep it simple, keep it fair, Zero Questions Need Be Asked. Those 100 customers are a rounding error in your eventual customer base. Get them in, let them grow, and spend your optimization energy on customers 101–10,000.

## Signature Numbers

- **~$0.0000232** — the actual cost of shipping one more copy of real software. There is no cost anchor. Get over it.
- **80% of market leader** — Low End of Normal pricing. The default Day 1 strategy.
- **2x** — how much more to ask on your next big deal. Most of the time you're not as far off as you fear.
- **10–15–20%** — the enterprise discount range buyers *expect*. Not getting one makes them feel ripped off. Mark everything up 20% so you can give it and still hit your target.
- **20%** — the across-the-board markup you bake in so discounts don't gut margin.
- **35–40%+** — over-discount territory. Structural damage. Signals desperation; leaves nothing to negotiate next.
- **5–6% a year, 3–4 months notice, applied equally** — the only price increase on existing customers that doesn't blow up NPS.
- **3 tiers** — free/low / mid / enterprise. Not 2, not 6.
- **$3k ACV** — the traditional floor for affording human sales reps in the US/Western Europe. AI has pushed that down to maybe $1.5k–$1k for inbound-heavy motions.
- **$20k ACV** — where blended inside + field becomes viable.
- **$100k+ ACV** — pure enterprise. Suits. Field. Jets.
- **4% → 13%** — growth in sub-1-year contracts for new logos, 2023 to 2026. Shorter deals are structural, not your pricing.
- **28% → 23%** — decline in three-year deals over the same period.
- **19 weeks** — median B2B sales cycle in H2 2025, down from 25 weeks in H1 2025. Faster decisions, shorter commitments.
- **48%** — share of companies using hybrid pricing (sub + consumption) as primary model.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $3M ARR selling workflow automation into mid-market. Priced at $29/seat because "that's what the competitor charges." Average deal is $12k. Biggest customer pays $60k. A board member asks why the top customer isn't paying $250k.

**Application:** Run the comps exercise properly. The top customer also pays Salesforce $800k, Atlassian $300k, and Slack $150k. The founder's product is more important to the CRO's team than Slack, and roughly half as important as Salesforce. Real comp is $300k–$400k, not $60k. On the next big prospect, quote $250k. Don't touch the existing $60k customer at all — grandfather them. Add an Enterprise tier to the pricing page at $150k+ so new F500 buyers self-align into it instead of the $29/seat lane.

**Result:** Founder quotes $275k on the next large prospect. Closes at $200k after procurement discount. Three months later a second large deal closes at $180k. ARR mix shifts: same number of customers, 3x revenue growth from the top decile. The $60k customer renews happy, still does reference calls. The "underpriced by default" prior was worth ~$1.5M a year.

**Situation:** Founder at $8M ARR just got an email from their biggest customer — Qualcomm — threatening to cancel. The founder had sent a renewal invoice jumping the price from $10k to $60k to "match what other customers pay." The champion called them "ballistic" and said it "wasn't fair."

**Application:** Cancel the price increase immediately. The champion is right — they took the early risk, did three webinars, two case studies, and four reference calls. That's not a $10k customer, that's a $250k customer when you count second-order revenue. Grandfather the pricing. Apologize. Next year when there's genuinely new value to offer, introduce a "Global Edition" at a higher price point as a *fully optional* migration — not a tax.

**Result:** Founder cancels the increase. Champion stays. 18 months later that customer expands organically to $45k as their usage grows, migrates voluntarily to the new edition, and does two more reference calls for deals that close $400k between them. Second-order revenue on that one logo exceeds the entire misguided price increase would have.

**Situation:** AI B2B startup at $30M ARR. New VP Sales is panicking because 40% of pipeline is asking for 1-year instead of 3-year contracts. He wants to offer 30% discount for multi-year to fight it. Board meeting next week.

**Application:** Don't fight it. The data is clear — sub-1-year contracts have tripled as a share of new logos since 2023. This is structural, not your deal. Offering a 30% multi-year discount creates a customer who resents the commitment by month 18 and churns at renewal anyway. Instead: accept the 1-year, price the 1-year fairly (no penalty), and rebuild the comp plan to reward NDR and NRR — not just TCV. Put the engineering energy into a 30/60/90 onboarding motion that makes the product indispensable by month 3.

**Result:** Six months later, 1-year contracts are 45% of new logos but NDR is 118%. The deals that would have been 3-year at a 30% discount are instead 1-year at full price with 40% expansion at renewal. TCV per logo over 3 years is higher than the discounted multi-year would have been. VP Sales stops panicking.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Price off cost plus a margin.**
*Why:* Your marginal cost is zero. You have no floor to defend. Founders who price "cost plus 50%" on software end up at $20/seat when the comp is $200/seat, and they spend years trying to claw back the ceiling they set on Day 1. Price off comps, always.

**Don't: Confuse being the cheapest with being the best deal.**
*Why:* Being cheaper often signals you *are* cheaper — worse, less secure, less serious. Customers pay for trust, brand, and the best solution. Being at the Low End of Normal is fine. Being 30% below normal screams "something is wrong." Worse, you can't out-cheap a free or freemium incumbent — Slack is already basically free, and Google is free forever. Don't try to beat them on price. Beat them on a different comp.

**Don't: Jack existing customers to make the quarter.**
*Why:* You'll make the quarter. You'll break the referral engine. Pricing increases on existing customers increase churn — it just takes 2–3 years to show up, especially on annual contracts. They damage NPS, they kill case studies, they turn champions into anti-champions. If you're growing 60%+ a year, the $1M → $1.2M uplift from an existing-customer raise won't matter. The lost referrals will. Put that energy into raising new customer pricing and driving up deal size instead.

**Don't: Let reps over-discount to close.**
*Why:* A 50%-off deal isn't a win. It's a signal the rep can't sell on value, and a commitment to renegotiate at 50% off forever. Worse, it trains every buyer in that segment to expect the same. Set bright lines — 10–15% on the rep, 20% on the manager, 30% on the VP, rarely more. Over-discounting leaves nothing to negotiate next. What do you discount to on the renewal? An over-over-discount?

**Don't: Design six-tier pricing pages with feature matrices the buyer needs a spreadsheet to decode.**
*Why:* Buying is already confusing. Your job is to remove friction, not add it. Three tiers. Clear anchors. Each tier self-explains to its target buyer. The F500 grabs Enterprise without asking. The solo user grabs Starter without asking. If your pricing page needs a sales call to explain, either the tiers are wrong or you're running a non-transparent enterprise model and the page exists only to anchor — which is fine, but know which game you're playing.

**Don't: Charge implementation fees when you're a brand new vendor.**
*Why:* A $2k "onboarding fee" on a $299/month product signals "this is going to be hard and expensive." It kills more deals than the $2k collects. Implementation fees work when you have a brand and a track record of enterprise customers paying them without blinking. Not on customer #17. Eat the setup cost. Win the logo. Charge later when you've earned the right.

**Don't: Force 3-year contracts on AI-era buyers with multi-year discounts.**
*Why:* The market moved. In AI categories the "right" vendor in 12 months may not be the "right" vendor today, and your buyers know it. Forcing a long commitment with a deep discount turns a willing buyer into a resentful one, and churn at month 18 will eat the multi-year math anyway. Better path: 1-year at full price, world-class onboarding, CS-led expansion. Earn the next commitment after the first one, not before.

**Don't: Build a field sales team at a $3k ACV.**
*Why:* Math doesn't work. At 25% of bookings going to sales comp, a $100k rep needs to close $400k a year. At $3k ACV that's 133 deals, or 10–15 per month — barely possible for an inside rep, impossible for field. If you want field sales, drive ACV up first — via tier design, via multi-product, via deal-size work — and *then* hire the field team. Pricing is a deal-size input; don't let it handcuff the motion you actually want to run.
