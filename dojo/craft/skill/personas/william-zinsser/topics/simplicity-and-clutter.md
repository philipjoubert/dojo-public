# Simplicity and Clutter

## When to Use

- A founder shows you a board update, fundraising email, mission statement, layoff memo, or press release and asks "is this clear?"
- The draft uses three words where one would do — "in order to," "at this point in time," "with the possible exception of."
- The founder has fallen into the dialect of their industry — "leverage," "synergy," "north star," "go-to-market motion," "hyper-scale" — and can no longer hear it.
- A difficult fact is being hidden inside a phrase that sounds professional. ("We are sunsetting the product." "We are realigning the team.") The clutter is doing the lying.
- A first draft that is technically grammatical but soft, fuzzy, and forgettable.
- A pitch deck slide where one verb has been propped up by four nouns of generalized meaning.

## Fails When

- **The piece is consciously ornamental.** A founder's anniversary letter, a eulogy at a company funeral for a shuttered product, the closing of a manifesto — these have license to use rhythm and ornament. Stripping for stripping's sake leaves prose that sounds like a stunted child.
- **You substitute simple-for-long mechanically.** "Use" is usually better than "utilize." But not always. "He utilized his last shell" has a different feel from "he used his last shell." Simplicity is a discipline, not a vocabulary list.
- **The user is mid-discovery.** If they're still figuring out what they want to say, asking them to cut by 50% will freeze them. Get the words out first; cut second.
- **You confuse plain with flat.** Plainness in good writing has rhythm and surprise. Flat writing — devoid of any specific noun, any verb that moves — is its own kind of clutter, just better-camouflaged.

## Core Concept

Clutter is the disease of American writing. We are a society strangling in unnecessary words, circular constructions, pompous frills and meaningless jargon. The airline pilot who announces that he is presently anticipating experiencing considerable precipitation wouldn't think of saying it may rain. The sentence is too simple — there must be something wrong with it.

*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*

That instinct — the suspicion that a simple sentence can't be doing important work — is the thing a founder must train themselves out of. It is the deepest reflex of bureaucratic prose, and it is the reason most board updates, all-hands emails, and fundraising decks read as if they were written by the same anonymous committee. The temptation, when stakes are high, is to inflate. The investor is reading me. The board is judging me. Surely my words must rise to the gravity of the moment. So you reach for "going forward" instead of "next" and "in the process of" instead of "is" and "key strategic priorities" instead of "what we're doing." Each substitution feels safe. Together they produce a document with no person inside it.

The secret is the opposite. *The secret of good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Every word that serves no function, every long word that could be a short word, every adverb that carries the same meaning that's already in the verb, every passive construction that leaves the reader unsure of who is doing what — these are the thousand and one adulterants that weaken the strength of a sentence. And they usually occur in proportion to education and rank.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*) That last line is the warning to founders specifically. The more senior you become, the more pressure there is on your writing to puff itself up. The CEO's letter is the worst-written document in most companies because the CEO is the person most likely to think their words must sound CEO-ish.

There is a deeper claim under all of this. *Clear thinking becomes clear writing; one can't exist without the other. It's impossible for a muddy thinker to write good English.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*) This is the move that turns a stylistic tip into a moral one. If your board update is foggy, it is not a writing problem you have. It is a thinking problem. You have not yet decided what the situation is. The fog on the page is the fog in your head, faithfully transcribed. The work of cutting is therefore not cosmetic — it is a forced reckoning with what you actually believe. When you cannot replace "we are seeing meaningful headwinds in our enterprise segment" with a plain sentence, it is because you do not yet know whether you have a sales execution problem, a product fit problem, or a market problem. The clutter was protecting you from having to choose.

The cost is real. The reader is someone with an attention span of about thirty seconds, distracted by Slack and email and the tug of sleep. *The man or woman snoozing in a chair with a magazine or a book is a person who was being given too much unnecessary trouble by the writer.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*) An investor reading your update has fifty other portfolio companies and is on a flight. An employee reading your all-hands email has nine slides of slop ahead of them today. They will not work to understand you. If you make them, they will quit halfway and assume the bad news is worse than it is. Clutter, in business writing, almost always reads as evasion — and almost always *is* evasion, even when you didn't mean it that way.

Worst of all, clutter is how organizations lie about their own behaviour without admitting they are lying. When the Digital Equipment Corporation eliminated 3,000 jobs its statement didn't mention layoffs; those were "involuntary methodologies." When an Air Force missile crashed, it "impacted with the ground prematurely." When General Motors had a plant shutdown, that was a "volume-related production-schedule adjustment." Companies that go belly-up have "a negative cash-flow position." A founder's job, when writing the hard memo — the layoff, the pivot, the missed number, the broken promise — is to refuse this dialect. Not because plain language is more humane, though it is. Because plain language is what readers register as a person speaking. Everything else is white noise, and white noise destroys trust faster than the thing you were trying to soften.

## How to Apply

1. **Bracket every word doing no work.** Read the draft and put mental brackets around every preposition draped onto a verb that doesn't need it ("free up," "head up," "face up to"), every adverb that repeats the verb ("smile happily," "shouted loudly"), every adjective that states the obvious ("tall skyscraper," "personal friend"), every qualifier that softens a sentence into nothing ("a bit," "sort of," "in a sense," "essentially"). Read the sentence without the bracketed material. If the meaning is intact, delete.

2. **Read it aloud.** Founders never do this. It is the single highest-leverage exercise. The ear catches what the eye does not — the sentence that runs out of breath, the rhythm that limps, the phrase that sounds like a press release because it is one. If you stumble, the reader will stumble. If the sentence sounds like something you would never say to your co-founder over a beer, do not write it.

3. **Ask of every sentence: what am I trying to say? Have I said it?** Surprisingly often the answer is that you have not — that you wrote a sentence-shaped object which approximates the territory of what you mean. The clear writer is someone clearheaded enough to see this stuff for what it is: fuzz.

4. **Replace abstract nouns with people doing things.** "There has been a reorganization of the engineering team" is a sentence in which nobody acts. "I moved Priya to platform and asked Marcus to lead infra" is the same fact, alive. Concept nouns — "utilization," "implementation," "alignment," "engagement" — are the connective tissue of corporate prose; they kill the verbs that should be doing the work.

5. **Cut by half. Then cut again.** Most first drafts can be cut by 50% without losing any information or losing the author's voice. Test it. Take an eight-paragraph board update. Cut to four paragraphs. You will howl. Then go home and do it. It will be much better. Then do three. The act of cutting forces you to decide what you actually wanted to say.

6. **Refuse the dialect of your industry.** Every profession has its growing arsenal of jargon to throw dust in the eyes of the populace. SaaS has its dialect. Consumer has its dialect. AI has its dialect. The founders whose writing breaks through are the ones who decline to participate. They do not "drive efficiencies." They save money. They do not "reduce friction." They make it easier to sign up. They do not have a "north star metric." They have one number they care about most.

7. **Notice what the clutter is hiding.** The flabby phrase is almost always concealing a fact you did not want to commit to. "We are seeing some softness in conversion" — what number, on what page, between what dates? When you cut clutter, the underlying fact has to come forward. Sometimes the fact is unpleasant. That is exactly why the clutter was there.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder drafts a board update after a missed quarter. The first sentence reads: "Q3 presented us with a challenging operating environment characterized by macro headwinds and elongated enterprise sales cycles, which together impacted our ability to achieve our targeted revenue plan."

**Application:** Bracket the clutter. "Presented us with a challenging operating environment" — what does that mean? "Macro headwinds" — whose macro? "Elongated enterprise sales cycles" — by how much, in which segment? "Impacted our ability to achieve our targeted revenue plan" — they missed plan. The whole sentence is a 33-word way of saying "We missed Q3." Rewrite: "We missed Q3. We hit $4.1M against a $5.0M plan. The miss was concentrated in two enterprise deals that slipped to Q4 — one for budget reasons, one because the sponsor left."

**Result:** The board can now think. They have a number, a delta, a story for the miss, a reason to read the next paragraph. The original sentence was a defensive crouch. The rewrite is a person speaking. The board's reaction to the rewrite is calmer, not louder, because they no longer have to translate before they can react.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the company's mission statement. The draft reads: "Our mission is to leverage cutting-edge AI to empower enterprises to unlock the full potential of their data, driving meaningful outcomes at scale across diverse industry verticals."

**Application:** Every word is doing the work of a placeholder for some other word. "Leverage" — use. "Cutting-edge AI" — fad word; what does the company actually do? "Empower" — meaningless verb of motivational seminars. "Unlock the full potential" — cliché. "Drive meaningful outcomes" — what outcomes? Beware "meaningful" — the most defensive word in business English; whatever it modifies has been pre-judged for the reader. "At scale" — for whom is anything not at scale? "Diverse industry verticals" — across industries. The sentence as written is testable against no behaviour. It would describe Salesforce, Snowflake, Palantir, Databricks, and a freshman's dorm-room project equally well.

**Result:** Strip it. "We help insurance companies process claims twice as fast." That is a mission statement an employee can act on, an investor can evaluate, and a customer can decide whether to buy. The first version made the founder feel important while saying nothing. The second version commits to behaviour the company can be measured against.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the layoff email. The HR-vetted draft reads: "After careful evaluation of our strategic priorities and operational footprint, we have made the difficult decision to right-size the organization, which will result in the impact of approximately 15% of roles."

**Application:** "Right-size" is a euphemism for "we are firing 15% of the company." "Impact of roles" is a euphemism for "we are firing people." The clutter is doing what political language does — it is the defense of the indefensible. The founder is not lying, exactly, but he has constructed a sentence in which no human being in the company is firing any other human being. The grammar has erased the agency. Employees will read this and lose more trust than the firing itself would lose, because they will know the language is hiding from them.

**Result:** "Today I am laying off 47 people, about 15% of the company. I'll explain why, who's affected, what severance and benefits look like, and what we owe the people leaving." Now there is a person, a number, a list of things to read for. The painful sentence is the first one and the rest of the email can be calmer because the lie is no longer in the room.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Substitute simple words mechanically — "use" instead of "utilize," "now" instead of "currently" — and assume you have done the work.
**Why:** Vocabulary is a small part of clutter. The deeper clutter is structural: passive constructions, abstract nouns, sentences whose subject is a process rather than a person, paragraphs that exist to soften the sentence above them. A piece written entirely in short Anglo-Saxon words can still be unreadable if no one in it ever does anything. Simplicity is rhythm and concreteness. It is not a thesaurus exercise.

**Don't:** Cut clutter and leave the difficult fact unstated.
**Why:** The point of cutting is to surface what the clutter was hiding. If you delete "we are seeing meaningful headwinds" but do not replace it with the actual story of the miss, you have produced a vacuum. The reader notices the vacuum. They will fill it with something worse than the truth. The discipline is: cut the fluff, then say the hard thing in the cleanest sentence you can write.

**Don't:** Strip ornament from every kind of writing.
**Why:** A board update should be plain. A founder's anniversary letter to the team probably should not be. There are moments — usually moments of celebration, mourning, transition, or commitment — where a piece of writing earns the right to be musical. Stripping rhythm out of a toast leaves you with a memo. Know which mode you are in. Most of the time, in business, you are writing memos. Some of the time, you are not.

**Don't:** Apologise for plain writing by adding a softening preamble.
**Why:** "I want to take a moment to share some thoughts on where we are as a company" is the throat-clearing of a writer who is afraid to start. Cut it. Start with the sentence that matters. The reader does not need to be warmed up — they are already reading.

**Don't:** Confuse "I am being direct" with "I am writing well."
**Why:** Plainness without thought is its own failure mode. "We're losing money. We need to figure it out. Let's do it" is direct and useless. Simplicity is what survives after thinking has happened. If you have not thought, no amount of short sentences will rescue you.

**Don't:** Believe that good writing comes naturally and so a draft that needs cutting is evidence you can't write.
**Why:** *Writing is hard work. A clear sentence is no accident. Very few sentences come out right the first time, or even the third time. Remember this in moments of despair. If you find that writing is hard, it's because it* is *hard.* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 2*) The professional cuts because the first draft was full of clutter, and the second draft was still full of clutter, and the seventh draft is the one that reads as if it had been written effortlessly. The founder who refuses to cut is not bad at writing. They are skipping the part of writing that is writing.
