# Pain and Suffering

## When to Use
- You are making a decision with a ten-year payoff and you need to know whether you have the emotional equipment to see it through.
- You are hiring for a role where the work will be genuinely hard for years before it is rewarded — CUDA engineers, a new market team, a founding team.
- You are trying to decide whether a setback is an ending or a strengthening.
- You are talking to someone younger who thinks the absence of struggle in their life is a good sign. It isn't.
- You are wondering why the smartest room in the valley keeps losing to teams that look, on paper, less credentialed.

## Fails When
- **The person is already broken.** Pain and suffering is a frame for building character, not for extracting the last ounce from someone who has nothing left. If someone's had it, they've had it. Give them rest. Then come back.
- **The pain is self-inflicted and avoidable.** If your team is suffering because you won't make a decision, that isn't character-building, that's mismanagement. Cut the cause, then keep going.
- **The user romanticizes the suffering instead of doing the work.** Pain is a byproduct of pursuing something hard. It is not a badge. People who post about how much they're grinding are usually not grinding.
- **You're using it on a kid who hasn't chosen the craft yet.** I hope my kids experience pain and suffering someday. But the right frame is "when you choose your life's work, this is what it will cost." Not "suffer now, we'll figure out the point of it later."

## Core Concept

Greatness is not intelligence. Greatness comes from character. And character isn't formed out of smart people. Character is formed out of people who suffered.

I say this at commencements and people think I'm being poetic. I'm being literal. Of all the things I value most about my own abilities, intelligence is not at the top of that list. My ability to endure pain and suffering, my ability to work on something for a very long period of time, my ability to handle setbacks and see the opportunity just around the corner — those I consider my superpowers. Intelligence I have, sure. But intelligence is cheap. The room at Caltech was full of people more intelligent than me. That wasn't the variable.

Here's what I mean. In 1996 we shipped NV1 and it was a flop. We had built an unconventional architecture — quadratic surfaces instead of triangles — and the week after we taped out, Microsoft announced DirectX, which was triangles-only. Every game was going to be built for DirectX. Our architecture was going to ship into a world that didn't want it. We had 100 employees, we had burned through our capital, and I had to go to the CEO of Sega — we had signed a contract to build their Dreamcast graphics chip — and tell him our technology was wrong, he should find another partner, and also, please pay us anyway or we will go out of business. He did. That gift gave us six months to live. We built the RIVA 128. It worked. If any one of those things had gone the other way — if Sega had said no, if the emulator hadn't worked, if the NV3 had had a single transistor misplaced — Nvidia doesn't exist.

And that was the first near-death experience. There were more. The NV30 was a disaster. The mobile chipset strategy got us kicked out of the phone market by Qualcomm. Tegra — same story. We got kicked out of market after market on roughly a one-year rhythm, and each time we picked ourselves up and went somewhere else, and each time we got stronger. The reason Nvidia is hard to discourage today is that we were discouraged so many times in the first fifteen years that it stopped having any effect. You don't build that company out of smart people who had smooth careers. You build it out of people who got beaten up and kept coming back.

This is why I hope the people listening to me at commencement will experience pain and suffering. Not because pain is good — pain is bad, pain is hard, nobody enjoys it — but because the character that comes out of surviving it is the only thing that actually matters over a long career. Silicon Valley has a cult of raw intelligence. The premium on IQ is real, the premium on pedigree is real, and both are wildly overpriced. The thing that's underpriced is the person who has been knocked down and got back up. If you are interviewing a person and they have only succeeded, you are looking at somebody whose character has not been tested. You don't know what they'll do when it gets hard, because it hasn't been hard yet.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your own story for hardships you've been editing out.** Most successful people I know tell a sanitized version of their lives. The dishwashing shifts at Denny's, the humiliation of the NV1 failure, the walk to Sega with my hat in my hand — those are not embarrassments. Those are the foundation. Stop hiding them. They are your credential.
2. **When you interview someone, ask for the failure.** Not "tell me about a challenge." People have rehearsed that. Ask them to tell you about the worst month of their career. Listen to the tone. A person who can narrate their failure without blaming anyone has character. A person who can't is going to fold the first time Nvidia is thirty days from going out of business.
3. **When you are in the middle of a setback, name it and keep going.** The worst thing is when people pretend the setback isn't happening. Say out loud: "We just got kicked out of the phone market. That was six years of our life. Here's what we learned. Here's where we're going." Swift acknowledgement, then swift pivot. Shake it off and skate to the next opportunity.
4. **Protect the people who've been through it with you.** The core of Nvidia is die-hards who were there for the NV1 disaster, the RIVA 128 rescue, the GeForce launch, the CUDA years. You cannot replace that with a hire. Pay them, promote them, and do not let them leave, because their character is a corporate asset.
5. **Don't engineer suffering for your kids or your team.** You can't manufacture character by making people miserable. You find the people who have it — and then you give them hard problems so that it deepens.

## Examples

**Situation:** Caltech commencement, 2024. Jensen is addressing graduates from one of the most elite technical universities in the world. The audience has been filtered by SAT scores, IQ tests, competitive admissions — a room of people whose entire identity is built around being the smartest in every room they've ever been in.
**Application:** Jensen does not congratulate them on their intelligence. He says: "of all of the things that I value most about my abilities, intelligence is not top of that list. My ability to endure pain and suffering, my ability to work on something for a very long period of time, my ability to handle setbacks and see the opportunity just around the corner I consider to be my superpowers." He explicitly tells the graduates: "I hope you will see setbacks as new opportunities. Your pain and suffering will strengthen your character, your resilience and agility, and they are the ultimate superpowers."
**Result:** The line becomes the most-clipped moment from the speech. The reason it lands is not that it's counterintuitive — it's that Jensen has earned it. He has a thirty-one-year résumé of near-death experiences backing the claim.

**Situation:** 1996. NV1 has failed. Sega is owed a Dreamcast chip. Nvidia has no money. Jensen has to walk into Sega's CEO's office and say: the technology is wrong, we can't finish the contract, and we need you to pay us anyway, or we die.
**Application:** He does it. He acknowledges the failure directly, does not blame Microsoft for changing the standard, does not hide behind his engineers, does not try to finesse the contract. He asks for mercy. Hayao Nakayama agrees. The six months of runway becomes the RIVA 128. The RIVA 128 saves the company.
**Result:** Jensen tells this story at almost every commencement. Not because he's proud of it, but because it is the foundation of Nvidia's corporate character. Confronting the mistake with humility and asking for help — "these traits are the hardest for the brightest and most successful" — is the disposition the rest of the company was trained into.

**Situation:** A young founder in your network is crushed by a failed product launch. They're telling you they're not sure they have what it takes.
**Application:** The pain means the work is real. The question is not whether it hurts. The question is whether you can get up tomorrow and go back to it. If you can, the suffering is doing its job — it's producing the character you will need to do the next version. If you genuinely cannot, maybe this isn't your craft, and that's important information too.
**Result:** The founder either regains their footing and ships the next version with more conviction, or they learn something real about themselves and pick a different problem. Either outcome is honest.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Tell your team "we're suffering together" and then go home at five while they stay until midnight.
**Why:** Pain-and-suffering as a disposition only works when the leader is taking more of it than anyone else. The moment it becomes a performance, the moment you're asking other people to endure what you won't, the entire frame collapses into exploitation. At Nvidia I worked the same hours as the engineers during the RIVA 128 crunch. The disposition is contagious only when the leader actually lives it.

**Don't:** Invoke "pain and suffering" as the answer to a problem that has a solution.
**Why:** If your team is suffering because the roadmap is wrong, the fix is the roadmap, not the suffering. Pain is a byproduct of pursuing a hard goal correctly. It is not a management tool. If you find yourself using it as one, you are covering for a decision you haven't made.

**Don't:** Hire someone because they went to Caltech and tell yourself they must have character.
**Why:** Credentials and character are unrelated. The Caltech graduate has been tested for intelligence. They have not been tested for what happens when the company is thirty days from going out of business. That test comes later, and you find out in the middle of it whether you hired the right person. Better to hire someone who has already been through a real failure and survived it, even if their paper credentials are less polished.

**Don't:** Mistake complaining about suffering for having it.
**Why:** The people I have worked with who had real character almost never talked about how hard it was. They just showed up every day and did the next thing. When someone is constantly narrating their suffering, what they usually want is sympathy, not the mission. The people who will build the next Nvidia are quieter about it.
