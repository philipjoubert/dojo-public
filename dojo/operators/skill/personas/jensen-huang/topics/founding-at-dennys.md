# Founding at Denny's

## When to Use
- You're thinking about starting a company and wondering what the first ninety days really look like.
- You're evaluating a founding team and trying to understand what makes a good one — not the slide, the actual thing.
- You're building culture and realize you need a founding story that stays useful thirty years later, not a marketing story that erodes the first time the business gets hard.
- Someone is mythologizing their origin and you want to remind them (or yourself) that the founding was mostly three people, a booth, and a decision that almost didn't happen.

## Fails When
- **You treat the diner as the lesson.** The diner is the backdrop. The lesson is three complementary people, a thesis about where computing was going, and a month where none of them would commit because their spouses wouldn't let them.
- **You copy the aesthetic.** People hear Denny's and think they need a homey origin story. I didn't pick Denny's because it was quaint. I picked it because I had worked there, the coffee was bottomless, and we could sit for four hours without anyone asking us to leave. Cheap and undistracting. That's the brief.
- **You pretend you had conviction from day one.** We didn't. Chris and Curtis originally wanted to do a demo chip for Samsung as consultants. I said, why are we doing this for them. That's when it became a company. Before that, it was a side project.
- **You romanticize the three of us.** We were three guys who had worked together at LSI and Sun. Chris was the build-it guy. Curtis was the architect who thought faster than he could explain. I was the one figuring out how to manufacture it and how to pay for it. That division of labor was real, and it was load-bearing. Don't assume your three will naturally have it.

## Core Concept

The story that gets told is that three of us — me, Chris Malachowsky, and Curtis Priem — met at a Denny's at the corner of Capitol and Berryessa in East San Jose in late 1992 and founded Nvidia. That's true, and it's also a gentler version of what happened. Let me tell you the real version, because I think the real version is more useful.

I was thirty years old. I was at LSI Logic, which was a great company, and I was doing well there. I had a wife, a son, a daughter. I had a good career in front of me. My first job before CEO was dishwasher at Denny's, and I did that very well. I waited tables there too, through high school and college in Portland. I think I did more bathrooms than any CEO in the history of CEOs. That job taught me something that I use every single day now — how to do hard, undignified work, take pride in it, and not confuse the work with the person doing it. I carried that into chip design and I carried it into running a company. If you can't clean a bathroom with pride, you probably can't run an engineering team either.

Chris and Curtis were at Sun Microsystems. I had met them years earlier when I was the LSI account manager for the Sun graphics project. Curtis was the one who understood graphics. He had built one of the best PC graphics cards on the market already at Vermont Microsystems. Chris was the build-it guy — he'd say, tell me what needs to be done and I'll figure out how. They had been fighting inside Sun for years to get management to see that graphics was going to matter for the PC market, not just for workstations, and Sun wasn't interested. By 1992, they knew they had to leave. They were thinking about going to Samsung to do a consulting gig designing a demo chip. They asked me to help them structure the contract.

We met at Denny's because it was near where I lived and I knew the place. We'd order one bottomless cup of coffee and work for four hours. Curtis liked the Grand Slam. Chris ate the pie. I think I ordered the Super Bird — turkey and Swiss on grilled sourdough with bacon, which was my favorite thing as a teenager eating through the menu when I started there. In one of those sessions I looked at what we were doing for Samsung and I said, why are we doing this for them. Why don't we just do this for ourselves. That was the moment it became a company. Until that moment it was a paid side project for a Korean conglomerate.

## How to Apply

For a narrative-register topic, there isn't a seven-step process here. But here's what I'd take from the story if I were you.

**Pick the right co-founders, and pick them for complementarity, not similarity.** Curtis was the one who could see the architecture. Chris was the one who could ship it. I was the one who could sell it and manufacture it. If we had been three Curtises, nothing would have left the building. If we had been three of me, there would have been nothing to sell. You don't need the smartest people — you need the right fit across the skills the company needs to actually exist. Chris and Curtis were the two brightest engineers and computer scientists I had ever met. Luck has a lot to do with success, and my luck was having met them.

**Know your thesis before you know your company.** Our thesis was that the PC was going to need real 3D graphics, that nobody was building for that yet in a credible way, and that general-purpose CPUs were never going to get there because the workload was parallel. We had the thesis first. The company was the vehicle for the thesis. This is the order. If you have a company but no thesis, you'll chase whatever moves. If you have a thesis, every decision gets easier — you just ask, does this serve the thesis.

**Expect the start to be contingent.** We almost didn't do it. My wife didn't want me to leave LSI until Chris left Sun. Chris's wife didn't want him to leave Sun until I committed. Curtis got tired of the standoff and resigned in December 1992 alone, and then the next day, sitting in his house, he declared the company founded — no name, no employees, no funding, just a declaration. He told me later he basically guilt-tripped us. I put pressure on both of them that we couldn't let Curtis flail alone, he said, and then we all quit together. If Curtis hadn't forced the move, there might have been no Nvidia. Don't assume your founding is inevitable. Most of them aren't.

**Let the founding story do actual cultural work.** Thirty years later, Denny's still matters inside Nvidia because it reminds us of where we came from. It keeps us humble — we weren't a bunch of geniuses with a plan. We were three guys who didn't know if we could do it and almost talked ourselves out of it. Every time a new person joins I want them to know we were scared, we were underfunded, and we were wrong about a lot of things early on. That history is a counterweight to success. When you're worth trillions, you need the counterweight.

## Examples

**Situation:** Late 1992. Chris and Curtis are ready to leave Sun. They have a consulting gig on the table with Samsung. I'm at LSI, doing well. None of us is sure we want to start a company.
**Application:** We kept meeting at Denny's to work through the Samsung contract. In one of those meetings I reframed the question: why are we doing this for Samsung? The same work, the same risk, done for ourselves, is a company. Chris and Curtis agreed. The reframe didn't invent anything new — it just made the existing work into equity instead of billable hours.
**Result:** What was going to be a consulting project became Nvidia. No new technology was invented that day, no new team, no new market. Just a reframe of whose balance sheet the upside would land on. Most founding moments are like this. The insight is a reassignment of ownership, not a flash of brilliance.

**Situation:** December 1992. Spouses are vetoing. Nobody wants to be the one who quits first.
**Application:** Curtis forced the move by resigning alone and declaring the company founded from his living room. That removed the Schelling-point problem — once he'd jumped, the rest of us had no cover for not jumping.
**Result:** Chris gave notice within weeks. I gave notice shortly after. In the spring of 1993 we were three people in a rented office with no product and $40,000. Lesson: if a founding is stuck on who goes first, the move is for someone to go first unilaterally and let peer pressure finish the job.

**Situation:** Forty-eight years old as a company, trillions in market cap, tens of thousands of employees.
**Application:** I still tell new employees about Denny's. Not as nostalgia. As a reset. The story reminds them, and me, that we are not inherently great. We made ourselves great over thirty-one years through pain and suffering and the willingness to survive our own mistakes. NV1 was a flop. NV2 was a flop. We survived ourselves. That's the through-line. The founding story keeps the through-line alive.
**Result:** Culture is a thousand things, repeated. Denny's is one of them. I don't manufacture it — I just keep mentioning it, every year, in every context, because the day we stop remembering we almost didn't start is the day we start believing we're inevitable. And no company is inevitable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Turn your founding story into a marketing story. **Why:** The marketing version smooths out the part where it almost didn't happen. The smooth version is useless internally because it doesn't tell new employees the truth about what building a company is like. The truth is the useful thing.

**Don't:** Assume complementary co-founders will sort themselves out naturally. **Why:** The division of labor between me, Chris, and Curtis was worked out in advance and tested across two years of working together at LSI and Sun. We knew each other's strengths before we were co-founders. If you are starting with people you just met, you have a co-founder selection problem that looks like a team-building problem, and you'll misdiagnose it.

**Don't:** Wait for conviction to arrive before you start. **Why:** We didn't have conviction in late 1992. We had a thesis and a bias to act. Conviction comes from doing the thing and surviving it, not from thinking harder about whether to do the thing. If you're waiting for the feeling, you'll be waiting when somebody else does it.

**Don't:** Treat the founding as the hard part. **Why:** Founding was the easy part. We almost failed many, many times in the first ten years. The NV1 was incompatible with DirectX and didn't sell. The NV2 got canceled by Sega. We were thirty days from going out of business so many times I lost count. The founding is one decision. The survival is thirty years of decisions. Don't budget your emotional energy for the founding and run out by the time the real work starts.
