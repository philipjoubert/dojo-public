# Conscious Leadership

## How it relates to its source

Conscious Leadership is not Mochary's framework. It comes primarily from Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, and Kaley Warner Klemp's *The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership* (2014), and from David Deida's teaching on empathy as the key to success. The Drama Triangle (Victim / Villain / Hero) is older — Stephen Karpman, 1968 — adopted into the Conscious Leadership canon.

Mochary's distinctive contribution is the *fear-and-anger / amygdala* reframing of why the work matters operationally for founders, the *bet that fear is wrong* practice that proves the work is worth doing, and the *joy beats fear* sequencing — the warning that if you do the inner work too fast you'll fall into a motivation valley and may quit your company. When you cite the framework, attribute Dethmer / Chapman / Klemp and Deida by name. Mochary always does.

## When to Use
- A founder is stuck in a problem they describe as caused by others — board, co-founder, exec, market — and therefore unsolvable from where they sit.
- A team is in recurring conflict where the same blame patterns surface and never resolve.
- A leader is more invested in being right than in learning, and you can see it shutting down options.
- A founder has done the *fear and anger* work, lost their fear, and now their business is dipping because the fear-engine is gone — they need to know joy is on the other side and how to get there.
- Designing the personal-development sequence for an exec team that's never done this work.

## Fails When
- **The user is asking a tactical question.** "How do I price this product?" does not need a CL intervention. Diagnosing CL when CL isn't the issue burns trust in the framework.
- **The situation involves a clear external bad actor.** The framework asks: *what did I do to create this situation?* That is a frame for shifting from victim to ownership, not a claim you caused everything. The first instance of theft, harassment, fraud, or violence is not where you start with "what was my role?"
- **The user is using the framework to delay decisions.** "I'm working on my Conscious Leadership" can become cover for not firing the underperformer or making the decision. CL is meant to *unblock* decisions. If the inner work produces more delay rather than more action, something is wrong.
- **The order is reversed.** Doing CL before you've discovered Joy at work (via the Energy Audit) loses your motivation engine before the new one is in place. Joy first, then drop fear.

## Core Concept

Conscious Leadership is *being more interested in learning than being right*. When the ego is afraid of being wrong, that fear leads us to defend our ideas at all costs and to convince others — often with anger. The cost is enormous: we cannot see other perspectives, we don't get complete information, and other people stop bringing us the truth because they know we're not actually open to it.

The shift is to a state of curiosity. Curiosity is the only state in which exquisite solutions appear. You can hold a strong view *and* be genuinely interested in what would change your mind — that is the working state of a Conscious Leader. The work is to notice when fear, anger, or sadness has gripped your thought process, release the emotion (or shift out of it — see `fear-and-anger.md`), and return to curiosity.

> "Conscious Leadership is about being more interested in learning than being right. When our egos make us afraid to be wrong, that fear leads us to defend our ideas at all costs and work hard to convince others that we are right — often with anger." (`articles/conscious-leadership.md`)

There are three operating layers here:

**Layer 1 — Empathy as the engine of trust.** David Deida teaches that empathy is the key to success, not a soft skill. To genuinely feel the feelings of those around you — customer, investor, employee — get curious about their situation and imagine yourself in their shoes. People sense whether you're doing this. When it's real, they trust and like you immediately, because they know you'll consider their interests. When it's a tactic, they sense that too, and the tactic stops working.

**Layer 2 — 100% Responsibility / Drama Triangle.** When something goes wrong, the untrained mind reaches for *Villain* (someone else caused this), *Victim* (and I am powerless because of it), or *Hero* (let me temporarily fix this without addressing the cause). The Drama Triangle is the loop these three roles spin in. *Above the line* — the way out — is to ask *what did I do to create this situation?* That question turns the externally-caused, externally-solvable problem into something you can actually move. (See the sub-section below.)

**Layer 3 — Joy beats fear.** Most CEOs are running on fear: fear of failure, fear of letting people down, fear of looking foolish. Fear is a powerful motivator. When founders start the Conscious Leadership work, they often quickly lose their fear — and then realize fear was the engine. Their personal life gets dramatically better, and their business suffers for a stretch. The work is to push *through* that valley to a place where joy is the engine. Joy is a better engine than fear: more sustainable, more creative, produces work that compounds for decades instead of a fear-fueled five-year sprint.

> "When people start diving into the Conscious Leadership work, they quickly lose their fear. And just as quickly, they realize that fear was their primary motivator — fear of failure, fear of letting people down. Once fear is gone, their life becomes much better, but their business suffers. If you find yourself in this situation, keep pushing forward... to get to a place where you are motivated by joy. Joy is an even better motivator than fear, so your business will thrive. And your life will be amazing." (`articles/conscious-leadership.md`)

## How to Apply

1. **Recognize the emotion.** When fear, anger, or sadness has gripped you (you'll often see it because someone you've authorized to tell you says *"I perceive you to be in fear"* — see `fear-and-anger.md`), name it. *I am in anger right now. I am in fear about X.* Naming it is half the shift.

2. **Release or shift.** Use the physical and mental shifts from `fear-and-anger.md` (move, breathe, run the prediction, journal). Or use Sabrina Wang's 90-second observation technique — close your eyes, body scan, name the strongest sensation, breathe through it without judgment. According to Jill Bolte Taylor's research, an emotion identified and observed dissipates in roughly ninety seconds. Suppressed emotion sticks indefinitely.

3. **Return to curiosity.** Ask: *what is the question I'm not asking? What information do I not have? What would change my mind?* In curiosity, brainstorm widens — you suddenly see options that were invisible while you were defending your position.

4. **Apply 100% Responsibility — ask what you did to create this.** Whatever the situation, ask: *what did I do, or fail to do, that helped create this?* Not in a self-flagellating way. In a *what's the action I can control here?* way. You will almost always find one. (Detail in the Drama Triangle sub-section below.)

5. **Lead with empathy, not as a tactic.** In any meaningful interaction — a customer call, an investor meeting, a co-founder conversation — get genuinely curious about what the other person is experiencing. Imagine yourself in their shoes. Ask the question that proves you've done it. The *Heard* protocol (`heard.md`) is the operational form of this.

6. **Sequence the inner work correctly.** Do the Energy Audit *first* to find the work that gives you energy and joy. Build a job around that work — your Zone of Genius. Only once joy is a daily experience at work should you systematically dismantle fear-based motivation. Otherwise you risk losing the fear engine before the joy engine is running, and falling into a motivation valley where you stop performing.

7. **Push through the dip.** If you've already lost your fear and your business is suffering, do not reverse course. The dip is real and it is short. Keep going. The other side is daily joy, peace, and a business that thrives — for the same reason fear-driven leaders eventually burn out and joy-driven leaders compound for decades.

### Sub-section: The Drama Triangle and 100% Responsibility

The Drama Triangle — Stephen Karpman's 1968 model, adopted by the Conscious Leadership canon — is the most useful diagnostic for stuck conflict. Three roles, all "below the line":

- **Villain:** *I blame [name] for [action].*
- **Victim:** *This negatively affects me in the following way... and I am powerless to permanently solve it because [reason].*
- **Hero:** *I temporarily solve this situation by [action].*

When a team is stuck in a recurring problem, almost every person is rotating through these three roles. Hero is the most seductive — it looks like leadership but it's temporary fixes that postpone resolution. Victim is the most paralyzing. Villain is the most divisive.

The way out — *above the line* — is the 100% Responsibility question: **What did I do to create this situation?** Not blame, not guilt. Just inventory. *What did I withhold? What did I not face? What feelings did I conceal? What was I trying to control that wasn't mine to control?* The inventory almost always reveals the action you can take to solve the problem.

**As a group exercise**, run it like this:

1. Identify the issue.
2. Each person in turn declares Villain, Victim, Hero — *I blame X for Y. This affects me by Z. I temporarily solve it by W.*
3. Cycle until there's no one left to blame (you can blame yourself).
4. Each person states what *they* did to create the situation — above the line.
5. Each answers: *Am I willing to easefully and playfully resolve this issue in this meeting?*
6. The group brainstorms solutions. With worst thoughts on the table and 100% responsibility taken, elegant solutions usually appear quickly.

Use a knowledgeable outsider as facilitator the first time. The point is not catharsis — it's surfacing the hidden information that kept the conflict alive, then resolving from shared ownership.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder calls. The head of sales and the head of marketing are at war. Each blames the other for missed pipeline. Both have been complaining to the founder separately for two months. Founder has been refereeing and resolving each instance, which the team interprets as her playing favorites. Nothing changes.

**Application:** This is the full Drama Triangle running on autopilot. Sales is Villain ("Marketing's leads are garbage"). Marketing is Villain ("Sales doesn't follow up"). Both are Victim ("I can't hit my number because of them"). Founder is Hero ("Let me handle each issue as it comes up"). Run the group exercise. Get a facilitator (probably a coach, definitely not the founder herself — she's a player in the dynamic). Work through Villain / Victim / Hero rounds until exhausted. Then 100% responsibility: each person inventories what they did to create the situation. Sales head sees they never specified what a qualified lead looked like. Marketing head sees they never asked. Founder sees that her "let me solve each one" pattern trained both teams to escalate rather than resolve.

**Result:** With shared 100% responsibility on the table, the actual fix takes thirty minutes — joint definition of qualified lead, weekly fifteen-minute alignment meeting, founder out of the referee role. The two-month war ends in one session. (The deeper insight: the war was being kept alive by everyone playing one of three Drama Triangle roles. Removing the roles removed the war.)

**Situation:** A founder six months into the Conscious Leadership work calls in a panic. Her business is suffering. Her one-on-ones feel "soft." She used to drive on fear of failure; the fear is mostly gone. She wonders if she should reverse the work and rebuild the old anxiety.

**Application:** Don't reverse. This is the predictable valley between fear-engine and joy-engine. The fix is the Energy Audit: identify activities that give you genuine joy and energy versus those that drain. Build your week around the joy-givers. Delegate or eliminate the drainers. Once joy at work is a daily experience, the engine is running — and joy is a better engine than fear was.

**Result:** Three months later: daily peace, more creative output, better decisions, business growing again. The valley was real; the bridge worked. She's no longer running her body into the ground at fear-speed.

---

**Situation:** Co-founder conflict that's been simmering for two years. Both founders are convinced the other is the problem. They've done two off-sites with consultants and the issue keeps coming back.

**Application:** The off-sites kept failing because both founders were operating below the line — Villain (each blaming the other), Victim (each feeling powerless), Hero (each trying to temporarily fix it via heroic effort). The 100% Responsibility move: each founder, separately first, writes out what *they* did to create the situation. Not what the other did. *What did I withhold? What feelings did I conceal? What "should" did I cling to? What did I try to control that wasn't mine to control?* Then they run the conflict resolution protocol from `heard.md` — fully express, prove they heard each other, write down impeccable agreements.

**Result:** The actual issue surfaces in the 100% responsibility step (founder A had been silently resentful that founder B made the original product call without consulting her; founder B had been silently resentful that founder A took external speaking credit while he ran the company). Neither had ever said it directly. Both had been running fear- and anger-driven stories for two years. With both stories on the table and both founders feeling heard, the resolution takes one afternoon. (The two-year delay cost: enormous.)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Skip the empathy step and go straight to 100% Responsibility.
*Why:* Telling someone to take 100% responsibility while they're still in the grip of fear or anger lands as blame, not as liberation. They have to feel heard and shift out of the activated state before the Above-the-Line question can do its work. Order: shift first (`fear-and-anger.md`), feel heard (`heard.md`), then 100% responsibility.

**Don't:** Use 100% Responsibility on someone else.
*Why:* The framework is for *yourself.* Telling another person *"what did you do to create this situation?"* — especially while they're upset — sounds like blame and feels like attack. Apply it inward. If you want them to apply it inward, model it first by doing it on yourself in front of them, audibly: *"Here's what I did to create this. I want to take that 100%."* Then they often follow.

**Don't:** Spiritualize the framework.
*Why:* "Conscious Leadership" can become vocabulary that signals enlightenment without producing changed behavior. The test is operational: are you less defensive in meetings? Are decisions getting made faster? Are you less reactive when criticized? If the answer is no, you're using the language without doing the work.

**Don't:** Reverse the sequence — drop fear before discovering joy.
*Why:* You'll fall into the motivation valley and may quit. The Energy Audit goes first. Build the joy-engine, *then* dismantle the fear-engine. Mochary is explicit about this — and consciousness training options are intentionally gated until the joy work is in place.

**Don't:** Confuse curiosity with permissiveness.
*Why:* Being more interested in learning than in being right does not mean abandoning your judgment. Conscious Leaders still make hard calls, fire underperformers, and hold the line. The state is: *I have a strong view, and I'm genuinely curious about what would change it.* Not: *I have no view because I'm so open-minded.* The first is leadership; the second is fog.

**Don't:** Treat empathy as a manipulation tactic.
*Why:* Mochary is explicit: the techniques work because they prove genuine care. Use them on someone you don't actually care about and you become the kind of person who burns trust at scale. The discipline is to deserve the trust the tools generate. The moment empathy becomes performance, the people on the other end feel it — and the tool stops working with them forever.

---

If the full sequence feels like too much, start with one move: when you next find yourself defending a position in a meeting, ask out loud *"What might I be missing? Help me see it."* Then close your mouth. That single intervention, run a hundred times, does most of the work of the framework on its own.
