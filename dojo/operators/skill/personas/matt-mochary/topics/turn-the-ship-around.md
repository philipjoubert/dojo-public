# Turn the Ship Around — "I intend to ..."

## How it relates to its source

The technique comes from **L. David Marquet**, the captain of the USS Santa Fe, written up in his book *Turn the Ship Around* (2012). Marquet inherited the worst-performing submarine in the US Navy fleet, on a class of submarine he had never trained on. He couldn't make the operational decisions himself — he didn't know the systems — so he had his department heads make them, with the protocol that they say *"I intend to ..."* before acting. Credit Marquet for the technique and the case study.

Mochary's contribution is operationalising it as a default in startup leadership: applying it to your direct reports, refusing the role of bottleneck, using it to develop judgment in the team rather than just speed up the queue. He pairs it with the rule that the leader should rarely override (override too often and the system collapses — the team starts asking permission again).

## When to Use
- You've become the decision bottleneck. People wait on you. Things stall when you're in a board meeting or on a flight.
- The team is competent but defaults to "let me check with you first." You want to shift the default.
- You're trying to build the next layer of leadership — people who can act like commanders, not just like operators.
- You're scaling past the size where one person can be on every call and you need to push decisions down without losing visibility.

## Fails When
- **The report genuinely doesn't have the context.** A new hire, a junior, someone in a domain they haven't been in long enough — they may not have enough information to form a good "I intend to." Don't drop them in cold; coach the context first. Once they have it, switch to the protocol.
- **You override too often.** The protocol works because the report owns the call. If you reverse half their "I intend to" statements, they revert to permission-asking and the system dies. Override only when you actually have material information they don't.
- **You want to delegate the responsibility but not the authority.** Common founder failure mode: "go ahead and decide, but check with me first." That's not delegation — that's permission-asking with extra steps. Either give them the call or don't.
- **The decision is genuinely Type 1 / irreversible.** Hiring an exec, signing the lease, taking the term sheet, killing the company. Some calls really are the founder's. Don't run "I intend to" on those — be honest that you're making them.

## Core Concept

The default in most companies is permission-asking: a report has a decision, walks it up to the boss, the boss decides, the report executes. Decisions are usually sound. They are also slow, the report's judgment muscle never grows, and the boss becomes the bottleneck for everything that matters. The org's speed is capped at the boss's calendar.

Marquet inherited a submarine where this default was structural — the commander made every call, period. He couldn't follow the default; he didn't know the systems on the new boat. So he flipped it. Every department head was instructed: when you have a decision, come to me and say *"I intend to ..."* and then state what you're going to do. If I have an objection, I'll raise it; if I don't, you act. If I'm not available, you act anyway.

> "Marquet prayed that nothing disastrous would happen. Instead, something miraculous happened. Department heads started coming to Marquet earlier and earlier with well thought out decisions. The actions occurred whether or not Marquet was available for feedback, so overall operations sped up dramatically. Whenever Marquet did have questions, they were often about how the decision would affect other departments."
> — *articles/turn-the-ship-around-i-intend-to.md*

Two effects compound. First, speed: actions happen whether Marquet is available or not, so the decision queue stops backing up. Second, judgment: each department head, knowing they will own the result, starts thinking like a commander — assessing how this decision affects every other department, not just their own. They train themselves into the next role every time they say "I intend to."

The Santa Fe went from worst-performing sub in the fleet to best-performing in several years. It also went from lowest promotion rate to highest. The department heads weren't just running their departments — they were practising the commander's job, every day, in public, with the actual stakes. By the time a promotion seat opened, three of them were ready.

The technique is portable. Anywhere you have a competent person, a leader who keeps getting consulted, and a decision queue — you can install it. It's a default, not a ritual; the report says "I intend to" on every call they have authority over, not just the ones they're nervous about.

## How to Apply

1. **Tell your direct reports the new default.** Explicit. "From now on, when you have a decision in your area, don't ask me what to do. Tell me what you intend to do. I'll raise objections if I have them. If I don't, you proceed. If I'm not reachable, proceed anyway. You own the result either way."
2. **Define the scope.** Most decisions, yes. Hiring an exec, taking on debt, anything that bets the company — flag those as Type 1 and bring them to you as decisions, not as "I intend to" statements. Get explicit about which calls fall in which bucket so the report doesn't guess.
3. **Hold the protocol literally.** When they come to you with "I intend to ..." you have three responses: (a) silence — they go and do it; (b) one or two clarifying questions — usually about cross-department impact; (c) genuine objection — say it once, but they still own the call. Don't gut the system by passive-aggressively making them feel they need your approval.
4. **Override rarely.** If you reverse them often, the protocol dies and they go back to permission-asking. Override only when you have material information they don't have — and when you do override, explain *why* so the next decision is sharper.
5. **Watch the cross-department questions.** Marquet's signal that the system was working: the questions he had were almost always about how this decision affected other departments. That means the report is starting to think like a commander. Reinforce it. Eventually they start including the cross-department analysis proactively.
6. **Coach context where it's genuinely missing.** A new hire doesn't have enough field information yet to form a good "I intend to." That's fine — pair them with someone, share the relevant docs, walk them through past decisions. Once context is there, switch them to the protocol.
7. **Use it as a leadership-development tool, not just a speed tool.** Each "I intend to" is the report rehearsing the next role. By the time you need a VP, three people on your team have been doing the VP's thinking out loud for a year. Promote from inside that pool.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder/CEO is on every product decision — pricing changes, feature scope, customer escalations, design tradeoffs. The product lead is competent but waits on every call. The CEO is in board meetings and travel half the week and the product team stalls during those windows.

**Application:** CEO tells the product lead: "From now on, on anything inside product — scope, pricing tweaks under a threshold, customer escalations, design calls — don't ask me. Tell me 'I intend to.' I'll raise objections if I have them; otherwise you go. If I'm in a board meeting, you go anyway. Hiring senior product people, anything over $X, anything that touches the company narrative — bring those as actual decisions."

For the first month, every "I intend to" message gets a quick acknowledgement and at most one cross-functional question ("how will this affect support load?"). The CEO catches herself wanting to override on a pricing change and stops — the lead has more information than she does, and the worst case is a reversible mistake.

**Result:** Three months in, the product team stops stalling when the CEO is unavailable. Six months in, the lead has started including cross-department impact analysis proactively. A year in, the CEO realises the lead is functioning as a VP of Product and they make the title official. The decision quality is better than when the CEO was making the calls — the lead has the context the CEO never did.

---

**Situation:** A founder has just promoted an engineer to engineering manager. The new manager keeps coming to the founder with technical and people decisions — *"What should I do about X?"* — and the founder has been answering. After a month, the team is no faster than it was, and the new manager has shown no growth.

**Application:** Founder switches the protocol mid-relationship. "I notice I've been answering your questions. That's my fault — I'm going to stop. Starting today, when you have a call to make, come to me with 'I intend to ...' and I'll either nod or raise an objection. If I'm in a meeting, proceed without me. The exceptions are: hiring above level X, firing, comp changes — bring those as actual decisions. Everything else, you decide."

First week, the new manager forces three "I intend to" statements that he's clearly nervous about. Founder resists the urge to second-guess. Two of the three were exactly what the founder would have done; the third was different and worked out fine. By month two, the manager has stopped pre-checking and starts proposing things the founder wouldn't have thought of.

**Result:** Manager develops the muscle in three months instead of the year it would have taken under permission-asking. Founder reclaims roughly six hours per week of decision-queue time. Team velocity in engineering doubles by quarter two.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Override the report's "I intend to" because you'd have done it slightly differently.
*Why:* The protocol works because the report owns the result. Override only when you have material information they don't. Otherwise, accept that "your way" and "their way" both work, let them own it, and let the variance teach you something. Frequent overrides kill the system; the team goes back to permission-asking inside a week.

**Don't:** Pretend to use the protocol while still wanting to be asked permission.
*Why:* Reports read this in a heartbeat. They'll learn that "I intend to" is a script that means "please approve me," and you'll have all the speed cost of permission-asking with none of the development upside. Either install the protocol genuinely or don't bother.

**Don't:** Use it on Type 1 / irreversible decisions.
*Why:* Hiring an exec, taking the term sheet, killing the company — these are the founder's calls and should be made deliberately, with the loudest-voice protocol if you're convening input. Don't blur the distinction. Be explicit about which calls are "I intend to" territory and which are decisions.

**Don't:** Skip the context-coaching for a brand-new hire.
*Why:* "I intend to" requires that the report has enough context to form a good intent. A new hire doesn't yet. Drop them in cold and the early "I intend to" statements will be poor, you'll override several, the report will lose confidence, and the protocol fails to take. Coach context first; then install the protocol.

**Don't:** Treat the technique as Mochary's invention or as generic delegation.
*Why:* The framing is Marquet's. Cite him by name when you teach the technique — *Turn the Ship Around*, USS Santa Fe. The credibility comes from the case study (worst-performing sub to best, lowest promotion rate to highest), and credibility is what gets your team to actually adopt a new default.

**Don't:** Use it to abdicate.
*Why:* "I intend to" still requires you to be informed. You read the messages. You raise the objection when you genuinely have one. You stay close enough that the team knows you'd catch a real mistake. Abdication looks like the protocol; it isn't.
