# Energy Audit

## How it relates to its source

Mochary learned the Energy Audit exercise from **Diana Chapman of the Conscious Leadership Group** — credit her for the audit mechanic itself. The four-zone framing (Incompetence → Competence → Excellence → Genius) is **Gay Hendricks**, from his book *The Big Leap*. Credit Hendricks for the zones. Mochary's contribution is the operational stack: pairing the audit with the Hendricks zones, the monthly cadence, the Outsource / Eliminate / Make Exquisite triage on the drains, and — most distinctively — the **Naval Ravikant / AngelList example**, which is now Mochary's canonical illustration of the Zone of Excellence trap. Naval told the story publicly with Mochary's permission; Mochary uses it because no other example makes the danger of Excellence land as fast.

## When to Use
- You are exhausted at the end of weeks where, on paper, you accomplished a lot.
- You are great at your role, well-paid, well-regarded — and you no longer want to do it.
- You can tell some of your time energizes you and some drains you, but you can't say which is which precisely enough to act on it.
- You're trying to decide what to delegate, and "delegate the things you're worst at" is producing the wrong answer (because the things you're worst at often don't take much of your time anyway).
- You have a Chief of Staff or COO and you're trying to figure out what they should take from you.

## Fails When
- **You audit a non-representative calendar.** Two weeks where you went on a big trip, or were on vacation, or had an unusual board cycle — the audit will tell you the wrong story. Pick a representative two-week window or wait.
- **You let yourself mark hours "neutral."** Neutral is the failure mode. Every hour is either + or –. Letting hours sit in the middle is how Zone of Excellence hides.
- **You only do it once.** The calendar drifts back. Energy Audit is a monthly practice until you're at 75–80%, then quarterly forever after.
- **You confuse "doing well" with "energizing."** Some of the most draining work is work you're excellent at. That's the entire point. Conflating them is the trap.

## Core Concept

You perform best when you are doing things that energize you. There is excellent brain science around this — Robert Sapolsky of Stanford has written about it at length. The practical version is simpler: you have a finite amount of energy each week, and the work that energizes you returns more than it costs. The work that drains you costs more than it returns, regardless of how good you are at it. Your goal is to spend most of your time — 75 to 80% — doing things that energize you. Below that, you are slowly burning down the engine. At 75–80%, magic occurs.

The audit is mechanical. Print the last two weeks of your calendar — past, not future. Pick two weeks that are representative of your everyday experience. Add in the activities you did between meetings, before work, and after work. Get two highlighters of different colors — green and red are ideal. Go through each workday hour by hour and ask one question: *did that activity give me energy or drain my energy?* Green for gave energy. Red for drained. **There are no neutrals.** Every hour gets one color. If an hour did not give you energy, it is red.

When you finish, write out the list of reds. Group them into similar buckets — one-on-ones, recruiting meetings, board prep, customer escalations, expense reports, whatever they happen to be. For each bucket of drains, you have exactly three options: **outsource it, eliminate it, or make it exquisite**. Outsource it: someone else does it, full stop. Eliminate it: it disappears from the calendar entirely (most CEOs underestimate how much can be eliminated outright). Make it exquisite: for the meetings you genuinely must attend, redesign them so attending them gives you energy instead of taking it. Turn each of these into actions with due dates and put them in your task manager.

This is where Hendricks' four zones come in. **Zone of Incompetence** is work others do better than you (fixing your car). **Zone of Competence** is work you do fine but others can do as well (cleaning the bathroom). Outsource both if they don't give you joy — easy decisions. **Zone of Excellence** is the trap. Excellence is the work you are *better than other people at*. People want you to keep doing it because they get significant value from you doing it. You're paid well for it. You probably built your reputation on it. But you don't love it. **Time spent in Excellence is time stolen from Genius.** **Zone of Genius** is what you are uniquely good at *and* love doing — the work where time and space disappear when you're in it. You should be driving toward spending most, if not all, of your time in Genius.

> "Your goal should be to spend most of your time (75-80%) doing things that energize you. If you do, magic will occur." (*Energy Audit*, Mochary curriculum)

> "Your zone of Excellence is what you're really good at, people really value about you and you probably make a lot of money doing it... this is the danger zone because you are better than other people at this... but it's not quite at your zone of Genius. It's scary to move out of your Zone of Excellence which you're getting a lot of accolades for and money for." (Mochary, Afore Capital keynote)

> "It's impossible to do a job really well if you hate it. In fact, it's impossible to do a job really well if you don't love it." (Mochary, Afore Capital keynote)

The canonical example: Naval Ravikant. Naval came to Mochary early, ten years into running AngelList. He told Mochary: *"I've been running AngelList for ten years and I don't like it. But I have to do it. I'm the famous guy. I'm the one all the investors are counting on. I'm the one who brings in the deals because I'm the well-known name. So I've got to keep doing it."* Mochary's response was direct: *"I think actually you suck at it, and someone else would do a much better job than you. The reason is, of all, because you don't love it. The first thing you told me was you hate it. And it's impossible to do a job really well if you hate it."* Naval thought it was crazy. An hour and a half later they had a roadmap. Six weeks later he had replaced himself with Kevin Laws. AngelList took off. Naval — given the space — found his Zone of Genius and built multiple companies that have done extraordinarily well. Naval rates the change from a 3 out of 10 (when he was CEO) to an 11 out of 10 (now). The company is more valuable. He is more valuable. The Zone of Excellence had been holding both of them back for years.

## How to Apply

1. **Print two representative weeks of past calendar.** Not future, not unusual. Add in the things you did between meetings, before work, and after work — those count too.

2. **Get two markers, different colors.** Green and red ideal. Any two will do.

3. **Mark every hour, no neutrals.** Green if it gave you energy. Red if it drained you. Every single hour. The shortcut version: just mark the reds and assume everything else was a green — but only if you're being honest, not avoiding hard calls.

4. **List every red and bucket them.** "1:1 with X, 1:1 with Y, 1:1 with Z" probably collapses to "1:1s." "Board prep, board call, board follow-up" collapses to "board cycle." Look for the patterns.

5. **Triage each red bucket: Outsource, Eliminate, or Make Exquisite.**
   - **Outsource** if someone else can do it — and they probably can. Hire, delegate to an existing person, or transfer to a Chief of Staff. The bias is to outsource more than feels comfortable.
   - **Eliminate** if you can simply stop doing it. CEOs are shocked by how much falls in this bucket. Most "required" meetings are required because nobody has tried killing them.
   - **Make Exquisite** if it's a meeting or activity you genuinely must attend. Redesign it: change the agenda, change the participants, change the format, change the prep. A drained one-on-one becomes an energizing one-on-one when both sides come pre-written and the conversation is about decisions rather than updates.

6. **Convert each triage into a dated action.** "Hand off expense approvals to the Chief of Staff by Friday." "Eliminate the Wednesday all-hands and replace it with a written update by next Tuesday." Put them in your task manager. Without the dated action, the audit becomes a journaling exercise.

7. **Map your remaining time against the four zones.** Of your green hours, which ones are Zone of Genius (you love them and you're uniquely great at them) and which are Zone of Excellence dressed up as Genius (you're great at them, you're rewarded for them, but you don't actually love them)? Excellence will sometimes mark itself green because of the social rewards — push past those and ask whether time and space disappear when you do this work. If they don't, it's Excellence, not Genius.

8. **Identify the role or piece of work that's locking you in Excellence.** This is the Naval question: *what role am I in because I'm the famous one / the experienced one / the irreplaceable one — and would the company be better off with someone who genuinely loves this work?* If the answer is "yes, the company would be better off," that's the seat to hand off.

9. **Crowdsource your Zone of Genius.** You're often the worst person to identify your own Genius — because time and space disappear, you don't notice yourself doing it. Send five to ten people who interact with you regularly the question: "What do I do that you experience as world-class? What do I do where I appear to be experiencing fun, peace, or joy? What do I do that I'm good at but don't appear to enjoy? What do I do where I'm not better than others? What do I do where I'm actually worse than others?" Their answers will surprise you.

10. **Repeat monthly until 75% green; then quarterly forever.** Calendars drift. New drains accumulate. The audit is a regular discipline, not a one-time exercise.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series C CEO has 60% red on her calendar audit. The biggest red bucket is "1:1 prep + 1:1s + 1:1 follow-up" — she has 14 direct reports, weekly one-on-ones with each, and the cycle eats roughly 18 hours a week.

**Application:** Triage on the bucket. *Eliminate* most of them: force the issues into the weekly group meeting where the team can actually solve them collaboratively, instead of in 14 separate one-on-ones where she has to be the routing layer for everything. Keep three structured one-on-ones (her two co-founders and her CFO) and convert the rest to "as-needed, you grab time when you actually need me." *Make exquisite* the three that remain: pre-read documents 30 minutes before, agenda is decisions and conflicts only — not status updates.

**Result:** She gets 12–15 hours back per week. The team's decision velocity goes up because issues now surface in the group where the people who can solve them are present. Her audit the next month shows 78% green. She's now spending most of her time on vision and conflict resolution — the CEO's two actual jobs.

---

**Situation:** A founder is the technical lead of a 25-person engineering team. He's a brilliant architect, the team respects him, the codebase is excellent. He says he's burning out. Audit shows 70% red, almost all of it in "code review, technical debt cleanup, on-call rotation, and engineering one-on-ones."

**Application:** This is Zone of Excellence. He's better at code review than anyone he could hire, he's rewarded for it, the team needs it — and he doesn't love it anymore. The work that lights him up is product discovery and customer conversations. He won't let the engineering management role go because he's the famous one in the role. Naval question: *would the company be better off with a VP of Engineering who genuinely loves running engineering teams?* Yes, obviously. The map is clear: hire a VP of Engineering, hand off code review and one-on-ones, keep architectural review for the hardest problems, free up his calendar for the product / customer work that's his Zone of Genius.

**Result:** Six months later, the engineering org is performing at the same level under the VP. He's spending 60% of his week on customers and product. He's not burned out. The company has shipped two new product lines that came directly out of customer conversations he wouldn't have had time for under the old setup.

---

**Situation:** A solo operator audits her calendar and finds 90% red. Almost everything drains her. She concludes she should quit her job.

**Application:** 90% red on the first audit usually means one of two things — and quitting is rarely the right read. Either (a) the audit was done in an unrepresentative period (a brutal launch week, a crisis cycle), in which case rerun it on a normal two weeks, or (b) the role itself is fundamentally Zone of Excellence at best, possibly Incompetence — in which case the question isn't "should I quit" but "what's the work I would *love* to do, and what's the lightest path from here to there?" The Zone of Genius interview from step 9 above usually surfaces the answer faster than introspection alone.

**Result:** When she ran the crowdsourced Genius question with 8 colleagues, the consistent answer was "you're world-class at translating ambiguous customer problems into clear product specs — nobody else does it like you do." The role she was in had her doing none of that. The path forward wasn't to quit; it was to redesign the role around the spec work, hand off the rest, and stop pretending the parts she was Excellent at were the parts she should keep doing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mark hours "neutral" or "depends on the meeting."
**Why:** Neutrality is where Zone of Excellence hides. Most "neutral" hours are actually slight drains the founder doesn't want to admit drain them, because admitting it would mean changing the calendar. Force the binary. There is no neutral.

**Don't:** Audit your *future* calendar.
**Why:** Future calendar is what you *think* you'll do. Past calendar is what you actually did. Energy is felt in the doing, not the planning. The audit only works on lived hours.

**Don't:** Confuse "I'm good at this" with "this gives me energy."
**Why:** This is the entire reason the framework exists. Excellence and Genius look identical from the outside. From the inside, Excellence drains; Genius restores. If you can't tell which is which, ask: when this hour ends, do I feel more alive or less alive? Less alive = Excellence (or worse).

**Don't:** Use the Zone of Genius framing to abandon a role you committed to.
**Why:** The framing is for *years-long career decisions*, not for ducking out of a hard quarter. If your audit is 60% red because you're in the middle of a layoff cycle, the answer is finish the layoff, then re-audit. Genius isn't about always feeling great; it's about whether the role itself, run well, would energize you.

**Don't:** Treat outsourcing the reds as "delegating to whoever is free."
**Why:** Your Zone of Excellence is someone else's Zone of Genius — but only if you actually find them. Hiring the wrong person for the role you're handing off creates worse output and a backlog of fixes that comes back to you. Find the person who *loves* the work; their Genius is your Excellence.

**Don't:** Run the audit and then keep all the reds because "the team needs me to do them."
**Why:** Almost every CEO who tells me their team needs them to keep doing X is, in fact, the bottleneck on X. The team has worked around your unavailability before; they'll do it again. The proof is to test it for a month. Hand it off, watch what happens. Almost always: nothing breaks, and the team's owner-mindset goes up because they're no longer waiting for you.

**Don't:** Stop after one audit.
**Why:** The calendar drifts. New "important" meetings creep in. Within a quarter you'll be back at 50% red unless you've made the audit a habit. Monthly until 75% green, then quarterly. This is hygiene, not a project.
