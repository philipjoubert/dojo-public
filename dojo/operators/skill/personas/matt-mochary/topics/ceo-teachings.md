# CEO Teachings — The Diagnostic Checklist

## When to Use
- The user has scaled past PMF and the early founder muscles aren't working anymore.
- A pattern of recurring problems — same conflict every quarter, same hire failing, same fire restarting — and the user wants to find the root.
- The user is preparing for an exec coaching session, a board check-in, or a self-review.
- The user asks something like *"what do most founders get wrong?"* — give them this rather than answering off the cuff.
- An honest audit. The CEO is willing to look at themselves, not just at the team.

## Fails When
- **The user is in fear or anger.** Self-diagnosis under emotional duress turns into self-flagellation. The story your brain tells you about all the things you're doing wrong is the same kind of distortion as fear about a layoff. Shift first; diagnose second. (See `fear-and-anger.md`.)
- **The user wants to grade the team, not themselves.** This is a self-applied checklist. The moment the user starts using it to compile complaints about their direct reports, the framework has failed.
- **The user wants a one-shot fix for everything.** Each item below has a topic file behind it. The diagnostic is for *finding* the work, not avoiding it.

## Core Concept

After coaching the CEOs of Coinbase, OpenAI, Reddit, Brex, Robinhood, Plaid, Discord, Chainguard, Flock Safety, V7, Motion, and many others, a small set of patterns shows up over and over. They are not weaknesses — they are predictable structural failure modes that come with the role. The best CEOs aren't the ones who avoid them. They're the ones who notice the pattern firing in themselves and have a method to course-correct quickly.

> "To me the bar is fear and how strongly do people feel fear. There are a few people that I coach that just don't feel fear at all and frankly with them we have very tactical conversations but they're the minority. The most feel fear to some degree. Some feel it a lot, some feel it less, but when they feel it, it grips their mind and prevents them from doing the thing that is difficult but necessary. A lot of what our coaching is me pointing out to them: 'hey, I think you're in fear.'"

The list below is a diagnostic. Run it on yourself once a quarter. Not as a report card — as an audit of where your attention is going and where your fear is hiding. Each item is a recurring failure mode. Each one has a remedy.

## How to Apply

Walk down the checklist. For each item, give yourself a 1 (this isn't happening), a 2 (this is starting), or a 3 (this is actively true right now). Anything that scores a 3 is the work. Pick one. Do it.

### 1. You're in fear and don't know it.

The single most predictive variable across Mochary's portfolio is whether the CEO is in fear right now. Fear hides as analysis ("I just want to think about it more"), as urgency ("we have to do this *next quarter*"), as protection of the team ("I don't want to put them through that"). The brain in fear makes exaggerated predictions and prevents the action that's needed. **Remedy:** notice the somatic signal — tight chest, looping thoughts, dread before a meeting. Make a prediction about the worst-case outcome. Make a bet on it. Then take the action. The prediction is almost always wrong. (See `fear-and-anger.md`.)

### 2. You're hiring too fast.

Investors push. Cap tables tempt. *"We need to win the race to market share."* No race exists pre-PMF, and even post-PMF every additional teammate adds coordination overhead geometrically. **Remedy:** stay 2–6 people until you have real PMF (not "good signal" — paying customers raving). Then scale, but every hire is a person you have to coordinate, motivate, and inform forever. The biggest companies built with the fewest people are the easiest to run.

### 3. You're staying in your Zone of Excellence.

You're great at it, the company pays you for it, you're better than anyone around you — and you don't love it. Every hour you spend in Excellence is an hour stolen from your Zone of Genius (great *and* love), and you're blocking someone who genuinely loves the work from doing it better. **Remedy:** Energy Audit (see `energy-audit.md`). Hire your replacement for the Excellence work, even if you're scared the company can't afford to lose you in that seat. Naval at AngelList is the canonical case: hated CEO work, was good at it, replaced himself, AngelList took off, Naval took off.

### 4. You're avoiding hard conversations.

Performance issues that should have been raised a quarter ago, an exec who's washed out but you keep "giving more time," a co-founder conflict you're managing around. Your fear is producing a story — "they'll quit," "the team will fracture," "she'll never speak to me again." That story is almost always wrong. **Remedy:** the difficult conversations method (see `difficult-conversations.md`). Run Heard first. Make them feel heard before any feedback lands. Then say the hard thing in a single sentence, then more Heard, then commit to a next action. Most "impossible" conversations resolve in twenty minutes.

### 5. You're micromanaging instead of delegating.

You've hired execs but you're still in their meetings, still rewriting their emails, still in the eng PR review. The story your brain tells: *"I can do it better."* The story is true and irrelevant. **Remedy:** AoRs and DRIs (see `aors-and-dris.md`). Each function gets a single Area of Responsibility holder. Each project gets a single Directly Responsible Individual. You ask for the outcome, not the method. If they can't deliver the outcome, that's a different conversation — and the answer is sometimes a new person, not more of you in their work.

### 6. You're running on fear, not joy.

Fear of failure, of letting people down, of looking foolish to your investors. Fear is a real engine — companies do get built on it — but it has a half-life. Most CEOs Mochary coaches discover this somewhere between Series B and Series C: the fear that drove you for five years is slowly killing you, and the company has plateaued because the fear has plateaued. **Remedy:** Conscious Leadership work (see `conscious-leadership.md`). Lose the fear. The business will dip for a while because the engine you were running on is gone. Push through. Joy becomes the new engine, and joy outlasts fear at scale. Life gets better *and* the business thrives.

### 7. You're scheduling 1-on-1s that prevent group truth-telling.

This is the 2025 update to the older Mochary playbook. *The Great CEO Within* (2018) takes 1-on-1s as foundational. The current curriculum reverses that. The CEOs Mochary now coaches at the top of his portfolio — Sam Chaudhary at ClassDojo, Alberto Rizzoli at V7, Jonathan Siddhart at Turing, Dan Lorenc at Chainguard, plus Jensen Huang at NVIDIA and Brian Chesky at Airbnb — have largely killed 1-on-1s. **Why:** issues raised in 1-on-1s route around the group; the team never builds the muscle of resolving issues in front of each other; the CEO ends up being the air-traffic controller for every conflict. **Remedy:** force issues and information into the group. Replace 1-on-1s with daily standups, weekly unblocking sessions for top 10–20 projects, and group feedback sessions. Frees up 10–15 hours per week for the CEO and produces a much more honest organization.

### 8. You're fundraising without product-market fit.

Capital arrives, the team grows, the burn rate locks in, and now you have to pretend you've solved a problem you haven't yet. Investors will push you to raise; they're not wrong from their portfolio math, but they're wrong from yours. **Remedy:** raise only what you need to find PMF. Once PMF is real (paying customers, organic growth, NPS through the roof), raise aggressively to scale. Premature scaling — of the cap table, the team, or the office — is the most common pre-mortem cause of death Mochary sees.

### 9. You're filling your calendar to feel productive.

The CEOs in Mochary's top tier have huge unscheduled blocks every day and entire days with nothing on the calendar. They're available for fires. They're available to think. They're available to call a customer or write the next version of the vision. CEOs running on fear fill the calendar with operating meetings — it feels productive but it makes the CEO unreachable for the only two things only they can do. **Remedy:** the CEO's two jobs (see `ceo-role-two-jobs.md`). Audit the calendar against paint-the-vision and resolve-conflict. Cut everything else.

### 10. You haven't named what your fear is predicting.

Mochary's distinctive coaching move: when a CEO is stuck, ask them to write down what they're afraid will happen. *"If I have this conversation, X will happen."* Then offer to bet the opposite. Mochary has run this bet hundreds of times. He has yet to lose. **Remedy:** the fear journal. Write the prediction down. Take the action. Compare the prediction to what actually happened. Over time, you stop believing your own catastrophe stories.

### 11. You're treating coaching as therapy or consulting.

Coaching is forward-looking architecture: designing the future, building things, moving into high performance. Therapy is backward-looking archaeology: digging into the past, fixing dysfunction. Both are valuable; many of Mochary's clients have both. But if you've hired a coach to be a therapist, you're using the wrong tool. If you've hired a consultant to be a coach, you're getting answers when you needed questions. **Remedy:** know which one you need today and ask for it explicitly.

### 12. You haven't fired the people you should have fired.

Most CEOs Mochary coaches have at least one person on the team they should have let go six months ago. The cost of keeping them is invisible — team morale, the standard slipping, the message it sends to A-players that B-performance is fine — and the discomfort of the firing conversation is visible and immediate. So the cost compounds. **Remedy:** Firing Well (see `firing-well.md`). Become the person's agent. Help them land their next role. The remaining team is watching how you do it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Series C founder, 400 people. Company growth has slowed for three quarters. CEO is exhausted. Six items above are firing simultaneously: she's in fear (1), she hired too fast last year (2), she's still doing all of product because no one is good enough (3), she has an exec underperforming for nine months (4 + 12), her calendar is full (9), she's running on fear of disappointing the board (6).

**Application:** Don't try to fix all six. Pick the most upstream one. Fear is upstream of all of them. Mochary coaches: *"I think you're in fear right now. What is your brain predicting will happen if you have the conversation with [exec]?"* CEO: *"He'll quit and we'll lose his entire team."* Mochary: *"I'll bet you the opposite. He'll stay through the transition and his team will perform better."* CEO accepts the bet, has the conversation. Exec stays for three months, hands off cleanly, leaves to start his own company on great terms. Team performance rises immediately.

**Result:** With the exec issue resolved, the CEO has bandwidth to do the Energy Audit (item 3). She delegates product to her VP Product, who has been waiting for the seat. Calendar opens up (item 9). Within six months, growth has restarted and the CEO is sleeping again.

---

**Situation:** First-time CEO of a 60-person company asks for a self-assessment.

**Application:** Walk the checklist. Score each item 1–3. The two items he scores 3 on: 5 (micromanaging) and 7 (1-on-1s preventing group truth-telling). He has weekly 1-on-1s with all twelve direct reports plus skip-levels — 18 hours per week of 1-on-1s alone — and is rewriting most of his execs' emails. **Remedy:** kill the 1-on-1s for two months. Replace with a single daily 15-minute exec standup and a weekly 90-minute exec team meeting structured around the issue/proposed-solution flow. Stop being CC'd on emails. Each exec gets explicit AoRs and is told *"I will ask for outcomes, not methods."*

**Result:** Two months in, the CEO has 14 hours per week back. Issues that used to surface in 1-on-1s now surface in the group meeting where the relevant people can actually resolve them in real time. Two execs who were quietly underperforming become visible to the team and self-correct (or in one case, leave). The org gets noticeably more honest within sixty days.

---

**Situation:** Founder asks: *"Which of these is most common?"*

**Application:** Not which is most common — which is most upstream. **Item 1, fear, is upstream of items 4, 5, 8, 9, and 12.** Most CEO failures look like a calendar problem or a delegation problem or a conversation problem. They are almost all secretly a fear problem. The way to find out: ask yourself, *"if I had no fear about how this turns out, what would I do today?"* The answer is usually the answer.

**Result:** The founder writes down the predictions her brain is making about three pending decisions. Reads them back. Realizes all three are catastrophic and none have an evidence base. Takes one of the three actions that day. The prediction does not come true. The pattern starts to break.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run the checklist on your team and present it as their problem.
**Why:** This is a self-applied diagnostic. The moment you compile a list of which of these your COO is doing wrong, you've defected from the framework. The point is to look at yourself. The team's behavior is downstream of the CEO's.

**Don't:** Try to fix all twelve items at once.
**Why:** Most are interconnected. The upstream fix (usually fear, sometimes Zone of Excellence) cascades. Pick one. Move it. Re-run the checklist in a quarter.

**Don't:** Treat the checklist as a final judgment.
**Why:** Every CEO Mochary coaches scores 3 on something at any given time. The score isn't a measure of whether you're a good CEO. It's a measure of where the work is right now.

**Don't:** Use this list to argue with your coach.
**Why:** A coach who is pointing out one of these patterns is doing the work. Pushing back ("I'm not in fear, I just have valid concerns") is itself a fear response. Notice it. Run the bet.

**Don't:** Conflate the 2018 book with the 2025 curriculum.
**Why:** *The Great CEO Within* taught 1-on-1s as foundational. The current Mochary curriculum recommends getting rid of them. Both are Mochary; the curriculum has evolved with what the top operators in his portfolio are actually doing now. Take the 2025 stance unless you're early-stage and need the older basics first.

**Don't:** Assume one good run-through means you're done.
**Why:** Each new stage of company growth surfaces new versions of the same patterns. The fear at Series A is different from the fear at Series C is different from the fear at IPO. Re-run the checklist quarterly. The patterns repeat. The remedy gets faster every time you've already used it.

**Don't:** Skip the bet.
**Why:** The bet — naming the prediction your fear is making, then taking the opposite action — is the operational heart of the entire diagnostic. Reading the list without making the bet is reading. Making the bet is changing.
