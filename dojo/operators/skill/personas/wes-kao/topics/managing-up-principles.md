# Managing Up Principles

## When to Use
- You report to someone — a manager, a CEO, a board, a co-founder, a client. You want more trust, faster approvals, more interesting work, and the benefit of the doubt.
- You're trying to figure out why your boss is "micromanaging" you, why approvals take forever, or why your great ideas keep stalling.
- You're a senior IC, manager, or operator who suspects you've been treating "managing up" as optional. It isn't.

## Fails When
- **Your manager is acting in bad faith.** If they undermine you, blame you for things outside your control, or are insecure about their own work, no amount of managing up will fix it. Use your judgment and get out.
- **You're performing loyalty instead of doing the work.** Managing up is not flattery, not "being seen," not Slack-presence theater. It's competent operating + appropriate communication. Without the operating part, the communication is hollow.
- **You over-rotate to the point of paralysis.** If you can't make a decision without a manager check-in, you've turned managing up into managing-down-on-yourself.

## Core Concept

Managing up is not optional. It is not something you graduate out of. It is not sucking up. It is the skill of being a competent operator who follows through, communicates appropriately, and drives toward business goals — and making sure your manager can see that you are doing all of those things.

> 🚫 "It's my manager's job to manage me. I'll manage up when I'm promoted."
>
> ✅ "I shouldn't have to manage up. But if I do, I'll take control and further my career."

The Jedi mind trick is this: **let your boss think they are managing you. But in reality, you are managing them.** When your boss loves you, you have options. You get more interesting projects. You get to push back and your boss respects that. You are treated like you know what you're talking about. You get the benefit of the doubt — and the benefit of the doubt can be everything.

The mental model that unlocks this: your area of responsibility is 100% of your time, but it might be 10% of your manager's time. They have other reports, they're producing their own output, they're drafting next quarter's priorities, they're shielding your team from lame requests, and more. Managing up is the act of meeting them where they are with the context they need, in the format they can use, at the moment they need it.

What follows is the spine of how to do this. There are fifteen principles in the original essay; here are the eight that do the most work.

## How to Apply

### 1. Embrace it. Expect to do it forever.

You don't graduate out of managing up. In your twenties: "When I'm a leader, I won't have to manage up anymore." In your thirties: "I'm going to have to manage up forever. This never ends." Founders manage up to their boards and co-founders. CEOs manage up to investors. Senior leaders are usually the *best* at it — that's how they got there.

There's no such thing as too much trust. Every leader manages up. Your manager is doing it too. And so is theirs.

### 2. Focus on the punchline (and use information hierarchy)

Give enough context to avoid back-and-forth, but don't bury the lede. Assume your manager is task-switching or checking your Slack message between meetings. Make it super easy for them to catch up on context and dive into what you want to discuss.

> 🚫 "This happened, then this other thing, this important thing that's completely buried, and then this other thing."
>
> ✅ "Here's the minimum viable backstory, so we can spend time on the juicy stuff."

The structural rule: **recommendation, action item, or question at the top — context below.** This lets folks read as much or as little of the context as they need. It is especially powerful in async/remote teams where so much communication is in writing.

> 🚫 Action items, backstory, bottlenecks, and context all jumbled together
>
> ✅ Action items or question at the top, context separately below

### 3. Show your thought process

Don't just give answers — give rationale. Even if your manager disagrees, you've given them something to push back on, which propels the process forward.

> 🚫 "I recommend this."
>
> ✅ "I recommend X. Here's the rationale, data, and examples of why this should work. The potential risks are Y, but we can address by doing Z. If you agree, I can start by doing [first step]."

This is also why you should **mention your criteria and assumptions** — what criteria you used, how many options you considered, what the trade-offs are. Otherwise it's not that helpful to just send a list. Your recipient still has to do the hard part of digging through everything and deciding.

### 4. Flag potential issues — and bring solutions, not complaints

Stay alert. Surface things bubbling underneath. You reduce your manager's decision fatigue *and* you sharpen your own judgment. This is win-win.

> 🚫 "This is a problem."
>
> ✅ "This is a problem. Here's why it matters. I suggest doing X."
>
> ✅ "An important customer said X. I don't think it's a big deal, but I wanted to bring it up and run it by you."
>
> ✅ "I'm not sure how to solve X but wanted to surface it. My hunch is to monitor for now, but let me know if we should brainstorm what to do."

You don't need the exact solution to every problem. Flagging is valuable. **Complaining itself is rarely valuable.**

> 🚫 "This sucks."
>
> ✅ "This sucks, so here's what I'm thinking we can do…"

A related move: **speak up before the train crashes.** If you sense something is wrong, don't sit on it so you can later say "I told you so." Once the wreck happens, you don't get the moral high ground. It's your responsibility to convince others — which means more than saying it once in passing. Present the situation in a way that accurately portrays the severity, so others can see what you see.

### 5. Keep your manager in the loop. Over-communication might be the right amount.

Trust isn't binary. The simplest loop:

1. Say what you're going to do.
2. Do it.
3. Tell your manager you're doing it.
4. Tell your manager you did it.

Repeat. The problem is when you skip a step. You might be doing the work, but if you forget to tell your manager, they have no idea if it's being handled.

This is also the reframe for "micromanagement." In Wes's words: at least some people who complain about being micromanaged are probably bad at communicating, and thus require more hands-on management.

> 🚫 "Micromanagers are the worst. I wish they'd get off my back and just let me do my job."
>
> ✅ "I wonder what's making them feel the need to micromanage. I'll review how I'm keeping them informed and align on what success looks like, so I can show I'm handling it. When we build up trust, the micromanaging should go down."

A direct report once told Wes: *"I was 'micromanaged' for a while in my career. I felt disrespected and frustrated, then I decided to find a way to resolve it. Every day, I'd over-communicate and share every detail about what I was working on, my reasoning, etc. After a while, the person said to me, 'You know, you don't have to tell me everything, I trust you.'"*

That's the loop. Over-communicate until you can taper. **Task-relevant maturity** (Andy Grove): how often you should be monitored is not based on what you can do in general, but on your experience and prior performance with this specific task. New things → more updates. Earned things → fewer.

### 6. Proactively assert what to do. Share your point of view, not just your questions.

Suggest next steps — don't wait to be told what to do. Almost every leader instinctively asks, "Okay, so what? What should we do with this information?" By making a recommendation, you take on some of the cognitive load.

> 🚫 "What should I do?"
>
> ✅ "I recommend X. Here's rationale, data points, or examples of why this should work. The risk is Y but we can address by Z. If you agree, I'll start by doing [first step]."

There's a lot of advice about asking great questions. Less about the flip side: form a point of view and make assertions despite working with imperfect information.

> 🚫 "Why? Why again? Why again? [Mic drop]"
>
> ✅ "Why? My initial POV is [insight and rationale, patterns you're noticing]. I'm not 100% sure yet, so I'm curious how you're thinking about it, where you agree, where you see gaps."

A great question can be game-changing, but don't expect that simply asking "why" five times will part the skies. You work with smart people. If it were really so easy to ask one question to change everything, they would have thought of it themselves already. The job of a high performer is to **turn vague ideas into more concrete, specific ones that allow you to have a productive conversation.**

The "just ask" trap: when your boss says "if you have questions, just ask," they don't actually mean ask reactively. They mean: ask yourself first what they're going to ask you back. Most boss follow-ups are predictable — *what's the cost? what are the alternatives? what's the risk?* Pre-answer them. You can dramatically shorten the back-and-forth and get work done faster.

### 7. Anticipate questions. Give the right amount of context.

Anticipating questions is a skill that keeps on giving. It makes you better at marketing, sales, negotiation, public speaking, teaching, and more.

> 🚫 "My idea is X. So what do you think?"
>
> ✅ "My idea is X. I thought you'd want to make sure the posts weren't just performing well on social, but actually drove customers down the funnel. So I pulled the top 20 posts that drove the most ideal customers this quarter and…"

The reason your boss takes forever to approve things: **your boss is ultimately responsible for any potentially bad decision you make.** Their head is on the chopping block in a way yours isn't. Empathize with the gravity of what their seal of approval means. Then give them what they need to confidently approve: clear context, anticipated questions answered, criteria stated, alternatives considered, risks acknowledged, mitigations proposed.

The amount of context to give depends on:
- How busy is your recipient? (If your boss has a million things going on, give more in one place so they can quickly understand.)
- How reversible is this decision?
- What's the magnitude of impact if things go wrong? (Lose a few hundred bucks vs. signing a 5-year legally binding contract.)
- What questions does your boss usually ask? Pre-answer those.
- How much detail does your boss usually want?

But also use the **minimum effective dose**. Don't take 800 mg of ibuprofen if 250 mg will work. Do the smallest amount of work to get the insight you're looking for. Don't review 500 student projects if 50 will tell you the same thing. Don't write a 10-paragraph context dump if 3 sentences would unblock the approval.

### 8. Be explicit about what you need. Match your manager's pace.

Don't make people guess what kind of feedback you want.

> 🚫 "Hey, can you take a look?"
>
> ✅ "Hey, can you take a look? I'm looking for directional feedback on whether it makes sense to go down this path, no need for line edits yet."
>
> ✅ "Can you approve this by 3pm today?"
>
> ✅ "I have updates on X. Let's discuss by tomorrow EOD because Y."

Mention if it's an FYI. Mention if you need an action. Mention the deadline and why.

For CEOs and very senior leaders, the same principles apply but the pace is different. CEOs and founders interrupt mid-sentence because they already get the idea you thought you needed three more slides to explain. **Being mentally prepared to move fast is half the battle.**

If you prepared a 15-slide deck and by slide three they say *"Agreed. What do you need from me to move forward?"* — don't insist on going through the rest. You achieved your purpose: you got their buy-in. Take the win and move on.

A few CEO-specific moves:
- Bring a meeting agenda *and* your own internal notes so you can instantly switch between topics.
- Have the next *few* steps ready for batch approval so you can run for longer without your CEO becoming a bottleneck.
- Show a draft or mock-up. *"You can spend 5 minutes describing your idea. Or you can show them a draft and get feedback within 30 seconds."*
- Be specific about where you're blocked — and propose what to do if they approve. *"You're on the critical path for X because we're waiting on your feedback. You've approved most of this copy before, so I recommend we move forward without your edits. I can show you once it's live. If there's anything you feel strongly about, we can make a quick edit."* They can simply say "sounds good."
- **Fewer status updates, more next steps.** Status is backward-looking; next steps are forward-looking. Give 10% context on the past, focus 90% on what's ahead.

## Examples

**Situation:** You're a marketing lead. The team's quarterly enrollment goal is 50 students. You're at 33 with 2 weeks left, and the leads in pipeline aren't going to be enough.

**Application (the silent failure):** You quietly grind. You don't tell your manager because "I should figure this out myself." Two weeks later, you miss the goal. Your manager finds out from *their* manager, who saw it in a dashboard. You blew up trust by trying to protect it.

**Application (Wes's way):** *"We have 2 weeks until the deadline. We're at 33/50 students, and with X leads in the pipeline, it's not enough to hit our goal. So this week, I'll reach out to 100 new leads. I'd love to get your input on Y. Could we review tomorrow?"*

**Result:** You've kept them in the loop, made an assertion about what to do, asked specifically for the input you need, and proposed a deadline. Your manager can quickly say "sounds good" and you keep moving. Even if you miss, they were not surprised — and that's most of what trust is.

---

**Situation:** You want to recommend a new analytics tool to your manager.

**Application (lazy ask):** "Hey, can we switch to Tool X?"

**Application (Wes's way):** *"I recommend Tool X. I evaluated A, B, and C using these criteria [cost, integrations, learning curve]. Here's why X wins on each. The risk is the migration takes ~2 weeks of engineering time. We can mitigate by phasing it. If you agree, I'll kick off with the engineering lead this week."*

**Result:** Most managers will reply "sounds good." You've answered the predictable questions (what alternatives? what criteria? what's the risk? what's the next step?) before they were asked. You saved a cycle of back-and-forth.

---

**Situation:** You sense the team is heading toward shipping a feature that's going to confuse customers, but everyone seems aligned and you're the new person.

**Application (the "I told you so" trap):** Stay quiet. Watch it crash. Earn the moral high ground.

**Application (Wes's way):** Speak up before the train crashes. *"I want to flag something. Looking at how customers describe similar features in our support tickets, I'm worried this framing will create confusion in onboarding. My initial POV is we should A/B test the framing first. Here's why I think this matters more than it might look on the surface — [evidence]. I'm not 100% sure, so I'm curious how you're seeing it."*

**Result:** Even if you're overruled, you've done your job. Once you've absolutely exhausted your options, you can sleep at night knowing you did everything you could.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Send your manager a wall of context with no recommendation, no question, no next step.
**Why:** This is the most common form of lazy managing up. You think you're being thorough. You're actually pushing all the cognitive work onto them. The fix: lead with the punchline. *"I recommend X — context below."*

**Don't:** Equate "I told them once" with communicating.
**Why:** Mentioning something in passing doesn't count if the train still crashes. If it matters, present the severity in a way that lets them see what you see. Persuasion is your job.

**Don't:** Use "I'll wait until they ask" as a strategy.
**Why:** "What should I do?" is the question that dies in your manager's queue. Proactively assert. The worst case is they correct your assertion — which is *still* faster than them generating an answer from scratch.

**Don't:** Confuse over-explanation with thoroughness.
**Why:** More context isn't more helpful past a point. Use the minimum effective dose. If your boss is busy and the decision is reversible, three sentences might be the right answer.

**Don't:** Diagnose every uncomfortable manager interaction as "micromanaging."
**Why:** Sometimes you actually do need to communicate better. The honest question is: *"Are you being micromanaged, or do you need to communicate better?"* Run the loop (say it, do it, tell them, tell them again) for 4 weeks before complaining.

**Don't:** Treat managing up as performance — Slack reactions, "great point," visibility theater.
**Why:** It collapses into sycophancy. The substrate is competent operating. Without follow-through, no amount of communication will save you. Managing up is the *amplifier*, not the substitute.

**Don't:** Try to manage up to a manager who's actively bad-faith.
**Why:** Managing up doesn't work in toxic environments. Use your judgment. The principle is: *"I'll find an organization that's a better fit and manage up to a manager who's eager to work with me."*

**Don't:** Assume seniority will eventually let you stop.
**Why:** Founders manage up to boards. CEOs manage up to investors. The phrase "direct reports" conjures a mid-level IC, but almost every manager is also a direct report themselves. Plan for forever.
