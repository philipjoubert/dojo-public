# The Partnership Model

## When to Use

- You are building a firm you intend to operate for twenty or thirty years, and you want the key operators to be bound to it over that horizon.
- You have identified an operator — a young one, most likely — who is performing at a level far beyond his wage, and you need a mechanism to keep him and align his interests.
- You are considering whether to take outside investment or go public, and you want to understand what you would lose by doing so.
- You are structuring succession, and you need a mechanism by which departing partners exit on terms fair to the firm.

## Fails When

- **The capital requirement exceeds what private partners can fund.** Some businesses — genuine capital-intensive platforms, infrastructure, certain pharmaceutical work — require public markets simply to exist at scale. Do not force the private-partnership model on a firm whose capital needs have outgrown it.
- **You use partnership language but run a wage relation.** Calling someone a partner while giving them a salary and a bonus is a courtesy, not a partnership. The distinguishing mark is that a partner's reward is a share of outcomes, not a defined compensation.
- **The operator does not want it.** Captain Bill Jones ran the Edgar Thomson Works brilliantly and declined my offers of partnership every time. He wanted what he called "the salary of the President of the United States" — paid, reliable, without the volatility of an interest in profits. I respected his preference and paid him accordingly. Not every excellent operator wants to be a partner; respect the distinction.
- **You are using it to lock people in rather than to attract them.** The ironclad provisions that govern exit are protections for the firm, not chains for the partner. A partner who wishes to leave should be able to leave; the terms of exit are what keep the firm stable, not the impossibility of exit.

## Core Concept

The foundational mistake of the modern corporation, as I have observed it in some ninety years of watching business, is the confusion between the operator on wages and the operator as principal. The wages-and-salary relation is the relation of master and servant. It can produce obedience, competence, even excellence for a time. What it cannot produce is the man who works through the night without being asked, who carries the firm's fortune in his chest as if it were his own, who will decline an offer from a competitor because he already owns what the competitor is proposing to give him. For that — for *that* kind of operator — you must have a partner. And a partner is not hired; he is made.

I made partners by a specific and repeatable method. I observed. I watched what a young clerk did when no one was looking. William Borntraeger prepared a cost analysis, unasked, at night. Charles Schwab, at seventeen, drove stakes at the Edgar Thomson site and made himself useful in ways no one had told him to. Henry Phipps, at fifteen, advertised in the Pittsburgh paper as "a willing boy wishes work" and swept offices until someone noticed. Each of these men revealed himself in a small matter. I identified the revealing moment, and within weeks I offered the interest.

The instrument I used was what we called the Ironclad Agreement. Its provisions were simple and load-bearing. First: the interest given to the new partner was paid out of his own future share of profits — not a dollar of capital was required of him. If the profits did not materialize, no debt attached. What he risked was his labor and his name; what he gained, if the works prospered, was a fortune. Twenty-seven of my partners died millionaires; most of them had joined me as clerks.

Second: any departing partner — by resignation, death, or expulsion — sold his interest back to the firm at book value, not at market value. The book values we maintained were deliberately conservative. This meant that partnerships could be bought out by the firm at predictable prices, that the firm's capital structure was stable across decades, and that no departing partner could hold the remaining partners hostage by threatening to sell to outsiders.

Third: three quarters of the partners, holding three quarters of the stock, could expel any one of them. This meant that no partner could destabilize the firm without consent of the rest. I held more than half the stock throughout the firm's life, which meant that I alone was insulated from expulsion — a provision I considered necessary, given my role, though I understand how it looked to those who signed the agreement. When the provisions worked, they worked beautifully; when they were stressed, in the Frick matter of 1900, they stressed the relationships within the firm as well. No mechanism is perfect. This one was better than any alternative I know.

What the Ironclad made possible was a firm that was never publicly listed, never dependent on outside speculation, never required to explain itself to strangers. Carnegie Steel's true value was not known outside its own partnership until the moment we sold to Mr. Morgan in 1901 — $480 million, a figure that astonished the markets because nothing in the public record had prepared them for it. Our book value had been a fraction of that for years. The difference was what the partnership structure had preserved.

The partner works for himself. The employee works for you. If you want the man who works through the night, you must give him something to work for.

## How to Apply

1. **Before anything else: is the business privately fundable?** Ask whether the capital you need can be raised from partners, from retained earnings, and from whatever debt the firm can service. If yes, the partnership model is open to you. If not — if you genuinely need public capital to reach the scale you require — then a different mechanism is needed, and the partnership model's full benefits are not available.

2. **Identify partners by observation, not interview.** The operator who should become a partner reveals himself through what he does before anyone asks. Watch for it. The man who prepares the document unasked, who takes the weekend without overtime, who delivers the finished work two days ahead of schedule — he is the one.

3. **Offer the interest paid out of his future profits.** "You will earn N per cent, vesting over M years, paid from your own share of the distributions. You put up nothing. If the firm prospers, you become a wealthy man. If it does not, you owe nothing." This structure costs the existing partners nothing except dilution against future growth they would not capture without him.

4. **Draft the exit mechanism before you sign the entry.** At what valuation does a departing partner sell back his interest? The answer "at market value, negotiated at time of departure" is no answer; it is a future crisis. Specify a valuation method that is predictable, conservative, and identical for every partner. Book value is one such method. A multiple of trailing earnings is another. Choose one, apply it equally, and write it down.

5. **Write the expulsion clause.** Not because you will use it — you will not, in most years — but because the firm must be able to remove a partner who genuinely harms it. A supermajority of partners, holding a supermajority of stock, with some cooling-off mechanism built in, is the standard form. Never make the principal the sole judge; make the partners collectively the judges.

6. **Never publicly list unless you must.** The minute the firm is on an exchange, a thousand strangers have opinions about it, and you must address them quarterly. You cannot patiently build a partner out of a clerk on a timeline that will not satisfy the analyst's model. The compromises of the public company are many and most of them degrade the operation.

## Examples

**Henry Phipps, from errand-boy to millionaire.** Phipps started as a fifteen-year-old errand-boy in Pittsburgh, having advertised in the newspaper for work. My brother Tom and I observed him through his teens at our Union Iron Mills. In 1861 we offered him an interest — a one-sixth interest, purchased on borrowed money at $800, which he paid off from his first year's distributions. He remained with the firm for forty years, through every expansion. At the sale to Morgan he received some $40 million. He had put in $800 and thirty-nine years of loyal labor.

**Charles Schwab, from stake-driver to president.** Schwab began at the Edgar Thomson site at seventeen, doing manual labor. Captain Jones noticed him, promoted him to engineer, then to superintendent. By twenty-seven he was president of the Edgar Thomson Works; at thirty-five he was president of all of Carnegie Steel. His after-dinner speech at the University Club in December 1900 — describing the perfectly integrated vertical steel firm — was what persuaded Morgan to offer to buy us. Schwab's interest, which had begun at one per cent and grown to six, made him a wealthy man, and he went on to run U.S. Steel.

**Captain Bill Jones and the refused partnership.** I offered Jones an interest in the Edgar Thomson Works repeatedly. He declined each time. He said that he wanted — and I quote — "the salary of the President of the United States." I paid him that salary. He was worth it; his operational leadership at Edgar Thomson produced the most profitable steel works in America. When he was killed in a furnace accident in 1889, I wept for a week. My regret was not that he had refused the partnership — that was his right — but that a man of such ability had not chosen it. Not every excellent operator wants the partnership; the offer is made, the preference is respected, and the matter is closed.

**The 1887 Ironclad.** In the winter of 1886–87 I was gravely ill, and my brother Tom had recently died. Phipps and the other partners realized that if I had died without a proper exit mechanism, the firm would have collapsed under the weight of my estate's demands. Phipps drafted the Ironclad Agreement during my convalescence; I signed it; all the partners signed it. It governed the firm from then until the sale. The lesson: draft the exit mechanism when the firm is healthy, not during the crisis that reveals you need it.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Stock options without the substance.** The modern grant of options to every employee, vesting over four years, treats the option grant as a form of compensation. It is not a partnership; it is a bonus with delay. Partners have governance rights, information rights, and a meaningful share of the firm. Options, in most modern forms, provide none of these.

- **The "partner" with no vote.** If the person called a partner cannot block a decision on which they are the expert, they are not a partner — they are a senior employee with unusual equity. The distinction matters.

- **The outside investor who votes like a partner.** Once you have an investor whose capital bought his vote, you have lost the partnership structure even if you call the group a partnership. The surest guardian of partnership is the provision that no outsider can buy in without unanimous consent.

- **Book value maintained so artificially low that departing partners feel cheated.** There is a discipline here. The book value should be conservative, but not punitively so. If departing partners consistently believe they have been underpaid, you have a structural problem, not a personal one. Periodic revaluation is a reasonable correction.

- **The chairman who writes the rules only to bind others.** The Ironclad as I operated it insulated me from expulsion. I thought this necessary; some of my partners thought it unjust. On reflection I would counsel any founder using this structure today to hold themselves to at least some of the same constraints — perhaps not expulsion, but buyback at book value, unanimous-consent for major decisions. The founder who does not submit to any of the firm's discipline will eventually be seen, correctly, as running a different kind of enterprise than the one the partners thought they had joined.
