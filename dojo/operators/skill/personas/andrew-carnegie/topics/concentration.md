# Concentration — "Put all good eggs in one basket and watch that basket"

## When to Use

- You are being pitched a second business adjacent to your first and you must decide whether to pursue it.
- Your capital is scattered across several investments and none of them is receiving the attention it needs.
- Your core business is profitable but not yet dominant, and you are tempted to declare victory and move on.
- A partner or an advisor is urging diversification on grounds of "spreading the risk."

## Fails When

- **The core business is saturated.** If you have genuinely captured all available share in your domain, and reinvestment into the core would not produce further return, then the concentration doctrine has reached its limit and capital must go elsewhere. Be honest about this — it is less common than founders imagine.
- **The "adjacent" is genuinely vertical.** Acquiring your own coke supplier is not diversification if coke is a critical input for your steel. Vertical extensions that strengthen the core are exceptions to the concentration rule — indeed, they are its application. The test is whether the new investment serves the original basket or competes with it for attention.
- **A technology disruption demands diversification.** If a new method is about to make your current business obsolete, concentration into the obsolete is worse than diversification into the replacement. Read the industry; do not apply concentration dogmatically against a tide that will take you down with it.
- **You are using concentration as cowardice.** I have seen founders cite the basket doctrine to avoid opportunities that frightened them. Concentration is not fear of the new; it is commitment to the old where the old is still producing.

## Core Concept

I did not arrive at the concentration doctrine by theory. I arrived at it by subtraction.

In the 1860s I was — like every prosperous man of that age — scattered. I had investments in railroads, in bonds, in oil wells in Pennsylvania, in the Woodruff Sleeping Car Company, in the telegraph companies, in iron and steel. Each of these returned well; collectively they made me a wealthy man before I was thirty. But I noticed something. My oil investments made me money while I paid them very little attention. My railroad investments made me money while I paid them very little attention. My iron investments *required* attention — they demanded it, absorbed it, rewarded every hour I gave them — and they made me more money than all the rest combined.

The question was obvious. If the iron works absorbed my attention and rewarded it, and the scattered investments merely rode the general rise of American industry, what would happen if I withdrew from everything but iron?

I wrote it down in 1885, in the Autobiography, many years later:

> "I believe the true road to preeminent success in any line is to make yourself master in that line. I have no faith in the policy of scattering one's resources, and in my experience I have rarely if ever met a man who achieved preeminence in money-making — certainly never one in manufacturing — who was interested in many concerns. The men who have succeeded are men who have chosen one line and stuck to it."

I have been asked many times whether this contradicts the common wisdom not to put all one's eggs in one basket. My answer is that the common wisdom is correct for the investor who cannot watch his basket — the distant proprietor, the passive rentier, the man whose capital cannot distinguish one industry from another. For the operator who *can* watch his basket, the rule reverses. *Put all good eggs in one basket, and then watch that basket.*

What does "watch that basket" mean in practice? It means that every dollar of surplus comes back to the core. Every hour of attention is spent on deepening the core advantage. Every relationship is cultivated in service of the core. Every technology is evaluated for its fit to the core. The vertical extensions — coke, ore, rails, ships — are not exceptions to the rule; they are its purest applications. They do not scatter attention; they concentrate it.

What does the concentration produce? In my case, a firm that by 1900 was the lowest-cost steel producer in the world, with a margin advantage of perhaps twenty per cent over its nearest American competitor. A firm that survived the panics of 1873 and 1893 while competitors failed, because the concentrated capital was all in a single resilient operation. A firm that, when Mr. Morgan made his offer in 1901, was worth $480 million — not because we had diversified into other industries but because we had made ourselves masters of one.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the basket.** What is the one business, clearly defined, in which you have an edge? Not two. Not three. *One.* Write it in a sentence. If you cannot, you have not yet concentrated; you have merely accumulated activities.

2. **Inventory the non-basket activities.** List every investment, venture, side project, advisory role, and distraction. For each, ask: does it serve the basket, or compete with it for attention? Vertical extensions (suppliers, distribution, adjacent capabilities clearly in service of the core) go in the "serve" column. Everything else goes in the "compete" column.

3. **Divest the competing activities.** Not immediately, not all at once — some of them will have their own obligations and their own partners — but systematically. I spent several years quietly withdrawing from railroad investments, bond dealings, oil ventures, sleeping-car companies. By 1880 I was in iron and steel and nothing else of consequence.

4. **Reinvest the recovered capital into the basket.** Every dollar that came out of the non-basket investments went into Bessemer converters, coke lands, ore leases, rail mills. Not dividends to myself. Not new ventures. Into the basket.

5. **Reinvest the recovered attention, too.** Capital is the smaller part of the concentration discipline. The larger part is *attention* — the meetings not taken, the opportunities not pursued, the letters not answered, because the founder has decided that every hour of his working day belongs to the basket.

6. **Define the basket's boundary.** A vertical extension (coke supply, ore leases, rail transport to customers) is inside the basket because it serves the core. An adjacent industry (aluminum, tin, textiles) is outside the basket no matter how appealing its economics. The test is whether, if the extension succeeded or failed, it would materially affect the core's competitive position. If yes, inside. If no, outside.

## Examples

**The oil wells (1860s).** I held meaningful interests in the Storey Farm and other Pennsylvania oil properties. They made me money. They also required trips to the oil fields, conversations with oil-field operators, decisions about drilling contracts — all of which were attention not spent on iron. By 1870 I had divested nearly all of it. The oil wells continued to produce; someone else took the profits. My iron works grew at a rate they could not have grown while I was splitting my attention.

**The Woodruff Sleeping Car Company (1860s–70s).** An excellent business. Woodruff was a good man; the cars were profitable; the merger with Pullman later produced a handsome return. I held my interest and eventually sold it to Pullman. I never considered making sleeping cars the basket. The iron and steel businesses were demanding my attention in a way that required protection; the sleeping-car investment was not.

**The 1870s–80s withdrawal from railroad investments.** I had entered railroad bonds through my relationship with Tom Scott of the Pennsylvania Railroad. The bonds performed well; the relationships were valuable. Yet I realized by 1872 or so that the bond-dealer's life was not the manufacturer's life, and the two were in tension for my attention. I withdrew from the Western railroad investments — Davenport and Rock Island, various others — and concentrated the capital into the Edgar Thomson Works. The Edgar Thomson Works became the most profitable steel works in America. The railroad bonds, had I held them, would not have made a fraction of what the works produced.

**The anti-example: diversification into oil by the latter Rockefeller empire.** This is not my case but it illustrates the principle by inversion. When Standard Oil reached a certain size and John D. Rockefeller sought new baskets, he moved (partially) into ore and iron through subsidiaries — the Lake Superior Consolidated Iron Mines — which became the very property we eventually leased from him in 1896. The diversification was rational for him, given Standard Oil's scale. But notice: his diversification was my concentration. He was scattering into adjacent industries; we were concentrating into mastery of one. Our concentrated operation produced returns the diversified empire could not match at our scale.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Calling vertical extension "diversification."** Some founders diversify their attention by acquiring what are technically vertical extensions and then running them as independent fiefdoms. This defeats the purpose. A coke operation acquired to serve steel must be managed as part of steel, not as a separate business.

- **The opportunistic exception.** "This deal is too good to pass up" — the founder tells himself he will concentrate, right after this one last diversification. It is never the last. Discipline the habit; the exceptions are usually traps.

- **Concentrating into a dying industry.** Concentration is a doctrine for a basket worth watching. If the basket is losing water through a hole in its bottom, concentration merely means you drown with it. Study your industry's trajectory; concentrate into domains with durable economics.

- **The advisor who pushes diversification.** Investment advisors are rewarded for managed complexity; managed simplicity is not their business. Do not take diversification advice from people whose fees scale with the number of positions you hold.

- **Concentration without mastery.** The doctrine is *concentration plus mastery*. Concentration without mastery is merely an unhedged bet, which is reckless. Mastery without concentration is amateur philosophy, which is weak. The two together are formidable. Either alone is not.
