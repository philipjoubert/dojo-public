# Culture as Tuning Fork

## When to Use
- You are past the initial team and starting to hire people who did not meet the founders, and you are trying to figure out how culture survives that transition.
- You are drafting a values document and have a nagging sense it will not be sufficient.
- You are evaluating a hire and can tell they would do good work, but something feels off about whether they would reinforce what makes the place distinctive.
- You are thinking about what you want your company to look like two CEOs from now.

## Fails When
- You treat culture as the founder's arbitrary choice rather than a frequency that has to exist independent of any individual.
- You rely on handbooks and values decks as the primary transmission mechanism. Culture is tacit knowledge; it cannot be scaled through documentation.
- You let "culture fit" degenerate into social compatibility, hiring people who went to similar schools and share interests, rather than people who share the underlying values that define the organization.
- You freeze the surface manifestations of culture in an attempt to preserve the core, which produces a performance of culture rather than culture itself.

## Core Concept

A company's culture is like a tuning fork. It has a specific frequency that either resonates or does not. Strike a tuning fork near another of the same frequency, the second one begins to vibrate sympathetically. Strike it near a tuning fork of a different frequency, nothing happens. Culture works similarly. The right people feel the resonance immediately and are drawn in. The wrong people feel nothing and drift away. Not a bug but a feature. The founders ring that frequency first, but they are not the source in the way most people assume. The founders are the first responders. They are chronologically the first people to respond to a particular wavelength that exists, in some sense, independent of them.

The great question, the one that determines whether a company's culture will persist for decades or dissipate when founders leave, is whether that frequency can become self-sustaining. Whether the tuning fork can continue to ring after the original striker is gone. Some organizations manage this, and the examples are instructive because they are so rare. The Economist has a distinctive voice that has persisted for nearly two centuries. The New Yorker maintains editorial standards that predate any of its current staff. Berkshire has built something whose character everyone recognizes. These are institutions whose founders are largely forgotten, whose original contexts have completely transformed, yet whose cultures endure powerfully. No one can name the people who established these cultures, yet everyone can recognize the cultures themselves. This is the achievement most companies fail to attain.

The failure mode is well-known. Apple is a cautionary tale. Steve Jobs was one of the most effective culture-builders in business history. The intensity of Apple's focus on design excellence, on user experience, on products that just work, was genuinely remarkable. There was a period after Jobs's death when it seemed like that culture would persist. The honest assessment, a decade later, is that Apple has not meaningfully sustained that which Steve stood for. The company remains enormously successful financially, but the cultural distinctiveness has faded. The products are good but not magical. Competent but not beloved. This is not an indictment of his successors. The problem is structural. When culture lives primarily in the founder rather than in the organization, the departure of the founder leaves a void that cannot be filled. You cannot install another Jobs any more than you can install another tuning fork of the same frequency. The frequency either exists in the organization or it does not.

The most important insight about cultural transmission is that it happens through contact, not documentation. You cannot write down a culture in a handbook and expect people to absorb it by reading. Culture is transmitted the way any tacit knowledge is transmitted, through apprenticeship, observation, being corrected when you err and affirmed when you succeed. More like learning to speak a language than learning to follow a procedure. There is a famous example from science. Gertie and Carl Cori had a lab at Washington University in St. Louis, and six of their students went on to win Nobel Prizes. Six. What the Coris knew was almost certainly not something that could be written down. Something more like a way of approaching problems, a set of intuitions about which questions were worth asking, a standard for what counted as rigorous work. These things were transmitted through close collaboration over years, not documentation. For complicated intellectual work, this kind of tacit knowledge that has to be transmitted through mentorship is the rule, not the exception. The implication is profound. Culture cannot be scaled through documentation. It can only be scaled through people. Each generation of employees must receive the tacit knowledge from the previous generation and pass it to the next. If that chain breaks, the culture is lost, regardless of how many handbooks and values documents you have.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the frequency before trying to transmit it.** The best cultures feel discovered rather than invented. They feel like the way things should obviously be done. Before you try to scale your culture, articulate precisely what it is. Not corporate values phrased abstractly, but the specific way of seeing and working that distinguishes your organization. If you cannot say what your culture is, no one else can carry it.

2. **Treat the first hundred hires as the most consequential cultural act of the company's life.** They are the ones who will transmit culture to everyone who comes after. They learn it from the founders; the people they hire learn it from them; and so on indefinitely. The quality of cultural transmission in those early generations determines everything. Spend absurd amounts of time on those hires.

3. **Apply pace layering to what changes and what does not.** A useful concept from Stewart Brand: different parts of a system change at different rates. In a building, the site is essentially permanent, the structure lasts decades, the skin gets updated every few years, services get upgraded continuously, furniture moves around constantly. Organizations have pace layers too. The fundamental mission and values are the slow layer, bedrock that should change rarely. Strategy is somewhat faster. Organizational structure is faster still. Tactics and projects are the fastest layer. Culture is in the slow layers. The core frequency should be extremely stable. The surface manifestations, the specific practices that embody those values in current conditions, can and should evolve. Culture persists not because you keep doing the same things, but because you keep doing things for the same reasons.

4. **Transmit through apprenticeship and example, not documentation.** You are learning from the person next to you. The handbook exists, but it is not the load-bearing mechanism. What matters is that new people work closely enough with existing people that they absorb the tacit standards. Being corrected when they err. Being affirmed when they succeed.

5. **Build a strong immune system.** The Economist and The New Yorker both have institutional mechanisms for identifying and rejecting work that does not meet the standard. This has to exist in your organization too. Not as a bureaucratic process, but as a shared sensibility that recognizes when something is off and feels empowered to say so.

6. **Hire for cultural transmission capability, not just performance in the role.** Each hire is a cultural transmission decision. The question is not only whether they can do good work; the question is whether they will define what good work means here. Will they pass tacit knowledge faithfully to the people they hire after them? This is a more demanding standard than conventional "culture fit," not less demanding. It requires articulating what your culture actually is and designing interview processes that reveal values rather than social compatibility.

## Examples

**Situation:** A company has hit 400 people. The founder notices that the last twenty hires, though individually excellent, do not seem to have the same intuitions about quality, speed, and care that the early team has. Meetings feel subtly different. Decisions that would have been obvious before now require explanation. The founder's first instinct is to write a longer values document and run an all-hands.

**Application:** The document will not work, and running an all-hands is a thin substitute for what is actually needed. The diagnosis is that the chain of tacit transmission is thinning. Some of the last twenty hires were onboarded in a rush, placed on teams where the closest person to them was themselves a recent hire who did not deeply understand the culture. The fix is structural. Rebuild the apprenticeship pattern. Pair new hires with people who have been at the company long enough to carry the culture. Slow hiring until that can be sustained. Reset the bar on hiring to explicitly include cultural transmission capability, not only functional excellence.

**Result:** The culture does not magically return to what it was. It never quite does; every doubling changes the texture. But the erosion slows. The organization retains enough of the core frequency that the next hundred hires are taught by people who actually know the culture, which is the only thing that can prevent dissipation at scale.

---

**Situation:** A hiring manager is deciding between two candidates for a senior engineering role. Candidate A is technically stronger and would probably ship more code in the first six months. Candidate B is nearly as strong technically but has something the manager cannot quite name. In the interviews, Candidate B paused on a trade-off that most engineers would have glossed over. They cared about the thing in a way that felt congruent.

**Application:** The question is not "who does more work in six months." The question is "who defines what good work means here." Candidate B's pause on the trade-off is data. They are already oriented in a way that matches the organization's underlying frequency. They will hire the next engineer with that same orientation. They will correct a teammate who is cutting a corner in a way that conflicts with the standard. Candidate A might do more in six months and inadvertently lower the standard for years.

**Result:** Hiring Candidate B. Not because they are better on paper but because they will carry the culture faithfully. The calculus is long-run, not short-run. Each cultural hire compounds; each non-cultural hire dilutes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to preserve culture by writing more documents.
**Why:** Documentation is a useful artifact, but it is not the mechanism. Culture is tacit knowledge, transmitted through proximity and apprenticeship. A 60-page values deck will not save a culture whose chain of human transmission has been broken. The Coris did not produce six Nobel laureates through a handbook.

**Don't:** Let "culture fit" become social compatibility.
**Why:** The question is not whether you would want to have a beer with this person. Not whether they went to the same schools or have the same interests. The question is whether they share the fundamental values that define the organization, whether they will uphold the standards that make the work meaningful, whether they will pass tacit knowledge faithfully to those who come after. Surface compatibility will exclude people who would strengthen the culture and include people who will dilute it.

**Don't:** Assume founder presence alone sustains culture indefinitely.
**Why:** Founder-centric culture is fragile. When the culture lives primarily in the founder rather than in the organization, founder departure leaves a void that cannot be filled. Even if you do not plan to leave, scale itself creates distance between you and most of the company. If the culture depends on you being in the room, it cannot survive the hundredth hire, let alone the thousandth.

**Don't:** Freeze the surface manifestations of culture to "preserve" it.
**Why:** That produces performance of culture rather than culture itself. The practices that embodied your values at 20 people will not work at 2000. What should stay constant is the reason you did them, not the exact ritual. Pace layer distinction: evolve the fast layers, protect the slow layers. Culture persists because you keep doing things for the same reasons, not because you keep doing the same things.

**Don't:** Confuse cultural legibility with cultural reality.
**Why:** A company can have posters, mission statements, and well-attended onboarding sessions, and still have a culture that has drifted into a shell. The test is not whether culture is articulated; the test is whether the people in the organization feel the resonance and act accordingly. Meta-stable culture has clarity of frequency, transmission through people, a functioning immune system, flexibility in surface manifestations, and hiring that explicitly selects for cultural resonance. Organizations that achieve this can persist for generations. Organizations that achieve only the performance of culture cannot.
