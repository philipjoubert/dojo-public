# The First Hundred People

## When to Use
- You're making the first ten, twenty, or hundred hires of a company and deciding how much time to invest.
- You're choosing between hiring for trajectory (slope) and hiring for accumulated expertise (intercept).
- You're debating whether founders should personally interview every candidate.
- You're trying to establish a culture that survives scaling past the founding team.
- You're recruiting in a domain where the network signal matters and early hires shape who later hires trust.

## Fails When
- **You treat early hiring as filling seats.** Framing the first ten as "positions" rather than "co-founder-adjacent roles" leads to mediocrity that compounds.
- **You hire for slope in a domain that rewards intercept.** In deep domains like payments, financial infrastructure, or regulated industries, "smart person who'll figure it out" can be years behind someone who already has context.
- **You rush.** The opportunity cost of slow hiring is real. The opportunity cost of wrong hiring is higher.
- **Founders delegate too early.** Pattern recognition takes hundreds of interviews to build. Handing hiring to recruiters before you have it means other people are calibrating on instincts you never developed.
- **You apply this framework past the window.** Once the company is 200+ people and culture is set, the logic changes. This is a framework for the malleable early period, not a permanent operating mode.

## Core Concept

The first hundred people at a company don't just do the work. They define what work means. That sounds like generic business advice until you think through the implications. The first hundred establish the standards that become defaults. They develop the processes everyone else inherits. They hire the next thousand, who hire the next ten thousand. Each early hire replicates themselves roughly ten times through people they recruit, mentor, and influence. The leverage is extreme enough to deserve extraordinary investment. We spent two years recruiting the first seven people at Stripe. Some individual hires took four years of conversation before joining. By typical startup advice, this is comically slow — move fast, ship quickly, grow the team. Yet the time invested in those early hires was some of the highest-leverage time we've ever spent.

Early culture becomes self-perpetuating. We ended up with something at Stripe that had tremendous momentum and endurance. It wasn't just that we hired good people. The culture became deeply embodied by a large fraction of the first hundred. Once embodied by them, it became the water they swam in, the air they breathed. New hires entered an environment where norms were established and expectations clear. Cultural transmission happens through people, not documents. You can write down your values, and you should, but the document isn't what propagates the culture — working alongside people who embody it is. When a new engineer joins and sees how existing engineers approach problems, how they communicate, what they care about, they're absorbing something difficult to capture in writing. There's a concept from philosophy of science called tacit knowledge — knowledge that can't be easily articulated but transmits through apprenticeship and practice. A lot of what makes a great engineering culture, or great product culture, is tacit knowledge. Things experienced practitioners know but couldn't write down. The first hundred people are the repository of that tacit knowledge, and everyone else learns from them.

There's a tension in early-stage hiring that isn't often acknowledged: slope versus intercept. When people talk about hiring, they emphasize trajectory. Hire people learning quickly. Prioritize potential over current ability. Look for people who will be great in five years, not just people great today. There's real wisdom in this. In fast-growing companies, you need people who can grow with the company — someone perfect for today's challenges but unable to adapt to tomorrow's will become a bottleneck. Slope matters. But intercept matters too, and Silicon Valley's conventional wisdom underweights it. In complex domains, there are real returns to experience and accumulated knowledge. Six years of context in payments and financial infrastructure gives you a tremendous advantage over someone who just arrived, no matter how smart they are. The question is always: what domain are you operating in? If it's superficial, where someone can come in basically knowing it already, intercept doesn't matter much — hire for slope, let them ramp up, move on. But if returns to experience are extensive, where understanding the nuances takes years, intercept matters a lot. You need people who have already accumulated that knowledge, who can make decisions based on context that would take newcomers years to develop. Payments and financial infrastructure are in the latter category. The regulatory landscape is complex. The technical systems are intricate. Customer relationships require trust that takes time to build. We valued experienced people more than typical startups because we recognized our domain was genuinely deep. This doesn't mean ignoring slope — you still want people learning rapidly. The ideal hire has both: trajectory to grow with you and foundation to be effective from day one.

One guiding principle in our early hiring was the "known to be good" filter. Early hires aren't just contributors. They're recruiting channels. The first employee you hire attracts a certain network of people. Some join. They attract their own networks. Network effects in talent acquisition are profound. When hiring early employees, you're not just evaluating individual contribution — you're evaluating the quality of people they'll attract. Someone can be genuinely talented without being known for it. Maybe they're early in their career, or they've worked at companies without visibility, or they're just not good at self-promotion. Good, but not known to be good. For most hiring, this doesn't matter. You evaluate on merits and decide. But for early hiring, it matters a lot. Early employees are signals about the company. They tell potential future hires what kind of place this is. If your first five engineers are people other engineers know and respect, that knowledge propagates through networks — "did you hear Sarah joined Stripe? She's amazing." That matters more than you'd think. The first employee is an archetype. They define, in the minds of potential future hires, what an employee of this company looks like. If the archetype is impressive, you attract impressive people. If mediocre, you attract mediocrity.

For the first two years of Stripe, John and I did a lot of the interviews for basically anyone who joined. This might seem like misallocation of founder time — surely there are more important things, shouldn't founders be selling, building product, raising money? Founders have to do many things. But the time we spent on hiring was not a distraction from core work. It was some of the highest-leverage work we did. First, founders have context nobody else has. They understand the culture they're building, the standards they're establishing, the specific people who will thrive in the environment they're creating. Nobody else can evaluate these things as well, at least not initially. Second, founder involvement signals importance. When a candidate interviews with the CEO, they understand hiring is taken seriously. That matters for candidates and for culture — it says hiring is not an afterthought. Third, the interview process is how founders develop pattern recognition to eventually delegate. You can't effectively calibrate other people's hiring decisions until you've done enough yourself to understand what good looks like. The first few hundred interviews build the intuition that lets you trust others later. Eventually you have to delegate — a company can't scale if founders interview everyone. But the question is when and how. The answer is: later than most people think, and only after building the judgment that makes delegation possible.

Why should the first ten people take about a year to hire? The naive answer is to move as fast as possible — every day without a full team is reduced capacity, opportunity cost of slow hiring is real. The sophisticated answer is that the first ten matter so much, with such profound effects on everything after, that the opportunity cost of hiring wrong is even higher. Get the first ten right, and they'll hire the next hundred. Get them wrong, and you'll spend years correcting the cultural and organizational problems they create. Mistakes compound. The right mental model: early hiring is more like choosing co-founders than filling positions. You're not finding someone who can do a job — you're finding people who will shape what the company becomes. The standards are different. The time investment is different. The evaluation criteria are different. Don't just evaluate: will this person do good work? Think: will this person define what good work means here? Will they attract others who raise the standard? Will they embody the values you're establishing? Harder questions, but the right questions.

Early-stage cultural formation is more fragile than people recognize. There's a window, early in a company's life, when culture is genuinely malleable. Norms aren't set. Expectations aren't established. What kind of company this will become is still open. This window doesn't last forever. At some point, culture solidifies. It becomes resistant to change. New people enter an environment where things are done a certain way, and they adapt to that environment rather than reshaping it. The first hundred are hired during this window of malleability. They have an opportunity to shape culture that later hires won't have — both a privilege and a responsibility. A company should be like the resonant frequency of a tuning fork. There's a wavelength, an idea, that defines what this company is about. The founders are chronologically the first people to respond to that wavelength. But the company has to become more than the founders. It has to become an agglomeration of people who share that idea, who resonate at the same frequency. The first hundred are where this starts. If they resonate authentically with what the company is about, they become carriers of that vision. If they don't, the frequency gets distorted, the signal muddied.

## How to Apply

1. **Frame early hiring as co-founder selection, not position-filling.** The first ten aren't people who will do a job. They're people who will define what the job means, who will hire the next hundred, who will embody the tacit knowledge that new hires absorb by osmosis. Write the job description accordingly — what are you really asking them to shape, not just produce?

2. **Audit the depth of your domain before setting the slope/intercept mix.** Ask honestly: can someone smart catch up in six months, or does real competence here take years? In superficial domains, bias toward slope. In deep domains — payments, financial infrastructure, regulated industries, hardware, biology — weight intercept more heavily than startup orthodoxy suggests. The ideal hire still has both, but the balance shifts with the domain.

3. **Spend founder time on hiring far longer than feels justified.** Plan for the founders to be in every early interview loop. This is not a distraction from the real work — it is the real work, for at least the first year or two. You're building the pattern recognition that lets you eventually delegate, and you're signaling to candidates and to the culture that hiring is not an afterthought.

4. **Apply the "known to be good" filter to early hires.** Look for candidates whose reputations propagate through the networks you want to recruit from. The first employees are recruiting channels — their presence at your company becomes a signal to everyone who might join next. This matters disproportionately in the first ten, less so by the hundredth.

5. **Expect the first ten to take about a year.** Two years for the first seven is not a failure of execution — it can be a deliberate choice. The math works because these ten will hire the next hundred, who will hire the next thousand. A wrong early hire is a cultural and organizational debt you'll pay down for years.

6. **Hire for values, because culture transmits through people.** You can write the values down, and you should, but the document doesn't propagate culture. The people do. Every new hire absorbs norms by watching existing employees. If the first hundred embody the values authentically, everyone who comes after inherits them. If not, you're teaching the next thousand the wrong defaults.

7. **Recognize when the window is closing.** The first hundred happen during a period of cultural malleability. By two or three hundred people, culture has usually solidified. The time to invest heavily in shaping culture is while the tuning fork is still resonating, not after the frequency is set.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is six months into building a company and has hired three people so far. They feel behind — peers are hiring faster, investors are asking about team size. A recruiter is pushing to put five more offers out this quarter. The founder is wondering whether to compromise on the standards they set for the first three to hit the hiring cadence everyone expects.

**Application:** Hold the bar. The first ten will hire the next hundred. If the next five are compromises, they'll pull in more compromises, and the cultural drift starts immediately. The sophisticated answer to "you're hiring too slow" is that the opportunity cost of wrong early hires is higher than the opportunity cost of slow hiring. Keep the founders in every interview loop. Use the time to build the pattern recognition that will eventually let the founder delegate safely. If this means taking a year to get to ten, take a year.

**Result:** The first ten people become genuine carriers of the culture. They embody the tacit knowledge that new hires will absorb by working alongside them. When the company scales to a hundred, those hundred people enter an environment where norms are clear and expectations high — and the culture transmits rather than erodes.

**Situation:** A founder building a company in a deep regulated domain — payments, fintech, healthcare, hardware — is getting advice to "hire for slope" and bring in junior engineers who will grow with the company. The advice is partly right but partly wrong, and the founder is trying to figure out the balance.

**Application:** Audit the domain honestly. In payments, six years of context gives you a tremendous advantage over someone who just arrived, no matter how smart. The regulatory landscape, the technical systems, the customer trust dynamics — they take years to develop. In that case, intercept matters at least as much as slope early on. Hire experienced people who have already accumulated domain knowledge, even if they're older and more senior than a conventional startup team. Pair them with high-slope people so the team has both trajectory and foundation.

**Result:** The company can make decisions on day one that would have taken newcomers two years to reach. Mistakes specific to the domain — regulatory missteps, technical shortcuts that break at scale, customer relationships mishandled — get avoided because someone in the room has seen them before. The compounding advantage of accumulated expertise starts earlier.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Delegate early hiring to recruiters before the founders have built pattern recognition.
**Why:** Other people end up calibrating hiring decisions on instincts the founders never developed. The standards drift silently, and by the time the problem is visible, it's already embedded in the culture. Founders need to do hundreds of interviews themselves to earn the right to delegate.

**Don't:** Treat "hire for slope" as universal advice.
**Why:** Slope-only hiring is right in superficial domains. In deep ones, it leaves you with a team of smart people who don't yet know enough to avoid domain-specific mistakes. Weight slope and intercept to the actual depth of the domain you're in, not to a generic Valley script.

**Don't:** Optimize for speed of hiring at the cost of fit.
**Why:** The opportunity cost of a wrong early hire is higher than the opportunity cost of a delayed one. Wrong hires propagate — they shape the next ring of hiring, they embody the wrong norms, they model the wrong behavior for the people who come after them. Mistakes compound in a way that's hard to undo once the window closes.

**Don't:** Treat the first hundred as just another stage of hiring.
**Why:** The first hundred are hired during a window of cultural malleability that doesn't recur. What the company becomes is still open, and the people you choose now are the ones who will resolve it. Acting as though hiring from zero to a hundred is the same discipline as hiring from five hundred to six hundred misses the leverage entirely.

**Don't:** Skip the "known to be good" check on early hires because it feels elitist.
**Why:** Early employees are signals and recruiting channels. If your first engineers are people other engineers know and respect, that knowledge propagates through networks and pulls in more good candidates. If your first engineers are unknown — even if genuinely talented — the network effect doesn't fire, and you lose a compounding advantage you only get once.
