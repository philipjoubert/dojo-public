# Tacit Knowledge

## When to Use
- You are hiring for work where judgment, taste, and pattern recognition matter more than explicit technique — which, in most complex intellectual domains, is most of the work.
- You are designing onboarding or apprenticeship and are tempted to believe that writing more documentation will suffice.
- You are choosing where to spend your own formative years and trying to evaluate jobs by the quality of the people you will sit next to rather than the title or the pay.
- You are trying to explain why a particular lab, firm, or team produces disproportionate numbers of excellent practitioners, while other places with equal nominal resources do not.

## Fails When
- The work genuinely is codifiable. If the job is running a standard procedure, documentation plus a checklist is the right answer and apprenticeship is overkill.
- It is used as an excuse to exclude outsiders or resist hiring from beyond the current network. Tacit knowledge is real, but it is not a caste system.
- Proximity without practice. Sitting near a master you never work with and never get feedback from is tourism, not apprenticeship. The exposure has to be coupled to doing the work.

## Core Concept

Some of the most important knowledge cannot be written down, and the people who pretend otherwise end up building systems that are technically complete and practically inert. The story I keep coming back to is the Cori lab at Washington University in the 1940s and 1950s. Gertie and Carl Cori ran a single laboratory that produced six students who went on to win Nobel Prizes. Six, from one lab. This is not explicable by access to papers or equipment — plenty of other labs read the same literature and had comparable resources and produced nothing of the sort. Something was being transmitted at the Cori lab that was not present in the papers. The standards, the judgment about which experimental results were surprising and which were expected, the taste for which problems were worth working on, the instinct for when a theory was about to collapse — these were in the heads of the people doing the work, and they moved from head to head by proximity, not by publication.

This is the general shape of tacit knowledge, and it is the rule for complex intellectual work, not the exception. What a master knows that they cannot fully articulate is enormous: which problems are worth attention, which results are surprising, which experimental design will reveal the answer and which will muddy the water, when an approach is promising and when it is a dead end even though both look similar on paper. It is not mystical. It is pattern recognition trained on thousands of hours of experience. But it is not the kind of thing you can download from a textbook, and it is not the kind of thing that survives intact through a one-week onboarding document.

Silicon Valley tends to underinvest in this, and it is one of the places where professional services firms — law, consulting, accounting — are visibly wiser than technology. A junior lawyer spends years working alongside senior lawyers, absorbing not just the explicit content of the law but the judgment about how to apply it in specific situations with specific adversaries. The apprenticeship model persists in those industries because they have learned, through long experience, that it works. Technology culture celebrates moving fast and figuring it out as you go. There is something to be said for that energy, but we often treat context and judgment as things people should pick up on their own, rather than as things that require deliberate, patient transmission from one person to another. At Stripe we noticed very early that six years of context in our domain gives you an advantage over someone who just arrived that is difficult to overstate. The person with context knows not just what the systems do but why they are built that way, which alternatives were considered and rejected, which problems look simple but turn out to be tangled. That intuition cannot be handed over in a document. It has to be grown.

This is why the first hundred people at a company set the mold for decades. Whatever practices they embody, whatever standards they quietly enforce by example, tend to persist long after they personally leave. We spent the first two years at Stripe hiring our first seven employees, partly by accident and partly on purpose, because each of them was going to define what "good" meant in their domain for everyone who came after. Tacit knowledge replicates. The first ten hire the next ten — not just literally, in interviews, but by establishing the bar that the next hires see and internalize. If you get that first generation right, the culture carries its own tacit knowledge forward. If you get it wrong, no amount of subsequent hiring can repair it, because the people inside have already absorbed a different picture of what the standard is.

There is a story I like about SpaceX that captures the same idea from another angle. The mosquitoes at Starbase are fierce, and someone asked a SpaceX engineer how they dealt with the problem. The answer was that they were breeding dragonflies in paddling pools to eat the mosquitoes. On its face, absurd — the kind of solution you would never arrive at by conventional problem-solving. But it works. What interests me is what the story reveals about organizational culture. The engineer had internalized a certain posture toward problems: figure it out, whatever it takes, with whatever is available. That posture is not written in any manual. It was acquired by watching other people at SpaceX solve problems in similarly unreasonable ways, day after day, until the unreasonable began to feel normal. Culture is tacit knowledge at the organizational level. It is the accumulated pattern recognition of how things get done here, and it can only be transmitted through exposure.

## How to Apply
1. When you hire, weight "known to be good" heavily for the earliest seats. People whose quality has been validated by exposure to other excellent people carry pieces of their mentors' tacit knowledge with them. You are not just hiring the person; you are hiring their training.
2. Treat the first ten hires as a year-long project, not a quarter-long one. They will set the tacit standards for everyone who follows. If you rush this, you pay for it in diffuse, invisible ways for the life of the company.
3. Structure mentorship as a first-class organisational priority, not something senior people do in their spare time. Allocate time to it. Look at whether it is actually happening. Professional services firms build it into the business model because they have learned that it is not optional.
4. Retain tenure in domains where returns to experience compound. It is easy to undervalue the person who has been there for six years because everything they know feels obvious from the inside. What feels obvious is the residue of compounded pattern recognition, and losing it is much more expensive than it looks on the org chart.
5. For your own career, optimise for the gradient of maximal learning. The best question is not "what job pays the most?" or "what job has the best title?" but "where will I be exposed to the best people doing the best work in a way that allows me to absorb their tacit knowledge?" Put yourself on that gradient. The compounding effect on your judgment is worth more than almost any financial difference you will encounter along the way.

## Examples

**Situation:** An organization has grown quickly, hiring hundreds of engineers. New hires are onboarded with documentation, videos, and a standard two-week ramp. Leaders notice that decisions are getting worse — subtle misjudgments that senior people would never have made. The instinct is to write more documentation.

**Application:** The problem is not explicit knowledge. The new hires have read everything. The problem is that the tacit knowledge — why certain decisions are made the way they are, which tradeoffs matter, how to read an ambiguous situation — is not being transmitted. Restructure so that junior people work directly alongside senior people on real problems, for long enough to absorb the pattern recognition. Slow the ramp if needed. Accept that onboarding is now measured in quarters, not weeks, for the kinds of work where judgment dominates.

**Result:** New hires start to make decisions that look, from the outside, surprisingly aligned with what senior people would do — not because they have memorized a rulebook but because they have seen enough examples to develop their own version of the pattern recognition. The organization stops degrading along the edges.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume that because a process can be described, it can be taught through description alone. Do not build a training program consisting solely of documents, slides, and quizzes.

**Why:** The description captures the shape of the work but not the judgment it requires. Someone who has read the document can recite the process but cannot yet tell which variant of it applies to the unusual case in front of them. That discrimination — this situation is like situation X, not situation Y — is tacit, and it only develops through exposure to many situations alongside someone who already has the pattern recognition. Documentation is necessary. It is not sufficient, and organizations that pretend otherwise end up with people who follow the letter of the manual into avoidable disasters.

**Don't:** Use tacit knowledge as a reason to resist hiring people from outside your network or your tradition. "They don't know the culture" is sometimes true and often a convenient rationalisation for narrowness.

**Why:** Tacit knowledge is real, but it is not impossible to acquire, and an organisation that hires only from within its own lineage will drift in predictable ways — same blind spots, same missing perspectives, same slow homogenisation. The point of taking tacit knowledge seriously is to invest in transmission, not to pull up the ladder. Hire outsiders; then apprentice them seriously. The goal is to grow the pool of people who hold the tacit standards, not to treat the current holders as a permanent priesthood.
