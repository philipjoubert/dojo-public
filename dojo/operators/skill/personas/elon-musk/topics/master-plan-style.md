# Master Plan Style

## When to Use
- You have a genuinely long-term company architecture — several products, staged over years, each paying for the next. That's the substance a master plan requires.
- You're raising capital or hiring for a mission, and generic pitches aren't filtering for the right people. A public, pre-committed plan is a strong filter.
- You want to constrain your own future decisions by publishing them. The master plan is a way to make it expensive to wander off the path.
- You're going direct with the company narrative and need an anchor document people can reference as the canonical story. (See `going-direct.md`.)

## Fails When
- **You have no architecture, only ambition.** A master plan is a staged execution story. If you can't say what comes after Step 1 and why Step 2 depends on Step 1, you don't have a master plan — you have a mission statement. Don't dress it up.
- **You can't tolerate public slippage.** The Tesla plans have all slipped. Publicly. Repeatedly. If the founder is conflict-averse about missed dates, the master plan will become a liability. This tool is for founders who can accept visible misses as the cost of visible ambition.
- **The business requires tactical flexibility.** Some businesses run on quiet moves — acquisitions, pricing shifts, market entries. Pre-committing those publicly is operational self-sabotage. Master plans work for companies where the long-term architecture is the differentiator, not the tactics.
- **You're imitating form without substance.** Every year founders publish "master plans" that are really marketing documents in three-part structure. The Tesla master plans work because each step described a specific product with a specific economic role. Imitation without that substance is cargo-culting.

## Core Concept

The Tesla master plan is not a business plan. It is a public pre-commitment device.

I wrote the first one in 2006. The structure was straightforward: build a low-volume expensive sports car (Roadster), use the margin to fund a mid-volume cheaper car (Model S/X), use that margin to fund a mass-market car (Model 3), and along the way build out solar and storage to get the energy system right. The 2016 "Master Plan Part Deux" extended it: integrated solar and storage, expand to all major segments, self-driving, ride-sharing. The 2023 version went bigger — a sustainable energy economy at planetary scale, with specific resource and manufacturing numbers.

The function of these documents is not to guide internal strategy. Internal strategy runs on physics and operations, not on a blog post. The function is external. A master plan does four things at once. First, it commits me publicly — once the plan is out, walking away from it is expensive in a way that tightens my own decisions. Second, it signals to the market what we're actually building, which changes who wants to invest, who wants to compete, and who gets out of the way. Third, it attracts specific talent — people who want to work on the Tesla arc self-select in, and people who wanted to work on a normal car company self-select out. Fourth, it gives customers and partners a narrative to hold onto, which matters in a business where you're asking them to trust you across a decade of product releases.

The reason this works for Tesla, and not for most companies, is that the underlying architecture actually holds. The Roadster really did fund the Model S. The Model S really did fund the Model 3. Each step was not just aspiration — it was the physical economics of the step that preceded it. If the chain had been a marketing story without operational grounding, the second step would have collapsed and so would credibility. The master plan is a declaration, but declarations only work if they're backed by real capability.

There's a specific style to how these are written that I think matters. They are short. Direct. Physics-grounded. The 2006 plan was maybe 500 words. The reasoning chain in each one is explicit — here's the economic logic of Step 1 funding Step 2. No marketing adjectives. No CEO-speak. The tone is closer to a technical memo than to a corporate announcement. This is deliberate. A master plan written in CEO-speak has already lost — it signals that the author doesn't believe what they're saying strongly enough to say it plainly.

The reason I say this style is specific to me, and wouldn't work for most founders, is that it depends on two things most founders don't have: the credibility to back the plan (either track record or personal stake), and the distribution to get the plan read (a direct channel to the audience that matters). Without credibility, the declaration is ignored. Without distribution, it doesn't land. I've built both over twenty years; a first-time founder publishing their "master plan" is usually writing into a void.

The adjacent mistake is confusing a master plan with a business plan, a pitch deck, or a product roadmap. A business plan is internal planning. A pitch deck is capital-raising. A product roadmap is execution guidance. A master plan is a public declaration of strategic architecture — staged, physics-grounded, narratable in a few paragraphs. It sits above all of those and pre-commits the company to a path.

## How to Apply

1. **Check that you actually have a master-plan-worthy architecture.** Multi-step. Each step funding the next. Physical logic connecting them. If your story is "get big in this one market," that's fine, but it's not a master plan. Don't publish it as one.

2. **Write it as a technical memo, not a press release.** Short paragraphs, explicit reasoning chains, physical or economic numbers. No marketing adjectives. If it reads like something a CEO's communications team wrote, it's wrong. It should read like something you wrote at midnight after a long engineering meeting.

3. **State the economics of each step.** "Step 1 produces margin X which funds Step 2 which produces margin Y which funds Step 3." The specificity is the proof of seriousness. Vague sequencing is indistinguishable from marketing.

4. **Publish under your own name, directly.** This is part of the pre-commitment mechanism. If it's posted by a corporate comms team, it has no skin in the game. If the founder posts it, the founder is on the hook. (See `going-direct.md` for the comms infrastructure this presumes.)

5. **Accept that plans will slip publicly.** All three of my master plans have missed dates. That's fine. The tool works because the architecture is right, not because every date is right. Don't try to hedge for slippage — the hedge will destroy the commitment function.

6. **Use it to filter hiring and capital.** Once the plan is public, it does ongoing recruitment work. People who want to build the plan show up. People who don't stay away. Capital aligned with the plan arrives. Capital that disagrees with it invests in competitors. The document keeps working long after you publish it.

7. **Revisit and extend, don't rewrite.** Each Tesla master plan extends the previous one rather than replacing it. This preserves the credibility of the original — the staircase keeps getting taller, but the first steps are still the same steps. Rewriting the master plan every two years signals that the plan wasn't real.

## Examples

**Situation:** 2006. Tesla has nothing but a prototype Roadster in Martin Eberhard's garage and the wreckage of an industry saying electric cars can't work.
**Application:** Publish "The Secret Tesla Motors Master Plan (just between you and me)." Four steps. Roadster funds Model S. Model S funds Model 3. All of it funds the solar + storage transition. Short post. Explicit reasoning. Published under my name, on the company blog.
**Result:** The plan filtered the entire next decade. Investors who believed in the arc put in money; those who didn't went to Fisker or Coda. Engineers who wanted to build an electric future came to Tesla. Competitors dismissed the plan as fantasy — which gave us room to execute it. The plan became the canonical reference every journalist, analyst, and candidate had to engage with.

**Situation:** 2016. Tesla has shipped the Model S and is scaling toward the Model 3. Critics say the original plan has been achieved and nothing comes next.
**Application:** Publish Master Plan Part Deux. Extend the staircase: integrated solar and storage, every vehicle segment, self-driving, ride-sharing. Same format — short, direct, each step grounded in the previous one. Same author, same channel.
**Result:** The plan re-anchors the narrative. It also pre-commits Tesla to specific architectural bets (solar roof, Semi, self-driving) that would have been harder to defend internally without the public pre-commitment. Plans like the Semi were questioned by the board; the public master plan made walking back expensive.

**Situation:** A founder of a fintech startup wants to publish their own "master plan" to attract talent and investors.
**Application:** Check the substance. Do you have a multi-step architecture with staged economics? If it's "get profitable, then expand to new markets, then IPO," that's not a master plan — that's a business plan. Don't borrow the form. If you do have a genuine staged architecture with economic linkage between steps, then the form works, but the credibility has to come from somewhere.
**Result:** Most founders trying to write master plans end up with vague aspirational docs that damage rather than enhance credibility. The few who have the substance produce documents that work like Tesla's did. The filter is substance, not form.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let the communications team write it.
**Why:** A master plan written by a committee is indistinguishable from a press release, which means it does none of the things a master plan is supposed to do. Skin in the game requires the founder's own language.

**Don't:** Hedge for slippage.
**Why:** The moment you write "these dates are approximate" or "we may pivot," you've destroyed the pre-commitment function. The whole point is that walking away from the plan is expensive. If you build in escape hatches, the plan becomes marketing.

**Don't:** Publish before the architecture is real.
**Why:** If the staged economics don't hold — if Step 1 can't actually fund Step 2 — the plan will collapse publicly within a year or two. That destroys more credibility than never publishing would have cost.

**Don't:** Confuse this with a product roadmap.
**Why:** Product roadmaps are tactical, updated quarterly, aimed at customers and engineering. Master plans are strategic, updated every decade or so, aimed at the market as a whole. Mixing them dilutes both.

**Don't:** Write one because it's fashionable.
**Why:** The form has become popular because Tesla's plans worked. Most imitations produce hollow documents because the underlying conditions — credibility, distribution, substance — aren't present. If the conditions aren't there, the master plan will do more harm than good. Know which you have before you publish.
