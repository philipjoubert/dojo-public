# The Bar Raiser

## When to Use
- Designing the hiring process at a company growing past the founder-recruits-everyone stage.
- Diagnosing why hiring quality has drifted downward over time — usually the answer is urgency bias.
- A team is under delivery pressure and wants to hire someone who has gotten soft "yeses" and hard "no but..."s.
- Every hire, at every level. No exceptions is part of the design.

## Fails When
- The Bar Raiser role is titled but not given real veto authority. The protocol collapses into ceremony.
- Bar Raisers are chosen by the hiring manager or someone on the hiring team. Independence is the point.
- The organization rewards filling seats but doesn't reward raising the bar. Incentives eat structure.
- Interviewers submit vague impressions ("seemed smart, passionate") instead of detailed behavioral evidence with verbatim Q&A.

## Core Concept

Every new hire should raise the average. You should be able to say, "I'm glad I joined when I did. If I interviewed for a job today, I'm not sure I'd be hired." That's the bar. Over time, the average of everyone on the team should go up. The alternative is Day 2 — mediocrity creeps in one hire at a time, and it creeps in for a specific reason: urgency bias. Teams under pressure hire the candidate who is good enough rather than the candidate who is actually better than average. The cost of an empty seat is immediate and personal; the cost of a bad hire is diffuse and delayed. People make the rational short-term choice — and kill the long-term quality of the team.

The Bar Raiser is a mechanism against urgency bias. A specially trained interviewer who is not on the hiring team joins the loop. They interview the candidate like everyone else, but they also run the debrief, and they have veto power over the hire. They are not subject to the hiring team's pressure to fill the seat. They care about one thing: is this hire above the current bar? If yes, approve. If no, veto. The team can argue. The team cannot override.

The whole process matters, not just the Bar Raiser. The job description maps to Leadership Principles. Each interviewer is assigned specific principles to probe. They use STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result — for behavioral interviewing, and they probe aggressively for individual contribution: "If you were assigned to a different project, what would have changed about Project X?" or "What was the toughest call on the project, and who made it?" The signal is specific, personal, verifiable. A weak candidate describes team accomplishments; a strong one has specific, verifiable examples of decisions they personally made. We call the weak pattern "he was a passenger, not a driver." Every interviewer writes detailed, data-driven feedback with verbatim Q&A before the group debriefs. Vague impressions are unacceptable. Written feedback first prevents groupthink at the debrief.

The Bar Raiser is a senior person outside the hiring team. They run the debrief. They make the recommendation. The hiring manager can disagree, but the Bar Raiser has the veto. This is the critical design point: the Bar Raiser's incentive is the long-term quality of the company, not the short-term filling of the role. The hiring manager's incentive is the opposite. You need both incentives in the room, and you need the long-term incentive to have the final say. That is what the veto encodes.

## How to Apply

1. **Map the job description to Leadership Principles.** Which principles matter most for this role? Assign each interviewer specific principles to probe. Every principle that matters gets covered by at least one interviewer.

2. **Train Bar Raisers separately from regular interviewers.** Bar Raising is a skill. It requires explicit training — how to run a debrief, how to weigh written feedback, how to use veto authority without becoming an obstacle.

3. **The Bar Raiser is always outside the hiring team.** No exceptions. The independence is structural. A Bar Raiser who depends on the hiring manager for future reviews is not independent.

4. **Use STAR with deep probes.** Not just "tell me about a time you..." but relentless probing into what the candidate personally did versus what the team did. "If you were assigned elsewhere, what would have changed?" "Who made the toughest call?" Keep probing until you have confidence in the individual contribution.

5. **Written feedback before debrief.** Every interviewer writes detailed feedback with verbatim Q&A before the group meets. Vague impressions are rejected. This prevents anchoring at the debrief.

6. **Debrief led by the Bar Raiser.** They synthesize the written feedback. They articulate the recommendation. They have veto.

7. **No hire is better than a bad hire.** This is the operating principle the Bar Raiser enforces. Empty seats hurt. Bad hires hurt more, for longer, across the whole team.

## Examples

**Situation:** A director at Amazon was hiring for a senior role. The team was behind on delivery, morale was suffering from unfilled headcount, and the director had a candidate who had come through the loop with mixed feedback — some strong yeses, a couple of soft reservations.

**Application:** The Bar Raiser ran the debrief. They read the written feedback from each interviewer. They noticed the pattern: the strong yeses came from people who had interviewed the candidate on skills the candidate clearly had; the reservations came from people who had probed into Leadership Principle fit and found weak signals. The Bar Raiser vetoed the hire. The director objected. The veto stood.

**Result:** The team stayed unfilled for another three months. Eventually a stronger candidate came through. Years later, the director agreed the veto had been the right call. The team's long-term trajectory would have been worse with the original hire. The empty seat was painful; the wrong hire would have been worse.

---

**Situation:** A candidate described a successful project in their previous role. The interviewer followed up: "If you had been assigned to a different project, what would have changed about this one?"

**Application:** The candidate paused. They started to describe the team. The interviewer kept probing — what decisions did you personally make? What was the toughest call, and who made it? The candidate's answers were consistently framed in "we" language. Specific decisions they owned were thin.

**Result:** The pattern was "passenger, not a driver." The hire was rejected. Strong candidates have specific, verifiable examples of decisions they personally made and outcomes they personally drove. Weak candidates describe team accomplishments and leave individual agency ambiguous. The probe is designed to surface the difference.

---

**Situation:** A fast-growing startup is hiring rapidly and does not have a Bar Raiser process. Hiring quality starts to drift. The founders notice that the company feels less sharp than it did a year ago.

**Application:** The drift has a structural cause, not an effort cause. Nobody is trying to lower the bar — they're responding rationally to local urgency. The fix is structural: train Bar Raisers, give them veto authority, and commit to "no hire is better than a bad hire." Some current pain (unfilled seats) in exchange for protection of the long-term quality.

**Result:** Companies that install the Bar Raiser process late in their growth have to accept a period where hiring feels harder and seats stay open longer. This is the correct cost. Companies that don't install it discover five years later that they have a team that collectively couldn't get hired by the team they were a decade earlier.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let the hiring team pick the Bar Raiser.
**Why:** Independence is the whole mechanism. If the Bar Raiser depends on the hiring manager for future goodwill, the veto is theoretical. Pick Bar Raisers from outside the team and rotate them.

**Don't:** Make Bar Raiser veto advisory rather than binding.
**Why:** Advisory veto is not veto. Under delivery pressure, hiring managers will routinely override advisory recommendations. The whole point is that the long-term incentive wins over the short-term incentive at the decision point.

**Don't:** Let interviewers submit vague impressions.
**Why:** "Seemed smart and passionate" is useless. The Bar Raiser needs detailed evidence — verbatim Q&A, specific behavioral signals, mapped to Leadership Principles. Without structured written feedback, the debrief collapses into whoever speaks loudest.

**Don't:** Let the debrief happen before written feedback is submitted.
**Why:** Groupthink at the debrief anchors everyone to the first strong opinion expressed. Written feedback first — independent, specific, data-driven — gives the Bar Raiser real material to work with and prevents social dynamics from dominating.

**Don't:** Accept a hire because the team is desperate.
**Why:** Urgency bias is the single most common reason hiring drifts downward. Every time you make the desperation hire, you teach the organization that the bar is negotiable. Over a year, the bar moves. Over five years, the team is unrecognizable.

**Don't:** Reward filling seats without rewarding quality.
**Why:** Incentives eat structure. If managers are evaluated on open headcount filled and not on new-hire performance, the Bar Raiser's veto becomes an obstacle they work around. Measure both.

**Don't:** Apply the Bar Raiser process to some hires and not others.
**Why:** Every hire. No exceptions. If senior roles skip the Bar Raiser — "they're too senior to go through the regular process" — the bar drops at the top of the organization, which is exactly where it's most expensive. The process covers every hire or it covers none.
