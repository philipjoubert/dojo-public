# Six-Page Narrative Memo

## When to Use
- Any meeting where a non-trivial decision will be made.
- Strategy reviews, business reviews, proposals for new investments.
- When you want the thinking — not the performance — to drive the decision.
- When you want the quality of the argument to matter more than the seniority or charisma of the presenter.

## Fails When
- Used for routine status updates — a memo is too heavy for "here's where we are this week." For that, use a WBR dashboard.
- Distributed in advance. Half the room will skim, a third will fake having read it, and the discussion will be uneven and unfair to the writer.
- Rehearsed. A meeting where the team has pre-scripted who says what is one where nobody is searching for truth.
- The author is allowed to perform the memo instead of letting it be read. If the author has to explain it aloud, the memo failed.

## Core Concept

PowerPoint should be banned for internal decisions. I mean that — this is not a style preference. Bullet points give permission to gloss over ideas, to flatten out relative importance, and to hide the interconnections that matter most. When you can't connect one bullet to the next with "therefore" or "however," the argument is broken, but slides let you skip past the break without noticing. A narrative forces you to notice. The reason writing a good four-page memo is harder than writing a twenty-page deck is that the narrative structure forces better thought and better understanding of what's more important than what, and how things are related. The pain of the blank page is the point.

Amazon's standard is the six-page memo: maximum six pages, 11-point, single-spaced. Appendices can carry supporting data, but the six pages must stand on their own. No names on the document — memos are collaborative team efforts, and ego attaches to authors in ways that distort review. Common sections include an introduction, tenets that anchor the decisions, accomplishments since the last review, misses with honest diagnosis, proposals for what to do next, P&L, and an FAQ that answers the hard questions the document raises. The document arrives at the meeting in silence. Everyone reads for eighteen to twenty minutes. Then discussion. Senior people speak last. Someone other than the presenter takes notes.

The hardest discipline inside the memo is honesty. There is no point in sugarcoating things, and it helps to state the tough issues up front. I read every sentence assuming it is wrong until the sentence proves otherwise to me. That is not hostility toward the writer — it is respect for the reader. A writer who hasn't pressure-tested every sentence is asking the reader to do it for them. A great memo probably takes a week or more. The people who think it takes a day or two produce mediocre work, and mediocre work wastes everyone's time at the meeting.

## How to Apply

1. **Set a target length and stop there.** Six pages of prose, 11-point, single-spaced. Appendices can extend, but the main memo must stand alone.

2. **Open with a logline.** One sentence that states the entire argument. If the reader stops reading after that sentence, they should understand the essence of what you're proposing.

3. **State tough issues up front.** Do not bury the misses, the risks, the dependencies, or the things you don't know. Sugarcoating wastes the reader's time and undermines the trust the memo is trying to build.

4. **Include an FAQ.** Anticipate the hard questions and answer them in the document. If a question is so hard you can't answer it, say so — that's useful information for the decision.

5. **Remove names from the document.** Memos are team output, not personal branding. Egos distort review.

6. **Distribute at the start of the meeting, not before.** Silent reading for 18–20 minutes. No exceptions for seniority — everyone reads, everyone starts from the same place.

7. **Let senior people speak last.** This prevents anchoring. If the CEO talks first, the rest of the room is now trying to agree with the CEO, not trying to find truth.

8. **Expect the meeting to be messy.** Crisp documents, messy meetings. A tidy, rehearsed meeting is a red flag — it means the team optimized for performance, not for search. A messy meeting means people are actually wrestling with the ideas.

9. **Re-draft the memo until you can read every sentence assuming it is wrong and still have it survive.** If a sentence collapses under scrutiny, cut it or rewrite it. The bar is: would I let this sentence stand if someone else had written it to me?

## Examples

**Situation:** A product proposal meeting at Amazon. Team had prepared a six-page memo over several weeks. The memo argued for a major new investment.

**Application:** Distribution at the start of the meeting. Silent reading for eighteen minutes. The CEO and senior executives read last in sequence — meaning they began discussion while remembering their cold first read, not watching who spoke first. Line-by-line discussion followed. Senior people spoke last. The memo carried the day not because the presenter was persuasive, but because the argument survived line-by-line reading.

**Result:** The decision was based on the quality of the argument. The meeting ran on the document, not on performance. The same decision, run with PowerPoint and advance pre-reads, would have been dominated by whoever spoke first.

---

**Situation:** Alexa team presented a six-page memo that led with engineering constraints — what the team could build, what the team could not build, why the timeline had to slip.

**Application:** The meeting did not go well. The memo answered the wrong question. Customers don't care about engineering constraints; they care about whether the product is magical. The redirect was: "You are going about this the wrong way. First tell me what would be a magical product, then tell me how to get there."

**Result:** Team went back and re-wrote the memo with the magical experience leading. The second memo produced a decision. The first memo produced a lesson: the narrative structure is not protection against bad framing. A well-written memo that starts from the wrong place fails just as surely as a PowerPoint deck.

---

**Situation:** A team rehearsed their presentation before meeting with the CEO.

**Application:** The CEO could tell in the meeting that the team had rehearsed. He asked. They admitted. He told them: "Don't do that again. For the CEO, you're seeking truth, not a pitch."

**Result:** The team learned that a rehearsed meeting is a failed meeting — not because it is polished, but because polish is the opposite of what this process is for. The memo carries the polish. The meeting carries the search.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Distribute the memo in advance "so people can prepare."
**Why:** People won't read it carefully. Some will skim in the parking lot. Some will pretend they read it. The study-hall format — silent reading at the start of the meeting — is the only way to guarantee the room starts from the same place. It also gives the writing team maximum time to refine the document right up to the last hour.

**Don't:** Let the most senior person speak first.
**Why:** Once the CEO says "I love this," half the room will now try to agree with the CEO, even if they disagreed five minutes earlier. Senior-last is a mechanism against anchoring. It's not politeness — it's epistemic hygiene.

**Don't:** Write the memo in one sitting.
**Why:** A great memo takes a week or more. If you can write it in a day, the thinking was already done, in which case the memo is just a transcription. If the thinking wasn't done, the memo will contain it in bullet-point compressed form — which is exactly what this process rejects.

**Don't:** Put names on the memo.
**Why:** Ego contaminates review. Reviewers instinctively soften feedback to the senior author and harshen it to the junior author. A nameless memo gets judged on the argument.

**Don't:** Rehearse the meeting.
**Why:** A rehearsed meeting is a performance, not a search. The CEO is trying to find out if the thinking is right. A polished performance hides the places where the thinking is wrong. Crisp documents, messy meetings — that's the standard.

**Don't:** Let the author defend the memo in real time.
**Why:** The memo must stand alone, because in every context where it matters — when an executive reads it at home the night before a decision, when a new team member joins six months later — the author isn't in the room. If the memo needs a human translator, it isn't done.

**Don't:** Sugarcoat.
**Why:** An honest, objective, non-emotional tone works best. The hard issues belong at the top, not buried at the end. If the reader has to go looking for the risks, the memo is selling, not thinking.
