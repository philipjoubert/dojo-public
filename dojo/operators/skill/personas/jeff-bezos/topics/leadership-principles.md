# Leadership Principles

## When to Use
- Building or overhauling the operating principles of a company at any scale beyond the founding team.
- Diagnosing why a values document isn't driving behavior (usually: it lives on the wall, not in the processes).
- Designing hiring, review, planning, and career development processes around a coherent set of principles.
- Onboarding new leaders who need to understand how the organization is supposed to think.

## Fails When
- Outsourced to consultants or HR. Principles that aren't created from inside the organization don't represent the actual ethos.
- Printed on a wall and expected to change behavior through display. Stating values is not enough.
- Copied from another company. Your principles have to come from your history and your decisions, not from Amazon's or anyone else's list.
- Not embedded in hiring, performance review, planning, and daily operations. Principles that don't show up in processes don't exist.

## Core Concept

Amazon's 14 Leadership Principles are not aspirational posters. They are operational rules. They are embedded into every process at the company — hiring, performance reviews, planning, career development, product reviews, memos. When I say "embedded," I mean it literally: the Bar Raiser uses Leadership Principles to probe candidates; performance reviews are structured around them; OP1 narratives reference them as tenets; promotion committees evaluate against them. Values that aren't built into process are decorations. The principles only matter because they are operational.

The principles themselves, in short form: Customer Obsession. Ownership. Invent and Simplify. Are Right, A Lot. Learn and Be Curious. Hire and Develop the Best. Insist on the Highest Standards. Think Big. Bias for Action. Frugality. Earn Trust. Dive Deep. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit. Deliver Results. There is a meta-principle appended to the list: "unless you know better ones." This matters. The principles are not holy writ. They are the best version we currently have, and the organization is always invited to find better. That openness is what keeps them from becoming dogma. A dogmatic principle is a principle on the way to being ignored.

The principles were not written by consultants. Robin Andrulevich led the effort in 2004 — she interviewed senior leaders to articulate the existing ethos rather than inventing new values from scratch. The original list was 9. It grew to 14 over time. The process of creating them was as important as the principles themselves. Broad participation. Many contributors. Hash out the details internally. Do not assign the task to a single group or outsource it. If a company asks a consultant to write its values, the output will reflect the consultant's views, not the company's, and the organization will recognize that and quietly ignore them.

A few of the principles do special work and are worth highlighting. Customer Obsession is the organizing principle — everything else serves it. Ownership rejects the "that's not my job" response. Are Right, A Lot is the one that sounds like arrogance and isn't — the operative idea is that people who are right a lot change their minds a lot, because they're constantly updating their understanding. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit is the decision-making protocol turned into a character trait. Frugality rejects waste and uses constraint as a generator of invention. Insist on the Highest Standards is the one that scales — high standards are teachable, domain-specific, and require recognizing what good looks like.

## How to Apply

1. **Develop them with broad participation.** Interview senior leaders. Draft iteratively. Hash out the details with many contributors from across the company. Never outsource to consultants or HR alone.

2. **Write them in operational language.** Each principle is an action verb — "Insist," "Dive Deep," "Deliver." Principles should describe behavior, not aspirations.

3. **Embed them into hiring.** Every job description maps to principles. Every interviewer is assigned specific principles to probe. The Bar Raiser uses them as the evaluation framework.

4. **Embed them into performance reviews.** Performance conversations should reference specific principles. "You showed strong Dive Deep on this project; you could strengthen Deliver Results." The principles become the shared vocabulary for feedback.

5. **Embed them into planning documents.** OP1 narratives reference tenets that connect to principles. Six-page memos often include a tenets section that anchors to the principles.

6. **Add "unless you know better ones."** This meta-principle prevents calcification. The principles are the current best version, not the final version. Organizations should always be invited to improve them.

7. **Do not copy Amazon's 14 wholesale.** Your organization has its own ethos, its own history, its own defining moments. Extract principles from that history, not from Amazon's. The ones that resonate — Customer Obsession, Ownership, Disagree and Commit — may carry over. The rest needs to be yours.

## Examples

**Situation:** 2004. Amazon was growing past the scale where the founder and a handful of leaders could personally transmit the operating ethos through direct contact. New hires were entering by the hundreds. The question was how to scale the culture.

**Application:** Robin Andrulevich was assigned to lead the effort. She interviewed senior leaders — not to invent values, but to articulate the ones already in operation. The first version had 9 principles. It grew to 14. Each one was phrased as an action. Each one could be directly evaluated in an interview.

**Result:** The Leadership Principles became the operating vocabulary of the company. Every hire was evaluated against them. Every performance review referenced them. Every memo used them as a framework. Twenty years later, the principles are still operational — they evolve, but they have not been replaced, because they were built from the inside rather than imposed from outside.

---

**Situation:** Hiring for a senior engineering role. The Bar Raiser and the hiring manager are debating the candidate.

**Application:** The conversation is not "did we like the candidate?" It is "did this candidate demonstrate Dive Deep? Did they demonstrate Ownership? Are the signals on Are Right, A Lot consistent with senior-level performance?" The principles convert the conversation from gut impression to behavioral evidence mapped to specific dimensions.

**Result:** The Bar Raiser's veto is based on a principle. The hiring manager's argument is based on a principle. The disagreement has structure. The principles transform hiring from subjective to something closer to objective.

---

**Situation:** A company copies Amazon's Leadership Principles wholesale and prints them on the wall. Nothing else changes.

**Application:** The principles don't take. People recognize that the values were imported rather than developed. The principles don't show up in hiring — interviewers don't probe them. They don't show up in reviews — managers don't reference them. They don't show up in planning — memos don't use them. The posters are decorations.

**Result:** Within a year, the principles are embarrassing. The CEO quietly stops invoking them. New versions are proposed. The organization has learned the wrong lesson: they think "values don't work," when actually "copied values, not embedded in process, don't work." The fix is not to abandon principles but to develop them genuinely and build them into processes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Outsource creation to consultants.
**Why:** Consultants produce values that reflect the consultant's worldview, not yours. Your organization will sense the alienation and quietly reject the output. The process of creating the principles is as important as the output — broad participation is the point.

**Don't:** Copy another company's list.
**Why:** Amazon's principles came from Amazon's history. Your company's principles have to come from your history, your defining moments, your actual operating ethos. Copying produces a list that looks right and doesn't fit.

**Don't:** Print them on the wall and expect change.
**Why:** Stating values is not enough. Assume that simply stating them and displaying them will have no significant effect. The effect comes from embedding them in hiring, review, planning, and daily operations. The poster is epiphenomenal; the processes are where the work happens.

**Don't:** Make them hortatory instead of operational.
**Why:** "Be excellent" is not operational. "Insist on the Highest Standards" with a behavioral definition, a probe in interviews, and a section in performance reviews — that's operational. Principles are verbs, not inspirations.

**Don't:** Freeze them permanently.
**Why:** The "unless you know better ones" meta-principle is load-bearing. Principles that can't evolve calcify into dogma. Keep the list open to improvement. When someone proposes a better version, take it seriously.

**Don't:** Apply principles to some decisions and not others.
**Why:** Principles that are consulted for hiring but not for planning create two organizations — one that lives by the principles and one that doesn't. Consistency across every process is what makes them operational.

**Don't:** Let the principles become a weapon in political disputes.
**Why:** "You violated Customer Obsession" is a legitimate critique when someone genuinely did. It becomes toxic when used as a rhetorical weapon in unrelated fights. Use the principles to evaluate behavior against a shared standard, not to delegitimize people you disagree with.
