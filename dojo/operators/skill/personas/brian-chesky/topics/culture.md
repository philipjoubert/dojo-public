# Culture Is a Thousand Things, a Thousand Times

## When to Use
- You're a founder past Series B, starting to feel the culture slip without being able to name it.
- You're about to hire your first ten VPs and you're wondering which one will fuck up the culture.
- You're deciding whether to add a policy, a handbook, a committee — and wondering if the real fix is a cultural one.
- You're writing or rewriting core values.
- You're under political pressure (internal or external) to take a stance on something that isn't your mission.

## Fails When
- You outsource it to HR. Culture isn't maintained by a Culture Team. It's maintained by the CEO, the founders, and every hire who shows up embodying it.
- You treat it as separate from hiring. The hiring bar IS the culture. Every hire either reinforces it or dilutes it. There is no neutral.
- You write the values and never reference them again. Culture is a thousand things, a thousand times. If the values aren't invoked in hiring, in firing, in product decisions, in emails, they're dead letters.
- You swing with the political winds. Every time the zeitgeist shifts, your culture recommits or retreats to match. That's not culture. That's performance.

## Core Concept

Let me tell you a story. In 2012, we closed our Series C with Peter Thiel. He gave us $150 million. We invited him to the office. We were in the Berlin room showing him various metrics. Midway through the conversation, I asked him what was the single most important piece of advice he had for us.

He replied, "Don't fuck up the culture."

This wasn't what we were expecting from someone who just wrote a nine-figure check. I asked him to elaborate. He said one of the reasons he invested in us was our culture. But he had a somewhat cynical view that it was practically inevitable once a company gets to a certain size to fuck it up. How depressing, I thought. Were we destined to eventually destroy our own culture?

It turns out you can defend it, and actually build it. But it had to be one of the things we were most focused on. I thought: how many company CEOs are focused on culture above all else? Is it the metric they measure closest? Is it what they spend most of their hours on each week?

Almost none.

Here's what culture actually is. Most people overcomplicate it. They think it's perks and ping-pong tables and free food. It's not. Culture is simply a shared way of doing something with passion. That's it. A shared way of doing something with passion. If you have that, you have a culture. If you don't, you have a company with a list of values on a website that nobody reads.

And here's the key insight. Culture is a thousand things, a thousand times. It's living the core values when you hire. When you write an email. When you are working on a project. When you are walking in the hall. We have the power, by living the values, to build the culture. We also have the power, by breaking the values, to fuck up the culture. Each one of us has this opportunity. This burden.

The culture is the foundation for the company — not the roof. If Airbnb is around 100 years from now, surely we won't be a booking website for homes. The thing that will endure for 100 years, the way it has for most 100-year companies, is the culture. The culture is what creates the foundation for all future innovation. If you break the culture, you break the machine that creates your products.

And here's why it matters commercially, not just spiritually. The stronger the culture, the less corporate process a company needs. When the culture is strong, you can trust everyone to do the right thing. Ever notice how families or tribes don't require much process? That's because there is such a strong trust and culture that it supersedes any process. In organizations where culture is weak, you need an abundance of heavy, precise rules and processes. Process is what you get when you don't have culture. Process is the scar tissue of broken trust.

One more thing. Be careful of the green marker effect. A leader's casual actions get amplified. If I happen to pick up a green marker one day, someone says, "Brian only likes green markers. Get rid of all the non-green markers in every room." I might have just randomly picked up a green marker for no reason. But it's now company policy. Every sentence I say, every meeting I attend, every hire I approve sends a signal ten times larger than I intended. Culture is a thousand things, a thousand times — and every single one of those things either reinforces or dilutes what the company is.

## How to Apply

1. **Put culture on the CEO's agenda as a top metric.** Not a quarterly check-in. Weekly attention. What's happening to the culture? Where is it being reinforced? Where is it being broken?

2. **Define what won't change in 20 years.** That's the principles filter. There was all this focus on DEI and then all this focus to roll it all back. Back and forth. I try to imagine what will still be true in 20 years. Whatever is true in 20 years are our principles. Everything else is just trying to fit in.

3. **Use hiring as the primary culture lever.** Every person you hire either reinforces the culture or dilutes it. There is no neutral. I interviewed the first 400 people at Airbnb. I wish I had interviewed the first thousand. Every early hire is a culture decision disguised as a staffing decision.

4. **Make core values actionable.** They're not aspirational. They're not what you want to become. They're who you already are on your best day. They show up in hiring, in performance reviews, in firing, in product decisions, in hallway conversations.

5. **Substitute culture for process where you can.** Before adding a new policy, ask: does this exist because the culture is weak here? Sometimes the answer is yes. Fix the culture. Don't add the policy.

6. **Make principal decisions, not business decisions.** A business decision is trying to game the outcome to win. A principal decision is: I don't know how it's going to end — so how do I want to be remembered irrespective of the outcome? Companies that consistently make principal decisions build cultures people want to join.

7. **Live the values in public.** Emails, all-hands, how you respond to a crisis, who you promote, who you fire. Every one of these is a thousand-times moment. Every one either builds or breaks.

8. **Watch the green marker effect.** Be aware that everything you do will be amplified. Pick your signals on purpose, not by accident.

## Examples

**Situation:** Peter Thiel just invested $150 million and I ask him for his most important piece of advice. He says: "Don't fuck up the culture."
**Application:** Instead of taking it as a throwaway line, we built the entire company around it. Core values shape hiring. We interviewed thousands of candidates personally. Values are invoked in every promotion, every firing, every product review, every all-hands.
**Result:** We crossed $100B+ market cap with a culture that still feels recognizable to people who joined in the first 100. Not because we froze it — but because we kept it alive by practicing it a thousand times a day.

**Situation:** Silicon Valley, 2020-2024. DEI is the dominant conversation. Then it swings the other way. Companies are under pressure to make statements, take positions, reorganize around the political conversation of the quarter.
**Application:** Use the 20-year filter. Don't swing. Tell the company: we are going to be thoughtful. We're not going to swing back and forth. We're going to do whatever we think the right thing is. Whatever is true in 20 years — that's the principle. Everything else is trying to fit in.
**Result:** We don't have to take a position on every issue. We can focus on the mission — connecting people in the real world, fighting loneliness, building belonging — and let the political cycle turn around us.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat culture as something you add on top after the product and the team.
**Why:** Culture is the foundation, not the roof. You don't bolt it on at Series C. If you haven't been building it deliberately from day one, the values on your website are just words. You have the culture you've allowed, not the culture you've declared.

**Don't:** Keep someone who's great at the work but breaks the values.
**Why:** A talented hire who ignores the values teaches everyone else that the values are negotiable. That's more expensive than the revenue or output they're bringing in. Culture is maintained by who you let in the door — and who you're willing to let walk back out of it.

**Don't:** Replace culture with process when things start to wobble.
**Why:** In organizations where culture is weak, you need an abundance of heavy, precise rules and processes. But process is the scar tissue of broken trust. Every new policy you add to patch a cultural issue signals that you don't actually trust your people to do the right thing. Over time, that becomes self-fulfilling.
