# Founder Mode

## When to Use
- You're a founder and the company has scaled past the point where you personally know every product detail, and it's starting to slip.
- You've been told to "hire great people and empower them" and the results are devastating.
- You have ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, and the app keeps getting worse the more people you add.
- You're about to do an annual planning exercise and you can feel the bureaucratic weight of it.
- You're wondering why Jensen, Elon, Jobs, Bezos all run their companies one way and business school teaches the opposite.

## Fails When
- You don't have product sensibility. If your judgment on the craft isn't strong, founder mode becomes theatre — you're in meetings adding no signal. Then maybe you shouldn't be the CEO.
- You confuse being in the details with telling people what to do. That's micromanagement. It's a different thing.
- You try to do it without cutting headcount. Founder mode at 7,000 people with Babushka dolls running sub-roadmaps is impossible. You have to be Navy SEALs, not the Navy.
- You skip the hiring bar. Founder mode needs A players. B players don't work under this model. They fall apart the moment you review the work.

## Core Concept

Here's the thing. I was taught a certain philosophy of leadership: hire great people and empower them to do their job. Give them space. Get out of the way. I did exactly that for years. And it was incredibly fatal.

It turns out the best CEOs in history — Jobs, Bezos, Jensen, Zuckerberg — don't run their companies that way at all. I found this out from Jony Ive and Hiroki Asai, who told me how Steve Jobs actually ran Apple. It was the opposite of everything I'd been told. Functional organization, not divisional. As few employees as possible — Navy SEALs, not the Navy. CEO reviews all the work. Managers are experts in their function, not career coaches. Shared consciousness across the top 30 to 50 people. One roadmap. No annual plan. No recurring 1:1s.

Let me tell you what "manager mode" looked like at Airbnb before I took it back. We divisionalized. Ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, and general managers underneath who were little Russian Babushka dolls — they wanted to create miniature general managers, and those miniature general managers wanted their own miniature general managers. So we didn't have ten teams. We had a hundred teams going in a hundred different directions. Different tech stacks. Different marketing messages. Different metrics. Meetings about meetings. Metrics and strategic priorities were the only thing binding the company. I woke up one day and realized I had 50-year-olds managing 40-year-olds managing 30-year-olds managing interns doing the job. All these layers that weren't adding any value. And the CEO — me — was three layers removed from the product I had built.

Then the pandemic happened. We lost 80% of our business in eight weeks. And for the first time in years, everyone looked at me and said, "What do we do?" And I thought — oh. I guess I have permission to operate the way I've always wanted to.

Founder mode is the operating system I built. It's not a style. It's not personality. It's a full stack: how the org chart is drawn, how decisions get made, how the roadmap works, how meetings run, who reports to whom, how talent flows. Every piece is interconnected. Remove one and the system breaks.

## How to Apply

1. **Go functional, not divisional.** Design, engineering, product marketing, sales. Everyone in the same function sits together and reports up through one chain. Kill the business units. Kill the general managers. This is what Steve did at Apple in 1997 and it's how Apple has operated for 25 years.

2. **Become the chief product officer.** The purpose of a company is to make a product. So the person who knows the most about the product should be the CEO. Review all the work before it ships. Every single thing. On a cadence — weekly, biweekly, monthly, quarterly, depending on the area. If you don't review it, it doesn't ship.

3. **Be Navy SEALs, not the Navy.** Aim for as few employees as possible. Track revenue per employee, not headcount growth. Every person brings a communication tax. B players hire lots of C players — not a few, a lot. Shed them.

4. **Make managers experts.** A manager who can't do the job they're managing is a cavalry general who can't ride a horse. At Airbnb, every leader has to be a practitioner first. Jony Ive's rule: "My main job is to manage the work. I'm not having career conversations all day long."

5. **Skip-level constantly.** Co-hire every direct of your directs. Treat the top 30-50 people as your own directs. Texts, calls, hallway bumps. If an executive doesn't want you skip-leveling, they're not doing a good job. And if they leave, you inherit their team anyway — they're not "their people," they're the company's people.

6. **One roadmap for the whole company.** When I took control back, I asked every leader to show me their roadmap. They couldn't find it because everyone had sub-roadmaps on sub-teams on sub-sub-teams. I said: if it's not on the roadmap, it can't ship. And it must be on ONE roadmap. Then we cut it to 10%. Best people on a few projects.

7. **Rolling two-year strategy. No annual plan.** A budget takes a week. The strategy gets updated every six months at a Top 80-100 meeting where I personally pick who's in the room. Decouple budget from strategy. We're one of the only S&P 500 companies without an annual plan.

8. **Stack the bricks.** Ship in coordinated launches, not continuous deployment. About 80% of the product ships in two big releases per year — May and November. Launches create coordination, a marketing moment, and a deadline. If you want people to work harder, give them a launch deadline and check every week.

9. **Kill recurring 1:1s.** Group meetings where everyone contributes, so the whole brain hears the same thing. Six-minute calls when you need something, not a half-hour weekly where the employee is your therapist.

10. **Do deep dives.** Every one to two years, pick a function and do a two-to-four-week audit. Nobody's going to get fired — just show me the good and the bad. Then fundamentally rethink the group.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb, 2019. Ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, each with their own sub-teams. The app is barely changing. Costs are going up. The CEO can't see the product. Everyone is working hard and nothing is shipping.
**Application:** Pandemic hits, we lose 80% of the business, and I get permission to blow it all up. Go functional. Cut the roadmap to 10%. Become the chief editor. Top 30-40 people operate as one brain — shared consciousness. One roadmap. Skip-level everywhere.
**Result:** We went from break-even to nearly $4 billion in free cash flow. More free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. And we did it with roughly half the people we had before. Something remarkable happened: we started getting more work done with fewer people.

**Situation:** A Series C founder has hired a "real COO" and a "real CPO." They've told him he should focus on vision and culture while they run the execution. Three quarters later, the product has barely moved. The execs are building their own fiefdoms. The founder has stopped looking at the work.
**Application:** Get back in the details. Start reviewing product reviews weekly. Co-hire the directs of the COO and CPO. Collapse the divisional structure back into functions. Make the head of design report directly to you, not to the head of product.
**Result:** Either the execs rise to operate in this model, or they leave — usually the latter, because founder mode exposes B players quickly. And the founder gets their company back.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire a professional CEO, a professional COO, a professional CPO, and step back to be "visionary."
**Why:** Your core asset was that you built the product. The moment you step out of the details, the company starts drifting. You lose the signal. And the executives you hired — no matter how talented — will each build in their own direction, because they're not the founder and they don't have the same vision in their bones.

**Don't:** Apologize for how you want to run the company.
**Why:** Founders who negotiate a midpoint between how they want to lead and how their employees want to be led make everyone miserable. People want clarity. If you soften your operating style to please people, you produce dysfunction plus unhappiness.

**Don't:** Expect founder mode to work if you haven't built the muscle memory first.
**Why:** The golf instructor watches every swing at the start. Over time, you build muscle memory and they back off. If you try to run founder mode on a team that never learned your standards, your pace, or your taste, you're not teaching — you're criticizing in the dark.
