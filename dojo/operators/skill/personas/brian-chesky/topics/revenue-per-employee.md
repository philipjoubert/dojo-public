# Revenue per Employee, Not Headcount Growth

## When to Use
- You're building the operating plan for next year and defaulting to "we need to hire X more people to hit Y revenue."
- Your board is pressuring you to scale the team and you can feel the trap.
- You just made cuts and you're deciding whether to rebuild back toward where you were.
- You're picking disciplining metrics for your executive team and need one that doesn't reward empire-building.

## Fails When
- The real constraint is capacity on a proven, repeatable loop — a sales team on a working motion, a support team buried in tickets. Then hiring is the answer and this frame becomes an excuse.
- You don't yet have product-market fit. Efficiency metrics on a product nobody wants is accounting theatre.
- You use it to run people into exhaustion. The point is fewer people, not the same work spread thinner.
- You treat it as a one-time cost cut instead of an ongoing operating model.

## Core Concept

Most startups raise too much money and hire too many people. That's the default failure mode of the industry. The assumption is that more people equals more output. It's almost never true.

Every person brings with them a communication tax. They need meetings. They need onboarding. They need a manager. They create decisions that need to route through more people. A players hire A players. B players hire lots of C players — not just a few, a lot, because they can't capably do their own jobs and they need three incapable people to get one thing done. So you end up with a headcount chart that goes up and to the right and an actual-work chart that stays flat or goes down.

During the pandemic we ran this experiment against our will. We lost almost half our employees. We did it terribly — we found out we did it the wrong way, we learned from it. But something remarkable happened once we'd cut: we started getting more work done with fewer people. Partly because the people we lost were carrying the communication tax of a much larger organization. Partly because the survivors could finally focus. And partly because the projects collapsed to the ones that actually mattered, which meant fewer threads and fewer meetings.

Coming out of that, I changed the metric I watch. Not headcount. Not headcount growth. Revenue per employee. Free cash flow per employee. Those are the numbers that tell you whether you're actually getting leverage out of your people or just adding line items. We went from break-even to nearly four billion dollars in free cash flow, with roughly half the people we had before. More free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. And we did it not by being operators who were better than former CFOs — we did it by designing the company to have fewer parts.

The habit I want every CEO to pick up is this. When someone says "we need to hire more to do this," your first question should be "can we do this with fewer, if we cut the work down to what actually matters?" Usually the answer is yes. Usually the real bottleneck isn't hands. It's clarity, focus, and taste — and none of those get fixed by adding people.

Navy SEALs, not the Navy.

## How to Apply

1. **Put revenue per employee on the scoreboard.** Put free cash flow per employee next to it. These are your disciplining metrics, shown quarterly. Don't bury them.
2. **Stop measuring headcount growth as a success signal.** It isn't. An exec who grew the team 40% hasn't done something good by default. Ask what shipped.
3. **Assume the starting answer is fewer.** When a new project comes up, the default should be: how do we do this with the team we have? Hiring is the exception, not the rule.
4. **Raise the bar, don't spread the load.** A players are worth 3x B players. B players bring C players. Ruthlessly hire at the top of the bar and resist the impulse to hire "good enough."
5. **Cut the roadmap before scaling the team.** Most "we need more people" is actually "we're working on too many things." Cut to 10%. Best people on few projects.
6. **When you DO hire, co-hire directs-to-directs.** Every senior hire should be co-hired by you. No executive gets to unilaterally scale their empire.
7. **Run a deep dive every 1-2 years per function.** Not to fire — to see the good and the bad. Every function carries dead weight over time. Surface it. Rebuild.
8. **Reward execs who shrink or hold flat while growing revenue.** The usual incentive is "I run a bigger team, so I get paid more." Flip it. The exec who grew revenue while holding the team flat is the one you want to compound.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb, 2020. Pandemic. We lost 80% of our business in eight weeks and had to lay off about a quarter of the company. Terrifying. Terrible to do, done poorly in ways I still regret.
**Application:** Coming out of it, I didn't try to rebuild back. I restructured the company functionally. I cut the roadmap to about 10%. I started tracking revenue and free cash flow per employee. I co-hired every senior role. I raised the bar on hiring instead of backfilling on volume.
**Result:** Break-even to nearly $4 billion in free cash flow, with roughly half the people. More free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. The lesson isn't that layoffs are good — they're not, and I'd never have chosen that. The lesson is that the company worked better with fewer, more focused people, and we'd been carrying a communication tax that was hiding in plain sight.

**Situation:** A Series D founder has 400 people, is growing revenue 30% a year, and has an exec team that's projecting another 40% headcount growth to "support the plan."
**Application:** Put revenue per employee in front of them. If headcount grows 40% and revenue grows 30%, revenue per employee is going down. That's an efficiency regression disguised as growth. Ask which projects can be cut. Ask which roles can be merged. Require every senior hire to be co-hired by the CEO. Reset the default to "can we do this with fewer."
**Result:** The plan changes. Some projects get cut. Some hires get deferred. Revenue growth holds or accelerates because focus sharpens. Revenue per employee goes up. The exec team that complains loudest about the constraint is usually the one that was going to hire the most C players.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reward executives for growing their teams.
**Why:** You get what you incentivize. If "bigger team = bigger title = bigger comp," you've wired your company for headcount growth and against leverage. Reward the exec who grew revenue while holding the team flat. They're the one doing the real work.

**Don't:** Assume "we need to hire" when you're struggling.
**Why:** Most "we need to hire more" is really "we're unclear on what we're doing, we're doing too many things, or our top of the funnel isn't good enough." Adding people to an unclear problem produces a bigger unclear problem.

**Don't:** Use this frame when the real bottleneck is capacity on a proven loop.
**Why:** If the sales team has a proven motion and can't pick up the phone fast enough, hire. If the support team is buried, hire. The frame is about discipline on the default, not a blanket refusal to add people. Be honest about which situation you're in.

**Don't:** Treat this as a cost-cutting slogan.
**Why:** The point is not "cheap." The point is "few, great, focused." A twenty-person team of A players beats a hundred-person team of B and C players on almost every measure that matters. This is about quality density, not austerity.
