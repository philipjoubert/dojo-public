# Meetings & the Communication Tax

## When to Use
- Your calendar is 90% meetings and 10% work.
- You've been asked "should we have meeting-free Wednesdays?" and you suspect that's not the real fix.
- You're deciding whether recurring 1:1s are earning their cost.
- You've just done a headcount audit and want to understand why adding people slowed everything down.
- You're designing the operating cadence of a leadership team.

## Fails When
- You try to fix meetings without fixing headcount. Every person brings a communication tax. You cannot meeting-discipline your way out of having too many people.
- You kill 1:1s and don't replace them with group forums. Then information goes underground and people feel unheard.
- You run big meetings with lots of spectators and a few talkers. That's a spectator sport, not a meeting.
- You confuse "everyone in the room" with "everyone talking." If they're not contributing, the meeting is too big or the wrong people are there.

## Core Concept

The reason there are too many meetings in a company isn't because they don't have "no meeting Wednesdays." It's because they have too many people. People create meetings. And the best way to get rid of meetings is to not have so many people. There's no other better way to do that.

I know that sounds harsh. Every person brings a communication tax. That's the phrase I use. When you add a person, you're not just adding one line on the payroll — you're adding all the meetings that person has to be in, all the coordination they require, all the decisions that have to route through them. B players hire lots of C players — not just a few, a lot — because they can't capably do their own jobs. So you end up with three incapable people going in three different directions, creating all these meetings and administrative tax. An employee once told me: "I work 80 hours a week but I'm only doing 10 hours of work." That's a communication tax of 70 hours. They described it as working on a treadmill.

We ran this experiment against our will during COVID. We lost almost half our employees. And something remarkable happened. We started getting more work done with fewer people. Partly because there was a massive communication tax we'd been carrying without realizing it.

Now to the meetings themselves. A bad meeting is a meeting with too many people, a few participants, and a lot of spectators. If everyone's not talking, most meetings should have everyone contributing and talking — or the meeting should be smaller. Every meeting needs a leader. The meeting needs an agenda. It needs to be prepared ahead of time. You need to be clear who the decision maker is. You need a sense of urgency and action. And notes should be taken so there's a transparent record of what was decided.

And here's the controversial one. I don't believe in one-on-ones. And almost no great CEO in history has ever done them. The one-on-one model is flawed. It's a recurring one-hour meeting where the employee owns the agenda. What happens is they often don't talk about the things you want to talk about. You become their therapist. They bring you problems that other people should be in the room to hear. You make decisions in private that affect people who weren't consulted.

Jensen Huang says: "I don't do one-on-ones because I want everyone to be part of the solution and get the wisdom." When you make lots of one-on-one decisions, people feel like they didn't have a voice. They didn't have buy-in. The process feels opaque.

What we do instead: group meetings where everyone contributes. Recurring meetings with the group, not the individual. Everyone hears the same thing. Notes are taken. It's transparent — what was discussed, what was decided, who was in the room. Then there are private communications. Calls and texts. If I need something, I'll call somebody. But it's a six-minute call, not a half-hour one-on-one where we sit together. It's me reaching out when I need to, not a recurring obligation where neither of us is quite sure why we're meeting.

There are a few times employees should come to you one-on-one: if they're concerned about something, if they're going through a difficult time personally, if they want to confide something they don't feel safe sharing in a group. But that should be infrequent. If it's happening frequently, that is a very ominous sign for your organization.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your headcount before auditing your calendar.** If you try to fix meetings before fixing headcount, you'll fail. People create meetings. Start with whether you have too many people.

2. **Kill recurring 1:1s.** Replace them with group meetings where everyone contributes and everyone hears the same thing. If a report needs to confide something personal, they can ask for a conversation — but it shouldn't be the default.

3. **Make every meeting earn its seats.** As few people as possible. Everyone should be talking, not spectating. If someone consistently doesn't contribute, they shouldn't be in the room.

4. **Assign a leader and an agenda.** Every meeting needs one. Prepared ahead of time. Clear decision maker identified. Sense of urgency and action.

5. **Take notes.** Transparent record of what was decided, who was in the room, who had input. This is how you build shared consciousness — the top 30-50 people operating as one brain.

6. **Use short calls and texts for the rest.** A six-minute call beats a half-hour scheduled sync. If you need something, reach out when you need it — not on a recurring weekly grid.

7. **Run product reviews on a cadence.** Weekly, biweekly, monthly, quarterly, depending on the function. The cadence matters more than the length. Consistent, predictable review is what builds quality over time.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb pre-pandemic. Ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, meetings about meetings. People working 80 hours a week but only doing 10 hours of real work. The CEO is in meetings all day and can't see the product.
**Application:** Cut headcount roughly in half during COVID. Go functional, not divisional. Kill recurring 1:1s. Run group product reviews on a cadence. Make sure every meeting has few people, all contributing.
**Result:** We started getting more work done with fewer people. I'm in many more details and have way more free time than when I wasn't involved. Because in the old way, you still had meetings — but for things going wrong. Long meetings because no one knew the decision maker. Now decisions happen every week, sometimes every day, sometimes every hour.

**Situation:** A VP at a scale-up spends 40+ hours a week in 1:1s with her 14 reports and her peers. She has no time to review work. The reports say they feel unheard in group meetings and rely on the 1:1s as the only place they're actually listened to.
**Application:** Flip it. Replace the recurring 1:1s with structured group meetings where every report contributes on the agenda. Hold office hours once a week for private concerns. Use texts and calls for the quick stuff.
**Result:** The VP gets her afternoons back to review work. The reports discover that the group meetings give them more signal, not less, because they're hearing each other now — the wisdom is shared. Decisions that used to happen in private and surprise people now happen in the open with buy-in.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Institute "no-meeting Wednesdays" and think you've solved it.
**Why:** You haven't addressed the root cause. The meetings will just compress into the other four days. The problem was never the calendar. It was the headcount and the structure. Every person brings a communication tax.

**Don't:** Keep 1:1s because the employee prefers them.
**Why:** What the employee actually wants is to be heard and to understand what's going on. Group meetings deliver that better — the whole team gets the wisdom, decisions feel transparent, no one feels blindsided. The 1:1 was a workaround for a broken group meeting culture, not a solution.

**Don't:** Run big meetings with spectators.
**Why:** A meeting with twelve people and three speakers is a theatre production. It wastes nine people's time, produces worse decisions than a smaller group would, and trains the silent nine to stay silent forever. If they're not talking, shrink the room.
