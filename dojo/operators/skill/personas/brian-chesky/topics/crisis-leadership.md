# Crisis Leadership

## When to Use
- The business is facing an existential threat — the kind that makes the team ask, "What do we do?"
- You've been wanting to restructure the company for years but every attempt has met resistance.
- You're getting pressured to make a consensus decision that feels like the middle of the road — and your gut says the middle is wrong.
- You need to decide quickly what is essential and what isn't, with incomplete information.
- The crisis is unfolding and everyone around you is telling you to be cautious and quiet.

## Fails When
- Used in normal weather. A quarterly miss is not a defining moment. If you invoke crisis for every bad week, the word loses meaning and the team stops responding.
- The founder doesn't already have a clear vision of what they'd do differently. A crisis gives you permission, not a plan. If you don't have the plan, the crisis just becomes chaos.
- Consensus is reached by compromise under pressure. In a crisis, the middle-of-the-road decision is usually the worst one. You have to go left or right.
- The leader uses crisis as an excuse to be cruel or reckless rather than clear. Crisis mode is about clarity, not brutality.

## Core Concept

Andy Grove from Intel once said: bad companies are destroyed by a crisis. Good companies survive a crisis. But great companies are defined by a crisis. I said — this is going to be our defining moment.

It was late January 2020. I'm in a meeting and someone says our China business has dropped 80% because of this thing called the coronavirus. I'm like — what is the coronavirus? Eight weeks later, we lost 80% of our business globally. That's like an 18-wheeler going a hundred miles an hour and slamming on the brakes. Nothing good happens.

Ken Chenault was on our board. He had been CEO of American Express during 9/11 and 2008. He'd once told me: "Sometime at some point you're going to have your 9/11. You're going to deal with your 2008. It's going to happen on your watch. Something in the world is going to happen and it will be your defining moment as a leader." On March 15th, Ken calls me. He says: "Remember that conversation about a 9/11? This pandemic is ten of them."

Ten of them. And he was right.

Here's the question I asked myself. If Airbnb is like a burning house and I can only take half the things out of the house, what do I take with me? It suddenly was really clarifying. That question strips away everything that doesn't matter. All the politics. All the divisions. All the sub-teams with their sub-roadmaps. All the bureaucracy and meetings about meetings. None of that survives the fire. You grab what's essential and you run. I didn't know if we could save the company. But I knew how I wanted to be remembered if we couldn't. And I knew what mattered.

Now here's the thing I want founders to hear. The pandemic gave me permission. For ten years, I'd been apologizing for how I wanted to run the company. I had this whole vision — functional organization, design at the center, CEO in the details, small and lean, Navy SEALs not the Navy — I just didn't have permission to do it. When things were good, people said: "Let me do my thing." But suddenly, when we lost 80% of the business, everyone looked at me and said: "What do we do?" And I said — oh. I guess I have permission to operate in the way I've always wanted to. I made what felt like five years of decisions in three months.

And one more rule, drawn hard from experience. Don't make decisions by consensus in a crisis. A consensus decision in a moment of crisis is very often going to be the middle of the road, and they're usually the worst decisions. Usually in a crisis, you have to go left or right. When EJ's apartment was ransacked years earlier, my advisers told me to be cautious and quiet. It made things worse. People were saying, "We need to discuss this, we need to do testing," and I said, "No, we're doing this." Being cautious and quiet was exactly what was making things worse. At one point I finally realized I needed to stop listening to those advisers.

A crisis is a terrible opportunity to waste. But man, I wouldn't have asked for that one. My hope is founders don't need a once-in-a-generation pandemic to get permission to run their company the way they want. That's the whole point of telling this story.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask the burning house question.** If the company is a burning house and you can only take half the things with you, what do you take? This question cuts through every political consideration, every sub-roadmap, every legacy project. It reveals what's actually essential.

2. **Name the defining moment out loud.** Tell the leadership team: this is Andy Grove's test. Bad companies are destroyed by a crisis. Good companies survive. Great companies are defined. We are going to be the third one. Now decide what that means.

3. **Take the permission the crisis gives you.** Suddenly everyone is asking "what do we do?" That's the window. Use it to make five years of decisions in three months. The structural changes that would have faced huge resistance in peacetime are now possible — but only in this window.

4. **Go left or right. Don't go middle.** Avoid consensus compromise. A consensus decision in a moment of crisis is very often the worst decision. You have to pick a direction, commit, and own the outcome.

5. **Manage for values, not for outcome.** A business decision tries to game the outcome to win. A principal decision is: I don't know how it's going to end — so how do I want to be remembered irrespective of the outcome? In a crisis, principal decisions compound over time. Business decisions corrode.

6. **Stop listening to cautious-and-quiet advisers.** When the house is burning, the people telling you to slow down and test are the people who don't have to live with the aftermath. Listen to operators who've been through defining moments before. Ken Chenault. Not junior partners on your board.

7. **Don't go back when it's over.** After the crisis passes, some people will say — not in these words — "Okay, when do we go back to the way things were?" The answer is: we don't. We never go back. The whole point of the crisis was that it gave you permission to change. Don't surrender the permission when the weather clears.

## Examples

**Situation:** March 2020. Airbnb loses 80% of its business in eight weeks. Journalists are writing "Is this the end of Airbnb?" Travel is frozen. Employees are terrified.
**Application:** Burning house question — what do I keep? Quote Andy Grove to the leadership team: this is going to be our defining moment. Take the permission. Go functional in org structure. Cut the roadmap to 10%. Become the chief editor. Review all the work. Go public with a letter to the team that owns the layoffs with dignity. Manage for values, not for the stock price.
**Result:** We went from "going out of business" to an IPO at $100 billion+ market cap. From break-even to nearly $4 billion in free cash flow. More free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. And I finally got to run the company the way I always wanted to.

**Situation:** EJ's apartment is ransacked by a guest, years before COVID. Advisers tell me to be careful, to be quiet, to run it through testing. The narrative is running away from us.
**Application:** Stop listening to cautious-and-quiet advisers. Make a principal decision — how do I want to be remembered regardless of the outcome? Write the response I can live with in twenty years. Go public. Own it.
**Result:** We got through it. And I learned that being cautious and quiet is often what makes things worse. The middle of the road decision was the wrong decision.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to reach consensus under existential pressure.
**Why:** Consensus in a crisis is almost always the middle of the road, and the middle of the road is usually wrong. Crises need clear direction — left or right. A room of smart people trying to avoid upsetting each other under pressure will produce the decision nobody hates, and that's rarely the decision the moment requires.

**Don't:** Waste the permission the crisis gives you.
**Why:** The window where everyone looks to the CEO and asks "what do we do?" closes fast. If you spend it running the company the same way you did before, you've missed the one chance you get to restructure without a civil war. A crisis is a terrible opportunity to waste.

**Don't:** Manufacture fake crises to justify changes you want.
**Why:** People can tell. Your team will smell the manufactured urgency, and the next real crisis — when it comes — they'll react more slowly because you've devalued the alarm. Reserve crisis mode for actual existential threats. Everything else has to be justified on its own merits.
