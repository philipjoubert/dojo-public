# The Roadmap Review (Top 80–100)

## When to Use
- You're rebuilding how strategy actually gets made at your company — not the deck, the operating system underneath it.
- You're about to kick off another annual plan and you can feel it's going to consume a quarter of the year and change nothing.
- You have too many projects and too many teams and you want one forum where "what we're doing" gets edited.
- You want the top of the company to operate as one brain, not ten departments passing memos.

## Fails When
- The company is still divisionalized. Sub-GMs will have sub-roadmaps that shadow the real one, and the Top 100 becomes political theatre.
- The CEO outsources the invitation list. The value is the CEO personally picking who's in the room. If HR runs it by title, you've lost it.
- You don't actually cut the roadmap. We cut ours to 10% of what it was. If everyone leaves the meeting still working on what they were working on, it wasn't a roadmap review.
- Budget and strategy aren't decoupled. The minute you try to do them together, the budget wins and strategy becomes a spreadsheet fight.

## Core Concept

For most of Airbnb's middle years we ran an annual planning exercise. It took a quarter of the year. It produced a document nobody used. It fed a budget that drove headcount growth. It gave the illusion of strategy while the company slowly fragmented into sub-teams running sub-roadmaps.

When I went to Jony and Hiroki to learn how Steve ran Apple, one of the first things they described was the Top 100 — twice a year, the CEO personally picks the hundred people who matter most to the product right now, and those hundred people review the rolling strategic plan together. That's the forum. That's where the roadmap lives.

So we stole it. At Airbnb we call it a Top 80 to 100. Twice a year. I personally pick every person who's in the room. It's not based on title. Some of the top hundred are junior. Some executives don't make the cut. At most companies there'd be protest — "these are just his favorites." I think a CEO should have favorites, on fair criteria. If you can't say "this is a high performer and this is what excellence looks like," you're going to be in big trouble.

We run a rolling two-year strategy and we update it every six months at the Top 100. Budget is decoupled. Budget takes a week — it's a financial exercise. Strategy is a product and narrative exercise. If you blend them, budget wins every time. We're one of the only S&P 500 companies without an annual plan, and the company has never been sharper strategically.

The roadmap itself is one roadmap for the whole company. Not one per function, not one per team. One. When I first took the company back, I asked every leader to show me the roadmap. They couldn't find one, because it existed as a hundred fragments across a hundred sub-teams. We pulled it all together, put it on a wall, and cut it to about ten percent. Best people on the remaining few projects.

Between Top 100s, the CEO — me — reviews the work on a cadence. Weekly, biweekly, monthly, quarterly, depending on the area. If it's not on the roadmap, it can't ship. If I don't review it, it can't ship. People said that would be a bottleneck. It turns out it massively sped up decisions — because decisions don't swirl when the decision-maker is actually in the room.

## How to Apply

1. **Kill the annual plan.** It's the wrong artifact. Replace it with a rolling two-year strategic plan that gets updated every six months.
2. **Decouple budget from strategy.** Budget is a one-week exercise. Strategy is a different exercise. Don't let the budget process eat the strategy process.
3. **Set the cadence: twice a year, Top 80 to 100.** Not annual. Not quarterly. Twice a year is enough to update and not so often the company is always planning instead of shipping.
4. **CEO personally picks the attendees.** Not by title. Not by HR. By who is actually doing the work that matters right now. Have favorites. Use fair criteria.
5. **One roadmap for the whole company.** Put every project on it. Cut aggressively — ours went to 10%. If it's not on the roadmap, it can't ship.
6. **Between Top 100s, review on a cadence.** Weekly for the fast-moving things. Biweekly, monthly, quarterly for others. Consistent. Predictable. The cadence is what builds quality.
7. **Pair it with coordinated launches.** The roadmap feeds two big release moments per year. The Top 100 is where you decide what goes in those launches.
8. **Take notes. Make them transparent.** The top hundred operate as a shared consciousness. Everyone hears the same thing. Everyone knows what was decided.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb, 2020. Pandemic hits. I have permission to rebuild. I ask every leader to show me their roadmap. They can't find it because it exists as sub-roadmaps on sub-teams on sub-sub-teams. The company has been working incredibly hard and shipping almost nothing that changes the product.
**Application:** Pull every project onto one wall. Cut to roughly ten percent. Set up the Top 100 twice a year — I personally pick each attendee. Kill the annual plan. Decouple budget from strategy. Review the work on a weekly cadence. Pair everything with two coordinated launches a year, May and November.
**Result:** The app started changing again. Decisions that used to take months happen weekly. We went from break-even to nearly $4 billion in free cash flow — with roughly half the people we had before.

**Situation:** A Series C founder runs an annual plan every Q4 that eats three months of leadership time. By Q2 the next year, reality has diverged from the plan, and everyone is privately ignoring the document. The company ships continuously with no coordination.
**Application:** Kill the annual. Switch to rolling two-year, updated every six months. Start a twice-yearly Top 80 where the founder personally picks attendees. Decouple the budget — spend a week on it separately. Cut the roadmap to what the company can actually focus on. Pair it with two launches a year.
**Result:** The company gets three months back. Strategy stops being a deck and becomes an operating reality. Because the forum is real and the cuts are real, people start disagreeing and deciding inside the room instead of freelancing after it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run a Top 100 that's just a presentation to the top hundred.
**Why:** If it's read-only, you've turned the most important forum in the company into a broadcast. The value is that the CEO uses it to edit — to cut, to redirect, to decide. If nobody's roadmap changes because of the meeting, the meeting is theatre.

**Don't:** Pick attendees by title.
**Why:** Titles are lagging indicators. Some of your best work this year is being done by people nobody would invite by title. Some titled executives aren't actually driving anything. The CEO should personally pick every seat. Have favorites. Defend them with criteria.

**Don't:** Let the annual budget process drive strategy.
**Why:** Budget is a financial constraint. Strategy is a product bet. Fuse them and the CFO wins — not because they're wrong, but because their exercise has numbers and the product exercise has judgment, and numbers always eat judgment in a joint meeting. Separate them.

**Don't:** Let the roadmap grow back.
**Why:** Organizations have antibodies. Every team wants their project back on the list. If you don't hold the line every six months, by year two the roadmap is back to where it was before you cut it. The cut is a practice, not an event.
