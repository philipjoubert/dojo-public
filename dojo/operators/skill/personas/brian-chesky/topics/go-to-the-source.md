# Go to the Source

## When to Use
- You're trying to learn a new domain fast and don't know where to start.
- You've been collecting advice from ten mentors and you feel more confused than when you started.
- You're the CEO and you realize you're making decisions from briefings instead of from direct contact with the thing.
- You're not sure whose opinion actually matters on a given question.

## Fails When
- You use it to bypass your team rather than to learn. Skip-leveling is a practice of presence, not a weapon for politics.
- You pick the famous person instead of the right person. "Famous expert on X" and "person who actually knows the most about X" are often different people.
- You hero-worship the source and stop using your own judgment. Sources inform; they don't decide.
- The person isn't actually reachable. This isn't a fantasy of emailing every billionaire. It's a practice of seeking out structural access to real experts within reach.

## Core Concept

There's a habit most people have when they want to learn something. They go talk to ten people and try to average the advice. The problem is that average advice is almost always mediocre advice. You get a blurry middle. You get the conventional wisdom because conventional wisdom is the signal that ten different people all inherited from the same source.

The habit I try to practice instead: spend half your time figuring out who the definitive source is on the question you're asking — the one person who can tell you more about that one thing than anyone else — and then go only to that person. If you pick the right source, you can fast-forward months or years.

I've done this my whole career. Paul Graham on how to start a company. Jony Ive on design. Hiroki Asai on creative direction and how Steve Jobs actually ran Apple. Jeff Weiner and Bob Iger on management. Mark Zuckerberg on product. Sheryl Sandberg on international expansion. John Donahoe on scaling operations and managing a board. I didn't survey each of those domains. I went to one person and listened.

The other half of "go to the source" is the one that matters even more once you're a CEO. It's going directly to the primary source inside your own company. The user. The host. The person who actually wrote the code. The designer who shaped the screen. The customer-service agent who took the call last night. Don't take the briefing. Don't read the summary. Go direct.

This is distinct from founder mode as a general posture. Founder mode is about being in the details in general. Going to the source is the specific move: bypassing three layers of summary and talking to the person who actually has the primary information. I've called hosts directly. I've spent hours watching Ricardo use the product. I've gone out with customer-service teams. I skip-level constantly — not to audit the manager, but because the skip-levels have information the layers in between have already smoothed off.

The reason most CEOs don't do this is social. It feels rude. It feels like you're undermining. And executives will tell you so. But the alternative is that every piece of information reaches you in a shape that someone else has decided you should hear. You become a CEO who makes decisions based on summaries of summaries. By the time something reaches you, it's wrong.

Go direct. Then decide.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask: who is the single best source on this?** Not "who are the top five." Who is THE person. Spend real time on this question — it's worth more than the conversation itself.
2. **Use your network to find them.** The source rarely shows up on a Google search. Ask three people you trust who the real expert is, and cross-reference. The right source is usually named by multiple trusted people.
3. **When you find them, go deep, not wide.** One hour with the actual expert beats ten hours with ten generalists. Prepare. Bring your real question. Don't small-talk.
4. **Apply the same move inside your own company.** Don't take briefings. Go to the designer who shaped the screen. Go to the engineer who wrote the code. Go to the support agent who took the call.
5. **Skip-level regularly — not annually.** Walk the halls. Text people directly. Join the standup. The top 30 to 50 people should feel like your own directs, not someone else's.
6. **Go to the user, always.** Your users are the most definitive source on your own product. Watch them use it. Travel with them. Knock on their door.
7. **Resist the "average of ten" trap.** When you hear yourself saying "well, some people say X and others say Y, so I'll do something in between," stop. Find the one person who has actually done it, and go to them.
8. **Make the source loop visible.** Tell your team you're doing this. "I'm going to call five hosts this week." That normalizes it. It stops being "going around the team" and starts being "how we learn."

## Examples

**Situation:** 2019. I knew the way we were running Airbnb wasn't working. Divisionalized. Too many layers. Product slowing. I didn't want a management consultant's synthesis of "how great companies operate." I wanted to know how Steve Jobs actually ran Apple — the operating version, not the hagiography.
**Application:** I went to Jony Ive. I went to Hiroki Asai. I didn't read books about Apple. I went to the people who spent twenty years sitting next to Steve. I asked specific operating questions. How were meetings run? Who reported to whom? How did Steve review work? How did functional managers actually manage?
**Result:** The conversations I had with Jony and Hiroki are the skeleton of how I run Airbnb today. Three-legged stool. Design reports to the CEO. CEO reviews all the work. No recurring 1:1s. Product marketing merges PM and outbound. I didn't invent any of it. I went to the source and listened. It saved me years.

**Situation:** Early Airbnb. Paul Graham asked where our users were. New York, we said. He asked what we were still doing in Mountain View. We said it wouldn't scale to meet them. He said: this is exactly the time it will never scale again. Go now.
**Application:** Joe and I flew to New York every week for three months. Knocked on every host's door. Sat in their living rooms. Watched them use the product. One host gave us dozens of pages of notes that became our roadmap.
**Result:** The roadmap often exists in the minds of the users you're designing for. The thing we shipped after those three months was fundamentally different from what we'd planned from Mountain View. Every major product improvement of that era came from those direct host conversations. Not from surveys. Not from summaries. From sitting in the living room.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Survey ten advisors and average the answers.
**Why:** Average advice is blurry. You'll end up with the common-denominator opinion that nobody is uniquely equipped to give. Conventional wisdom is what you get when you listen to everyone. Go to the one person who can actually tell you the thing.

**Don't:** Pick the most famous expert instead of the real one.
**Why:** Fame and expertise aren't the same. The actual expert is often one layer behind the famous person — the head of design, not the CEO who did the keynote; the engineer who wrote the code, not the VP who presented it. Find who really knows. That's the source.

**Don't:** Use skip-leveling as a political weapon.
**Why:** The move is about learning, not about undermining. If executives feel you're going around them to gather ammunition, they'll stop giving you real information. Skip-level to be informed, not to be accusatory. And tell your execs you're doing it.

**Don't:** Outsource product understanding to a research team.
**Why:** Research teams are great, and you should have them. But they don't replace sitting in a user's living room yourself. A summary of ten interviews is not the same as being in one of them. Go watch the product being used by someone you don't know. Do it regularly.
