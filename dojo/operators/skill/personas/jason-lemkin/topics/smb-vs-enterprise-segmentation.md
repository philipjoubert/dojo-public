# SMB vs Enterprise Segmentation

## When to Use
- You're picking a GTM motion and deciding between SMB velocity and enterprise ACV.
- You're at $5M–$10M ARR, SMB is working, and wondering whether to build an enterprise edition.
- Your $5k-ACV business just landed a one-off $200k deal — should you chase more?
- You hired an SMB rep for $150k deals, or vice versa, and it's not working.
- Your SMB economics aren't penciling and you're trying to tell if it's a sales or pricing problem.
- You're at $20M–$30M ARR with a blended team and wondering whether to split SMB and enterprise.
- Your NRR is stuck at 90% and you know enterprise shops do 120%+.

## Fails When
- You're pre-$1M ARR. Sell to whoever says yes. Segment later.
- You're running pure PLG/freemium with zero humans in the loop — no segmentation yet.
- You don't know your ACV distribution, ARR, lead mix, or churn by segment. Those four numbers tell you what motion you're really running.
- You're consumer or B2C. The math is B2B.
- You're using "go upmarket" to avoid fixing SMB churn. That hides the problem under bigger logos for 18 months, then the bill comes due.

## Core Concept

Look, SMB sales and enterprise sales have nothing in common. Literally nothing. The cadence, the skills, the comp, the contract length, the objections, the buyer, the lead mix, the close rate, the NRR — all different. And yet founders keep acting like they're the same job with a different-sized check at the end. They aren't. That's why the SMB rep you hired flails on $150k deals, and why the enterprise rep hates life answering 50 SMB leads a day. The reps aren't bad. You put them in the wrong motion.

SMB SaaS companies have to be at least twice as efficient as enterprise ones at almost every sales and marketing motion. Why? Enterprise coasts on 120%–140% NRR. A $100k deal that grows 30% next year is $130k on one logo without closing anything new. SMB does not do that. SMB churns 2–3% a month at the low end and expansion is small. To get past 100% NRR in SMB you either add a second product, go at least partially upmarket, or both. That's why every "SMB company" you know — Zoom, Shopify, HubSpot, Zendesk — eventually sold bigger deals. They didn't abandon SMB. They added on top.

The ACV floors are math, not opinion. Below ~$1k ACV / $99 a month you cannot afford a human sales team in the U.S. or Western Europe — self-serve plus AI chat at best. $1k–$3k is the boundary zone. $3k–$25k is classic inside sales — 1–2 touches to close, 8–15 deals/month/rep, $80k–$100k OTE, 2–3 demos a day. $25k–$100k is hybrid. $100k+ is field sales — multi-stakeholder, security, procurement, legal, months-long cycles, senior reps at $250k+ OTE. Move a rep more than one bracket and watch them flail. Not attitude. Muscle memory.

The "move upmarket" conversation usually shows up at $5M–$10M ARR, and the answer is almost always yes — at least partially. Even if 80% of revenue stays SMB, having 20% from deals above $25k–$100k ACV stabilizes NRR and funds marketing. Zoom's mix at $425M ARR was mostly SMB, but 30% was deals above $100k. That 30% made the math work.

## How to Apply

1. **Map your real ACV distribution.** Pull the data. How many deals under $1k? $1k–$5k? $5k–$25k? $25k–$100k? $100k+? Averages lie. You probably have three distinct segments and one motion running across all of them.
2. **Pick your primary motion where 60%+ of revenue sits today.** If most is $3k–$10k ACV, you're SMB inside sales — stop pretending you're enterprise. If most is above $100k, stop running velocity playbooks. A 50/50 split means you already have a segmentation problem.
3. **Apply the ACV-to-motion floor.** Under $1k: self-serve + AI chat. $1k–$3k: AI SDR + thin inside sales if ACV expands. $3k–$25k: classic SMB inside sales, 8–15 deals/month/rep, $80k–$100k OTE. $25k–$100k: hybrid. $100k+: field sales with the full enterprise stack.
4. **Do the $3k check.** Reps keep ~25% of what they close in comp. A $100k-OTE rep at $3k ACV needs ~133 deals a year — 10–15/month, the ceiling. Below $3k ACV with U.S. human reps the math breaks.
5. **"Too soon" for enterprise = you can't deliver 90 days after close.** Enterprise buyers give slack to emerging vendors and sign on features you haven't shipped — but only if you ship them. Don't chase enterprise if deployment can't make good.
6. **Budget the enterprise cost stack.** SOC2. Security reviews. Legal redlines. SLAs (when they ask, give it). Procurement. Deployment services. A dedicated CSM. If you can't afford that, your ACV isn't high enough yet.
7. **Hire reps within one ACV bracket.** $5k-ACV reps don't close $200k deals. $200k-deal reps don't run SMB inbound. One bracket up or down is teachable. Two is almost never.
8. **Never put enterprise reps on SMB, or vice versa.** Not because they're lazy — cadence and comp don't work. Enterprise reps won't do 50 calls a day. SMB reps can't navigate a 6-month security review.
9. **Split SMB and enterprise into dedicated teams at $20M–$30M ARR.** Below that, a blended team runs both if the spread isn't wide. Above that you need two VPs (or VP + Director), two comp plans, two lead routing rules, two CS functions. The day a blended rep says "I don't have time for $500 MRR deals" — split.
10. **Go upmarket by adding on top, not abandoning SMB.** Build an enterprise edition at 5x–10x SMB ACV. One customer who wants it means ten do. Let SMB hum at $3k–$5k ACV while a small enterprise team lands $50k–$200k deals. That mix takes $10M to $100M.
11. **Price enterprise as a different SKU, not a discount off SMB.** Enterprise buyers want the enterprise edition — SSO, audit logs, SLA, CSM, premium support. Let them pay. Notice: the SMB vendor charging $50k to its biggest SMBs is often pricier than the enterprise vendor charging $15k to its smallest enterprises. Know where you sit.
12. **Fix SMB NRR before going upmarket — or go upmarket deliberately for the lift.** If SMB NRR is 85% and no second product: fix retention or add an enterprise tier to prop up blended NRR. Going upmarket purely to mask 70% SMB gross retention is a trap.
13. **When enterprise lands unexpectedly, don't chase. Build.** One $200k deal in a $5k-ACV shop is a data point, not a strategy. Don't hire two enterprise reps on one deal. Land it, service it, wait for a second. Two means a segment. One is a freak.
14. **Forecast SMB and enterprise separately.** SMB: 2–3 touches, 3 weeks. Enterprise: 6–9 months, 8–15 stakeholders, security review, pilot or RFP. If your model treats them the same you'll miss the number twice.

## Signature Numbers

- **$3k ACV** — the floor for a U.S. / Western European human inside sales team.
- **$5k ACV** — where traditional SMB sales really starts working without heroics.
- **$25k ACV** — where field-assist and deal sophistication start to make sense.
- **$100k ACV** — the threshold for true field sales and the full enterprise cost stack.
- **2–3 touches to close** — SMB. Enterprise is 15+ touches over 6–9 months.
- **8–15 deals / rep / month** — SMB inside sales ceiling at $3k–$10k ACV.
- **50 calls a day** — SMB AEs at the low end. Nothing like enterprise.
- **120%–140% NRR** enterprise. **85%–100% NRR** SMB without a second product or upmarket tilt.
- **3% per month** — churn at the low end of SMB / prosumer. The gravity you're fighting.
- **5x–10x ACV** — where an "enterprise edition" needs to be priced vs. your SMB core to shift the mix.
- **2x efficiency requirement** — SMB has to be 2x more sales-and-marketing efficient than enterprise.
- **$20M–$30M ARR** — where a blended team usually needs to split.
- **30% from $100k+ deals** — Zoom's mix at $425M ARR. Most SMB, enough enterprise to make the math work.
- **90 days** — the window to deliver on enterprise commitments after close.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $6M ARR, $5k ACV, SMB inside sales, NRR 92%. One enterprise customer just signed a $180k deal via inbound referral. Founder wants to hire two enterprise reps and "go upmarket."
**Application:** Don't blow up the GTM on one data point. One enterprise deal is a freak event, not a segment. Service this customer beautifully — over-invest in deployment, assign a human CSM — a great reference is worth more than two mediocre enterprise reps. Build an enterprise edition at 5x SMB ACV as a SKU. See if a second deal lands. Two deals in 6 months, then hire one enterprise AE. Two deals first, then the team.
**Result:** Founder holds. Six months later a second $120k deal lands, then a third. They hire one enterprise AE. By $15M ARR enterprise is 25% of revenue and blended NRR climbs to 108%.

**Situation:** Founder at $3M ARR, $2k ACV, 10 inside sales reps at $90k OTE. CAC payback 22 months, gross margin 58%. Board asking why reps don't close more.
**Application:** The reps aren't the problem. The math is. At $2k ACV, a $90k-OTE rep needs ~180 deals a year — 15/month every month, almost nobody does that. Two paths. Path A: raise prices 2x to ~$4k ACV. Conversion drops a quarter but the ones who pay fund the team. Path B: cut humans to 2–3, move the rest to self-serve + AI SDR + AI chat, keep humans on the biggest SMBs and expansion signals. Either works. Both is better.
**Result:** Founder picks Path A plus a lighter Path B. Raises to $3.5k average ACV, cuts team to 6, layers in AI SDRs. Twelve months later ARR is $4.5M with a smaller team and positive unit economics.

**Situation:** Founder at $12M ARR, a few $150k enterprise deals landed, hired a VP of Sales from a $300M ARR enterprise SaaS. Six months later SMB new bookings are down 40%, the VP hired three senior enterprise AEs at $250k OTE, and two deals are "in procurement." SMB motion is starving.
**Application:** Wrong VP for your actual company. He knows enterprise — great for the 15% above $100k — but he's running the whole team like an enterprise shop. The 85% that's SMB is dying of neglect. Split the team. Keep the VP over 3–4 enterprise AEs, hire a Director of SMB to restore the velocity motion and comp plan that worked at $10M. If he can't run a segmented motion, move on. Worst option: let him keep flattening SMB while betting on two enterprise deals that may not close.
**Result:** Founder splits. Promotes an internal SMB Director. VP stays over enterprise. SMB recovers in two quarters, enterprise grows to 25% within a year.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Put an SMB rep on enterprise deals because "they're a great closer."**
*Why:* A great $5k-ACV closer runs 2–3 demos a day, closes in 2 touches, moves on. That same person on a $200k deal tries to close in week 2, freaks when it doesn't, drops it. Enterprise cycles are 6–9 months, 15 stakeholders, security, procurement. Muscle memory doesn't cross the bracket.

**Don't: Put an enterprise AE on SMB.**
*Why:* Bored by week three, insulted by week five, gone by month three. A $250k-OTE rep used to 3–5 deals a year at $200k each will not do 50 calls a day at $5k ACV. And while they're there, they quietly "enterprise-ify" every $5k deal into a 3-month process.

**Don't: Build a human sales team below $3k ACV in the U.S. without AI or offshore.**
*Why:* A $90k-OTE rep closing 10–15 deals/month at $2k ACV produces $240k–$360k in bookings — after comp, marketing, product, and hosting, underwater. AI SDRs, offshore reps (LatAm, Eastern Europe), or self-serve can push the floor lower. A standard U.S. playbook below $3k ACV has never worked at scale.

**Don't: Use "go upmarket" to avoid fixing SMB retention.**
*Why:* If SMB gross retention is 70%, going upmarket doesn't fix it — enterprise NRR masks the number for 18 months, then SMB churn catches up, enterprise stalls because your product isn't enterprise-ready. Fix onboarding, add a second product, go vertical. Going upmarket to hide SMB churn is a feel-good with a permanent bill.

**Don't: Treat one enterprise deal as validation of a new segment.**
*Why:* One deal is a fluke. Could be a buyer with budget to burn, a champion from a past company, a use case that won't repeat. Don't hire two enterprise AEs, don't build an enterprise funnel, don't tell the board it's the new strategy. Service it, wait for a second. Two is the minimum.

**Don't: Promise enterprise features you can't ship within 90 days of close.**
*Why:* Enterprise buyers sign on future commitments — fine — but only if you hit the window. Miss it and they won't churn in month 12, they'll quietly never deploy, never be a reference, never expand. A signed enterprise deal that never deploys is worse than no deal.

**Don't: Assume one VP of Sales can run both motions past $20M ARR.**
*Why:* Most can run one well. Very few run both. An SMB VP who built a velocity machine rarely navigates a $500k RFP. An enterprise VP who closed $1M+ deals rarely knows how to comp a 50-call-a-day AE. When you split teams, split leadership too.
