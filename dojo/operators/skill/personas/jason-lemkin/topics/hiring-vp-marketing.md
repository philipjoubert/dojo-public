# Hiring a VP of Marketing

## When to Use
- You're around $20K MRR / $250K ARR, you have your first 10 paying customers, and you're trying to decide if it's too early for a VP Marketing. It isn't.
- You have 1–2 sales reps working and the founder is still running every piece of lead flow, webinar, and piece of collateral personally.
- You're interviewing VP/Head of Marketing candidates and trying to tell the real demand-gen operators from the Blue Pen people.
- You hired a VPM 30–90 days ago and you're trying to figure out if it's working.
- Your VP of Sales keeps dragging marketing into their org, and you're weighing whether to just let marketing report to sales.
- You're about to offer a VP Marketing a base-heavy comp plan with no lead commit attached.

## Fails When
- You're pre-product, pre-revenue, with no sales reps and no lead flow. There's nothing for a VPM to multiply. Hire them when you have at least 10 paying customers and 1–2 reps.
- You hire a "marketing manager" instead of a real VP/Director, because you got scared on budget. A Marketing Manager can't own a lead commit. And if they can't own a lead commit, they aren't accretive.
- You expect one VPM to do all ten things in the marketing playbook well. Every great VPM does the top 3–4 deeply. They hack the rest. The founder fills the rest of the gap, period.
- You hire a Corporate Marketing or Product Marketing person from a BigCo and expect them to build a demand engine. They can't. It's hopeless, in fact. Demand Gen folks can hack Corporate and Product Marketing. It never works the other way.
- You let the VPM off the hook for a lead commit — "she owns brand" or "she owns positioning." No lead commit, no variable comp, no real number. You just hired $300K of blue pens.

## Core Concept

Look, the single starkest difference I see between first-time founders and second-time founders is when they hire their VP of Marketing. Second-timers *know*. They hire the VPM before they hire the first sales rep in some cases. First-timers wait until they're at $3M ARR with a chaotic lead funnel and eight reps they can't feed. I hired my VP Marketing at Adobe Sign / EchoSign at $20K MRR. It wasn't a week too early. You almost can't hire a VP Marketing too early, but it's very, very easy to hire one too late.

Here's the mental model. A great VPM is accretive the day they start — if you have even 10 customers and any lead flow at all. She'll manage the leads you already have better through the funnel. That alone can be a 20% revenue bump just on pipeline hygiene. She'll increase your revenue per lead. She'll help you hire better reps even before you have a VP Sales, because she's seen a good sales team built before. And she'll get you more leads than you're getting today, even with a tiny budget. On a $5K–$10K+ ACV, she pays for herself almost immediately. If you're sitting at 10+ customers and telling yourself you can't afford one, the math is upside down — you can't afford *not* to hire one.

Now the part most founders get wrong. There is no VPM who does all 10 things in the marketing playbook well. Every great one I've ever worked with does the top 3–4 things deeply and with passion, and hacks or ignores the rest. My VPM at EchoSign crushed webinars, lead scoring, lead routing, sales team support, SEM, testimonials, and funnel metrics. She didn't do much field marketing, steak dinners, BDRs, reviews, SEO, PR, or product marketing. 9 times out of 10 that's the pattern. So when you interview, ask them to list everything they want to do. Then realistically assume they'll do the top 3–4. If those top 3–4 are what *you* need right now — hire. If not, keep looking. It's not about the candidate being great or not, it's about the match to your top gap.

There are three archetypes, and hiring the wrong one is how startups end up with a bunch of blue pens with their logo on them. **Corporate Marketing** is about protecting a brand at scale — billboards, TV, reinforcing awareness in a buyer who already knows you. **Product Marketing** is about positioning — where you sit in a market that already knows you exist. **Demand Generation** is about leads, funnel, scoring, routing, conversion, CAC, and revenue per lead. At a SaaS startup from $1M to $20M ARR, demand gen is king. Brand marketing makes almost no sense at $1M ARR, starts making sense at $10M, becomes most of your budget at $100M, and becomes everything at $1B. Until you're post-$20M ARR, hire Demand Gen. Full stop. BigCos are full of smart Corporate and Product marketers. Almost none of them can get you leads. It's not their job and never was. Demand gen folks can hack corporate and product marketing. Corporate and product marketers cannot hack demand gen. Ever.

And one more non-negotiable: marketing does NOT report to sales. I know it looks efficient on paper. Your CRO is your strongest leader, sales is marketing's customer, alignment becomes "automatic." In practice it almost never works, unless you have unlimited capital. Three reasons. Budget — sales doesn't care about CAC, sales cares about hitting the number this quarter, so spend explodes. Patience — sales is quarter-to-quarter, and anything long-term (brand, events, content, SEO) gets gutted. And the CEO *is* the #1 marketer at almost every tech company — look at Benioff, look at Zuck. You can offload the function, but you can't offload ownership. If you hand marketing to sales because you don't want to own it, you're telling the company what you actually think marketing is worth. The VPM and the VPS should be the Mom and Dad of Revenue — two peers, joined at the hip, both reporting to the CEO. Don't let anyone see Mom and Dad fight.

## How to Apply

1. **Hire the VPM around $20K MRR / 10+ paying customers.** Not before you have product and revenue — a VPM can't manufacture demand that doesn't exist. But as soon as you have 10 customers and any lead flow at all, the math works. Waiting until $3M ARR is how you lose 12 months of compounding. Second-timers hire earlier because they've paid the price for hiring late.
2. **Do not underhire. Ever.** The single biggest mistake I see is hiring a "marketing manager" or a Director-lite because you got scared on comp. A Marketing Manager rarely has enough experience to own a lead commit. If they can't own a commit, they aren't accretive. And a non-accretive junior hire with a budget is *more* expensive all-in than a senior hire with a bigger salary. Hold out for a real VP or a strong Director+. If you really can't get there, stay unhired and keep doing it yourself.
3. **Hire for Demand Gen first, everything else later.** Three archetypes: Corporate Marketing, Product Marketing, Demand Gen. Until you're past $20M+ ARR, hire someone who lives, breathes, and loves Demand Gen. Lead scoring, funnel management, drip, webinars, SEM, CAC, revenue per lead. Their LinkedIn should scream "I own a number," not "I rebranded." Corporate and Product Marketing candidates from BigCos almost always fail at this stage. It's hopeless, in fact.
4. **Accept they'll only do 3–4 things well — match to your biggest gap.** Ask every VPM candidate to list everything they want to do and rank it. Realistically, they'll do the top 3–4. If their top 3–4 are webinars, lead scoring, sales collateral, and SEM — great, if that's what you need. If their top 3–4 are brand, PR, events, and positioning — fine candidate, wrong company, wrong stage. Move on. It's not a ding on them. It's a match problem.
5. **Remember: the CEO is always the #1 marketer.** You can hire a great VPM. You cannot delegate ownership. Benioff was the #1 marketer at Salesforce forever. Zuck is the #1 marketer at Meta. If you the founder don't want to think about marketing, you're going to have a bad time no matter who you hire. Plan to spend real time on marketing strategy and narrative yourself. The VPM is your partner, not your replacement.
6. **Do NOT have marketing report to sales.** I know it looks clean. It almost never works. Budget explodes because sales doesn't care about CAC. Long-term investments die because sales lives quarter to quarter. And the CEO abdicates what should be their own job. The VPM reports to the CEO, period, unless you're very well capitalized and genuinely don't care about CAC. Even then — rarely works.
7. **Set the lead commit before the offer letter.** Your VP Sales has a quota. Your VP Marketing has to have a lead commit. Call it a lead commit, MQL commit, opportunity commit, SAL commit — the name matters less than that there is one. Opportunity Commit works best for $20K+ ACV. Something further up the funnel is fine for smaller ACVs. Pick one, write it down, make it the #1 KPI.
8. **Put variable comp on that commit.** A VPM without variable comp gives you suboptimal results. They'll optimize for the squishy stuff that's fun and doesn't get measured. I structure it like this: part of their variable is the lead/opportunity commit, part is tied to the overall company ARR goal. Both need to hit for the full bonus. That way they can't game volume with crap leads, and they can't hide behind a missed ARR number by pointing at "quality."
9. **Interview Question #1: What was your lead commit at your last company?** If they can't answer fluently in about 60 seconds — pass. I don't care how strong the resume looks. 95 out of 100 candidates who claim Demand Gen experience stumble on this question because they never actually owned a number. "I supported the team that owned MQLs" is a fail. Ask *how* it was determined, too. Doesn't matter if the CEO picked it or the VPS or the VPM — but if they don't know how, they weren't really part of the conversation.
10. **Interview Question #2: I want to hit $Xm ARR by year-end. What should we do in the first 90 days?** They won't know the full answer. But a real one will start doing the math out loud — average deal size, close rate, growth rate needed, incremental leads required. If their first move is a "brand review for 30 days" — pass. Their first move should be figuring out how to grow leads Y% to create the incremental pipeline required.
11. **Interview Question #3: How should sales and marketing work together to hit plan?** Sounds generic. It's a tell. A shocking number of VPM candidates don't see the roles as tightly connected. If they talk about "throwing leads over the wall," pass every time. Ask to talk to the VPS they last worked with. Would they do it again? What kind of leads did they actually deliver?
12. **Score the Mom-and-Dad alignment test.** Your VPS and VPM need to be in the office together, sitting near each other, meeting at least twice a week, at least once formally. If they're not, you make them. If they don't naturally want to — that's a flag on both. A weak VPS blames marketing for "crap leads" in public. A weak VPM disappears when sales is missing. Both get corrected or both get replaced. Don't let anyone on the team see Mom and Dad fight.
13. **Set the 30 / 60 / 90-day bar before day one.** First 30 days: mastered the product, understands the voice, knows the ICP cold, has kicked off the first always-on motion (webinars at EchoSign — my VPM never missed a week). By 60: lead scoring, routing, and funnel segmentation in place; first measurable lift on revenue per lead. By 90 days (one full sales cycle): owns an actual lead number with variable comp attached, and you can see growth in LVR. If at 90 they still don't own a number, it's over.
14. **Run the accretion test at 90 days.** Simple: are leads up, revenue per lead up, sales team happier, CEO less bogged down in marketing tactics? If yes on 3 of 4 — keep going. If yes on 1 of 4 — move on. A great VPM is accretive fast. A mediocre one will keep telling you "brand takes time" while the numbers don't move.
15. **Watch for the two most common failure patterns.** First: the Blue Pen VPM — beautiful collateral, great decks, new logo, no leads. Came from a BigCo, came from Corporate or Product Marketing, was never actually accountable for a number. Second: the VPM who wants to be CRO. Slowly tries to absorb sales into their org. That's a positional grab, not a sales-and-marketing partnership. Both are fireable. Neither improves with more time.

## Signature Numbers

- **$20K MRR / 10+ paying customers** — earliest the math works for a VPM hire. Wait any longer and you're leaving leverage on the table.
- **3–4** — the number of marketing functions even a great VPM will actually do deeply. The other 6–7 get hacked or skipped.
- **3 archetypes** — Corporate Marketing, Product Marketing, Demand Gen. For $1M–$20M ARR SaaS, hire Demand Gen. Full stop.
- **$1M ARR: brand marketing makes little sense. $10M: ok let's do some. $100M: most of marketing. $1B: all of marketing.** That's the arc.
- **60 seconds** — how long a real VPM needs to answer "what was your lead commit at your last company?" If they can't, pass.
- **95 of 100** — VPM candidates who claim Demand Gen experience but can't fluently explain the lead commit they actually owned.
- **20%+ revenue bump** — what a good VPM typically gets from pure funnel hygiene on existing leads in the first quarter.
- **2x a week** — minimum VPS↔VPM meeting cadence. At least once formal. Sitting physically near each other in the office.
- **1 lead number** — not two, not three. Pick one (MQL, SAL, SQL, or Opportunity Commit) that VPS and VPM both agree is the #1 marketing KPI, and put variable comp on it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $25K MRR, 12 paying customers, 2 sales reps working. Just closed a seed round. Board pushing to hire a VP Sales first. Founder is drowning in lead routing, webinars, collateral, and website updates.
**Application:** Hire the VPM first, or at least at the same time. At $25K MRR with 12 customers and 2 reps, the VPM is accretive immediately — funnel hygiene alone gets 15–20% more revenue out of the leads already coming in. The VPS at this stage can wait another 2–3 quarters; the founder can keep being VP Sales for 2 reps. What the founder *cannot* keep doing is marketing, because they're bad at it and they hate it. Hire a Demand Gen VPM (not BigCo, not Corporate Marketing). Set a lead commit before the offer letter. Put variable comp on it.
**Result:** Founder hires a strong Director of Demand Gen from a $15M ARR startup (not a Super Hot name — a Tweener). Within 60 days she's put lead scoring and routing in place, launched always-on webinars, and revenue per lead is up 22%. At 90 days LVR is tracking 9%/month. Founder hires the VP Sales 6 months later with a much cleaner funnel for the VP to inherit.

**Situation:** Founder at $4M ARR. Has a VPM in seat, ex-Salesforce, beautiful brand work, great at the board meeting, conference speaking circuit. Leads have been flat for 6 months. VPS keeps muttering about "crap leads."
**Application:** This is a Blue Pen hire. Run the diagnostic. Pull the last 3 quarters of LVR under the original lead definition. Ask the VPM what her lead commit was last quarter and this quarter — if she hedges, there was no commit. Ask her to sketch the first-90-days math for hitting plan. If she goes to "brand refresh" — she's the wrong archetype. She came from Corporate Marketing at Salesforce, where the brand closed the deals for her. She has no idea how to get you from $4M to $15M on demand gen muscle. Not her fault — but she'll fail.
**Result:** Founder moves on. Paints a clean job spec: "Demand Gen VPM, lead commit attached, comp plan variable-heavy." Hires a Tweener from a $10M ARR SaaS company who had a real commit at her last role. Within 90 days she's rebuilt lead scoring, dropped SEM waste, hired an SDR leader, and LVR is running 10%/month again. Revenue recovers 5 months after that — exactly the lag.

**Situation:** Founder at $8M ARR with a strong VPS and a weak VPM. VPS is pushing hard: "Just have her report to me, it'll be cleaner, I'll get alignment automatically." Board thinks it's efficient.
**Application:** Don't. I almost never see this work. Two reasons. Budget — the VPS will burn money chasing leads this quarter with no regard for CAC. Patience — anything that takes longer than 90 days to pay off (brand, content, SEO, events, PR) will die. Plus the founder just told the company that marketing is a sub-function of sales, which is the exact wrong signal at $8M ARR. Instead: either upgrade the VPM to a peer of the VPS (and force them to be Mom and Dad of Revenue), or replace the VPM. Not both reporting to the same person.
**Result:** Founder replaces the VPM with a Demand Gen operator who has a real lead commit. Sets up a twice-a-week VPS/VPM standing sync. They sit near each other. The VPS stops blaming marketing in public. 12 months later ARR is at $16M and CAC is actually down, not up, because a real VPM was watching the spend.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Wait until $3M ARR to hire your VPM because you're "not ready."**
*Why:* You were ready at $20K MRR. You just didn't know it. Every month you delay past 10 paying customers, you're leaving a 15–20% revenue-per-lead uplift on the table, and you're burning CEO cycles on marketing tactics you're bad at. First-timers wait. Second-timers don't. The compounding cost is enormous — getting to $1M and $10M even 10–20% faster changes the whole shape of the company in a recurring revenue model.

**Don't: Hire a Corporate Marketing or Product Marketing VPM from a BigCo.**
*Why:* They've never had to manufacture leads. At Salesforce or Adobe or Google, the brand does the heavy lifting — leads just walk in the door, and marketing's job is to protect the brand and tune positioning. Your startup has no brand yet. You need someone who knows how to build a lead engine from zero, not protect a lead engine at scale. Demand Gen can hack Corporate and Product Marketing. The reverse is hopeless. Their LinkedIn will tell you which archetype they are — read it carefully.

**Don't: Hire a "marketing manager" or generalist because you got scared on comp.**
*Why:* A junior hire can't own a lead commit. They can't push back on the VPS. They can't build a team. And the worst part — you'll give them a budget, and they'll spend it. The most expensive person a SaaS startup can hire is a junior marketer with a budget. You'll pay a VPM-level salary across base plus wasted spend and get none of the VPM-level impact. Hire a real VP or strong Director+, or stay unhired and keep being the VPM yourself.

**Don't: Let marketing report to sales because it "looks efficient."**
*Why:* Budget explodes (VPS doesn't care about CAC, they care about the number this quarter). Long-term marketing dies (brand, SEO, content, events all get gutted). And you the CEO just abdicated what should be one of your top 2 jobs. Unless you have unlimited capital, this almost never works. The VPM and VPS should be peers — Mom and Dad of Revenue — both reporting to the CEO. Not a parent-child relationship.

**Don't: Hire a VPM without a lead commit in the offer letter.**
*Why:* No lead commit, no variable comp, no accountability. "She owns brand" is a non-goal. Your VP Sales has a quota; your VPM has to have a quota, too. Whatever you call it — lead, MQL, SAL, SQL, Opportunity Commit — pick one, agree on it with your VPS before the offer letter, and put variable comp against it. VPMs without variable comp on a commit give you suboptimal results. Every. Single. Time.

**Don't: Expect one VPM to do all 10 things in the marketing playbook well.**
*Why:* They won't. Nobody does. Every great VPM I've ever worked with did 3–4 things deeply and hacked or skipped the rest. My VPM at EchoSign crushed webinars, lead scoring, SEM, and testimonials — and never really did events, BDRs, customer conferences, PR, SEO, or product marketing. That's not a flaw, that's the job. The flaw is pretending otherwise in the interview. Ask what they want to do, assume they'll do the top 3–4, and decide if those 3–4 match your biggest gaps. If they don't — keep looking.

**Don't: Give a struggling VPM "more time" past the 90-day mark.**
*Why:* One full sales cycle is the call. By 90 days you should see a measurable lift in revenue per lead or in LVR, and they should own a lead number with variable comp attached. If they don't, they won't. Every month you hold on, they burn budget on the wrong things and you delay the real hire. Same rule as VP Sales — empty seat beats bad seat. Fire fast, own the mistake, and recruit the right archetype.

**Don't: Promote a great IC marketer into the VPM role because they're loyal.**
*Why:* Being the best content marketer or the best demand gen operator doesn't make someone a VPM. VPMs recruit. VPMs set comp plans. VPMs own a number across a team. VPMs partner with the VPS as a peer. Your great IC may never have done any of that. Promoting them is how you lose your best IC *and* botch the VPM hire at the same time. If they're growing into it, pair them with an outside VPM hire first. Most of the time, hire from outside.
