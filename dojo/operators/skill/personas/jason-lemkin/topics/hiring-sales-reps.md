# Hiring Sales Reps

## When to Use
- You've closed 10–20 deals yourself and you're ready to hire your first sales person.
- You have one rep hitting plan and you're deciding whether to add a second before looking for a VP Sales.
- You're staring at 5 candidates, all of whom sound great, and you're trying to figure out who will actually produce.
- You're writing the comp plan for your first AE and the Salesforce-style template you downloaded feels wrong.
- Your new rep is 60 days in, the pipeline looks thin, and you're wondering if it's them or you.
- You're getting enough inbound that round-robin is starting to feel sloppy and you're thinking about territories.
- You're debating SDR-first vs AE-first for rep #1.

## Fails When
- You're pre-revenue, or you haven't personally closed a handful of deals. There's no motion for a rep to run. You're asking a rep to invent what the founder hasn't invented yet. Almost never works.
- You hire one rep "to keep it simple" or "save money." It's the single most common mistake. One rep teaches you nothing.
- You hire a rep from a BigCo with a strong brand and tons of support and assume they'll figure out your no-brand, no-support startup. 90–95% of them won't.
- You hire someone you yourself wouldn't buy from. Every time. Don't do it.
- You've already got a VP Sales and you're trying to hire reps yourself around them. That's the VP's job. If you're doing it, you don't trust your VP, and that's a different problem.

## Core Concept

Look, your first sales reps are the most consequential hires you'll make before your VP Sales. Get them right and you end up with a repeatable motion a VP can actually scale. Get them wrong and you burn 12 months, a chunk of cash, and — worse — a pile of leads that will never come back. The math here is brutal: 90–95% of salespeople won't make it at your startup. Not because they're bad. Many of them will thrive at a bigger, brandier, more-supported company. They just won't make it at yours. Your job hiring is filtering for the 5–10% who will.

Two rules sit above everything else. First: hire two, never one. I made this mistake myself at EchoSign. I narrowed it down to two candidates, picked the smart eloquent one, and he was genuinely Great. And I learned absolutely nothing. I didn't know why he was closing. I didn't know if it was him, the product, the leads, or luck. It wasn't until we had a second rep that I could compare, see new segments, learn what actually scaled. One rep is uninterpretable — you can't A/B-test a population of one. Two is the minimum viable sales team. Second rule: hire reps you'd personally buy from. This is the cheat code. If you wouldn't trust them with your own checkbook, you won't trust them with your leads, and you'll keep stealing deals back, and the thing will fall apart. Non-negotiable.

After that, the rest is math and management. 50/50 base-to-variable, OTE rolling up to roughly 20–25% of ACV all-in. Target $2M ARR per AE at scale, ramp over 2 full sales cycles. Round-robin the leads until you're at reps 4–5, then segment. Give every new rep exactly one sales cycle to put points on the board — if they've got zero closed and zero real deals in the pipe 1.5 cycles in, 9 times out of 10 they're not going to make it, and keeping them is more expensive than firing them because they're burning your leads. It's just math. The leads that come in this week will never come in again. Don't waste them on reps who can't close.

## How to Apply

1. **Close 10–20 deals yourself before you hire rep #1.** Full stop. If you haven't personally closed real deals, the rep has nothing to run. You don't know the objections, the buyer, the ICP. Neither will they. You'll both flail. Do the founder-led work first.
2. **Hire two, never one.** I cannot say this more plainly. One rep is an A sample of size one. You learn nothing. Two lets you A/B test — segments, price points, styles, objection handling, everything. And if one quits on month 4 (they do), you still have a sales function. I made the one-rep mistake at EchoSign. Don't make it yourself.
3. **Only hire reps you'd personally buy from.** The single most useful interview filter. Run the demo yourself from the prospect seat. Would you trust this person with your credit card? No? Pass. The best B2B reps are mini-solution architects who make the buyer's life easier, not harder. If the candidate makes the buyer's job harder, they'll fail.
4. **Run the 6-part "won't make it at your startup" filter.** For every candidate: (a) have they sold without a brand behind them? (b) have they sold a product at least as hard as yours? (c) have they sold at your price point and ACV? (d) have they sold without a ton of RevOps/SDR/enablement support? (e) is your industry more complicated than theirs has been? (f) would you buy from them yourself? One weak "no" is a yellow flag. Two is a pass. 90–95% of candidates fail this.
5. **Screen for the 9 qualities of a great rep.** Really listen (not script). Adjust the pitch. Treat every prospect like they matter. Help the prospect — mini-solution architect, not vendor. Know the product cold. Create urgency when none exists. Prepare obsessively. Map all stakeholders. Efficient with their time. The uniting pattern: prospect-centric, not rep-centric. They solve problems; the close is what happens when they do it well.
6. **Watch for the 8 signs of a failing rep, hard.** Immediate massive discounting. Doesn't understand the product. Fear (not respect) of competition. Blames leads, marketing, product. No updates or the same updates again and again. Badmouths the boss. Doesn't know the buyer, the stakeholders, the status of their top 10 deals. Doesn't believe in the product. One or two of these early is a yellow flag. Three or more, in the first sales cycle, move on.
7. **Pay 50/50 base-to-variable, all-in ~20–25% of ACV.** OTE has to be market-competitive — reps won't leave a good job for a bad offer. Split it roughly 50% base and 50% variable on bookings. The rep should take home about 20–25% of every dollar of Year 1 ACV they close, all in. Simple. Understandable. If a rep can't explain their own comp plan in 90 seconds, you've overcomplicated it. No caps on commission. Ever.
8. **Cover the base before any commission kicks in.** My EchoSign version of this: no bonus at all each month until the rep has cleared their base salary + benefits in closed revenue. Then the full commission rate on every dollar after. It's similar to a draw, but the base is a salary, not a loan. The effect is automatic: losers cycle out because they never get into bonus and the money isn't worth it; A-players print money because the sky's the limit once they clear. No ratcheting quotas, no decelerators, no BigCo circus.
9. **Target $2M ARR per AE at scale.** That's the benchmark to build the plan around — an average, fully-ramped AE should be closing roughly $2M per year in new ARR. Lower for very short SMB cycles, higher for enterprise with big ACVs. Set initial quotas in the $400K–$550K range for early-stage reps and push them up over time as the motion tightens. At SaaStr itself we're running the same playbook — our average rep closes $3M+ a year, top rep has closed $25M. It works.
10. **Ramp to full quota over 2 full sales cycles.** SMB with 30-day cycles: closing deals in cycle 1, fully ramped to quota by cycle 2. Mid-market with 60–120 day cycles: same — points on the board in the first 60 days, fully ramped in 5–6 months. Enterprise with 6–12 month cycles: real deals in the pipe within 90 days, fully ramped in 6–9 months. If they have nothing — no closes, no real deals — after 1.5 sales cycles, that's your answer.
11. **Give every new rep exactly one sales cycle to put points on the board.** Not a quarter, not "let them settle in." One cycle. Some great reps crush it week one. Some need a month. But if a rep is giving 110% and has no closes and no real pipeline at the end of cycle 1, they're not going to. The excuses — product, leads, marketing, timing — might all be true. The best reps close anyway. It's not cruel to fire them. It's cruel to the next customer to keep them, and cruel to the leads you're burning.
12. **Round-robin leads until reps 4–5, then segment.** Don't get clever early. Route leads automatically, in order, same playbook, same quality. You need every rep running the same game so you can compare them. Once you hit reps 4–5 and leads naturally bucket into Small / Medium / Large, start specializing. Some reps are better at high-velocity SMB, others at longer-cycle enterprise. Put them where they win. Later — maybe rep 5+ — segment by industry if verticals matter. Don't do this at reps 1–3. You don't have the sample size.
13. **Implement a lead SLA the moment you have 3 reps.** This is the #1 thing most founders skip. Any lead not followed up within 2 hours gets reassigned to the newest rep. Any lead untouched for 2 weeks gets reassigned. The B leads are gold that goes stale fast, and reps will starve them once their A pipeline fills up. The newest rep is hungriest. Give them the scraps. They'll convert some.
14. **AE first. SDR second.** For most early-stage B2B SaaS with ACVs above $10K, hire closers, not prospectors. Your 1–2 founders are already doing outbound themselves. You need humans who can close inbound + light outbound. Add SDRs once the AE math is proven and the AEs are constrained by prospecting time — usually around rep 3–5 for the team. And when you do add SDRs: promote them fast to AE. The best source of new AEs is your own SDR bench.
15. **Listen to calls every week. At least 5–10 of them.** Gong, Chorus, whatever. You'll be shocked at what you hear. Reps butchering the pitch, missing buying signals, forgetting to follow up. Cheap fixes. Huge returns. You don't stop being the sales coach once you hire reps — you just add it to the job.
16. **Give the first 1–2 reps 2x–3x the equity you'd give later reps.** They took the real risk. Pre-brand, pre-playbook, pre-VP. That first magical rep is who every later rep gets compared to. Get her more equity than you think you should. You won't regret it.

## Signature Numbers

- **2 reps** — the minimum. Never one. One teaches you nothing.
- **90–95%** — of salespeople who won't make it at your startup. The base rate. Plan around it.
- **10–20 deals** — closed personally by the founder before hiring rep #1.
- **50/50** — base to variable split for reps.
- **20–25% of ACV** — total rep comp all-in (base + commission). If it's above 25%, you overpaid. Below 20%, good reps will leave.
- **$2M ARR per AE** — the target per fully-ramped rep at scale. At SaaStr we hit $3M+ average, $25M top rep.
- **$400K–$550K** — typical initial ACV quota for an early-stage AE at EchoSign-era pricing. Scale up over time.
- **1 sales cycle** — the time a new rep gets to put points on the board.
- **1.5 sales cycles** — the time after which, with nothing closed and no real pipeline, 9 times out of 10 they're not going to make it.
- **2 full sales cycles** — the ramp window to full quota for most reps.
- **Reps 4–5** — where you stop round-robin and start segmenting.
- **Reps 3+** — where you must implement a lead SLA. Before that, it's overkill.
- **2 hours / 2 weeks** — the lead SLA windows. Untouched faster than that, reassign.
- **Reps 3–10** — where you have to really train. You could skate on osmosis with 1–2. From 3 onwards, most reps can't train themselves.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $1.2M ARR, closed 15 deals herself, has budget for one AE and is tempted to hire just that one to "keep it simple" and extend runway. Great candidate, ex-Salesforce, sold the 3 of them on himself in the interview.
**Application:** Don't. Hire two, not one — and the ex-Salesforce candidate fails the 6-part filter anyway. He sold with a massive brand, a full SDR army, RevOps holding his hand. Your $1.2M ARR startup has none of that. The 90–95% base rate says he's one of the 90–95%. Keep looking. Find two stretch hires who've sold at a $5M–$15M startup at your ACV, who you'd personally buy from. Hire them simultaneously. If budget is the real blocker, delay the hire a quarter and keep selling yourself — founder-led sales through $2M+ is very doable. What you can't do is hire one and hope.
**Result:** Founder passes on the ex-Salesforce guy. Three months later hires two reps from smaller-company backgrounds, both pass the "would I buy from them" test. Both ramp in 2 cycles, both hit $450K quota in year one. Now she has a real A/B test and a real motion. Starts the VP Sales search with data, not prayers.

**Situation:** Founder at $2.5M ARR has two reps. Rep A is killing it — $180K closed in quarter 1. Rep B has closed $15K in 75 days, blames the lead quality, blames the competition, complains about product gaps in every 1:1. Founder is agonizing about whether to give him "one more quarter."
**Application:** Move on this week. Rep B has hit 4 of the 8 signs of a failing rep in 75 days: blames the leads, blames the competition, probably blames marketing too, and clearly doesn't believe in the product. It's been 1.5 sales cycles assuming a 60-day cycle. He has no real pipeline. The excuses might even be partially true — your product has gaps, some leads are mediocre. Doesn't matter. Rep A is closing in the same environment. The leads that went to Rep B are gone — you can't give them to a real rep now, they're cold. Every additional week Rep B stays, more leads die in his pipeline. Fire this week, reassign the untouched leads to the newest rep (or Rep A if she has capacity), start recruiting replacement.
**Result:** Founder fires Rep B. Painful conversation, 20 minutes. Two weeks of reassigning warm leads — Rep A pulls another $40K out of the ones that weren't fully dead. New rep starts 6 weeks later, fits the "would I buy from them" test, closes her first deal in cycle 1. Founder realizes in hindsight she'd known by day 45. Everyone always does.

**Situation:** Founder at $4M ARR has 5 reps, all on round-robin, all selling the same playbook. Leads are starting to bucket — about 30% SMB quick-hit, 50% mid-market, 20% larger enterprise ones with 6-figure ACVs. Founder is wondering if round-robin is still the right call.
**Application:** Time to segment. You're past the learning phase with round-robin — you know the motion works. Now you're leaving money on the table because your high-velocity SMB rep is fumbling 6-figure enterprise deals, and your best enterprise rep is wasting pipeline on $3K SMB deals that should close in one call. Split: 2 reps on SMB/high-velocity, 2 reps on mid-market, 1 rep on the emerging enterprise segment (plus you, still, on the biggest). Implement a lead SLA now if you haven't — at 5 reps, the B leads are definitely dying in someone's inbox. And this is about when you think about your first SDR. The AEs are starting to hit their time ceiling on outbound; offload it.
**Result:** Segmentation ships in 3 weeks via simple Salesforce routing rules. Revenue per lead climbs 20%+ in the following quarter because each rep is now matched to the deals they win. The enterprise rep closes two 6-figure deals in Q2 that would have died in an SMB rep's pipeline. First SDR starts at month 4 of the new structure and doubles outbound pipeline.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Hire one rep instead of two to "save money."**
*Why:* This is the single most common first-sales-hire mistake, and I made it myself at EchoSign. The "savings" are illusory. One rep is uninterpretable — if they crush, you don't know why; if they fail, you don't know why. You've paid a year of salary and learned nothing about what's actually scalable. Two reps is cheaper in the end because you learn faster. And the redundancy matters: reps quit. Even great ones. One-rep startups that lose that rep lose the whole sales function overnight.

**Don't: Hire a rep you wouldn't personally buy from.**
*Why:* This is the cheat code filter and founders violate it all the time because the candidate is "polished" or "has the right background." If you wouldn't trust them with your credit card, you won't trust them with your leads. You'll hover, second-guess, steal deals back. The rep will fail not because they can't sell but because you never actually let them. Non-negotiable. Run the demo from the buyer's seat. Would you buy? Yes or no.

**Don't: Copy a BigCo sales comp plan for your first 2 reps.**
*Why:* I did this at EchoSign — we literally copied Salesforce's plan. Revenue per lead fell 50%. That was our Year of Hell. BigCo plans are designed to manage thousands of reps across mature markets with a strong brand — low commission per deal, high quota, heavy accelerators/decelerators, reps handed off the moment the contract is signed. At a startup, every lead is precious and every deal matters. You want reps who love small customers as much as big ones and who get rich when the company wins. 50/50 base-to-variable, cover-your-base-first, ~20–25% all-in, no caps. Simple beats sophisticated at your stage.

**Don't: Give a non-producing rep "another quarter."**
*Why:* 9 times out of 10, reps who don't put points on the board in 1 sales cycle don't produce in the next one either. The excuses are usually partially true — product, leads, support — but real reps close anyway in the same environment. Every week you delay, the rep burns more leads that you will never get back. The cost is asymmetric: keeping a bad rep for a quarter costs you a quarter of leads + a quarter of salary. Firing them and running 1 rep short for a few weeks while you rehire is much cheaper. Empty seat beats bad seat.

**Don't: Jump to a VP Sales with only one rep hitting plan.**
*Why:* A VP Sales is a multiplier. They take what's working with 2 reps and scale it to 3–300. If you only have one rep on plan, you don't have a motion yet — you have a data point. The VP will burn cash trying to invent the motion themselves, which is not what they're good at, and you'll be back on every call yourself within 6 months. Get to 2 reps on plan first. That's the gate. The VP is a different topic — see hiring-vp-sales.md.

**Don't: Over-engineer lead routing before rep #4 or #5.**
*Why:* Territories, complex round-robin with overrides, vertical assignment, account tiering — save it. Before reps 4–5 you don't have the sample size to measure any of it, and the complexity will hide problems, not solve them. Start with basic round-robin, same playbook, same lead quality, all reps. Compare apples to apples. When a rep is clearly better at a segment, start specializing them. Before that, you're just adding noise.
