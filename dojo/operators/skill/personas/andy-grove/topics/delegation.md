# Delegation

## When to Use

- Any time you hand a task to a subordinate and you remain accountable for the result. Which, if you're a manager, is most of what you do.
- When freeing up your own time is the explicit goal — but freeing it up *without* the work quality collapsing.
- When deciding whether to delegate a task you know how to do versus one you don't.
- When deciding how often to check on an active delegated task.

## Fails When

- **You treat delegation as a transfer of responsibility.** It isn't. The delegator remains responsible for the output. Treating handoff as final is abdication, not delegation.
- **You monitor at the polished-output stage.** By the time the report is in final form, the cost to fix structural problems is enormous. Monitor at the rough-draft stage, where a comment costs nothing.
- **You set a single check-in frequency for everyone.** A new task in the hands of a new subordinate needs frequent monitoring; a familiar task in the hands of a proven performer needs almost none. One frequency for all is wrong half the time.
- **You delegate only what you don't know how to do.** Counter-intuitive but true. Delegate what you know best. You can only monitor effectively what you can read at a glance, and you cannot read what you do not understand.
- **You hide behind "he has to make his own mistakes."** That's coaching language for what is actually abdication. The customer pays the tuition.

## Core Concept

Delegation is not a transfer of responsibility. It is a transfer of execution while you keep the responsibility. If your subordinate ships bad work, *your* organization shipped bad work. That single sentence rules everything else in this framework.

The trap most managers fall into is binary thinking. Either I'm "doing it myself" or I've "delegated." Both are wrong as descriptions of how the work actually gets done. The right unit of analysis is the *monitoring relationship* — what stage you check, how often you check, and what you do when the check turns up a problem. Monitoring is not meddling. Meddling is dictating the steps. Monitoring is checking that the result is on track at the lowest-value-added stage at which a check is meaningful — the rough draft, not the polished memo. Apply quality-assurance principles: sample randomly, sample early, sample more aggressively when the subordinate is new to this specific task and less aggressively as performance accumulates.

The frequency dial is not set by your view of the subordinate as a person. It is set by their *task-relevant maturity* on the specific task in front of them. The same person can be a fully autonomous senior on a familiar problem and a near-novice on something adjacent. Your monitoring should track the task, not the org chart. And it should ratchet down — never disappear — as the work history accumulates. The right to fly solo has to be earned, and even then, the senior surgeon's work gets reviewed.

## How to Apply

1. **State the output, not the activity.** Before delegating, write down what completed, satisfactory work looks like — in the language of the result, not the steps. If you can't state the output, the subordinate can't either, and you're delegating confusion.

2. **Pick the lowest-value-added stage to monitor at.** For a written report, that's the outline or rough draft. For a hire, that's the candidate slate before interviews start. For a capital request, that's the structure of the analysis before it's typed up. You are looking for an artifact early enough that a comment changes the trajectory rather than triggering a rewrite.

3. **Set frequency by task-relevant maturity, not by personality.** First time this person has run this kind of task: weekly checks at minimum, with substantive review of an early artifact. Tenth time, with a track record: a quick read on the final artifact may be enough. As performance on this task accumulates, ease up — but never to zero.

4. **Sample, don't audit.** You're not 100%-inspecting; you're sampling. Look at the random page, the random call, the random output. The point is to confirm the trajectory, not to redo the work. If the sample is bad, increase the frequency; if the sample is consistently good, decrease it.

5. **When you check decision-making, monitor the process not the conclusion.** Ask the subordinate to walk you through the analysis. If the thinking is sound, approve. You don't have to redo their reasoning to evaluate it; you have to interrogate it.

6. **Prefer to delegate what you know best.** Counter-intuitive but right. You can only monitor what you understand. Delegating an unfamiliar task means you can't tell from the rough draft whether it's on track. Hand off the familiar work first; learn the unfamiliar yourself before you delegate it.

7. **Earn-back the autonomy explicitly.** When a subordinate is ready for less monitoring, name the conditions: "When you've shipped three of these without a major rework, I'll move to spot-checks." Make the upgrade earned, visible, and reversible.

## Examples

**Situation:** Brochure writer hires her first editor.

**Application:** She is afraid hiring help will sink quality. The framing she needs is that "now that you manage, your employee's work output is also *your* work output." She trains the editor in the customer's house style, then monitors closely on the first several pieces — at the *outline and first-draft stage*, not at the proof stage. As the editor's work demonstrates consistency, she eases the monitoring frequency but never drops it entirely.

**Result:** Capacity grows without quality collapsing. The fear was real but the answer was structural — train, monitor, evaluate — not "do it all yourself forever."

> "Even though you are just a one-person business, your problem is similar to that faced by many growing companies. There is no way to expand your enterprise while you continue to do everything. You must learn to bring help aboard — necessary for growth — without letting the quality of your organization's work suffer." (1:1 Q&A)

---

**Situation:** A manager argues his subordinate should be allowed to "make his own mistakes — that's how he learns."

**Application:** The framing is wrong. Mistakes happen, but the question is who pays for them. The subordinate is paid to learn on the job, but the *customer* should not pay the tuition. Surgeons aren't trained by letting them experiment on patients. The supervisor's job is to look over the shoulder closely enough that calamities are intercepted before they reach a third party — and to back off as the subordinate's work begins to clear that bar consistently.

**Result:** Most "learning from mistakes" rhetoric is a manager rationalizing reduced effort on the supervision side. Naming it correctly — *abdication dressed as coaching* — is the precondition to fixing it.

> "The subordinate did poor work. My associate's reaction: 'He has to make his own mistakes. That's how he learns!' The problem with this is that the subordinate's tuition is paid by his customers. And that is absolutely wrong." (HOM, quoted in Horowitz foreword)

---

**Situation:** A pencil. (Grove's own demonstration.)

**Application:** Picture this. I am your supervisor and I walk over to you with a pencil in my hand. I tell you to take it. You reach for it — and I won't let go. So I say, *what is wrong with you, why can't I delegate the pencil to you?* The point isn't about pencils. The point is that we all have things we secretly don't want to delegate because we like doing them ourselves, and we conduct insincere delegation theatre — going through the motions while keeping the grip. The cost is enormous *negative* leverage: the subordinate gets the assignment but not the authority, and the work stalls between two people pretending to have handed it over.

**Result:** Either delegate it or don't. Insincere delegation is worse than either. If you've decided to keep the pencil, hold onto it consciously and stop pretending you've handed it off.

> "Delegation without follow-through is *abdication.* You can never wash your hands of a task. Even after you delegate it, you are still responsible for its accomplishment, and monitoring the delegated task is the only practical way for you to ensure a result." (HOM ch.3)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the handoff conversation as the end of the delegation.
**Why:** It's the beginning. The hard part — picking the right monitoring stage, setting the right frequency, varying the frequency by performance — happens in the weeks after, not in the meeting where you assign the task.

**Don't:** Wait to review the work in its polished, final form.
**Why:** By then it's expensive to redirect. The whole leverage of monitoring comes from catching trajectory problems at the rough-draft stage, when a five-minute comment changes the outcome. Late review is just inspection — it confirms the disaster, it doesn't prevent it.

**Don't:** Apply the same monitoring intensity to every subordinate and every task.
**Why:** Effectiveness depends on task-relevant maturity, not on what feels nice or fits your self-image as a "delegator." Hands-off supervision of an immature task burns the customer; structured supervision of a mature task is meddling. One mode for all is wrong half the time.

**Don't:** Quietly drop the monitoring as time passes because nothing has gone wrong.
**Why:** The reason nothing has gone wrong may be that you're monitoring. Ratchet down deliberately and visibly as the subordinate earns it — name the conditions, name the new frequency, and keep the right to ratchet it back up if a sample turns up bad.

**Don't:** Delegate the unfamiliar task to escape having to learn it.
**Why:** You can't monitor what you don't understand. The rough draft will look fine to you because you don't know what good looks like. Delegate what you know best; learn the unfamiliar yourself before handing it off.

**Don't:** Reach for "he has to make his own mistakes" as a closing line.
**Why:** It's the manager's escape hatch from the harder work of looking over the subordinate's shoulder before customers feel the failure. Mistakes will happen anyway. The question is whether they happen at a draft you can correct or in front of a customer you can't take it back from.

**Don't:** Confuse monitoring with meddling.
**Why:** Meddling is taking over the steps. Monitoring is checking the result. The subordinate runs the task; you check that the trajectory is right. If you find yourself prescribing the next three actions, you've crossed from monitor to meddle, and you've now both demoralized the subordinate and removed the leverage you were trying to gain by delegating in the first place.

**Don't:** Stop checking once you've handed something off "to a senior person."
**Why:** Even the senior surgeon's work gets reviewed. Seniority shifts the frequency and the stage of monitoring; it doesn't eliminate them. The accountability never transfers — only the execution does.
