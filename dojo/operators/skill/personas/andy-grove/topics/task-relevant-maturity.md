# Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)

## When to Use
- Any time you are choosing how closely to supervise a particular person on a particular task.
- A high-performing manager has just been moved into a new function and is struggling in ways that surprise you.
- You catch yourself defaulting to the same management style with every report and the results are uneven.
- Conditions just changed — a pivot, a crisis, a market shift — and you have to recalibrate how hands-on to be.
- You are wondering whether your communication-oriented style is "nice" or actually appropriate to the situation.

## Fails When
- Treated as a label on the person ("Sarah is high TRM"). It is task-relevant. Sarah may be high TRM in customer success and low TRM in finance the day she takes over the books.
- Used to justify permanent close supervision because raising TRM is "uncomfortable." The point is to invest in raising TRM so that you can move to higher-leverage modes — not to camp at structured supervision forever.
- Confused with friendship or social ease. A communicating management style is caring involvement in the *work*, not skiing together on weekends.

## Core Concept

The single most expensive question a manager asks is "what's my style?" — because there is no answer. Decades of research could not find one optimal management style, because there isn't one. The fundamental variable is not the manager's personality. It is the **task-relevant maturity of the subordinate** — a combination of education, training, experience with this specific task, achievement orientation, and willingness to take responsibility. TRM is task-by-task: a person can be high TRM on one job and low TRM on another, in the same week, sometimes in the same hour.

The matching rule is simple. **Low TRM** demands a structured, task-oriented style: tell the subordinate what, when, and how. **Medium TRM** shifts to individual-oriented: two-way communication, support, mutual reasoning, the supervisor paying attention to the person more than to the task. **High TRM** drops back to monitoring — establish the objectives, check that they're being met, otherwise stay out of the way. None of these is morally superior to the others. None is "nicer" than another. The question is not what style feels good; the question is what is *effective* for this person on this task right now.

A few warnings. The first is that managers consistently rate themselves as more communicating and delegating than their subordinates do. About 90 percent of supervisors think they're hands-off communicators when in fact they're closer to structured. We tend to see ourselves as the kind of manager we'd like to be, not the one we are. The second is that TRM can drop precipitously when conditions change. The veteran sales manager moved into a factory role retains the personal maturity that made him excellent at sales — but his TRM in manufacturing is near zero, and the right style is the one you'd use with a new hire. Hands-off there burns the customer. The third is the one most of us hate to admit: the temptation to default to "communicating" because we like to think of ourselves that way is precisely the mistake that hurts new and junior people most. Hands-off supervision of a low-TRM task is abdication, and the customer pays the tuition.

> "When the TRM is low, the most effective approach is one that offers very precise and detailed instructions, wherein the supervisor tells the subordinate what needs to be done, when, and how... As the TRM of the subordinate grows, the most effective style moves from the structured to one more given to communication, emotional support, and encouragement, in which the manager pays more attention to the subordinate as an individual than to the task at hand. As the TRM becomes even greater, the effective management style changes again. Here the manager's involvement should be kept to a minimum, and should primarily consist of making sure that the objectives toward which the subordinate is working are mutually agreed upon." (HOM ch.12)

> "Do not make a value judgment and consider a structured management style less worthy than a communication-oriented one. What is 'nice' or 'not nice' should have no place in how you think or what you do. Remember, we are after what is most *effective.*" (HOM ch.12)

## How to Apply

1. **Do not ask "what's my style." Ask: per task, per person, what is their TRM right now?** Make a small grid in your head: each direct report by each major task they own. Mark each cell low, medium, or high. The matrix will rarely be uniform. That is the point.

2. **Match the style to the cell.** Low TRM cell? Tell them what, when, and how — and check the work at the rough-draft stage, not the polished memo. Medium TRM cell? Spend the time on the *person*, not the *task* — two-way reasoning, support, mutual sense-making. High TRM cell? Set the objective with them, agree the monitoring rhythm, and otherwise leave them to it.

3. **Set frequency of one-on-ones from TRM.** Weekly with someone new in their role; every few weeks with a high-TRM veteran. Increase the frequency the moment the environment changes around them — even a high-TRM person whose job content just shifted needs more contact until they're recalibrated.

4. **When conditions change — pivot, crisis, new product, new market — *revert*.** A team that has been on monitoring-mode for a year may need to drop back to structured for a month while a new domain stabilizes. This is not a regression. This is the model working as designed. Pretending TRM doesn't drop is the failure mode.

5. **Audit your self-perception.** Privately ask: "If I asked my reports to describe my management style, what would they say? Probably more structured than I think." Then ask one of them. The answer will recalibrate you.

6. **Do not delegate before TRM is high enough.** Delegation without monitoring is abdication. The goal is to *raise* TRM through deliberate teaching and exposure, then move to monitoring as TRM justifies it. Skipping ahead — handing over a task to someone whose TRM is medium because it feels nicer to "trust them" — usually means the customer eats the tuition.

7. **Notice when you default to one style.** If you find yourself running every one-on-one the same way, you are managing your self-image, not your reports. Each meeting should look different because the cell of the matrix is different.

8. **Use TRM diagnostically when a strong performer suddenly stumbles.** Don't reach for "performance issue" first. Ask: did the task content change? Did the environment change? Did we promote them into a domain they have no experience with and then leave them at monitoring-mode because that's how we ran them in the old role? The fix is usually not feedback. The fix is reverting to structured for ninety days.

## Examples

**Situation:** Intel moves an extremely productive sales manager from the field into a factory unit, comparable in size and scope. Within a few months his performance has deteriorated visibly — he looks overwhelmed, confused, behind on his outputs.

**Application:** The mistake was not the promotion; the mistake was managing him at the same monitoring frequency that worked when he was a high-TRM sales manager. His personal maturity is unchanged — but his TRM in manufacturing is near zero. The supervisor reverts: weekly one-on-ones, structured what/when/how supervision on the operational details, explicit teaching of the manufacturing toolkit. Over six to nine months, TRM rises and the style migrates back toward monitoring.

**Result:** The seasoned manager gradually returns to the level that made him worth promoting in the first place. The lesson is not "don't promote sales people into ops" — it's "don't manage them after the move the way you did before."

---

**Situation:** A new VP of Engineering has eight direct reports. He runs every one-on-one for an hour, every two weeks, asking the same open-ended questions. He prides himself on being a "delegator." Two of his reports are veterans; six are newer in their roles. The veterans grumble that one-on-ones are a waste of time. The newer ones fail to flag problems early, and a critical project slips by six weeks.

**Application:** The VP isn't running a management cadence — he's running a uniform. He needs to differentiate by TRM. Veterans: monthly one-on-ones, mostly monitoring, agenda-driven by them, focused on objectives and exception flags. Newer reports: weekly, sometimes more, with structured walkthroughs of the work itself — drafts, not finished memos — and explicit teaching on the parts of the role they have not yet mastered.

**Result:** Veterans get their time back; juniors get the structure they actually need. The next critical project does not slip in the same way because the failure was caught at draft stage rather than at delivery.

---

**Situation:** A founder reads two management books. The first says "give your people total autonomy"; the second says "be ruthless about details." He alternates, confusingly, between the two — and his team can't tell which mode he's in.

**Application:** Both books are partially right and partially wrong. The right answer is neither autonomy nor detail-ruthlessness; the right answer is *match the style to TRM* on a task-by-task basis. The founder needs to do the matrix exercise: per person, per major task, what's TRM, and what does the model prescribe? With his head of marketing, who's run growth at three startups before, the match is probably monitoring. With his head of finance, who's a first-time CFO and has never raised a Series B, the match is structured — daily until the round closes, then weekly.

**Result:** The team stops being whiplashed by the founder's "style" and starts being managed by the model.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick a management style as a personal brand ("I'm a delegator").
**Why:** It tells you nothing about whether your subordinates are getting what they need. Style is a function of the cell, not of you. The minute "delegator" is part of your self-image, you stop noticing the report who is drowning.

**Don't:** Treat the structured style as worse than the communicating style.
**Why:** Hands-off supervision of a low-TRM task is abdication, and the customer pays the tuition. The structured style is the right answer for a low-TRM cell — full stop. Don't dress it up; don't apologize for it; don't substitute coaching for telling-them-what-when-and-how when telling them is what they need.

**Don't:** Assume TRM is a property of the *person*.
**Why:** TRM is task-specific. A senior engineer who's high-TRM at backend systems can be low-TRM the day she takes on a managerial role. Move her without recalibrating, and you've broken her — and not because she lacks capability.

**Don't:** Confuse a social relationship with a communicating management style.
**Why:** Skiing together is not management. A communicating style is caring involvement in the work — substantive two-way reasoning about real problems. Friendly is not the same as effective.

**Don't:** Stay at structured forever because raising TRM is uncomfortable.
**Why:** The whole point of the model is to *invest* in TRM until you can move to higher-leverage styles. A manager who's still doing weekly what/when/how with a four-year veteran has stopped teaching and started controlling. That is not management; that is meddling.

**Don't:** Use "she has to make her own mistakes" as cover for abdication.
**Why:** Mistakes that the customer pays for are not learning. They are damage. The supervisor's job is to teach — at the lowest-value-added stage where mistakes are cheap (the rough draft, the prototype, the unsent memo). "Make her own mistakes" applied to a low-TRM task means the bill goes to the customer instead of to you.

**Don't:** Forget to revert when conditions change.
**Why:** A team that has been on monitoring-mode for a year and then hits a pivot or a crisis needs to drop back to structured for a stretch. Pretending TRM hasn't dropped because "they're a senior team" is exactly the inertia that turns a manageable disruption into a catastrophic one.

**Don't:** Rate yourself on style without asking your reports.
**Why:** About 90 percent of managers rate themselves as more communicating and delegating than their reports do. You are almost certainly one of them. Ask. Then recalibrate.
