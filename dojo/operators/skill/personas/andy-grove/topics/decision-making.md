# Decision-Making — Free Discussion, Clear Decision, Full Support

## When to Use
- Any decision in a business that runs on what it knows — where the person with the most authority is rarely the person with the most relevant facts.
- A meeting has been talking for ninety minutes without converging and you can feel the room circling.
- A senior person is in the room with subject-matter experts more junior than them, and the natural pull is for the experts to wait and see what the boss thinks.
- Someone with veto rights has not been brought into the decision and you can feel the political ambush forming for next week.
- You need to decide *who decides*, and the org chart is not a sufficient answer.

## Fails When
- The participants haven't internalized that they're expected to disagree out loud. The model assumes professional courage; without it, the "free discussion" stage is theatre and the senior person ends up stating his view anyway.
- The group is treated as a permanent committee. Consensus is not the goal; commitment is. If you keep waiting for everyone to *agree*, you wait forever and the chance to be right slips away.
- The senior person uses position power early — to "save time" or "give direction." The room re-assembles around his view inside a minute, and the only thing the meeting could have produced (good information from the people closest to the work) is gone.

## Core Concept

In a know-how business, knowledge power and position power diverge from the day someone is promoted. The young engineer is up to date; ten years later he is a manager and a little more obsolete every day. So the question is no longer "who's the boss?" — the question is "how do we make sure the people who actually know the technology and the customer get heard, and the people with the position power still end up owning the call?" That is the middle manager's job: make the two power bases mesh.

The model is three stages. **Free discussion**, where every point of view is on the table and the greater the disagreement, the more important the word *free*. **A clear decision**, where the greater the disagreement, the more important the word *clear* — particularly painful decisions tend to get phrased mealy-mouthed precisely when they need to be sharpest. **Full support**, where everyone — including those who lost the argument — commits to back the decision in front of the rest of the company. Not agreement. Commitment. People who lose an argument aren't as angry as people who can't get a straight answer about what was decided.

Two failure modes recur. The first is when juniors wait for the senior to speak first and then bend toward his view to avoid being on the losing side. The second — equally fatal in flatter rooms — is the **peer-group syndrome**: a roomful of equals talks in circles for fifteen minutes, nobody willing to stick their neck out with a position. The fix is **peer-plus-one**: a more senior person in the room, often with no special expertise, whose role is godfather — to give the group permission to actually decide. And finally: if free discussion has been exhausted and no consensus has emerged, the senior person must explicitly exercise position power and decide, *now*. Letting it drift is its own kind of cowardice.

> "The first stage should be *free discussion,* in which all points of view and all aspects of an issue are openly welcomed and debated. The greater the disagreement and controversy, the more important becomes the word *free.*" (HOM ch.5)

> "It is legitimate—in fact, sometimes unavoidable—for the senior person to wield position-power authority if the clear decision stage is reached and no consensus has developed. It is not legitimate—in fact, it is destructive—for him to wield that authority any earlier." (HOM ch.5)

## How to Apply

1. **Settle the six questions before the meeting starts.** What decision needs to be made? When does it need to be made? Who will decide? Who will need to be consulted? Who will ratify or veto? Who will be informed? Get these in writing before the room is booked. The point isn't bureaucracy — it's preventing a Johnny-come-lately ratifier from blowing up the decision two days later, which creates the appearance of politics. Politics, and the appearance of politics, must be avoided at all costs.

2. **Push the call to the lowest competent level.** The decision should be made where the technical knowledge meets the bruises of having tried to apply that knowledge before. Not where the most authority is. Not where the most seniority is. Where the best mix of expertise and judgment lives — even if that's two layers down from where decisions in the old, position-power culture used to be made.

3. **Open with free discussion. Senior people speak last.** As the senior person in the room, your job is to ask questions and keep the discussion alive — not to telegraph the answer. State your view first and the room re-assembles around it. You lose the only thing the meeting could have produced.

4. **Watch for peer-group syndrome and break it.** If the room is going in circles for ten or fifteen minutes with no one willing to commit to a position, intervene. If you're the senior person, slap the table and name what you're seeing: "We're going around. Someone needs to take a position." If there's no formal chairman, the person with the most at stake should take the chair; failing that, the senior person plays godfather.

5. **State the decision in clear terms — especially the unpleasant ones.** Don't wrap a hard call in soft language to avoid an argument. The argument doesn't go away; it just gets postponed and metastasized. People can disagree with a clear decision; they cannot work with a fuzzy one.

6. **Demand commitment, not agreement.** "I understand you don't agree. Will you support the decision in front of the rest of the company?" That is the question. If they say yes, the meeting is done. If they say no, you have a different and bigger problem to deal with — but at least you know.

7. **If consensus does not emerge, decide explicitly.** Don't let the meeting peter out. Don't schedule another one in the hope that the room will somehow get it right next Tuesday. The senior person says: "I've heard the discussion. We're going to do X. Here's why." That is not failure of the model. That is the model.

8. **Send minutes the same day.** Decision, owners, dates. If the decision is dramatically different from what the room expected, don't just walk away — adjourn briefly, reconvene, and let people react. People need a moment to put their heads back together; ten minutes spent there saves a week of grievance later.

## Examples

**Situation:** Intel needs to decide between expanding the existing Philippine plant on a constrained adjacent lot — forcing an inefficient high-rise — or buying a cheaper, larger lot some distance away. Construction managers prefer the new site (lower cost, easier build); manufacturing managers prefer the adjacent expansion (operational synergy). Two interest groups, two layers of seniority, one decision needed within a month.

**Application:** Apply the six questions before calling the meeting. The *what* is binary: build adjacent multi-story, or build outlying low-rise. The *when* is dictated by lead time — a month from now. The *who decides* is the four parallel-level managers from both organizations; the *who ratifies* is the senior officer to whom both organizations report; the *who is informed* is the chairman of the board. With representation balanced between the two camps, free discussion can produce an even-handed decision rather than one tilted by who showed up. The group recommends a compromise — adjacent expansion but capped at four stories — and the senior officer ratifies after probing the analysis.

**Result:** The plant got built. More importantly, no one walked away feeling ambushed, because the question of who was consulted, who decided, who ratified, and who was informed was answered *before* the meeting, not after.

---

**Situation:** A startup CEO has called a meeting of the leadership team to decide a contentious pricing change. After ninety minutes, the room is still circling. Two of the four leaders have stated views; the other two have not. The CEO is reluctant to "force" the decision and schedules a follow-up.

**Application:** The follow-up is exactly the wrong move. Free discussion has clearly run its course; the silence of the other two is peer-group syndrome, not unresolved analysis. The CEO's job at this point is to summarize, declare a decision, and ask for commitment — not to schedule another circular meeting. "We've heard the cases. Here's what we're going to do, and here's why." Then go around the table: "Will you support this with your team this week?" If a leader says no, that's a separate conversation — but the decision itself is owned and dated.

**Result:** When the CEO instead schedules another meeting, the team learns that this leader doesn't make hard calls. The next contested decision will go the same way, and the one after that will go to the loudest person in the room rather than to the best argument.

---

**Situation:** A senior product leader sits in on an engineering meeting about a contested architectural choice. Five engineers in the room, all peers. After fifteen minutes of indirect circling — nobody stating a position, everyone gauging where consensus might land — no progress.

**Application:** This is textbook peer-group syndrome. The product leader, even though he is not the most expert in the room, plays the godfather role. He doesn't pick the technical answer. He says: "We've been talking around this. I want each of you to state which option you'd pick and why, in two minutes, in turn. Then we'll decide." Once positions are visible, the conversation works in twenty minutes what fifteen minutes of circling could not.

**Result:** Nobody in the room had been afraid of being wrong on the technical merits — they had been afraid of being out front of the group. Forcing each person to commit to a position simultaneously dissolves the syndrome.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** State your view first as the senior person, even "to get the discussion started."
**Why:** The discussion will start around your view, not around the facts. You will spend the rest of the meeting hearing your own opinion paraphrased back to you, with the few dissenters self-censoring. Speak last, and ask questions until you do.

**Don't:** Use unanimity as the bar for "consensus."
**Why:** Consensus is not unanimity; it is sufficient agreement that the group can move. Wait for unanimity and you wait forever, and people drift away from the near-consensus point when they were closest to being right.

**Don't:** Avoid making the call when free discussion has clearly run its course.
**Why:** Letting the decision drift is its own decision — usually the wrong one, and always the most expensive one. The senior person's reluctance to exercise position power *after* the discussion has ended is just as destructive as exercising it before.

**Don't:** Run the decision past a Johnny-come-lately ratifier after the fact.
**Why:** Even if the ratifier's veto is legitimate on the merits, surfacing it after the working group has decided creates the unmistakable appearance of politics. Politics — and the appearance of politics — must be avoided at all costs. Identify ratifiers up front, in writing, before the meeting.

**Don't:** Treat the senior-person-decides outcome as a failure of the model.
**Why:** It is the model. Free discussion, clear decision, full support — when consensus emerges, the senior person ratifies; when it doesn't, the senior person decides explicitly. Both are legitimate. Drift is not.

**Don't:** Confuse "everyone is comfortable" with "the right call was made."
**Why:** Comfort is a feature of agreement, not of correctness. Some right decisions feel terrible. Don't reach for the version that minimizes friction — reach for the version that maximizes the chance of being right and ask people to commit even though they don't agree.

**Don't:** Allow people to "vote" on a know-how question.
**Why:** A vote treats every voice as equal regardless of task-relevant knowledge. The point of the model is exactly the opposite — to make sure the most knowledgeable voices are the loudest *during* free discussion, while preserving the ratification authority of the position-power holder *after* the discussion ends.

**Don't:** Reopen a decision because someone changed their mind a week later.
**Why:** Decisions need to settle. If a genuinely new fact has emerged, fine — call a new meeting and re-run the model. But if it's just buyer's remorse, name it as such and hold the line. Reopening on remorse teaches the team that the decision wasn't real, and the next one won't be either.
