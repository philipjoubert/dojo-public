# Planning and the Calendar

## When to Use

- Always, but especially when today is on fire and you can't see why.
- When deciding whether to take on a new commitment with no visible slack in the schedule.
- When the calendar has become a passive dump for other people's requests.
- When the gap between what your environment will demand of you in a year and what you'll be producing in a year is large and you haven't identified it yet.
- When you're tempted to react to today's gap by hammering harder, instead of looking back to find the planning decision that produced it.

## Fails When

- **Planning is treated as one of the loftier responsibilities of management.** It isn't. It's an ordinary, everyday activity. You already plan whether you'll need gas on the drive in. The job is to apply that same elementary forecasting to your work.
- **The bound annual plan is treated as the output.** The output of planning is the *actions* it causes you to take and the *decisions* it forces you to make. The bound document is exhaust. I almost never look at it after it's printed.
- **Today's gap drives the response.** When you find a gap today, the urge is to ask "what decision do I need to make right now to close this?" But today's gap is the *result* of yesterday's planning failure. The action you can still take today is to plan now for the gap that's coming a year from now.
- **You confuse forecasting with reacting.** A factory runs on forecast, not on incoming individual orders. A manager who lets every request that arrives drive the schedule is running a job shop, not a factory.
- **You say "no" only when capacity actually breaks.** By then the work has reached a high-value stage and aborting it costs you everything you've already invested. Say "no" early or pay the full cost late.

## Core Concept

Planning is the highest-leverage activity a manager can perform. One hour of careful planning ahead of a major undertaking changes the trajectory of dozens or hundreds of people for months. One hour of "responding to today's emergency" — most of which is a response to *yesterday's* planning failure — changes nothing structural. The leverage gap is enormous, and yet planning is what gets bumped first when the day gets busy. That tells you the discipline you need.

The mechanics are not exotic. Step one: what will my environment demand of me, not now, but a year from now? Step two: what will I actually be producing a year from now if I keep doing what I'm doing? Step three: what's the gap, and what do I have to do *today* to close it? That's it. The same logic that runs a factory — match output to forecasted demand by adjusting starts now — runs a manager's planning. The fact that the work is "knowledge work" doesn't change the structure; it makes the structure harder to see, which is why so few managers do it well. *I have seen far too many people who upon recognizing today's gap try very hard to determine what decision has to be made to close it. But today's gap represents a failure of planning sometime in the past.*

The calendar is the production-planning tool. Most managers use the calendar as a passive dump where other people's orders arrive and automatically claim a slot. That is mindless passivity. The calendar should be used the way a factory uses a production schedule: identify the immovable limiting steps (the things that have to happen on a fixed clock — the board meeting, the customer review, the launch), build the rest of the work around them, and *say "no" at the start* when something would break the system. Saying "no" later, when the work is already half-done, is the same answer at much higher cost. Time is the only resource that cannot be expanded; every "yes" to one thing is an implicit "no" to another, and the discipline of a planner is to make the trade explicit.

Two further mechanics make the calendar work. First, build in slack — a freeway running at theoretical maximum capacity comes to a halt the moment one extra car enters; a fully booked calendar collapses the moment one unanticipated phone call arrives. Optimum loading is not maximum loading. Second, carry an *inventory of discretionary projects* — work that needs to be done but doesn't have to be done this week. When unexpected slack opens, the inventory tells you what to do with it. Without that inventory, you'll fill the slack by meddling in your subordinates' work, which is the worst possible use of free time a manager can find.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the three-step planning loop on a regular cadence.** Not weekly — but at least quarterly, and once a year on the major plan. Step one: what will my environment demand of me a year out? Step two: what will I be producing if nothing changes? Step three: what do I do *today* to close the difference? Write the answer to step three on this week's calendar.

2. **Treat the bound plan as exhaust.** The output of the planning process is the changes in your week-to-week activity that the process forces you to make. If your calendar two months after the plan looks identical to what it would have looked like without the plan, the plan didn't happen. Do not confuse the document with the work.

3. **Use the calendar as a production schedule, not a request inbox.** Identify the immovable limiting steps — the events that have to happen on a fixed clock. Schedule everything else as offsets around those. When a request arrives, evaluate it against the schedule, not against politeness. Block recurring time for non-time-critical-but-necessary work (planning, one-on-ones, individual work). Defend those blocks.

4. **Say "no" at the start, not at the end.** Saying "no" to work after you've half-done it costs you the work-in-process. Saying "no" before it starts costs you a conversation. Same answer; very different price. When the calendar is full, declining the next request is not rude; it is the only honest response.

5. **Build in slack — about 15-20% of the day should be uncommitted.** A freeway runs best somewhat below theoretical capacity, because one unexpected car at the limit causes a jam. A calendar at 100% load collapses on the first unexpected phone call. Plan for it.

6. **Maintain an inventory of discretionary projects.** Make a list — actually write it down — of high-leverage work you want to do that has no fixed deadline. When a meeting falls through or a slot opens, pull from the inventory. Without the inventory, you will fill the slack by interfering with subordinates' work, which is negative leverage.

7. **Forecast vs. back-predict every gap.** When you hit a gap today (missed launch date, missed quality target, missed hiring number), don't only ask "what do we do now?" Ask: "what planning decision, three or six months ago, made this gap inevitable?" Then go fix the analogous decision being made today for the gap that arrives in three months.

8. **Plan as far ahead as needed, but only commit to the near window.** Intel ran a five-year strategic plan; what was actually being decided was the next year. The other four years were re-planned next year. Look long enough to set context; commit short enough to retain the option to course-correct.

## Examples

**Situation:** A manager runs a department whose responsibilities have outgrown its staffing. The team is fighting brushfires and there's no time for planning.

**Application:** This is the trap. The reason there's no time for planning is that nothing was planned, which is why every fire is a surprise. The way out is not to "find time for planning" as a generic exercise; it is to apply selectivity. Assess what you and the team can actually do. Stack-rank the work. Concentrate on the top item. When that's done, re-rank and start again. Saying "we'll do everything more or less at the same time" is what produced the brushfires; the discipline is the opposite — finish the top thing fully before starting the next.

**Result:** The brushfires reduce because the structural cause — too much work in process, none of it finishing — is removed. Quality recovers because nothing is being shortcut. The capacity to plan returns because the team isn't drowning.

> "We always seem to have more work than we are staffed for. Our response then follows an equally common pattern: We try to do all that is expected, more or less at the same time. Even as we work harder and harder, we fall 'behinder and behinder,' as the saying goes." (1:1 Q&A)

---

**Situation:** A senior manager's calendar is full of incoming requests and he is missing the things he most needs to be doing — the leveraged work.

**Application:** Treat the calendar as a production schedule. Identify the limiting steps — the meetings and events that genuinely cannot move. Block recurring time for the high-leverage activities that *should* be on the calendar but aren't because nobody is putting them there: planning, one-on-ones, individual thinking, walking the floor. Then, when a new request arrives, the answer is not "do I want to take this?" but "where in the production schedule does this fit?" If it doesn't fit, decline at the start. Most requests look acceptable when treated one at a time and impossible when treated as a queue. The calendar surfaces the queue.

**Result:** The high-leverage activities stop getting bumped. The decline rate goes up, briefly produces awkwardness, and then settles — most requesters have alternatives once they discover this one is full. The work that defines your output gets done.

> "To use your calendar as a production-planning tool, you must accept responsibility for two things: 1. You should move toward the *active* use of your calendar, taking the initiative to fill the holes between the time-critical events with non-time-critical though necessary activities. 2. You should say 'no' at the outset to work beyond your capacity to handle." (HOM ch.3)

---

**Situation:** A founder is squeezed by a non-negotiable travel deadline and finds that everything still gets done.

**Application:** A firm departure date forces ruthless selectivity. The plane leaves whether or not you're done, so the prior week becomes a forced exercise in deciding which meetings, calls, and visitors meet a stringent bar — and disposing of everything else by deferral, delegation, or a clean "no." The lesson generalizes: the selectivity that emerges under deadline pressure is what should govern the calendar all the time. The reason most managers don't operate this way is that without an external deadline, the cost of "yes" is invisible until later. The discipline is to act as if the plane leaves every week.

**Result:** When the plane leaves, all matters are either finished or properly handed off — none are left dangling. The surprise is how achievable that turns out to be when the bar for taking something on is set high enough.

> "The principles that guide me are not to get involved in anything that I can't finish and to dispose (by referring the task to someone else or simply by saying no) of all other matters. If I succeed, I board my plane exhausted but with no dangling matters left behind." (1:1 Q&A)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat planning as the activity that takes place in the planning meeting.
**Why:** The meeting is the trigger. The actual planning is the change in your week-to-week behavior afterward — the new project on the calendar, the cancelled commitment, the headcount request that goes in this week instead of next quarter. If your calendar didn't change, no planning happened.

**Don't:** Plan only at the strategic level and assume the daily calendar will sort itself out.
**Why:** The daily calendar is *where* the strategy gets executed or doesn't. If the strategy says "shift focus to X" and the calendar still has 40% of your time on Y, the strategy is a fiction. Strategic change starts with the calendar.

**Don't:** Say "yes" to a new commitment and assume you'll find the time.
**Why:** Time is the only resource that doesn't expand under pressure. Every "yes" is an implicit "no" to something else; the difference between a disciplined planner and an undisciplined one is whether the trade is conscious. If you can't name what you're saying "no" to, you can't say "yes" honestly.

**Don't:** Wait to say "no" until the work is in progress.
**Why:** You pay for the work-in-process you abort and again for the disappointment of the person you committed to. "No" at the start costs a five-minute conversation. "No" three weeks in costs the three weeks of work and the relationship.

**Don't:** Run your calendar at theoretical capacity.
**Why:** Optimum loading is not maximum loading. A freeway at full theoretical capacity stops moving the moment one extra car enters; a calendar at full capacity collapses on the first unexpected call. Build in slack, or the next surprise will spend it for you.

**Don't:** Operate without an inventory of discretionary projects.
**Why:** Free time will arrive — meetings cancel, weeks go light — and without a list of high-leverage things to pull from, you'll spend the slack on the lowest-leverage activity available, which is usually meddling. The inventory turns slack into leverage instead of damage.

**Don't:** Treat today's gap as a problem to solve in isolation.
**Why:** Today's gap is yesterday's planning failure made visible. Hammering harder doesn't fix the planning. The action that matters is the planning decision being made today for the gap three months from now. React only to today's gaps and you're permanently three months behind your own business.
