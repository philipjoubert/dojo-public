# The Trust Battery (and Trust-Falling vs. Paying Attention)

## When to Use
- Evaluating working relationships honestly and explaining to yourself why you trust certain people more than others.
- Deciding how much autonomy to extend to a new hire versus someone with two years of delivery history.
- Having a direct conversation about relationship quality — yours with a report, or two colleagues with each other.
- Diagnosing why your delegation has been failing: you're "trust-falling" and the blind spots are accumulating.

## Fails When
- Used as a threat or weapon ("your battery is low"). It's a description of reality and an invitation to change, not a leverage point.
- Used instead of direct feedback. Telling yourself someone's battery is low without telling *them* is worse than not having the framework.
- Treated as permanent. Low batteries can recharge. If you refuse to let someone rebuild, you've made a hiring decision without admitting it.
- Swung in the opposite direction — after trust-fall failures, overcorrecting to constant oversight. That's the other failure mode, not a fix.

## Core Concept

Everyone you work with has a battery that represents your trust in them. When someone is hired, the battery starts at 50% — benefit of the doubt, neutral position. Every interaction charges or discharges that battery based on what transpires. This isn't metaphor. This is how relationships actually work. The trust battery makes it explicit.

Most companies pretend trust is binary. You either trust someone or you don't. In reality, trust exists on a spectrum and it changes over time. Someone might start strong and gradually prove unreliable. Someone else might seem awkward at first but consistently deliver. Your working relationship should reflect this — and the trust battery lets everyone name what would otherwise be unspeakable.

The framework does several things at once:

- **It legitimizes differential treatment.** Why does person A get more autonomy than person B? Because their batteries are at different levels. That's not favoritism — it's earned trust.
- **It makes trust bidirectional.** You have a battery for everyone. They have one for you. If you're unresponsive, unclear, or unreliable, your battery drains in their eyes too.
- **It separates past from present.** A low battery isn't a permanent judgment. It's a current state based on recent interactions. Batteries recharge.
- **It enables honest conversation.** "My trust battery with you is pretty low after that project — here's what would charge it back up" is a real sentence that can lead to real change. Vague mistrust goes nowhere.

The second half of this framework is about *how you maintain batteries over time*: the distinction between trust-falling and paying attention.

**Trust-falling** is when you delegate and disengage. "You seem passionate, you seem to know what you're doing — I trust you to do the right thing." Then you walk away. This is how my early leadership worked, and it failed. The reason it failed is rational on the other side: people figure out what you're interested in, and they tell you about that and nothing else. You don't hear about the things that later blow up. You think you're giving autonomy. You're actually building blind spots.

**Paying attention** is trust with engagement. You still delegate. You still give autonomy. But you stay genuinely curious. You ask about the projects you didn't explicitly ask about. You rotate your deep-dives so the team can't predict which thing you'll care about this week. You reward surfacing problems, not just successes. The vibe is curious collaborator, not anxious micromanager — but you're present.

The fix for trust-fall isn't the opposite (micromanagement). It's engaged trust. You trust, *and* you stay in the information flow. Failure to make this distinction is where founders lose touch with their companies and get blindsided.

## How to Apply

1. **Make the trust battery concept explicit with your team.** Part of the power is the shared language. When everyone knows what "battery" means, honest conversations get easier. "I need to be honest — my battery with you is around 30% after last quarter. Let's talk about how to change that."

2. **Track patterns, not incidents.** One failure doesn't drain the battery. One success doesn't fill it. The battery reflects accumulated behavior. Don't react to single events.

3. **Calibrate your management style to the battery level, not to the person in the abstract.** High battery: set direction and get out of the way. Low battery: tighter feedback loops, smaller milestones, more frequent check-ins. This isn't punishment; it's appropriate management given current state.

4. **Recognize bidirectionality.** Before judging someone's reliability, ask: what's their battery with me? Have I been clear? Responsive? Reliable? Trust problems are usually mutual.

5. **Create recharging opportunities when a battery is low.** Don't just hope it improves. Create specific, achievable commitments the person can meet. Small wins rebuild trust. Vague "try harder" mandates don't.

6. **Stop trust-falling. Start paying attention.** Ask about things you didn't ask about. Make "what's been hard?" a regular question. Rotate attention across your direct reports' work unpredictably. If you only ever hear about Projects A and B, your blind spot is everything else — and the people working on C, D, and E are learning that those projects don't matter.

7. **Don't swing to micromanagement.** The opposite of trust-falling isn't constant oversight. It's engaged trust. Autonomy stays; presence increases. If your check-ins feel like interrogations, you've overcorrected.

## Examples

**Situation:** A new hire joins with a 50% battery. In the first month, they miss a deadline but communicate proactively and recover well. Battery stays neutral, maybe ticks up slightly for good communication. Over the next quarter, they consistently deliver, raise problems early, help colleagues without being asked.
**Application:** Battery climbs to 75%, 80%. That earns them more autonomy, trust on their judgment, inclusion in bigger decisions. This isn't favoritism; it's a legible pattern of earned trust. The new hire's peers aren't at the same level yet, which is fine — they can earn it too.
**Result:** The high-battery person gets calibrated with more scope. Peers aren't resentful because the mechanism is visible and individually available to each of them.

**Situation:** A senior report with 85% battery takes on a critical project. They miss warning signs, don't communicate problems, the project fails badly, and they blame the team.
**Application:** The battery drops, not because projects fail — projects fail — but because of the *pattern revealed*: poor judgment, poor communication, no accountability. Those matter for future trust. Battery goes to ~40%.
**Result:** Management style recalibrates. Smaller milestones, tighter check-ins, explicit accountability for surfacing issues. The person can rebuild — the battery isn't permanent — but the rebuilding is a deliberate process, not a return to default.

**Situation:** A CEO realizes they've been trust-falling. The engineering team has been quietly not shipping the infrastructure upgrades because the CEO never asked about them. The CEO only asks about the product features they're excited about.
**Application:** This is rational behavior by the team: they're optimizing for apparent attention. The fix isn't punishing them or escalating — it's paying attention. Schedule a tools-and-infrastructure review. Ask probing questions. Be visibly interested in what you hadn't been interested in before. Let the team recalibrate around your actual attention pattern, which should now include the things you were neglecting.
**Result:** Infrastructure work starts surfacing in regular syncs. Problems escalate earlier. The blind spot closes. The CEO didn't need to fire anyone — they needed to pay attention to the things they were signaling didn't matter.

**Situation:** A team lead is frustrated with a direct report. They can't quite name why the delegation isn't working. They're considering restructuring around the person.
**Application:** Run the bidirectional check. What's the lead's battery for the report? What's the report's for the lead? If the lead has been unclear, missed meetings, changed priorities without explanation — the report's battery is drained, and the lead's impression of the report's "unreliability" might largely be the report responding to a leader with a drained battery. The conversation is mutual trust, not unilateral blame.
**Result:** Explicit bidirectional conversation resets the baseline. Both sides commit to specific reliability behaviors. The relationship recovers, sometimes; sometimes it doesn't — but now both parties know the real diagnosis.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use battery language as a weapon.
**Why:** "Your battery is low" spoken as a threat does the opposite of what the framework is for. It turns a neutral diagnostic into a humiliation. The right use is descriptive and invitational — here's where we are, here's what would change it.

**Don't:** Treat low batteries as permanent.
**Why:** The whole point of the metaphor is that batteries recharge. If you won't let someone rebuild trust, you've made a hiring decision — act on it, but don't keep them in a low-battery purgatory indefinitely. That's worse for everyone.

**Don't:** Use "low battery" as a substitute for direct feedback.
**Why:** If you're frustrated with someone's reliability, tell them. Naming the battery state is useful; it's not the whole conversation. A private note that someone's battery is draining while saying nothing to them is just silent resentment wearing a framework.

**Don't:** Trust-fall. The delegation half of this framework has its own dedicated anti-pattern.
**Why:** Pure trust-falling — delegate and walk away — creates information asymmetry, blind spots, and the illusion of autonomy. It also trains your team to optimize for your visible interests and hide everything else. Paying attention is the fix: stay curious, rotate focus, reward surfacing problems.

**Don't:** Swing to micromanagement after a trust-fall failure.
**Why:** The opposite of trust-falling is engaged trust, not constant oversight. If check-ins become interrogations and autonomy disappears, you've traded one failure mode for another. Talent leaves under micromanagement as surely as it drifts under abandonment.
