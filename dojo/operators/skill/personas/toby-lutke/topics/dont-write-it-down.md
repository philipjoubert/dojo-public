# Don't Write It Down

## When to Use
- You're hiring for roles where taste, judgment, and initiative are load-bearing — design, early product, anything cultural.
- Someone is pushing to "systematize" hiring, culture, or values so "we can scale."
- You're about to produce a culture deck, a values document, or a detailed hiring rubric.
- You want to preserve what made the first team great while growing.

## Fails When
- Applied to technical specs, security procedures, compliance requirements, or processes that must be reproducible. Those absolutely should be documented. The principle is about taste and judgment, not about avoiding clarity.
- Used as cover for being unclear about basic expectations. "Don't write it down" isn't "be mysterious about what the job is."
- Applied past the scale where some process is genuinely required. A 500-person company needs more explicit structure than a 15-person one. The principle protects specific things from codification, not all structure.

## Core Concept

The first team you hire, you hire on your gut. You just know these are the people you need — they show up, you recognize them, you bring them on. Then the company grows, and someone says: we should systematize this. Write down what makes great hires, document the culture, create the onboarding checklist. Scale the taste.

Don't.

Here's the paradox: the act of documenting what makes great hires destroys your ability to find them. The people you want — the ones with taste, judgment, initiative — don't optimize for checklists. They don't go looking for the answer key. The people who *do* find your careers page rubric and perfectly match every bullet are precisely the wrong people. They're good at following lists. You don't want people who are good at following lists for the roles where taste and judgment matter. You want people who show up as themselves and happen to be what you need.

Companies have a reflex to systematize everything that works. Sales approach working? Document it. Hiring going well? Create a rubric. Culture feeling strong? Write a manifesto. But some things die when you write them down.

Taste, quality — if it can't be put in numbers or written as a list, companies hate it. Yet almost everything you have to do to build a great company is these things. The tension is permanent: the parts of the company that can be managed through documents aren't the parts that produce excellence. The parts that produce excellence aren't manageable through documents. The executive skill is holding both — documenting what benefits from documentation, protecting what doesn't.

The mechanism: when you codify hiring criteria, you create a game. People optimize for games. The people best at optimizing for written criteria are exactly the kind of systematic-checklist-followers you probably don't want in the roles where taste matters. The people who would naturally fit your culture — the ones operating on intuition and craft — don't study the answer key. They just show up.

The same applies to culture documentation. Write down "we value bold thinking" and watch it become a performance. Leave it undocumented and observe who actually thinks boldly under pressure. Documented values attract values-mimics.

This connects to the trust battery: what you want is tacit knowledge transmitted through example and lived experience, not explicit knowledge transferred via documents. Culture is a thousand small moves. You can't spreadsheet them.

## How to Apply

1. **Resist the scaling reflex on judgment-heavy things.** When someone says "we need to document this so we can scale hiring," pause. Ask: what kind of person reads this documentation and optimizes against it? Is that who we're trying to attract? Usually the answer is no.

2. **Hire for what can't be listed.** Taste, judgment, initiative, curiosity. If you can't list it, you can't game it. Interview for patterns in life stories: how they reacted to unexpected upsets, whether they created things or participated, whether they say "I" for what they did and "we" for what the team did.

3. **Apply the filter test: would writing this down attract or repel the right people?** Some things should be documented (technical specs, processes, safety). Others — culture, judgment criteria, what "good taste" looks like — should remain tacit. The repel-test is decisive.

4. **Use Life Story interviews instead of rubrics.** Have the candidate walk you through what they've built, decisions they made, how they handled specific moments. Listen for specifics. The people who have done the thing can tell you the details. The people who've read about doing the thing can't.

5. **Example over abstraction.** Instead of "we value craft," tell the story of a specific shipping decision where craft won over speed. Instead of "we're low-ego," tell the story of the co-founder carrying the bag for a junior engineer's idea. Specifics transmit culture. Abstractions transmit nothing — or worse, a script that people perform.

6. **Be explicit that certain things are intentionally undocumented.** This avoids the "clarity avoidance" trap. Say so: "We don't document this because when we've tried, it attracts the wrong profile. Here's how we actually work." Transparency about the decision not to document is different from vagueness about what's expected.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 25-person startup is about to grow to 50. HR says they need a hiring rubric for engineers so the bar stays consistent.
**Application:** Separate the components. Technical standards (does the person understand systems, can they ship, can they write good code) — document those. The cultural-fit parts — how they talk about the work, how they engage with disagreement, whether they prototype before theorizing — leave those tacit. Teach them through doing interviews together, not through a shared rubric.
**Result:** Technical quality stays consistent because technical standards *can* be documented. Cultural coherence stays strong because the tacit parts are transmitted through lived apprenticeship. Three years later the team is still recognizable rather than sanded down to a rubric-matching average.

**Situation:** The executive assistant hire. A founder is interviewing for EAs. The conventional rubric would focus on tools, scheduling experience, calendar etiquette.
**Application:** Toby's version: "When my band's bassist didn't show up for a European tour, I learned the bass lines myself and filled in." That's nowhere in any rubric. But that's the signal — the person with initiative, agency, and the disposition to just solve it. The rubric would never have surfaced that person.
**Result:** You hire them. This is the canonical "don't write it down" example — the thing that matters most is exactly the thing that wouldn't be on the list.

**Situation:** A company is growing fast and a consultant recommends writing a values document. They pitch "The Ten Principles."
**Application:** Test the document against the filter: what kind of person reads "we value bold thinking" and optimizes for it? Probably someone who recites bold-sounding things in interviews. What about the person who actually thinks boldly? They don't read the values page; they just are. So the document attracts performers and repels (or at least doesn't attract) actual boldness.
**Result:** Skip the document. Instead, canonicalize a small number of specific stories — things that actually happened in the company — that demonstrate what good looks like. Pass those stories forward via onboarding, via example, via explicit storytelling in all-hands. Story transmits more than the values deck ever would.

**Situation:** Shopify's trust battery concept works at scale without being a spreadsheet. Everyone knows what it means. Everyone roughly knows their battery state with various colleagues. But there's no "trust battery score" to optimize.
**Application:** If the battery were quantified, people would optimize for the score rather than for actually earning trust. The fact that it's a shared understanding rather than a metric is what makes it work. Formalizing it would destroy it.
**Result:** The framework is load-bearing across a ~10,000 person company because it stayed tacit. Had someone tried to build a "Trust Battery Dashboard," the concept would have collapsed into theater within a year.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "don't write it down" as an excuse for never being clear.
**Why:** Some things absolutely require documentation — security protocols, incident response, compliance procedures, technical specs. Don't hide behind the principle to avoid those. The principle is specifically about taste and judgment, not about basic clarity.

**Don't:** Use "don't write it down" as mystical gatekeeping.
**Why:** If no one can understand what you're looking for, that's not preserving culture — it's just bad communication dressed up. The goal is *tacit knowledge transmitted through experience*, not a priesthood. If you can't transmit it, document it or accept that people can't learn it.

**Don't:** Apply it at a scale where explicit coordination is required.
**Why:** A 2,000-person company needs more explicit structure than a 25-person one. The principle protects specific things (taste, culture, judgment criteria) from codification. It doesn't protect the entire operating system from structure. Know the difference.

**Don't:** Codify the wrong things.
**Why:** When companies do write down culture, they almost always pick the things that can be written down rather than the things that matter. Beware: the constraint "can be written down" silently biases the output toward what *can* be documented rather than what *should* be. If the list of documented values looks generic, that's a sign.

**Don't:** Use the principle to hire weirdly.
**Why:** "We don't write things down" isn't an invitation to hire on pure vibes with no process at all. Structured interviews, reference checks, and clear expectations still apply. What's undocumented is the *cultural fit rubric*, not the hiring process itself.
