# The Four Constraints (Money / Time / Risk / Attention)

## When to Use
- Resource allocation across projects, especially when the team is asking for "just one more initiative."
- Prioritization conversations that feel stuck because everything looks important.
- Diagnosing why a team is missing targets despite having plenty of money and time.
- Evaluating new opportunities — is the real cost budget or leadership bandwidth?
- Strategic moves against a bigger competitor: what fragments *their* attention?

## Fails When
- Used to justify doing less-ambitious work. The point isn't to under-load; it's to focus. An un-taxed attention constraint is wasted capacity.
- Treating the four constraints as equal. In most companies one is dramatically more binding than the others, and the framework's value is in spotting which.
- Applied to avoid hiring when the binding constraint genuinely is headcount on a repeatable motion. Sometimes more people is the right answer.

## Core Concept

Every business decision operates under four constraints: money, time, risk, and attention. Most people obsess over the first two, sometimes think about the third, and dramatically underweight the fourth. Attention is almost always the scarcest resource — and it's the one nobody is counting.

**Money.** The most visible constraint. Finance teams exist to manage it. CFOs worry about it. Boards worry about it. It tracks well on a spreadsheet. The trap: assuming more money solves problems that are actually attention problems. Hiring ten more people when the real issue is strategic focus just creates ten more attention sinks.

**Time.** Also visible, though harder to recover than money. You can raise more capital; you can't manufacture more time. The trap: using time pressure to force decisions before you have information, or extending timelines indefinitely because you're not making the hard choices.

**Risk.** Less obvious but increasingly understood. Large companies build risk budgets — they know how many risky bets they can make simultaneously without threatening the core. VCs think in portfolios for the same reason. The trap: treating risk as binary (safe vs. dangerous) rather than a budget that depletes.

**Attention.** The master constraint. Everything else depends on it. A team drowning in context-switching can't execute a well-funded, well-scheduled, appropriately-risked project. The trap: not recognizing attention as a finite resource, loading teams with "just one more thing" until everything suffers.

I learned this playing StarCraft. In competitive RTS, you have minerals and gas (money), build times (time), army positioning (risk), and your attention as a player. The best players don't just have better economies — they fragment the opponent's attention so effectively that the opponent makes mistakes even with superior resources. Resource management isn't just quantifiables. It's also your attention. And frankly, attacking other people's attention is much more profitable than attacking their resources.

Business works identically. If you overtax a team's ability to pay attention, they will make mistakes. Work will slip. Quality will degrade. And no amount of additional budget or timeline extension will fix it, because the constraint is elsewhere.

The strategic corollary is even more interesting. If attention is the scarcest resource, then attacking a competitor's attention — not their money, not their time, not their risk posture, but their *attention* — is often the most profitable move. Launch in a way that forces them to respond on three fronts. Make them scramble. Watch them make errors in all three. This is how small players beat bigger ones.

And a humbler version: most companies fail at attention without realizing it. They have runway, they have the team, they have the nominal scope of the quarter — and nothing ships, because the leadership team is fragmented across a dozen initiatives and nothing gets the concentrated attention it needs.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit all four constraints before every new initiative.** Not just budget and timeline. Ask: what does this do to leadership's attention? What's the risk budget hit? Is the limiting factor actually one of the invisible two?

2. **Identify the binding constraint on any specific problem.** For most small companies, it's attention. For larger ones, it varies. You can't prioritize correctly until you know which constraint is actually scarce. Treating them equally hides the answer.

3. **Resist the "more money / more people" reflex when the real problem is attention.** Hiring doesn't scale attention linearly. Each new person requires attention to onboard, manage, coordinate. Sometimes adding people makes the attention problem worse. The fix is usually fewer initiatives, not more bodies.

4. **Manage your own attention like the scarce resource it is.** Block for the top and bottom (see top-and-bottom.md) — deep product work and deep strategic work. Everything in the middle layer should be delegated or killed. Your calendar is a declaration of what you think matters; most leaders' calendars declare that the muddy middle matters most, which is backwards.

5. **Pick three — not ten — things that need concentrated attention.** If you can't name the three things you're paying obsessive attention to this quarter, you probably aren't. "Everything is important" is "nothing is important" wearing a costume.

6. **Strategic play: fragment the competitor's attention.** Identify what forces them to split focus. Multi-front launches. Moves in product categories they didn't expect. Pace changes they can't match. The best competitive strategy against a larger player is rarely a head-on fight — it's making them manage more simultaneously than their attention can bear.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 40-person startup is missing every target. Runway is fine. The team is talented. The founder is about to hire ten more engineers to "unblock velocity."
**Application:** Diagnose the constraint. If the founder and their top three direct reports are each nominally overseeing six to eight initiatives, the constraint is attention, not headcount. Adding ten engineers will lengthen context-switching, add coordination overhead, and make the attention tax worse, not better.
**Result:** Instead of hiring, cut the initiative list to three. Reassign existing people to those three. Velocity increases noticeably within a month, not because more work is happening per person but because less is being lost to context-switching.

**Situation:** A CEO wants to launch a new product while also improving the core product and expanding into a new geography — three things at once.
**Application:** Which attention slot does each one require? Launches, core improvement, and geographic expansion each need concentrated leadership attention for weeks. Trying to run three in parallel is a predictable failure mode: all three will slip, and nobody will be able to say why.
**Result:** Sequence them. Pick the one with the highest compounding value. Finish it. Move to the next. The total throughput across 12 months is higher than doing three in parallel.

**Situation:** A startup is competing with an incumbent that has 20x the engineering headcount. The obvious moves all lose.
**Application:** Flip the frame. You're not fighting their money or their time — you're fighting their attention. Where does the incumbent have attention bandwidth to respond to what you do, and where don't they? Build in the gaps. Launch in a way that forces them to respond across three product areas simultaneously. Their attention fragments; yours doesn't.
**Result:** The smaller company outmaneuvers on cadence and focus. The incumbent's size becomes a liability because it distributes their attention across more surface area than they can defend.

**Situation:** A founder looks at their computer and has six AI agents running simultaneously, coordinating via emails to each other. They notice it feels like StarCraft — zooming in for micro on one, zooming back out to macro on the rest.
**Application:** This is the shape of future work. The bottleneck isn't compute or API cost. It's the conductor's attention. Which means the skills that transfer from real-time strategy games — attention management across multiple fronts, knowing when to micro and when to macro — are directly applicable to working with AI systems. Strange skill transfer; real edge.
**Result:** Founder leans into the frame. Designs workflows that let them hold the macro view while diving into specifics as needed. The ability to manage attention across agents becomes a distinct competitive skill.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire to solve an attention problem.
**Why:** Each additional person is an additional attention draw — onboarding, 1:1s, coordination, status updates. If the root cause is that leadership is stretched across too many initiatives, adding people stretches leadership further. The answer is almost always fewer things, not more bodies.

**Don't:** Treat the four constraints as equally binding.
**Why:** The framework's power is in identifying the actual bottleneck. Running a parallel analysis on all four every time is elaborate but useless if you don't then act on which one is scarcest. Usually it's attention for small companies, capital for early-stage high-burn, time for pre-revenue chasing a window. Name the binding one.

**Don't:** Use "attention is scarce" to under-load your team.
**Why:** The point isn't protecting people from work. Well-managed attention can absorb an enormous amount of focused work — it just can't absorb fragmentation. Three hard problems are fine; twelve medium ones will break the same team.

**Don't:** Forget that attention management is strategic, not just operational.
**Why:** The framework applies both ways. Your attention is scarce; so is your competitor's. Most strategic thinking focuses on capital and time; attention is where small players genuinely have an edge because they can concentrate what larger competitors cannot. Make moves that exploit that asymmetry.
