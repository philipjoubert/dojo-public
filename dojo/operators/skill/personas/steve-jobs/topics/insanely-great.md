# Insanely Great

## When to Use
- Someone on your team is arguing to ship something because it's "good enough."
- You're debating whether to cut corners on a part customers won't see.
- You're setting the bar for a new product and want to know what standard to hold.
- You're reviewing work and deciding whether it clears — or whether it needs another pass.
- You're hiring and trying to separate people who chase "great" from people who chase "insanely great."

## Fails When
- You have no taste yourself. "Insanely great" without the ability to judge is theatre. Then the standard becomes whatever the loudest person says it is.
- It turns into polishing the wrong surface — endless iteration on the corner of a screen while the fundamental experience is broken.
- It becomes an excuse to never ship. That's perfectionism paralysis — a different disease, not this one. Real artists ship.

## Core Concept

There's a difference between *great* and *insanely great*. The Lisa people wanted to do something great. The Mac people wanted to do something insanely great. The difference shows. It always shows.

One of the ways I believe people express their appreciation to the rest of humanity is to make something wonderful and put it out there. You never meet the people. You never shake their hands. You never hear their story or tell yours. But somehow, in the act of making something with a great deal of care and love, something's transmitted. The person receiving it feels that someone cared. They feel that somebody gave a damn.

Most of us didn't make the clothes we're wearing. We didn't cook or grow the food we eat. We're speaking a language that was developed by other people. We use another society's mathematics. There's very little that we do that doesn't depend on people who came before us. The ability to put something back into the pool of human experience is extremely neat. That's why quality matters. That's why the back of the fence matters.

When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it. You'll know it's there, so you're going to use a beautiful piece of wood on the back. For you to sleep well at night, the aesthetic, the quality, has to be carried all the way through.

This is not perfectionism. This is integrity. The craftsman knows the truth about his work. The back of that chest exists whether anyone sees it or not. And the craftsman has to live with what he put there. Most people think nobody will know. Somebody will always know. You.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the standard before the work starts.** Not "make it good." Not "make it better than the competition." Insanely great. The word matters. It tells the team what they're reaching for.

2. **Build it for yourselves.** We built the Macintosh for ourselves. We were the group going to judge whether it was great or not. We weren't going to do market research. When you're building for yourselves, every hourly decision is different — the arguments you have, the compromises you refuse.

3. **Paint the back of the fence.** Inside the case. The parts of the schema nobody queries. The line in the API docs nobody will read. If you know it's there, treat it like everything else. Anything less and the disease spreads.

4. **Sign the work.** When we built the Macintosh, every member of the team signed their name on the mold that stamps the inside of every case. Nobody would see those signatures. You had to open the machine to find them. We knew they were there. When your name is on something, you hold yourself to a different standard. Real artists sign their work.

5. **Ship only what transmits.** When you make something with care, something is transmitted. If you handle the product and nothing comes off it — no feeling, no intention — don't ship. Go back.

6. **Refuse "good enough."** It's a disease. It starts with one compromise and spreads. Excellence is a habit, not a single act. It's what you do every day — the hard conversation, not the easy one; the person who sets the bar higher, not the one who fills the role.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple is about to ship the Macintosh. Internal builds work. External shell is close but not right. Team is exhausted. Timeline is tight. Someone proposes shipping and fixing later.
**Application:** Hold. Real artists ship, but not before the work is what you meant it to be. At the shareholders' meeting for the Mac, everyone in the auditorium stood up and gave it a five-minute ovation. The team in the front rows was crying. That reaction doesn't happen to products that shipped because the team was tired. It happens to products where every trade-off was argued through to the right answer.
**Result:** The Mac changed what people expected from personal computers — the first with beautiful typography, proportionally spaced fonts, a mouse, a graphical interface. And the people who worked on it will tell you it was the happiest they'd ever been.

**Situation:** The iMac team wanted a design with no internal screws where nobody could see. The engineers came back with 38 reasons it couldn't be done.
**Application:** Push. "We're doing this." When they ask why — "Because I'm the CEO and I think it can be done." Engineers will always give you reasons something can't be done. Manufacturing will tell you why it can't be built. Marketing will tell you why it won't sell. You have to push through, because the simple elegant solution is usually the one nobody believes is possible.
**Result:** The iMac shipped with the design, and it was a big hit. The lesson: you cannot let "impossible" be decided by the first person to say it. The decision of what's possible is yours.

**Situation:** The carpenter making a chest of drawers. Nobody's ever going to see the back. There's a cheap piece of plywood that would work. Saves time, saves money, never noticed.
**Application:** Use the beautiful piece of wood anyway. You'll know. That's the whole test. Not whether anyone else will see it — whether you can sleep with what you put there.
**Result:** You've either built a chest of drawers, or you've built a cheap imitation of one. You know which. The customer knows which, too — not because they saw the back, but because a person who used cheap plywood on the back cuts corners on things they *do* see.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Argue about whether the quality bar is worth the cost.
**Why:** If the bar is what you actually believe it should be, it's not a cost. It's the price of making the thing. Arguing the bar down is how "good enough" enters the building and never leaves.

**Don't:** Settle because the schedule is tight.
**Why:** The schedule is a variable. The product is what ships. A slipped date is recovered by the next product; a shipped mediocrity is what customers hold in their hands for the next five years. Pick the right variable to bend.

**Don't:** Let anyone who doesn't have taste adjudicate a quality argument.
**Why:** If the person deciding can't see the difference between great and insanely great, they will optimize for whatever they *can* see — cost, schedule, features on a spec sheet. Get the decision to someone who can actually feel the difference.

**Don't:** Ship the first solution.
**Why:** When you first start trying to solve a problem, the first solutions are very complex, and most people stop there. If you keep going — live with the problem, peel the onion — you arrive at elegant, simple solutions. Most people just don't put in the time or energy.
