# Focus and Simplicity — Say No to 1,000 Things

## When to Use
- You can't explain your product line to a friend in under a minute.
- You have dozens of products, each one reasonable in isolation, that collectively confuse everyone — including your own sales force.
- Your A-team is scattered across so many projects you have B and C teams on the important ones.
- You're about to add "one more feature" and it feels harmless.
- You're in a strategic review and everything on the list looks good — which means you haven't chosen.

## Fails When
- You have a genuine multi-product, multi-segment business that actually *is* different products for different needs. Forcing a 2x2 on Amazon circa 2010 would have been dumb.
- "Simplify" becomes a slogan. People reach for the delete key before they understand what they're deleting and ship a worse product that looks cleaner.
- You treat it as a one-time purge. Complexity creeps in gradually. Focus is a posture, not an event.

## Core Concept

People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. That's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done.

Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things.

When I came back to Apple in 1997 I found a zillion and one products. It was amazing. I started to ask people questions. Why would I recommend a 3400 over a 4400? When should somebody jump up to a 6500 but not a 7300? What's the difference between the 4400 and the 7220? After three weeks I couldn't figure it out. And if I couldn't figure it out — I've been in this business for twenty years — how could our customers? They couldn't.

So we drew a simple chart on the whiteboard. Two columns, two rows. Consumer. Professional. Desktop. Portable. Four products. That was it. We reviewed the roadmap of new products and axed more than 70 percent of them, keeping the 30 percent that were gems.

Here's what changes when you do this. You can put your A-team on every single product instead of having B and C teams on any. You can turn them faster. Your marketing gets simpler. Your supply chain gets simpler. Your sales force knows what they're selling. Your customers know what they're buying. The organization becomes clean and simple to understand, and very accountable.

But it takes discipline. We had to say no to a lot of products that people had worked on. A lot of good ideas that weren't great ideas. A lot of things that made sense individually but didn't make sense for the company. Focus requires sacrifice.

And it's not a one-shot. The first solutions to a problem are always very complex. Most people stop there. If you keep going, live with the problem, peel more layers of the onion off, you can oftentimes arrive at elegant simple solutions. Most people just don't put in the time.

Simple can be harder than complex. You have to work hard to get your thinking clean, to make it simple. But it's worth it — once you get there, you can move mountains.

## How to Apply

1. **Name what you will NOT do.** List the things you're saying no to. Be proud of that list. If it's short, you haven't focused.

2. **Draw the 2x2.** Most businesses have a natural 2x2 if you look for it. Apple: consumer/pro × desktop/portable. Find yours. Everything outside the grid is a candidate for deletion.

3. **Axe 70%.** When you go through a roadmap of new products or features, keep the gems — the top 30%. Cut the rest. Not because they're bad. Because they're not great, and "not great" is no longer the bar.

4. **Put A-teams on survivors.** The benefit of cutting isn't saving money — it's concentrating talent. Four products with A-teams beat fifteen products with B-teams every single time.

5. **Peel the onion past the first answer.** When you have a solution, ask: is this the complex first answer or the simple final answer? If you haven't discarded three earlier versions, you probably haven't found the simple one yet.

6. **Protect simplicity with authority.** When engineers tell you 38 reasons the elegant solution can't be built, you sometimes have to say: we're doing this. Because I think it can be done. Complexity will creep back in unless someone stands at the door.

7. **Watch for sales-driven feature creep.** Salespeople don't understand focus. More products mean more things to sell. They'll push you to add variants and options. That's how monopolies are lost — through the slow accumulation of complexity.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple in 1997. Products called the 3400, 4400, 6500, 7220, 7300, etc. Nobody — not even the CEO coming back to run the company — can figure out which to recommend.
**Application:** Draw two columns and two rows on a whiteboard. Consumer and Pro. Desktop and Portable. That's the company. Axe 70% of the roadmap. Put the best people on the four survivors.
**Result:** Within a few years Apple is the most innovative product company in the world. The survivors — iMac, PowerBook, iBook, Power Mac — drive a decade of growth. The 30% that shipped was 100% of the competitive advantage.

**Situation:** A SaaS company with 40 features in the roadmap for the next two quarters. The team is working hard. Nothing is shipping. The product gets incrementally worse because every new feature needs plumbing to everything else.
**Application:** Kill 28 of them. Not the bad ones — the merely good ones. Keep the 12 that would genuinely change the customer's life if you did them insanely well. Now the same people have 3x the capacity per surviving item. Ship them.
**Result:** Faster velocity. Better quality per shipped feature. Clearer product story. The sales team can finally explain what the product is. Revenue follows.

**Situation:** You're about to add a feature because a customer asked for it. It's reasonable. It would take a quarter. It might help close a few deals.
**Application:** Ask: is this a gem or a good idea that isn't great? Nine times out of ten it's the second one. Cut it. Add it to the proud list of things you said no to. Protect the quarter for the few things that will matter in three years.
**Result:** You lose the deal. And six months later, when the other ten good-idea features don't exist either, you've saved yourself from a feature-fat product that confuses every prospect and ships nothing that matters.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Add features to match a competitor's spec sheet.
**Why:** You can't win by advertising features and specifications. Great brands connect through feeling and values, not through spec parity. "Our benchmark is 12% higher" is the motto of second place.

**Don't:** Keep a feature just because someone worked hard on it.
**Why:** Sunk cost. The work is done; the question is whether shipping it makes the product better or worse. If it makes the product worse, kill it. The team can work on the next insanely great thing. That's respect for their work, not dismissal.

**Don't:** Confuse process improvement with focus.
**Why:** You can have the best processes in the world, but if you're working on the wrong things you'll still fail. And if you're focused on the right things, you can overcome almost any process problem. Discipline is in *what* you choose to work on, not *how* you organize the work.

**Don't:** Let every feature request expand the scope.
**Why:** Each addition seems reasonable in isolation. The cumulative effect is paralysis. The moment you can't explain the product in one sentence, you've lost. Get back to the 2x2.

**Don't:** Ship the first solution.
**Why:** The first solution is always complex. Live with the problem longer. Peel more layers. The simple elegant solution is what's left after the understanding is complete — not what's left when you're tired of the problem.
