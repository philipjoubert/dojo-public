# Direct Feedback — Call the Work What It Is

## When to Use
- A high performer's last few deliverables aren't at their standard and you're debating whether to say so.
- Your team is shipping work that isn't good enough and the culture is too polite to call it.
- You're coaching someone who wraps every piece of criticism in compliments until the point is lost.
- You're deciding how to tell someone who respects you that their work didn't land.
- You've hired A-players and you're wondering whether the normal feedback rituals are slowing them down.

## Fails When
- Applied to someone fragile, junior, or in personal crisis. Directness works when the base is trust and competence. Without both, it lands as contempt.
- Used as license for cruelty. "That's shit" is not an invitation to belittle. It's a description of the work, not of the person.
- Given without the information they need to fix it. Saying work is bad without saying why, with specifics, is just demoralization — not feedback.

## Core Concept

What does it mean when you tell someone their work is shit? Well, it usually means their work is shit.

Sometimes it means, I think your work is shit, and I'm wrong. But usually it means their work is not anywhere near good enough. When you get really good people, they know they're really good. You don't have to baby people's egos so much. What really matters is the work. And everybody knows that. That's all that matters — the work.

People are being counted on to do specific pieces of the puzzle. And the most important thing you can do for somebody who's really good and who's really being counted on is to point out to them when their work isn't good enough. To do it very clearly. To articulate why. To get them back on track.

You need to do that in a way that does not call into question your confidence in their abilities, but leaves not too much room for interpretation that the work they've done for *this particular thing* is not good enough to support the goal of the team. That's a hard thing to do. I've always taken a very direct approach. If you talk to people who've worked with me, the really good people have found it beneficial. Some people have hated it.

### Why the Softening Fails

Most managers soften feedback to protect the feelings of the person receiving it. The impulse is decent. The effect is worse. When you wrap a criticism in three compliments, the A-player hears the compliments, misses the criticism, and keeps shipping work that isn't good enough. Your kindness in the moment becomes their confusion for the next month. Eventually they fall further than they would have if you'd been direct on day one.

Softening also insults the listener. It says: I don't trust you to hear the truth. For a person whose professional identity is built on shipping great work, that's a deeper injury than the direct version.

### Testing Conviction

Many times in an interview, I will purposely upset someone. I'll criticize their prior work. I'll find out what they worked on and say, "God, that really turned out to be a bomb. That really turned out to be a bozo product. Why did you work on that?"

I want to see what people are like under pressure. I want to see if they just fold or if they have firm conviction, belief, and pride in what they did. The worst thing that someone can do in an interview is agree with me and knuckle under. We need people who will push back. Who will defend their ideas when they believe in them. Who won't just tell you what you want to hear.

I don't care about being right. I just care about success. You'll find people who will tell you that I had a very strong opinion, and they present evidence to the contrary, and five minutes later I completely change my mind. I don't mind being wrong. I'll admit that I'm wrong a lot. What matters is that we do the right thing. And you can only do the right thing if people tell you when you're wrong.

### Direct ≠ Cruel

Direct feedback is about the work. Not about the person. "This deck isn't good enough to ship — here's why" is direct. "You're not a good designer" is cruel and unhelpful. The distinction is not subtle. One is information that lets the person improve the work. The other is a verdict on their identity.

The best direct feedback has three parts: the specific thing that isn't working, why it isn't working, and what good would look like. Without the why, the person can't fix it. Without the "what good looks like," the person is guessing. Without the specificity, the person fills in the gap with worse interpretations than you meant.

## How to Apply

1. **Call it clearly.** Don't wrap. Don't sandwich. "This work isn't good enough. Here's why." If the message requires decoding, it'll be decoded wrong.

2. **Point at the work, not the person.** "This deck doesn't communicate the story yet" — not "you're not a great presenter." The former is fixable; the latter is a verdict. Always critique the work.

3. **Say why, with specifics.** "The first three slides bury the real news. The data on slide 5 contradicts the headline. The ask at the end is vague." That's feedback. "It didn't land" is noise.

4. **Say what good looks like.** Describe the bar clearly enough that the person could re-attempt the work and know whether they hit it. If you can't describe it, you don't know it yet either, and the criticism isn't ready to be delivered.

5. **Reserve it for people who can take it.** This approach works with A-players and people who share the commitment to the work. It fails with people who are fragile, junior, or in personal crisis. For those, scaffold more. Directness is a tool; it's not the only tool.

6. **Don't back off when they push back.** The point is a real conversation about the work. If they defend the work and the defense is good, change your mind — you might be wrong. If the defense is weak, name that too. What you don't do is apologize for having had an opinion.

7. **Separate confidence from criticism.** Be explicit: "I'm criticizing this work, not my confidence in you. You shipped great stuff last quarter; this one isn't there yet; let's fix it." That sentence, literally said, prevents the common mistake of hearing critique as withdrawal of trust.

8. **Be willing to be wrong.** If you're directly critical, you also have to be directly persuadable. Five minutes after the critique, if the evidence says you were wrong, say so. That's what makes directness a two-way street instead of a one-way power move.

## Examples

**Situation:** A top designer's last three mocks have been below her usual bar. The manager wants to say something but is afraid of seeming harsh.
**Application:** Sit down with her. Specific: "These three mocks don't have the polish your work usually has. The typography is inconsistent, the spacing feels default-grid, the color hierarchy doesn't match the product story. That's not your bar and I know it isn't. What's going on?" That's direct, specific, pointed at the work, and opens a real conversation.
**Result:** She either tells you what's blocking her (personal situation, unclear brief, overloaded), or she pushes back (the brief was different than I thought), or she agrees and fixes it. All three outcomes are better than the "these look great, just a few small notes" approach that lets the decay continue.

**Situation:** A junior engineer who's only been on the team three months ships a PR that's clearly rushed and buggy. The lead is tempted to "go direct."
**Application:** Don't. Directness requires a base — they need to know the bar, have the context, and trust that critique is about the work. A junior three months in doesn't have that yet. Scaffold more. "Here's the bar. Here's what's missing in this PR. Let's go through it together." Over time, as they build both competence and trust, you can escalate to full directness. Not yet.
**Result:** The engineer learns the bar with support. In six to twelve months, if they're an A-player, you can shift to direct. Applied too early, direct feedback would have been experienced as contempt and you'd have lost a good engineer who just needed more onboarding.

**Situation:** A VP is presenting a strategic plan to the CEO. The plan is defensible but not great. Everyone else in the room is nodding.
**Application:** Say it. "This plan is the safe version of what we could do. It's defensible. It's also not exciting, and I don't think it will get us to where we need to be in two years. Here's what I think would be more ambitious — and here's the specific risks I'd need to understand." That's direct, specific, and opens the real conversation instead of the polite one.
**Result:** Either the VP defends the plan and you change your mind (they knew something you didn't), or the plan gets replaced with the more ambitious version, or you compromise toward a third option. Any of those is better than the room nodding through an average plan.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Sandwich the critique between compliments.
**Why:** The listener hears the compliments, misses the critique, and keeps shipping mediocre work. The "kindness" is cruelty on a delay.

**Don't:** Soften the message until it's unclear.
**Why:** If the person has to decode what you meant, they'll decode it wrong. Unclear feedback produces unclear fixes. Be clear at the cost of being uncomfortable for ten seconds.

**Don't:** Use directness as cover for contempt.
**Why:** "I'm just being direct" is what contemptuous people say. Directness is about the work. Contempt is about the person. The test: would you say the same thing if the person were more senior than you?

**Don't:** Go direct without specifics.
**Why:** "This is bad" is not feedback; it's an insult. "This is bad because X and Y; good would look like Z" is feedback. The specifics are what make the message useful.

**Don't:** Deliver public-performance critique.
**Why:** Direct feedback in private is a gift. Direct feedback in public is a power move. Don't confuse the two.

**Don't:** Apologize afterwards for having had the opinion.
**Why:** If you apologize for the directness itself, you've undone it. Stand behind the content of the critique. If you were wrong about the substance, say that. Don't apologize for having stood for a standard.
