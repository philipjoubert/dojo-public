# Undiscovered Talent

## When to Use
- You're building anything pre-Series C and you can't outbid Google, Meta, or OpenAI.
- You're staring at a shortlist of "obvious" candidates with perfect resumes and wondering why each one feels wrong.
- You're trying to figure out where to source the next ten hires.
- A proven senior hire just turned you down for more money somewhere else.

## Fails When
- You apply it to the CFO prepping your IPO, or the general counsel keeping you out of jail. Those are value-protection roles. Experience is the point. Don't be clever there.
- You interpret "no one over 30" as literal. It's Peter's shorthand for a pricing argument, not an age discrimination policy. The real rule: don't hire people the market has already correctly priced.
- You use "undiscovered" as permission to lower your bar. Undiscovered talent is harder to find than senior talent, not easier. The assessment work is more, not less.

## Core Concept

You can't win a bidding war against Google. Stop trying.

Peter told me this on a jog around the Stanford campus in 2000. He said you can't hire anyone over 30. What he meant: by 30, everyone on the planet knows how to assess you pretty accurately. There's enough data — resume, LinkedIn, references — that the market converges on a consensus view of what you're worth. The moment there's a consensus view, you're priced. And once you're priced, Google is going to spend a lot of money on you. Or OpenAI. Or Meta. You can't outspend them. You shouldn't try. The people motivated by that compensation may not even be the right builders for what you're doing.

The game is finding people the large organizations don't know how to process. People who haven't been priced yet. It's a little bit like drafting elite athletes out of college — Derek Jeter in spring training, not Derek Jeter in year 15. The skill is seeing the signal before the market sees it. The people who become famous PayPal Mafia founders were interns when I found them. Max Rhodes was the smoothie intern at Square. If I'd waited until he had a track record, I'd have been bidding against every fund in the Valley.

Undiscovered talent has a specific profile. Something to prove. A screw loose — not in the dangerous way, in the productive way. A spike on some dimension that doesn't fit the generic resume template. A side project. A weird interest they went deep on. A sport they played obsessively. A question they ask that nobody else asks. When someone is world-class at anything, there's almost always an anomaly somewhere early. Your job is to look for anomalies, not to check boxes. The checkbox people are the ones Google is already buying.

Where do you find them? Mine your existing network first. On day one every new hire writes down the five or ten most impressive people they know. You reach out to those people that week. Then you do gene pool engineering — pick 15 or 20 companies whose people have the skills you want, and systematically surf LinkedIn for people with one or two jobs, a few years tenure, a steep trajectory. Email them yourself from the CEO seat. Hire interns. Recruit from your soccer team, your Barry's class, the Yelp Elite party, anywhere you meet humans. In a hot market, you find talent wherever you can.

The highest compliment is "relentlessly resourceful." Paul Graham's phrase. It's the right one. What you're looking for is the ability to positively manipulate the world around them. You see it at the micro level — how they handle a small obstacle — and you bet it scales.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop competing in auctions you can't win.** Delete the senior ex-FAANG candidates from your pipeline unless they're a value-protection hire or a close personal friend who wants to join. You're wasting cycles.
2. **Mine your network one degree out.** Every new hire's first week: write down the five to ten most impressive people you know. You, the CEO, email each one. Personally. That week.
3. **Do gene pool engineering.** Pick 15 or 20 companies with the skills you need. Crawl LinkedIn. Target people with one or two jobs, several years tenure, upward trajectory. CEO-to-candidate email. This is systematic, not opportunistic.
4. **Hire interns aggressively.** The best interns become founders. Intercept them at 16-to-22 before they know they can found. The smoothie test is the entry ramp.
5. **Look for anomalies, not boxes.** When you read a resume or meet a candidate, hunt for what's unexpected. Built something strange on the side? Quit a good job to do something weird? Asked a question no one asks? That's the signal. A perfectly expected candidate is a perfectly expected hire.
6. **Ask risk-taking questions from any domain.** Most of the best hires I ever made showed risk-taking in something unrelated to work — a sport, a side project, a move. The hire I made off my soccer team had an aggressive goalkeeping style. I bet that translated. It did.
7. **Use the "something to prove" filter.** The strongest early-career hires have a chip on their shoulder. Didn't go to the right school. Got passed over somewhere. Grew up without the advantages. That chip is fuel. Don't be polite about it — lean into it.
8. **Watch for future founders and hire them.** Someone says "I want to start a company in two years" — that's a plus, not a minus. Your job is to make the company so compelling they stay longer. If they leave, that's on you for not making the company interesting enough.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder wants to hire a Head of Growth. The obvious candidates are Growth leaders at Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash. Each one has four offers. Budget is limited.
**Application:** Don't hire any of them. Do gene pool engineering on the second and third hires at those companies — the people who actually built the growth engines two layers below the VP. Find the ones with one or two jobs, three to five years in, steep trajectory. Email them yourself. Sell them on running growth at your place, earlier than they'd run it anywhere else.
**Result:** Hire someone who is two years from being the VP at Uber and pay them a fraction. They ship more in six months than the obvious candidate would have shipped in eighteen.

**Situation:** A founder meets a McKinsey associate at a dinner. The first instinct is to pass — McKinsey means "risk-averse, boring, process-heavy." Not the profile.
**Application:** Look for the anomaly. Ask about what they did outside work. A weird side project. An aggressive sport. Anything that shows they can positively manipulate the world. If you find it, bet on the spike, not the resume. I did this with Jeff. McKinsey guy, crazy aggressive soccer goalie. I bet on the goalie trait. He turned out to be one of the best hires I ever made.
**Result:** You get someone hungry and high-horsepower whose pedigree hides the upside. The market hasn't priced the anomaly.

**Situation:** It's Friday and you're about to move offices. You need someone to set up the new space by Monday 9am — Wi-Fi, furniture, lease, signage, the full stack. You ask your chief of staff.
**Application:** This is the smoothie test at a higher scope. The office being ready on Monday at 9am is not the actual goal — the test is whether this person can execute under pressure on something novel. If they pull it off, you give them the next bigger thing. That's how Jack at Square got progressively larger scope. Undiscovered talent is built by progressively raising the ceiling until you find where they break.
**Result:** You discover real capacity on small bets before you risk anything important.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to outspend Google for proven talent.
**Why:** You can't. Even if you could, the people motivated by that much compensation are the wrong builders. Stop entering auctions you can't win.

**Don't:** Filter for industry passion.
**Why:** "Do you wake up passionate about supply chain software?" is an arrogant question. Nobody does. Your job is to convey why your company is the most compelling thing this person could do with their life. Patrick Collison is right on this. If your recruiting strategy depends on candidates already caring about your industry, you've outsourced your pitch to Google's HR department.

**Don't:** Screen out candidates because they say they want to be founders.
**Why:** These people have higher ceilings, not lower ones. They have ambition. They have the trait you want. If they leave in two years, it's because you failed to make the company interesting enough to keep them. Eric at Ramp puts it right: worry when that caliber of person stops applying, not when they leave to found.

**Don't:** Filter out people with egos or a screw loose.
**Why:** Almost everyone world-class at anything has an ego. The weirdest people at PayPal created the most value. If you screen for "normal" and "low-drama," you will hire ammunition and miss the barrels. Max Levchin told me I was the most normal person at PayPal. He meant it as an insult. He was right.

**Don't:** Only track false positives — the bad hires you made.
**Why:** False negatives — the great people you passed on who became amazing elsewhere — are arguably more important. Most companies never track these. Your hiring system is calibrated blind. Keep a file on the passes. Revisit it once a year. Adjust.

**Don't:** Outsource the search to a recruiter.
**Why:** Recruiters drive inputs — they ensure you contact N people per day. That has some value for tempo. But recruiters use the same screens that Google uses, so they surface the same priced candidates. They will not find you undiscovered talent. That is a founder-and-CEO job. You cannot delegate it.

**Don't:** Require remote-first for an early-stage company hiring undiscovered talent.
**Why:** Undiscovered talent is early-career. They learn by osmosis — watching, overhearing, absorbing. Osmosis does not work remotely. If you hire undiscovered talent remote, they stay undiscovered.

> "Peter Thiel taught me: you can't hire anybody over 30. By the time you're 30, everybody on the planet knows how to assess you pretty accurately. Google is going to spend a lot of money on this person."

> "You have to be able to find people that organizations like Google don't know how to process, don't know how to evaluate, don't know how to assess. That's a skill in and of itself."

> "It's a little bit like drafting elite athletes out of college to play baseball, football, basketball."

> "Anywhere you go, you might run into people who have talent. I've hired people off my soccer team. I've hired people I've met at Barry's."

> "These people have more potential. My job is to make sure the company is so interesting, so valuable, and such a challenge for any of the employees, they don't want to leave."

> "Very few companies track the false negatives very well — the people they didn't hire that could have been great. Those actually arguably are more important than the mistakes you make."

> "Most recruiting at large organizations becomes a homogeneous function. If you understand why this person is going to get thrown into a black box and not get processed accurately, it's pretty easy. Younger people, by definition, have less data. It's like FICO scores for employment. By the time you're over 30-some-odd, there's so many data points that this black-box machine is going to process you like many other people. If there's no data points, it's very hard for a black-box machine to evaluate you. So there is alpha, by definition, for people that have no data points."

> "They're also not maybe the people you want. It's a salary cap. Most sports these days have salary caps. When you're a startup, not only do you have a salary cap, you probably have one-tenth the salary cap of the people you're competing with. So you've got to figure out how to leverage less assets to more success."
