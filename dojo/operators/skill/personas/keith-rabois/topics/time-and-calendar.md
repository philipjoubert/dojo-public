# Time and the Calendar as a Strategy Document

## When to Use
- You're a CEO, exec, or founder and your week keeps getting eaten by meetings you didn't design.
- You've told your board recruiting is the #1 priority but can't remember the last time you ran a recruiting pipeline yourself.
- It's Sunday night and your Monday-to-Friday is already fully booked before the week has started.
- You're coaching someone into an exec role for the first time and they're drowning.
- You feel busy but can't point to progress on anything that actually matters.

## Fails When
- You treat the calendar audit as a one-off. It's a weekly discipline, like weighing yourself. One snapshot tells you nothing; the pattern tells you everything.
- You use "I need to batch" as cover for avoiding the one hard meeting that week. Batching is about cognitive tax, not courage.
- You block the calendar but don't defend it. Open calendars get invaded. If you don't have the nerve to tell people no, the block means nothing.
- You audit but don't change anything. The only point of the audit is to delete or move time. If nothing changes, you've just generated a spreadsheet.

## Core Concept

Peter taught me this in 2002 and it's the single most load-bearing idea I ever got from him: people systematically undervalue their time. That's the sentence. Your time is your most valuable asset. Not your cap table, not your bank account, not your social capital. Your calendar. And every CEO I've ever worked with has a calendar that is a bald-faced contradiction of their stated priorities. They tell me recruiting is number one. Then we pull up Google Calendar and recruiting is maybe 8% of their week. That's not a disagreement about tactics. That's a disagreement with themselves.

The calendar is a strategy document. It reveals what you actually believe, as opposed to what you tell your board. If you want to know a CEO's real priorities, don't listen to the all-hands, pull up the calendar for the last month. Categorize every block. Then put the list next to the stated priorities. It never matches. Not once, in fifteen years of doing this with people, have I seen it match on the first pass. Recruiting is the most common disconnect — almost every CEO will say it's in their top three, and almost no CEO allocates more than single-digit percent of their time to it.

The countermove is three-part and simple. One, design the calendar on Sunday night. Before the week starts, identify the top three priorities, and allocate 80%+ of your calendar to them. Two, keep half the calendar empty on Sunday night. Vinod used to teach that — an empty calendar on Sunday night means you can be proactive, because Monday through Friday will fill itself with other people's priorities whether you want them to or not. Three, every Sunday do what Jensen Huang does: scan the week and cancel any meeting that isn't serving a current priority. Just kill them. Without apology. The people in those meetings will thank you later, because you weren't adding value there anyway. If half your time is already gone on Sunday night, you're probably going to have a bad week. That's the sentence I repeat to every founder I invest in.

## How to Apply

1. **Sunday night, write down your top three priorities for the week.** On paper. Not in your head. If you can't compress them into three, you don't actually have priorities, you have a to-do list.
2. **Pull up your calendar and categorize every block by priority bucket.** Priority 1, Priority 2, Priority 3, or Other. Everything in Other is suspect.
3. **Delete or move everything in Other unless it has a structural reason to stay.** If a meeting shows up in Other and you can't name a reason it's non-negotiable, it's gone. Ping the organizer. Reschedule for next quarter. Decline.
4. **Verify 80%+ of designed time is on the top three.** If it's not, you're lying to yourself about your priorities. Either fix the calendar or admit the real priorities.
5. **Protect at least one long uninterrupted block for deep work.** Mathilde Collin at Front blocks Thursday afternoons. Pick yours. Defend it.
6. **Batch the similar tasks.** All 1:1s one day. Coffee meetings in a single afternoon. Context-switching is a cognitive tax you pay in quality of thinking, and you pay it every time.
7. **Quarterly, audit the past quarter.** Go back through three months of calendar. Categorize every block. This is the honest mirror. Look at the disconnect. Fix it in the next quarter.
8. **When you get to the point where half your calendar is gone on Sunday night despite all of this, you need a COO.** That's the actual signal — not revenue, not headcount. Calendar saturation.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series B CEO tells me recruiting is priority #1. Can't close senior hires. Pipeline is dead.
**Application:** I ask her to rank her priorities on a piece of paper. Then we open her Google Calendar and add up the blocks. Recruiting is 6% of her time. The biggest block is customer success — 28% — which she doesn't even list in her top five.
**Result:** She deletes the standing customer success review (her VP CS runs it fine without her) and replaces it with two 90-minute exec candidate blocks per week. Closes three senior hires in six weeks. Nothing else about her company changed except the calendar.

**Situation:** A founder is exhausted and producing nothing. Calendar is completely full Monday through Friday, 8am to 8pm.
**Application:** Jensen's Sunday purge. We go through the week and delete half the meetings on sight. Any recurring meeting that doesn't tie to a current priority — gone. Any coffee he accepted out of politeness — canceled. Any meeting where he wasn't the decision-maker and didn't need to be — moved to a written update.
**Result:** He reclaims about 14 hours a week. Uses half of that for deep work on the one product problem that had been stuck for three months. Ships the fix in a month.

**Situation:** A first-time CEO two years in, company growing fast, says fundraising is the #1 priority for the next quarter because the round needs to close.
**Application:** Same drill. We pull up the calendar. Fundraising blocks are 11%. Internal meetings and Slack triage are 60%. I tell him: for the next six weeks, fundraising gets 40% of your designed time, not 11%. The internal meetings delegate to your COO or cancel. Your job this quarter is to close the round. That's it.
**Result:** Round closes four weeks earlier than the original timeline. Nothing about the deal got better. He just stopped pretending he could close a round in 11% of his time.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let the calendar be assembled by your assistant or by inbound requests.
**Why:** That's the definition of a reactive calendar. Your assistant doesn't know your priorities well enough to design the week. You do. The calendar is a CEO-level artifact. Outsourcing it is outsourcing your strategy.

**Don't:** Keep recurring meetings alive because they've always existed.
**Why:** Recurring meetings are the biggest source of calendar rot. They were set up to solve a problem six months ago. The problem is gone. The meeting is still there, and everyone in it has forgotten why. Jensen deletes these on sight. So should you.

**Don't:** Use "coffee meetings" as a category that floats across the week.
**Why:** One coffee is thirty minutes. Six coffees scattered across the week is four and a half hours plus six context switches, which functionally wipes a day. Batch them into one afternoon or say no.

**Don't:** Tell people recruiting is your top priority and then let your week prove otherwise.
**Why:** It's self-discrediting. Your team can see the calendar. Your VPs can see the calendar. Your board can see the calendar. The gap between stated priority and allocated time is public and corrosive. If recruiting really isn't the top priority right now, say so. If it is, act like it.

**Don't:** Conflate "responding to Slack" with "working."
**Why:** Slack is not a priority. It's an inbox for other people's priorities. If you're in Slack, you're serving someone else's calendar, which means you're not defending your own.

**Don't:** Assume you can always add one more meeting "if it's important."
**Why:** The Days-Since-Launch counter applies here. Every day you spend on a not-top-three priority is a day you didn't spend on the top three. There is no free meeting. The cost is always paid somewhere, usually in the form of the A+ problem that didn't get solved this week.
