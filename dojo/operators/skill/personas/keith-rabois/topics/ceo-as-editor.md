# CEO as Editor

## When to Use
- Any moment you catch yourself actually producing the artifact. Writing the tweet. Drafting the job description. Fixing the landing page copy. Authoring the support macro. Redlining the contract. Writing the board deck slide. Writing the all-hands script.
- When evaluating whether a given function actually has the right leader in it. If you are writing in their department, their department is broken.
- When the company's surface area feels stitched-together — the website sounds like one company, the recruiting page sounds like another, the support emails sound like a third.

## Fails When
- **You are the fifth employee.** You have to write. The writers come later. Don't confuse "I edit" with "I do nothing" at seed stage.
- **You edit into oblivion.** Truman got asked what the hardest part of being president was. He said: learning to sign a bad letter. When something is not existential, let it ship at 80%. Learn to sign a bad letter.
- **You start rewriting instead of editing.** The minute you stop asking clarifying questions and start producing the work product, you just fired the person whose job that is — emotionally, if not formally.

## Core Concept

Your job as an executive is to edit, not to write. Every time you do something you should think through in your brain: am I writing or am I editing? You should immediately be able to tell the difference. This is the best metaphor I have ever seen in 14 years of running stuff. Jack used it at Square. It's natural, it's transmissible, and every person on the team can apply it to themselves.

An editor does not produce the work product. A reporter writes the story. The editor asks clarifying questions, simplifies, extracts, deletes, occasionally reorganizes, requires follow-up. The most important task of an editor is to simplify simplify simplify, and that usually means omitting things. The more you simplify, the better people perform. People cannot understand and keep track of a complicated set of initiatives. You have to distill the company to one, two, or three things that every single employee can repeat in their sleep.

It's okay to write once in a while in any part of the organization. But if you are writing on a consistent basis in marketing, in legal, on product, on customer acquisition, on HR, on recruiting — there is a fundamental problem with that team. That is the diagnostic. You are not being heroic by covering for them. You are masking the fact that you have the wrong person in the role. Andy Grove estimated 30 to 50 percent performance improvement from this kind of clarity. That number is not small.

The second thing editors do is enforce consistent voice. Pick up any article in The Economist. It feels like one person wrote the whole magazine. Your company should feel exactly the same way — your website, your recruiting page, your PR releases, your packaging if you have a physical product, your support emails, your onboarding, your internal tools. All of it should feel like one person wrote it. This is extraordinarily difficult. Even Apple under Steve never got the internal tools to feel like the products. You will not hit 100 percent. You aim for it anyway.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the writing-vs-editing test every single time.** Before you touch a document or open a Figma file or hit reply, ask: am I writing or am I editing? If writing, ask why. Is this because I want to? Because it's faster? Or because no one else in this function can produce this at the quality the company requires? The answer determines whether you have a people problem.

2. **Track red ink month over month.** One measure of how well you are communicating what matters is how much red ink you are pulling out. A bad day is fine. A bad week is fine. But if next month you are pulling out more red ink than last month, and the next quarter more than this, you are regressing. Your team is not internalizing what good looks like.

3. **Do the voice audit.** Print your landing page, a recruiting page, a support email macro, your last PR release, a product spec, an investor update. Lay them on a table. Would a stranger believe these came from one company? Where does it drift? That is where your next conversation goes.

4. **Simplify every initiative to one sentence.** Don't accept the excuse of complexity. You can change the world in 140 characters. You can build the most important companies in history in a very simple to describe concept. You can market products in less than 50 characters. If a teammate tells you their initiative can't be compressed because it is too complicated, they are wrong. Sit them down and help them compress it. Then hand them the red pen.

5. **Ask clarifying questions in every meeting.** When people present to you, your real job is to find the one or two places where the logic is ambiguous and probe. "Do you mean this or that? Do you have an example? Seven days a week or six? Where is our competitive advantage here?" Most investors ask a billion questions. A good editor narrows to the two or three questions that matter and forces a crisp answer on those.

## Examples

**Situation:** Square has a recruiting page that doesn't feel like Square.
**Application:** Don't rewrite the recruiting page. Go to the leader whose team owns it. Print the recruiting page and print a product page side by side. Ask: why do these feel different? Then: who on your team is supposed to hold the voice? If the answer is "no one," that's the real problem, and you fix the org chart, not the copy.
**Result:** The page gets fixed by the right person, and the problem doesn't come back six months later with a different surface.

**Situation:** A first-time founder is writing most of the marketing emails themselves because the marketing hire is "not quite getting it."
**Application:** The diagnostic is sharp. Either you misread that person's task-relevant maturity — they cannot do this job and you need to replace them — or they can do it and you haven't given them the voice to aim at. You have a week to figure out which. You do not have a year.
**Result:** Either the hire levels up or they're out. What you do not do is keep quietly writing the emails for another six months while telling yourself you'll deal with it.

**Situation:** Kyle at Square wants to run an "Inner Square" marketing program. Keith has ten years of experience saying it won't move the needle meaningfully. The consequence of failure is low.
**Application:** This is editing, not writing. Keith could overrule and kill the program. Instead, he lets Kyle run it. Kyle learns to measure it, learns what moves the metric and what doesn't, and develops his own judgment. The company loses a small amount of operational complexity. Kyle gains ten years of pattern recognition faster.
**Result:** The program doesn't meaningfully move the metric — exactly as predicted — but Kyle is now a better marketer and Keith didn't burn social capital playing "I'm the boss."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Rewrite the deck at midnight before the board meeting.
**Why:** You just told your deck owner they cannot own this. Next quarter they won't even try. You are creating learned helplessness in your own senior team.

**Don't:** Edit everything to the same 100 percent standard.
**Why:** Truman was right. Learn to sign a bad letter. If you polish every artifact to perfection, you are either doing your directs' jobs or you are signaling that nothing ships without your fingerprint. Both are poison at scale.

**Don't:** Cover for a weak leader by writing on behalf of their function.
**Why:** The org sees you doing it. They stop bringing problems up. The weak leader stays in seat longer than they should, and the best people on that team leave because they have no editor.

**Don't:** Confuse "I edit" with "I don't produce anything." Founders write the first dashboard on a whiteboard. Founders write the first value proposition. Founders write the seed pitch. Writing is correct at the zero-to-one moment. Editing is correct at the one-to-ten. Know which one you're in.
**Why:** "CEO as editor" is a pattern for operating a company, not for starting one. Applied too early it just means you're abdicating.

**Don't:** Accept "the business is complicated" as a reason for a complicated strategy document.
**Why:** You can build the most important companies in history in a very simple to describe concept. Complexity in the narrative is a tell that the author hasn't done the thinking yet.

**Don't:** Let voice drift because you're busy with "more important things."
**Why:** There are no more important things. Recruits, customers, and press all infer the company from the surface. A company with three different voices on three different pages looks like three different companies to the outside world. That directly hurts close rates on hires and on deals.
