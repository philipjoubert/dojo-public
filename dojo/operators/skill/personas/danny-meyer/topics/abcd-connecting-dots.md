# ABCD — Always Be Collecting (and Connecting) Dots

## When to Use
- Building or improving a customer-facing business that depends on repeat patronage and word-of-mouth.
- Designing your CRM, customer notes system, or guest-preference tracking.
- Coaching a team that interacts with customers but treats every interaction as a one-off.
- Daily practice for any operator who wants to actually know their customers as humans, not as records.
- Sales pipeline development — the same discipline that makes a great host makes a great salesperson.

## Fails When
- **Collecting becomes surveillance.** ABCD only works when the gathering is *in service of the guest's experience* — to remember what they like, to surprise them, to make them feel seen. Used as data extraction for the seller's metrics, it produces creepy, brittle relationships and erodes trust.
- **You collect but never connect.** Most teams collect dots and never use them. A guest-preference field that no manager ever reads is worse than no field at all — it's the *appearance* of attention without the substance.
- **The dots are private to the founder.** If only the founder reads the reservation sheets and connects the dots, the system collapses the moment the founder isn't there. The discipline must be transmissible.

## Core Concept

The lesson came from fly-fishing. In Woody Creek, Colorado, a young guide picked up a rock from the streambed, turned it over, and showed Danny dozens of tiny aquatic insects hatching on its underside — the data that told him exactly which fly to tie to catch the trout actually feeding that morning.

> "There was a world of information under that rock, if only one knew or cared enough to look for it."

That became the central operating discipline of every Danny restaurant: *turn over the rocks*. There's always a story behind the story if you look for it. The guest's glance at her watch, the untouched onion rings, the curious gaze at the artwork, the appreciative tap on the wineglass — every one of these is a rock. Underneath each is information that lets you connect with the person, learn what they need, and create the small move that turns a transaction into a relationship.

The discipline has a memorable handle:

> "The best way to do this is to first gather as much information as I can about our guests. I call this collecting dots. In fact, I urge our managers to ABCD — always be collecting dots."

But collecting is only half. The point of collecting dots is to **connect** them — to use what you learn to create *meaningful connections that make other people feel good*.

> "Dots are information. The more information you collect, the more frequently you can make meaningful connections that can make other people feel good and give you an edge in business... If I don't turn over the rocks, I won't see the dots. If I don't collect the dots, I can't connect the dots."

A worked example: the maître d' notices on the reservation sheet that a regular has dined at three other USHG restaurants this week. That's a dot. The maître d' connects it by quietly thanking the guest for the loyalty and pre-ordering them their preferred wine. The guest feels remembered. They tell two friends about the welcome. The dots become the relationship.

The deeper version is *benevolent manipulation* — using the collected dots to seat a publisher near another publisher, a chef near a food journalist, a tourist couple near someone who can recommend museums, so that the room creates value for the guests beyond the meal itself. *"I call this planting like seeds in like gardens in order to extend our community."*

The framework also names a specific test for when to approach a table — the **eye-bisection rule**:

> "If I see that the direction of their eyes intersects at the center of the table, I know that they are actively engaged with one another and I'm confident that everything is fine. This is an inopportune time to visit. Whenever I see that the direction of someone's eyes is not bisecting the center of the table, then a visit may be warranted."

Same logic applies to email, support chat, customer success calls, sales follow-up. Read the room. Approach when the signal says yes; leave alone when it says no.

## How to Apply

1. **Build the *daily walk*.** Whatever your business, walk through the room (the inbox, the support queue, the order pipeline) every day looking for dots. The discipline is not occasional research — it is constant low-level pattern recognition.

2. **Capture dots in a system everyone can use.** Customer notes in your CRM. Guest preferences in your reservation system. Detailed reservation sheets that the morning team reads before service. *"All this adds up to a gold mine of information, which allows us to connect all sorts of dots."* The dots have to be *recorded*, *visible*, and *actively used* — not just collected.

3. **Read the dots before the moment of contact.** Before service, the manager reads the reservation sheet for the night. Before the sales call, the rep reads the prospect's notes. Before the support chat, the agent reads the customer's history. The pre-read is what turns *generic interaction* into *seeing the person*.

4. **Connect dots into actions, not just observations.** Knowing the regular's birthday is on the sheet is *collecting*. Calling them in the morning to thank them for choosing your restaurant for it is *connecting*. The collected dot that produces no action is wasted.

5. **Use the eye-bisection rule (or its analogue).** Read the room before you speak. The well-engaged guest doesn't want to be interrupted; the disengaged guest may welcome the visit. The discipline is restraint plus initiative — *initiative when the signal says yes, restraint when it says no.*

6. **Practice "Do you know So-and-so?".** The classic dot-connecting move. Ask where the guest is from, what they do, where they went to school. Often you find a connection — same school, same hometown, same favourite place. The dot becomes the relationship. Same approach works in business development.

7. **Look at the dots in aggregate.** Over time the dots reveal patterns — the regular who's drifted away, the demographic that's growing, the dish that's getting less popular, the day-part that's softening. The micro-discipline (per-guest dots) feeds the macro-strategy (where the business is heading).

8. **Make the discipline transmissible.** Train every host, every manager, every server in the practice. The system doesn't depend on the founder being present — it depends on every team member being a dot-collector and dot-connector.

## Examples

**Situation: Eve of LSAT, 1984. Danny is in a foul mood at dinner with his uncle Richard at Elio's, dreading the test. Uncle Richard turns to him: *"You know you don't want to be a lawyer. Why don't you just do what you've been thinking about doing your whole life?"***

> "What do you mean, 'What's that'? Since you were a child, all you've ever talked or thought about is food and restaurants. Why don't you just open a restaurant?"

**Application:** This is dot-connecting at the family-dinner level. Uncle Richard had been collecting dots about Danny — interests, conversations, energy, what made his eyes light up — for twenty years. At the right moment he connected them: *the dots all point to restaurants, why are you about to take an LSAT?* The connected dots redirected an entire life.

**Result:** Danny took the LSAT the next morning relaxed, never applied to law school, and started building a restaurant career. The dot-connecting moment was the inflection point.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Collect dots without using them.
*Why:* A guest-preference field that no manager ever reads is worse than no field. It promises attention and delivers nothing. The data has to feed action, or the discipline rots into theatre.

**Don't:** Use the dots to pitch.
*Why:* ABCD works when the dots are connected *in service of the guest*, not in service of the upsell. *"I notice you celebrated your anniversary here last year — congratulations"* lands beautifully. *"I notice you celebrated your anniversary here last year — would you like to upgrade to the chef's tasting menu?"* lands as data-driven sleaze. The intent is felt.

**Don't:** Make the practice founder-only.
*Why:* If only the founder reads the reservation sheets, only the founder can connect the dots, and the system collapses outside the founder's presence. The discipline has to be transmissible — every host, every manager, every server is a dot-collector and dot-connector.

**Don't:** Approach every guest at every meal.
*Why:* The eye-bisection rule matters. A well-engaged table doesn't want a visit; a visit there is *interruption*, not hospitality. Read the signal first; act second. The discipline includes restraint.

**Don't:** Confuse data collection with attention.
*Why:* A CRM full of structured fields is not the same as actually seeing the person. The fields support the attention; they don't replace it. The maître d' who reads the file *and* looks up at the guest is doing the work. The maître d' who reads the file with their head down is doing performance theatre.

**Don't:** Skip the small dots because they "don't matter."
*Why:* The dots that produce the biggest moments are usually the smallest ones — a glance, a wineglass tap, an offhand comment about a Midwest dessert. The discipline is to take *every* dot seriously enough to record and consider, even if you only act on a fraction of them.
