# The Four Quadrants of Performance

## When to Use

- You're trying to decide what to do about a specific person on the team and the question is harder than it looks.
- You're noticing that you keep "saving" certain people because firing them would feel like *you* failed.
- You're auditing the whole team to see who you've been over-investing in and who you've been quietly ignoring.

## Fails When

- **You use it to rate, not to act.** The point isn't to label someone "won't / can't" and walk away. Each quadrant pairs with an action point and a timeframe. No timeframe = no movement.
- **You hide the model from the team.** Danny puts these quadrants on mirrors in the staff locker rooms. If your team can't self-assess against the same model you're using, you've built a secret performance system, which becomes a fairness problem the first time someone gets surprised.
- **You let "can" mean "good enough."** Technical competence is the floor, not the ceiling. The whelmers framework lives next door — somebody can be in the "can / will" quadrant and still be a whelmer if "can" only means barely-meets-spec.

## Core Concept

Two axes. The horizontal axis is **technical** — *can* / *can't*. The vertical axis is **emotional** — *will* / *won't*. That gives you four quadrants, and each one points to a different action.

> "Some restaurateurs in California gave me this model that I just absolutely love and we've turned it into something that we do. They basically created a four-quadrant thing. You've seen these axes many times with a y-axis and an x-axis... someone's technical abilities — that can and can't — and then you have somebody's emotional willingness or aptitude — that's will and won't... So if you have someone who *will but can't*, that's a very different thing than someone who *won't and can*."
> — Danny Meyer, *Tim Ferriss Show #665*

The four readings:

- **Can and will.** Your sunflowers. Celebrate them, water them, replicate them. The trap here is that you ignore them because they're easy — Johnny always gets it right, so you stop talking to Johnny. Wrong move. The flowers are exactly who you should be spending your attention on. Their growth pulls everyone else toward the sun.
- **Will but can't.** This person has the heart for the work but doesn't yet have the chops. **Train them.** This is the highest-leverage development move you have. Specific timeframe, specific skill, specific check-in. If they don't progress in the window, the question becomes whether they're trainable in the role at all — and that's a different conversation than firing them for attitude.
- **Can but won't.** This is the one that breaks managers. Technically capable, emotionally checked out (or worse, openly cynical, or skunking the team). The trap is that their output looks fine in isolation, so you keep them. But the *won't* is contagious — it teaches the rest of the team that effort is optional. **Have the conversation.** Name the gap. Specific behaviors, specific feedback. Watch what they do with it. If it shifts, water that flower. If it doesn't, the next conversation writes itself.
- **Won't and can't.** Easy. Why are they still here? You probably already know. The harder question is what you've been telling yourself to keep them.

The mirrors in the locker room exist because Danny doesn't want this to be a secret managerial tool. Every staff member can stand in front of it before a shift and ask themselves where they sit today, on this task, with this team. The model is a self-management tool first; a manager-decision tool second.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the person.** One human at a time. Don't generalize across your whole team — the quadrant moves around by task and by season.
2. **Plot them honestly on this task, this month.** Same person can be top-right on one part of their job and bottom-left on another. The quadrant is task-specific and time-specific, not personality-specific.
3. **Identify the action that pairs with the quadrant** — celebrate / train / confront / part ways. Write it down.
4. **Set the timeframe.** "By the end of next month, here's what I expect to see different." No timeframe means no movement and no fairness.
5. **Tell them.** This is the part most managers skip. The other person needs to know which quadrant you've put them in and why, or the system is something happening *to* them — which violates the *for vs to* test that runs the whole house.
6. **Re-plot at the timeframe boundary.** If the dot moved, your read was right. If it didn't, you have a different problem and likely a different conversation.

## Examples

**Situation:** Long-tenured manager hits every operational target but two strong juniors just resigned and the team has gone quiet around her.

**Application:** She's *can* on technical (operational targets, hits them every quarter). She's *won't* on emotional (the team going dark, two good juniors leaving — that's the signal). The action that pairs with *can / won't* is the conversation, not the firing. Name the specific behaviors. Be specific about what you're seeing. Set a 60-day window and tell her what you need to see different. If it shifts, water the flower. If it doesn't, you already know what the next conversation is, and so does she.

> "She's the kind of hire I used to keep forever because firing her would feel like *I* failed. That's family-business thinking. It's a business, not a family. The longer she stays, the more your remaining team reads it as: *this is what gets tolerated here.*"

**Situation:** A new line cook, six months in, has the heart of a chef but keeps over-seasoning. Other cooks are starting to redo his plates after he leaves the line.

**Application:** *Will* (heart, work ethic, asking for feedback) but *can't* (technique not there yet). The pair-action is training, not firing — and not "asks his teammates to fix it after the fact." Specific intervention: a senior cook spends one shift a week alongside him. Specific window: 60 days. Specific bar: he plates clean for a full week without rework. If he gets there, you've trained a hospitalitarian. If he doesn't, you have data, not feelings, for the harder call.

**Situation:** Quarterly all-hands review of the dining-room team — twelve servers.

**Application:** Plot all twelve. The aggregate picture matters. Are most of your stars (*can / will*) getting your time, or have you been spending it putting out fires in the bottom quadrants? The honest answer is usually the latter. The reallocation isn't to ignore the bottom — it's to remember that watering the flowers is the highest-leverage move you have, and the weeds rarely take care of themselves when the canopy is gone.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the quadrants without timeframes.
**Why:** A label without a clock is a stereotype. Time-boxes turn a diagnosis into a contract — both parties know what changes by when.

**Don't:** Plot the *person* instead of the *task*.
**Why:** Your best server is *will / can* on the floor and *won't / can't* on inventory paperwork. Treating either reading as their identity is wrong, and they'll feel it.

**Don't:** Hide the model from the team.
**Why:** If staff don't know what you're using to assess them, every action you take based on it lands as something happening *to* them. The locker-room mirror is the antidote. (See `dialogue-not-monologue.md` on the *for vs to* preposition test.)

**Don't:** Conflate *can / will* with "no notes."
**Why:** Sunflowers still need water. The reason your stars get bored and leave is that nobody talks to them — you're saving your management attention for the weeds. Reverse the default.

**Don't:** Skip step 5 (telling them).
**Why:** A quadrant assignment they don't know about is a private grudge with calendar entries. Every action you take based on it will feel arbitrary because, to them, it is. The system only works when both of you can see the same dot.
