# Market Category

## When to Use
- You've done the work on alternatives, attributes, value, and target customer — now you need to decide "what is this thing, in language the buyer already knows?"
- The current category you're in is making buyers compare you to the wrong products or expect features you don't have.
- A new or adjacent category has emerged that might make your unique value more obvious.
- You're deciding whether to position Head to Head (win an existing category), Big Fish / Small Pond (win a subsegment), or Create a New Game (invent a category).

## Fails When
- **You pick a trendy category whose assumptions don't actually match your product.** Buyers will expect things you don't deliver, compare you to leaders you can't beat, and cut you at the first gap between their expectations and your reality.
- **You try to create a new category when an existing one would work.** Creating a category is slow, expensive, and rare. Most companies who think they need a new category are actually Big Fish / Small Pond inside an existing one.
- **You let customers tell you what category you're in.** Customers will pick the closest category they already know. That's rarely the one that makes your strengths obvious.

## Core Concept

Your market category is the frame of reference buyers use to make sense of your product. It's the answer to "what is this thing?" — and more importantly, it's the label that triggers a set of assumptions in the buyer's mind about your competitors, your features, your pricing, and your use cases.

Declaring a category is a powerful move, because you inherit everything that comes with the category. If you say "we're a CRM," customers automatically assume your competitor set (Salesforce, HubSpot), your feature baseline (contacts, activities, opportunities, reports), and your pricing model (per-seat monthly subscription). You didn't have to explain any of that. The category did the work.

The flip side: if the category's baked-in assumptions don't match your product, you spend every sales call fighting them. "We're email for lawyers" sets up the assumption that you have a calendar — and if your product doesn't have a calendar, you just created a confusing gap. Meanwhile your actual differentiator (secure, context-aware file sharing) gets buried because it's not what "email" is supposed to do. The category is working against you.

So category choice is strategic. You pick the frame whose assumptions *help* you. Not the frame you started in. Not the frame that's trendy. The frame that puts your strengths at the center and turns your differentiation into category-obvious value.

There are three styles of category play, and they require different moves.

**Head to Head — win an existing category outright.** You're going after the leader on their own terms. You're saying: the category is defined correctly, the purchase criteria are correct, and we're simply better at the things that matter. This almost never works for a startup. Beating an incumbent at their own game is a billion-dollar fight. Only attempt this if (a) you're already the leader and want to stay there, or (b) you have a massive structural advantage — a technology shift, a regulatory change, an economic dislocation — that genuinely disrupts the leader's position.

**Big Fish, Small Pond — dominate a subsegment of an existing category.** You aim to be the obvious best choice for a specific slice of the market where buyers have needs the overall leader doesn't serve well. You keep the category (customers still know what you are) but you narrow it. "Email for lawyers." "CRM for insurance brokers." "Project management for creative agencies." This is the right play for most startups. You inherit the category's clarity and you carve out a defensible position where your unique value actually matters.

**Create a New Game — invent a new market category.** You declare that the existing categories don't fit and you're going to define a new one. This is slow, expensive, and rare. You have to prove the category deserves to exist, educate customers about what it is, and establish yourself as the leader. If you pull it off, the rewards are huge — whoever owns the category owns the narrative. But most companies who think they need a new category are kidding themselves. Check Big Fish / Small Pond first. Only create a new game if no existing frame can accommodate your product and there's enough budget and runway to do the category education.

## How to Apply

1. **Start from value and target, not from market trends.** Your category choice has to make your differentiated value obvious to your best-fit customers. The trendy category may be the wrong one. The category where the air is thick with funding and press may not be the one where your unique value lands.

2. **Use abductive reasoning.** Look at your features and the value they deliver. Ask: what types of products typically have these features? What category of products typically delivers this kind of value? If the answer is an existing category that already exists in buyers' minds, that's a strong candidate.

3. **Check adjacent markets, especially fast-growing ones.** Sometimes your default category is the one you started in, but an adjacent market has emerged that's a better frame. Clearpath's robots were initially "manufacturing robots" but the better frame was "self-driving industrial vehicles." Same product. Different category. Dramatically different outcomes.

4. **Run the assumption check.** For any category you're considering, write out the assumptions buyers hold about that category — competitors, features, pricing, use cases, buying process. For each assumption, ask: does this work for us or against us? If more than a couple of major assumptions work against you, the category is wrong.

5. **Pick your style deliberately.** Head to Head only if you're leader or disruption is real. New Game only if no existing frame works and you have the resources to educate. For everyone else: Big Fish, Small Pond. Pick the subsegment where your value is the thing that tips the deal.

6. **Listen to customers, but don't let them pick the category.** Customers will tell you what market they see you in, and that's a useful data point. But they'll pick the closest category they already know — which is rarely the one that makes your strengths shine. Treat customer category-guesses as inputs, not answers.

7. **Use trends as a spice, not a main course.** "AI-empowered CRM" tells the buyer what you are (CRM — the category) and why you matter right now (AI — the trend). "AI-powered platform" tells them nothing. The category always comes first; the trend comes on top.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup with a novel database product keeps getting compared to Oracle and losing.
**Application:** "Database" was the wrong category. Its baked-in assumptions — general-purpose, transactional, Oracle as the leader, expensive enterprise licensing — worked against the product. The real strength was fast analytical queries. Adjacent category: "data warehouse." Same product. The data warehouse category's assumptions — analytical, not transactional, smaller competitive set, different price points — worked in its favor.
**Result:** The product repositions from database to data warehouse. Customers stop comparing to Oracle. Meetings stop ending with "we already have a database." Revenue takes off.

**Situation:** A product is "email for lawyers" but doesn't have a calendar.
**Application:** "Email" is the wrong category. Every email product has a calendar; the absence of one is an immediate dealbreaker. The real differentiator is secure, context-aware file sharing. That's not email. That's team collaboration software. Recategorize.
**Result:** The product repositions as "team collaboration software for lawyers" (Big Fish, Small Pond inside team collaboration). Buyers stop asking where the calendar is. The secure file-sharing differentiator, previously buried, becomes the lead message.

**Situation:** A founder wants to create a new category because "there's nothing else like us."
**Application:** Stress-test the claim. What would your customer have done if you didn't exist? If there's an answer — and there almost always is — then there's an existing category or adjacent category you could position inside. Creating a new category is a last resort. Do the Big Fish, Small Pond check first. Only if no existing frame fits AND you have resources for multi-year category education should you try to invent one.
**Result:** Nine times out of ten, the founder ends up picking an existing category (or a subsegment of one) because the resources required for a new-category play aren't there, and the existing frame actually does the job once it's sharpened.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick a category because it's trendy and well-funded.
**Why:** A trendy category gets more attention but also more competition, more buyer skepticism, and a louder feature race. If the trendy category's assumptions don't genuinely fit your product, you're paying the category's tax without getting its benefits. Pick the category where your *specific* value is obvious, not the one where the most money is flowing.

**Don't:** Default to "we're creating a new category."
**Why:** Creating a category is the hardest positioning move. It requires multi-year education, analyst relationships, content investment, and the ability to survive while buyers slowly learn the new frame. Most companies claiming to be a new category are actually a misunderstood subsegment of an existing one. Creating a category should be a last-resort decision, not a default pose.

**Don't:** Keep the default category you started with just because you've always used it.
**Why:** Your product evolved. The market evolved. The frame you started in may no longer fit. "We've always been a database" is a comforting thought that's often wrong. If you haven't questioned your category in the past year, question it.

**Don't:** Use the category label as the tagline.
**Why:** The category is the context; the tagline is the compression of your specific differentiated value. Leading with the category — "We're a CRM" — tells the buyer nothing about why to pick you. Category in the positioning doc; sharper differentiated value statement on the homepage.

**Don't:** Change category every six months because a new trend emerged.
**Why:** Category changes are expensive — every artifact has to shift, every customer has to re-orient, every sales rep has to relearn. Change only when the evidence is clear that the current category is hurting you. Not every month a new buzzword emerges.
