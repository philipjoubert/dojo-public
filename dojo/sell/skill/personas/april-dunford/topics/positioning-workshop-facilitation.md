# Positioning Workshop Facilitation

## When to Use
- You're preparing to run or commission a positioning workshop and need the playbook for who's in the room, how the day goes, and what happens after.
- A team tells you "we tried doing positioning but it didn't work." Nine times out of ten, it's a facilitation failure — wrong people in the room, no methodology, no follow-through.
- You're diagnosing why post-workshop positioning isn't sticking. Often the answer is the workshop itself was staffed or structured badly.
- You've been asked to facilitate and you want a battle-tested structure rather than winging it.

## Fails When
- **The CEO or business unit leader isn't in the room.** Positioning is business strategy. Without the person who owns the business strategy fully participating, you'll produce output they don't agree with and will have to re-sell to them afterward. That almost always fails.
- **The product is pre-launch with no real customers.** You can run the workshop, but what you'll produce is a *positioning thesis* — theoretical, based on guesses about the market. That's fine, as long as everyone agrees it's a thesis and revisits it after the first wave of customers.
- **Marketing owns the workshop alone.** Marketing feels the pain of weak positioning first, but they can't own positioning any more than they can own the business strategy. Without sales, product, and CS in the room, the positioning won't match reality and won't survive the jump to sales.

## Core Concept

A positioning workshop has three phases: pre-work, the session, and activation. The session itself is the easy part — a structured march through the five components. Most failures happen in pre-work (wrong people, wrong framing, undecided scope) or post-work (no pitch, no testing, no training). Get the pre-work and post-work right and the session almost runs itself.

**Who must be in the room.** The business leader — CEO for startups, division leader for larger companies. Non-negotiable. If they won't attend, don't run the workshop; reschedule until they will. Then: 1-2 senior marketing people, multiple sales voices (including experienced AEs, not just leadership), product leadership, customer success. Add observers for downstream content creators. Include anyone on the exec team with big opinions ("head of HR has strong views on company positioning") — it's easier to surface those opinions now than to defend against them after.

**Team size guidelines:** three minimum, six to twelve optimal, fifteen maximum. Past fifteen, you can't hear everyone. Past that, facilitation collapses.

**Pre-work decisions — make these before you assemble.** Is the product launched with real customers? If not, you're building a thesis, not positioning. Are we positioning for customers? Not investors (vision), not recruits (career paths). What are we positioning — a product, the company, a suite? Decide before the workshop or you'll fight about it in step one. Who is the champion persona in a typical deal? Positioning has to resonate for them first.

**Pre-work preparation.** Write the weird outlier customers on the board and cross them out — every B2B company has them, and they'll derail the discussion if you don't name them as out-of-scope. Align on positioning vocabulary — the first hour is definitions. What positioning is, what each of the five components means. Prevents "I didn't think that's what you meant" on day two. Let go of positioning baggage — the CEO/founder has to acknowledge the history of how the product has been positioned and grant permission for the new answer to be different.

**During the session: five steps, in order.** Competitive Alternatives → Distinct Capabilities → Differentiated Value → Best-Fit Accounts → Market Category. Each step builds on the previous. Don't skip to Market Category before Value. Don't define Value without Capabilities. The logic chain matters.

**Facilitation principles I come back to:** Stay focused on the present — positioning is about winning business now, not where the roadmap will be in two years. Keep the customer perspective — alternatives from the customer's view, value that matters to customers, categories customers understand. Use the process as the arbiter — "Let's park that and see what the data tells us" ends most arguments. Make disagreements visible on the board; surface them, don't bury them.

**Common sources of disagreement.** Product vs. sales on competition — product tracks theoretical/future competitors, sales knows who actually shows up in deals. Sales wins on current landscape. Founder vs. team on positioning history — founders identify with original positioning even when the product has moved. Have the founder hear from bag-carrying reps. Marketing vs. reality on competitors — "we see them everywhere" doesn't mean they're on shortlists; check with sales.

**If you can't reach agreement:** either test both positions (build pitches for both, test with the best AE), or the CEO makes the call — which is why they have to be in the room. Sometimes disagreement reveals genuine segment differences, in which case acknowledge the nuance rather than forcing false unity.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the readiness check before committing to a date.** Has the product launched? Is there customer data? Is leadership willing to attend? Have we decided what we're positioning? If any answer is no, fix that before you schedule the workshop.

2. **Block a half-day minimum, full day for most teams.** Thirty-minute "positioning reviews" do not produce positioning. Expect 4-8 hours of focused session time, plus pre-read for participants.

3. **Distribute pre-reading.** Obviously Awesome (or relevant chapters), the team's own win/loss data, happy-customer list, current competitive battle cards. The first hour of the session is still vocabulary alignment, but pre-reading reduces the burn.

4. **Open the session with baggage-letting.** CEO/founder acknowledges that the product was created with a certain market and audience in mind and that may no longer be the right positioning. Newer employees often don't carry the same baggage; surface that asymmetry.

5. **Work the five steps in order with visible documentation.** Whiteboard or shared screen. Write down decisions. Write down unresolved disagreements. Get explicit agreement at each step before moving on ("are we all aligned on this list of competitive alternatives?").

6. **Time-box debates.** "Let's spend ten minutes on this, then decide." "We can revisit if the next step shows something different." Don't let one component consume half the day.

7. **Don't stop at the canvas — build the sales pitch immediately.** The first post-workshop activation step. Marketing and sales collaborate on translating positioning into the 8-step pitch structure. Then test the pitch with the best AE on real prospects before enabling the wider team.

8. **Schedule the six-month review now.** Positioning drifts. Put the review on the calendar at the end of the workshop. Trigger an earlier review if a credible competitor enters, a major regulatory or tech shift happens, a major product change ships, or sales cycles start lengthening.

## Examples

**Situation:** A team has scheduled a positioning workshop. The CEO "can't make it but will review the output."
**Application:** Reschedule. Positioning is a strategic exercise, and the person who owns the strategy has to participate in the decisions, not review them after. A CEO who wasn't in the room will almost always disagree with some component and send the team back to rework, at which point the positioning never ships. Workshop dates are cheap; positioning that dies in review is expensive. Move the date.
**Result:** CEO attends the rescheduled session. Positioning ships because the person who has to enforce it helped build it.

**Situation:** In a workshop, product insists the real competitors are two startups nobody on sales has seen in a deal.
**Application:** Use the process as arbiter. Ask sales: "In your last twenty qualified deals, how many times did either of these names come up on a shortlist?" If the answer is zero, those are ghost competitors — product can keep tracking them as horizon threats, but they don't belong in the positioning's Bucket 2. Write them on the board, cross them out, note the reason. If sales says "one of them showed up twice recently," note it and set a threshold — "if they appear in 10% of deals next quarter, we'll add them."
**Result:** Product's concern is heard and documented. The positioning doesn't carry ghosts. Everyone knows what would change the decision.

**Situation:** Post-workshop, the team produces a beautiful canvas, emails it to sales, and nothing changes.
**Application:** Classic activation failure. The canvas is an input, not a pitch. Marketing and sales need to collaborate on building the 8-step pitch from the canvas. Test it with the best AE on real prospects. Iterate until the rep says "I like this better than the old pitch and I want to use it on every call." Then use that rep to enable the rest of the team. Peer credibility beats marketing presentations.
**Result:** The positioning actually makes it to customer conversations. Reps adopt it because a peer validated it on real deals, not because a deck was emailed to them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run a positioning workshop without the business leader.
**Why:** Positioning is business strategy. Without the person who owns the strategy participating in the decisions, whatever you produce will need to be re-sold, and typically won't survive that re-sell. Reschedule until they can attend.

**Don't:** Let marketing run the workshop alone.
**Why:** Marketing feels the pain but doesn't own the truth. Sales knows the competitive reality. Product knows the capabilities deeply. CS knows the post-sale experience. A workshop without those voices produces positioning that sales won't use and product won't support.

**Don't:** Start with "what problem do we solve?"
**Why:** Customers can't articulate problems well, and the team will fill the gap with internal opinion. Start with competitive alternatives — grounded in what customers actually compare you to, observable in real deals.

**Don't:** Try to position for every customer you've ever closed.
**Why:** Every B2B company has weird outlier customers. Including them drags the positioning into mush. Put them on the board, cross them out, move on.

**Don't:** Let ChatGPT generate the competitor list.
**Why:** AI lists competitors based on internet content, not deal reality. Same problem as the product team's theoretical list — you get ghosts. The people who really understand who a customer compares you to are the sales team and the customers themselves.

**Don't:** Skip straight from workshop to the website update.
**Why:** Website is the last thing to change, not the first. The order is: workshop → positioning canvas → sales pitch → pitch testing → sales enablement → then messaging → then website. Updating the website first just codifies a positioning the team hasn't validated yet.
