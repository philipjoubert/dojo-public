# The 10-Step Positioning Process

## When to Use
- You're repositioning a product (or positioning it deliberately for the first time) and need an ordered process rather than a set of concepts.
- You have a decent base of happy customers whose patterns you can study.
- The CEO or business unit leader is willing to own it and put their time into a multi-day exercise.
- The team has cross-functional representation: marketing, sales, product, customer success. At least one senior person from each.
- You're ready for the positioning output to actually change things — the sales pitch, the homepage, the roadmap, the target-account list.

## Fails When
- **You try to run this solo in marketing.** Positioning impacts product, pricing, sales, customer success. If those groups aren't in the room, they won't execute the output. You'll end up with a nicely formatted document nobody uses.
- **The product is genuinely pre-market.** This process assumes you can look at a group of happy customers and see patterns. If you don't have that yet, you're doing a *thesis* — a best guess to test — not real positioning. Keep it loose.
- **You skip steps.** The steps are ordered because the components interlock. You cannot pick a market category without knowing the value. You cannot know the value without knowing the unique attributes. You cannot know the unique attributes without knowing the alternatives. Don't jump to the end.

## Core Concept

Positioning is a team exercise with a specific order. Because every component depends on the components before it, you cannot do this in parallel, and you cannot do it backwards. Here's the order, and here's why each step matters.

The key insight: start with your happiest customers, not your product. Your best-fit customers hold the key to your positioning. They're the ones who understood the product fast, bought fast, became raving fans, and told their friends. If you study them carefully, everything else — alternatives, unique attributes, value, segment, category — falls out of what you learn about them.

The second key insight: you have to consciously let go of your baggage. You built the product with a certain market in mind. You've been describing it a certain way for years. That history may no longer be the best frame. Arm & Hammer was baking soda for 130 years before they realized they were really a deodorizer. Your default positioning is almost always not the best positioning. The process forces you to consider other frames.

## How to Apply

1. **Step 1 — Understand the customers who love your product.** Make a short list of your happiest customers. Not the moderately satisfied ones. The fans. What do they have in common? What problem were they trying to solve? What did they almost buy instead? Why did they pick you? Call them. Interview them. This list is your reference point for every step that follows. If you don't have enough happy customers to see a pattern, stop the exercise. Come back when you do.

2. **Step 2 — Form a positioning team.** The CEO or business unit leader must be in the room and visibly driving. Add one or two senior people from marketing, sales, product, customer success. Keep it between three and twelve people. If it's too big, the loud voices dominate and the quiet ones disengage. If the CEO won't show up, don't start — the output won't be adopted. I strongly recommend an outside facilitator; it's very hard to challenge long-held assumptions when you're sitting inside them.

3. **Step 3 — Align vocabulary and let go of baggage.** Spend the first hour establishing shared definitions: what positioning means, the five components, the styles of positioning. Then explicitly name the positioning baggage. "We've always been a database." "We built this for lawyers." "Our pricing has always been per-seat." You're not killing those assumptions — you're marking them so you can consciously suspend them for the duration of the exercise. You have to be able to consider that maybe you're not a database. Maybe you're a data warehouse.

4. **Step 4 — List your true competitive alternatives.** From the perspective of your best-fit customers, what would they actually do if your product didn't exist? Start with the status quo (Excel, do nothing, manual process, existing legacy tool). Then add any direct competitors that genuinely show up on shortlists. Group them. Rank them by frequency. Ignore competitors that never appear in real deals — those are ghosts.

5. **Step 5 — Isolate your unique attributes.** For each group of alternatives, what do you have that they don't? Be broad. Include technical features, business model, pricing model, delivery, integrations, partnerships, expertise. Include attributes some team members think are negatives — someone else on the team might see them as positives for a different segment. Don't filter yet. And stop listing things you can't prove. "Great customer service" is an aspiration, not an attribute, unless you can prove it with data or third-party validation.

6. **Step 6 — Map attributes to value themes.** Walk down the attribute list and for each one ask "so what? What does a customer actually get from this?" Keep asking "so what?" until you hit a business goal the customer cares about. Cluster the results into value themes. You're aiming for one to four themes. Often one. Fewer is better.

7. **Step 7 — Determine who cares a lot.** For each value theme, identify the segment who cares the most. Not "SMBs" or "enterprises" — that's too broad. The specific combination of situation, industry, stack, size, and buying trigger that makes your value a must-have rather than a nice-to-have. These are the characteristics of a best-fit prospect, in language that a sales or marketing team can actually use to go find more of them.

8. **Step 8 — Pick a market frame of reference and decide how to win in it.** Given your target and your value, which market category makes your unique value obvious? There are three styles here: **Head to Head** (win an existing category — rarely the right choice for a small company taking on a leader); **Big Fish, Small Pond** (dominate a subsegment of an existing category with specific needs the leader doesn't serve — usually the right answer for startups); or **Create a New Game** (invent a new category — rare, hard, and only appropriate if no existing category can accommodate what you do). Pick deliberately.

9. **Step 9 — Layer on a trend (carefully).** Optional. If there's a current trend your buyers care about and your product legitimately plays to it, use it to make your offering feel important right now. AI, sustainability, remote work, whatever. Trends are a layer on top of your category, not a replacement for it. Don't use a trend if it doesn't genuinely apply; customers will sniff it out, and you'll look like a bandwagon chaser.

10. **Step 10 — Capture the positioning so it can be shared.** Document it on one page, organized by the five (or six) components with supporting detail. This is the reference doc. From here, you build out the sales pitch, the homepage, the messaging, the target-account list, the product roadmap review. If the team walks out without a one-page document, the exercise didn't happen.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup with a database that's fast at analytical queries keeps getting kicked out of sales meetings because customers already have a database.
**Application:** The process walks them through the steps. Step 1: happy customers. Who loves them? Banks doing real-time fraud analysis. Step 3: let go of baggage — they're not positioned as a database forever. Step 4: alternatives — turns out customers would buy a data warehouse or a BI tool if this didn't exist, not another database. Step 5–6: their unique attribute is the query speed; the value is real-time answers on huge datasets. Step 7: financial services doing time-critical analytics. Step 8: big fish, small pond — dominate the data warehouse category's real-time-analytics segment.
**Result:** The product is repositioned from "database" to "data warehouse." Sales meetings transform. Prospects stop pushing back and start listening. Revenue accelerates.

**Situation:** A robotics company with an autonomously moving industrial robot is being compared to traditional stationary manufacturing robots, and losing.
**Application:** Run the process. Alternatives are traditional industrial robots that they have to keep explaining they are not. Unique attribute: autonomous mobile navigation. Value: flexible material movement without rearranging the shop floor. Target: manufacturers needing flexible logistics. The team realizes their product fits the "self-driving vehicle" frame better than the "robot" frame.
**Result:** They launch a sub-brand (OTTO Motors), redesign the industrial look to evoke cars — even add headlights and taillights — and reposition as self-driving industrial vehicles. Sales accelerate. They raise $30M in new funding.

**Situation:** A team has "done positioning" but nothing changed.
**Application:** Usually one of three failures. The CEO wasn't in the room. The team filled in the components with current assumptions instead of doing new thinking. Or the output never translated into action — no new sales deck, no homepage rewrite, no target-account shift. The process only works if it actually flows into execution.
**Result:** Redo it properly. Get leadership in the room. Commit up front to rewriting the sales pitch at the end. Schedule the homepage revision. Tie roadmap decisions to the new target segment. Otherwise don't bother running the exercise.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run this exercise in a half-day offsite with a whiteboard.
**Why:** This takes time. A full workshop is usually one to two days of intense work, plus pre-work (happy-customer interviews) and post-work (documenting, sharing, translating into pitch). A half-day whiteboard session gives you the illusion of progress without the output.

**Don't:** Let the team "quickly vote" on alternatives or value themes.
**Why:** This isn't democracy. Positioning decisions have to be made against evidence — customer interviews, win/loss data, what actually shows up on shortlists. If the team votes, they'll vote for their default assumptions, which is the trap you're trying to escape.

**Don't:** Finish the exercise without a follow-through plan.
**Why:** Positioning has to flow into artifacts. If you don't schedule the sales pitch rewrite, the homepage update, the roadmap review, and the target-account list revision on the day of the workshop, they won't happen. The exercise is only as useful as what changes because of it.

**Don't:** Position for where the product will be in two years.
**Why:** Positioning is rooted in the present. Your current differentiated value, your current target market, your current category. Your vision goes to investors. Your positioning sells today's product to today's buyers. If you position against the future, you're giving buyers a reason to wait — and they will.
