# Good Meeting / Bad Meeting

## When to Use
- Right after any meeting that involved your product or pitch — to grade it before you self-soothe.
- During a meeting, when you're heading toward the close and need to decide whether to push for a next step or accept the stall.
- During team review of customer notes — to keep each other honest about what actually happened versus what felt like it happened.
- When you're noticing a pattern of meetings that "went well" but produce no advancement — the framework will tell you what's actually happening.

## Fails When
- **The meeting was a pure-discovery chat with no product on the table.** A great discovery meeting might end with no advancement — that's fine, you weren't selling anything. The good/bad framing applies most cleanly to product/sales meetings where there's something to commit to. For early discovery, use `avoiding-bad-data.md` to grade the conversation instead.
- **You use the rubric to over-engineer light interactions.** Not every coffee chat needs to end with a signed letter of intent. The rubric is for meetings where you actually had a product or proposition to evaluate.

## Core Concept

It took me years to realise this: **there's no such thing as a meeting that just "went well."** Every product or sales meeting either succeeds or fails. There is no neutral. The middle ground — the friendly chat where they nodded along, said nice things, and didn't commit to anything — is *failure wearing pleasant clothes.* You've lost the meeting. You just don't know it yet.

A meeting succeeds when it ends with one of two outcomes:

- **Advancement** — they take a concrete next step that brings them closer to becoming a real customer (introducing you to their boss, agreeing to a paid pilot, scheduling a follow-up with stated goals, signing an LOI, or actually buying something).
- **Clear rejection** — they tell you, plainly enough that you can act on it, that they're not the customer. Painful, but free information. You can move on.

A meeting fails when it ends with anything else: compliments, stalling tactics, vague promises, "let me think about it," "send me an email," "we'll be in touch." None of these are progress. All of them feel productive. The drift-along, no-resolution meeting is the worst kind because it costs you the same time as a hard rejection but produces zero learning. You leave thinking "that went well!" and you're back in the office a week later wondering why nothing's happening.

The fix is mostly upstream: **decide what advancement looks like before the meeting.** If you don't know what next step you're going to push for at the end, the meeting is going to drift no matter what — you have nothing to drift *toward*. Walking in with a clear desired next step (intro / pilot / deposit / scheduled follow-up with criteria) means you can either land it or learn that they won't agree to it. Both are wins.

The signal hierarchy for evaluating what you heard:

- **Pure compliment** ("That's so cool. I love it!") → Bad meeting. Zero data.
- **Compliment + stall** ("Looks great, let me know when it launches") → Bad meeting. Polite rejection.
- **Fuzzy intro offer** ("There are a couple people I could intro you to when you're ready") → Almost-good meeting. Worthless until concrete.
- **Future-tense commitment** ("I would definitely buy that") → Bad meeting. Most expensive sentence in startups; sunk one of my early companies for ten million dollars.
- **Trial start** ("When can we start the trial?") → Maybe good. Depends on whether the trial actually costs them something.
- **Concrete next step** ("What are the next steps?" — and you have an answer) → Good meeting.
- **Multi-stakeholder ask** ("When can you come back to talk to the rest of the team?") → Good meeting. Real B2B advancement.
- **Buy the prototype** ("Can I buy the prototype?") → Great meeting. Earlyvangelist behaviour.

The rule of thumb that does most of the work: **if you don't know what happens next after a product or sales meeting, the meeting was pointless.**

## How to Apply

1. **Decide your ideal advancement before walking in.** Write it on a card. *"I'm going to push for: a paid 2-week pilot starting next month."* If you can't articulate the desired next step, postpone the meeting until you can. A meeting with no target is a meeting that will drift.

2. **Listen for the signal type.** As the meeting moves toward the close, classify what you're hearing in real time. Compliments → deflect, ask for currency. Stalls → recognise as polite rejection, attempt to convert, otherwise accept. Fuzzy intros / vague commitments → ask the concretising question (*who specifically? when specifically?*). Concrete next step → close it down with a calendar slot.

3. **Force the resolution at the end.** Don't let the meeting drift. *"Great chat. Where do we go from here?"* If they have an answer, ask: *"What's a realistic timeline for that?"* If they don't, you propose: *"I'd love to do X by Y — would that work?"* The proposal forces a decision.

4. **Convert future-promise into current-commitment.** *"I'd buy that"* → *"To make sure we build it right for you, would you put down a deposit / sign an LOI / commit to a launch-week pilot?"* If yes, real signal. If no, the original "would buy" was empty.

5. **Accept rejection as a win.** When someone clearly says they're not the customer — they don't have the problem, they don't have the budget, they're not the buyer — thank them, find out *why* (if they're still talking), and leave. You just got the most reliable possible information for free. Don't waste it by pushing harder; treat it as data and move on.

6. **Diagnose drift-along meetings honestly.** If the meeting ended without advancement and without rejection, ask: *did I not know what to ask for, or did I ask and they dodged?* The first is your fault — fix it for next time. The second tells you they're not actually a customer, just being polite. Both diagnoses are useful; mistaking the second for "they liked us" is what creates zombie pipelines.

7. **Don't pitch blind.** Even in a sales meeting, start with a few open-ended learning questions before going into the demo. *"Before I show you what we've built, can I ask how you're handling this stuff today?"* This gives you 5 minutes of real signal that lets you tailor the rest of the meeting and gets you out of the *"we do X. Want to buy it?"* / *"No thanks"* binary.

8. **Track the symptoms of zombie pipelines.** Pipeline that keeps having "good meetings" but never closes. Meetings that end with compliments. Meetings that end with no clear next steps. Meetings where they "haven't given up anything of value." All of these are the same disease — failure to push for advancement. Force commitment-asks into every meeting and the pipeline will sort itself: real customers move, polite ones drop off.

## Examples

**Situation:** Meeting ends with: *"That's so cool. I love it!"*

**Application:** Pure compliment. Zero data. Recognise it as a warning, not as a win. Deflect: *"Thanks — to make sure we're building something you'd actually use, could I [ask for currency: intro to budget owner / 2-week pilot / deposit / case study commitment]?"*

**Result:** If they engage with the commitment ask, you save the meeting. If they brush it off ("oh, well, you know, when you're ready..."), you've just discovered the "love it" was a polite goodbye. Either way, you walk out with real information instead of a glow.

---

**Situation:** Meeting ends with: *"Looks great. Let me know when it launches."*

**Application:** Classic compliment + stall. Polite *"Don't call me, I'll call you."* The big mistake is treating this as a pre-order ("he said he'll buy it when it launches!"). Try to convert it into a current commitment: *"That's awesome — to make sure I'm solving the right thing for you, could I get an intro to your tech team / your boss / the budget holder so I'm building exactly what you need?"* Or, if you're slightly further along: *"Could you be one of our alpha users? We could roll it out to a subset of your team and you could be a launch case study."*

**Result:** If they bite, the meeting becomes real. If they don't, you've learned this person was never actually a buyer — they were being polite. Move on without burning the relationship.

---

**Situation:** Meeting ends with: *"What are the next steps?"*

**Application:** Good meeting — *if* you have an answer ready. The classic blunder is to respond with *"Let me think about that and get back to you,"* which torches an otherwise great meeting. Have your next step in your pocket: *"Great. I'd like to come back in two weeks with the working version of [feature], have you walk through it with your team, and if it does what you need we'd start a paid pilot the following week. Does that work?"*

**Result:** Concrete plan, calendar slot, defined success criteria. That's a meeting that succeeded. The advancement is real because both sides agreed to a specific next thing with a specific date.

---

**Situation:** Meeting ends with: *"When can you come back to talk to the rest of the team?"*

**Application:** Bingo — particularly in B2B. They're committing reputation (vouching for you internally) and time (scheduling a multi-person session). That's two currencies in one move. Lock the date immediately: *"Awesome. How about [specific date] — does Tuesday or Thursday work better for the team?"*

**Result:** Real advancement. You've moved from one stakeholder to multiple, which is how enterprise deals actually progress. If they won't make this intro after a positive meeting, that's a strong signal you're at a dead end with this account, regardless of how warm the chat felt.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Walk into a product/sales meeting without a planned next step.
**Why:** No target, no advancement. You'll drift to the natural end of the conversation, exchange pleasantries, and leave. Every drift-along meeting is a meeting that failed because you didn't bring the question that would have made it succeed (or fail honestly).

**Don't:** Tell yourself the meeting *"went well"* without naming what changed.
**Why:** This is the diagnostic question for zombie pipelines. If you can't say what concrete thing the prospect committed to (next call with team, signed something, made an intro, paid you), the meeting didn't go well — it went *politely.* The two get confused because they feel similar in the moment.

**Don't:** Accept the first "no" of a stall as definitive when you haven't tried to convert.
**Why:** Sometimes a *"keep me in the loop"* is a polite rejection — and that's fine. But sometimes it's just default-mode politeness, and they'd actually engage if you asked for something specific. One conversion attempt is worth it. *"To make sure we build the right thing, could I [specific commitment ask]?"* If yes — meeting saved. If no — confirmed rejection, move on.

**Don't:** Push hard enough that you bully a fake yes.
**Why:** "I'll commit to anything just to get this person out of my office" is the worst possible meeting outcome — you walk out with what looks like a strong signal that's actually a polite escape. The line is: ask for the commitment once, frame it well, accept their answer. If you have to ask twice, you're now extracting a lie.

**Don't:** Confuse "I'll try the free trial" with commitment when the trial costs the user nothing.
**Why:** I've personally signed up for a hundred software trials and used zero of them. Free + low-friction = no cost = no signal. To convert a trial into real commitment, add cost: ask them to set up with their team, integrate with their data, put a card on file with cancel-grace-period, or commit to be a case study. The bigger the give-up, the more meaningful the trial.
