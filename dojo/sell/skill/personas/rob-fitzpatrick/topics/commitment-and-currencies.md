# Commitment and Currencies

## When to Use
- Once you've moved past pure discovery and are starting to show people something — a prototype, a deck, a wireframe, an early product.
- When you're trying to separate real prospects from people who like you and your idea but will never actually buy.
- When you've collected a lot of "yes, definitely" and you need to convert it into something testable.
- When meetings keep ending with friendly stalling tactics ("let me know when you launch", "send me an email when it's ready") and you need to force a decision.

## Fails When
- **You apply this in the discovery stage.** When you don't have a product, asking for "commitment to buy" is meaningless — there's nothing to buy. Discovery commitments look different: time (a follow-up call in 3 weeks conditional on you having something to show), reputation (an intro to a peer who'd care about this), or secrets (telling you about their real budget process). Don't try to extract financial commitments from chats that are still about learning.
- **You weaponise it.** Pushing for commitment isn't strong-arming. If you push too hard, you'll get fake yeses to make you go away — exactly the false positives you were trying to avoid. The point is to give them a graceful chance to either move forward or reject you, not to refuse to leave the room until they sign something.

## Core Concept

In sales, moving a relationship to the next stage is called *advancement* — pushing the prospect into the next step of your real-world acquisition funnel. They either move forward, or they make it clear they won't. **Both are good results.** The bad result is the meeting that "went well" with no rejection and no advancement — that's the friend-zone. You haven't lost them, you haven't won them, you've just convinced yourself something is happening when it isn't.

The way to escape the friend-zone is to ask for *commitment* — they show they're serious by giving up something they value. Compliments cost them nothing, so they're worth nothing. Real signals cost something. There are three currencies, and you should be tracking which (if any) the prospect is willing to spend:

- **Time.** Sitting down for a follow-up meeting with clear goals. Reviewing wireframes. Running a non-trivial trial. Filling out a real spec. Staying late to onboard their team.
- **Reputation.** Introducing you to peers, to their boss, to a decision-maker, to legal/IT. Giving a public testimonial. Putting their name on a case study. Vouching for you to a partner.
- **Money.** Letter of intent. Pre-order. Deposit. Paid pilot. Signed contract.

Strong commitments stack currencies — *"I'll run a paid trial with my whole team"* spends time, reputation, AND money in one move. That's a real signal. Compare it to *"I'd definitely buy that when it launches"* — zero of the three. The first is a customer. The second is a polite goodbye.

The thing to internalise: **the more they give up, the more seriously you can take their kind words.** A compliment from someone who just signed a deposit is meaningful. A compliment from someone who didn't agree to anything is a stalling tactic in a hat. If you can't list what currency the meeting cost the other person, the meeting was a lie that felt good.

There's also a meta-rule worth holding: **"no" is gold.** A clean rejection is one of the most useful results you can get. It's reliable information, it's free, and it lets you move on. The friend-zone meeting that drifts along with no commitment is *worse* than rejection — it costs you the same time and gives you no learning. If you have to pick, pick the meeting that ends in either commitment or rejection. The drift-along meeting is the worst outcome.

*Rule of thumb: "Customers" who keep being friendly but aren't ever going to buy are a particularly dangerous source of mixed signals.*

## How to Apply

1. **Before the meeting, decide what advancement looks like.** What's the next step you're going to push for at the end? Sometimes it's "schedule a demo with you and your tech team." Sometimes it's "run a trial for two weeks with your top three salespeople." Sometimes it's "give me an intro to your VP of operations, who actually owns this budget." If you walk in without an answer, you're going to leave with vibes.

2. **Convert future-promises into current commitments.** When you hear *"I'd definitely buy that when it's ready"* — don't accept it. Translate it into a *now* version: *"That's great to hear — to make sure we're building exactly what you need, would you be up for [letter of intent / a deposit / introducing me to your boss / committing to a 2-week paid pilot when we ship]?"* If they balk, the original "yes" was empty.

3. **Match the currency to the stage.** Pre-product, you can't ask for money — but you can ask for time and reputation. *"Could we schedule a follow-up in three weeks to walk through what we've built?"* / *"Is there someone on your team who'd be the actual user — could I talk to them too?"* / *"Would you be willing to be one of our first beta users when we ship?"* These are real commitments at the right stage.

4. **Make the commitment as concrete as possible.** *"Yeah, sometime"* is worthless. *"Yes, Tuesday at 3pm"* is data. *"Sure, I can intro you to a couple people"* is fluff. *"I'll email Jenny right now" or "send me a paragraph and I'll forward it tonight"* is real. Drag every fuzzy promise into something with a time, a name, or a number on it.

5. **Beware "free" trials that cost the prospect nothing.** I've personally signed up for a hundred CRM apps and never used them. Login takes 10 seconds and I never log in again. If your trial is too cheap to try, increase its cost — ask them to commit to a 2-week run with their team, agree to be a case study after, give you their credit card with charge-on-cancel-grace-period, or train two of their staff. The bigger the give-up, the more you can trust the validation.

6. **Treat compliment + stall as polite rejection — and respond accordingly.** *"Looks great, let me know when it launches"* is the classic. Don't accept it. The recovery move is: *"Awesome — to make sure I build something you'll actually use, could I [intro to budget-holder / 2-week alpha / be on the case study list now]?"* If they engage, great. If they brush off the commitment ask, you've just learned they weren't really going to buy. Both are useful.

7. **Look for emotional disproportion.** First customers are crazy in a good way — they want what you're making so badly they're willing to bet on a half-built thing. There's a difference between *"yeah that's a problem"* and *"THAT IS THE WORST PART OF MY LIFE AND I WILL PAY YOU RIGHT NOW TO FIX IT."* The second is your earlyvangelist. Track the emotional intensity, not just the words.

8. **Don't be annoying about it.** You're not strong-arming. You're giving them a clear, dignified chance to either commit or reject. If they reject, thank them, leave them to their day, and move on. The push for commitment is one ask, gracefully framed. Not a wrestling match.

## Examples

**Situation:** Prospect ends a meeting with: *"I would definitely buy that."*

**Application:** This is the most expensive sentence in customer development. Translate it into something with currency: *"Awesome — I want to make sure we're building exactly what you'd buy. Would you be willing to put down a deposit / sign a letter of intent / commit to a paid pilot the week after launch?"* Or, if pre-product: *"Could we schedule a follow-up where I show you what we've built, and if it does what you need, you'd be willing to roll it out to your team?"*

**Result:** If they say yes to the converted ask, you have a real signal. If they back-pedal — *"oh well, I mean, depending on how busy I am"* — the original "definitely" was fluff. Either result moves you forward. The unconverted "definitely buy" is the trap that sunk one of my early companies for ten million dollars. Don't take it at face value.

---

**Situation:** Prospect says: *"There are a couple people I can introduce you to when you're ready."*

**Application:** This is *almost* a good signal, but the fuzziness has to be removed. *"That'd be amazing — who would you intro me to, and what does 'ready' mean? If I had a working prototype next month, would that qualify, or do you mean shipping product?"*

**Result:** A real intro offer becomes specific — names, conditions, timeframes. A polite gesture evaporates under pressure ("oh, you know, just in general..."). You learn the difference. As a bonus, the conditions they cite tell you what your near-term goals should be: if "ready" means a deployable product, you know when to come back; if "ready" means logo on a real website, that's a much smaller hurdle.

---

**Situation:** A product designer is showing me a prototype smartphone tripod he's designing.

**Application:** I demo it, like it, and immediately ask: *"How much would it cost to buy the prototype?"*

**Result:** He laughs — he's gone through about a dozen prototypes already because people keep buying them off him. That's the rarest and best meeting outcome there is: the prospect *demanding* to give you money for the half-built thing in your hand. When you see this signal, drop everything else. These people are your earlyvangelists. Charge them. Ship them what you have. Then iterate.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat compliments as data when they cost the giver nothing.
**Why:** Compliments are the cheapest possible signal. They don't tell you the person will buy, will use it, will recommend it, or will even remember the conversation. Currency is the filter. If they're not spending time/reputation/money, the warm words are noise.

**Don't:** Accept *"keep me in the loop"* or *"send me an email when it's ready"* as anything but a polite goodbye.
**Why:** Both are stalls. They cost the other person nothing — they're not committing to read the email, take the next meeting, or do anything when "it's ready." The fix is to ask for a current commitment. If the meeting can be saved, this conversion attempt will save it. If it can't, you've just learned the truth.

**Don't:** Mistake a free trial sign-up for a real commitment.
**Why:** Trials are signal-grade only when they cost the user something — time to set up, training their team, integrating with their data, putting their credit card on file. A login-and-forget trial is just a compliment with a UI. Engineer real cost into the trial or stop counting it as advancement.

**Don't:** Push for commitment when you don't actually know what the next step should be.
**Why:** If you walk into the meeting unsure what advancement looks like, you can't push for it. You'll end up asking *"what do you think we should do next?"* — which is the prospect's job, not theirs. Decide your ideal next step before the meeting. Then, if you have to negotiate it down, fine. But know what you're aiming for.

**Don't:** Confuse commitment with conversion.
**Why:** A commitment isn't a sale. Sometimes the highest-currency commitment you can get pre-product is *time + reputation* (intro to the buyer, scheduled follow-up). That's a great early-stage signal even though no money has changed hands. Track currency, not just revenue. If you only count cash, you'll miss the strongest validation a discovery-stage conversation can give you.
