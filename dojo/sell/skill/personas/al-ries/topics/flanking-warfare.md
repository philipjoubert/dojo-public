# Flanking Warfare

## When to Use
- When a genuine uncontested territory exists — a segment the leader does not occupy and, structurally, cannot occupy without abandoning something fundamental about its position.
- When you can achieve real tactical surprise: no extensive test-market, no pre-launch publicity, no advance notice to the competition.
- When you have the resources to pour it on after an initial breakthrough — to reinforce success rather than declare victory and reallocate budget.
- When neither defensive nor offensive warfare fits your position: you are not the leader, and you are not strong enough to go head-to-head with the leader either.

## Fails When
- **You cannot pursue.** "Without pursuit, no victory can have a great effect," Clausewitz wrote — and Ries and Trout made pursuit the third principle of flanking warfare for precisely this reason. The Altair was the world's first personal computer, launched by MITS in 1975. Four years later it was dead, not because the product failed but because the company lacked resources to hold what the product had won. Apple and IBM stepped into the market MITS had created. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*
- **You test-market too long.** Datril's mistake was not its concept — attacking Tylenol on price was a reasonable flanking idea. The mistake was test-marketing in Albany and Peoria while Johnson & Johnson watched. By the time Datril was ready to launch nationally, Tylenol had already cut its price. The flanking move was dead before it started. "Test-marketing a proposed flanking attack is a catch-22 proposition. If it fails, it fails. If it succeeds, it alerts the leader." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*
- **You enter defended territory.** A flanking move must go into uncontested ground. The moment you are fighting over established market share, you are in an offensive attack — a much harder battle with much worse odds.

## Core Concept

Ries and Trout described flanking warfare as "the most innovative form of marketing warfare." In Marketing Warfare's strategic square, flanking is what you do when you are not the leader and not strong enough to fight the leader head-on. But flanking is not a fallback strategy — it is the boldest and most potentially rewarding of the four forms. "A general accepts offensive and defensive assignments as a normal part of the job, but lives for the day he is chosen to lead a flanking attack." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

The first principle defines the terrain: **a good flanking move must be made into an uncontested area.** This is not merely segmentation. Every marketer talks about finding niches. The difference in a true flanking attack is that you must be first to occupy the segment. Once a segment is occupied, attacking it is offensive warfare — and the math of offense against an entrenched defender is brutal. Undefended, a position can be taken by a squad; defended, the same position might require a full division. When Digital Equipment flanked IBM with minicomputers, the category "minicomputer" did not exist. DEC created it. When Miller flanked Schlitz and Budweiser with Lite Beer, the light beer segment had zero volume. "What was the market for light beer? Zip, of course." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)* Today Americans drink 35 million barrels of it annually.

The second principle governs timing: **tactical surprise ought to be an important element of the plan.** The flanking attacker's greatest advantage is that the leader doesn't see it coming — or dismisses it as beneath its attention. The longer the leader takes to react, the more time the flanker has to establish a position in the mind of the prospect. When IBM miscalculated the personal computer market, Apple received four years of runway before IBM arrived. Four years is enough to establish a durable position in the mind. Volkswagen received a decade before General Motors could build a competitive small car — by then, the Japanese had joined the invasion. Surprise is not a nice-to-have; it is the mechanism that makes flanking work. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

The third principle is where most flanking campaigns die: **the pursuit is just as critical as the attack itself.** "Ancient military maxim: Reinforce success, abandon failure." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)* Most companies do exactly the opposite. They have five products, three winners and two losers. Who gets management's time and attention? The losers. This is emotional accounting, not strategic thinking. The flanking product that begins to succeed needs maximum resources immediately, before the leader can cover and before copycat competitors can dilute the new segment. Fantastik spray cleaner, Close-Up toothpaste, Lite Beer — all three spent heavily *before* they were successful, not after. Success breeds success only if you pour fuel on it.

The structural variety in flanking moves is broad. Flanking with low price (Days Inns against Holiday Inn, Budget against Hertz, PEOPLExpress in airlines). Flanking with high price (Mercedes against Cadillac, Haagen-Dazs in ice cream, Orville Redenbacher in popcorn — at 2.5 times the price of the leader, Orville became No. 1 in four years). Flanking with small size (Sony's Walkman, Tummy Television, and above all, the Volkswagen Beetle against the entire American car industry: "Think small" against Fortress Detroit). Flanking with distribution (Timex through drugstores, Avon through door-to-door). Flanking with product form (Close-Up gel against paste-based toothpastes, Aim fluoride gel, Aqua-fresh two-in-one). The specific vector matters less than the principle: find uncontested ground, arrive with surprise, and pursue without mercy.

## How to Apply

1. **Map the category and find the white space.** Where is the leader not? More precisely: where can the leader *not* go without undermining what makes it the leader? Cadillac could not go down-market without damaging the Cadillac mystique. Budweiser could not go light without implicitly admitting the original was heavy. Kodak could not refrigerate film without admitting its shelf product aged. These structural constraints define the territory a flanker can occupy safely.

2. **Define the new category before the leader can deny it exists.** DEC called it "minicomputers." Miller called it "light beer" rather than "watered-down beer." The name of the new category is part of the flanking strategy. If you let the leader name your category, they will name it pejoratively.

3. **Move without test-marketing.** Or if you must test, do it fast, quietly, and with a cover story that doesn't reveal the real strategy. "Base your strategy on what your enemy is able to do, not just on what he is likely to do." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)* A leader who sees your test succeed will do what Johnson & Johnson did to Datril — move decisively before you can go national.

4. **Calculate pursuit resources before launch.** Ask: if this product succeeds, do we have the money and organization to run at full speed for three to five years? If the answer is no, reconsider whether this is flanking or whether it should be a guerrilla play — a smaller defended niche rather than a segment large enough to interest the leader.

5. **When the flanking product takes off, stop managing the losers and feed the winner.** Cut your losses. Let your winners ride. Reorganize resources around the breakout product immediately. The window of opportunity before the leader can cover is short. Use every month of it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Miller Brewing Company enters a beer market dominated by Budweiser and Schlitz.
**Application:** Rather than attacking on price, quality, or advertising (all positions Bud and Schlitz already own), Miller flanks with a category that doesn't yet exist: light beer. No test-marketing. Bold national launch with heavyweight advertising investment before revenue justified it.
**Result:** Americans now drink 35 million barrels of light beer annually. Miller created — and for years dominated — a category that had zero volume at the time of launch. Pursuit spending before the market existed was the key to establishing a durable position before competitors could pile in. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

**Situation:** Volkswagen enters the American automobile market dominated by the Big Three — General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler.
**Application:** GM, Ford, and Chrysler all make big, chrome-heavy, American-looking cars. VW flanks into completely uncontested territory: small, rear-engined, ugly. "Think small" makes the deficiency the virtue. General Motors cannot build a competitive small car for a decade; by the time they do, the Japanese have followed VW through the gap.
**Result:** VW captures 67 percent of the imported car market — at 19 times the volume of the No. 2 importer. Then VW abandons its flanking position by trying to become General Motors ("Different Volks for different folks"). Toyota, Datsun, and Honda pour through the thinned VW line. VW's share collapses from 67 percent to under 7 percent. The flanking position made VW. Abandoning it unmade VW. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

**Situation:** Orville Redenbacher's Gourmet Popping Corn enters a commodity market.
**Application:** Flanks with high price — 2.5 times the cost of the leading brand — in a category where no brand had previously used price as a flanking mechanism. Advertising investment of $6 million in the first year in a category generating only $85 million in annual sales. Bold, concentrated pursuit.
**Result:** The nation's No. 1 popcorn brand within four years. High price, not despite the high price. "The Orville Redenbacher approach to marketing success" is itself a model for high-price flanking in commodity categories. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Test-market a flanking concept in your standard test markets.
**Why:** Every major competitor watches those markets. Johnson & Johnson saw Datril in Albany and Peoria, mobilized in two weeks, and killed the product before it launched nationally. A flanking attack depends on surprise. Extended testing destroys surprise by design.

**Don't:** Expand the flanking product line once it succeeds.
**Why:** The flanking position is valuable precisely because it is narrow and distinct. VW was "small." The moment VW introduced the Wagon, the 411, the 412, the Dasher, and the Thing, it was no longer small — it was nothing. Volkswagen thought it was becoming a full-line manufacturer. What it actually was doing was vacating the only territory it owned. The Japanese walked through the empty space VW left behind. "Thinking small made Volkswagen big. Thinking big made Volkswagen small again." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 9)*

**Don't:** Launch a flanking attack you cannot afford to pursue.
**Why:** The Altair was the first personal computer. MITS didn't have the resources to hold the position it created. Apple and IBM took the market. Pioneering a flanking position you cannot defend is not marketing success — it is market development on behalf of better-capitalized competitors. If your resources cannot sustain three to five years of aggressive pursuit, reconsider whether guerrilla warfare — a smaller, more defensible niche — is the right strategy.
