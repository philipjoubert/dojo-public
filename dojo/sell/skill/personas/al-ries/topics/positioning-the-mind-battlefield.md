# Positioning: The Mind Battlefield

## When to Use
- Before any ad campaign, rebranding, website rewrite, or messaging overhaul is attempted.
- When a company with a better product is losing to an inferior competitor.
- When someone frames their marketing problem as a communication problem — "we just need people to understand how good we are."
- When a client says "our product speaks for itself" and then wonders why sales are flat.
- When marketing and product teams are in conflict about what the brand "really stands for."

## Fails When
- **The product is genuinely undifferentiated.** Positioning is not cosmetic. "Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect" — not what you spray on top of a product that offers nothing distinctive. *(Positioning, Introduction)*
- **The team expects messaging to do the heavy lifting.** You can't advertise your way past an entrenched mental position. Trying to is expensive, almost always unsuccessful, and occasionally counterproductive.
- **The category is brand new with zero prospect awareness.** A truly novel category needs category education before positioning takes hold. You are not positioning against anything yet.

## Core Concept

Positioning is the most fundamental idea in marketing, and the most consistently misunderstood. Most marketing people think they are fighting a product battle. They believe the goal is to demonstrate that their product is better — better features, better specs, better value — and that eventually the marketplace will recognize this and reward them. They are wrong. "Marketing is not a battle of products. It's a battle of perceptions." *(22 Laws, Law 4)*

Here is the definition Ries and Trout gave it in their 1972 Advertising Age series and expanded in the book: "Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect." *(Positioning, Introduction)* Everything else follows from this sentence. You are not changing the product. You are not even primarily communicating product features. You are finding, claiming, and defending a piece of mental real estate. The mind is the battlefield. Everything that happens in a factory or on a product roadmap is secondary to what happens inside the prospect's skull.

Why does this matter? Because the human mind is not a neutral receiver of information. "The mind rejects new information that doesn't 'compute.' It accepts only that new information which matches its current state of mind. It filters out everything else." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* In our overcommunicated society — where "the per-capita consumption of advertising in America today is $376.62 a year" — the average consumer has learned to slam the gate on incoming messages. The only way in is to work with the existing structure of the mind, not against it. "The basic approach of positioning is not to create something new and different, but to manipulate what's already up there in the mind, to retie the connections that already exist." *(Positioning, Ch. 1)*

This means the entire orientation of marketing has to shift. Instead of starting with the product and asking "what's great about this?" you start with the prospect's mind and ask "what position is already there, what position is empty, and how do we connect our product to either?" You look for the solution not inside the product, not even inside your own mind — "you look for the solution to your problem inside the prospect's mind." *(Positioning, Ch. 1)* The message is subordinate to the mental landscape you are operating in. Get that landscape wrong and no amount of creative execution will save you.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit the mind before touching the product.** Before writing a single word of copy or briefing a creative team, conduct a perception audit. What word, image, or association does your brand produce in the prospect's mind right now? What about your top competitors? What rungs on the mental ladder does each brand occupy? You cannot map a strategy without knowing your current position. You may discover you have no position at all — which is information.

2. **Identify the real battlefield.** The product battlefield is irrelevant. Specs, features, and price points matter only insofar as they affect mental positioning. Define the category the prospect uses to slot your product. Is that the right category, or has it been assigned by default? Identify who your true competitors are in the prospect's mind — not the competitors you believe you have, but the ones the prospect thinks about when they consider your category.

3. **Find the honest position, not the aspirational one.** Positioning must be grounded in something real. "To be successful today, you must touch base with reality. And the only reality that counts is what's already in the prospect's mind." *(Positioning, Ch. 1)* A position you cannot sustain, defend, or deliver on will collapse. Bragging and boasting about superiority without a genuine hook is "brag-and-boast" advertising, and prospects see through it immediately.

4. **State the position in one simple word or phrase.** If you cannot say what position you own in one word, you don't own it yet. The entire architecture of positioning rests on radical simplification: "In communication, as in architecture, less is more. You have to sharpen your message to cut into the mind. You have to jettison the ambiguities, simplify the message, and then simplify it some more if you want to make a long-lasting impression." *(Positioning, Ch. 1)*

5. **Hold the position against all internal pressure to abandon it.** Companies fall into what Ries called the "F.W.M.T.S. trap — Forgot What Made Them Successful." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* The moment a brand achieves a position, internal forces push toward expansion, line extension, and brand broadening — all of which dilute the position. Defend it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Avis has been losing money for thirteen straight years trying to compete head-on with Hertz. Their advertising focuses on quality of service — "Finest in rent-a-cars."

**Application:** The campaign fails because it claims a position the prospect already knows belongs to Hertz. Avis is second on the rent-a-car ladder in the prospect's mind. Rather than ignore or fight that position, they acknowledge it and use it: "Avis is only No. 2 in rent-a-cars. So why go with us? We try harder." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)*

**Result:** Avis starts making money — $1.2 million the first year, $2.6 million the second, $5 million the third. Not because the service improved, but because the positioning aligned with the reality already in the prospect's mind and turned it into an asset.

---

**Situation:** Miller wants to introduce a new kind of beer in a market crowded with virtually identical "regular" beers. They could emphasize taste, quality, or brewing heritage.

**Application:** Instead, they look for an open position in the prospect's mind. Light beer as a category doesn't exist yet — there is no ladder, no occupant. "Lite beer from Miller" introduces the category and claims the top rung simultaneously. The message is the position itself: a beer with fewer calories without sacrificing the experience.

**Result:** A category-defining success. "A runaway success that spawned a host of me-too brands." *(Positioning, Ch. 3)* Miller Lite occupied the top rung before anyone else could climb the ladder.

---

**Situation:** A software company has built a better project management tool than Asana or Monday.com. Their marketing focuses on feature comparisons.

**Application:** Feature comparison is product-battle thinking. The prospect already has a mental slot labeled "project management" and Asana owns significant real estate there. The question is not "how are we better?" but "what position in the prospect's mind is still available?" Is there a specific segment — engineering teams, agencies, distributed teams — where no tool has claimed leadership? Is there a specific word — simplicity, reporting, client visibility — that no competitor has planted their flag on?

**Result:** Competing on features against an entrenched leader is an expensive way to lose. Finding and owning a position the leader hasn't pre-empted is the only viable path. The battle is in the mind. The product just has to be good enough to hold the position once you've won it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat positioning as a creative brief or a tagline exercise.
**Why:** A tagline is an artifact of positioning. It's what the position looks like after it's been compressed into advertising language. Creating the tagline first and working backward is like painting the walls before you've built the house. The tagline "We try harder" emerged from the honest acknowledgment of Avis's actual position. The position came first.

**Don't:** Lead with product superiority claims.
**Why:** "Marketing is not a battle of products, it's a battle of perceptions." *(22 Laws, Law 4)* Superiority claims are what every competitor makes. The prospect has no way to evaluate them and no reason to believe yours over anyone else's. Claims of superiority without a prior claim to a specific position in the mind produce only noise. They reinforce the leader's position while spending money on your own behalf.

**Don't:** Ignore your competitors' positions when building your own.
**Why:** "In today's marketplace the competitor's position is just as important as your own. Sometimes more important." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* A position only means something relative to other positions. Avis only means something because Hertz exists. 7-Up only works because Coke and Pepsi dominate the adjacent ladder. Your position is always a position in a context — map the context or you will position yourself into empty space.

**Don't:** Believe you can change a mind that is already made up.
**Why:** "You can't change a mind once a mind is made up." *(22 Laws, Law 3)* Millions of dollars have been wasted attempting this. Xerox spent twenty-five years and $2 billion trying to establish a computer position — and failed. Wang spent millions promoting personal computers after the world had filed it under "word processor." When the mind has locked in a perception, the energy required to shift it is almost never worth the investment. Work with the mind's existing structure; don't fight it.
