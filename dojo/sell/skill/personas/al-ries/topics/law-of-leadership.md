# The Law of Leadership

## When to Use
- When a client wants to build a marketing plan around product superiority over an established leader.
- When a company is considering entering a mature category where a brand is entrenched.
- When evaluating the long-term viability of a market position.
- When someone asks why the best product doesn't always win.
- When advising on product launch strategy — specifically whether to attack an existing category or create a new one.

## Fails When
- **The timing is wrong.** Being first to a market that isn't ready doesn't establish leadership — it establishes a cautionary tale. "Not every first is going to become successful, however. Timing is an issue — your first could be too late." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* A first in a dying category doesn't lead; it just sinks faster.
- **The category doesn't take hold.** Frosty Paws was the first ice cream for dogs. That doesn't matter. The category never became real.
- **The leader has seriously violated their own position.** If the leader has drifted badly — through line extension, inconsistency, or category confusion — a challenger can sometimes attack a weakened mental position. But this is the exception, not the rule.

## Core Concept

The Law of Leadership is the first law in *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* for good reason: everything else flows from it. It states, flatly, that it is better to be first than to be better. Not usually better. Always better. "The basic issue in marketing is creating a category you can be first in." *(22 Laws, Law 1)*

The classic proof is two questions: What's the name of the first person to fly the Atlantic Ocean solo? Charles Lindbergh. What's the name of the second? Bert Hinkler — who was, in fact, a better pilot than Lindbergh. He flew faster, consumed less fuel. Nobody has heard of Bert Hinkler. "Yet who has ever heard of Bert Hinkler?" *(22 Laws, Law 1)* The mind stores firsts. It has no filing category for "the second-best version of something that already exists."

The mechanism behind the law is mental, not market. Once a brand occupies the top rung of the mind ladder in a category, it becomes the standard against which everything else is measured — and implicitly, everything else is found inferior. "The leading brand in any category is almost always the first brand into the prospect's mind. Hertz in rent-a-cars. IBM in computers. Coca-Cola in cola." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* Heineken was the first imported beer to establish itself in America. Four decades later, with 425 competing imported beers on the market, Heineken still holds 30 percent of the category. Is it the best-tasting imported beer? Probably not. Does it matter? No. It was first.

The law has a compounding effect that is often missed. When a brand is first into the mind, its name tends to become generic for the category. "People will stand in front of a Ricoh or a Sharp or a Kodak machine and say, 'How do I make a Xerox copy?'" *(22 Laws, Law 1)* Kleenex. Band-Aid. FedEx as a verb. Scotch tape. When your brand name becomes the generic word for the category, your position is essentially unassailable without a structural shift in the category itself. The name becomes the rung. The brand and the category are fused in the prospect's mind.

The implication for market entry strategy is blunt: "In spite of the evident superiority of the Lindbergh approach, most companies go the Bert Hinkler route. They wait until a market develops. Then they jump in with a better product, often with their corporate name attached." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* This is the standard playbook — wait, watch, learn, then launch with something better and more polished. And it almost never works at scale. By the time you launch, the first mover has been filing itself into the prospect's mind for months or years. You are starting a race that the other brand finished last year.

The law applies universally. Colleges: Harvard (first in America) remains first in perception. Magazines: *Time* leads *Newsweek*, *People* leads *Us*, *Playboy* leads *Penthouse* — "The law of leadership applies equally as well to hard categories like automobiles and computers as it does to soft categories like colleges and beer." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* The pattern holds across consumer goods, B2B, institutions, and people. First into the mind wins; second is an afterthought.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask "first what?" before asking "how do we win?"** When considering a new product or market entry, the question is not "how is this product better than what exists?" The question is: "First what? What category is this product first in?" *(22 Laws, Law 2)* If you cannot answer that question with a genuine "first," you need to either create a new category or think harder about the segment of an existing category where you can be first.

2. **Enter new categories fast, even imperfectly.** The instinct to refine and perfect before launch is the Bert Hinkler mistake. "It's not enough to be better than the competitor. You must introduce your product before someone else has a chance to establish leadership." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* A good product launched first beats a great product launched second. First impressions in the mind are almost permanent.

3. **Protect the leadership position relentlessly.** Once you own a category, the biggest threat is self-inflicted. Leaders lose not because competitors beat them but because they dilute their own position through line extension, brand expansion, and category drift. The word they own in the mind gets blurred. When the position blurs, the leadership fades. "Once you have your word, you have to go out of your way to protect it in the marketplace." *(22 Laws, Law 5)*

4. **Use the law to kill bad competitive strategies before they start.** If a client is second or third in a category with an entrenched leader and is planning a "better product" campaign, apply this law as a diagnostic. Ask: What would it take for the prospect to mentally refile the leader below you? The answer is almost always "more than this campaign can accomplish." The better investment is finding a category where leadership is available.

5. **Name the first.** If you are creating a new category, invest in naming it well. The brand that names the category has a structural advantage over every brand that follows. Charles Schwab didn't open a better brokerage. He opened the first discount broker. "Charles Schwab didn't open a better brokerage firm. He opened the first discount broker." *(22 Laws, Law 2)*

## Examples

**Situation:** Miller Brewing Company observes the success of Schlitz's "light" positioning claim — "real gusto in a great, light beer" — and decides to take it seriously.

**Application:** Rather than compete with Schlitz on taste or heritage, Miller asks: what happens if you actually position a beer as a light beer? No one has done it for real. The category doesn't exist in the prospect's mind. Miller introduces Lite beer — and by doing so creates both the category and the first-mover position simultaneously. "Miller introduced 'Lite' beer. And the rest is history." *(Positioning, Ch. 3)*

**Result:** Lite beer becomes the defining example of Law of Leadership applied through Law of Category. Miller owned rung one. Every light beer that followed was fighting for lower rungs — including Schlitz Light, the ironic me-too brand from the company whose positioning claim inspired the original idea.

---

**Situation:** IBM was not the first to make a computer. Sperry Rand built UNIVAC — the first commercial computer. By the product-quality logic, UNIVAC should dominate the computer market.

**Application:** Sperry Rand failed to translate a marketplace first into a mind first. IBM mounted a massive marketing effort and established the mental position before UNIVAC could consolidate its physical lead. "IBM wasn't first in the marketplace with the mainframe computer. Remington Rand was first, with UNIVAC. But thanks to a massive marketing effort, IBM got into the mind first and won the computer battle early." *(22 Laws, Law 3)*

**Result:** IBM owned the word "computer" in the prospect's mind for decades. UNIVAC, despite its genuine marketplace priority, faded. The lesson: being first in the marketplace is only useful to the extent that it enables being first in the mind. The mind is the only battlefield that matters.

---

**Situation:** A fintech startup builds a product that is demonstrably faster, cheaper, and more user-friendly than Stripe for online payments processing. They want to run a comparison campaign.

**Application:** Stripe holds rung one on the payments ladder in the minds of the developers and founders who make purchasing decisions. A comparison campaign puts your brand next to Stripe — which legitimizes Stripe, not you. "Unfortunately, benchmarking doesn't work. Regardless of reality, people perceive the first product into the mind as superior." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* The honest strategic question: is there a segment of the payments market — a type of merchant, a geography, a use case — where Stripe is weak or absent, and where being first to that sub-category is still available?

**Result:** If yes: enter that sub-category, own rung one, and grow from a position of leadership rather than perpetual second place. If no: prepare for a very expensive fight for market share against a deeply lodged mental position.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Wait for the market to develop before entering.
**Why:** Waiting allows someone else to be first into the mind. "In spite of the evident superiority of the Lindbergh approach, most companies go the Bert Hinkler route. They wait until a market develops. Then they jump in with a better product." *(22 Laws, Law 1)* By the time you arrive with your superior product, the first brand has already been filed in the prospect's mind as the standard. You are not competing with their current product; you are competing with their mental position — which they didn't stop building while you were refining yours.

**Don't:** Assume quality will eventually win out.
**Why:** "Marketing is not a battle of products. It's a battle of perceptions." *(22 Laws, Law 4)* Heineken is not the best-tasting imported beer. Hertz is not always the cheapest or most convenient rental car company. Coke Classic was ranked worst in blind taste tests while holding the market lead. Quality is table stakes. It gets you in the game. It does not get you the mental position.

**Don't:** Measure success by the product launch, not by mental penetration.
**Why:** A product can be in market, well-reviewed, and award-winning without having established a mental position. The product era is over. Having a great product is necessary; having a word in the prospect's mind is the objective. "Being first in the mind is everything in marketing. Being first in the marketplace is important only to the extent that it allows you to get in the mind first." *(22 Laws, Law 3)*

**Don't:** Let the leader's name become generic without fighting to maintain trademark distinctiveness.
**Why:** When your brand becomes the category name, you gain maximum mental penetration — but you also risk losing trademark protection. "Lawyers advise the opposite, but what do they know about the laws of marketing?" *(22 Laws, Law 1)* The tradeoff is real but usually worth taking. The brand whose name becomes a verb — FedEx, Xerox, Google — has won the deepest possible mental position.
