# Repositioning the Competition

## When to Use
- When every obvious creneau in a category is occupied and Law of Category doesn't offer a viable new ladder.
- When the leader's position has a genuine structural vulnerability that the prospect hasn't yet noticed.
- When a new entrant needs to displace the current leader from mental occupancy before it can establish its own position.
- When the prospect's reason for not considering you is loyalty or familiarity with the existing leader, not genuine preference.
- When a brand needs to make space in the mind before it can occupy space in the mind.

## Fails When
- **The repositioning claim is "we're better than them."** This is comparative advertising, not repositioning. "There's a psychological flaw in the advertiser's reasoning which the prospect is quick to detect. 'If your brand is so good, how come it's not the leader?'" *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* Claiming superiority does not move the prospect's perception of the competitor — it invites skepticism about you.
- **The negative fact about the competitor isn't true or can't be verified.** Repositioning works when it reveals something real. If the competitor can refute the claim or the prospect's own experience contradicts it, the repositioning fails and you look dishonest.
- **The customer doesn't care about the vulnerability.** If you expose a fact about the competitor that the prospect considers irrelevant, you produce no motion. The customer must find the new information meaningful enough to reconsider their mental filing.
- **You mention a competitor without changing anything.** Simply naming a competitor in an ad is not repositioning — it's free advertising for them. "A look at comparative ads suggests why most of them aren't effective. They fail to reposition the competition." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

## Core Concept

Ries and Trout introduced repositioning the competition as the strategic move for when the category is full. The situation it addresses is the one that happens when cherchez le creneau turns up nothing: "There comes a time when you can't find a creneau. With hundreds of variations in each product category on the market, the chances of finding an open hole today are very slim." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

The solution is to create a hole by dislodging what is already there. "In other words, to move a new idea or product into the mind, you must first move an old one out." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* This is not a rhetorical trick. It is a structural necessity. The human mind has a finite number of rungs per ladder and will not add new ones just because you ask politely. If every rung is occupied, the only way onto the ladder is to shake loose one of the brands already on it. Repositioning is the mechanism for doing that.

The mechanism requires precision. "For a repositioning strategy to work, you must say something about your competitor's product that causes the prospect to change his or her mind, not about your product, but about the competitor's product." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* This is the counterintuitive heart of it. Most marketing thinking is self-referential — we make claims about ourselves. Repositioning is other-referential — you make a claim about them. The goal is to shift the prospect's perception of the competition, and only then fill the vacancy with your brand.

The classic demonstration is Tylenol against aspirin. Aspirin was an institution in American medicine. It had enormous awareness, deep trust, and category dominance. There was no logical hole to fill. So Tylenol didn't look for a hole — it created one. "Tylenol went out and burst the aspirin bubble. 'For the millions who should not take aspirin,' said Tylenol's ads. 'If your stomach is easily upset … or you have an ulcer … or you suffer from asthma, allergies, or iron-deficiency anemia, it would make good sense to check with your doctor before you take aspirin. Aspirin can irritate the stomach lining,' continued the Tylenol ad, 'trigger asthmatic or allergic reactions, cause small amounts of hidden gastrointestinal bleeding. Fortunately, there is Tylenol …'" *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

Notice the structure. Sixty words of copy about aspirin. No claim of superiority. No "Tylenol is better." Tylenol's ad first repositioned the competitor — exposing a real vulnerability in aspirin that the prospect had never thought about — and only then, in the last two words, introduced the alternative. "Sales of Tylenol acetaminophen took off. Today Tylenol is the No. 1 brand of analgesic. Ahead of Anacin. Ahead of Bayer. Ahead of Bufferin. Ahead of Excedrin. A simple but effective repositioning strategy did the job. Against an institution like aspirin. Amazing." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

The vodka case is equally instructive. Stolichnaya needed to establish itself as genuinely Russian in a market where every competitor had Russian-sounding names (Smirnoff, Wolfschmidt, Samovar) and Russian imagery. Saying "we're really Russian" would have been invisible. So the ads named the competitors and exposed the truth: "'Most American vodkas seem Russian,' said the ads. And the captions said: 'Samovar: Made in Schenley, Pennsylvania. Smirnoff: Made in Hartford, Connecticut. Wolfschmidt: Made in Lawrenceburg, Indiana. Stolichnaya is different. It is Russian.'" *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* The competition was repositioned first. The moment the prospect learned that Smirnoff was made in Hartford, the Stolichnaya claim "Made in Leningrad, Russia" became meaningful. "Stolichnaya sales began to soar as a result." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

The ethical question comes up often. Is it acceptable to attack a competitor in advertising? Ries's answer is direct: disparagement is not the same as repositioning, and repositioning done honestly is not unethical. "Culture and refinement may be admirable qualities, but not in advertising." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* The key word is honesty. Repositioning that exposes a genuine vulnerability — something true, verifiable, and meaningful — is legitimate competitive strategy. Repositioning that distorts or fabricates is a different matter. Done honestly, "it keeps the competition on their toes." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

## How to Apply

1. **Audit the leader's position for genuine vulnerabilities.** Every market leader's strength is also a potential liability. Age can be framed as dated. Size can be framed as slow or impersonal. Premium positioning can be framed as overpriced. Ubiquity can be framed as generic. The question is: which vulnerability is (a) real, (b) verifiable by the prospect, and (c) actually meaningful to the prospect? All three conditions must hold.

2. **Frame the vulnerability in the prospect's language, not your own.** Tylenol didn't say "aspirin has problems." It said: "If your stomach is easily upset … if you have an ulcer … if you suffer from asthma." The prospect self-identified. The ad didn't tell the prospect their belief about aspirin was wrong — it helped the prospect discover for themselves a reason to reconsider. "People like to see the high and mighty exposed." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* Give them the evidence; let them draw the conclusion.

3. **Lead with the competitor, not yourself.** The structural discipline of repositioning requires spending your first and most credible copy real estate on the competitor. This is psychologically counterintuitive — you are paying to make someone else famous — but it is strategically necessary. The prospect must change their mind about the competitor before they can change their mind about you. Your brand is the resolution. Let the problem statement belong to the competition.

4. **Follow the exposure with your brand as the logical alternative.** Once the vulnerability has landed, you step in. The step must feel inevitable: if aspirin has these risks, Tylenol makes sense. If those other vodkas aren't really Russian, Stolichnaya makes sense. If Lenox is American, Royal Doulton from Stoke-on-Trent, England makes sense. The sequence is: reveal the problem, present the solution.

5. **Ensure the claim is bulletproof.** "The crux of a repositioning program is undercutting an existing concept, product, or person." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* If the competitor can factually refute your claim, the repositioning collapses and your brand absorbs the credibility damage. The Tylenol claims about aspirin's gastrointestinal effects were medically documented. The Stolichnaya claims about where competitors were manufactured were printed on the label. The Pringle's claim about ingredients was on the box. Evidence that is publicly verifiable is far more powerful than evidence you assert.

## Examples

**Situation:** Royal Doulton china is competing with Lenox, which has a strong position as a premium china brand in America. Many buyers assume Lenox is an English import — the name sounds British.

**Application:** Royal Doulton runs a single headline that exposes the positioning gap: "Royal Doulton. The china of Stoke-on-Trent, England vs. Lenox. The china of Pomona, New Jersey." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* No product claims. No superiority assertions. Just a geographic fact that shifts the prospect's perception of Lenox from "imported British prestige" to "New Jersey." Once that perception shifts, Royal Doulton's authentic British origin becomes a meaningful differentiator.

**Result:** "Royal Doulton credits a 6 percent gain in market share to this one advertisement." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* One ad. One reframing. A measurable market share shift.

---

**Situation:** Scope mouthwash is competing with Listerine, which owns the halitosis category with the position "the taste you hate, twice a day."

**Application:** Scope doesn't claim to be better at killing germs or taste better in an absolute sense. It uses two words to reposition Listerine: "medicine breath." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* These two words don't attack Listerine's functional efficacy — they attack the social desirability of Listerine's outcome. You killed the germs, but now your breath smells like a pharmacy. The prospect, who was using Listerine to avoid social embarrassment, discovers that Listerine might be creating a different kind of social embarrassment.

**Result:** "These two words were enough to torpedo Listerine's highly successful 'the taste you hate, twice a day' theme. The Scope attack carved a few share points out of market leader Listerine and firmly established Scope in second place." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)*

---

**Situation:** Borden's Wise potato chips responds to Pringle's, which has captured 18 percent of the market with a uniform chip in a distinctive canister.

**Application:** Wise reads the two ingredient labels on television. "In Wise, you find: Potatoes. Vegetable oil. Salt. In Pringle's, you find: Dehydrated potatoes. Mono- and diglycerides. Ascorbic acid. Butylated hydroxy-anisole." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* No taste claims. No heritage claims. Just the labels. The repositioning works because it changes what the prospect thinks about Pringle's — from "innovative new chip" to "processed product with chemical additives."

**Result:** "Sales of Pringle's came tumbling down. From a respectable 18 percent of the potato chip market to 10 percent. A far cry from P & G's goal of 25 percent." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* The repositioning was not just effective — it stuck. The perception of Pringle's as a chemically processed product proved very difficult to dislodge.

---

**Situation:** A B2B software company is entering a market where Salesforce has deep enterprise penetration. Feature comparison campaigns are producing nothing.

**Application:** Salesforce's strength is its breadth and installed base — but that same breadth makes it slow, expensive to implement, and complex to customize. For the right target segment (mid-market companies, or specific verticals), these are real vulnerabilities. A repositioning strategy doesn't say "our CRM is better than Salesforce." It says: "Salesforce was built for enterprise. Your 50-person sales team is paying enterprise prices for enterprise complexity you don't need, configured by a consultant who left six months ago." The prospect self-identifies if this is their situation. The alternative — a focused product built for exactly their size and workflow — then has space to land.

**Result:** The prospect's perception of Salesforce moves from "industry standard" to "overkill for our situation." The new entrant fills the vacancy not as a generic Salesforce alternative, but as the right tool for the specific segment that just became available.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run comparative advertising that claims you are better.
**Why:** "There's a psychological flaw in the advertiser's reasoning which the prospect is quick to detect. 'If your brand is so good, how come it's not the leader?'" *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* Comparative advertising that frames your brand as superior — rather than the competitor as problematic — triggers skepticism, not persuasion. The prospect already has a filing system. Your superiority claim is inconsistent with the ladder as they know it. It gets filtered out.

**Don't:** Name a competitor without changing something about how the prospect perceives them.
**Why:** Naming a competitor without repositioning them is free advertising for them. It makes the category more salient, reminds the prospect of the competitor's brand, and does nothing to your advantage. "A look at comparative ads suggests why most of them aren't effective. They fail to reposition the competition." *(Positioning, Ch. 8)* Ban's ad listing eight competitors it claimed to outperform — "Ban is more effective than Right Guard, Secret, Sure, Arrid Extra Dry, Mitchum, Soft & Dry, Body All, and Dial" — reinforced all eight competitor names while producing no change in how prospects perceived any of them.

**Don't:** Reposition on a vulnerability the prospect doesn't find meaningful.
**Why:** Exposing a true fact that the prospect considers irrelevant produces no motion. If you tell a Listerine devotee that Listerine tastes medicinal and they say "I know, that's why I trust it," you have not repositioned anything. The vulnerability must be one that the prospect actually cares about — one that connects to a real anxiety, desire, or aspiration they already hold. Test the repositioning on prospects before running it at scale.

**Don't:** Use repositioning as a short-term tactic rather than a long-term positioning foundation.
**Why:** The most effective repositioning programs establish a durable contrast that the brand builds on over years. Tylenol's "for those who can't take aspirin" became the foundation for a multi-decade category leadership. Scope's "medicine breath" established the functional territory it still occupies. Stolichnaya's "actually Russian" became its brand essence. Repositioning that works must be maintained consistently — not run once and abandoned. "More than anything else, successful positioning requires consistency." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)*
