# Publicity Over Advertising: Brands Are Born, Not Made

## When to Use
- When introducing a new brand, new product, or new category to market.
- When budget is limited and you must choose between PR investment and paid advertising investment.
- When an existing campaign is running but awareness is not building — a signal that advertising is doing the wrong job.
- When a startup founder asks how to build credibility and visibility without a large media budget.
- When a company is trying to figure out the right sequence: what comes first, what comes second.

## Fails When
- **The brand is already the established leader.** Publicity is for birth; advertising is for maintenance. Once Microsoft, Starbucks, and Wal-Mart became dominant brands, they shifted — by necessity — to advertising to defend their positions. Trying to sustain a forty-year-old brand purely on earned media is not realistic.
- **The product is not genuinely first or different in any meaningful way.** "The best way to generate publicity is by being first. In other words, by being the first brand in a new category." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* If you are the twelfth entrant in an established category with no legitimate angle of newness, there is no story for the media to tell. Publicity cannot be manufactured; it must be earned with something genuinely worth writing about.
- **The company tries to use advertising-style language in PR.** Press releases that read like ads do not generate media coverage. Publicity is third-party credibility. The moment it feels self-promotional, it stops working.

## Core Concept

"The birth of a brand is achieved with publicity, not advertising." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)*

Most of America's 15,000 advertising agencies are committed to building brands with advertising. They believe that better, fresher, more powerful creative work is the key to brand leadership. Ries is blunt about this: "Building brand leaders with better, fresher creative work? We think not. Most marketers confuse brand building with brand maintenance." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* Advertising can maintain a powerful brand. Advertising cannot give birth to one.

The evidence is hard to argue with. Anita Roddick built The Body Shop into a global brand with no advertising at all. She traveled the world pushing her ideas about the environment and ethical sourcing, generating a "relentless torrent of newspaper and magazine articles, plus radio and television interviews" that literally created the brand. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* Starbucks spent less than $10 million on advertising in its first ten years — a trivial amount for a brand that grew to $2.6 billion in annual sales. Wal-Mart became the world's largest retailer with annual sales over $200 billion with very little advertising. Sam's Club, a Wal-Mart sibling, averaged $45 million per store with almost no advertising. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)*

On the other side: Miller Brewing spent $50 million to launch a brand called Miller Regular. The brand generated "almost no perceptions in the minds of beer drinkers, and very little sales — $50 million down the drain." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* The problem was not the creative. The problem was structural: there was no publicity potential in a regular beer with a line-extended name. There was no story. No category. No first. Nothing for the media to amplify.

The reason publicity works where advertising cannot is credibility. "What others say about your brand is much more powerful than what you say about it yourself." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* Advertising is self-promotion. Every claim you make in a paid medium is received with at least mild skepticism, because prospects know you paid for the space and have an obvious interest in the message. A story in the Wall Street Journal, Business Week, or Forbes carries the implicit endorsement of an independent editor who decided the story was worth telling. That endorsement is worth more than any media buy.

"All of the big global marketing powerhouses — Microsoft, Intel, Dell, Compaq, Gateway, Oracle, Cisco, SAP, and Sun Microsystems — were first created in the pages of The Wall Street Journal, Business Week, Forbes, and Fortune. By publicity, not by advertising." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* These are not small exceptions. These are the defining brands of the technology era. Every one of them was born in editorial coverage of a genuinely new category or genuinely first product.

The key to publicity is being first. Not first in quality. Not first in features. First in a new category. Band-Aid was the first adhesive bandage. Charles Schwab was the first discount stockbrokerage. CNN was the first cable news network. Heineken was the first imported beer widely available in the United States. Hertz was the first car-rental company. Samuel Adams was the first microbrewed beer. Rollerblade was the first in-line skate. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* Every one of these firsts generated enormous publicity because the media wants to write about what is new, not about what is better.

"There's a strong relationship between the two. The news media wants to talk about what's new, what's first, and what's hot, not what's better. When your brand can make news, it has a chance to generate publicity. And the best way to make news is to announce a new category, not a new product." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)*

Now consider the sequence — because sequence matters enormously. "Today brands are built with publicity and maintained with advertising. The cart is now driving the horse." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* For years, brands formulated marketing strategies as advertising slogans first, then asked PR people to reinforce the advertising. That is backwards. Strategy must be developed first from a publicity point of view. If you cannot generate a credible, newsworthy story with your positioning, you do not yet have a viable brand strategy.

Advertising takes over when the publicity potential runs out. Sooner or later, every successful brand exhausts its story. Xerox pioneered the plain-paper copier in 1959 and generated hundreds of magazine and newspaper articles through two phases: the announcement of the new category, and then the rise of the company that pioneered it. By the time everyone knows Xerox invented xerography, there is no news story left to tell — so advertising takes over. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* Compaq, Dell, SAP, Oracle, Cisco, Microsoft, Starbucks, and Wal-Mart were all born in a blaze of publicity. As the publicity died out, each had to shift to massive advertising to defend its position.

The advertising budget at that maintenance stage is not an investment; it is insurance. "Your advertising budget is like a country's defense budget. Those massive advertising dollars don't buy you anything; they just keep you from losing market share to your competition." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* Leaders like Heinz, Budweiser, and Coca-Cola spend fortunes on advertising not because those dollars are building the brand but because failing to spend them would invite competitors to erode the position that was built with publicity years earlier.

Most companies develop their branding strategies as though advertising were the primary communications vehicle. "They're wrong. Strategy should be developed first from a publicity point of view." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)*

## How to Apply

1. **Before writing a brief, ask: is there a story here?** A story requires novelty. What category is this the first in? What legitimate claim to priority or difference exists? If the answer is "we're better than the competition," that is not a story — that is an advertising claim. You need a category claim: "the first X to do Y," "the only X built specifically for Z."

2. **Position the launch as a category creation, not a product launch.** Media covers categories and trends, not products. Lotus Notes became an enormous success because the PR strategy framed it as "the first successful groupware product" — not as a better piece of collaboration software. "This idea caught on like crazy with the media, which ran story after story on the new groupware concept." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* IBM ultimately paid $3.5 billion for Lotus Development Corp.

3. **Build the PR strategy first, then the advertising strategy.** Advertising should be designed to amplify and maintain what publicity establishes, not to pioneer it. Once you know what your category story is — and can prove it is genuinely new and first — then brief your advertising agency on how to build on that story.

4. **Identify the journalists and media properties that matter for your category.** Where does your target customer go to learn what is new in the world? Those are the outlets whose editors must find your story genuinely worth telling. Anita Roddick did not take out ads; she gave interviews. Howard Schultz did not run campaigns; he opened a coffee shop that was so unlike anything else that journalists came to it.

5. **Let the advertising maintain what the publicity builds.** Once your category story is established and the media cycle has run its course, shift investment to advertising that reinforces the leadership position publicity created. "What should a brand leader advertise? Brand leadership, of course. Leadership is the single most important motivating factor in consumer behavior." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* Heinz (America's favorite ketchup). Budweiser (king of beers). These are leadership claims that sustain the positions publicity established.

6. **Do not try to substitute advertising for publicity in a new launch.** Miller Regular is the cautionary tale. $50 million in advertising cannot substitute for a genuine category story. "There is no publicity potential in a regular beer with a line-extended name like Miller." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* If there is no publicity story, the advertising spend is waste.

## Examples

**Situation:** A fintech startup is launching a new type of business account designed specifically for freelancers and it has a modest budget. The question is whether to spend on Google Ads or on building a PR program.
**Application:** Freelancers are a large, underserved segment — that is a legitimate category story: "the first business account built from scratch for the independent economy." This is the kind of claim that editors at Fast Company, Inc., and Bloomberg Businessweek will find interesting. The strategy should be to land that story in tier-1 business press before spending a dollar on paid advertising. First-mover credibility in this category, established by a Wall Street Journal story, is worth more than any keyword campaign.
**Result:** The startup gets coverage that establishes it as the category creator. Prospects who search for freelance banking solutions encounter the editorial coverage before they encounter the ads. Third-party endorsement creates trust that advertising would take years and millions to approximate.

**Situation:** A consumer brand has launched a new line of products via a $2M advertising campaign. Sales are below forecast. The leadership team wants to increase the advertising budget.
**Application:** Before increasing the advertising budget, diagnose whether the advertising is doing a job it structurally cannot do. Ask: what category is this brand first in? What genuine news story drove any third-party coverage? If the answer is "we launched an ad campaign," that is a signal that the brand was built on advertising, not on publicity — meaning it was built on the wrong foundation. More advertising will not fix what publicity never established.
**Result:** Either the team finds the legitimate publicity story (the product IS first in something real) and pivots strategy to earn that coverage, or it accepts that the brand must compete as a second-or-third entrant and positions accordingly. More advertising without a genuine story typically produces diminishing returns.

**Situation:** An established market leader is watching a new focused competitor take share.
**Application:** The leader's job is now advertising, not publicity. The time for publicity is over — the brand is known. "Advertising is a powerful tool, not to build leadership of a fledgling brand, but to maintain that leadership once it is obtained." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* The leader should advertise its leadership ("America's #1 in X") loudly and consistently, making it expensive for the newcomer to erode the position. Raising the advertising floor is the correct defensive move.
**Result:** The advertising budget functions as insurance. The competitor must outspend an entrenched leader to move even a small amount of share — which is often economically untenable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the PR department as a support function for the advertising department.
**Why:** "For years public relations has been treated as a secondary function to advertising. PR people even used to measure their successes in terms of advertising space." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* This gets the hierarchy backwards. PR establishes brand credibility through third-party validation. Advertising can only amplify and maintain what PR first creates. When PR is subordinate to advertising, the strategy always starts from a paid message — which is the wrong foundation for a new brand.

**Don't:** Launch a new brand with only an advertising campaign, however large.
**Why:** "Today brands are born, not made. A new brand must be capable of generating favorable publicity in the media or it won't have a chance in the marketplace." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* The era when a beefy advertising budget was the key ingredient in brand-building is over. We live in an overcommunicated society; each prospect is hit with hundreds of commercial messages daily. Against that noise, paid messages have diminishing power. Third-party credibility cuts through.

**Don't:** Confuse "good advertising" with "brand-building advertising."
**Why:** "Advertising executives in particular are inclined to slight public relations. 'If the advertising is brilliant, the PR will fall out of that,' said one particularly brilliant advertising executive recently." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 3)* This is a category error. Brilliant advertising maintains an established brand. It does not create third-party credibility. It does not make a reporter decide your story is worth telling. The skillsets are different; the mechanisms are different; the results are different.

**Don't:** Use the advertising budget to defend against a PR attack.
**Why:** When a competitor is generating genuine editorial buzz and you respond only with advertising, you are fighting a battle of credibility with self-promotion. The correct response to a competitor's PR advantage is your own PR strategy — a more compelling story, a more specific category claim, or a direct challenge of the competitor's positioning in media. Advertising cannot substitute for the credibility gap.

**Don't:** Assume the publicity phase can last forever.
**Why:** "Publicity is a powerful tool, but sooner or later a brand outlives its publicity potential." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 4)* This is not a failure; it is the natural lifecycle. The mistake is clinging to publicity tactics past their usefulness. Microsoft and Starbucks shifted to advertising when they reached the maintenance phase. Refusing to make that shift — on principle, or to avoid advertising costs — leaves the brand exposed to competitive attack.
