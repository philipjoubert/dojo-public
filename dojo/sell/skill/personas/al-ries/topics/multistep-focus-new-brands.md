# Building a Multistep Focus with New Brand Names

## When to Use
- When a company's core category is dividing and a new segment has emerged that the core brand cannot credibly own.
- When growth requires entering a meaningfully different price tier, customer segment, or category.
- When the question is: do we extend the brand name or launch a new brand?
- When the answer to "what does our brand mean?" is incompatible with the new product.

## Fails When
- **Insufficient investment** — New brands require "not only money, but also an idea or concept." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* A new brand launched half-heartedly will fail. The failure then gets cited as evidence that new brands don't work, when the real evidence is that underfunded new brands don't work.
- **Too early in the category** — "Timing is also important. You can be too early to exploit a new category." *(22 Laws, Ch. 10)* The multistep approach requires a real category to step into.

## Core Concept

When a company wants to grow beyond its existing category, the instinct is to leverage the brand name it has built. The logic: we already have awareness, credibility, distribution, and goodwill — why not deploy those assets in a new direction? This logic is exactly wrong. The brand name is powerful because it means something specific. Using it in a new direction does not extend its power — it dilutes what the name means, weakening the original position while gaining nothing durable in the new one.

The alternative is the multistep focus: maintain the original brand in its original category, then step into the new category with a new brand name that can own its own position from scratch. "When Honda wanted to get into the luxury car market, it didn't call its expensive cars Honda Ultra or Honda Super or even Honda Automotive Science. It left the Honda name on the near bank and jumped the ditch with the 'Acura' name. So did Toyota with the Lexus brand." *(Focus, Coping with Change)*

The results are instructive in every dimension. When Honda launched Acura as the first Japanese luxury car in the United States, the new brand established a separate identity through separate dealerships. "Contrast Neon with Acura. When Honda decided to go upmarket, it didn't put its more expensive cars into its existing Honda dealer organization, even though this would have been the less expensive way to launch the new model. No, it set up a separate Acura dealer organization. This had two advantages. One, it maintained Honda's focus as an inexpensive Japanese car. And two, it allowed Acura to be established as the expensive, or luxury, Japanese car brand." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* "Now Honda has a single-focus strategy with two steps instead of one. Because Acura was first to establish the luxury Japanese car in the United States, it became the largest-selling brand in its category." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)*

Volkswagen made the opposite choice and paid the price. When the Beetle category aged, VW chose to follow the market upward under the same name — "Different Volks for different folks" — introducing the 412, the Dasher, the Sirocco, the Jetta, the Golf GL. "What destroyed the brand was the unfocusing effect of trying to market cars that were inconsistent with Volkswagen's perception in the mind." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* A prospect looks at a big, expensive Volkswagen and thinks, "That's not a Volkswagen." The car on the showroom floor doesn't match the perception in the mind. Volkswagen's 67 percent share of the U.S. imported car market collapsed to under 4 percent. A new brand at the higher price point — as Honda and Toyota did — would have preserved both positions.

The magazine business makes the principle transparent. "The magazine business is a good template for building a multistep focus." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* Time Inc. did not call its second magazine "Time for Business" — it called it Fortune. Life magazine wasn't "Time for Pictures." Sports Illustrated wasn't "Time for Sports." Money wasn't "Time for Finance." People wasn't "Time for Celebrities." "Time, Fortune, Life, Sports Illustrated, Money, People, Entertainment Weekly. Multiple steps in a corporate strategy with a single focus: news." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* "When you sacrifice these individual identities in order to emphasize the corporate connection, you are asking for problems." Saturday Review tried the opposite approach — Saturday Review: The Arts, Saturday Review: Education, Saturday Review: The Society, Saturday Review: The Sciences. "It was an unmitigated disaster. A little over a year later, Saturday Reviewed its situation and decided to call it a day, losing a reported $17 million in the process." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)*

The General Motors model — conceived by Alfred Sloan in 1921 — is the structural archetype. Faced with Ford's dominance, Sloan did not try to make Chevrolet do everything. He created a phalanx of separate brands, each at a non-overlapping price point: Chevrolet ($450–$600), Pontiac ($600–$900), Oldsmobile ($900–$1,200), Buick ($1,200–$1,700), Cadillac ($1,700–$2,500). "A car for every purse and purpose." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* By 1931, GM had overtaken Ford. For the next fifty years, GM controlled nearly half the U.S. market. The strategy worked as long as the brands remained distinct. When divisions drifted toward the same middle price range, the whole architecture collapsed.

## How to Apply

1. **Establish whether the existing brand can credibly own the new position.** Ask: what word does the existing brand own in the mind? Is the new product consistent with that word? "You can't change what the word Subaru means. You can't change what any word in the mind of a customer or prospect means. The only thing you can change is the word Subaru itself." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* If the new product contradicts the existing word, the only viable route is a new brand name.

2. **Create non-overlapping steps.** The Sloan model worked because there was no price overlap between divisions. "In the Sloan scheme, there were no price overlaps. No division competed directly against another, except to serve as the next step in the customer's climb up the ladder to Cadillac at the top." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* When General Motors allowed divisions to drift into the same price territory, each brand lost its identity. The lesson: define each step by the segment it owns exclusively.

3. **Set up separate operations where needed.** The most expensive but most effective move. "Honda allowed [Acura dealers] to sell the cheaper four-cylinder Integra model... One, it undermined Acura's focus. Is Acura a luxury car like the Legend, which sells for $36,000 to $44,000, or is Acura a middle-of-the-road car like the Integra, which sells for $16,000 to $21,000?" *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* When the budget forces sharing infrastructure between steps, be explicit about the trade-off and watch for focus erosion.

4. **Keep the brand name off the new product.** The impulse to attach the parent brand name is powerful — management wants the new brand to benefit from the parent's awareness. This is the line-extension trap in a multistep disguise. "There's nothing wrong with buying a brand to fit into a multistep focus. Too many acquisition-minded companies look for a 'fit' rather than a 'step.'" *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* Wrigley adds new gum brands (Juicy Fruit, Spearmint, Doublemint, Big Red, Extra, Freedent, Winterfresh) under different names within its single category focus — not "Wrigley's Big Red" but Big Red. Each is its own step.

5. **Treat each step as a separate brand investment.** "New models come and go, but new brands should be launched only rarely. But companies should keep in mind that the road to success is paved with brands, not models. New brands are expensive to be sure, but who ever said you could nickel-and-dime your way to success?" *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* The multistep approach requires patience. "It takes time for a focus to take hold. It takes time for prospects to learn the new brands and what they stand for." When GM's multistep strategy was launched in 1921, Ford still outsold GM for another decade. By 1931, GM passed Ford and never looked back.

## Examples

**Situation:** Toyota wanted to enter the U.S. luxury car market in the late 1980s. Toyota owned "reliable, affordable Japanese car." A "Toyota Luxury" would have contradicted that position.
**Application:** Toyota created Lexus as an entirely new brand — new name, separate dealer network, positioning aimed directly at Mercedes-Benz. "Toyota with the Lexus brand" jumped the ditch rather than walking across it under the old name. *(Focus, Coping with Change)*
**Result:** Lexus became the largest-selling import in the U.S. luxury car market. "Lexus sells only six-and eight-cylinder cars in the $32,000 to $51,000 price range. In luxury cars only, Lexus outsells Acura more than two to one." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* Toyota's core brand was preserved intact. Two positions, two brands, two market leaderships.

**Situation:** Time Inc. in the 1920s–1970s wanted to expand beyond its first magazine (Time, founded 1923) into other news verticals.
**Application:** Each new launch received a new brand name: Fortune (business news), Life (photographic news), Sports Illustrated (sports), Money (personal finance), People (celebrity news), Entertainment Weekly (entertainment news).
**Result:** "Time, Fortune, Life, Sports Illustrated, Money, People, Entertainment Weekly. Multiple steps in a corporate strategy with a single focus: news... Life was tripped up by television, but the other six remain powerful, profitable publications." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* The strategy created a portfolio of category leaders, not a diluted parent brand stretched across seven categories.

**Situation:** Wrigley, in the 20th century, wanted to grow its chewing gum business beyond its original three brands (Juicy Fruit, Spearmint, Doublemint).
**Application:** Each new trend in chewing gum received a new brand: Big Red for cinnamon, Extra for sugar-free, Freedent for stick-free, Winterfresh for breath freshening. No new product was called "Wrigley's Cinnamon" or "Wrigley's Sugar-Free."
**Result:** "Sales have risen every year, from $590 million in 1984 to $1.6 billion in 1994. Even more astonishing is the fact that net profit margins have steadily increased, from 7 percent of sales in 1984 to 14 percent today." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* Wrigley has half the U.S. chewing gum market. Multiple steps, single focus.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the "sub-brand" as a compromise.
**Why:** Sub-brands — "Honda Acura," "Toyota Luxury," "Saturday Review: The Arts" — are the worst of both worlds. The parent brand's associations contaminate the new brand's ability to own a fresh position. The new brand's existence dilutes the parent's meaning. "When you sacrifice these individual identities in order to emphasize the corporate connection, you are asking for problems." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* A sub-brand signals management's unwillingness to commit to either position fully.

**Don't:** Confuse a model name with a brand name.
**Why:** "There's an enormous difference between a brand like Volkswagen and models like Golf, Jetta, and Passat. Not so much in the dictionary but in the mind. When a prospect sees a strange automobile, the first question is 'What kind of a car is that?' If the answer is 'Passat,' the next question is 'Who makes the Passat?' 'Volkswagen makes the Passat.'" *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* A model is a descriptor within a brand's line. A brand is what the prospect files in their mind as a separate company making a distinct product. Neon sold 178,960 cars in its first year and created almost no brand recognition. Saturn sold far fewer but built an identity people talked about on the street.

**Don't:** Add steps that point in inconsistent directions.
**Why:** Acura added the NSX sports car to "create an image." "Why did Honda introduce the NSX? 'Mainly to create an image.' This is faulty thinking." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* Image cars at prices inconsistent with the brand's ladder position confuse rather than reinforce. The step must point in the direction of the overall focus — not back toward the parent brand, not sideways into an unrelated segment. Subaru's XT sports car was introduced as "the transition from cheap-and-ugly to upscale." Dealers loved it. Consumers bought it once out of curiosity, then stopped. "It's a common phenomenon. Whenever a company introduces a product totally different from its normal product line, the 'shock' value creates an initial burst of interest. After the shock wears off, so do sales." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)*
