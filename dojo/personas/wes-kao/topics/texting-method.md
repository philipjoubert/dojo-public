# The Texting Method

## When to Use
- The user is stuck on a writing task — a newsletter, blog post, talk script, customer email, internal memo — and can't get the first draft moving.
- The user has tried "talking it out" and ended up with rambling audio transcripts that don't translate to writing.
- The user is staring at a blank Google Doc and the cursor is winning.
- The user is trying to extract a draft out of a client, founder, or interview subject who "doesn't know what to say."

## Fails When
- The user treats text-method output as the final draft. This is a writer's-block dissolver, not a finished product. Always polish.
- The user texts a friend who's genuinely busy or doesn't have capacity. The receiver agreeing to listen is the foundation of the method.

## Core Concept

When you have writer's block, you have two default options. Both are bad.

**Talking out loud:** We talk fast. We say a lot. It's unfiltered and seems to make sense in the moment. But your tone of voice, speed, and pauses are verbal — they don't translate into a written medium. A full page of transcribed audio is often completely unusable.

**Staring at a blank page:** You think you have a lot to say, until you sit in front of your computer. The blank page. The blinking cursor. It's hard to know where to begin.

There's a third approach: **the Texting Method.** Type your ideas to a friend in a series of texts.

It works for three reasons:

**1. Prioritization is built in.** Texting with your thumbs takes more effort than talking or typing on a keyboard. Every word costs something. You unconsciously start asking "Is this worth typing out?" and the writing self-prioritizes.

**2. Physicality.** Texting is something you usually do with friends. The physical act puts you in a casual, informal state of mind. It's much easier to press send on a message to your friend than it is to hit publish on a blog post. The medium dissolves the block.

**3. Real audience.** Your natural instinct is self-preservation — once aimed at lions, now aimed at avoiding embarrassment. Knowing a real human will see your texts (even if they don't read them in real time) makes you try harder than typing to yourself. You're writing for someone, not into the void.

The genius of the method is that **the receiver doesn't have to do anything.** They don't have to read in real time. They don't have to ask questions. They don't even have to read the texts at all. You still get the benefit of writing to a real audience.

A note on scripts: when you have a good draft from texting, save it. But don't worship it. **The script is a means to an end** — the end is to close the sale, build rapport, make eyes light up, get the idea across. When you over-rely on a script, you stop reading the room. Wes's husband Zak ran a college painting business and gave students sales scripts. One day a student asked an elderly customer "Will all decision makers be present?" The customer said, "Well, my wife died." The student, glued to the script, said, "Soo… will all decision makers be present?" Cringe. The script became a crutch. Use the texting method to find your draft, then keep your brain on when you actually deploy it.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick a friend.** Someone you'd casually text anyway. Not a writing partner, not an editor — a friend.
2. **Ask if they're up for the Texting Method.** Frame it: "I'm working on [thing] and stuck. Can I text you my ideas? You don't have to read or reply — I just want a real audience."
3. **Be open to reciprocating.** If they want to try it later, say yes.
4. **Start texting.** Don't outline. Just text the idea like you're explaining it to a friend who's curious. One thumb, one thought at a time.
5. **Don't expect a reply.** If they react ("hmm interesting"), great. If not, also great. The point is your output, not their input. Resist the urge to wait for validation.
6. **Resist the urge to invite questions.** If your friend asks questions, the questions themselves lead you down their paths, not yours. The whole point of the texting method is to surface what's already in YOU. (You can use questions later, when you're ghostwriting for someone else and need to draw THEIR ideas out.)
7. **Move to a doc.** Once you have a stream of texts you can work with, copy/paste into a Google Doc. Now you have a baseline.
8. **Polish.** Apply the sharper-writing toolkit: robot voice, delete fillers, flip negatives, check the last word of each paragraph, etc. The text version was the casual draft. The doc version is the published one.

## Examples

**Situation:** Wes is trying to write a newsletter post about feedback but keeps starting and deleting paragraphs.

**Application:** Texts a friend: "Ok I want to write about how nobody is good at receiving feedback and that's actually the bigger lever than learning to give it. Like — we obsess over how to deliver feedback gently but the real bottleneck is the receiver getting defensive. If you got even slightly better at hearing feedback you'd be ahead of 90% of people because everyone else is terrible at it. Most people think being open to feedback means saying 'thanks.' That's not it. It's about staying curious when your gut is screaming defend-yourself…" Five more texts in this register. Friend doesn't reply.

**Result:** Wes copies the texts into a doc. The structure is already there: claim, contrarian frame, mechanism, definition. Polish takes 30 minutes instead of 3 hours.

---

**Situation:** A founder is trying to write a Substack intro post and has been stuck for two weeks.

**Application:** Texts a college friend instead of opening Google Docs. "So basically I started this newsletter because I kept giving the same advice 5x a week and I figured I should write it down once. Here's the gap I see — most ops content is either super tactical (use this tool) or super abstract (think strategically). Nobody is teaching the middle layer, like how to actually decide what to say in a meeting…" Twelve texts later, they have an outline.

**Result:** The Substack post writes itself in an afternoon. The casual register from the texts becomes the voice of the published piece.

---

**Situation:** A ghostwriter needs to write in a client's voice for a thought leadership post. The client is busy, hates writing, and freezes when asked to draft.

**Application:** Use the Texting Method in reverse. Drive the conversation forward with follow-up questions. "What's a hill you'd die on about [topic]? Why? Tell me about the moment you realized that. Who pushed back on you? What did they say?" The client texts answers without realizing they're producing content.

**Result:** The ghostwriter has the client's authentic phrasing, examples, and convictions — captured in their actual voice — without the client ever opening a Google Doc.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the texting method on someone who hasn't agreed to be the receiver.
**Why:** A friend who's blindsided by 30 stream-of-consciousness texts about your essay topic is going to be confused or annoyed. The agreement is what makes the method casual instead of stressful. Ask first.

**Don't:** Wait for your friend to engage.
**Why:** The whole point is that the receiver does nothing. If you wait for them to react before sending the next text, you've reintroduced the validation loop you were trying to escape. Send the texts as if they're a one-way stream. If they engage, bonus. If not, fine.

**Don't:** Send the raw texts as the final piece.
**Why:** Texting register is great for getting through the block, but it's not publication-ready. It's casual, sometimes ungrammatical, often missing structure. The texts are scaffolding. The polish is a separate step.

**Don't:** Outline before texting.
**Why:** Outlining is the thing you couldn't do — that's why you have block. The texting method bypasses outlining by going straight to "explain it to a friend." If you could already outline, you wouldn't need this technique. Trust the medium.

**Don't:** Ask for feedback in the same thread.
**Why:** "Does this make sense?" or "Is this any good?" pulls you back into self-criticism mode, which is what the method is escaping. Save feedback for a later round, with an editor or a different friend.

**Don't:** Treat the texting method as a substitute for editing.
**Why:** It's a draft-generator. It dissolves the block. The actual craft work — sharpening sentences, cutting fillers, reading in robot voice, structuring — still has to happen. The texting method gets you to a starting line; it doesn't get you to the finish.

**Don't:** Use it for high-stakes async messages directly.
**Why:** A casual, unedited text-style message to your CEO or a customer can read as sloppy or unprofessional. Use the method to draft. Then run the sharper-writing toolkit before pressing send.
