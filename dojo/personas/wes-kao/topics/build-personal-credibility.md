# Build Personal Credibility, Not Personal Brand

## When to Use
- The user is deciding whether to invest time in a personal-brand strategy (Twitter posting, podcast, ghost-written newsletter, paid PR).
- The user is trimming or expanding a portfolio, bio, LinkedIn, or "About" page and unsure what to include.
- The user is considering whether to take on a low-quality side project to "stay visible."
- The user is debating sharing a failure publicly, "building in public," or doing a vulnerability-driven post.
- The user feels stuck — solid work, no opportunities — and wonders if they need to be louder.

## Fails When
- The user has zero distribution and is using "credibility over brand" as an excuse to never publish anything. — Wes's whole framework assumes you eventually show your work to people who can verify it; quietly being good in a vacuum doesn't compound.
- The user is in a field (entertainment, performance, media) where brand and craft are intertwined. — The categories blur; the framework still holds but the calibration is different.
- The user already has strong credibility and now needs distribution to amplify. — At that stage the question flips, and adding a brand layer to verified substance is appropriate.

## Core Concept

Wes hates the phrase "personal branding."

Remember school projects? There was always that one kid who didn't do much work but was great at presenting. The teacher thought they did all the work. Personal branding reminds her of that person — now all grown up, still talking their way through life. A lot of good people are too busy actually doing the work to spend energy managing the optics of it. Unfortunately, this means well-deserving people often get the least credit.

The reframe: stop aiming for personal branding. Start aiming for personal credibility.

🚫 **Personal branding** has a superficial undertone. It assumes you have your work, then you tack on an artificial layer of "branding" to shape perceptions. It doesn't respect the intelligence of your audience because it assumes people will believe whatever brand you put out there.

✅ **Personal credibility** is about substance first. Then showing the folks around you what you can do and how you can contribute. It's about earning trust to do more of the work you want to do.

When you have a strong brand, you might get likes. When you have strong credibility, you have a deeper connection with people who believe in your work — which means more options, more control, and a more fulfilling career. There are lots of people who don't have strong personal brands but have strong personal credibility. Google them and you might not find much. But they have plenty of interesting opportunities in the circles and networks they belong to.

That counts for a lot.

Three sub-frameworks shape how you build credibility deliberately:

1. **Average, not cumulative**: people judge the average of your achievements, not the sum.
2. **Heroes on Stage**: pick a scope big enough to feel like blasphemy, specific enough to be defensible.
3. **Failure punctuates success — it doesn't replace it**: vulnerability lands when it's spoken from a baseline of wins.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Build the things that make people believe you're credible

Credibility is the residue of work people can verify. Wes's list of what builds it:

- Social proof, testimonials, warm referrals
- Accolades, awards, a track record of contribution
- Good design and strong writing
- Being articulate (in talks, interviews, comments)
- Work samples — actual artifacts of what you've done
- A strong, named point of view
- A body of work that shows your thinking over time

These are the same signals that make *you* think someone else is credible. Reverse-engineer: what makes you think "this person knows their craft"? Then build those things for yourself.

### Step 2: Drop the weakest item from your portfolio (Average, not Cumulative)

When we want to impress, we feel the urge to list everything we've ever done. We think more equals stronger. It's the opposite. People judge the average of your achievements, not the sum — and adding a weaker item drags the average down.

🚫 **Cumulative**: 3-time world champion in Brazilian jiu jitsu, computer science major, beginner at watercolor painting, beginner at choir singing, beginner at the trombone.

✅ **Average**: 3-time world champion in Brazilian jiu jitsu, computer science major, fluent in French.

Or with clients:

🚫 **Cumulative**: Worked with Nike, widgetsforcheap.com, Warby Parker, cogsandwidgets.com, Microsoft, morewidgets.co, widgets4u.com. (7 things, 3 strong → 42% strong. Some sound spammy. Reader isn't sure how legit any of it is.)

✅ **Average**: Worked with Nike, Warby Parker, Microsoft. (3 things, all 3 strong → 100% strong. Reader assumes the unlisted ones were similar caliber and you ran out of room.)

The next time you're debating whether to add an accomplishment, ask: would adding this *increase my average*?

The exception: you can humanize with a weakness if you're already established. When a head of state says they're terrible at physics but love YouTube videos about it, it doesn't diminish their power — it makes them relatable. Calibrate for your stage.

### Step 3: Run the Heroes on Stage exercise

Most of us think too small. Personal credibility isn't about getting more likes on your next tweet or describing your niche better. It's about your body of work and legacy in a chosen field. *How will your field be different after you, because of you?*

The exercise:

1. Imagine you're invited to speak at a panel at the biggest event in your industry.
2. The other panelists are leaders who have shaped your field. Pick 3-4 people you admire, where you think *"I hope to one day be on their level."*
3. Jot down what each person represents in the field. Then jot down what *you* represent. What's a scope where you would feel comfortable debating with your heroes?

You're on stage with them as an *equal*. Not a moderator. Not an audience member.

This should feel like blasphemy. *"So-and-so is a god. I couldn't possibly be on stage with them as an equal!"* Suspend disbelief. The point of the exercise is to break through the noise in your head and force you to think bigger.

The scope has to be specific enough to differentiate you from the existing giants. If your topic is negotiation and Chris Voss, William Ury, and Stuart Diamond are on the panel, you can't just say "negotiation" — they all do that. You have to be able to say "Ah, I hear you Chris, but the thing is, we have to think through the lens of X. We have to consider Y." That X is your differentiated scope.

And the topic isn't "taken." Every time someone said "the topic is taken," another person rose up and claimed a scope inside it. Each person on stage with you wouldn't be there if they stopped because their topic was claimed. There's room — for them, and for you.

### Step 4: Lean into your own texture

Stop fighting your instincts. Go deeper into what you're naturally drawn to. Wes's heuristic: we spend a lot of time wishing we had someone else's skill set. The Heroes on Stage exercise is about leaning into what *you* already do better than others. Your work isn't just a recombination of the people you've studied — over time you've borrowed, read, lived, tried, failed, tested, walked, run, stumbled. You have your own approach, your own texture, your own instincts. Use them.

### Step 5: Punctuate success with failure — never lead with it

Startup culture glorifies failure. Edison "found 10,000 ways that won't work" — but the part not on the motivational poster is that he eventually invented the light bulb, holds 1,000+ patents, and is called America's greatest inventor. He was, in fact, successful.

In a work setting where stakes are involved, most people are better off highlighting their successes. Society doesn't judge all failures equally. From a practical standpoint, be discerning about sharing stories of failure.

The rule: **failure should punctuate a strong track record**.

A rich person says "I struggled a lot. Now I'm here doing this cool thing." → "Rags-to-riches story, I'm so inspired."

A broke person says "I struggled a lot. Actually, I'm still struggling." → "I feel sad."

The successful people in the media talking about how often they've failed are *speaking from a position of success*. Failure stories punctuate a baseline history of wins, give them depth of character, and make them more relatable.

Marc Randolph, Netflix cofounder, can post on LinkedIn about being wrong about many things at Netflix. The mistake would be an early-stage founder reading that and thinking "I want to mimic Marc verbatim because he's being vulnerable, and I'll proclaim I'm usually wrong too." The messenger and the messenger's track record matter. If you simply proclaim you're wrong all the time, you don't inspire confidence in investors, employees, or customers — you just seem like you have poor instincts.

You don't want to be vulnerable at the expense of your goal — at work, the goal is generally inspiring confidence in your abilities so your startup gets funded, you get hired, your manager trusts you more.

For interview candidates: over-emphasizing failure makes you seem like a risky hire. The hiring manager thinks: *Wow, I appreciate that they shared this, but it was kind of a poor judgment call, they missed obvious signs, and it probably could have been avoided. Maybe we should pick another candidate with better judgment.*

### Step 6: Audit before publishing

Before posting anything publicly, run two checks:

1. **Average check**: Does this raise or lower the average impression of my work?
2. **Position check**: Am I speaking from a baseline of success that earns the right to be vulnerable, or am I just venting / performing humility / chasing sympathy?

If either fails, don't post. Build more substance first.

## Examples

**Situation:** A senior operator with strong work but no public presence is being passed over for board roles. They're considering hiring a ghostwriter to build a "thought leadership" Twitter presence.

**Application:** Wes's lens: pause the brand push and audit credibility first. Do they have testimonials and warm referrals from the industry's most respected people? A named POV they can articulate in 60 seconds? Work samples that demonstrate craft? If those are weak, ghostwritten Twitter content will get likes but won't move the needle on board roles, because boards verify with backchannels. Build the credibility layer (1-2 keynotes at industry events, 1-2 deeply-considered written pieces, warm intros to the right people), *then* layer brand on top to amplify.

**Result:** Board opportunities surface from the credibility work itself — not from the Twitter feed. The brand push, if it ever happens, has substance to point at.

---

**Situation:** Founder is updating their bio. Current draft lists 7 customers — three Fortune 500, four obscure startups.

**Application:** Average vs. cumulative. Cut the four obscure startups. The reader will see "Worked with [3 Fortune 500 names]" and assume the unlisted clients were similar caliber. Listing all seven dilutes to 42% strong and makes the reader wonder how legit any of it is.

**Result:** Tighter bio, higher perceived credibility, better warm-intro response rate.

---

**Situation:** Early-stage founder reads Marc Randolph's vulnerable LinkedIn post about being wrong at Netflix and wants to write something similar.

**Application:** Stop. Wes's diagnostic: Marc is post-economic. He has decades of validated instinct. His "I was wrong" lands as humble seasoning on a baseline of obvious success. An early-stage founder doing the same gets read as "this person doesn't know what they're doing." At the early stage, the only thing you're selling is your instinct as a founder — don't undermine it. Save vulnerability for after you've earned the right to wield it.

**Result:** Founder writes a different post — about a sharp insight from running their company — that builds credibility instead of leaking it.

---

**Situation:** An aspiring negotiation expert says their topic is "taken" because Chris Voss has cornered the market.

**Application:** Heroes on Stage exercise. Put Voss, William Ury, Stuart Diamond, and themselves on a panel. Each panelist has a different angle — FBI background, Harvard-academic, specific-tactical-frameworks. The aspiring expert's job is to find the X they uniquely bring to the table. Maybe it's negotiation in cross-cultural startup contexts, or negotiation for technical hires, or negotiation in family businesses. Specific enough to differentiate, big enough to feel like blasphemy to claim equal status.

**Result:** A defensible, specific scope that no existing giant occupies. The aspiring expert can now publish, speak, and build credibility around that exact scope without being mistaken for a Voss imitator.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pad your portfolio with mediocre work to look prolific.
**Why:** Average, not cumulative. Five strong projects beats fifteen mixed-quality ones every time. The weak items pull the average down and make readers question how to interpret the strong ones.

**Don't:** Lead with vulnerability before you've established a track record.
**Why:** "Failure should punctuate a strong track record." Speaking from a position of success makes failure stories sound like depth of character. Speaking from a position of struggle makes them sound like incompetence. The same words land differently depending on the messenger's verified history.

**Don't:** Define your scope as "marketing" or "leadership" — too broad to be defensible.
**Why:** Personal credibility depends on choosing a scope that's believable and defensible. Claiming "marketing" feels weird because no one can credibly be an expert in a field that broad. Pick a scope you'd be willing to defend on a panel with your three heroes.

**Don't:** Pick "near-peer" comparisons in the Heroes on Stage exercise.
**Why:** The point is to break through the size of your current ambition. Pick people who feel like gods. Putting yourself on stage as an equal with someone two steps ahead doesn't force you to think bigger.

**Don't:** Use "I'm not a personal-brand person" as cover for never showing your work.
**Why:** Credibility requires that someone, somewhere, can verify what you've done. Quiet excellence in a closed network can compound for a while, but the framework assumes you're shipping artifacts (writing, talks, samples, testimonials) that the right people can find. Don't confuse "no brand" with "no signal."

**Don't:** Mistake follower count for credibility.
**Why:** A strong personal brand gets likes. Strong credibility gets opportunities. The two correlate sometimes, but they're not the same thing — and chasing likes will not get you the deeper connections that produce real career options.

**Don't:** Add a hobby or weak skill to your bio because you think it humanizes you, when you're not yet established.
**Why:** Famous leaders mention what they're bad at to seem relatable — *because their power is already established*. Earlier-career folks who do this just dilute their average. Save the humanizing texture for after the substance is locked in.

**Don't:** Confuse "post-economic" signaling with credibility-building.
**Why:** A bio that "shows you DGAF" (Chrissy Teigan: "demotivational speaker") signals you've made it. Until then, give people reasons to engage with you. The dry-humor bio works only when everyone already knows who you are. Be boring and substantive until you've earned the right to be playful.
