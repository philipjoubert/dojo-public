# Activation Energy

## When to Use
- User is choosing between an elaborate launch plan and a scrappy one.
- User wants to start a podcast, newsletter, event, or content series.
- User has a track record of starting things that don't reach critical mass.
- User is asking "should we do this?" and what they really need is "can we sustain this?"

## Fails When
- **The project has no clear feedback signal.** — Activation energy assumes you can detect when the reaction starts. If the metric is fuzzy ("brand awareness, eventually"), you'll never know whether you hit threshold or not.
- **You're already past the threshold.** — A founder with a million-follower audience launching a podcast doesn't need to worry about activation energy. The constraint is taste, not reach.
- **It's a one-shot tactic, not a system.** — A single press release or Super Bowl ad doesn't have a sustained activation energy in the same sense. The framework is for repeated, system-shaped efforts.

## Core Concept

Activation energy is a term from chemistry. If you mix diluted hydrochloric acid with carefully measured strips of magnesium, you'll get hydrogen — but only if you have enough of each. Below threshold, the reaction doesn't happen. You get silence.

Many projects work the same way. There's a level of energy you have to put in before you start to see feedback — negative or positive. Maybe you're an inch away from a result. Maybe you're a mile away. Either way, below threshold: silence.

🚫 "Let's just try a podcast and see if it works."
✅ "Let's only start a podcast if we can run it consistently enough to hit activation energy."

The core move: **only start something if you're going to reach the activation energy of that project.** Match the resources you have to the resources the project actually requires.

If you want to start an airline, find out how much money it took for other airlines to start. If you don't have that, pick another idea — you have plenty of ideas. A podcast can be a great idea if you plan to be consistent enough to build a following. My friend Rich did 600 episodes in two years and is now the center of an AI/blockchain community. Launching a podcast and only doing scattered episodes probably won't get the results you're looking for.

Some efforts have softer thresholds. Working out for general health doesn't have a strict activation energy — any movement beats sitting all day. But if you want six-pack abs, there's a real threshold: a certain frequency and intensity of exercise plus diet, before abs appear. Most marketing tactics behave more like the abs case than the general-fitness case.

**Events have activation energy.** A nightclub doesn't work with seven people sipping drinks looking around an empty room. A floor overflowing with a line out the door has hit threshold — everyone has a more enjoyable time because it feels like what a club should feel like.

Activation energy lets you pick tactics with more awareness. **Design tactics around the resources you have.** A webinar should work if five people show up or fifty. Digital events have a leg up. For an in-person talk, it's always better to have a smaller room and standing-room-only than a half-empty big room — same number of people, different activation level. If you only have ten attendees, perfect: the activation energy of a fireside chat is about ten people. You changed the activation energy of the format so you could hit threshold.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the project's activation threshold honestly.** What's the minimum input that would actually produce a noticeable output? Not what you wish were enough — what is genuinely enough.
2. **Look at examples of others who succeeded at this same project.** What did it take them? 600 episodes? $50M of capital? Three years of weekly content? If your plan is 5% of theirs, you're not going to hit threshold.
3. **Audit your real resources — not aspirational ones.** How much time per week can you actually sustain over a full year, not the first three weeks of motivation? How much budget will the team protect when the next emergency hits?
4. **Match the format to your actual capacity.** Don't pick a tactic that requires 10x the energy you can sustain. If you have time for one good newsletter a month, don't promise weekly. If you can run a 10-person fireside chat, don't book the 200-person conference room.
5. **If you can't reach threshold, pick a different tactic — not a smaller version of the wrong one.** A scattered podcast is not a small podcast — it's a failed podcast. A small fireside chat is a complete fireside chat.
6. **For events: design for the lower bound.** If the format works at five attendees and at fifty, you're safe either way. If it requires fifty and you get five, the event is dead in the room.
7. **Re-check threshold quarterly.** Conditions shift. A podcast that needed weekly cadence in 2018 might need different inputs in 2025. The threshold isn't fixed.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder wants to launch a thought-leadership podcast. They're considering an elaborate format — produced episodes, guest pre-interviews, two episodes a month for the next year.

**Application:** "Look at what 'works' looks like in podcasting. Most podcasts that build communities are putting out 50+ episodes a year, consistently, for at least a year before signal shows up. Two a month for one year is 24 episodes. Below threshold. Either commit to weekly for two years, or pick a different tactic — say, a weekly LinkedIn post, where the activation energy matches what you can actually sustain."

**Result:** The founder does the LinkedIn post weekly for 18 months instead. Audience grows. They didn't waste a year producing a podcast nobody found.

**Situation:** Marketing team plans a flagship in-person event for 200 people. Three months out, RSVPs are at 35.

**Application:** "Don't run a 200-person event with 35 people in a giant room. The activation energy isn't met — the room will feel dead, attendees will tell each other 'this was kind of empty,' and word-of-mouth will work against the next one. Switch the format. Move to a smaller venue. Run it as a 35-person fireside chat where the activation energy matches the headcount. Same audience, completely different feel."

**Result:** Attendees describe the event as intimate and high-quality. Future events benefit from the social proof.

**Situation:** Team is debating whether to start a paid newsletter, a free newsletter, a YouTube channel, or a Twitter posting cadence — all at once.

**Application:** "You can sustain one of these. Possibly two. Pick the one whose activation energy matches your real bandwidth, do it well, hit threshold. Then add the next. Starting four below-threshold projects gets you four silent reactions."

**Result:** Team picks one channel, hits threshold, and the audience compounds. The other three get queued.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Start a project at "let's just try it" energy and convince yourself that's enough.
**Why:** "Just trying" is below the activation threshold of almost every project worth doing. You'll quit at month three with no signal, conclude the tactic doesn't work, and move on — when really you never reached the reaction. Either commit to threshold or don't start.

**Don't:** Compare your output to competitors' output and ignore that they hit threshold and you haven't.
**Why:** A competitor with 200 podcast episodes is operating in a different physics. You can't benchmark against them at episode 12. You're comparing pre-reaction silence to post-reaction signal.

**Don't:** Pick the most ambitious format because it's impressive on paper.
**Why:** A 30-person dinner that hits activation energy beats a 500-person conference that doesn't. Pick the format whose threshold you can actually clear, not the one with the biggest room.

**Don't:** Use activation energy as an excuse to never start anything.
**Why:** The framework is for choosing the right tactic, not for paralysis. If every option fails the activation energy check, the answer isn't "do nothing" — it's "design a smaller-threshold version of the same outcome." Fireside chat instead of conference. Local meetup instead of global summit.

**Don't:** Confuse "we shipped it" with "we hit threshold."
**Why:** Shipping a podcast is different from sustaining a podcast. Activation energy is a function of input over time, not output at a single moment. The reaction is in the consistency, not the launch.
