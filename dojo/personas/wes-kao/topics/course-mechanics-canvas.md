# Course Mechanics Canvas

## When to Use
- The user is starting a cohort-based course from scratch and is overwhelmed by interdependent decisions (price × length × student count × intensity all influence each other).
- The user has run a cohort or two and something feels off — engagement, completion, retention, willingness to pay — and they need a structured way to debug which lever is misaligned.
- The user is benchmarking against a course they admire (altMBA, On Deck, Section4, Build a Second Brain) and trying to decide what to copy and what to leave.
- The user is moving from a one-directional product (book, blog, podcast, MOOC) to a cohort-based course and underestimating the bar.

## Fails When
- The user hasn't yet picked a student transformation. — The whole canvas pivots on "where is the student now" vs. "where do they want to be," and without that the levers have no center of gravity.
- The user wants permission to copy another course wholesale. — Wes's whole point is that you have different assets and constraints than the person you're copying. Doing exactly what someone else did will produce different results.
- The user treats a CBC like passive income. — Even Seth Godin's "no-instructor-involvement" altMBA required a high-performing team and 1:10 coach ratio. The leverage comes from design, not absence of work.

## Core Concept

A cohort-based course is an interdependent system. The parameters influence the cost. The cost influences the parameters. Pull one lever and three others move. New course creators try to attack each variable in isolation or in sequence and end up paralyzed.

The Course Mechanics Canvas is twelve levers you can plot on spectrums:

1. **Length**
2. **Number of students**
3. **Price**
4. **Intensity**
5. **Project-driven**
6. **Group interaction**
7. **Coach involvement**
8. **Instructor involvement**
9. **Production value**
10. **Pre-recorded content**
11. **Application process**
12. **Cohort frequency**

The canvas is not a recipe. It's a diagnostic. Use it to make decisions confidently, debug a struggling course, and have shared language with collaborators about why your CBC is set up the way it is.

🚫 "How long should my course be? How much should I charge? How many students?"
✅ "What's the student transformation? Given that, what assets do I have? Given those, where on each spectrum does my course need to land?"

The chicken-and-egg trap dissolves once you start with student transformation and assets, then let those anchor the rest of the levers in sequence.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Define the student transformation (this is the anchor)

Two questions:
1. What transformation do you want to create in your students?
2. What will help your ideal student achieve their outcome?

Plot where students are now (`x`) and where they want to be (`y`). The greater the distance, the more leverage you need — meaning higher intensity, more projects, more coaching, more instructor or peer involvement.

Distinguish a binary promise from a spectrum promise:

✅ **Binary promise** ("Write your first TV pilot script"): you either have the script or you don't. Higher bar. More levers needed.

📈 **Spectrum promise** ("Improve your script writing, sharpen your craft"): any movement toward `y` is value. Lower bar. Levers can be lighter.

Most courses are spectrum promises. That's fine. Just don't accidentally market a binary promise while delivering a spectrum experience — students will feel cheated.

### Step 2: Take stock of assets and constraints

You're not starting from a blank slate. This is good news. Your existing assets pre-narrow the levers for you.

Assets that matter:
- Great at your craft
- Audience on Twitter / email list of engaged subscribers
- Strong relationships and warm referrals
- Reputation in your industry
- Existing body of work that shows your thinking
- Frameworks and ideas you've already named
- Charismatic personality / great speaking voice
- Teaching ability (distilling complex topics)
- Spiky points of view about your topic
- Money to invest, time to invest

Wes's heuristic: you don't need a big audience. You need a distribution channel to reach your target students, credibility with that audience, and a juicy value proposition. Audience size is a proxy, but only because the creator has spent years adding free value. "It's lazy thinking to point to a creator with 50k followers and say 'Well it's easy for them to teach.' Actually, they just started the process years before you did."

🚫 **Person A**: An operator who is mediocre at their craft, hasn't written anything online, doesn't have many relationships—and now wants to teach a course.
✅ **Person B**: An operator who has built a reputation, has been building their email list, and has sharpened their skills—and now wants to teach a course.

### Step 3: Start with the lever you care most about

Don't try to decide all twelve at once. Pick the one you have an intuitive read on — often price or student count or intensity — and let it serve as an anchor.

These early choices serve as anchors. You can always change them, but pick some. Lock in one, then walk through the trade-offs with the second, third, fourth. By lever five, the rest of the canvas will start to take shape on its own.

### Step 4: Look at the lever groupings (they move in bundles)

The twelve levers cluster into three high-leverage groupings.

**Grouping 1 — Intensity, Project-driven, Group Interaction.**
Courses promising high transformation tend to be high on all three. The altMBA was 15-20 hours/week with mandatory group sessions three times a week — that turns off casual learners and attracts the people who want a drill sergeant. Low-intensity courses (AliveOS, On Deck) can still be transformative, but the value comes from a different place — vulnerability and reflection, or the network. Choose the bundle that matches what your students are actually paying for.

Projects and group discussions create active learning, which is scarce relative to free content. If your course is just lectures and content, why should anyone pay? They'd take a $25 video course or read a free article. Even short exercises, Zoom breakouts, Slack reflection prompts count as "projects" in this sense.

**Grouping 2 — Number of students, coach involvement, instructor involvement.**
Design the course for the eventual student count, not the first cohort. The dynamic is entirely different at 10-15 students vs. 50+. With a small group, instructor charisma fills gaps. With a large group, you need structure or the experience falls apart.

Once you cross 50 students, coaches add leverage. The altMBA ran a 1:10 coach ratio. Section4 runs 1:100. Neither is "right" — it depends on your promise.

A general rule: the lower your instructor involvement, the higher your coach involvement must be. And designing a course where students get more value from peers and coaches than from you is harder, but it's worth it. That's where the leverage lives.

**Grouping 3 — Length and price.**
Making a course longer to justify the price is lazy. Avoid it. Some students think "if I'm paying this much I want it to last longer," but others think "I'd rather pay more to get to my goal faster." Know which type of student you're selling to. Price below or above $1,000 is a psychological line — three vs. four figures. Most CBCs land at 2-4 weeks or 6-8 weeks.

### Step 5: Run the rubber-band exercise

Stretch yourself to plot scenarios you wouldn't naturally pick. Imagine your course as:

- 3 days, 3 weeks, 3 months
- 15, 150, 500 students
- $700, $1,500, $5,000

Force yourself through each combination. Even if your only insight is what you don't want, you'll feel more confident about what you do want. Many times the new options end up more appealing than you thought.

### Step 6: Use the canvas to debug, not just design

Every lever has trade-offs. Use them to reverse-engineer what's broken:

- **Completion is dropping?** Check intensity vs. student commitment. The altMBA hit 96% completion (vs. 4% MOOC average) by being mandatory-synchronous, having a tight 1:10 coach ratio, and an application that selected for readiness.
- **Engagement on Zoom is dying?** Group interaction lever is too low. Add active learning.
- **Willingness to pay is soft?** Length-and-price grouping is misaligned with the perceived value, or your value prop is a vitamin (nice to have) instead of a painkiller (urgent problem).
- **Instructor is burned out?** Instructor-involvement lever is too high. Add coaches. Train peers. Build mechanisms (peer feedback prompts, structured projects) that don't require you to show up.
- **Cohort feels overwhelming?** Break into sub-cohorts (altMBA did this — concurrent groups of ~120 each, identical logistics, no compromise on intimacy).

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder wants to launch a high-priced premium course but hasn't decided between high or low instructor involvement.

**Application:** Wes anchors three levers first — intensity, price, instructor involvement — because they're the most consequential. For the altMBA the team picked: high intensity, premium price, near-zero instructor involvement. That forced a cascade: project prompts had to be exceptional (no instructor halo to lean on), there had to be a real team running operations, and a 1:10 coach ratio was non-negotiable. From there, length, student count, and frequency fell into place. "It had to be high intensity because, to use a hyperbolic example, a one hour conversation is usually not enough to change someone long term." It had to be premium because they wanted students who'd take it seriously and they had resources to invest. Once those three anchors were set, the rest of the canvas plotted itself.

**Result:** The altMBA hit 96% completion at $4,500 for 4 weeks, run 4x per year with sub-cohorts of ~120 each. The instructor (Seth) didn't show up to lectures and there were no pre-recorded videos — but the course still ran, because the canvas was internally consistent.

---

**Situation:** Section4 with Professor Galloway. Goal: appeal to a wide range of working professionals who want practical strategy lessons.

**Application:** Wes (who advised on the format) inverted altMBA's choices. Length was 2 weeks instead of 4. Intensity was a sweet spot — leveraged but not overwhelming, because if it were as intense as altMBA, working professionals couldn't participate. The team had high production value as an existing asset (animators, designers, motion graphics), so they pulled the production lever hard — which justified more pre-recorded content than average and a 1:100 TA ratio. Price started at $500/student, then iterated to $650, then $750.

**Result:** A repeatable, scalable sprint format that runs many cohorts per year at 1,000 students each, with sub-cohorts to keep intimacy. Same canvas, different assets, completely different course — both work.

---

**Situation:** First-time creator, mediocre at their craft, no audience, no email list, wants to teach a $2,000 cohort-based course on a generic topic.

**Application:** Per Wes's framework: "You are not starting from a blank slate. Some assets are available to you, and some aren't practical choices for you." This person doesn't have the credibility lever, the distribution lever, or the spiky-point-of-view lever. The canvas tells them: don't open at $2,000 with a premium 8-week format. Either build credibility for 12-18 months first (write online, ship a body of work, sharpen the craft), or start with a lower-priced, shorter beta cohort and use it as customer development. Position it as a beta, gradually increase price.

**Result:** Avoids the trap of pricing at the level Person B can charge while only having Person A's assets. The course either gets built on a sustainable trajectory or the creator realizes early that they need to build a different lever first.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Make your course longer because you feel like it's the only way students will pay.
**Why:** Length is a lever, not a justification mechanism. If you genuinely think your course should be a certain length, frame the rationale to your target student. If you're just stretching it to make $1,000 feel "fair," students will feel the padding and disengage.

**Don't:** Plot the altMBA's canvas, copy it, and assume your course will run itself.
**Why:** Wes intentionally avoids using altMBA as the example when teaching, "because I don't want everyone to get too excited about a course that 'runs itself.'" The altMBA worked without Seth in lectures because (a) the project bar was much higher, (b) there was a high-performing team, and (c) there was a 1:10 coach ratio with a heavily-trained alumni-coach community. Those preconditions are the ones most creators don't have. Copying the levers without copying the assets produces a hollow course.

**Don't:** Invest in pre-recorded content in your beta cohort.
**Why:** "Optimize for learning, then for scale and efficiency." You'll change entire sections of your curriculum after your first few cohorts — you'll trim modules, reorder, realize where students are confused. Pre-recorded video is rework debt. Wait until the curriculum is stable.

**Don't:** Pull production value as a lever just because it looks impressive.
**Why:** Production value isn't what most students are buying. If they wanted that, they'd find a flashy MOOC. They're paying for community, interaction, hands-on participation, networking, accountability. Production value is a smart differentiator only when it's genuinely an asset you have (Section4) — not when it's a vanity expense.

**Don't:** Treat all 12 levers as equally weighted decisions to make in parallel.
**Why:** That's the analysis-paralysis trap. Start with the lever you care most about (or have the strongest intuitive read on). Let it anchor. Move to the next. By lever five the others start falling into place.

**Don't:** Confuse audience size for course-readiness.
**Why:** A creator with 50k followers built that over years of free value. The lever you need is credibility with a target audience and a juicy value prop — not raw follower count. People with small but engaged email lists run successful $2,000 cohorts all the time. People with huge follower counts launch courses that flop because the audience doesn't trust them on this specific topic.

**Don't:** Sell a binary promise but design a spectrum experience.
**Why:** "Write your first TV pilot in 6 weeks" is a promise that requires high intensity, projects, group critique, and instructor or coach involvement. If you sell that and deliver a low-intensity, lecture-heavy, no-projects course, you'll have a refund problem and a reputation problem. Match the canvas to the promise on the marketing page.

**Don't:** Forget the bi-directional bar of cohort-based courses.
**Why:** Books, podcasts, and tweets are one-directional — you can hide BS in those formats. Cohort-based courses are bi-directional. Students get a microphone. Skepticism spreads. If your content is surface-level, you will be revealed in real time. The canvas is necessary but not sufficient — you also need depth.
