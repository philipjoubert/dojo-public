# The Super Specific How (SSH)

## When to Use
- Creating curriculum for a cohort-based course, a paid workshop, or internal training where transfer matters more than inspiration.
- Building any teaching material where the audience already knows the topic is important — they just don't know how to do it.
- Writing playbooks, SOPs, or onboarding docs that have to actually change behavior, not just describe behavior.
- Diagnosing why your course or content "feels light" or gets feedback like "interesting but I don't know what to do with it."

## Fails When
- The audience genuinely needs convincing on the what or why first — for new grads, "communication is important" might actually be groundbreaking. — In that case, take time to unpack the concept; SSH is for audiences past the convince-me stage.
- The instructor is a one-idea wonder trying to fill a course with a single insight. — SSH exposes thin material faster than anything else; if there is no real "how," dressing it up in screenshots won't save it.
- The content is meant to be inspirational, not instructional (keynote, manifesto). — Different format, different goal.

## Core Concept

Cohort-based courses have zero room for BS. Books and blog posts and tweets focus on the *what* and the *why*. That's fine for those formats — they're one-directional, and people are scrolling. But the moment you charge $500 to $5,000 for a course, students' expectations shift categorically. Their patience runs out fast if your course is a blog post masquerading as something more.

Wes calls this the **Content Hierarchy of BS**. Twitter has the most room to hide. Then keynotes and podcasts. Then blog posts. Then books. Then video courses. Then cohort-based courses, which have zero room. If your content is surface-level in a CBC, students will see it — because they get a microphone, they ask questions in real time, and skepticism spreads laterally between students before you can react.

The antidote: the Super Specific How.

🚫 Most instructors spend 80% on the what and why, and 20% on the how.
✅ In cohort-based courses, you want to spend 20% on the what and why, and 80% on the how.

The Super Specific How is "how" on steroids. It's the literal click-by-click, line-by-line, word-by-word version of teaching. The actual screenshot of the email you sent. The actual Slack message you wrote. The actual Notion page setup. The exact script you used in the negotiation. Examples are your secret weapon — they give 10x more tactical information than what words alone can describe.

Behavior change is the goal. Students can't change if they only know the *what*. They have to learn the *how*.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Audit your existing material against the BS test

Take any module, slide, lesson, or section. Ask: *Is this what/why or is this how?* If it's mostly what/why, mark it for either deletion or compression — your audience already agrees. Move on to the how.

A useful gut check: imagine your version of "Jiminy Cricket Wes" sitting on your shoulder, asking "Great! How do I do this?" after every point you make. If the answer is "well, you should…", you haven't taught anything yet.

### Step 2: Replace abstract claims with specific artifacts

For every concept you want students to apply, ask: what would a screenshot, a script, or a worked example look like? If you don't have one, build one. If you can't build one, the concept may be too abstract to actually teach.

What you can show screenshots of:
- Emails (the actual copy)
- Calendar setups (your actual event types)
- iMessage / Slack messages (actual texts)
- Paid ads (actual creative)
- Tweets, Google Docs, spreadsheets, Notion pages, Airtable, GitHub
- Invitations, applications, rejection letters, replies

Wes's contrasts make this concrete:

💩 **BS:** Stating the importance of using warm yet authoritative language. (We already agree this is important.)
✅ **SSH:** Showing the actual Slack messages you sent to a direct report giving constructive feedback — articulating what was sloppy and what to change — with the person feeling glad for the feedback.

💩 **BS:** Explaining how to use Notion well.
✅ **SSH:** Screen-sharing your own Notion setup.

💩 **BS:** Explaining the importance of setting boundaries.
✅ **SSH:** Sharing the actual word-for-word script you used to set boundaries with your coworker — including the situation, the relationship dynamic, the words used, and what happened as a result.

💩 **BS:** Describing that you sent an email about X.
✅ **SSH:** Sharing the screenshot or pasted script of the email copy.

### Step 3: Define student outcomes with verbs

Fill in this blank: *"By the end of the course, you'll be able to do X without Y (usual blocker)."*

Examples:
- *By the end of the course, you'll be able to ship your first email to prospective clients.*
- *By the end of the course, you'll have a system for publishing weekly content.*
- *By the end of the course, you'll have a TV pilot script written and ready to pitch.*

Then list 20 actions students will be able to do after your course. 20 feels like a lot — that's the point. You're a rubber band, and you have to stretch further than feels necessary so you'll snap back to the right level. From those 20, pick the 3 most crucial ones. That trio drives your curriculum and your landing-page copy.

### Step 4: Reduce cognitive load with helper text

A vague prompt is the silent killer of course rigor. If a student reads an assignment and has no idea where to start, the prompt is broken — and all your hard work upstream is wasted.

😐 **Vague:** "What positive beliefs do you hold?"
✅ **With helper text:** "What positive beliefs do you hold? Example: I believe I am a good leader; I believe I am an empathetic friend; I believe I am a critical thinker."

😐 **Vague:** "What tactics will you use to help bring students together?"
✅ **With helper text:** "What tactics will you use? E.g., Slack, social media, in-person events, monthly newsletters, accountability groups, social events to get to know each other, 1:1 buddy calls."

Recall is hard. Recognition is easy. Always give students an example list to anchor against — they can pick from your list or come up with their own, but they can't start cold.

### Step 5: Pull both top-down and bottom-up

**Top down:** Go through your applications, surveys, DMs, podcast questions. What patterns do you see? What questions are coming up repeatedly? Prospective students are literally telling you what they want to learn — listen to that data. Add a question to every application: *"What do you most want to learn in this course?"*

**Bottom up:** Gather and assemble your own examples and put them in buckets. Ask: What would your students find juicy? What are the best docs you've ever created? What's the best email you've ever written? What was a transformative moment you can share?

Both approaches together produce a more comprehensive set of examples than either alone.

### Step 6: Run the TACS check

Before any module ships, ask:

- What are students learning to *do*?
- Is it necessary?
- Is it as hands-on as possible?
- Is it as direct as possible?
- Could this be turned into a visual?
- Could this be turned into an interactive experience?
- Where might students be confused, and how can I proactively address that?
- How can I be more **T**actical, **A**ctionable, **C**oncrete, **S**pecific?
- What screenshots, scripts, or examples can I share?

## Examples

**Situation:** A creator launching a CBC on email marketing. Draft curriculum says "Module 3: How to write subject lines that convert."

**Application:** Wes's lens: that title is the *what*. The SSH version is — show the actual subject lines that worked, the open rates next to each, the A/B test results, and the deliberation behind each word choice. Show the bad subject lines too, with the open rates that proved them bad. Then give students 5 product/audience pairs and have them write 10 subject lines each in the workshop, with peer critique. Module 3 is no longer "how subject lines work" — it's "by the end of this session you'll have written and tested 10 subject lines for your own list."

**Result:** Students leave the workshop with subject lines they can deploy that afternoon. The course earns its price because no blog post can replicate the artifact-plus-feedback-plus-practice combination.

---

**Situation:** Wes herself, teaching her course on Calendly best practices.

**Application:** Instead of "3 bullet points on why it's important to operationalize repetitive processes," she shares the actual screenshot of her Calendly. She narrates: "I have separate event types so I can triage calls based on importance and amount of time needed. Instead of hoping the recipient won't want a Zoom call, I send a calendar link that's for phone calls only. I have an invite type that's Fridays only for less urgent calls."

**Result:** A student can clone the system that night. The 30-second screenshot delivers more transferable value than three bullet points on why operationalizing matters.

---

**Situation:** Internal training: a manager wants to teach their team how to give peer feedback.

**Application:** Don't write a doc explaining the importance of feedback. Show the actual Slack message you sent a peer last quarter, including the awkward parts. Walk through what you almost said, why you cut it, and what happened next. Have everyone practice: take a real situation from your week, draft the message, paste it in the room, get five people to sharpen it. The "what" (feedback matters) is implied. The "how" (this is the actual sentence) is the entire content.

**Result:** Behavior change. Two weeks later, people are sending the messages. A doc-only training would have produced agreement and no change.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Spend 30 minutes of your workshop convincing students that the topic is important.
**Why:** They already know — that's why they paid. "Respect the intelligence of your audience." If your what isn't groundbreaking, move past it fast and spend the saved time on the how.

**Don't:** Use vague prompts like "What positive beliefs do you hold?" without helper text.
**Why:** Students will stare at the screen, get frustrated, and either skip the exercise or write something generic. You did 90% of the work building the prompt; the missing 10% (examples) is what makes it usable. It's a pet peeve of Wes's because it's such an avoidable waste.

**Don't:** Build pre-recorded content for your alpha or beta cohort.
**Why:** You'll change huge sections of your curriculum after the first few cohorts. Pre-recording is rework debt. Optimize for learning first, then scale.

**Don't:** Use stock screenshots or generic examples instead of your real artifacts.
**Why:** The whole point of SSH is the realness. Students can tell when a screenshot is illustrative vs. when it's an actual artifact you used. Real artifacts carry the texture, the awkwardness, the context — that's the transferable value.

**Don't:** Confuse SSH with hand-holding or dumbing things down.
**Why:** SSH is rigorous, not condescending. You're going one level deeper than feels comfortable, not three levels lower. The Super Specific How is for capable adults who want to be able to do the thing tomorrow morning, not for beginners who need every step narrated.

**Don't:** Use SSH on every single concept regardless of audience.
**Why:** When the *what* or *why* is genuinely new for your audience (e.g., a brand-new framework, a paradigm shift), take time on it. SSH is for the moments after agreement — when students nod and want to know how. Read the room.

**Don't:** Pull SSH out at the end of the lesson as a bonus.
**Why:** It's the lesson. The SSH is what students paid for. Front-load the artifacts and let the conceptual scaffolding wrap around them — not the other way around.
