# Eyes Light Up (ELU)

## When to Use
- The user is testing positioning, messaging, a pitch, or a value prop in real conversations.
- The user is iterating on how they describe a product, role, or idea — and needs a signal that doesn't lie.
- The user is at a conference, on customer calls, or in informal settings where they can vary the framing each time.

## Fails When
- The user is running scaled async marketing (paid ads, cold email) where they can't see faces. Use other signals — clicks, replies, conversion rates — for that.
- The user treats one person's enthusiasm as a green light without testing across multiple conversations.

## Core Concept

You already know when people are excited to hear a story. They perk up. Their energy shifts. All of a sudden they look alive — and they want to hear what you have to say.

This is the **Eyes Light Up moment.** ELU for short.

It's the moment when your audience is viscerally, undeniably excited about what you're talking about. Whenever you tell a story, share your value proposition, or test your positioning and messaging — **eyes lighting up is the ONLY reaction that matters.**

Not "Hmm interesting." When someone says that, they do NOT think your idea is interesting. They're saying it because they don't know what else to say. They're being polite. If they were actually interested, their eyes would light up. They'd be interrupting you with questions. You'd be having a lively conversation.

Ogilvy said it best: "The problem with market research is that people don't think how they feel, they don't say what they think and they don't do what they say." That's why intellectual questions get you intellectual responses, and intellectual responses are usually not helpful at all. Hypothetical questions are even worse.

ELU sidesteps all of that. You're not asking what they think. You're watching what their body does.

When you tell a story, the only thing you need to remember is this: **ELU.** Look for moments when your audience's eyes light up. Those are the clues.

The mechanic is simple: **Every time you talk to someone, describe the new product/idea/role in a slightly different way. Notice what makes people's eyes light up. Jot those down.** Then emphasize and aggrandize those elements. Trim the parts people didn't care about.

This is also why before-and-after comparisons are so powerful in marketing — they create a visceral reaction by showing change. Your mind skips over the part where two states are similar and naturally focuses on the difference. Comparisons heighten drama. ELU is the live, in-conversation version of that same principle: you're testing which framing creates the visceral pull.

## How to Apply

1. **Generate 3–7 different framings of your message.** Different angles, different opening lines, different metaphors, different value props. Don't decide which is best yet.
2. **Use real conversations as your lab.** SXSW, conferences, customer calls, dinners, Twitter DMs, podcast intros. Anywhere you talk to a real human about your thing.
3. **Vary the framing each time.** Don't repeat the same pitch. Try angle A with the first person, angle B with the second, angle C with the third.
4. **Watch their face. Watch their body.** Did they lean in? Did they stop scrolling? Did they interrupt with a follow-up question? Or did they nod politely and change the subject?
5. **Jot down the ELU moments.** Specific phrases, specific framings, specific words. The hot key words that made them perk up.
6. **Emphasize and aggrandize what worked. Trim what didn't.** Move the ELU material to the top of your pitch. Cut or de-emphasize the parts that drew polite nods.
7. **Re-test.** Run the new version through more conversations. Iterate.

ELU answers all of these:
- Which parts of your story are boring?
- What parts do prospective customers actually care about?
- What should you spend more time talking about?
- Where are they confused?
- What did they remember and repeat back to you?
- What hot key words got them to perk up?

## Examples

**Situation:** A Wes Kao client is heading to SXSW with a new product positioning they're not sure about.

**Application:** Wes: "This is a great chance to test your new positioning. Every time you talk to someone, describe the new product in a slightly different way. Notice what makes people's eyes light up. Jot those down and let's discuss when you're back."

**Result:** The client returns with insights on what resonated in conversation. They emphasize and aggrandize the ELU elements in the next iteration of the pitch and trim the parts people didn't care as much about. Their official messaging gets sharper because it was tested against bodies, not surveys.

---

**Situation:** A founder is preparing for a fundraise. Their pitch deck has 14 slides covering market size, team, traction, technology, vision, etc. They don't know which to lead with.

**Application:** Test each angle on warm investor conversations BEFORE the formal pitch. Pitch the team angle to one. The market angle to another. The traction angle to a third. Watch which one makes the investor lean forward, ask a follow-up, or say "tell me more about that." Lead the deck with the angle that produces the strongest ELU.

**Result:** The first 60 seconds of the pitch hit the part the audience actually cares about. The rest of the deck is supporting material.

---

**Situation:** A founder is rehearsing how to describe their startup at a dinner. They've been saying "we use AI to optimize supply chain logistics for mid-market manufacturers." Eyes glaze over.

**Application:** Try different framings: "We help mid-market factories stop running out of parts." "We're the reason a CEO doesn't get a 2 AM call about a stalled production line." "We do for factory floors what Stripe did for online payments." Watch which one makes the dinner companion ask a follow-up question.

**Result:** The framing that triggers ELU becomes the new top of the pitch.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Ask "Does this resonate?" or "What do you think of this positioning?"
**Why:** Intellectual questions get intellectual responses. People will give you polite, considered, useless feedback. ELU is observational, not interrogative. Watch the face, don't survey it.

**Don't:** Treat "Hmm interesting" as a green light.
**Why:** "Interesting" is the polite-disengagement word. Real interest comes with a follow-up question, an interruption, a story of their own, leaning in. If you got "interesting" and nothing else, the framing didn't land.

**Don't:** Memorize a tactic-stack instead of looking for ELU.
**Why:** When you tell a story, the way to prepare is NOT to memorize a bunch of tactics. That makes you nervous — like an athlete trying to remember to keep their knees bent, but not too bent, back straight, follow through. The only thing to remember is ELU. Look for moments when your audience's eyes light up.

**Don't:** Test only with friendly audiences.
**Why:** Friends and existing customers will perk up at almost anything you say because they already love you. ELU only works as a signal when the audience is neutral or skeptical at baseline. Test on cold conversations.

**Don't:** Conclude from a single ELU moment.
**Why:** One person's enthusiasm could be politeness, personality, or coincidence. Look for ELU patterns across at least 5–10 conversations before you commit to a framing.

**Don't:** Skip writing the ELU moments down because "you'll remember."
**Why:** You won't. By the time you sit down to revise the pitch, the specific phrasing that made someone light up will be a vague memory. Capture the actual words, the specific framing, the metaphor — in the moment or right after.
