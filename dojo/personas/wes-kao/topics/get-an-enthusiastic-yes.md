# Get an Enthusiastic Yes

## When to Use
- You need to make an ask where the other person has full discretion to say no or ignore you: warm intros, marketing favors, sales closes, project sign-off, asks for a job, asks for a raise, asks for time.
- You've been getting rejected and you're starting to think the problem is the asks themselves — not the people you're asking.
- You're procrastinating on an email or message because you're not sure what to say. (Hint: that's almost always the diagnostic. *"When we are not sure what to say, we usually delay."*)

## Fails When
- **The idea is genuinely bad for the other person.** No amount of framing rescues an ask that doesn't actually serve them. If you can't think of why they'd eagerly say yes, *go back to the drawing board.* Don't hit send.
- **You confuse persuasion with manipulation.** This framework only works when the upside for the other person is real. If you're constructing a fake "what's in it for them" — they'll smell it.
- **You ask the wrong person.** Most of the time, rejection is a casting problem before it's a copywriting problem. If your funnel is full of unqualified asks, polishing the note won't save you.
- **You've prepared into paralysis.** "Just ask, but do some preparation first" is a balance, not an excuse for never sending.

## Core Concept

You and I rarely have enough leverage to get something done all by ourselves. We live in an interconnected world. We need cooperation. And cooperation isn't free — every "yes" is a choice the other person made because something in your ask gave them a reason to.

So why do people say no? **They say no because they don't see why a situation is beneficial to them.** That's not selfish. That's how everyone works. You wouldn't do something if there was no upside for you either.

Here's the trap: when you write or speak to other people, you are thinking about why an idea is good for *you*. But that is THE most important time to position why the idea is good for *them*.

> 🚫 "Here are 10 reasons this matters to me, my project, my company."
>
> ✅ "Here is why this is a great use of your time, status, network, or money."

The truth, as Wes puts it: *"a lot of your requests — whether it's sales material, marketing material, or people management — are a bit selfish. You aren't trying to be selfish, not at all. It's simply that we see the world from our own point of view. Your default state is 'me'-centric."*

The Enthusiastic Yes framework is a forcing function to break out of that default. It has three moves: (1) **plan before you ask** — make sure you've earned the right to ask this person, this way, right now. (2) **Audit your draft for me-vs-them** — line by line, color the parts that benefit you vs. them. (3) **Set the emotional tone** — the way they feel about the topic is half the conversation; you get to lead the mirroring.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Plan before you ask

The status quo is to do nothing. "Doing nothing" includes delaying and stalling. So your job is to give your request the fuel it deserves before you send it.

Before you send anything, run this check:

- **Did you give them enough reasons to say yes?**
- **Did you give them *any* reason to say yes?**
- **If the answer is no — go back to the drawing board.** Whew, you caught it before hitting send.

A note on social capital: *"It doesn't hurt to ask!" is a lie.* Asking when you shouldn't is expensive — it can use up your social capital, make someone question your judgment, create awkwardness, strain a relationship, or change the nature of a relationship in ways that are hard to undo. So don't "just put it out there." Think about whether what you're asking *this specific person* makes sense given your level of trust with them.

### Step 2: Rethink the shape of your funnel — cylinder, not cone

Most people imagine asks as a cone: spray a wide top of funnel, expect to be rejected 99% of the time, narrow down to the few yeses. Wes's reframe: **think of it as a cylinder.**

> 🚫 "I need to ask a lot of people, and I'll get rejected 99% of the time."
>
> ✅ "I'm going to ask people who I think have a reason to say yes."

You'll still get rejected sometimes. But you had a point of view about why this person, specifically, would say yes — *before* you asked. That POV is what separates a strategic operator from someone playing the rejection lottery.

### Step 3: The line-by-line me-vs-them audit

This is the central exercise. After you draft the message, do this:

1. **What part of this note mentions things that mostly benefit me?**
2. **What about this benefits the other person?** (And be honest — do they really care, or is this a thinly-veiled attempt at positioning something that still mainly serves me?)
3. **Why would they eagerly say yes to this?**

Then literally go through, line by line, and color-code: yellow for "this benefits me," green for "this benefits them." You will be surprised at how much of your note is self-serving. *"The key is to edit so the final version is 90% about the other person."*

The reframe to get the language right: **show people what they gain, not what you gain.** Not "I would love an intro to your friend" → "I think your friend would find a 20-minute call useful because [specific value]." Not "Could you share our event?" → "I thought of you because your audience cares about X — and our event has Y for them."

### Step 4: Strip the "me" residue

Run this second pass:

- "Do I need to share these details?"
- "What do I expect the person to do with this information?"
- "How can I frame this with the other person in mind?"

If the message is too long, this exercise almost always finds something to trim. *"You almost always have room to make it less about you. And when you do that, you're more likely to get a positive result."*

### Step 5: Set the emotional tone before you press send

Every conversation has two conversations happening at once: the topic, and the way both people are feeling about the topic. The way we feel about a topic completely changes how we talk about it.

Some degree of mirroring naturally happens. **You get to set the tone — and have the other person follow.** You don't need to get pulled into the other person's frame, especially if it's negative. Setting the emotional tone is more helpful than memorizing any phrase or script.

Practically: if you walk in (or hit send) jumpy, brooding, or apologizing-in-advance, the other person picks up on it. If you walk in grounded, confident, and holding the topic lightly, they pick up on that too. **You can choose to hold the topic lightly. You can invite the other person to mirror you.**

In writing: this means cutting the "sorry to bother you, I know you're so busy" preamble. It signals weakness, primes them for friction, and dilutes the ask. Replace it with grounded confidence — *"I have a request that I think is a good use of 5 minutes of your time. Here it is."*

### Step 6: Ask yourself first (the "Just Ask" companion)

Before you press send, run the boss-follow-up simulator. When you ask your boss something, what do they always reply with?

- *"What's the context?"*
- *"What are the alternatives?"*
- *"What's the cost?"*
- *"Is there a reason we are exploring something new?"*
- *"Do we trust this provider?"*

Now pre-answer those. Don't make them ask. The structural template:

> "Do you approve X? I recommend it relative to [A, B, C] options because [rationale]. The risk is [D] but we can mitigate it with [E]."

This applies to almost every ask, not just approvals. Internal ask, external ask, sales close — preempt the predictable follow-up questions and you collapse the back-and-forth into one yes.

## Examples

**Situation:** You want a busy industry contact to make a warm intro to someone at a target company.

**Application (selfish first draft):**
*"Hi Sarah, hope you're well! I'd love an intro to your friend Joe at Acme — we're trying to build a relationship there and I think it would really help us. Let me know if you're open to it!"*

Audit: 100% about you. The intro helps you. "I think it would really help us" is your win, not Sarah's. There's no reason for Sarah to say yes.

**Application (rewritten for enthusiastic yes):**
*"Hi Sarah — quick one. I'm working on [specific thing] and I noticed Joe at Acme has been writing about exactly this. Based on what I've built, I think a 20-minute conversation could give him a useful data point on [specific thing he cares about] — and it could spare him some of the early mistakes I made. If you're open to it, here's a 3-line forwardable he can decide on himself. No pressure if it's not a fit."*

**Result:** Sarah's social capital is protected. Joe gets a frame in which the call is *for him*. Sarah can forward without thinking. Enthusiastic yes.

---

**Situation:** You're closing a sales conversation and you've been getting "let me think about it" rejections.

**Application (rejection-prone):** *"It sounds like we're aligned. Do you want to move forward with this contract?"*

This is a fine line, but it's defaultable. The buyer's path of least resistance is to delay — because nothing has been said about the cost of delaying.

**Application (Wes's way):** Reframe with the buyer's actual upside, the cost of doing nothing, and pre-empt the predictable objection. *"Based on what you described — [their actual problem in their words] — moving forward this week means you'll have [outcome] before [their stated deadline]. The two things people typically push back on are [X] and [Y] — here's how we handle each. Want me to send the contract today, or would Thursday work better for you to review with [their stakeholder]?"*

**Result:** You've made the upside their upside, named the cost of delay (which was always there but invisible), pre-empted the two predictable objections, and offered a binary choice. Same content. Different yes-rate.

---

**Situation:** You're asking your boss to approve a $2k/day Facebook spend (up from $1k/day).

**Application (procrastination-prone):** *"Hey, do you have time to chat about ad spend?"*

You'll wait three days. They'll come in cold. You'll explain context for 10 minutes. They'll have ten follow-up questions. You'll need a second meeting.

**Application (Wes's way — from the CEO meeting playbook):** *"Budget meeting — get approval for doubling FB spend from $1k/day to $2k/day. CAC has been $X (target $Y), and current spend is hitting saturation by 11am daily. At $2k/day for 2 weeks, I expect [projected outcome]. Risk is [Z] — I'll cap and pull if [trigger]. If you agree, I'll move it tonight."*

**Result:** They reply "sounds good." You moved a 30-minute meeting into a one-line Slack message.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead with "I'm sorry to bother you, I know you're really busy."
**Why:** This sets a low-status emotional tone before the ask even lands. It primes the recipient to feel imposed-on, which is the opposite of the frame you want. Cut the preamble.

**Don't:** Try to make the upside up. ("This will be a great opportunity for you to…")
**Why:** People can tell. If you can't articulate a real, specific, concrete benefit to *them*, you don't have a yes-able ask yet. Go back to the drawing board.

**Don't:** Ask the same person three times in a month.
**Why:** Asking uses social capital. Repeated asks erode the relationship. If you've gotten a soft no twice, the third ask is louder than you think.

**Don't:** "Spray and pray" because volume feels like progress.
**Why:** Cone funnel thinking. You're going to get rejected by people who shouldn't have been on the list to begin with — and the rejections will color the rare good leads on the list, because you'll start to expect no.

**Don't:** Audit the email until it's perfect, then never send.
**Why:** "Just ask, but do some preparation first." Preparation is in service of asking — not a substitute for it. The prepared ask, sent today, beats the perfect ask sent never.

**Don't:** Try to set the emotional tone with words while your body language / phrasing leaks insecurity.
**Why:** People mirror your *energy*, not your phrasing. If your message says "this is going to be amazing!" but the structure is hesitant, they pick up on the structure. Match the tone you want them to mirror.

**Don't:** Confuse "asking with confidence" with "demanding."
**Why:** Confidence is grounded; demand is brittle. The difference is whether you've actually thought about why this is good for them. Confidence has the receipts. Demand doesn't.
