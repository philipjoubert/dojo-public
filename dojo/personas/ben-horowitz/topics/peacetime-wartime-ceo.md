# Peacetime CEO / Wartime CEO

## When to Use
- You are trying to figure out why the management playbook that worked six months ago is producing nothing now.
- The external landscape has changed — a competitor is eating you, cash is a problem, a macro move just detonated your category — and you are still running the old process.
- You have a temperament (calm, consensus-driven, or the opposite) and you're trying to decide whether to impose it on the company or change it.
- You are reviewing a CEO — yours, a founder you back, yourself — and you need to know if the person's style matches the moment.
- A board is considering replacing a peacetime CEO who is presiding over a war. Or firing a wartime CEO who has been at war so long they can't stop.

## Fails When
- The question is actually "is our product good enough?" — that's lead bullets, not a mode question. Switching modes doesn't fix a product problem.
- The question is emotional — "I can't sleep, I don't know if I can keep doing this." That's The Struggle. Peacetime / wartime is a diagnostic of the terrain, not of your nervous system.
- The company hasn't actually entered wartime — the CEO just feels dramatic. Wartime CEOs in peacetime companies burn the culture and drive out the people who would have built the next decade. Don't confuse anxiety with war.

## Core Concept

In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank had six weeks of runway on a bank run. Depositors were pulling money. The stock was falling. Greg Becker needed to shore up the balance sheet immediately. This was an imminent existential threat — the definition of wartime.

Here's what a wartime CEO would have done: picked up the phone, called Warren Buffett, and offered him the deal of a lifetime. Buffett bailed out Goldman Sachs in 2008 on terms spectacularly favorable to Berkshire. When your house is on fire, you don't negotiate the price of the fire truck. You get the fire truck.

Here's what Becker did: he called his friends at General Atlantic and offered them a market-rate deal. A peacetime deal. A deal that assumed the bank had time, leverage, and options. It didn't. Within days SVB had collapsed in the second-largest bank failure in American history.

Becker wasn't stupid. He was a peacetime CEO who stayed in peacetime mode when the bombs started falling. And that's one of the most dangerous things that can happen to a company.

I was a peacetime CEO for about nine months. Then I was a wartime CEO for the next seven years. My greatest management discovery through that transition was that peacetime and wartime require radically different management styles — not slightly different, not shaded differently. Radically different. Opposite in most places that matter.

Peacetime in business means the company has a large advantage over the competition in its core market and the market is growing. The job is to expand the pie and reinforce your strengths. In wartime the company is fending off an imminent existential threat — a competitor, a macro shift, a supply chain collapse, a cash runway. The threat can kill you.

This matters because almost every management book ever written describes peacetime. The basic principle in most management books is that you should never embarrass an employee in a public setting. On the other hand, Andy Grove — a wartime CEO all the way, who took Intel from memory chips to microprocessors in one of the most violent pivots in business history — once said to an employee who entered a meeting late, in a room full of people: *"All I have in this world is time, and you are wasting my time."* Same job title. Completely different job.

Here is what the contrast looks like in practice:

Peacetime CEO knows that proper protocol leads to winning. Wartime CEO violates protocol in order to win.

Peacetime CEO focuses on the big picture and empowers her people to make detailed decisions. Wartime CEO cares about a speck of dust on a gnat's ass if it interferes with the prime directive.

Peacetime CEO builds scalable, high-volume recruiting machines. Wartime CEO does that, but also builds HR organizations that can execute layoffs.

Peacetime CEO spends time defining the culture. Wartime CEO lets the war define the culture.

Peacetime CEO always has a contingency plan. Wartime CEO knows that sometimes you gotta roll a hard six.

Peacetime CEO strives to tolerate deviations from the plan when coupled with effort and creativity. Wartime CEO is completely intolerant.

Peacetime CEO works to minimize conflict. Wartime CEO heightens the contradictions.

Peacetime CEO strives for broad-based buy-in. Wartime CEO neither indulges consensus-building nor tolerates disagreements.

Peacetime CEO sets big, hairy, audacious goals. Wartime CEO is too busy fighting the enemy to read management books written by consultants who have never managed a fruit stand.

Peacetime CEO aims to expand the market. Wartime CEO aims to win the market.

When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company was weeks away from bankruptcy. Classic wartime. He needed everyone to move with precision and follow his exact plan; there was no room for individual creativity outside of the core mission. When Google achieved dominance in search and had all the cash in the world, Google's management fostered peacetime innovation by enabling every employee to spend twenty percent of their time on their own projects. Both were right. For their situations.

The fatal mistake is applying the wrong one. Peacetime techniques in wartime kill the company politely — everyone got consulted, nobody was told the truth, the runway ran out while the steering committee was forming. Wartime techniques in peacetime drive out the people who would have built the next decade — every decision feels like a crisis, every mistake is intolerable, the culture calcifies around fear.

Larry Page at Google in 2011 is the other example. The iPhone had shipped, mobile had detonated, and Google was behind. Page came back as CEO and ran a classic wartime reorg — fast, intolerant of protocol, sharp decisions from the top. He had been a peacetime CEO during the company's dominant cruising years. When the terrain changed, he changed with it. That is rare.

Almost no CEOs can function in both modes. And here is the asymmetry most people miss: **going from wartime to peacetime is harder than the reverse.** A wartime CEO who stays in wartime after peace arrives exhausts the organization and drives away talent. The paranoia that kept you alive becomes the paranoia that makes every meeting miserable. The intolerance that was a survival trait becomes a culture of fear. It is easier to learn to fight when your life depends on it than to learn to stop fighting when it doesn't.

The record industry is the other side of the coin. The professional CEOs who inherited those businesses were peacetime managers through and through. Every original founder had been bought out, retired, or died. When Napster and then the iPod and then streaming came, those CEOs could not switch. They were proficient at running the current business. They lacked both the courage and the moral authority to jeopardize the old model by embracing the new technology. Netflix, run by cofounder Reed Hastings, faced the same transition — from physical distribution to electronic — and navigated it brilliantly. Hastings wasn't married to the old distribution model precisely because he invented it. He could throw it out. A professional CEO cannot throw out an assumption she didn't make.

**The diagnostic question is simple: has the external landscape fundamentally changed, and has your management style changed with it?**

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the terrain, not your temperament.** Look at the external situation. Is there an imminent existential threat — competitor eating you, cash problem, category collapse, macro shift — or do you have a large advantage in a growing market? The situation picks the mode. You don't.

2. **Be honest about which CEO you are.** Most founders are natively one or the other. Most CEOs who say "I can be both" cannot. Ask three people who worked for you last year which mode they saw. Believe them.

3. **If there is a mismatch, switch your behavior — not your values.** In wartime: make sharp fast decisions, go low in the org when needed so people get the object lessons, be paranoid, kill protocol that slows the target hit, tolerate no deviation, tell the truth about how bad it is. In peacetime: tolerate sloppiness in service of creativity, build consensus, invest in options, expand the market, protect the culture.

4. **If you cannot switch, bring in someone who can — and move to the seat where you are useful.** This is not failure. This is a founder doing the right thing for the company. Larry Page stepping aside at Google for Schmidt during the scale years, then coming back as CEO when the terrain changed to war, is the canonical example of a founder who understood the mode question better than his own ego.

5. **Never use wartime style as cover for being an asshole.** Wartime permits intolerance in service of survival. It does not permit cruelty, contempt for people, or theatrical intensity. If your team can't tell why you're swearing more, you're not a wartime CEO, you're just angry.

6. **When the war ends, end the war.** Watch for the moment the existential threat passes. The runway is secure, the category settled, the product clearly winning. Then deliberately change behavior — more tolerance, more consensus, more oxygen for creativity. The most common mistake at this stage is not noticing peace has arrived.

## Examples

**Situation:** A B2B SaaS founder has a well-funded competitor that just closed a $100M round and is winning 60% of new deals in a segment she used to own. Her board meeting is in two weeks. She is considering a pivot to a smaller segment and is running her usual weekly all-hands where everyone debates strategy.

**Application:** Diagnose first. She is in wartime — she has a well-capitalized enemy taking her market, and the trajectory is existential. Stop the all-hands debate. Stop seeking consensus on strategy. Make the call on what the product has to be to win the deals she's losing, tell the company, and go. Violate protocol if protocol slows the product cycle. Go low in the org — sit with the engineers building the critical feature, not with the VPs running the process. And tell the company the truth: we are losing, here is why, here is the plan, here is what we need.

**Result:** The company either builds the product that wins the deals back within nine months or it doesn't. But the wartime CEO moves fast enough to find out. The peacetime CEO spends those nine months in alignment workshops.

---

**Situation:** A Series C founder just closed a strong round. Product is working, retention is strong, the market is growing 40% a year, his closest competitor is distracted with a CEO transition. He has been running the company at wartime intensity — weekly "state of the company" calls, interrupting his executives, demanding visibility into every decision — for three years. He is losing senior people and doesn't understand why.

**Application:** The war is over. He needs to notice that. Peacetime has arrived and his wartime behavior is no longer a survival skill — it's exhausting the people who built the thing. Delegate. Give his execs the air to make detailed decisions. Replace the weekly "state of the company" with a monthly. Stop interrupting. Tolerate some sloppiness. The creativity that builds the next cycle requires oxygen and he has been sucking it out of the room.

**Result:** If he can make the switch, the company gets the flywheel of the peacetime years — the hiring, the product expansion, the second act. If he can't, he drives out the executives he needs, and either the board removes him or he discovers he was replaceable all along.

---

**Situation:** A founder is reviewing her own performance and says: "I think I'm a wartime CEO stuck in a peacetime company."

**Application:** Test the diagnosis. Is there actually an existential threat she's seeing that her team isn't? Sometimes the wartime CEO is right and the company is in denial — that happens. Sometimes the wartime CEO is manufacturing the war because she doesn't know how to be at peace. Ask specifically: what is the threat, what is its timeline, what is its magnitude? If the threat is real and the team is asleep, her job is to wake them up. If the threat is not real, her job is to stop creating one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick your mode based on your personality.
**Why:** You don't get to pick. The company's situation picks. Peacetime CEOs who stay peaceful during wartime kill the company politely. Wartime CEOs who stay wartime during peace burn the culture. Your job is to diagnose the terrain and adjust — not to impose your preferred style on a situation that requires the opposite.

**Don't:** Use "we're in wartime" as permission to be cruel or theatrical.
**Why:** Wartime CEOs are intolerant of deviation from the mission. They are not intolerant of people. Grove's line — *"all I have in this world is time, and you are wasting my time"* — was about a specific behavior, not a character assassination. If your wartime style makes people afraid to bring you bad news, you've failed as a wartime CEO. Bad news has to travel faster in wartime, not slower.

**Don't:** Declare wartime and then run consensus meetings anyway.
**Why:** The worst mode is ambiguous mode. If you have told the company this is a wartime moment but your operating cadence — weekly alignment calls, offsite workshops, "let's get everyone's input on this" — is still peacetime, you have communicated that you don't believe your own diagnosis. The company will stop believing you too. When you say wartime, behave wartime. Make the call. Own it. Move.

**Don't:** Stay in wartime mode after the war is won.
**Why:** Going from wartime to peacetime is harder than the reverse, and most wartime CEOs never make it. They've been rewarded for paranoia and intolerance for so long that those traits are hardwired. But the people you need to build the next decade — the senior hires who weren't there during the war, the creative engineers who need room to build the next product — cannot operate in sustained wartime. Notice the war is over. Put the sword down.
