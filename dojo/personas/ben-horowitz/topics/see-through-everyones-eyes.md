# See the Decision Through Everyone's Eyes

## When to Use
- You're about to make a personnel, compensation, or policy decision that involves one person in the room — a demotion, a raise, a promotion, a transfer, a severance, an exception to the rules.
- You're about to grant an exception to a policy for a high performer because they're "worth it."
- You're writing a new rule or changing an existing one, and you haven't mapped how every employee who isn't in the room will read it.
- You feel the "kind" option pulling you — keep them in a smaller role with the VP title, give them the raise they asked for, let the star skip the new process — and you need to pressure-test that decision.

## Fails When
- **The decision is genuinely private and has no precedent effect.** Some severance conversations, some compensation details, some personnel matters are invisible to the rest of the company by design. In those cases, the question is about the individual alone.
- **"What will the team think" is being used as a shield.** Sometimes founders hide behind the precedent argument to avoid making the hard correct decision. If the analytical answer is clear and you're using "the team will be upset" to rationalize inaction, that's not this framework — that's a courage problem.
- **It's a coaching or feedback moment, not a precedent-setting action.** A one-on-one is not a decision that reverberates through the company. Don't over-apply the framework.

## Core Concept

This is the single management concept I consider most important and most commonly botched. I'll say it as plainly as I can: **when you're talking to somebody, realize you're not talking to that person. You're talking to everybody in the company.**

Every personnel decision — every hire, every fire, every raise, every demotion, every exception — sets a precedent. Every decision sends a signal. What it takes to succeed here. What happens when you fail here. What this company actually values, as opposed to what the values poster on the wall says.

The person in the room gets one version of the decision. The eighty people who aren't in the room get a different version — usually a worse, flatter, more extreme version, because they are seeing it through the lens of their own ambition, their own comp, their own fairness calibration. You have to think through both versions before you act. The version in the room is almost always secondary. The version outside the room is the one that shapes the culture.

**You have to represent the people who are not in the room.**

Let me walk you through three cases where this gets botched in identical ways at every startup I've ever been near.

**Case one: The Demotion That Seems Kind.**

A CEO wants to demote an underperforming VP rather than fire him. The VP has worked hard. He has relationships across the company. You've known him for years. Surely a demotion is kinder than termination. From the CEO's perspective, this looks like a win-win: the VP keeps his job, the company gets a better executive, nobody has to deal with a firing.

From every other employee's perspective, it looks like something very different.

That VP has 1.5% equity — VP-level stock. The engineers in the company have 0.1% to 0.2%. If you demote him but let him keep his equity, every engineer in the company asks the same question: *why does the guy who failed at his job get ten times my equity while no longer performing a VP-level role?*

You think you are dealing with one person's feelings. What you are really doing is defining, for the entire company, what it means to fail on the job. The "kind" decision creates an unfairness that poisons the entire organization.

This is why the "kind alternative" is almost always the wrong decision. The kindness flows to the one person in the room, and the damage flows to every person who isn't. If you net it out across the whole company, the math is disastrous. The framing that makes this clear: *is the net kindness positive across all eighty employees, or is the kindness to one person paid for by resentment and cynicism across the other seventy-nine?*

**Case two: The Raise Because Someone Asked.**

An excellent employee comes to you and asks for a raise. She's good. Maybe she's even underpaid relative to market. You give her the raise on the spot.

You have just written a new rule for your company, and you did not realize you were writing it. The rule is: *the way to get ahead here is to ask.* Squeakiness beats performance. You have just punished every equally good employee who did not ask, and you have incentivized every future employee to spend their political energy on self-advocacy rather than on work.

You think you solved a compensation problem for one person. You just created a compensation problem for every person in the company — including, eventually, the person you gave the raise to, because now she has to keep asking.

The correct move is not to deny the raise. The correct move is to run your compensation as a system — regular reviews, market-calibrated, based on performance against agreed-upon criteria — and to refuse to give ad hoc off-cycle raises in response to asks. When someone asks, the answer is: *I hear you. Here is how our comp process works. Your next review is in eight weeks. I'll make sure we look at this carefully then, based on the work, against market data.* That is not unkind. That is running a company where the thing that gets rewarded is the work, not the asking.

**Case three: The Exception for the Star Performer.**

You have a star engineer. She is extraordinary. She misses practice, in the Dennis Rodman sense — misses meetings, skips the new compliance training, shows up late, operates outside the process. Everybody sees it. You tolerate it because her output is worth it.

Remember what Phil Jackson said when someone asked him about Dennis Rodman. The question was: *since Rodman is allowed to miss practice, does that mean Jordan and Pippen can miss practice too?* Jackson said: *Of course not. There is only room for one Dennis Rodman on this team. In fact, you really can only have a very few Dennis Rodmans in society as a whole; otherwise, we would degenerate into anarchy.*

John Madden said the same thing about Terrell Owens. *If you hold the bus for everyone on the team, you'll be so late you'll miss the game. You can't do that. The bus has to leave on time. Sometimes you'll have a player so good that you hold the bus for him. Only him.*

This is the Hold-the-Bus Rule. You can hold the bus for one. Not two. *One.* The moment you hold the bus for a second Dennis Rodman, you have two sets of rules — one for the stars, one for everyone else — and the entire system of accountability collapses. Everyone else stops believing the rules apply to them. Everyone starts asking: what do I have to do to become a person the rules don't apply to? You have just made your culture a political game about who gets to be an exception.

So: if you make an exception for a star, make it rare, make it visible, and manage the cultural damage yourself. Otherwise, the exception becomes the precedent and the precedent becomes the culture.

The underlying principle in all three cases is the same. The CEO was looking at one person. The organization was looking at a precedent. The decision that was merciful for one person was corrosive for eighty. **The second-order cultural precedent is almost always stronger than the first-order decision.**

I have seen this concept botched at every stage, at every size, in every industry. It is not a complicated idea. It is a hard idea to hold in your head in the moment, when the person is in your office, when the pressure is on, and when the "kind" option is right in front of you. That is why I put it this way: *represent the people who are not in the room.*

## How to Apply

1. **Before any personnel or policy decision, write the second sentence.** The first sentence is "I'm going to do X for this person." The second sentence is: "*Every employee who hears about this will conclude that the rule in our company is Y.*" Write that Y down. If Y is a rule you would endorse, make the decision. If Y is a rule you would not endorse, do not make the decision — the damage will be larger than the kindness.

2. **Run the mental simulation from three positions.** The person in the room. The person's direct peer. A random engineer in the company. What does each of them conclude about what you value, what you reward, and what you tolerate? If the three conclusions are coherent and match the culture you are trying to build, proceed. If they diverge — if the person in the room gets a kindness and the random engineer gets cynicism — stop.

3. **For demotions specifically: use the Confucian frame.** The good of the individual must be sacrificed for the good of the whole. Consider first the entire company; consider the individual second. If you cannot do that, you cannot be CEO, because the CEO's job is to represent the people not in the room. If you find yourself about to demote someone as the "kind" alternative to firing, stop and check whether the real net cost to the company is positive or negative across all eighty employees. It is almost always negative.

4. **For compensation specifically: never give a raise to someone because they asked.** Build the comp process. Run it on a cycle. Respond to off-cycle asks by referencing the next cycle, not by caving. If your comp process is broken, fix it — don't patch around it with political one-off decisions that teach every employee to ask next.

5. **For exceptions to rules: ask how many exceptions you can afford before the rule stops existing.** Hold the bus for one Dennis Rodman, not two. If you are already holding the bus for someone, the next star does not get the same treatment. The honest answer to the next star is: *yes, the rule applies to you. I know Dennis doesn't have to follow it. That's on me. One is the max, and we're at one.*

6. **Model the decision out loud with one trusted person before you make it.** Not to get permission — to pressure-test the second-order effect. Ask them: *if you heard that I did this, as an employee in the company, what would you conclude?* If their answer is something other than what you intended, rewrite the decision.

## Examples

**Situation:** Head of marketing is not working out. He's been with the company since Series A. The CEO wants to "demote him" to director of content, where he can still contribute but isn't running the function. His equity grant is VP-level — 1.5% of the company. She's planning to leave the equity intact because "he's earned it."

**Application:** Model the decision from the engineering team's seat. Each engineer has 0.15% equity. The head of marketing has just been removed from his job for not being good enough at it — a fact everyone will infer regardless of what the public messaging says. He now has ten times their equity while performing a job that is, in their view, smaller than theirs. The conclusion every engineer draws is: *VPs don't face consequences here. The rules for VPs are different from the rules for me. Failing at your job is a rounding error if your title is big enough.* That conclusion, spread across the whole company, is a cultural wound that takes eighteen months to heal, if it ever does. The right answer is almost always: fire the head of marketing, handle it with dignity, run a real search. If he is genuinely valuable in a different specific role — not "a smaller version of the same role" — move him to that different role at the appropriate equity for that role. Do not leave the equity intact. That is the part that poisons everything.

**Result:** You lose one VP, which is hard. You preserve the fairness signal for seventy-nine other employees, which is much harder and much more valuable. The company's capacity to recruit and retain — especially among the senior engineers who are watching this event most carefully — is intact.

**Situation:** Strong PM asks the CEO for a $20K raise. She's underpaid relative to market by about that amount. The CEO is tempted to grant it on the spot.

**Application:** The raise may be deserved. The decision structure is wrong. If you give it now, in response to the ask, you have created a new rule in your company: *ask and you shall receive.* Every PM who did not ask — some of whom are doing equally good work — just got punished for not asking. Within ninety days, every PM in the company will have asked. You will either cave and inflate the comp structure, or you will refuse some and not others, and you will be in the middle of a political comp war. The correct move: acknowledge the concern, do not grant it off-cycle, and accelerate the compensation review for the full PM band. If she is underpaid, she will be correctly paid in eight weeks, along with every other PM who is underpaid. The system rewards the work, not the asking. And the CEO communicates back: *I heard you. Here's what I'm doing. I'm not giving you a raise right now because I'm not going to run this company politically, but I am going to make sure the system corrects for what you raised.*

**Result:** The PM feels heard and is paid correctly eight weeks later. Every other PM gets the same treatment. No political comp culture emerges. The signal to the company is: *the thing that gets rewarded is the work, reviewed systematically, against market data.* That is a signal worth defending.

**Situation:** CEO is about to grant a star engineer an exception from the new mandatory management training program because "his time is too valuable." The engineer runs the most critical team in the company.

**Application:** Model the decision from the next manager's seat. Another engineering manager — not a star, but a solid contributor — will hear within a day that the star skipped the training. The conclusion the second manager draws is: *the training is optional if you're good enough.* The third manager, hearing this, draws the same conclusion and skips it too. Within six weeks, the training is effectively dead as a mandatory program. You have not saved the star engineer forty hours. You have destroyed a training program you spent six months designing, plus sent a signal that rules apply selectively in your company — which is the worst signal a culture can receive.

**Result:** Make him attend. Or, if you have genuinely decided he is your one Dennis Rodman, make the exception explicit, name it, and manage the cultural damage personally — *"I'm holding the bus for exactly one person here, and it's him."* But do not pretend the exception has no cost. It does. Most of the time, the right answer is to make the star attend and send the signal that rules apply. The star's lost forty hours is trivial compared to the organizational cost of a rule that applies to some and not others.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Make a personnel decision based only on how the person in the room will feel about it.
**Why:** You are optimizing for one person's feelings and taxing eighty people's sense of fairness. The one person walks out of the room with a pleasant experience. The eighty form a slower, grinding, harder-to-reverse conclusion about what this company values. The net cultural damage is almost always larger than the relief you delivered to the individual. Represent the people who aren't in the room.

**Don't:** Demote someone as the "kind" alternative to firing, and let them keep their VP-level equity and title.
**Why:** Every employee in the company knows the equity bands. Every employee will interpret the intact equity as proof that VPs face no consequences. This single decision will slowly, quietly erode the fairness signal across the whole organization. If a lateral move to a different specific role is genuinely valuable, make the move at the appropriate equity for the new role. Otherwise, fire with dignity and run a real replacement search.

**Don't:** Grant a raise to an employee who asks for one, outside the comp cycle.
**Why:** You have just established that the way to get ahead in your company is political, not meritocratic. Every employee who didn't ask just got punished. Every employee will ask next. Your compensation will be run by whoever has the most nerve, not by whoever does the best work. Build the comp process. Run it on a cycle. Respond to asks by pointing to the process, not by caving to them.

**Don't:** Grant policy exceptions to star performers without naming the precedent you are setting.
**Why:** You can hold the bus for one Dennis Rodman. Not two. The moment you silently grant a second exception, you have two sets of rules, and the accountability system collapses for everyone below the exception line. If you are going to hold the bus for someone, name it explicitly, manage it personally, and be prepared to defend the one-exception rule ruthlessly when the next star asks. Most of the time, the right answer is to enforce the rule and send the signal that it applies to everyone — even the stars. Especially the stars.

**Don't:** Conflate private decisions (invisible to the company) with precedent decisions (visible to everyone). 
**Why:** Not every personnel decision sends a precedent signal. Some severance terms, some private feedback, some individual coaching moments are genuinely between you and one person. Applying the "represent the people not in the room" frame to those is over-indexing. The framework is for decisions whose effect is knowable across the company — which is almost every comp, promotion, demotion, and exception decision, but not every individual feedback conversation.
