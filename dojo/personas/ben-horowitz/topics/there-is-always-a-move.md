# There Is Always a Move

## When to Use
- The founder has decided there are no options. Every advisor agrees. The responsible move is to shut it down, take the acqui-hire, accept the down round, or walk away.
- The option that would actually work has already been named and rejected — usually because it felt crazy, humiliating, out of category, or was a thing "nobody does."
- The founder is at a board meeting tomorrow and the board is going to vote the company into a wind-down, and some part of the founder knows there is a play they haven't looked at hard enough.

## Fails When
- **The market genuinely does not exist.** There is a difference between "this is hard to sell" and "nobody on Earth wants to buy this." The first is a product fight. The second is a shutdown. Don't use "there is always a move" as a reason to burn two more years on something that was never going to work.
- **The blocker is courage, not analysis.** If the founder already knows the move and just can't make themselves do it, this isn't the right framework. Route to the-hard-correct-decision.
- **The "move" is a silver bullet — a pivot, a reposition, an acquisition to avoid the real fight.** If your product is losing head-to-head, the move is not a clever workaround. It's lead bullets. Route there.

## Core Concept

It was March 2001. Loudcloud had $2 million in trailing revenue, 340 employees, six weeks of cash, and a plan that said we'd do $75 million the next year. Every person I respected told me the same thing: it's over. Shut it down. Save what you can.

I took the company public instead.

Not because I had some brilliant strategic insight. Not because I had a plan nobody else could see. I did it because it was the only move left. We had explored every other option and they were all worse. Going public in the worst IPO market in a generation, with essentially no revenue and a mountain of costs, was insane. But bankruptcy was more insane. And doing nothing was the most insane thing of all. There had been 221 tech IPOs in 2000. There would be 19 in 2001. We were one of the 19.

That IPO — and the six years of knife fights that followed — taught me the single most important lesson of my career. *The technology business is so complex and so dynamic — the underlying technology moves, the competition moves, the market moves, the people move — that what looks like a dead end almost never is. Like playing three-dimensional chess on* Star Trek, *there is always a play somewhere. You just have to be willing to look in places that seem impossible.*

This is not checkers. This is motherfuckin' chess.

Here's what people miss about dead ends. They're almost all illusions generated by four things: (1) the tech has already moved while you were looking at the old map, (2) the competition is moving and will expose a flank you can't see yet, (3) the market is moving and a customer who wouldn't talk to you last quarter is three weeks from needing exactly what you sell, (4) the people are moving — the no you got from the CIO last year is not the same no you'll get from her replacement next quarter. In any one of these dimensions a "dead end" can become "obvious in retrospect" in ninety days. And there are four of them moving simultaneously.

The reason smart founders still decide they have no moves is not that the moves aren't there. It's that the moves that are there got rejected because they felt crazy. Going public with six weeks of cash felt crazy. Selling half the company to save the other half felt crazy. Telling your team the truth — we're getting our asses kicked by BladeLogic and if we don't stop the bleeding we will die — felt crazy, until I did it at the Opsware all-hands and the engineers stopped cruising and started building the product that saved us.

Bill Parcells, in his first head coaching season with the New York Giants, got hit with a rash of injuries. He called his friend Al Davis, the Raiders owner, and said: *"Al, I am just not sure how we can win without so many of our best players. What should I do?"* Davis said: *"Bill, nobody cares, just coach your team."* That might be the best CEO advice ever given. The corollary: when you're out of best players — when your plan has broken, when your runway has vaporized, when the market has moved — the move is not to explain why you can't win. The move is to go look in the place that seems impossible and find the play that's actually there.

Every single origin story I have as a CEO started as a dead end. The 2001 IPO. Selling Loudcloud's managed services business to EDS and becoming Opsware. Nine months of head-down product work to overtake BladeLogic. Selling Opsware to HP for $1.6B after the stock had traded at thirty-five cents. Every one of those moves was named "crazy" by someone I respected before it became a plan. Several of them were named "crazy" by me.

## How to Apply

1. **Separate the analytical question from the courage question.** What is the hard correct move here? Then — separately — do I have the courage to make it? These are different questions. Most of the time the first one has an obvious answer, and the founder is presenting it as a dead end because the second answer is no. Diagnose which question you're actually stuck on.

2. **List the options you have rejected as "crazy."** Write them down on paper. Not the ones you considered — the ones you dismissed in under thirty seconds because they felt out of category, humiliating, unusual, or because no one you know has done them. That list is where the move usually is.

3. **Test the dead end against the four moving variables.** Is the tech going to change in the next two quarters in a way that helps? Is a competitor vulnerable in a way you haven't mapped? Is the market turning — macro, buyer behavior, regulation? Are the people moving — new CIO at your biggest no, old champion landing at a new logo? If any one of these is moving, the dead end isn't a dead end; it's a waiting game you can survive.

4. **Refuse the consensus framing.** When every advisor tells you the same thing, that's not proof the answer is right. It's usually proof the answer is conventional. The most valuable CEO decisions happen precisely where you disagree with the people around you — *if everybody agrees, you didn't add any value.* The consensus is almost always optimizing for the advisor's regret minimization, not your company's survival.

5. **Play long enough to get lucky.** Tomorrow in tech looks nothing like today. The move you need may not exist yet this week. What you need is to still be in the game when it shows up. Every decision should be scored in part on: does this keep us alive long enough for the tech, the competition, the market, or the people to move?

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder calls with four months of runway. They have a term sheet for an acqui-hire at a valuation that would return roughly 20% of investor capital and leave the team with jobs but no equity. Their lead investor thinks taking it is the responsible move. Their CTO thinks it's over. They have one enterprise deal in late-stage pilot that would, if it closed, triple their ARR.

**Application:** Wrong framing. You are presenting this as a dead end with one responsible exit. It is not a dead end. Your tech moved — you have a working deployment of the product inside a real enterprise. The market moved — that pilot exists because a buyer got pain bad enough to take a risk on a four-month-of-runway vendor. The people moved — whoever championed you inside that account is now a reference for the next three deals. That's not dead. That's the first hand of the game. The acqui-hire is the consensus move. It's also the regret-minimization move for everyone around the table except you. What have you dismissed as crazy? A bridge note from the existing investors at a punitive valuation to get you to close the pilot. Asking the pilot customer to prepay annual to fund the production push. Selling a minority stake of the company to a strategic. Any one of them gets you to the close. One of them probably works. The acqui-hire is only the move if you've genuinely tested those and they've all failed — not if you've rejected them as awkward.

**Result:** Founder spends 72 hours running the three "crazy" plays in parallel. The existing investors bridge at a flat valuation because their loss is capped either way. The pilot closes. Six months later the company is at a real Series B.

---

**Situation:** CEO at an all-hands. Competitor is eating them in deals. Board wants to pivot to SMB. Executive team wants to acquire a weaker competitor for the tech. The CEO is sitting in the meeting feeling like every option has been considered.

**Application:** All three of those options are silver bullets. They are ways to not do the fight. The move you haven't looked at hard is the one sitting in front of you: *build a better product than the one that's beating you in deals.* That is not a clever strategy. That is the front door. Every smart person on the team will bring you a silver bullet — mine did at Opsware when BladeLogic was beating us — and every one of them is a way of not doing the nine months of grinding product work that actually wins. The "no move" you're feeling is the absence of a clever move. The move is the unglamorous one. There are no silver bullets, only lead bullets. Go through the front door. The big ugly guy standing in front of it is called "build a better product." You have to go through him.

**Result:** Nine months of head-down product work. Opsware went from losing 0-for-8 in the last quarter to winning most of the enterprise accounts by the end of the following year. Ultimately sold to HP for $1.6B.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept the consensus of every advisor as proof you're out of moves.
**Why:** When every person you respect says the same thing, that's not analytical signal. That's social signal. The advisors are optimizing for how they look giving the advice, not for the survival of your company. The 2001 IPO was unanimous no from everyone I respected. It was the only move that worked.

**Don't:** Confuse "I don't have a clever move" with "I don't have a move."
**Why:** Most real moves in a crisis are not clever. They are ugly, humiliating, out of category, or just long and hard. The IPO with six weeks of cash was not clever. The nine months of product grinding against BladeLogic was not clever. They were the only moves that existed. Cleverness is a peacetime luxury.

**Don't:** Use "there is always a move" as permission to burn 18 months on a business that has no market.
**Why:** There is a difference between a hard fight and a non-existent fight. If no customer on Earth wants what you're selling, that is not a move-finding problem. That is a market-does-not-exist problem. Use the four moving variables as a real test — tech, competition, market, people — and if none of them is plausibly moving in your direction on any horizon, the move might actually be to shut it down with dignity. But that is rare. In my experience the "no market" diagnosis is almost always actually a "the product isn't good enough" diagnosis in disguise.
