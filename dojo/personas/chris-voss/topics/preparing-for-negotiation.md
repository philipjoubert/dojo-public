# Preparing for Negotiation

## When to Use
- You have a real negotiation on the calendar. Salary, sales, renewal, board conversation, vendor contract, hard meeting with a report. Anything where the outcome actually matters.
- You have at least an hour. Full prep is a few hours of work. If you've got 48 hours, use them. If you've got twenty minutes, run the compressed version.
- You're coaching someone else and they asked you "how should I prep?" The answer is never "read up on them and wing it."
- You tend to go in sharp and walk out thinking of the right line three hours later. Prep is how you move those lines earlier.

## Fails When
- You rehearse what you're going to say. Prep is writing down what you're going to hear, and what you'll say back to that. Scripts of your own position crumble the moment the counterpart says something you didn't predict.
- You produce a deck instead of a worksheet. Slides are for your audience. Prep is for you, and you need four pages you can read under pressure, not a polished presentation.
- You skip the accusation audit because naming the worst things they could think about you feels bad. That's exactly the page that defuses the attack before it lands.
- You do it in your head instead of on paper. The moment the pulse goes up, the head-version evaporates. Writing it down is the whole point.

## Core Concept

Preparation is not rehearsing your position. Preparation is writing down what you're going to hear and what you'll say back. Four pages, not a deck. The goal is not to sound polished. The goal is to stay curious when your pulse goes up.

The worksheet sits on five layers, and in a real prep session you work through them in order. The team layer: who's on my side, who's on theirs, who's the decision-maker I'm not talking to. The Black Swan layer: what might I not know that would change this whole deal. The tactical layer: what labels and calibrated questions do I have pre-written so I'm not inventing them in the room. The numbers layer: what's my target, my walkaway, my Ackerman sequence if there's a price. And the one-liner: if this whole negotiation came down to a single sentence of what I'm here to achieve, what is it. Write it. Make it short enough to say out loud without breathing.

Then rehearse the scenarios. Not your lines. Theirs. Run through the three or four ways the conversation could go. What do you do if they open with a lowball? What do you do if they bring in a second person you didn't expect? What do you do if they say yes too fast? If you've rehearsed the branches, you don't have to invent them live.

## How to Apply

1. **Read both teams.** Write down who's on your side (and what each person actually wants out of this), and everything you can infer about theirs. Who's the decision-maker? Who's the influencer? Who's on their side who's not in the room? What are their internal politics, quarter-end pressures, competing priorities? Read pronouns in their emails. Heavy "we" and "they" means the real decision-maker isn't at the table.

2. **Pre-identify the Black Swans.** Assume three to five pieces of information exist that, if you knew them, would change everything. You don't know what they are. That's the definition. Write down your guesses. What might they be feeling that they can't say out loud? What pressure are they under? What were they burned by last time? Unspoken stakes are where most deals live.

3. **Write the accusation audit.** Every negative thing they might think about you, your company, your offer. Use their exact worst words. "You're probably thinking we're too small. You might feel like we haven't proven ourselves at your scale." Pre-writing this is how you deliver it calmly in the room instead of getting defensive when it comes at you.

4. **Script the first two or three labels and calibrated questions.** Labels: "It seems like..." "It sounds like..." "It looks like..." specific to this counterpart, not generic. Calibrated questions: "How" and "What" only. "How would this work for your team?" "What's the biggest challenge you're facing?" Four to six of each. Every one you pre-write is a tool you don't have to invent under pressure.

5. **Plan the Ackerman anchors.** If there's a price, write your target. Then your four offers: 65%, 85%, 95%, 100% of target. Use precise, non-round numbers ($37,893, not $38,000). Write what non-monetary sweetener you'll add at the final offer. If it's not a price negotiation, write the equivalent: your opening ask, your step-down, your walkaway.

6. **Know your BATNA and theirs.** Your best alternative if this deal dies. Their best alternative if this deal dies. These aren't your opening move; they're your floor and your read on their floor. A bad deal is worse than no deal. Knowing the line you won't cross is how you walk before you settle below it.

7. **Write the one-liner.** One sentence. What are you there to achieve? Not the strategy, the outcome. "I'm here to leave this call with a signed renewal at a rate that keeps us profitable." Say it out loud before you start. When the conversation drifts, this is what brings you back.

8. **Run the scenario drill.** Close your eyes. Play the call forward. What do they open with? What's your first line back? What do you do if they push back hard? If they agree too easily? If a second person joins the room? Rehearse the branches, not the monologue. The pulse goes up less when the situation isn't new.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has 48 hours before a meeting with a much larger potential partner. She's intimidated by the size asymmetry and wants to "win."
**Application:** She drops the "win" frame and opens the worksheet. Page one, labels pre-written for what she expects to hear: "It seems like getting this done before end of quarter matters more than the headline number." Page two, calibrated questions: "How does this deal look to your team?" "What's the biggest challenge on your side?" Page three, accusation audit: "You're probably thinking we're too small for this. You might feel like we haven't proven ourselves at your scale." Page four, numbers and a one-liner: "I'm here to leave with either a signed LOI or a clear no."
**Result:** She walks in curious instead of nervous. When the counterpart opens with a lowball, she doesn't counter. She labels: "It sounds like there's real pressure on the budget side." He elaborates. She learns two of the three Black Swans she guessed at. The deal gets structured differently than either side expected going in.

**Situation:** A consultant has an hour before a renewal call with a $200K client who's been slow to respond.
**Application:** Compressed prep. Labels: "It seems like the last quarter didn't land the way we both hoped." Calibrated questions: "What's the thing that made this week the week to raise it?" Accusation audit: "You're probably feeling like we haven't earned another year." BATNA: she knows her floor on this account. One-liner: "I'm here to find out whether this relationship is actually fixable or whether they've already decided."
**Result:** She opens with the accusation audit. The client softens. The real issue surfaces in the first ten minutes, not the last ten. Deal gets renewed at a slightly reduced scope she can live with.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write your opening statement and memorize it.
**Why:** The moment they say something you didn't predict, the statement is dead and you're improvising. Pre-writing labels and calibrated questions gives you flexible tools. Pre-writing a monologue gives you a script that only works if they follow it, and they won't.

**Don't:** Skip the accusation audit because writing down your own weaknesses feels bad.
**Why:** Those accusations are loaded in their head whether you write them or not. The only question is who says them first. If they do, you're defending. If you do, you're defusing. The page that's hardest to write is usually the one that saves the call.

**Don't:** Prep only your own side. Walk in knowing nothing about their team's politics, pressures, and decision process.
**Why:** Two-thirds of your counterparts have a different negotiation style than yours. Half your Black Swans live in their internal dynamics. Showing up with zero read on the other side's team means you're negotiating blindfolded, and they'll feel it.

**Don't:** Rehearse the call only in your head.
**Why:** The head-version collapses under stress. A written page doesn't. Four pages you can glance at during the call is worth more than ten hours of mental rehearsal. Print them. Bring them. Use them.
