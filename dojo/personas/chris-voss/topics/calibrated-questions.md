# Calibrated Questions

## When to Use
- Someone has made a demand you can't or won't meet, and you want to push back without creating conflict. "How am I supposed to do that?" does the work.
- You need to know whether the person across from you has real authority, or whether there's a boss, board, partner, or spouse who will kill the deal later.
- You want to test whether a "yes" is actually going to get implemented, or whether it's a polite nothing.
- You're piling up information before you move to proposals and closed questions would just shut the conversation down.

## Fails When
- You start a question with "Why." Across every culture, "Why did you do that?" lands as an accusation. Amygdala lights up, defensiveness kicks in, and they stop thinking with you and start defending against you.
- You fire three questions in a row. They'll answer the last one, or they'll give you a rushed non-answer. The silence after a calibrated question is where the work happens. Don't fill it.
- You ask questions you don't actually want answered. "What's the biggest problem with this proposal?" will get you a list of problems. Be ready to work with what comes back.
- You use them to manipulate rather than to genuinely invite problem-solving. People feel the difference. You'll get stiff, guarded answers instead of real ones.

## Core Concept
Calibrated questions are the closest thing to a superpower in negotiation. A calibrated question is an open-ended question that starts with "How" or "What" and is built to put your counterpart's mental energy on solving your problem. These aren't innocent requests for information. They are carefully crafted instruments that give the other side the illusion of control while you steer.

Think about the difference between "Can you lower the price?" and "How am I supposed to do that?" The first is a closed question that invites a flat "no." The second suspends the other side's unbelief by asking for help. It's deferential. It's curious. And it puts them to work on a problem that benefits you. The greatest calibrated question ever invented is this: "How am I supposed to do that?" I've used it hundreds of times. In kidnapping negotiations, in business deals, in conversations with my kids. When someone makes an impossible demand, this question gently pushes back without creating conflict. It says no without using the word. It invites them to reconsider their position while feeling like they're helping you figure things out.

Two words only: "How" and "What." "Why" is dangerous because it sounds accusatory. "Who," "When," and "Where" are fine for logistics but they don't do the psychological work. "How" and "What" open people up. They invite elaboration. They make your counterpart feel smart and in charge, which is exactly where you want them. Because when they start problem-solving with you, they're no longer fighting against you. That's the whole ballgame.

## How to Apply
1. When a demand or position lands that you can't accept, default to "How am I supposed to do that?" Say it slowly. Sound like you mean it. You are not rejecting, you are asking for help.
2. Pick "How" or "What" to open every calibrated question. Never "Why." The one exception is when you want them to defend a position that benefits you: "Why would you ever leave a company like ours?" "Why would you ever want to do business with us?"
3. Ask one question. Then stop. Let the silence do the work. Their brain has to process and respond. If you jump in with a follow-up, you short-circuit the whole mechanism.
4. For hidden stakeholders, ask: "How on board are the people not on this call?" "What do your colleagues see as the main challenges here?" These surface the second table before it kills your deal.
5. For implementation, ask: "How will we know we're on track?" "What do we do if we get off track?" "How are you going to make this happen?" This converts a vague "yes" into a concrete plan, and people who explain their own execution are far more likely to actually execute.

## Examples

**Situation:** Harvard Negotiation Course, 2006. Chris Voss is the only non-Harvard, non-MIT, non-Tufts outsider in a room of 144 of the best negotiation students in the world. His first mock partner, a sharp kid in khakis, throws out anchor offers with airtight logical justifications.
**Application:** Every time Andy floats a price and an argument, Voss answers with some variation of "How am I supposed to do that?" He never argues the logic. He just keeps asking how he could possibly accept it. Andy has to explain. Each explanation exposes a crack. Each crack widens.
**Result:** "You got literally every dime he had," the instructor tells the class. In his brief, Andy was supposed to hold a quarter of it back in reserve. He gave it all up. The next day it happens again with a different partner. The instructor finally asks: "Seems like all you do to these Harvard Law students is say 'No' and stare at them, and they fall apart. Is it really that easy?" It really was. The calibrated question was doing every bit of the heavy lifting. It forced the other side to negotiate against themselves.

**Situation:** Kidnapping case in Ecuador. Kidnappers' ransom demand is far beyond what the family can pay. Arguing the demand creates conflict and risks the hostage.
**Application:** Voss coaches the family to respond, call after call, with "How am I supposed to do that?" Not a refusal. Not a counteroffer. Just the question.
**Result:** The kidnapper starts explaining his side. In explaining, he reveals constraints and timelines. More importantly, he starts to confront the impracticality of his own demand. Over multiple conversations, the ransom comes down dramatically. The family didn't have to say the demand was unreasonable. The question made the kidnapper say it himself.

**Situation:** A deal is going suspiciously well. The person across the table is agreeing to everything. Something feels off.
**Application:** "How on board are the people not in this room?" Long pause. "Well, my business partner has some concerns."
**Result:** Black Swan surfaced. The real decision-maker wasn't at the table. Without that question, the deal gets agreed to in the room and killed outside the room. Instead, the next hour is spent working through the partner's concerns directly, and the final deal actually sticks.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead with "Why did you..." even when you're genuinely curious.
**Why:** "Why" sounds accusatory in every language and every culture. "Why did you do that?" triggers defensiveness before the sentence is finished. Your counterpart stops thinking with you and starts building a defense. Rephrase to "What led you to..." or "How did you end up..." and you get the same information without the hit to the relationship.

**Don't:** Stack questions. "How are you going to make this happen, and what's your timeline, and how will we measure success?"
**Why:** They'll answer the last one, or they'll give you a surface-level response to all three. A calibrated question is supposed to pull their mental energy onto one problem. If you pile on, you dilute the pressure and lose the silence that does most of the work. One question. Then wait.

**Don't:** Use "Can you..." or "Will you..." when you actually need information or movement.
**Why:** Closed questions get closed answers. "Can you lower the price?" invites "No." "Do you have authority here?" invites "Yes," whether it's true or not. Reframe: "What flexibility do you have on price?" "How does the decision-making process work on your end?" Now you get real information, and the other side feels respected instead of pressed.
