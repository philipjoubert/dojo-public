# Disagree and Commit

## When to Use
- Reasonable people have debated a decision and further debate will not produce new information.
- A senior leader wants to give ground on a call where they genuinely disagree but the team has more ground truth.
- A team is stuck in a war of attrition where whoever has the most stamina wins.
- You want to avoid the failure mode where a decision gets made but half the room executes with reservations.

## Fails When
- Used as a silencing mechanism. "We disagree-and-committed on this, so stop pushing back" is not what the protocol is for. The protocol happens before the commitment, not after.
- The commit half isn't real. If the person who "disagreed and committed" is telegraphing "I told you so" behind the scenes or slow-walking execution, the protocol failed and trust erodes.
- Applied to one-way doors where further deliberation is warranted. Disagree and commit is a speed mechanism; it is not a replacement for the heavyweight process that actual one-way doors require.
- Invoked to avoid honest debate. If the disagreement was suppressed rather than expressed, there's nothing to commit to afterwards.

## Core Concept

I disagree and commit all the time. I'll debate something for an hour, or a day, or a week — and then I'll say, "You know what? I really disagree with this, but you have more ground truth than I do. We're going to do it your way. And I promise I will never tell you I told you so." The protocol is simple in structure and easy to get wrong in practice. The debate has to be candid — not polite, not guarded. If I didn't actually express my disagreement, I have nothing real to commit to afterwards. When the commitment comes, it has to be full. Not sulky compliance. Not "I'll do what you say but I won't really try." Real commitment.

The protocol exists because without it, organizations default to one of two failure modes. The first is the war of attrition: whoever has the most stamina in the meeting wins, and decisions get made based on who can outlast whom rather than on the quality of the argument. That's bad for every reason. The second is false consensus: everyone agrees in the room and then sabotages execution afterwards. That's worse, because it looks like success until it produces failure. Disagree and commit is a third path. You express disagreement with conviction. If the other party has more ground truth or if the decision is a two-way door, you commit — fully — to their approach. You do not tell them I told you so. You do not hold it in reserve. You execute as if it were your own call.

The most important thing to understand about the protocol is that senior people should use it more than junior people. Counter-intuitive, but true. When the CEO disagrees and commits, they're modeling that disagreement is safe and that commitment is real. When a junior person is the one who has to "disagree and commit" because the boss said so, the protocol degenerates into a polite way of overruling them. That's not what it is for. The move I make most often is: I disagree, I say so, and I commit to your approach because you have the ground truth. The team knows I disagreed. The team also knows I'm not going to sabotage the execution or gloat if it fails. That trust is what makes the protocol functional.

## How to Apply

1. **Express disagreement candidly.** Not in a memo six weeks later. In the room, with evidence, with conviction. If you didn't disagree aloud, you have nothing to commit to.

2. **Recognize when the debate is done.** Signals: no new information is being introduced, the parties are restating their original positions, ground truth lives with one side.

3. **Name the commitment explicitly.** "I disagree, and I'll commit to your approach." Say it out loud. Put it in writing if it helps. The explicit naming is part of what makes the commitment real.

4. **Execute as if it were your own call.** No hedging. No back-channel complaints. No "I told you so" if it fails. If you're going to withhold commitment, don't pretend to give it.

5. **If you're the senior person, use the protocol more, not less.** Every time you disagree and commit, you're demonstrating to the organization what good practice looks like. Every time you pull rank instead, you're teaching people that rank wins over ground truth.

6. **Escalate fast when the parties genuinely can't resolve.** The world needs decisions. Don't let a team sit in unresolved disagreement for a quarter. Escalate to whoever can make the call, have them hear both sides, make the call, and ask everyone to disagree and commit.

## Examples

**Situation:** Advertising on Amazon. For years I resisted. I thought advertising compromised the customer experience. The team argued repeatedly that sponsored-product ads would improve the customer experience by helping customers discover relevant products, generate significant revenue, and could be structured to keep customers on Amazon's own pages.

**Application:** I disagreed with the premise for a long time. Eventually I realized: the team has more ground truth than I do about customer response to specific ad formats. They had tested, they had data, they had customer signal. I disagreed and committed. I also publicly changed my mind when the data proved the team right.

**Result:** Advertising became a major profit center for Amazon. Had I pulled rank and blocked it, we would have left tens of billions of dollars of value on the table and demoralized a team that was right. The protocol prevented that.

---

**Situation:** Fire Phone. After the failure, I met with VP Ian Freed, who had led the initiative. The product had flopped. Write-downs were substantial. The team was demoralized.

**Application:** I told Freed: "You can't, for one minute, feel bad about the Fire Phone. Promise me you won't lose a minute of sleep." The point was that we had all committed to the bet. I didn't disagree at the time. The fact that it failed doesn't retroactively mean I was right to disagree — because I hadn't disagreed. Pretending I had would have been the exact behavior the protocol is designed to prevent.

**Result:** The team that failed on Fire Phone went on to ship Echo and Alexa. A culture that punishes committed failures loses the courage to take swings. A culture that honors committed failures — even when they fail badly — keeps that courage intact.

---

**Situation:** Third-party sellers on Amazon product pages. Internal debates were intense. Senior executives argued we shouldn't let competitors sell the same product on our own pages — it would cannibalize our direct sales.

**Application:** I disagreed with the internal consensus. I thought customer choice was the right answer, even when it meant competitors appeared on our pages. I made the call. Many senior executives disagreed. They committed. Over time, some of them changed their minds; others continued to privately disagree but kept executing.

**Result:** Third-party sellers became more than half of Amazon's retail volume. The people who disagreed and committed honored the protocol, even those who never came around to agreeing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use disagree and commit as a way to shut down debate.
**Why:** The protocol is not a silencing mechanism. It activates after genuine debate, not instead of it. If the debate never happened, or was cut short, there's nothing to commit to — there's only compliance, which isn't the same thing.

**Don't:** Fake the commit.
**Why:** Passive-aggressive compliance — executing while telegraphing "I disagreed with this" — is the worst failure mode. It looks like agreement, but it produces sabotaged execution. Either give real commitment or keep arguing.

**Don't:** Tell them I told you so when the decision fails.
**Why:** The entire reason senior people can safely disagree and commit is that the person they committed to trusts them not to gloat if it fails. Break that trust once and nobody brings you hard calls again.

**Don't:** Apply this to one-way doors.
**Why:** Disagree and commit is a speed mechanism for two-way doors. For irreversible decisions, you do not want to resolve disagreement by "let's just commit." You want to resolve it by doing the heavyweight work — more data, more scenarios, longer deliberation. Speed is not the priority on one-way doors.

**Don't:** Require junior people to disagree and commit while senior people never do.
**Why:** That's just a dressed-up way of saying the boss is always right. The protocol depends on senior people using it visibly and often. Otherwise the junior version is coerced, not committed.

**Don't:** Let a team sit in unresolved disagreement indefinitely.
**Why:** The world needs decisions. A quarter of deadlock costs more than making the wrong call and course-correcting. Escalate fast. Whoever has the authority hears both sides, makes the call, asks for commitment, and moves on.
