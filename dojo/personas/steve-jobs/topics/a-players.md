# A Players Only — 50 to 1

## When to Use
- You're about to compromise on a hire because the role has been open for three months.
- Your team used to ship great work and now ships average work, and the headcount is higher than ever.
- You're wondering how much of your calendar should go to recruiting. (It's more than you think.)
- You're reviewing a top performer's work and it's not good enough — but you're not sure whether to say so.
- You're considering letting a B player stay because "they're not that bad."

## Fails When
- The role genuinely has a 2-to-1 dynamic range. Not every job is 50-to-1. A great customer service rep is better than an average one, but not 50 times better. Know when this framework applies.
- The standard is used to brutalize people. Directness is a gift to people who are good. Contempt is not a gift to anyone.
- "A player" becomes a pedigree signal (brand names on the resume) rather than a work signal (can you actually see what they shipped and how). Real A-players hunt for other real A-players; they don't hire resumes.

## Core Concept

Most things in life have a narrow range between average and best. An average taxicab driver versus the best — you get to your destination maybe 30% faster. An average car versus the best? Maybe 20%. An average CD player versus the best? Maybe 20%. In most of life, two to one is a big dynamic range.

But in software — and it used to be true in hardware too — the difference between average and best is 50 to 1. Maybe 100 to 1. Easy. Very few things in life are like this. I was lucky enough to spend my life in one of them.

Once you understand this, everything about how you build teams changes. If you're in a business where there's a 50 to 1 difference between great and average, and you settle for average, you've already lost. You can't make it up with process, management, or working harder. An average team will simply never produce what a great team can produce.

Here's the thing I observed over my career: A players hire A players. B players hire C players.

A players want to work with other A players. They're secure enough to seek out people as good as or better than themselves. They're not threatened by excellence — they're attracted to it. B players are different. They're insecure. They hire slightly worse than themselves to avoid being outshone. Those C players hire D players. Before long, you've got a company full of mediocrity. This is why you cannot compromise on hiring. Not even once. The cascade feeds on itself.

When you get enough A players together — when you go through the incredible work to find five of them — they really like working with each other. Most have never had a chance to before. They push each other. They don't want to let each other down. And they don't want to hire B and C players. It becomes self-policing. They only want to hire more A players.

That's what the Mac team was like. All A players. It was the hardest they've ever worked in their life. Many of them will tell you it was the happiest they've ever been.

### The Rock Tumbler

When I was young, there was a widowed man up the street, maybe in his eighties. One day he said, "Come into my garage, I want to show you something." He pulled out this dusty rock tumbler — a motor, a coffee can, a little band between them. We went out and got some regular old ugly rocks, put them in the can with a little bit of liquid and grit powder, closed it up, turned the motor on. "Come back tomorrow." The can was making a racket as the stones went around.

The next day we opened it. Inside were these amazingly beautiful polished rocks. The same common stones that had gone in — through rubbing against each other, creating friction, making some noise — came out polished.

That's always been my metaphor for a team working really hard on something they're passionate about. It's through the group bumping up against each other, arguing, fighting, making some noise, working together — they polish each other. They polish the ideas. What comes out are these really beautiful stones.

You don't get that from a team of B players. You get silence, or you get politeness, or you get mediocre agreement. You don't get polish.

## How to Apply

1. **Spend 20% of your time on recruiting. At least.** Even as CEO. Recruiting is the heart and soul of what we do. Letting HR handle it is not recruiting — that's administration. Real recruiting is hunting.

2. **Hunt, don't post.** Some of the best people aren't looking for jobs. They're doing great work somewhere else. Find them. Know what they built. Know who shipped it. Then convince them to take a chance on something new.

3. **Test conviction under pressure.** In interviews, purposely upset people. Criticize their prior work. "That really turned out to be a bozo product. Why did you work on that?" You want to see if they fold or if they have firm belief and pride in what they did. The worst thing in an interview is someone who agrees with you and knuckles under.

4. **Hire for content, not process.** The best people really understand the content. They're a pain in the butt to manage because they push back and argue. You put up with it because they're so great at the content. Be suspicious of candidates who are great at meetings and unclear about the product.

5. **Never compromise. Not once.** One B player leads to a team of C players which poisons the organization. The moment you settle because the role has been open too long, you've started the cascade. Better to leave the role open another quarter than to hire the wrong person.

6. **Hire broadly, not just technically.** The best people on the Macintosh team were musicians and poets and artists and zoologists and historians who also happened to be the best computer scientists in the world. Taste is exposure. Narrow backgrounds produce linear solutions. Hire people with diverse dots to connect.

7. **Keep the team small.** You can never have too many great people. But you can have too many people. Small teams force discipline — you can't do everything, you can't carry dead weight, everyone has to contribute. The Mac team was about 100. Once you start splitting into teams that coordinate through meetings, the golden age is ending.

8. **Fire the cascade.** If you inherited B players, the kindest and fastest thing is to remove them. Every day they stay, they're hiring more B and C players under them. The team you love will quietly leave unless you act.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has a role open for four months. Two "okay" candidates came through. Pressure to close the role is mounting.
**Application:** Don't hire. The cost of an okay hire is not the cost of their salary; it's the cost of the people they will hire, the people they will tolerate, and the people they will drive away. Raise the bar, take the slow cycle, keep looking. The role being open for another quarter is the cheap option. A four-month-open role is not a crisis; a B-player hire is.
**Result:** Either you find an A player on the fifth cycle, or you learn the role was overspec'd and redesign it. Either way you haven't poisoned the team.

**Situation:** The CEO of a 200-person startup says they can't spend 20% of their time on recruiting — there's too much to do.
**Application:** "Too much to do" and "B players are quietly hiring C players while you're in product meetings" are the same sentence. The highest-leverage thing you do is shape who gets into the building. If recruiting doesn't come first on your calendar, you're deciding that the long-term direction of the company matters less than this quarter's execution. It doesn't. Move recruiting to the top of the calendar.
**Result:** Slower short-term operating, better long-term company. And usually, shockingly, the operating gets *better* too — because the people the CEO hired are better than the people HR filtered to.

**Situation:** A top engineer's last three deliverables have been below her usual bar. You're not sure whether to say anything.
**Application:** Say it. Clearly. "This work isn't good enough. Here's why." Don't hide it in feedback sandwiches. Don't delay. When you get really good people, they know they're really good. You don't have to baby their egos. What really matters is the work. That's all that matters. Directness is the gift you owe them.
**Result:** Either she rises to the standard and ships the next thing great, or she tells you she disagrees and defends the work (which may be correct — you should be willing to change your mind). Or she doesn't, and you've learned something about whether she's still right for the role. All three outcomes are better than pretending.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire from resumes alone.
**Why:** Resumes measure pedigree, not work. Find out what the candidate actually built. Ask to walk through it. Ask a follow-up. Then a second follow-up. You never want the first answer; you want the third. If they run out of detail on the second follow-up, they didn't actually build it.

**Don't:** Sandwich critical feedback.
**Why:** "Good, bad, good" landings obscure the point. Good people find direct feedback beneficial. You do need to do it in a way that doesn't call into question your confidence in their abilities — but leaves no room for interpretation that *this particular work* isn't good enough.

**Don't:** Settle because "the team needs headcount."
**Why:** The team needs *people who make the work better*. A B-player hire isn't "extra capacity" — it's a tax the A-players pay in every meeting, every code review, every recruit conversation they then have to overrule.

**Don't:** Protect C players because they're loyal.
**Why:** Loyalty to a C player is disloyalty to the A players who deserve to work with peers. The long-tenured C player is the most damaging hire you have, because the pattern is baked in and the cascade is downstream of them.

**Don't:** Lock people in with contracts.
**Why:** "They're locked in for another two years so we don't have to worry" is the moment you've stopped making the company a place people want to be. The best retention is an environment people don't want to leave. If they want to leave, they probably should.
