# Marketing Is About Values, Not Specs

## When to Use
- Your marketing team just handed you a comparison chart showing you beat competitors on 14 of 20 features, and that's the proposed ad.
- The brand feels generic. Customers can't articulate what you stand for that's different from anyone else.
- You're rebuilding a brand that's been neglected. People used to care; now they don't.
- You're launching a campaign and the team is debating which product feature to lead with.
- Your sales team is winning on specs and losing anyway to a competitor people *feel* something about.

## Fails When
- Your product is a true commodity and customers are buying on price. Talk price then. Values-based brand marketing of interchangeable widgets is theatre.
- You stake the brand on values the product doesn't deliver. That's not marketing, that's fraud, and customers catch on fast.
- You're in a B2B enterprise context where the buyer legitimately needs spec justification for internal procurement. Values still matter, but they're packaged differently.

## Core Concept

When you think about the great brands — Nike, Disney, Coke, Levi's — the brand means something. And Apple is one of those great brands. But even a great brand needs investment and caring if it's going to retain its relevance and vitality. When I came back to Apple in 1997, the brand had clearly suffered from neglect. We had to bring it back.

The way to do it is not to talk about speeds and feeds. Not to talk about MIPS and megahertz. Not to talk about why we're better than Windows. Customers don't care.

The best example of all, and one of the greatest jobs of marketing the universe has ever seen, is Nike. Nike sells a commodity — sneakers. And yet when you think of Nike, you feel something different than a shoe company. In their ads they don't ever talk about the product. They don't talk about air soles or how they're better than Reebok's air soles. They honor great athletes, and they honor great athletics. That's who they are. That's what they stand for.

The dairy industry tried for twenty years to convince you that milk was good for you. It's a lie, but they tried anyway. Sales went flat. Then they tried "got milk" and sales went through the roof. "Got milk" doesn't even talk about the product. It talks about the *absence* of the product. That's what great marketing does — it makes you feel something about what your life is like with or without this thing, not about what the thing's specs are.

Apple at its core, its core value, is that we believe people with passion can change the world for the better. That's what we believe. And the first ad we made after I came back was Think Different. It didn't show a single product. It didn't mention specs. It was a tribute — to Einstein, Gandhi, Edison, Ali, Dylan. The crazy ones. The misfits, the rebels, the troublemakers. The round pegs in the square holes. The ones who see things differently. We honored them. Because that's who we are. That's who we build for.

Everything else — the products, the technology, the business — flows from that core belief.

### Remembering Who We Were

When I came back to Apple in 1997, the company was three months from bankruptcy. Products were confused. Brand was damaged. Best people had left. What we had to do wasn't invent something new. We had to remember who we were.

So we simplified. Cut the product line from dozens to four. Stopped licensing the Mac OS. Focused on making insanely great products again — products we were proud of, products we wanted to use ourselves. And we told the world what Apple stood for. Think Different. We honored the crazy ones. We reminded people — and ourselves — why Apple had mattered in the first place.

The company that was three months from bankruptcy became the most valuable company in the world. Not because we found some new strategy. Because we remembered who we were.

That's the marketing lesson. The marketing didn't make Apple great. The marketing *reminded* Apple what it was, so that everything else — product, design, hiring, operations — could flow from that clarity. If you don't know what you stand for, no amount of clever ad copy will save you. If you know, the marketing will mostly write itself.

## How to Apply

1. **Define what you believe, in one sentence, and say it often.** Not what you do. What you *believe*. "We believe people with passion can change the world for the better." "We honor great athletes." "We believe in craftsmanship." It has to be short, true, and sayable internally without embarrassment.

2. **Never lead with specs in consumer marketing.** Specs are support. Values are the lead. If the ad shows a chip or a feature before it shows a person or a belief, it's an engineering brochure, not marketing.

3. **Make the customer the hero.** The ad is about them — what they're doing, what they stand for, what they care about. Nike doesn't put Nike shoes at the center. They put the athlete at the center. The shoe is implicit.

4. **Use the absence test.** What would the customer's life be like *without* this? "Got milk" is about the absence. Think Different was about the absence of the spirit of innovation in the computer industry. Absence is often more powerful than presence.

5. **Refuse the comparison chart.** "We beat them on 14 of 20" is the voice of second place. If you have to prove you're better point by point, you've already lost the narrative. Lead with the belief, let the belief do the work.

6. **Make the brand a tribute, not a boast.** Great brand marketing honors something outside the brand — the athlete, the artist, the creator, the rebel. The customer sees the honor and recognizes themselves in it. Boasting about yourself makes customers defensive. Honoring them makes them feel seen.

7. **When the brand has decayed, remember who you were.** Don't invent a new identity. Find the old one. Simplify back to the core. Tell the world what you've always stood for, then rebuild the product and the company to match.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple 1997. Product line confused. Brand damaged. Nearly bankrupt.
**Application:** Think Different. Not a product ad. A tribute to the crazy ones — Einstein, Gandhi, Edison, Ali, Dylan. The ad doesn't mention a single Apple product. It reminds customers and employees what Apple believes.
**Result:** The ad doesn't sell a Mac that quarter; it rebuilds the permission to care about Apple. Over the next five years, Apple ships the iMac, the iPod, the retail stores, the iTunes Store — every one of them executed from the values the ad articulated. The marketing didn't save the company; it framed what the company was rebuilding *toward*.

**Situation:** The milk industry has spent twenty years saying "milk is good for you." Sales flat.
**Application:** "Got milk." The ad doesn't argue a nutritional fact. It shows a person at a pivotal moment — the cookie, the moment — with no milk. The absence is the ad.
**Result:** Sales rise dramatically. Not because the spec message got stronger. Because the emotional frame changed. The customer stopped being a patient receiving a health lesson and became a hero in a small domestic story.

**Situation:** A SaaS company's marketing leads with feature-parity charts vs the incumbent. Their product is genuinely better on craft, but charts don't show craft.
**Application:** Stop the charts. What do you actually believe? "We believe customer support shouldn't be an afterthought." "We believe software should feel made, not assembled." Lead the marketing with the belief. Show customers being treated with that care. Let the specs be the implicit proof.
**Result:** The charts stop being the basis of competition. You start competing on a dimension the incumbent is too large to match — care. Craft-obsessed customers self-select. You lose the customers who genuinely only want specs. You keep the ones whose values match, and those are worth more.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Market what you wish you were.
**Why:** Customers can tell. If the brand values craft and the product is sloppy, the gap is the story and the story isn't flattering. Marketing can point at real values; it cannot manufacture absent ones.

**Don't:** Lead with the technology stack.
**Why:** Customers don't care about the stack. They care about what their life is like with the product. "Built on React with GraphQL" is engineering trivia, not a value.

**Don't:** Argue on the competitor's ground.
**Why:** Every comparison ad trains the customer that the dimensions *they* set are the important ones. Change the dimension to one you own. Nike didn't compete on air-cushioning specs; it competed on who honored athletes better.

**Don't:** Commission a new brand every few years.
**Why:** Continuity is a value. A brand that reinvents itself every three years has no values, just rotations. The brands that last find a core belief and deepen it over decades.

**Don't:** Mistake "we have to educate the market" for good marketing.
**Why:** If you need to educate the market on why your technical feature matters, you're selling a feature, not a value. Find the underlying human thing that feature enables and sell *that*.
