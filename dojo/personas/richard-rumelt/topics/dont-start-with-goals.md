# Don't Start with Goals

## When to Use
- Someone hands you a number and asks for a strategy to hit it.
- The retreat is scheduled to begin with "let's first agree on goals."
- The CEO has a 20/20 plan, an On-to-the-Next plan, an SSC ("Success Score Card"), or a list of twelve "priorities" — and the strategy work has been collapsed into how to deliver those numbers.
- A vision statement or mission statement is being treated as the foundation that strategy is then "deduced" from.

## Fails When
- A hard external constraint is real (a regulator's deadline, a survival threshold, a debt covenant). Treat the constraint as a given and start the diagnosis downstream of it.
- Operational targets inside a settled strategy — sales quotas, cycle times, defect rates. Those are management, and management needs goals. Strategy is the upstream question of *which* numbers should be chased.

## Core Concept

Goals are an *outcome* of strategy, not its input. Most pop-culture advice on strategy gets this exactly backward — *first agree on goals, then figure out how to get there.* That is not strategy work. That is the most common pathway to bad strategy. Set a goal arbitrarily, without an analysis or understanding of a critical challenge or opportunity, and what you have is an *unsupported goal* — a decision masquerading as a goal. Unsupported goals like hitting a specific profit target in the next twelve months are *Dilbert-style corporate management*, because such goals are disconnected from the reality of the situation.

The pop-psychology belief is that goals motivate. Stupid arbitrary goals don't motivate achievement. They motivate cynicism and fabrication. *Good strategic goals are the outcome of strategy, not its input.* When organizational leaders face the issue of strategy, they are building a bridge between general desires and ambitions and the specifics of action in the here and now. If they do their job well, *one outcome* will be good strategic objectives. Confusing the output for the input is the origin of wheel-spinning frustration in many strategic retreats.

The same trap shows up at the other end of the goal hierarchy: the cascade of vision / mission / values / strategy / goals — what I call the **Standard Narrative**. The Standard Narrative is a literary form used to *substitute* for an Action Agenda. It is fine for the website and for the employee handbook. It is not a strategy. *If A stands on, or is deduced from, B, then where did B come from?* The mission statement comes from the vision statement comes from the statement of core values — turtles all the way down. None of it tells you what to do tomorrow.

And then there are the priorities. The word *priority* means *first*. A list of twelve priorities is a confession of unwillingness to choose. *You wouldn't want to be on a plane and hear an air traffic controller say I'm giving priority on Runway 3 to the following list of airplanes.* Having a list of priorities is a way of finessing the fact that we don't want to make a choice. It is the political residue of trying to give every interested party a present under the Christmas tree.

## How to Apply

1. **When a goal is presented as the starting point, refuse the framing.** Ask: *what is going on in your situation that makes this the right number?* If they can't answer in a paragraph, the goal is unsupported.
2. **Replace "what do we want?" with "what's actually going on here?"** Diagnosis comes before objectives. *Strategy should be based on judgments gained by examining changes, problems, skills, resources, and opportunities.*
3. **Distinguish ambitions from objectives.** Ambitions are broad ("survive, increase profit, be respected"). Objectives are tasks ("set up operations in Australia," "land a Whole Foods national account," "develop a brushless-fan platform that fits our existing skills"). A good objective resolves ambiguity, defines a problem simpler than the original challenge, and is something the organization knows how to achieve or can be expected to work out how to achieve.
4. **Cap the priorities at two or three.** Tell the client to push the rest into next year. Refuse to call the long list a strategy. Strategy is the focused application of strength against weakness — and there is no focus in twelve.
5. **Separate the Standard Narrative from the Action Agenda.** If the company needs a vision statement for the website and the all-hands deck, fine — produce one. Keep it short. Then put it aside and do the actual work: identify the key challenge and design the action steps to overcome it. Don't confuse the public face of strategy with strategy.
6. **Treat unsupported numerical goals as red flags, not destinations.** When a CEO sets "double revenue in five years" with no diagnosis, the consequence is rarely growth. It is PowerPoint hogwash, manipulated forecasts, fabrication, and eventual termination.

## Examples

**Situation:** Curtiss-Wright, 1974. CEO Ted Berner had guided the company since 1960. The Wankel rotary engine bet had collapsed (oil crisis, emissions standards, GM canceled its Wankel program); the stock had fallen from $60 to $5. Berner brought in Rumelt, a young academic with a research book on diversification, to help "shape the corporate strategy" with a small top-management team. He opened the Friday meeting with: *"We should first agree on what we are trying to accomplish. Once that is clear, we can dig into how to get there."*

**Application:** The morning's two-hour discussion of goals was painful. Executives offered broad goals like "grow," "diversify," "increase return on capital." No one could argue with these — and no one could act on them either. The instant someone got specific (say, *enter the pollution-control equipment business*), the "goal" was obviously a powerful decision in disguise about what to do, and the room had no basis for agreeing or disagreeing. Rumelt didn't sleep that night. The next morning he handed out a short note: *good strategic goals are the outcome of strategy, not its input.* The point was not to debate desires; it was to look at the consequential changes already in motion — the price of oil up from $3 to $12 a barrel, the Wankel collapse, the sleepy aircraft-component business — and ask which of these Curtiss-Wright could weather and use. The group began, finally, to talk about what the company was actually good at: high-performance equipment design and construction for difficult environments.

**Result:** Berner did not internalize the lesson. Within a year Curtiss-Wright started a conglomerate-acquisition spree (Cenco, then a proxy battle for Kennecott copper). Over three years its market value sank from $10 billion to $2.9 billion. *Goals proclaimed without a diagnosis are decisions in disguise.* It took two decades for the company to drift back to its original center.

**Situation:** Sendia Products. Top-five-hundred U.S. company. Board indicated growth had not been "up to par" and wanted "to take the company to the next level." New CEO responded with a strategy entitled *On to the Next* and a goal of doubling revenues from $50 to $100 billion in five years. The senior team was gathered around a screen showing a complex waterfall chart for the upcoming board meeting. The CFO, asked the implied market-share assumption for the headline new initiative, said it was 85 percent in five years — starting from zero, against a strong incumbent, in a market where the company had failed once before.

**Application:** *"That doesn't seem reasonable."* They iterated. At 40 percent share, the present value went negative. So they backed it out: 50 percent share gets to a positive present value and we'll just make the second initiative more aggressive to close the gap. *Why did this CEO focus on manipulating numbers on slides rather than a clear-eyed appraisal of technology and competition?* The board had been reshaped over a decade for independence and diversity — every member competent, only one with even a superficial grasp of the company's complex technology. The only language they all shared was financial accounting. So the board pressed for growth projections every quarter, and the CEO produced PowerPoint hogwash. *In their hearts, they knew the slides were hogwash. But the pressure of "On to the Next" had them making up numbers and promises that couldn't be kept. More courage would have helped. It always amazes me when executives taking down millions of dollars a year lack the courage of the average fireman.*

**Result:** In the three years after that meeting, both new initiatives failed. The CEO was terminated. *Stupid arbitrary goals don't motivate achievement. They motivate cynicism and fabrication.*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open a strategy retreat by "agreeing on goals."
**Why:** Universal buy-in usually signals the absence of choice. Broad goals everyone agrees with ("grow," "be excellent," "respect our people") cannot guide a single decision. The instant anyone proposes a *specific* goal, the room realizes a goal is just a powerful decision wearing a hat — and the political fight starts. Better to start with diagnosis. Look at what has changed, at the company's distinctive skills, at the pivot points where strength can be applied. Goals fall out the back end.

**Don't:** Treat a vision statement, mission statement, or values statement as the foundation of strategy.
**Why:** It's turtles all the way down. Mission statements are not enduring (Microsoft has had at least three; the CDC rewrites theirs every few years). They are also not actionable. *Your publicly expressed mission is more advertising and social signaling than guidance.* If you must have one, keep it to a motto. *Just Do It. Semper Fi. There Is No Substitute.* Then put it on the website and get back to the Action Agenda.

**Don't:** Accept "twelve priorities" as a strategy.
**Why:** Priority means first. Twelve firsts is no firsts. The list is a political artifact — every executive got their pet item in. Strategy is concentration of strength; concentration is the opposite of a list. Push ten items into next year. Refuse to call the long list a strategy.

**Don't:** Substitute motivation for analysis.
**Why:** "Reaching for what appears to be the impossible" is fairly standard motivational fare. The slaughtered British troops at Passchendaele in 1917 did not suffer from a lack of motivation. They suffered from a lack of competent strategic leadership. *Motivation is an essential part of life and success, and a leader may justly ask for "one last push," but the leader's job is more than that. The job of the leader is also to create the conditions that will make that push effective, to have a strategy worthy of the effort called upon.*

**Don't:** Hand the goal-setting page back to the CEO and call it strategy work.
**Why:** When the CEO says *our overall strategy is set — we are going to grow at 20 percent at 20 percent margin — what I need is coaching for my top people on strategic thinking*, the right answer is not to coach. It is to say: *I think you have a lot of ambition, but you don't have a strategy. I cannot honestly recommend that as a way forward because business competition is not just a battle of strength and wills; it is also a competition over insights and competencies.* If they don't want to work on the diagnosis, decline the engagement.
