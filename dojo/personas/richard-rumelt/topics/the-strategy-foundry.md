# The Strategy Foundry

## When to Use
- A leadership team has been generating the same vague "strategic plan" — a list of priorities indistinguishable from a budget — for years, and the CEO recognizes that the process is the problem.
- A new CEO inherits a senior team that has been doing strategy as goal-setting, and wants to break the pattern before it ossifies.
- A board has asked the management team to "come back with a real strategy," and there is no existing process for doing that work.
- A genuinely gnarly challenge has surfaced — competition shifting, technology turning, a regulatory change, a dysfunctional structure — and the standard annual planning machinery cannot address it.
- A division-level team needs to do strategy work that genuinely diagnoses the situation rather than translating corporate goals into local promises.

## Fails When
- The senior executive cannot stop dominating the room. If the boss has already decided, the foundry is theater. The facilitator has to be able to treat the boss as just another participant; if the room knows that won't be tolerated, deferred judgment collapses.
- It is bolted onto the budget cycle. A foundry held a month before budgeting decays into a budget exercise — the group will translate everything into quarterly numbers because that is what the next meeting demands. Hold it on a cadence not divisible by three, four, or twelve.
- Too many people. With more than eight or ten participants, hierarchy takes over and people stop saying what they think.
- Participants cannot disconnect — phones, texts, urgent calls, leaving the room — sustained discussion cannot be achieved. Defer the foundry or change the participant list.
- A participant agrees in the room and then bad-mouths the choices to subordinates afterward. This is endemic in public-sector and matrixed organizations. Without the swearing-in, two-faced managers can quietly kill anything decided.
- The group is composed of people who only manage financial goals and have no first-hand grasp of product, market, technology, or competition. There has to be knowledge in the room or the foundry has nothing to work with.

## Core Concept

A Strategy Foundry is a methodology for getting a leadership team to actually do strategy together — meaning: diagnose the challenge, identify the crux, and design coherent action against it — rather than performing a budget-with-aspirations under the name "strategy." It exists because, after years of running solo and small-team consulting engagements, I observed a recurring failure: the analysis was done, recommendations were made, and then the company would convene its annual "strategic plan" meeting and produce a list of eight goals (customer satisfaction, supply-chain excellence, pay down debt, reduce emissions by 15%) untouched by any of it. The strategy work was the warm-up routine; the budget-shaped "strategic plan" was the starring show. Delegating strategy to a VP of strategy, or to a consulting boutique, did not break this pattern. *The crux,* I came to see, *is the general belief that strategy is in the service of preset goals.* The foundry is built to break that belief.

The structure is deliberate and small. **Eight people, three days, no slide decks, no consultants presenting.** The leader of the company or business unit must be in the room, committed to a challenge-based approach, and willing to be one voice among the eight rather than the source of the answer. The session is held off-site, separated from the budget cycle by months, and protected from interruption — the foundry will fail if executives are taking calls and ducking out. The facilitator (sometimes me, sometimes someone internal who can withstand the political kickback) prepares by reading the company's history and past plans, conducting a confidential ninety-minute interview with each participant plus a few key non-participants, and asking each to send back written answers to a standard set of seven questions. None of this material is presented as "consultant findings." Quotes appear on slides without attribution. The participants are doing the thinking; the facilitator is running the structure that lets them do it.

The structure has three movements. **Day one** opens with change — what has happened over the past five years, what may happen in the next three to five — because change is the easiest topic to get a group talking honestly. Then a review of what has worked and what has not in this organization's recent history; this is *attention to history,* and it surfaces the conditions that have actually generated wins or failures here. Then the facilitator hands out the assembled "list of priorities" drawn from the executives' own answers — typically twenty items, vague, all good things to want — and the group sees the absurdity of calling that a strategy. Then the afternoon: identify the *challenges,* one to a five-by-eight-inch card, pinned to a board, with the facilitator probing each: *what makes this important? what makes this difficult?* Discussion of *how to solve them* is deferred. **Day two** brings in the confidential quotes, which surface the disagreements and discomforts the group hadn't been willing to put on the table. Then the hard move: of the ten or twelve challenges, which two or three are most important *and* addressable — important enough that the team would lose their jobs if they didn't make progress, addressable enough that focused effort would actually move it? Then small groups draft action plans for the chosen one or two. Not goals — *actions* with a reasonable chance of being accomplished within eighteen months. **Day three** consolidates the guiding policy and specific actions, makes the key assumptions explicit (so they can be checked later — *strategic navigation*), drafts the public face if needed, and ends with a *swearing-in:* the participants gather in a circle and affirm to each other that these choices are binding until the next foundry, that they will not bad-mouth the decisions afterward, and that they will support each other in carrying them out. The swearing-in is not ceremony. It is the only known cure for the two-faced manager.

## How to Apply

1. **Get the preconditions right or don't run the foundry.** The senior executive must commit to challenge-based strategy and to being one voice among the group, not the answer. The team has to be eight or fewer. They have to be people with first-hand knowledge of product, market, competition, and technology — not just financial managers. They have to be able to disconnect from their phones for three days. The foundry has to be off-site and separated from the budget cycle by months. Without these, the foundry will produce theater. Defer it or change the participant list.

2. **Prepare with confidential interviews and written questions.** Spend ninety minutes one-on-one with each participant before the foundry. Ask them, in writing, the seven questions: changes in the past five years and their impact; expected changes in the next three to five years; programs in the last five years that worked, and why; programs that didn't work, and why; current "priority issues" and the projects aimed at them; the two critical challenges facing the organization (where a critical challenge is *the underlying difficulty,* not a financial shortfall); and structural or policy issues that may block progress on those challenges. The written answers feed the session. The interviews build trust and surface what people will not say in the room.

3. **Day one: change, history, the priority list, then challenges on cards.** Don't start with the challenges. Start with what is happening in the world. Move to what has worked and not worked here. Then hand the group their own combined list of "priorities" — the twenty-item shopping list — and let them see how vague and goal-laden it is. Then, after lunch, surface the *challenges* and put each on a card. Probe each: *what makes this important? what makes this difficult?* Defer all discussion of solutions.

4. **Day two: surface the uncomfortable interview material, then narrow to two or three.** The confidential quotes from the interviews — without attribution — are the device that brings unsayable views into the room. Use them to drive a real diagnosis. Then make the hard move: of the challenges on the board, which two or three are critically important *and* addressable in eighteen months? Take the rest off the table physically. Break into small groups. Each group designs an action plan for one of the chosen challenges. Not goals. *Actions.*

5. **Day three: consolidate, make assumptions explicit, draft the public face, swear in.** Lock the guiding policy and the specific actions for the next eighteen months. Write down the key assumptions explicitly — these are what *strategic navigation* will check against in the next foundry. Decide what, if anything, needs a public-face statement (often less than executives expect). Then gather the group in a circle and conduct the swearing-in: each member affirms that these choices are binding until the next foundry, that they will not undermine or disparage them, and that they will support each other in carrying them out. *Sometimes managers are of two minds about the new direction. The leadership group must have the discipline to act as a coherent whole, at least until the next Strategy Foundry.*

## Examples

**Situation:** FarmKor, an agricultural-tech company with hardware and software for monitoring farms, had pioneered the field but its margins were eroding. Revenue per acre had fallen 34% over a decade. The annual "strategic plan" was a list of eight goals (customer satisfaction, supply-chain excellence, branding image positioning, etc.). The CEO, after hearing a talk on challenge-based strategy, agreed to try a foundry instead.

**Application:** Eight participants, three days. Confidential interviews surfaced what the official plan had buried — the regional baronies hated the corporate harmonization push; the CFO was preoccupied with the P/E ratio; one engineer broke down crying in his office because he had watched whole systems being eliminated. The day-one priority list ran to twenty vague items. The afternoon produced ten challenges on cards: diminished advantage, big players' R&D budgets, founders' interests in the boardroom, falling revenue per acre, too many parts, precision farming as a new wave, regional baronies, less talent per acre, nutrient substitutes, high-tech start-ups. Day two narrowed to three: diminished advantage, regional baronies, falling revenue per acre. Two small groups designed actions. The crux that emerged: FarmKor had expanded into low-value crops where it had to compete with the global majors, *while losing the differentiation it had pioneered.* The guiding policy: refocus on high-value crops — orchards, vines, gourmet mushrooms, saffron — where customers can afford specialized sensors and tailored nutrients. Specific actions: identify two lead-collaborator customers, build a special team for orchard/vineyard tech, eighteen-month horizon. Day three: the CEO surprised the room by announcing she had been in quiet board discussions to sell off one of the regional divisions — which would raise cash and signal seriousness about regional discipline. Swearing-in: the eight members pressed fists together in the center of the room.

**Result:** A guiding policy and a small set of coherent actions, owned by the people who would have to implement them, with the political cover to actually do it. Compare to the OKCo case in the same period: a similar climate-control company where I and the VP of Strategy did the analysis, found that the product line was technologically obsolete, and recommended a microprocessor-based platform with a heavyweight cross-functional team. The CEO and CFO presented the "strategic plan" in the fall: eight priorities, projected EBITDA, no mention of the platform recommendation, no mention of the technology shift, just "pay down debt and cozy up to key customers." Three years later OKCo was sold; the top three retired with payouts; half the employees were let go. *The problem at OKCo was that the key challenge, already apparent to many, was not owned by the major policy makers. If a challenge is not owned, it cannot be surmounted.* That is what the foundry exists to fix — to put the senior team in a room, with the challenge on a card in front of them, and to give them no place to hide from owning it.

**Situation:** Donald Rumsfeld, in 2004, asked me a question that lodged in my head for years. We were discussing how the Defense Department managed shifts in spending. As an aside, I asked him about strategy creation. He said: as defense secretary, he had access to any expertise imaginable — tribal histories, weather patterns, internal politics of any country. "The real problem is pulling all of this expertise together into a coherent strategy. Each morsel of expertise came with an agenda attached. It came from a person or group with a perspective, an ax to grind, a budget to manage, a contract to renew, a career to push forward. Professor — have you academics found a way to deal with this issue?"

**Application:** I had to admit our technology hadn't much improved from ancient times: *put a small group of smart people in a room and see what they come up with.* The honesty of his question forced me to confront how little I — or the field — actually knew about how a group should combine information to create strategy. Rumsfeld's question reignited a years-long effort that became the Strategy Foundry. The answer wasn't a new technique. The answer was *structure:* a small group, off-site, three days, the boss in the room as a peer, the challenge in front of them on cards, the action deferred until after diagnosis, the assumptions written down, and the group swearing to act as a coherent whole.

**Result:** The foundry doesn't promise brilliance. It promises that the leadership team will have actually done the work — diagnosed, identified the crux, designed action against it, and committed publicly to one another. That is not a small thing. Most "strategy" failures are failures of process before they are failures of insight, and the foundry is the only process I know that consistently produces the conditions under which insight and ownership can both occur.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run a foundry tied to the budget cycle.
**Why:** If the foundry is held a month before budgeting, the group will reflexively translate everything into quarterly financial targets, because that is what the next meeting demands of them. The whole point of the foundry is to break the conflation of strategy with budgeting. Hold it on a cadence not divisible by three, four, or twelve — every eleven months, every thirty-one months — to enforce the discipline that *this is not a financial forecasting exercise.*

**Don't:** Run the foundry with the boss as authority figure.
**Why:** If the senior executive cannot help dominating the discussion, the foundry will fail. The room will defer, the disagreements won't surface, and the output will be an articulate version of what the boss already thought. The facilitator has to be able to treat the boss as just another participant — which is one of the strongest arguments for an outside facilitator who has nothing to lose. *If the leader behaves as if he or she has the answer to all important questions, the foundry will not work.*

**Don't:** Skip the swearing-in because it feels theatrical.
**Why:** The most common failure mode after a foundry is the two-faced manager who agreed in the room and now bad-mouths the decisions to subordinates. This is endemic, especially in public-sector and matrixed organizations where senior executives inherit the personnel and rarely hold office long enough to form a cohesive group. *The basic cure is to not permit two-faced managers. Executives who say one thing to the boss and another to subordinates should be sent back to junior high school.* The swearing-in is the only mechanism that puts the group on record to one another, and the only one that gives the leader cover to discipline a defector. Skip it and you've made all the previous work optional.

**Don't:** Let the group end with a list of ten "priorities."
**Why:** *The word "priority" means superiority in rank or privilege.* When you assign too many priorities, the concept loses its meaning. The output of the foundry is two or three coherent actions against the chosen challenge over an eighteen-month horizon — not a laundry list. If the group cannot get below ten, the facilitator's job is to physically take cards off the table, including by saying to the leader: *this is where you earn your pay.* The leader picks. If the leader cannot pick, the foundry has not worked.

**Don't:** Mistake the foundry for a framework to apply on top of the existing process.
**Why:** It is not a tool layered on the standard planning machinery. It is a *replacement* for a particular part of that machinery — the part where strategy gets confused with budgeting and goal-setting. Bolted on top, it will be absorbed and neutralized. Run separately, in time and in tone, it has a chance of producing actual strategy.
