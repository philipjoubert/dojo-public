# Proximate Objectives — Feasibility-Bounded Aims That Drive Action

## When to Use
- Someone hands you a "strategy" whose central object is a financial target, a market-share aspiration, or a ten-year vision, and asks how to execute against it.
- An organization is paralyzed by ambiguity — too many unknowns, no defensible path forward.
- A leader's goal is the right *kind* of thing but at the wrong distance — too far away, too abstract, too dependent on factors no one in the room controls.
- You are decomposing a high-level aim into something subordinates can actually take responsibility for.

## Fails When
- Mistaken for an OKR or KPI exercise. Proximate objectives sit upstream of operational targets; they shape what the targets are *about*. Treating them as just-another-quarterly-goal collapses the concept.
- Used to manufacture false feasibility. If the engineers genuinely don't know what the lunar surface is like, picking the easy assumption to "drive action" is dangerous; you have to actually believe the assumption is reasonable, and accept that if you're wrong, the whole program is in trouble.
- Applied to motivational messaging. A proximate objective is not a slogan to inspire the troops. It is an operating decision that resolves enough ambiguity that real engineering work can begin.

## Core Concept

One of a leader's most powerful tools is the creation of a good proximate objective — one that is close enough at hand to be feasible. A proximate objective names a target the organization can reasonably be expected to hit, even overwhelm. It is not the same thing as the long-range ambition. It is the next problem worth solving — close enough that it is solvable, important enough that solving it materially changes the situation, and concrete enough that it tells the organization what to do tomorrow morning.

Most writers on strategy seem to suggest that the more dynamic the situation, the farther ahead a leader must look. This is illogical. The more dynamic the situation, the *poorer* your foresight will be. Therefore, the more uncertain and dynamic the situation, the more *proximate* a strategic objective must be. The proximate objective is guided by some judgment about the future, but its essential logic is taking a strong position and creating options — not looking far ahead. It is the chess-master move that improves position rather than the move that aims at checkmate.

There is a hierarchy here. High-level proximate objectives create goals for lower-level units, which set their own proximate objectives in turn, in a cascade of problem-solving at finer and finer levels of detail. They also cascade in time: this year's proximate objective, once accomplished, opens the door to a more sophisticated objective next year that would have been infeasible before. PJ the helicopter pilot can land on a pitching deck in heavy weather only because flying itself has long ago become automatic; he cannot concentrate on the crisis if flying isn't automatic. The same applies to organizations. A small startup's proximate objective is coordinating engineering, marketing, and distribution. Asking it to "concentrate on opening offices in Europe" is pointless until the basics of flying the business are mastered. What is proximate for one company may be wildly out of reach for another, and the burden is on the leader to read the rung the organization is standing on.

The leader's deepest responsibility is to absorb a large part of the situation's ambiguity and pass on to the organization a simpler problem — one that is solvable. Many leaders fail badly at this. They announce ambitious goals without resolving any of the ambiguity about the specific obstacles to be overcome. To take responsibility is more than a willingness to accept blame. It is setting proximate objectives and handing the organization a problem it can actually solve.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the long-range aim, then set it aside.** "Beat the Russians in space," "be a top business school in the region," "reduce drug use." These are ambitions, not strategies. Acknowledge them so the organization knows the direction, then refuse to operate on them directly.
2. **Ask what is feasible from where we stand.** Given current capabilities, technology, and resources, what is the most consequential thing this organization can plausibly accomplish in the next horizon? "Plausible" is doing real work here — it has to be a problem someone in the room knows how to attack, even if it will take effort and time.
3. **Force the objective close enough that it can be hit.** If the answer is "we'd need a 10x performance jump," that's the leverage point — von Braun's memo to LBJ noted that the moon landing required a 10x rocket performance leap, which the Soviets did not have in their pipeline either, which is exactly why it was a strategic objective and not a fantasy. If no plausible feasible objective exists, the right answer may be that this isn't where to fight.
4. **Absorb the ambiguity.** Where the situation is genuinely uncertain, write the spec anyway and pass downstream a problem that *is* solvable. The cost of being wrong is real — but the cost of leaving every subordinate paralyzed by full ambiguity is usually higher. Be honest about what you've assumed.
5. **Cascade.** A proximate objective at the top sets up the next-level proximate objective for each unit, which sets up the next, and so on, in a structure of problem-solving at increasing detail. Don't try to do this all at the top. Each level absorbs ambiguity for the level below.

## Examples

**Situation:** Kennedy in 1961 facing the question "How can we beat the Russians in space?" The Soviet Union had a big lead in heavy-lift rockets, which meant they would beat the United States to a manned orbiting laboratory, an unmanned moon vehicle, or any of several other near-term space spectaculars.
**Application:** Werner von Braun wrote LBJ a memo arguing that the moon landing required a performance jump by a factor of 10 over current Soviet rockets — a jump neither side had — which gave the U.S., with its larger resource base, "an excellent chance" of getting there first. Kennedy chose the moon, not because it was audacious, but because it was *proximate*. The technology was largely understood from the ballistic missile program; the steps along the way (unmanned exploration, larger boosters, parallel liquid and solid fuel, landing vehicle) were laid out explicitly in the speech. The objective was feasible — engineers knew how to design and build rockets and spacecraft. It was just a matter of marshaling resources and political will.
**Result:** The mission was treated by everyone afterward as an act of vision, but it was actually a carefully chosen feasibility-bounded operating objective. Note the contrast with the "War on Drugs" or "Energy Independence" — desirable, but not feasible in the present legal and resource framework, and consequently producing decades of effort with little to show.

**Situation:** NASA's Surveyor program, early 1960s. The vehicle had to soft-land on the moon. The problem was that nobody knew what the lunar surface was like — there were three or four competing scientific theories. Engineers couldn't defend any one design against someone else's story about the possible horrors of the lunar surface. The project was stuck.
**Application:** Phyllis Buwalda, who directed Future Mission Studies at JPL, wrote a specification for a lunar surface that looked a lot like the southwestern desert of the United States — hard and grainy, slopes no more than 15 degrees, scattered small stones, and boulders no larger than two feet across. Pressed on whether she actually knew the moon was like this, she said: "Look, the engineers can't work without a specification. If it turns out to be a lot more difficult than this, we aren't going to be spending much time on the moon anyway." The specification wasn't the truth — the truth was that nobody knew. It was a strategically chosen proximate objective that absorbed most of the ambiguity and passed the engineers a problem that was hard but solvable.
**Result:** Five Surveyors landed successfully in 1966–67; *Surveyor 3* sat in the Ocean of Storms two years before *Apollo 12* landed two hundred yards away and Pete Conrad walked over and took its picture. The PhD thesis lurking behind Buwalda's call is the options analysis — if the lunar surface wouldn't support a straightforward lander, the program had a far bigger problem than design, and the right answer was to know that early.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** confuse a proximate objective with a financial goal.
**Why:** "Grow revenue 20% next year" is an ambition stated as a number. It is not feasibility-bounded — it does not name what the organization will *do*, only what it would like to be true at the end of the year. A real proximate objective answers "what's the next problem we're going to solve, and how?" Numbers fall out of the answer; they are not the answer.

**Don't:** set proximate objectives at multiple levels that contradict each other.
**Why:** If the corporate proximate objective is "consolidate the platform" and the division's is "launch three new product lines," the cascade is broken. Subordinates are absorbing different assumptions about the situation. The point of cascading is that each level absorbs ambiguity for the one below; if the levels don't reason from each other, you've just created parallel kingdoms with strategy-flavored slogans.

**Don't:** pretend an infeasible ambition is "proximate" by squinting.
**Why:** "Eliminate poverty by 2030" or "Win the war on drugs" are not proximate objectives because no one in the room knows how to do them with the available legal and resource framework. Calling them proximate produces decades of effort, raised street prices, displaced small-time smugglers, and no movement on the underlying aim. If the objective isn't actually feasible, the honest move is to defer it and find one that is. Picking the right rung of the ladder is the work.

**Don't:** treat "stretch goals" as proximate objectives.
**Why:** A stretch goal is a motivational device — it is meant to push effort beyond what is comfortable. A proximate objective is a structural device — it is meant to absorb ambiguity and produce a problem the organization can actually attack. They can look similar in PowerPoint and behave very differently in practice. A stretch goal that is wildly infeasible produces cynicism and fabrication; a proximate objective that is wildly infeasible produces the same, plus a wasted year.
