# Don't Confuse Strategy with Management

## When to Use
- A leader brings you a Balanced Scorecard, an OKR tree, or a KPI dashboard and asks for help "tying it to strategy."
- "Driving results" is being used as a synonym for "strategy."
- Senior management's strategy work has collapsed into the 90-Day Derby — guidance, consensus estimates, hitting the number.
- The "strategy" is *we will be the leading global X* or *to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more*.
- Someone says they have a clear ten-year vision and wants you to help operationalize it.

## Fails When
- The user is genuinely managing an already-strategized business and needs a scorecard to keep the trains running on time. KPIs and dashboards are vital — they're just not strategy.
- The numerical target is the right way to track an objective that *fell out of* a real diagnosis. That is not the imposter you're hunting.

## Core Concept

Management work and strategy work are two different things. *A collection of goals or metrics is not a strategy. A strategy is a reasoned argument about the forces at work in a situation and how to deal with them. Don't let the metrics drown out thought.* Motivating and measuring performance is the vital heartbeat of organizations that "keeps the trains running on time." You cannot improve most operations without measuring efficiency. But measuring efficiency is not the same as deciding *which trains to run*. Confuse the two and you get McNamara in Vietnam, counting bodies, while losing the war. *Driving results is management work, not strategy work.*

The most common imposters are easy to name: KPIs. OKRs. Balanced Scorecards. Management by Objectives. Quarterly earnings guidance. Vision and mission statements. Each of these has a real management job to do — *and* each is routinely smuggled into the strategy retreat as a substitute for actually thinking about competition, change, and organizational capacity. The reason is the same in every case: they are easier than strategy. They feel rigorous. They produce documents. They generate meetings. They do not require anyone to diagnose what is actually going on or to make a painful choice.

Vision-as-strategy is the same imposter in a different costume. *If you have a clear vision of the future ten years hence, you're a psychotic. It's a hallucination, not a vision.* The world is too uncertain for ten-year visions to be anything but wishful thinking. *Good strategy is more like surfing a wave than having a clear vision of the future.* You find a wave of change, you ride it with skill. The same goes for "we will be the most profitable and respected science-driven chemical company in the world" — it is not a strategy, because it does not say *how*, it does not name a challenge, and it does not commit the company to any specific action. It is a piece of public-facing decoration. Useful for the website, perhaps. Useless for guiding action.

Strategy work *defines* the goals and objectives to be sought. Good strategy work begins with recognizing a challenge and understanding the difficulties in overcoming it. It produces policies, actions, and objectives. *Then* — and only then — management work picks them up and drives them home. Both are needed. Don't merge them. Don't let one masquerade as the other.

## How to Apply

1. **When a scorecard arrives, ask what challenge it was built to address.** If the answer is "to drive results" or "to align the organization," the scorecard is doing management work. Useful, but it is not the strategy. The strategy is the upstream argument about *which* results matter, against *which* obstacles.
2. **Translate every vision-as-strategy claim into the question it dodges.** "We will be the leading global X" → *what is the central challenge that stands between you and being that, and what are you going to do about it?* If they cannot answer, the vision is a hallucination wearing a strategy hat.
3. **Refuse the ten-year vision request.** Replace it with: *what wave of change are you riding, and where is the pivot point you can apply force to?* Good strategy is finding a wave and riding it with skill, not predicting the future and committing to a fixed plan against it.
4. **Distinguish driving results from strategy.** When asked to "design a session on driving results," demur. Suggest someone else. The work of strategy is not motivating people to hit harder targets — it is figuring out which targets are worth hitting.
5. **Quiet the 90-Day Derby.** When current quarterly earnings are being treated as the strategy, point out that current earnings are the harvest of investments and decisions made by generations long gone. Today's profits do not equal today's strategy. Cutting R&D to bounce a quarter (Zotich), buying a competitor for $175 million instead of building the technology in-house for $20 million to avoid hurting earnings (Softways) — these are wasteful decisions induced by mistaking financial results for strategy.
6. **Keep both — don't merge them.** Tell the leader: you need both a strategy and a management system. Don't let one masquerade as the other. The Balanced Scorecard is a fine tool *for managing* a strategy. It does not produce one.

## Examples

**Situation:** Robert McNamara, Harvard Business School, November 1966. Secretary of Defense, mid-Vietnam ramp-up. Speaking to a small crowd outside Baker Library: *"As a technology, management has rapidly developed in the last thirty years. We now know how to manage anything — the Ford Motor Company, the Catholic Church, or the Department of Defense. You break the overall objective into parts which can be measured. You put someone in charge of each part and measure their progress, holding them accountable for results."* At that moment, McNamara was supporting General Westmoreland in a goal of killing more Vietcong and North Vietnamese than they could replace.

**Application:** Rumelt's reading: McNamara was a brilliant manager. *Management by measuring progress didn't work. The "progress" led into a trap — a war of attrition and combat of wills.* The North could and did replace troops faster than the U.S. could kill them; the body counts kept ticking up; the war was being lost. Writing in 1995, McNamara himself: *"I clearly erred by not forcing — then or later, in either Saigon or Washington — a knock-down, drag-out debate over the loose assumptions, unasked questions, and thin analyses underlying our military strategy in Vietnam.… I doubt I will ever fully understand why I did not do so."* Clark Clifford, who replaced him: *"In reforming the Pentagon, his talents had served him well, but… Vietnam was not a management problem, it was a war."*

**Result:** A scar on American society that lives on. *McNamara's dilemma in Vietnam is a vivid illustration that management work and strategy work are two different things.* No scorecard can substitute for a diagnosis when the challenge is competition, change, or organizational dysfunction.

**Situation:** DelKha, 2010. CEO Felicia Kha called Rumelt for help "tying strategy to the Balanced Scorecard." A nicely printed Scorecard hung on her office wall — Mission ("To be the most successful computer-components company in the world, delivering the best customer experience"), Goals, KPIs across Financial / Customer / Internal / Innovation. The system had kept the company on track for years. Her difficulty: declining prices, slowing unit sales, PCs topping out, components business under existential pressure as laptops and pads took over.

**Application:** Rumelt's reading: *the Scorecard, together with the more detailed operating information and budgets, had kept the company on track. It was a reasonable system of management, but what Felicia needed in 2010 was strategy work, and statements of mission and basic goals were not helpful.* He formed a strategy working group of five and refused to start from the Scorecard. Instead they looked at what was changing in customers' worlds, what skills and knowledge DelKha had that could travel. After some false starts (supply-chain advisory, drone fans for the soon-bankrupt FlyKo), the team noticed that DelKha's brushless-fan capability — the cooling fans for PC cases — could be applied to a wholly different industry: cordless tools, robotics, R/C model cars. Felicia bought FlyKo's radio-and-electronics assets at fire-sale prices for $100,000.

**Result:** By 2014 DelKha was an established player in the high-performance brushless-motor industry. Stock had quintupled. Employee count had quadrupled. *In DelKha's strategy quest, the scorecard had not been helpful. Its use is in managing a business. It is not much help when the challenge is not a failure of efficiency in current operations.* The 2019 Scorecard was completely different — built around innovative engineering and motor-performance parameters. Both tools matter; don't confuse one with the other.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Frame strategy as "execution."
**Why:** *"Strategy is important, but it's really about execution"* — that's silly. That's like saying we have a military strategy but our soldiers are too fat to walk. Execution is part of strategy. Strategy is about what is important and the challenges you face. *If one of these challenges is that the organization is dysfunctional, then that's strategic.* Don't let the execution mantra excuse the absence of a diagnosis.

**Don't:** Treat KPIs and dashboards as the unit of strategic thinking.
**Why:** Dean Foods built a KPI system covering volume, revenue, sales discounts, expenses, cost elements, and customer margins by district. It rolled up forty to sixty local milk processors into a "national" footprint. *Most of the costs in the dairy system are related to feed prices and herd size. Pressing the existing system for efficiency was not going to solve these fundamental challenges.* Dean filed for bankruptcy in 2019. *Measuring something doesn't always mean it can be improved.*

**Don't:** Build a strategy from a vision statement.
**Why:** It's a hallucination, not a vision. Microsoft's 1990 mission was *a computer on every desk and in every home.* Clear, but useless once the Internet, smartphones, and the cloud arrived. The 2013 update was an undecipherable pile of words. The current version — *to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more* — is enduring precisely because it doesn't say anything about what Microsoft does. Mission statements are advertising and social signaling, not guidance. Keep it to a motto and move on.

**Don't:** Optimize the company for the 90-Day Derby.
**Why:** Current earnings are the harvest of decisions made by people long gone. Cutting R&D to bounce next quarter while competitors ramp theirs up is *strategically disastrous*. The CEO who paid $175 million for a competitor's technology because building it for $20 million would hurt earnings made the company worse. Tell the board: stock prices are noisy, uncertain estimates of true value. Build a clientele of investors, not speculators. *Tell them that to build long-term value, there will be experiments that don't work. Tell them that if they see very smooth corporate performance, someone is either fudging the numbers or milking the cow.*

**Don't:** Run a strategy retreat as success theater.
**Why:** When everyone in the room is performing optimism for the board, the deck, or the press, the strategy work is dead. *I worked with a government group that spanned 12 different agencies and they had a strategy that had 22 "priorities." They insisted they could achieve them. They worried that if they said "we have a problem," Congress might not give them money — so they had ambition theater focused on "We can do it!"* Strategy starts with admitting it's difficult. If the room can't say *this is hard*, the room cannot do strategy.

**Don't:** Conflate "strategy" with "management" because both are senior-leadership work.
**Why:** They share leaders. They do not share content. Strategy decides which trains to run; management makes the trains run on time. *Driving results in the absence of a clear strategy is putting the cart before the horse.* You need both. Don't merge them.
