# Coherent Action

## When to Use
- When you are designing the action layer of a strategy and need to test that the actions reinforce one another.
- When a "strategy" is actually a list of unrelated initiatives (close a plant, spend more on advertising, run 360-degree feedback) — coherent action is the test that exposes it.
- When an organization is pursuing multiple deep transformations simultaneously and getting nowhere on any of them.

## Fails When
- The organization is so large and resource-rich that *minimum* coherence (no direct contradictions) is the realistic standard — Petzl-style tight design only works for narrowly-focused outfits. The whole US Navy cannot be SEAL teams. Match the level of coherence to the kind of organization you have.
- You confuse coherence with comprehensiveness. A coherent strategy says no to most things. A "strategy" that has something for every interest group is the *opposite* of coherent — that is the space-shuttle pattern.

## Core Concept

Strategy is about action. Many people call the guiding policy "the strategy" and stop there. That is a mistake. The kernel of a strategy must contain action, and the actions within the kernel should be **coherent** — the resource deployments, policies, and maneuvers should be consistent and coordinated, building on one another rather than canceling each other out. *The coordination of action provides the most basic source of leverage or advantage available in strategy.*

Coherence is not "everyone gets along." It is fit — the engineering of fit among parts, specifying how actions and resources will be combined to attack the diagnosis. That fit is *imposed* on a system; it would not occur without the hand of strategy. It is unnatural, painful, and political, because most organizations naturally drift toward a portfolio of locally-rational compromises that please everyone and add up to nothing. The leader's job is to absorb that ambiguity and force the choice the organization is unwilling to make on its own. *Strategy is at least as much about what an organization does not do as it is about what it does.*

The opposite of coherent action has two flavors. The first is **direct conflict** — actions that cancel each other out. Ford under Jacques Nasser bought Volvo and Jaguar to "build brand," then put them on a common platform to "preserve economies of scale" — diluting the brand equity of both marques and annoying the most passionate customers, dealers, and service shops. Volvo buyers don't want a "safe Jaguar"; Jaguar buyers don't want a "sporty Volvo." The actions were in conflict, not coherent. The second flavor is the **dog's-dinner / list-of-stuff** pattern — actions that don't directly contradict each other but don't add up to anything either. A 2003 client's "strategy" was: (1) close a plant in Akron and open one in Mexico, (2) spend more on advertising, (3) initiate a 360-degree feedback program. These may all be sound operational management. They are not a strategy. Strategy coordinates action to address a specific challenge. It is not defined by the pay grade of the person authorizing the action.

The cost of coherence is **focus and trade-off**. Coherence is hard precisely because focusing on one thing slights another, and the slighted parties have voices and budgets. *The cost of coherence is saying no to many interests with reasonable values and arguments.* A strategist tries not to be a politician. The art of compromise — building the big tent everyone can shelter under — is not the strategist's art; that is the politician's. The politician arrives after the strategist's victory, sharing the gains.

## How to Apply

1. **Anchor the actions in the diagnosis and guiding policy.** Each action should be doing work against the named challenge. If you can swap an action for a different one without changing the diagnosis or the policy, that action is operationally chosen, not strategically chosen.
2. **Test for direct conflict.** Walk through each pair of actions and ask: do these cancel each other out? Cut R&D to make the numbers, while basing the competitive edge on continuing development? Outsource software while claiming data wizardry as your edge? Shut a warehouse to cut cost while marketing on quick delivery? These are violations of *minimum coherence* — the floor below which you don't have a strategy at all.
3. **Test for reinforcement.** Do the actions build on each other? Wal-Mart's bar-code scanners, integrated logistics, frequent just-in-time deliveries, large stores in small towns, and central regional management were each shaped and specialized to the others — pieces not interchangeable, the whole an integrated design.
4. **Refuse to do two deep transformations on the core at once.** DEC's "do Boxes and Chips and Solutions" was not a compromise; it was a guarantee of no transformation at all. Each genuine transformation requires wholly new skills, work practices, and resource commitments. The unwillingness to choose is the most common pathway to bad strategy. "Do all three" is what it sounds like.
5. **Specify a proximate objective where useful.** A clear, feasible state-of-affairs near at hand can do enormous work coordinating action. It gives the various functions a single target to fit around without requiring continuous central direction.
6. **Match coherence intensity to organization shape.** Petzl-tight coherence is for narrow-focus organizations (Petzl, Southwest's original strategy, IKEA, Enterprise Rent-a-Car, Ryanair, Progressive, Netflix's original DVD-by-mail). Larger, more complex organizations cannot have that degree of fit; they compensate with depth of resources but must at least meet *minimum coherence* — actions that do not directly conflict.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple, 1997 — Steve Jobs returns to a company two months from bankruptcy with fifteen desktop models, multiple portable lines, printers, peripherals, six national retailers, and full in-house manufacturing.
**Application:** The actions were ferociously coherent around a single guiding policy — *shrink Apple to a niche-producer scale and scope that could survive*. Cut fifteen desktops to one. Cut all portables to one laptop. Cut printers and peripherals entirely. Cut development engineers and software development. Drop five of six national retailers. Move manufacturing offshore to Taiwan. With a simpler product line manufactured in Asia, cut inventory by more than 80 percent. Open a Web store to sell direct, cutting out distributors. Each action enabled the others — the simpler product line made offshore manufacturing feasible, which made the inventory cut feasible, which made the cash recovery feasible. Get Microsoft to invest $150 million by exploiting Bill Gates's concerns about a failed Apple worsening Microsoft's antitrust troubles. *He did not announce ambitious revenue or profit goals; he did not indulge in messianic visions of the future. And he did not just cut in a blind ax-wielding frenzy — he redesigned the whole business logic around a simplified product line sold through a limited set of outlets.*
**Result:** Apple survived, then thrived. The coherent return is the textbook case — the actions did not just avoid contradicting each other, they actively reinforced each other. Each cut made the next cut possible.

**Situation:** Wal-Mart, mid-1980s. A class of MBA students lists the apparent reasons for Wal-Mart's success: big stores in small towns, everyday low prices, computerized warehousing and trucking, non-union workforce, low administrative expenses. If those are the reasons, why did Wal-Mart run rampant over Kmart for the next decade? Why didn't anyone copy the formula?
**Application:** Walton's policies were not a list — they were an integrated design, each piece shaped and specialized to the others. The bar-code scanners, integrated logistics, frequent just-in-time deliveries, large stores with low inventory — they fit. *The unit of management was not the store; it was the network of 150 stores.* A regional network served a population of millions. The store could not survive alone — it needed the data flows, the management circuits flying out of Bentonville Monday and back Thursday, the regional distribution center cross-docking to outbound trucks. Walton broke not only the conventional wisdom that "a full-line discount store needs a population base of at least 100,000" but the deeper conventional wisdom of decentralization — the doctrine that each kettle should sit on its own bottom. Kmart kept giving each store manager authority over product lines, vendors, and prices; Wal-Mart concentrated those choices at the network level so the integrated logistics and shared learning could actually pay off.
**Result:** Competitors copying elements of the strategy piecemeal got nothing. The whole design had to be adopted, not parts of it. Kmart, the original leader in low-cost variety retailing, spent the 1970s and 1980s expanding internationally and ignoring Wal-Mart's logistics innovations. It filed for bankruptcy in 2002. Coherence was the moat.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** present a "strategy" that is a list of items requiring top-management approval.
**Why:** A list of approvals is not coherent action; it is a budget. *A strategy coordinates action to address a specific challenge. It is not defined by the pay grade of the person authorizing the action.* If your "strategic initiatives" could be done in any order, by any combination, by different teams, with no expected reinforcement among them — you have an operating plan, not a strategy.

**Don't:** try to do two deep transformations on the core at once.
**Why:** DEC tried "Boxes and Chips and Solutions" simultaneously. Microsoft, in the years after the iPhone, tried to fix mobile, break into web search, *and* deliver Bill Gates's database-oriented Windows redesign — Vista — at once. The tragic result of putting the A-team on Longhorn was a widely-disliked Vista that delivered none of the promised advances, plus a never-mastered mobile strategy and a Bing search engine that didn't turn profitable until 2016. Each genuine transformation requires wholly new skills, work practices, and resource commitments; you cannot perform two of them on a company's core at once.

**Don't:** confuse a long list of admirable goals with a coherent strategy.
**Why:** The UN's seventeen Sustainable Development Goals are admirable aspirations, but they are not coherent — many directly conflict (energy for all vs. climate change; food production vs. ecosystem preservation; ending poverty without using fossil fuels under current technological constraints). Having seventeen inconsistent goals is the indulgence of politicians. A strategist would face such an exuberance of inconsistent ambition by selecting a consistent subset and pushing the rest aside, at least for a while.

**Don't:** design by committee, where every interest group gets a piece.
**Why:** That is the space-shuttle pattern. NASA wanted post-moon exploration; the Air Force wanted a flying space plane with wings (hence the thirty-five thousand thermal tiles); Congress wanted low cost-per-pound; the Office of Management and Budget overruled NASA on engine design and forced "lower-cost" solid boosters. The result was a design with something for every interest group and economic incoherence at its core: a system supposed to economize on cost-per-launch that had to keep humans alive on ascent, in orbit, through reentry, and on landing — a huge premium for routine satellite launches. Two of five shuttles destroyed, fourteen astronauts killed, $28,000 per pound to orbit instead of the promised $100. *The cost of coherence is saying no to many interests with reasonable values and arguments.* A strategist tries not to be a politician.

**Don't:** mistake mutual non-interference for coherence.
**Why:** Three actions that don't conflict are not yet coherent. Coherence is when actions *reinforce* — the bar-code scanners only pay off because of the integrated logistics, which only pay off because of the network model, which only pays off because the stores are positioned to share regional demand. Non-interference is the *floor* (minimum coherence). Reinforcement is the *win*. Aim for reinforcement; settle for non-interference only when the organization's scale forces you to.
