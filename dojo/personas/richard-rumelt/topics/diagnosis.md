# Diagnosis — The First Move

## When to Use
- Before any recommendation, any action, any guiding policy. Diagnosis is the first move — full stop.
- When the "problem" you've been handed is a result (lost share, missed quarter, declining margin) rather than a cause.
- When everyone in the room has jumped to solutions without anyone having yet said, in a sentence, what is actually going on.

## Fails When
- You let diagnosis become a permanent state — endless data-gathering as a way to dodge action. A diagnosis is good enough when it gives you leverage over outcomes; perfect explanation is not the goal.
- You confuse a *framework* (Five Forces, Blue Ocean, BCG matrix, SWOT) with a *diagnosis*. A framework calls attention to what might be important. A diagnosis is your judgment about what *is* important in this specific situation. The framework cannot do the work for you.

## Core Concept

A great deal of strategy work is trying to figure out what is going on. Not just deciding what to do, but the more fundamental problem of comprehending the situation. The single question I ask of every case is: *what is going on here?* That is diagnosis. It comes first, and almost everything that gets called "strategy" never starts because nobody ever did the work of comprehending the situation.

A diagnosis names or classifies the situation, linking facts into patterns. It replaces the overwhelming complexity of reality with a simpler story — one that calls attention to certain crucial aspects and lets you set others aside. A good diagnosis can transform your view of the situation, bringing a radically different perspective to bear; when it classifies the situation as a certain type, it opens access to knowledge about how analogous situations were handled in the past. And, crucially, an explicit diagnosis is what makes it possible to evaluate the rest of the strategy. Without a diagnosis, you cannot judge any proposed guiding policy, much less anyone else's. The strategy is built on sand.

A strategic diagnosis is not the same as a social-scientific one. The social scientist seeks the diagnosis that best *predicts* outcomes; good strategy seeks the diagnosis that gives you *leverage* over outcomes. K-12 student performance is better explained by social class and culture than by spending or class size — but that knowledge does not lead to many useful policy prescriptions. A diagnosis like Bill Ouchi's, which framed school performance as one of *organization* — decentralized schools perform better — may explain less of the variation, but it points at something a policy maker can actually move. The right diagnosis is the one that opens a domain of action.

The three workhorse moves of diagnosis are **reframing**, **analogy**, and **comparison**. Reframing: change the point of view. If you've been looking at the company as a portfolio of products, look at it as a network of stores; if you've been looking at the situation as a competitive problem, look at it as an organizational one. Analogy: ask what this situation is *like* — what other situation, in another industry, country, or century, has the same shape, and how was that one handled? But analogy is also the largest source of bad diagnoses — a *false* analogy will drive you straight off a cliff with high confidence (Ballmer thinking smartphones would evolve like PCs is the canonical example). Comparison: how does this company's costs / margins / cycle times / customer-satisfaction scores stack up against competitors, against international peers, against its own history? Sharp comparisons are how you see asymmetries you could not have invented from inside the building.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse the goal-first framing.** When someone walks in with "we need to grow at 15%" or "we want to be the leading X," that is not a strategy question — it's an evasion of one. Ask where the goal came from and what challenge stands between them and it.
2. **Ask "what is going on here?" — and keep asking.** Don't accept the first frame. Try a second. Try a third. Each frame highlights some issues and dims others. Being clearheaded means being aware of which frame you're using and what it makes invisible.
3. **Reach for analogy — and test it.** What other situation has this shape? Look across industries, across countries, across eras. Once you have an analogy, attack it: where does it actually map and where is it false? A good analogy points at causal structure; a bad one only flatters your current intuition.
4. **Reach for comparison.** Sharp data from outside the building — competitors, international peers, historical base rates — is the antidote to the inside view. The international comparison on US light-rail construction costs (Paris Line 14 at $230M/km vs. New York Second Avenue at $1.7B/km) is biting precisely because no internal frame would have surfaced it.
5. **Name the challenge in a sentence.** If you cannot, you are not done. The point is not to write a 200-page report; the point is a clear-eyed simple story that calls attention to the critical aspects. Diagnosis is a *judgment about the meaning of facts*, not a proof.
6. **Be wary of using a framework as a substitute.** Porter's Five Forces, BCG matrix, SWOT, Blue Ocean — these are scaffolding for thinking, not a diagnosis. If your "diagnosis" is "we are in the Differentiation quadrant," you have not diagnosed anything; you have categorized.

## Examples

**Situation:** Carolyn, a fully-employed MBA student running business planning at a health-products company, comes to office hours. Her new CEO has asked her to deliver "at least 15 percent annual profit growth." She wants help with "a simple road map — a plan the CEO can take to the board, with steps for achieving the goals." She is looking for a system that turns goals into a logical strategy.
**Application:** Carolyn's boss has not wrapped his head around the crux of his situation. He is focused on performance goals and outcomes rather than opportunities and problems. The diagnosis Carolyn needs is not "how do we hit 15%" — that is the result her boss wants, not the challenge. The challenge is upstream: what is actually going on in this business that makes 15% growth a question worth asking? Is the product line mature? Has the basis of differentiation eroded? Is the channel concentrating? Is the founder/leadership unwilling to face what's actually going on? The diagnosis question reframes the assignment from *how do we hit the number* to *what is the structure of the situation that is producing the gap?* Until that is named, every "strategy" is just a goal restated with a timeline.
**Result:** The point of Carolyn's Dilemma in *The Crux* is that no "strategy calculator" exists. The work is the diagnosis. Until her boss can name the actual challenge — not the gap to a number, but the situation in the business — she has nothing to recommend. The 15% target is a decision masquerading as a goal.

**Situation:** Apple's iPhone, 2007–2009. Microsoft's Steve Ballmer dismissed it: "There's no chance the iPhone is going to get any significant market share. No chance." Tech columnist John Dvorak called it doomed. Nokia's chief strategist said: "Even with the Mac, Apple attracted much attention at first, but they have remained a niche manufacturer. That will be true in mobile phones as well."
**Application:** All three made the same diagnostic move — they used an analogy. Smartphones are like PCs; Apple is the boutique high-margin player; therefore Apple will be a small high-margin niche, the volume will go to the open-system standard. The analogy *felt* obvious. It was wrong. PCs became clones because IBM made specific design and intellectual-property mistakes; the PC market exploded because of office word-processing demand. Phones were a different shape — business demand was already sated by BlackBerry, the explosion came from consumer demand for web-enabled smartphones, and Apple had not made IBM's mistakes. The trick to using analogy well is access to *more than one or two* additional situations, understanding the logic in each, and *checking how that logic maps* to the current situation. None of that was done.
**Result:** Nokia and Motorola were swept into the dustbin of history. Microsoft "whiffed its swing" at the mobile market. Apple became the world's first trillion-dollar company. A bad analogy is not a small mistake — it is the kind of mistake that makes industry leaders walk off cliffs in formation.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** write a diagnosis that is just a restatement of the result.
**Why:** "Sales are declining," "margins are compressed," "customer satisfaction is poor" — these are *results*. A diagnosis names the cause, not the symptom. Underperformance is a result; the true challenge is the *reason for* the underperformance. If your diagnosis sentence has the same nouns as your problem statement, you have not diagnosed anything.

**Don't:** confuse a consulting framework with a diagnosis.
**Why:** Frameworks like Porter's Five Forces, the BCG matrix, Blue Ocean's Strategy Canvas, or SWOT are designed to call attention to what *might* be important in a situation. They cannot, by themselves, guide you to specific actions or constitute a diagnosis. A 200-page consultants' deck full of frameworks, with no diagnostic sentence at the end, is exactly the situation in which most "strategies" never start. Use the framework as a scaffold; do the diagnostic judgment yourself.

**Don't:** treat diagnosis as a one-time exercise that ends when the deck is finished.
**Why:** An explicit diagnosis is what makes the rest of the strategy *revisable* when circumstances change. If the diagnosis is a sentence everyone can name, the strategy can be re-evaluated against it as facts change. If the diagnosis is implicit and buried, the strategy ossifies and gets defended for political reasons long after its premise has stopped being true.

**Don't:** mistake the inside-view story for the situation.
**Why:** "Our case is different." "The market is telling us." "We have the best people." These are how the closed circle of internal narrative dodges diagnosis. The antidote is independent judgment grounded in evidence from outside the room — comparisons across industries, base rates, history of analogous companies, the dumb-question outsider. Reframing is most useful precisely where the insiders most resist it.
