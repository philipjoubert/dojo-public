# The Law of the Category

## When to Use
- When a client is locked out of the top rung of an established category and needs a viable alternative to head-on competition.
- When evaluating a new product or service concept — specifically, what category it could first in, not just what features it has.
- When a market is crowded and the conventional wisdom says "there's no room for another player."
- When a brand has a genuine point of difference that the market hasn't named or filed yet.
- When someone asks what to do after they've already lost the battle for an existing category.

## Fails When
- **The new category is a factory creneau, not a mind creneau.** "One common mistake in looking for creneaus is filling a hole in the factory rather than one in the mind." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* The Edsel was Ford's attempt to fill a price gap in their lineup. There was a hole in the Ford product line — there was no hole in the prospect's mind. New categories must exist in the prospect's head first.
- **The differentiation is cosmetic.** A new name for the same thing does not create a new category. Framing an existing service as "the first X for Y" only works if the prospect genuinely files X-for-Y differently from the broader category. If they don't, you've invented a label, not a position.
- **The category is too small.** Leadership of a tiny category is still leadership of a tiny category. The ladder has to be large enough to support a business. Sometimes a new category must be allowed to grow before the business can grow.

## Core Concept

The Law of the Category is the direct consequence of the Law of Leadership. If being first into the prospect's mind is what determines lasting success, and you are not first in the existing category, then the logical move is to find or create a category in which you can be first. "If you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you can be first in." *(22 Laws, Law 2)*

This is not a consolation prize strategy. It is the correct strategic move. The French call it chercher le creneau — look for the hole. "Cherchez le creneau and then fill it." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* Not the hole in your product line. The hole in the prospect's mind. The distinction is critical. Your job is to find what the prospect does not yet have a mental slot for, and then be the first brand to fill that slot.

The strategy cuts against the grain of how most companies think. "That advice goes against the 'bigger and better' philosophy ingrained into the American spirit." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* The natural instinct when facing an entrenched leader is to build a better version of what the leader has. Work on the product. Improve the features. Spend more on marketing. And then beat the leader on their own terms. This almost never works. "Most products fail to achieve reasonable sales goals because the accent is on 'better' rather than 'speed.'" *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* It's not that quality doesn't matter — it's that fighting the leader on the leader's ground, for the leader's category, is fighting to lose.

The alternative reorients the entire question. When IBM became dominant in computers, the companies that tried to compete directly — Burroughs, Control Data, General Electric, Honeywell, NCR, RCA, Sperry, dubbed "Snow White and the seven dwarfs" — went nowhere. "The most successful computer company of the seventies and eighties, next to IBM, was Digital Equipment Corporation. IBM was first in computers. DEC was first in minicomputers." *(22 Laws, Law 2)* DEC didn't build a better IBM. It built a new rung on a new ladder. Then others followed the same logic — Tandem in fault-tolerant computers, Cray in supercomputers, Convex in mini supercomputers — each creating their own category leadership rather than fighting for scraps from IBM's table.

The same principle explains Amelia Earhart. "What's the name of the third person to fly the Atlantic Ocean solo? It's Amelia Earhart. Now, is Amelia known as the third person to fly the Atlantic Ocean solo, or as the first woman to do so?" *(22 Laws, Law 2)* She didn't win by being third. She won by redefining the competition. Not "best Atlantic solo pilot" but "first woman to fly the Atlantic solo." New category. New rung. New leader.

The category move also applies to business model and channel. "There are many different ways to be first. Dell got into the crowded personal computer field by being the first to sell computers by phone." *(22 Laws, Law 2)* Same product. New category: direct-sale computers. When the law says "category," it means any meaningful angle that creates a separate ladder in the prospect's mind — not just a product category, but a segment, a channel, a price tier, a customer type, a use case. Lear's was "the first magazine for the mature woman" in a world full of women's magazines. Charles Schwab was "the first discount broker" in a world full of brokerages. The category doesn't have to be defined by product features. It just has to create a new mental slot.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop thinking about the competition and start thinking about the prospect's mind.** Category creation begins not with the product but with an inventory of what ladders already exist in the prospect's head, what positions are occupied on those ladders, and where there are gaps. "When you launch a new product, the first question to ask yourself is not 'How is this new product better than the competition?' but 'First what?' In other words, what category is this new product first in?" *(22 Laws, Law 2)*

2. **Look for the opposite.** The most productive strategy for finding creneaus is often to go against the current. "To find a creneau, you must have the ability to think in reverse, to go against the grain. If everyone else is going east, see if you can find your creneau by going west." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* When Detroit went long and low on cars, Volkswagen went short and fat. "Think small." When every beer was competing on taste and heritage, Miller created "light." When every premium car was a rolling davenport, BMW focused on "driving." The gap in the prospect's mind is often directly behind whatever the category leader is facing forward toward.

3. **Find the angle: size, price, sex, age, time of day, distribution, customer type.** Creneaus come in many forms. "Sex is one. Marlboro was the first national brand to establish a masculine position in cigarettes." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* Age is another: Aim toothpaste aimed at children carved 10 percent of a Crest-and-Colgate-dominated market. "Time of day is also a potential positioning possibility. Nyquil, the first night-time cold remedy, is one example." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* Distribution is a creneau: L'eggs was the first hosiery brand distributed in supermarkets and became the leading brand. Each of these is a fresh ladder.

4. **Promote the category, not just the brand.** When you are first in a new category, the correct marketing message is "here is why this new category exists and why you need it," not "here is why our brand is better." You have no competition yet. Your job is to make the category real. "When you're the first in a new category, promote the category. In essence, you have no competition. DEC told its prospects why they ought to buy a minicomputer, not a DEC minicomputer." *(22 Laws, Law 2)*

5. **Name the category, not just the product.** The company that names a category has a powerful structural advantage. Competitors who follow are forced to use your category language. Every "fault-tolerant computer" competitor after Tandem was fighting on Tandem's terrain. Every "overnight delivery" competitor was living in Federal Express's world.

## Examples

**Situation:** After IBM became a massive success in mainframe computers, Anheuser-Busch could have simply launched an imported beer to compete with Heineken — the market-proven success.

**Application:** Instead, they asked a different question: "If there's a market for a high-priced imported beer, maybe there's a market for a high-priced domestic beer." Nobody had created that category. The premium domestic beer ladder had no rung one. "And so they started to promote Michelob, the first high-priced domestic beer, which today outsells Heineken two to one." *(22 Laws, Law 2)* Carlsberg, the me-too imported beer Anheuser-Busch also brought in, went nowhere.

**Result:** Two different outcomes from two different strategies: find the open category and own it (Michelob); follow the leader directly (Carlsberg, irrelevant). Same company. Same resources. Opposite results based on category strategy alone.

---

**Situation:** Miller Lite has dominated the domestic light beer category. An importer wants to compete in the light beer segment.

**Application:** Rather than launch another domestic light beer, an importer asks: if there's a domestic light beer market, is there an imported light beer market? Five years after Miller Lite's success, "Amstel Light became the largest-selling imported light beer." *(22 Laws, Law 2)* Not because it was better than Miller Lite, but because it was first on a new ladder: imported light beer.

**Result:** Amstel Light led a category it created. Miller Lite stayed on its own ladder. Neither competed with the other for the same rung.

---

**Situation:** A SaaS startup is entering project management software. Asana, Monday.com, and Linear already have strong positions with different user bases. The product is well-built but not meaningfully different on features.

**Application:** The "better features" approach will produce a product with no category leadership and a slow death by comparison shopping. The creneau search asks: who is not well-served by existing tools? Construction teams? Creative agencies doing client work? Regulated industries with compliance requirements? Each of these is a potential new ladder. The product may need to be narrowed — some features cut, some added — to authentically own the category. But the goal is rung one of a real ladder, not rung six of an existing one.

**Result:** Leadership of a specific-enough category that the word you own in that vertical is yours. Every generic "project management" tool looks like your competition, not your better.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Create a category that only exists in your factory, not in the prospect's mind.
**Why:** "One common mistake in looking for creneaus is filling a hole in the factory rather than one in the mind." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* Ford's Edsel was positioned to fill a product lineup gap between Ford/Mercury and Lincoln — a real internal gap, an imaginary external one. There was no hole in the prospect's mind that the Edsel filled. Category creation requires that the prospect genuinely think differently about the new category, not just that your product spreadsheet has found a gap to exploit.

**Don't:** Fall into the "everybody" trap.
**Why:** "Some marketing people reject the 'cherchez le creneau' concept. They don't want to be tied down to a specific position because they believe it limits their sales." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* This is the logic of companies that want to serve every segment, claim every benefit, and occupy every niche simultaneously. "You can't win by not making enemies, by being everything to everybody." *(Positioning, Ch. 7)* A new category position is always narrower than "all customers everywhere." That narrowness is the source of its power.

**Don't:** Launch a new category without committing fully to it.
**Why:** Half-measures in category creation produce neither category leadership nor a meaningful position in the existing category. If you're entering as "the minicomputer company," do not also keep competing on the mainframe ladder. DEC's success came from complete commitment to minicomputers. "When you're the first in a new category, promote the category." *(22 Laws, Law 2)* Hedging produces ambiguity, and ambiguity produces no rung at all.

**Don't:** Confuse a new product name with a new category.
**Why:** Relabeling an existing product with a new name is not category creation. "The first automobile, for example, was called a 'horseless' carriage, a name which allowed the public to position the concept against the existing mode of transportation." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* A new category is one that creates a genuinely new slot in the prospect's mental filing system — one that didn't exist before. If the prospect just thinks "oh, that's a different word for [existing category]," no new ladder has been built.
