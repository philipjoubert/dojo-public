# Defensive Warfare

## When to Use
- When your brand is the recognized category leader in the prospect's mind — not a self-declared leader, but the one customers and prospects name first.
- When a No. 2 or flanking competitor begins gaining ground and you need to decide whether and how to respond.
- When you are tempted to diversify or extend your line aggressively — defensive warfare is a reminder to protect the hill you already own before trying to take new ones.
- When setting annual marketing strategy for a dominant brand in a mature category.

## Fails When
- **You are not actually the leader.** Half the companies in any industry claim leadership positions. Customers don't care about your internal rankings. If the prospect doesn't put you at the top of the mental ladder, you are not playing defense — you are deluding yourself. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*
- **You wait and see.** "Let's monitor the situation" is the classic leadership failure mode. Wars often occur in pairs: the loser starts the second war. A leader who watches a competitive move develop and says "we'll see if it takes hold" is surrendering the only real advantage a defender has — time. The attack takes time to reach the mind. Use that time.
- **Ego overrides strategy.** Leaders get caught up in dismissing new competitive moves as beneath them. Detroit called the Beetle "overrated." IBM called the minicomputer niche too small. The pattern repeats in every industry. "Let's monitor" and "that's not really our market" are phrases that have ended more leadership positions than any offensive campaign.

## Core Concept

"The defensive form of war is in itself stronger than the offense," wrote Clausewitz — and Ries and Trout built the first principle of Marketing Warfare's strategic square on exactly this idea. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 3)* The leader in any category sits on the high ground of the prospect's mind. That position is almost impossible to dislodge head-on. In the 1923 survey of 25 leading brands, 60 years later 20 were still in first place and four were in second. One was in fifth. Only five out of twenty-five fell from the top spot in six decades. Defensive warfare explains why.

There are three principles in Marketing Warfare's typology of defensive strategy. The first is the most counterintuitive: **only the market leader should play defense.** This sounds obvious until you realize that every company thinks it's a leader. The leader Ries and Trout have in mind is the one who owns the position in the mind of the prospect. There are many computer companies, but there was only one IBM. There are many rental car companies, but there is only one Hertz. Defensive warfare belongs exclusively to that company — the true market leader as defined by the customer, not the boardroom. "Companies don't create leaders — customers do." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

The second principle is the most surprising: **the best defensive strategy is the courage to attack yourself.** Because the leader already holds the dominant position, the safest way to hold it is to make it a moving target. Gillette is the textbook case. Wilkinson Sword beat Gillette to the stainless blade in the early 1960s. From that moment on, Gillette changed its philosophy entirely. It introduced Trac II (two blades), then Atra (adjustable double blade), then Good News (disposable — a deliberate attack on its own high-margin Trac II business to block Bic). "It's better to take business away from yourself than have someone else do it for you," is the lesson Gillette lived. The company grew to 65 percent of the wetshaving market by continuously obsoleting its own products. Short-term profit sacrificed; long-term market share protected. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

The third principle is: **strong competitive moves should always be blocked.** The leader has a second chance that no one else gets. If the leader misses the opportunity to attack itself first, it can still recover by copying the competitor's move — but only if it moves rapidly, before the attacker establishes a foothold in the mind. The battle for Migraine Mountain (Tylenol vs. Datril) is the definitive illustration. Two weeks before Datril's advertising broke, Johnson & Johnson cut Tylenol's price to match, issued credit memos to all retail outlets, and launched an aggressive public relations offensive against the competitor's claims. Datril never cleared 1 percent market share. Tylenol became the best-selling product in American drugstores. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

A useful reserve strategy: Clausewitz noted that "the number of fresh reserves is always the chief point looked at by both commanders." Anheuser-Busch used this with Budweiser — "pulsing" advertising, deploying marketing budgets only when competitive pressure demanded, keeping reserves for emergency counterattacks. The leader doesn't need to spend maximally. It needs to spend enough to maintain dominance and keep the rest in reserve.

## How to Apply

1. **Establish whether you are genuinely the leader.** Not the leader in some carefully chosen slice of the market, but the leader in the category as the prospect understands it. Ask: If someone needs your category, whose name comes to mind first? If it isn't yours, defensive warfare is not your strategy. Read the offensive or flanking chapters instead.

2. **Map your own strongest positions and ask what would happen if a competitor occupied them first.** For every major attribute your brand owns, ask: could a competitor introduce a product that makes ours look outdated? If yes, introduce it yourself. IBM did this repeatedly with mainframe lines — each new release made the previous one obsolete before competitors could. The moving target is always harder to hit.

3. **Put blocking moves on a hair trigger.** Establish the rule internally: any competitive move that achieves measurable traction in test markets triggers an immediate response. Not a committee review. Not a wait-and-see. An immediate response. Tylenol's price cut was executed in two weeks. That speed is the entire strategy.

4. **Keep reserves.** Never deploy your full marketing budget in any given period. The reserve is not waste — it is insurance, and Clausewitz said it was the variable that most often decided the battle.

5. **Resist the temptation of horizontal expansion.** Defensive warfare means protecting the mountain you own, not invading the adjacent valley. A leader who diverts resources to attack new categories weakens its defensive position in the core category. The legal system agrees: "The mere possession of monopoly power was not necessarily illegal," ruled Judge Kaufman in the Berkey-Kodak case, "but it was unlawful for a monopoly to use its power in one area to gain a competitive advantage in another market." Good legal advice and good strategic advice simultaneously. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

## Examples

**Situation:** Bic threatens Gillette's wetshaving monopoly with low-cost disposables.
**Application:** Gillette introduces Good News, an inexpensive disposable razor, before Bic can establish the category. Good News costs more to make than it sells for — Gillette sacrifices margin deliberately to block the segment.
**Result:** Bic loses $25 million in its first three years in the disposable business. Gillette's total market share grows to 65 percent. The defensive attack on itself was more expensive than inaction in the short run, and far cheaper in the long run. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

**Situation:** Bristol-Myers launches Datril at half the price of Tylenol.
**Application:** Johnson & Johnson cuts Tylenol's price to match Datril two weeks before Datril's advertising breaks. They issue credit memos to 165,000 retail outlets simultaneously. They complain aggressively to networks about Datril's advertising claims.
**Result:** Datril never passes 1 percent. Tylenol becomes the largest-selling product in American drugstores. Speed and decisiveness — not product superiority — won the battle. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

**Situation:** General Motors faces decades of product innovations from Ford and Chrysler — power steering, power brakes, alternators, and the like — without originating any of them itself.
**Application:** GM copies, covers, and then markets the innovations as if it had pioneered them. The power of the majority opinion sustains the GM "engineering excellence" reputation in the mind of the prospect.
**Result:** As John DeLorean documented, neither Ford nor Chrysler made substantial cuts into GM's half the market. The mind, not the product, is the battleground. Covering a competitive move — even one you didn't invent — is a legitimate defensive act. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Declare yourself a leader based on internal definitions.
**Why:** The customer decides who the leader is. If your "leadership position" requires qualifying words — "in the Eastern region," "among mid-market customers," "in Q3" — you are not the market leader in the sense that matters. Defensive warfare executed by a non-leader is an expensive waste of resources that could be spent on flanking or guerrilla moves.

**Don't:** Wait to see if a competitive move gains traction.
**Why:** By the time it gains traction, it has gained ground in the mind of the prospect. The mind is the battleground. Once a position is established there, dislodging it costs three to ten times what it would have cost to block it when it was new. "Too often what happens, happens too fast. All of a sudden, it's too late to get into the new ball game." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 7)*

**Don't:** Treat self-attack as a failure.
**Why:** Every leader who refuses to obsolete its own products eventually watches a competitor do it instead. The disposable razor was going to happen whether Gillette cooperated or not. Gillette chose to be the one who launched it. That decision cost short-term margin and protected a 65 percent market share. The company that hesitates to cannibalize itself gets cannibalized by someone else.

**Don't:** Spend every dollar in the marketing budget every year.
**Why:** Clausewitz identified reserves as the decisive variable. A leader with no reserves cannot respond decisively to a sudden competitive move. The budget should include an emergency reserve — held back, not deployed — specifically for rapid blocking operations.
