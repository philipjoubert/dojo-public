# Guerrilla Warfare

## When to Use
- When your company is small relative to the dominant competitors in your category — regardless of your absolute size. American Motors was considerably larger than Gillette, but AMC should have fought guerrilla warfare and Gillette should have fought defensive warfare. Size is always relative to the competition.
- When you need to pick a territory small enough that you can be the biggest fish in that specific pond — not the biggest fish overall.
- When your advantage is speed and flexibility, not resources. Guerrilla warfare trades budget for agility.
- When you are tempted to launch a flanking attack but honestly lack the resources to pursue for three to five years after initial success.

## Fails When
- **The guerrilla acts like a leader.** "The day the guerrilla company orders its first Cadillac limousine for the chairman of the board is the day the guerrilla company starts to go downhill." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* The big company playbook — formal org charts, career path documentation, staff functions for every function — is appropriate for the Fortune 500. Applied to a guerrilla, it adds cost, slows decisions, and destroys the organizational flexibility that is the guerrilla's only weapon against the giants.
- **The guerrilla line-extends.** Rolls-Royce tried introducing cheaper cars. Packard introduced the Clipper, a lower-priced version of a high-priced car. The cheaper cars sold; the expensive ones didn't. The Clipper killed the Packard name. One name cannot simultaneously stand for "most exclusive" and "more affordable." The guerrilla position is the guerrilla's castle. Breach it and there is no fallback.
- **The guerrilla refuses to retreat.** "A company that runs away lives again to fight another day." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* Flexibility is the guerrilla's most valuable asset. When conditions turn against you — when the leader finally decides the niche is worth contesting, or the segment shrinks, or a better-capitalized flanker arrives — the right move is to redeploy, not to hold and die.

## Core Concept

Guerrilla warfare is, numerically, the most relevant of Marketing Warfare's four strategy types. "Out of every 100 companies, as a glittering generality, one should play defense, two should play offense, three should flank, and 94 should be guerrillas." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* Most of America's 5 million corporations — and most companies in every market around the world — should be waging guerrilla warfare. Yet guerrilla strategy is consistently misunderstood as "the small company that fights dirty." It is nothing of the sort. Guerrilla warfare is a rigorous methodology for achieving and defending market leadership within a territory precisely sized for the resources available.

The first guerrilla principle is foundational: **find a segment of the market small enough to defend.** A guerrilla does not change the mathematics of a marketing war — the big company still beats the small company in a direct confrontation. What the guerrilla does is reduce the size of the battleground until local superiority is achievable. Computervision held a 21-to-19 advantage over IBM — not overall, but in CAD workstations. That 21-to-19 lead is worth protecting at all costs; it is the guerrilla's entire competitive existence. Rolls-Royce owns the market for cars over $100,000. Nobody tries to compete with Rolls-Royce because the market is small enough that the math works for Rolls and against everyone else. Crain's Chicago Business has 40,000 paid subscribers versus Business Week's 800,000 nationally — but in Chicago, Crain's outguns Business Week. The guerrilla doesn't need to be bigger everywhere; it needs to be bigger where it operates. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*

The second guerrilla principle governs organizational behavior: **no matter how successful you become, never act like the leader.** In Vietnam, the United States went in with cooks, bakers, clerks, chaplains, and public relations officers. In 1968, with 543,000 troops deployed, only roughly 80,000 were combat soldiers. The rest served in supply and service functions. The guerrilla's lean structure — maximum people on the firing line, minimum overhead — is not just a cost strategy. It is the mechanism that produces faster decisions, faster product launches, faster responses to market shifts. "In a small company, one person's hunch can be enough to launch a new product. In a big company, the same concept is likely to be buried in committees for months." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* KangaRoos athletic shoes (zippered pocket on the side, born from one importer's personal frustration) went from idea to $75 million in annual sales on the back of this organizational agility.

The third guerrilla principle governs survival: **be prepared to bug out at a moment's notice.** This is Che Guevara applied to business strategy. When a guerrilla holds a position stubbornly despite deteriorating odds, it fights like a regular army — and it loses like a regular army. When Nalley's Foods learned that Kraft was dropping its imitation mayonnaise, Nalley's launched a similar product in nine days. The guerrilla's willingness to jump in and out of positions — without the weight of executive titles, legacy investments, or hierarchical infighting that would require months of committee work at a large company — is the source of its tactical advantage. Bugging in is as important as bugging out: when an opportunity opens up, the guerrilla fills it before the national brand even schedules a meeting. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*

Guerrilla territory takes many forms. Geographic guerrillas — a local department store that knows its market better than Sears, a regional bank that outguns Chase Manhattan in a specific suburb. Demographic guerrillas — *Inc.* magazine, the first national publication for small-business owners, which launched with the most successful first year in magazine history by targeting the exact segment that Business Week implicitly excluded. Industry guerrillas — Triod Systems, a computer company for automotive parts wholesalers, which reaches over $100 million in revenue by going narrow and deep in a single vertical. Product guerrillas — Tandem Computers' fault-tolerant NonStop systems, a unique enough product that the big players have no incentive to compete. High-end guerrillas — Steinway pianos, Cuisinart food processors (priced four times higher than GE), Concord watches. In each case, the market is small enough that the giants leave it alone, and the guerrilla is big enough within it to be unchallenged.

## How to Apply

1. **Define your segment with precision — then make it smaller.** The natural instinct is to define the territory as broadly as possible. Resist it. "The tendency is to do the opposite, to try to grab as big a market as possible. This could be a mistake." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* Pick a segment small enough that you can become the undisputed leader within it. If you cannot be the leader of your chosen segment, the segment is too large.

2. **Achieve leadership within the segment and protect it absolutely.** When a guerrilla begins to lose market leadership in its home territory, it starts going downhill rapidly. Leadership provides the credibility that makes everything else possible. Computervision's 21-to-19 edge over IBM in CAD workstations is that company's entire raison d'être. A management team that allows that edge to erode has failed at the only job that matters.

3. **Strip the organization to the fighting line.** Avoid formal org charts, elaborate job descriptions, and staff functions that serve internal needs rather than customer ones. Every person who does not directly face a customer or a competitor is overhead — and overhead is the guerrilla's enemy. The objective is to get as high a proportion of personnel on the firing line as possible.

4. **Make fast decisions.** The guerrilla's speed advantage over the large competitor depends on a short decision cycle. Protect that cycle. The moment decisions require committee approval, sign-offs, and "stakeholder alignment," the guerrilla has started the process of becoming what it is competing against.

5. **Establish the bug-out threshold in advance.** Define the conditions under which you will exit a position before you are under pressure to defend it. The worst guerrilla decisions are made in the heat of battle by managers who have staked their reputations on a specific market. Know in advance what signals will trigger redeployment, and commit to acting on them.

## Examples

**Situation:** American Motors competes against General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler.
**Application:** AMC is too small to launch offensive attacks against GM. Even initially successful flanking moves — like the Nash Rambler — cannot be sustained because AMC lacks the dealers, manufacturing capacity, and marketing resources to hold new territory. The only consistent winner: the Jeep. Classic guerrilla territory — big enough to be profitable, too small for GM to bother defending seriously.
**Result:** The Jeep is a consistent winner for AMC. The passenger car lines — where AMC tried to act like the big players — are consistent losers. "Head for the hills, put on your black pajamas, and become a guerrilla." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*

**Situation:** *Inc.* magazine enters a market dominated by Business Week, Fortune, and Forbes.
**Application:** Demographic guerrilla strategy. Founder Bernard Goldhirsh recognizes that Business Week should really be called "Big Business Week" — it effectively ignores the 5 million small corporations in America. *Inc.* targets small-business owners exclusively. First national magazine for this segment. No attempt to compete across the whole business-reading public.
**Result:** Most successful first year in magazine history — 648 pages of advertising worth nearly $6 million in year one. The segment was large enough to be profitable and specific enough to be defensible. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*

**Situation:** Computervision competes in the computer business against IBM.
**Application:** Concentrates entirely on CAD (computer-aided design) workstations — a segment IBM could dominate but hasn't fully committed to. Computervision becomes bigger than IBM in this one category, holding a 21-to-19 market share advantage.
**Result:** A company nobody outside the industry knows about becomes "bigger than IBM" in its chosen territory. The math changes completely once the battleground is defined correctly. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to do both — maintain the guerrilla position while launching a flanking attack into adjacent territory.
**Why:** "We call this line of thinking the 'line extension trap.' One name can't support two different concepts." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)* Rolls-Royce can't simultaneously mean "the world's most exclusive car" and "affordable luxury." Packard proved this at the cost of the nameplate itself. The guerrilla position is a mental monopoly in a small territory. Dilute it and you lose both the guerrilla stronghold and whatever you were trying to build alongside it.

**Don't:** Respond to success by building overhead.
**Why:** Overhead is the mechanism by which successful guerrillas transform themselves into slow, vulnerable mid-sized companies. Staff functions, formal processes, career hierarchies — each addition reduces the decision speed that is the guerrilla's primary weapon. Watch the Cadillac limousine. It will tell you exactly when the company stopped being a guerrilla.

**Don't:** Hold a losing position out of sunk cost.
**Why:** The guerrilla's flexibility is its survival mechanism. A guerrilla that digs in and fights to the last dollar over a deteriorating position is no longer a guerrilla — it is a poorly equipped regular army unit. PEOPLExpress started as a low-end guerrilla, then bought more planes, opened more routes, and tried to fight American, United, and Delta. It didn't have the resources to take on those air forces, and it had given up the flexibility that made the guerrilla viable in the first place. "A company that runs away lives again to fight another day." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 10)*
