# The Law of Line Extension

## When to Use
- When a client wants to put their successful brand name on a new product.
- When a company reports declining market share after years of "successful" extensions.
- When anyone invokes the phrase "brand equity" as a reason to enter a new category.
- When you need to explain why the full-line generalist keeps losing to the focused specialist.

## Fails When
- **Both competitors are line-extended** — In a market where every competitor has diluted their name equally, one of the line-extensions will "win." This produces apparent success that gets cited as proof the law doesn't apply. It does. The winner is still weaker than a focused specialist would be.
- **Short measurement window** — Line extension increases sales in the short term. If you measure within eighteen months of launch you will see "success." The damage surfaces in years three through ten, by which time the executive who made the decision has moved on.

## Core Concept

By far the most violated law in marketing. "If violating any of our laws was a punishable offense, a large portion of corporate America would be in jail." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* Line extension is the process of taking a brand name that stands for something in the prospect's mind and stretching it across products it was never meant to cover. It sounds like efficiency. It is, in fact, dilution.

The logic that drives it feels airtight: "We make A-1, a great sauce that gets the dominant share of the steak business. But people are switching from beef to chicken, so let's introduce a poultry product. And what better name to use than A-1. That way people will know the poultry sauce comes from the makers of that great steak sauce, A-1." The problem is that "marketing is a battle of perception, not product. In the mind, A-1 is not the brand name, but the steak sauce itself. 'Would you pass me the A-1?' asks the diner. Nobody replies: 'A-1 what?'" *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* In spite of an $18 million advertising budget, the A-1 poultry launch was a dismal failure.

The same dynamic plays out at every scale. "What does IBM stand for? It used to stand for 'mainframe computers.' Today it stands for everything, which means it stands for nothing." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* IBM had $65 billion in revenues in 1991 and still lost $2.8 billion — that is $8 million a day disappearing into an unfocused operation. In a narrow sense, line extension is a desk drawer that fills up with no conscious effort. One day a company is tightly focused on a single product that is highly profitable. The next day the same company is spread thin over many products and is losing money. *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)*

The core mechanism is this: a brand name exists because it means something specific. It owns a word in the prospect's mind. When you attach that name to a second, third, fourth product — especially products in different categories — you blur the meaning. You undermine the very asset you were trying to exploit. "The more products and services you hang on a name, the less focused and less powerful it becomes." *(Focus, Divide and Conquer)* Levi's did this with shoes, luggage, hats, and action suits. "Top management," said Business Week at the time, "seriously overestimated the power of Levi's name." *(Focus, Divide and Conquer)*

The beer industry is the laboratory that proves this law without ambiguity. Miller High Life was growing at 27 percent per year in the early seventies. Then Miller introduced Miller Lite. In the short term, the two Millers coexisted. The high-water mark for Miller High Life was 1979 — five years after the Lite launch, High Life had almost tripled in volume to 23.6 million barrels. Then came the long-term effect: thirteen consecutive years of decline, to just 5.8 million barrels by 1991. "What made him successful in the short term is exactly what caused him to fail in the long term. Line extension." *(22 Laws, Ch. 11, on Trump; same dynamic)*

## How to Apply

1. **Name the word the brand currently owns.** Before evaluating any extension, ask: what single word does this brand own in the prospect's mind? "Overnight" for FedEx. "Ketchup" for Heinz. "Steak sauce" for A-1. If you cannot name the word clearly, the brand has already been diluted.

2. **Ask whether the new product lives in the same camp.** A brand name has power only in the camp where it earned its credentials. "Names have power, but only in the camp in which they have credentials. When they get out of their camp, when they lose focus, they also lose their power." *(Focus, Divide and Conquer)* IBM has no power in the home. Apple has no power in the mainframe world.

3. **Measure long-term, not short-term.** Line extensions increase sales in the short term by filling out the shelf and giving the salesforce more to sell. They destroy brands in the long term by diluting the meaning of the name. "If a bullet took five years to reach a target, very few criminals would be convicted of homicide." *(22 Laws, Ch. 11)* Build the measurement window accordingly.

4. **Look at the leader in every category.** "Invariably, the leader in any category is the brand that is not line-extended." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* Gerber has 72 percent of the baby food market. Beech-Nut and Heinz — both line-extended brands — split the remainder. Smucker's has 35 percent of jellies. Kraft, which means everything, has 9 percent.

5. **Prescribe the alternative.** The antidote to line extension is either: (a) maintain the narrow focus and let a competitor own the new category, or (b) launch a new brand name for the new category. Both require courage. "The antidote for line extension is corporate courage, a commodity in short supply." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)*

## Examples

**Situation:** 7-Up had a 5.7 percent share of the soft-drink market in 1978 as the simple lemon-lime Uncola.
**Application:** The company added 7-Up Gold, Cherry 7-Up, and assorted diet versions.
**Result:** 7-Up's share fell to 2.5 percent. The original focus — lemon-lime Uncola — was diluted by the extensions. *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)*

**Situation:** Pierre Cardin was the dominant name in men's fashion — a focused, prestigious brand.
**Application:** Cardin licensed the Pierre Cardin name broadly across dozens of unrelated categories — wine, chocolate, cigarettes, anything that would pay a licensing fee.
**Result:** The brand became associated with everything, and therefore with nothing in particular. Ralph Lauren seized the focused prestige position that Cardin had abandoned. *(22 Laws, Ch. 12; Focus, Ch. Crossing the Trench)*

**Situation:** Miller Brewing was winning with Miller Lite and wanted more volume.
**Application:** Over the years Miller added Genuine Draft, Genuine Draft Light, Reserve, Reserve Light, Reserve Amber Ale, Miller Clear, High Life Light, Genuine Red, Lite Ultra, Lite Ice.
**Result:** "Did Miller increase its share of the beer business? Short-term, yes, but long-term, no. Furthermore, in the wake of its line-extension successes, Miller has fallen farther behind Anheuser-Busch." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mistake line-extension "success" for a refutation of the law.
**Why:** When two line-extended brands compete against each other, one will win. Coca-Cola's Diet Coke succeeded — but not because line extension works. It succeeded because Diet Pepsi, its main competitor, was also a line extension. "When two line-extended brands compete against each other, one line-extended brand has to win. So there's always plenty of line-extension successes like Diet Coke to brag about." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* The real test is what a focused competitor could do to both of them.

**Don't:** Confuse brand awareness with brand permission.
**Why:** A well-known name is not a license to go anywhere. The name Xerox is one of the most recognized in business. Xerox spent $2 billion over twenty-five years trying to leverage that name in computers. Zero market share. *(22 Laws, Ch. 3)* Awareness without focus is inert.

**Don't:** Assume the marketing department can fix the mess with better advertising.
**Why:** "More is less. The more products, the more markets, the more alliances a company makes, the less money it makes." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* The problem is structural, not communicative. Advertising cannot reposition an unfocused brand. You can't communicate your way out of a line-extension disaster.
