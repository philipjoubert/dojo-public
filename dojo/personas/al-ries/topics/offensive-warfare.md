# Offensive Warfare

## When to Use
- When your company holds a genuine No. 2 or No. 3 position in a category — strong enough to sustain a campaign, with real resources behind the attack.
- When you have identified a weakness that is directly tied to and inseparable from the leader's strength (not a random product gap, but a structural vulnerability).
- When you are ready to concentrate force on a single narrow front rather than spreading resources across a broad product line.
- When your planning starts with the leader — what the leader has, where the leader is strong, how the leader's strength can be turned against itself — rather than with your own internal capabilities.

## Fails When
- **You study yourself instead of the leader.** Most companies react to competitive problems by analyzing their own products, prices, and salesforce. "What a No. 2 company should do is to focus on the leader." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* A company that spends its planning cycle on internal strengths analysis is wasting the one thing offensive warfare requires: a clear picture of the enemy's position.
- **You attack a weakness that is just a weakness.** Tylenol's high price was not an inherent weakness — Johnson & Johnson could cut it instantly, as they proved against Datril. IBM's pricing was not a durable attack target — IBM has manufacturing scale that lets it make money at almost any price. Attack a random weakness and the leader will simply fix it. Attack a weakness born from strength and the leader cannot fix it without undermining the strength.
- **You go wide.** "The full line is a luxury only leaders can afford." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* Chrysler's president once said the company "didn't have the luxury of passing up any automobile market in the United States." That attitude contributed directly to Chrysler's problems. American Motors complained publicly that it only participated in 25 percent of the market — as if the solution were a broader product line. Narrowness of focus is not a weakness in offensive warfare. It is the weapon.

## Core Concept

Marketing Warfare's typology contains four forms of warfare, and their assignment is strict: offensive warfare belongs to No. 2 and No. 3 companies that are strong enough to mount a sustained attack on the leader. It does not belong to guerrillas pretending to be challengers, and it does not belong to leaders, who should be playing defense. "What's good strategy for a leader is bad strategy for an also-ran, and vice versa." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

The first offensive principle is that **the main consideration is the strength of the leader's position** — not your own. This runs against every instinct of the typical marketing manager, who wants to focus on what he or she has built. The leader owns a position in the mind of the prospect. That position is the thing that must be moved before yours can be put in its place. "It's not enough for you to succeed; others must fail. Specifically, the leader." The objective of offensive warfare is not "increase our share of the market." It is: how do we decrease the leader's share? Those are different questions. The first leads to me-too products and vague claims of superiority. The second leads to a genuine strategy. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

The second principle is the operational core: **find a weakness inherent in the leader's strength, and attack at that point.** Note carefully: weakness inherent *in* the strength, not weakness separate from it. Hertz is the largest car rental company. That size is its strength. But size means long queues at the counter — a weakness that Hertz cannot fix without becoming smaller, which would undermine the strength. "Rent from Avis. The line at our counter is shorter." Avis did not attack Hertz for having bad cars or high prices. It attacked the one thing Hertz's biggest advantage made inevitable. The same thinking applies to General Motors: the more cars sold up front, the more service problems created out back. American Motors' Buyer Protection Plan attacked the poor service reputation of GM dealers — a weakness inseparable from GM's dominant sales volume. Achilles had a heel. The bigger the leader, the easier it is to find it. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

The third principle governs execution: **launch the attack on as narrow a front as possible.** Military precedent is unambiguous. In World War II, offensive attacks went down a single highway. Only after a breakthrough did forces expand laterally. Clausewitz: "Where absolute superiority is not attainable, you must produce a relative one at the decisive point by making skillful use of what you have." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* Federal Express understood this. Its first strategy — Priorities One, Two, and Three — tried to compete across the board. Two years, $29 million in losses. Then it reorganized around a single concept: "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight." One product. One promise. The narrowest possible front. Federal Express went on to dominate small-package air express with revenues over $1 billion a year — more than Emery and Airborne combined. The narrow front was not a limitation; it was the strategy.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop the internal analysis. Start an external one.** Map the leader's position in the mind of the prospect. What does the leader stand for? What word does the leader own? What is the single strongest thing the leader has? Your planning should start and end with that answer.

2. **Find the structural weakness.** Ask not "where is the leader weak?" but "where does the leader's biggest strength create an unavoidable problem?" Size creates service issues. Heritage creates rigidity. Ubiquity creates quality control problems. The leader's biggest asset always has a shadow — and that shadow is your attack point. Kodak's strength was ubiquitous distribution (200,000 outlets). The structural weakness: film sitting on unrefrigerated shelves gets old. The attack: refrigerated professional-grade film for consumers. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

3. **Choose one front. Close everything else.** Identify the single product, claim, or segment where you can mass forces to achieve local superiority. This feels wrong to marketing managers trained to "cover the market." Override that instinct. The market leader has enough resources to blunt a broad attack at every point. The only way to break through is concentration at a single decisive point.

4. **Size the attack honestly.** Clausewitz observed that in a survey of 600 companies over two years, only 20 percent enjoyed market share gains of 2 percent or more. Four out of five made negligible gains or lost ground. Of companies 20 years old or older, only 17 percent increased share. Offensive warfare is hard. Enter it with enough force to sustain a multi-year campaign, or don't enter it at all. If you lack that force, read the guerrilla warfare chapter instead.

5. **Aim at the leader, not the weak competitor.** The temptation is to attack the smallest, most vulnerable player in the category. Resist it. "As Willie Sutton used to say, 'I rob banks because that's where the money is.' Ford should attack General Motors because that's where the market is." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* A 10 percent gain at the leader's expense delivers far more volume than a 50 percent gain at the smallest competitor's expense.

## Examples

**Situation:** Avis is No. 2 in the car rental market behind Hertz.
**Application:** Rather than claiming superior cars, better prices, or more locations — which Hertz could match or exceed on all counts — Avis attacks the inherent weakness in Hertz's leadership: queue length. "We're No. 2. We try harder." The line at the Hertz counter is a real problem that Hertz, as the dominant operator, cannot eliminate.
**Result:** Avis builds a durable No. 2 position that outlasts decades of competitive pressure. The positioning works because Hertz cannot fix it without becoming smaller. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

**Situation:** Federal Express enters the package-express business against established air freight forwarders like Emery and Airborne.
**Application:** First strategy (broad line, three service tiers) fails with $29 million in losses over two years. Second strategy: eliminate everything except Priority One. "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight." Single product. Single promise. Maximum force concentration.
**Result:** Federal Express reaches $1 billion in annual revenues, surpassing Emery and Airborne combined. The narrow front that felt like a limitation became the entire competitive advantage. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

**Situation:** A competitor wants to attack The Wall Street Journal, the dominant business publication.
**Application:** Don't try to out-write the Journal — that's fighting strength with strength. The Journal's dominance means it covers everything: business news and financial news in one publication. That breadth is a weakness for specialized advertisers. Attack with a pure business-news daily — "Business Times" — that lets advertisers avoid paying for the financial-news circulation they don't need.
**Result:** Concentrated, focused attack on a specific weakness born from the Journal's comprehensive strength. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Position yourself as a better version of the leader.
**Why:** The leader owns the original concept in the mind. A "better version" registers as an imitation. Schenley's Ne Plus Ultra was a 12-year-old Scotch at the highest price in the market — competing directly with Chivas Regal on the same terrain. The Chivas position was unassailable. Ne Plus Ultra achieved near-zero sales. The name is Latin for "nothing finer." The results were the opposite. *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)*

**Don't:** Launch a full product line as your opening attack.
**Why:** "The full line is a luxury only leaders can afford." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* Every product you add to the attack disperses force. The leader can defend on every front because it has resources on every front. You cannot. The one thing an attacker can do that a defender cannot is achieve local superiority through concentration. A full-line launch is a self-imposed defeat.

**Don't:** Attack a weakness the leader can simply eliminate.
**Why:** Tylenol's price looked like a weakness until Johnson & Johnson showed they could match any price Datril could offer, and do it faster. Attacking fixable weaknesses is an expensive lesson in the defender's strength. Only attack what the leader cannot fix without dismantling what made it the leader.
