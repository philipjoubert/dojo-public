# Focus Entropy: Why Companies Unfocus (and What to Do About It)

## When to Use
- When a company needs to understand why it has lost its sharpness over time.
- When a CEO needs to understand that refocusing is not a failure of past management — it is the correction of a natural physical process.
- When diagnosing any large company that once dominated a category and no longer does.

## Fails When
- **No original focus to lose** — Entropy assumes there was a focused state to decay from. A company that was always a mess has a different problem.
- **All competitors are equally unfocused** — In categories where every player is a sprawling generalist, execution and cost can temporarily substitute for focus. The entropy law still applies, but the competitive disadvantage surfaces more slowly.

## Core Concept

Unfocusing is not a strategic choice. It is the default state of corporate nature. "In the physical world, unfocusing is called entropy, or disorder. And Rudolf Clausius's law of entropy states that over time, the entropy of any closed system increases. Let's say you straighten out your clothes closet. A month later, the closet is a mess. You have witnessed the effects of entropy, one of the fundamental laws of nature. Corporations are no different from clothes closets." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)*

The pattern is universal and almost perfectly predictable. "A successful company usually starts out highly focused on an individual product, service, or market. Over time, the company becomes unfocused. It offers too many products and services for too many markets at too many different price levels. It loses its sense of direction." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* Such a scenario could describe General Motors in the sixties, Sears in the seventies, IBM in the eighties, and Microsoft in the nineties. In each case, the same story: dominant position built on sharp focus, then slow expansion into everything, then the red giant stage — an enormous burned-out hulk of a company. "You become a red giant, a burned-out hulk of a star hundreds of times larger than the sun, but with a surface temperature half as hot." *(Focus, Introduction)*

The primary driver of unfocusing is growth pressure. "What's the driving force in Corporate America? In a word, growth. Management demands substantial increases in annual sales and profits, even when companies are in markets that show no overall growth. Predictably, in order to meet these targets, companies offer more varieties and flavors. Or they branch out into other markets. Or they acquire other firms or products." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* That is why IBM can have $63 billion in revenues and lose $8 billion, and General Motors can have $133 billion in revenues and lose $23 billion. "While growth might be an admirable result of other initiatives, the pursuit of growth for its own sake is a serious strategic error. It's the major reason why so many American corporations have become unfocused." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)*

There are two distinct causes of unfocusing, both operating simultaneously. The first — diversification — has been widely discredited. Dozens of companies in the 1970s and 1980s went down the financial services or multi-industry route, including Sears, American Express, Xerox, Westinghouse Electric, and Chrysler. All announced eventual retreats. Lee Iacocca: "We didn't need a holding company... If we went astray — you know, people do go astray now and then in many areas — man, we got focused in a hurry." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)*

The second cause — line extension — is still active and still being vigorously pursued despite the evidence. "What makes the situation even worse is that most companies make a conscious effort to become unfocused. That sounds impossible, but it's not. We call this process 'line extension,' or in the immortal words of management consultants, 'extending the equity of the brand.'" *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* The maddening thing is that line extension can be a short-term winner. Management sees the quarterly sales increase and concludes the strategy is working. "If a bullet took five years to reach a target, very few criminals would be convicted of homicide." *(22 Laws, Ch. 11)* The damage is delayed. The connection to the cause is invisible. So the extension continues.

The CEO's job is to fight entropy actively and continuously. "Stars can't be focused, but companies can. That is the message of this book. The time has come to develop a company's power by narrowing its focus." *(Focus, Introduction)* Refocusing is not a special project — it is the permanent operating condition of good management. "I sometimes wonder if the people at the top of most big U.S. corporations are afflicted with attention deficit disorder," says consultant Barry Spiker. "They don't stay focused." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* That attention — maintained across years, against the constant institutional pressure to expand — is the rarest and most valuable executive quality.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose the entropy stage.** Ask: what did this company stand for at its peak competitive moment? What word did it own? Then compare that to what the company stands for today. The distance between those two answers is the measure of entropy. IBM at peak: mainframe computers, inevitable. IBM today: everything — which is nothing. The gap is not a strategy failure; it is entropy operating over decades.

2. **Trace the expansion decisions.** List every major product, service, or market the company entered in the last ten to fifteen years. For each, identify the stated justification at the time (distribution efficiency, manufacturing leverage, customer lifecycle, geography, pricing). "There are six different areas that seem to generate these line extension efforts: Distribution, Manufacturing, Marketing, Customer life cycles, Geography, Pricing." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* Each individually seemed logical. The cumulative effect was defocusing.

3. **Apply the A.1. test.** Ask about any extension: what word does the brand name mean to a stranger on the street? If the extension requires the brand name to mean something different from what it currently means, the extension is erasing the brand. "In a narrow sense, line extension is taking the brand name of a successful product (A.1. steak sauce) and putting it on a new product you plan to introduce (A.1. poultry sauce)." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* "Nobody replies: 'A.1. what?'" because A.1. is the steak sauce itself. Making it a poultry sauce does not add meaning — it removes it.

4. **Refocus through subtraction, not addition.** The path back is not "launch a new focused initiative" alongside the existing sprawl. It is "exit the categories that don't belong." General Electric sold hundreds of businesses and cut its workforce almost in half. "In the past decade, the company has sold hundreds of businesses and cut its workforce almost in half. General Electric has sold off major businesses like computers, television equipment, and small appliances." *(Focus, Introduction)* The spin-off is the antidote to the acquisition. "Corporate spin-offs are gathering momentum, fueled by investor pressure to unlock hidden values, compelling tax advantages, and evidence that stocks of both the parents and the new companies outperform the overall market." *(Focus, Divide and Conquer)*

5. **Build focus maintenance into the governance structure.** Because unfocusing is entropic and natural, it cannot be stopped by a single refocusing exercise. It must be resisted continuously. The mechanism is a leadership team that treats every proposed extension as a threat to the brand, not an opportunity for growth. The question is not "what can we sell with this brand name?" but "what will happen to this brand name if we put it on this?"

## Examples

**Situation:** General Motors dominated the U.S. automobile market from the 1930s to the 1970s with a tightly managed multistep focus: Chevrolet, Pontiac, Oldsmobile, Buick, Cadillac — each at a separate price point with no overlap.
**Application:** Over decades, without iron-fisted control from the top, each division drifted toward the high-volume middle of the market. Price ranges overlapped. What Sloan's original plan had kept apart, competitive pressure and growth-seeking management pushed together.
**Result:** By the 1990s, the highest-priced Chevrolet cost more than the cheapest Cadillac. "What's a Chevrolet? A large, small, cheap, expensive, domestic, imported car. In other words, a brand that has lost its focus. In the past decade, annual Chevrolet car sales in the United States declined 36 percent." *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* General Motors fell from 44 percent of the domestic market to 33 percent.

**Situation:** IBM when focused on mainframe computers: made a ton of money, was "inevitable" in the mind of every corporate buyer.
**Application:** Over decades: minicomputers, workstations, desktop computers, home computers (PCjr), copiers (sold to Kodak), Rolm telephone equipment (sold to Siemens), Satellite Business Systems (shut down), Prodigy network, SAA, TopView, OfficeVision, OS/2, and more.
**Result:** "IBM's revenues were $65 billion. Yet they wound up losing $2.8 billion. That's almost $8 million a day." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* The company that was inevitable in mainframes became a cautionary tale across computing. "What does IBM stand for? It used to stand for 'mainframe computers.' Today it stands for everything, which means it stands for nothing." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)*

**Situation:** Sears in the 1970s was dominant in hard goods. Management saw the brand as strong enough to extend into soft goods, fashion, financial services (Allstate, Dean Witter, Coldwell Banker, Discover card), and real estate.
**Application:** The logic was leverage — Sears had traffic, brand recognition, and distribution. Why not put all those assets to work across categories?
**Result:** "Why is Sears in trouble? Because the company tried to be all things to all people." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* Each extension made internal sense at the time. The cumulative effect was a brand that meant nothing in particular. Sears surrendered category leadership to focused specialists: Home Depot in hardware, Circuit City in electronics, The Limited in fashion. The brand is still in entropy.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat refocusing as a crisis measure.
**Why:** By the time a board calls in a turnaround expert to refocus, enormous damage has been done. Focus maintenance should be the CEO's daily operating discipline, not an emergency response. "The primary job of corporate management is to find the future... A focus is the future in the sense that it makes a prediction about where the future lies and then takes specific steps to make that future happen." *(Focus, Preface)* The fight against entropy is proactive, not reactive.

**Don't:** Blame external market forces for internal entropy.
**Why:** "What's a marketing person doing writing a management book?" *(Focus, Preface)* The consistent answer: most corporate problems that look like competition problems or technology problems are actually focus problems. GM did not lose 10 points of market share primarily because of Japanese quality. It lost market share because it destroyed the identities of its brands. "GM's problem wasn't a competitive problem, although competition did increase. It wasn't a quality problem either, although GM obviously wasn't delivering top-notch quality. It was very definitely a marketing problem." *(22 Laws, Introduction)* Own the unfocusing diagnosis.

**Don't:** Believe that "back to basics" announcements cure entropy.
**Why:** Companies announce "back to basics" regularly. They then proceed to add new products and markets within months, because the underlying growth pressure has not been resolved. Entropy is a structural feature of management incentive systems, not a momentary deviation. Unless the CEO's compensation is tied to focus (not size), the announcement will fade. The measurement system is the only governance mechanism that works over time.
