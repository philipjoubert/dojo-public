# The Law of Duality

## When to Use
- When evaluating the long-term prognosis for any brand that is not No. 1 or No. 2 in its category.
- When planning investment levels across a brand portfolio — the duality lens helps prioritize which brands to fund and which to manage for profitability while they still have it.
- When a No. 3 brand's management team is lulled by healthy current revenues into believing their position is stable.
- When deciding whether a company should fight for the No. 2 position or retreat into a defensible niche.

## Fails When
- **The market is still in early, rapid growth.** The law of duality is a long-run prediction. In a new category with many early entrants, third-place and fourth-place positions can be very profitable and quite stable — for now. The law describes where the market is going, not necessarily where it is today. Early in a category, customers are still figuring out the options. Over time they gravitate toward the leaders.
- **The brand finds a genuinely differentiated niche.** Royal Crown cola's error was not being third — it was continuing to compete as if it were a mainstream cola alternative. If Royal Crown had deployed the Law of Focus and carved out a specific niche (a demographic segment, a flavor variant, a regional stronghold), duality in the mainstream cola market would have been irrelevant to it. Duality governs the general market; guerrilla strategy can build a durable position in a specialist market within the same category.

## Core Concept

Law 8 of *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* states: "In the long run, every market becomes a two-horse race." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* The pattern is consistent across categories and decades. In batteries: Eveready and Duracell. In photographic film: Kodak and Fuji. In car rental: Hertz and Avis. In mouthwash: Listerine and Scope. In hamburgers: McDonald's and Burger King. In sneakers: Nike and Reebok. In toothpaste: Crest and Colgate. The specific brands are different. The structure is the same: two players who between them own the mental real estate of the category, with everyone else competing for the scraps.

The mechanism is not arbitrary. "Early on, a new category is a ladder of many rungs. Gradually, the ladder becomes a two-rung affair." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* In the early phase of a new category, customers are entering unfamiliar territory. They don't always know which brands are the leaders, so they experiment. Third-place, fourth-place brands get tried and can show impressive growth. This creates a false comfort. "As time goes on, however, these customers get educated. They want the leading brand, based on the naive assumption that the leading brand must be better." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* The prospect's mental model simplifies. Two brands define the category: the established original and the upstart alternative. Everything else becomes noise.

The historical evidence is explicit and brutal. The Coke-Pepsi-Royal Crown data from 1969 to 1991 illustrates the dynamic precisely. In 1969: Coke at 60 percent, Pepsi at 25 percent, Royal Crown at 6 percent. Twenty-two years later: Coke at 45 percent, Pepsi at 40 percent, Royal Crown at 3 percent. The leader lost share; No. 2 gained share; No. 3 was cut in half. *(22 Laws, Law 8)* Royal Crown tried to respond. In 1969 the company revitalized its franchise system, hired veteran Coke and Pepsi executives, retained a high-powered New York ad agency. Mary Wells Lawrence declared to the Royal Crown bottlers: "We're out to kill Coke and Pepsi. I hope you'll excuse the word, but we're really out for the jugular." The only brand that got killed was Royal Crown.

The automobile industry is the most complete historical demonstration. In 1904, 195 different cars were assembled by 60 companies. Within the following ten years, 531 companies were formed and 346 perished. By 1923, only 108 car makers remained; by 1927, 44. Today Ford and General Motors dominate the domestic industry, with Chrysler's future — already doubted by the time Ries and Trout were writing — a recurring concern. Five hundred and thirty-one entrants, and eventually the market converged toward two. *(22 Laws, Law 8)*

The same dynamic plays out in every category regardless of pace. Video games: Nintendo had 75 percent share in the late 1980s; two also-rans, Sega and NEC, competed for second. Nintendo and Sega ended up neck and neck. NEC fell away. The video game market played out in two or three seasons. Long-distance telephony might take two or three decades. The time frame varies; the end state does not. Knowing this, the strategic question for any No. 3 or below becomes: do we fight for second place, or do we find a niche small enough to own permanently?

Jack Welch said it clearly: "Only businesses that are No. 1 or No. 2 in their markets could win in the increasingly competitive global arena. Those that could not were fixed, closed, or sold." Procter & Gamble, in 32 of its 44 U.S. product categories, commands the No. 1 or No. 2 brand. This is not coincidence — it is a strategy built on awareness of duality. *(22 Laws, Law 8)*

## How to Apply

1. **Determine honestly where you stand on the ladder.** Not where you would like to stand, and not where you stand on some carefully chosen metric. Where does the prospect put you when asked to name the brands in your category? If you are not No. 1 or No. 2, duality predicts your position will erode over time in the general market.

2. **If you are No. 2 and have the resources, fight hard for the position.** No. 2 is sustainable; No. 3 typically is not. "Knowing that marketing is a two-horse race in the long run can help you plan strategy in the short run." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* The window to establish a durable No. 2 position is when the category is still developing. Use the Law of the Opposite — position against the leader, not alongside it. Build the challenger brand identity while the category still has room for it.

3. **If you are No. 3 or below, do not try to fight the leaders at the general market level.** Royal Crown tried to kill Coke and Pepsi. Royal Crown was killed instead. The right move for a No. 3 is to find and lock down a specific segment — geographic, demographic, product-form — where duality in the general market is irrelevant. Become the leader in a smaller pond before the two-horse race in the big pond makes your position untenable.

4. **If you are No. 1, do not become complacent.** Duality is also a warning for leaders. The No. 1 that fails to play active defensive warfare — attacking itself, blocking competitive moves — creates the conditions for the upstart's rise. Every leader that ended up sharing a duopoly with an aggressive No. 2 had a period when the No. 2 was small enough to be ignored. They ignored it too long.

5. **Recognize when a category is still early enough that position is not yet locked.** In laptop computers at the time Ries and Trout were writing, Toshiba held 21 percent and five companies each held 8–10 percent. The law of duality predicted that most of those 130 laptop brands would not exist in the next century. The question was which one of the five close No. 2 contenders would break away. "It ought to be fun to watch six horses come around a turn where there's room for only two." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* Resources invested in second place during the consolidation phase produce compounding returns once the field narrows. Resources invested in third or fourth place during that same phase are largely wasted.

## Examples

**Situation:** The cola market has three major players — Coke (60%), Pepsi (25%), Royal Crown (6%) — and Royal Crown is growing.
**Application:** The Law of Duality predicts this structure is unstable. The leader will lose share; No. 2 will gain; No. 3 will be squeezed. Royal Crown's response — new management, new agency, declared war on both leaders — is the wrong strategy for a No. 3 in a maturing category.
**Result:** Twenty-two years later: Coke at 45%, Pepsi at 40%, Royal Crown at 3%. Royal Crown's share was cut in half despite a well-funded competitive effort. The Law of Duality is not a prediction about a company's effort; it is a prediction about the structure of customer minds. *(22 Laws, Law 8)*

**Situation:** The American automobile industry in 1904 has 195 cars assembled by 60 companies.
**Application:** The Law of Duality operates over decades in complex industries. Watch the consolidation curve: 531 companies formed and 346 perished in the following decade; 108 remaining by 1923; 44 by 1927. The market converged.
**Result:** Ford and General Motors dominate the domestic market. Chrysler holds an uncertain third position. Over 500 entrants, and the end state is functionally a two-horse race with a distant third. *(22 Laws, Law 8)*

**Situation:** The long-distance telephone market has AT&T (65%), MCI (17%), and Sprint (10%).
**Application:** Duality predicts MCI has already won the battle for No. 2 — the contest between MCI and Sprint is over. The future contest is between AT&T (old, established, universal) and MCI (upstart alternative). Sprint's 10 percent feels comfortable — $6 billion in annual sales — but the law predicts erosion. Sprint is Royal Crown.
**Result:** The analysis holds: Sprint faced persistent structural pressure, and the long-distance market evolved toward a concentrated duopoly. *(22 Laws, Law 8)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Mistake current profitability at third place for long-term security.
**Why:** "Sprint is probably feeling very comfortable on the third rung of the ladder. Nine percent doesn't sound like much, but it translates to $6 billion in annual sales." *(22 Laws, Law 8)* Revenue is a lagging indicator. The mental position is already eroding while the revenue line still looks healthy. By the time profitability shows the erosion, the opportunity to respond has often closed.

**Don't:** Try to attack both leaders simultaneously from third place.
**Why:** Mary Wells Lawrence declared Royal Crown would go "for the jugular" against both Coke and Pepsi. The market had already decided who those two brands were. A No. 3 attacking two established leaders is dispersing force in every direction — the mathematical opposite of what Clausewitz recommended for the weaker force. Concentrate. Find a niche, own it, defend it.

**Don't:** Use duality as an argument for line extension or brand consolidation.
**Why:** The common misreading of duality is that "we need to be in everything to be one of the two survivors." General Motors, IBM, and Volkswagen all fell into this trap. The lesson of duality is the opposite: pick your position on the two-rung ladder and concentrate on it. The company that line-extends to "cover" more of the market usually ends up owning less of any specific position in the mind. Duality is a law about mental real estate, not product catalog breadth.

**Don't:** Interpret duality as inevitable defeat for the leader.
**Why:** The law says No. 1 will typically give up some share as No. 2 strengthens. It does not say No. 1 will lose. Coke went from 60 percent to 45 percent while Pepsi went from 25 percent to 40 percent — but Coke remained the leader. The defensive warfare playbook exists precisely to help leaders maintain No. 1 through the period when No. 2 is growing strongest. Duality is not a death sentence for the leader; it is a prediction about the long-run structure that good defensive strategy can navigate.
