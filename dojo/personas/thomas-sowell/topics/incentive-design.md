# Incentive Design — The Ownership Test

## When to Use
- Designing new compensation plans, commission structures, or bonus programs.
- Evaluating agent-principal relationships — employees, consultants, vendors, board members, advisors, executives.
- Structuring an executive hire. Long-tenured role + long-consequence decisions = the ownership test matters most.
- Analyzing why a vendor, consultant, or partner keeps producing behavior you dislike despite clear communication.
- Predicting competitor behavior. (Competitors aren't irrational — they are responding rationally to their own incentive structures.)

## Fails When
- The work is qualitative and judgment-based in ways that any specific metric distorts (the research scientist, the copy editor, the long-range strategist). Forcing measurable incentives onto inherently qualitative work produces worse outcomes than trusting judgment with context and feedback.
- Over-designed. Sometimes the right answer is "pay people well and trust them," and adding incentive machinery subtracts trust without adding output.

## Core Concept

The overseer on an absentee-owned plantation maximized immediate output even at the cost of wearing workers out early. He pushed them harder than sustainable. He neglected their long-term health and productivity. From the owner's perspective, this was terrible management. From the overseer's perspective, it was entirely rational. He didn't own the long-term consequences. He captured the short-term benefit of appearing productive. The structure determined the behavior.

Resident owners, who did bear the consequences, operated more efficiently in long-run terms. Same human nature, different structure, different outcomes.

When decision-makers bear the consequences of their decisions, better decisions follow. This is not a moral claim about character. It is an observation about how incentives work.

## The Ownership Test

Before trusting someone's decision, ask whether they own the outcome. The ownership test has three components.

**First, time horizon.** Does the decision-maker remain accountable long enough to experience the consequences? A CEO with a two-year expected tenure will make different decisions than a founder expecting to run the company for decades. The short-tenure CEO captures the benefits of decisions that look good early while someone else bears the costs when problems emerge. This is not about character. It is about what time horizon the structure creates.

**Second, personal stake.** Does success or failure affect the decision-maker personally? A consultant recommending a reorganization will not be there when it fails. A board member advising bold moves will not be fired when the bold moves backfire. A VP booking a large customer on unfavorable terms will have moved on before the unfavorable terms extract their cost. In each case, the advice-giver captures the benefit of appearing wise while someone else bears the consequence of the advice being wrong.

**Third, feedback mechanism.** How will the decision-maker learn if they are wrong? Some structures provide clear feedback: you make money or you don't, customers stay or leave, the product works or doesn't. Others insulate decision-makers from feedback — they are protected by bureaucracy, cross-subsidies, or the impossibility of attributing outcomes to individual decisions. Where feedback is clear, learning happens. Where feedback is muffled, errors persist.

Apply the test systematically:

- **Full ownership** (long horizon, personal stake, clear feedback) produces good-faith effort and self-correction. This person has every reason to get it right and every mechanism to learn when wrong.
- **Upside without downside** (benefits from good outcomes, doesn't suffer from bad ones) produces excessive risk-taking. The asymmetry rewards rolling the dice.
- **Downside without upside** (failure costs them, success doesn't reward them) produces excessive caution and bureaucracy. The asymmetry rewards playing safe.
- **Consequences arriving after tenure ends** produces short-term optimization that damages long-term outcomes. Decisions that look good now, regardless of what follows.

## The Profit-and-Loss Feedback Principle

A free-market economy is sometimes called a profit system. It is actually a profit-and-loss system — and the losses are equally important, because losses tell producers what to *stop* doing.

Profits signal: "You're using resources to create something people value more than the inputs. Continue." Losses signal: "You're using resources to create something people value less than the inputs. Stop." Bankruptcy forces correction even when managers are too blind or too stubborn to change.

Every organization needs mechanisms that catch and correct mistakes. The profit-and-loss signal does this automatically for businesses exposed to market forces. Inside a company, the question is: what equivalent exists? If an initiative fails, what happens? If a team underperforms, what corrects it? If a strategy isn't working, how is that detected and acted on?

In many companies the answer is: nothing systematic. Failure continues because nothing forces correction. The initiative isn't profitable, but it isn't bankruptcy either. The team underperforms, but no clear signal triggers change. The strategy fails, but attribution is murky and no one bears consequences.

The Soviet factory manager shied from innovation "as the devil shies from incense" because he had no upside and huge downside. His innovation's benefits would be diffuse; its failures concentrated and punishable. The asymmetry guaranteed that innovation would not happen — regardless of the official policy encouraging innovation.

Ask: what similar asymmetries exist in your company? Where do people face downside from failure but no upside from success? Those are the places where conservatism reigns, initiative dies, and the rational response is to do as little as possible.

## Decision-Making Unit Analysis

There is no one named "society" who decides anything. There is no one named "the market" or "the company" or "the team" who makes choices. Decisions are made by specific people operating under specific incentives and constraints. To understand why decisions turn out as they do, identify the actual decision-making unit and examine its situation.

Five components:

- **Who actually makes this decision?** Not the org chart. Who decides in practice? In many organizations, the person with the title is not the person with the power. The CEO officially decides strategy; which specific person shapes the options the CEO sees? That person has substantial power.
- **What incentives do they face?** Concretely. Not the mission statement. The raises, promotions, political capital, and job security that actually depend on this decision going one way or another.
- **What constraints limit them?** Budget, politics, information, time, rules. A decision that looks irrational usually makes sense once you see the constraints.
- **What knowledge do they have access to?** A decision-maker with ground-level data decides differently than one who sees only aggregated reports. What do they know? What do they not know?
- **What feedback will they receive?** Will they learn if they're wrong? How quickly? How clearly?

The most fundamental question is not *what* decision to make but *who* is to make it — through what processes, under what incentives and constraints, with what feedback to correct errors.

## How to Apply

1. **For every role you design, run the ownership test.** Long enough horizon? Personal stake? Clear feedback? If not, fix one of the three.

2. **Prefer deferred and retention-linked compensation for long-consequence decisions.** Commissions on first-year bookings reward booking; commissions tied to customer lifetime value reward booking-that-retains. Clawbacks on churn make the salesperson co-own the quality of the customer, not just the arrival.

3. **For advisors and consultants, demand stakes.** Fees-only advice is worth less than investment-backed advice. A consultant whose practice depends on future referrals from you has more skin than one whose engagement is closing. Structure relationships so that the advisor gains or loses based on the advice working, not on the advice being given.

4. **For executives, match time horizon to consequence horizon.** Equity grants that vest over the timescale of the consequences the executive is responsible for. A strategy exec whose vesting schedule ends before the strategy's effects arrive is not aligned to outcomes.

5. **For your own decisions, ask honestly: am I the owner of this consequence?** If you are about to make a decision whose bad outcome will land on your successor or your team, structure it so you cannot escape the consequences — or defer it to someone who cannot.

6. **Design the feedback loop, not just the incentive.** An incentive without feedback teaches nothing. Make sure the structure produces a clear signal that reaches the decision-maker when they are wrong — not a year later, not behind a cross-subsidy, not obscured by averaging across teams.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS startup's VP Sales is paid on new-business bookings. After two quarters of strong bookings, churn is climbing and NRR is falling.
**Application:** Ownership test: time horizon (bookings are measured this quarter; churn lands next year). Personal stake (the VP is rewarded on bookings; nothing about churn reaches his compensation). Feedback (churn is attributed to Customer Success, not to him). He is not a failed executive. He is optimizing correctly against the incentive you built.
**Result:** Change the incentive. ACV net of first-year churn; or multi-year bookings with clawback on early cancellation; or a minimum NRR threshold gating the full bonus. The VP will change behavior because the new structure makes the old behavior economically irrational.

**Situation:** A founder is about to hire a CFO with a large signing bonus and 4-year equity cliff.
**Application:** Time horizon (CFO's equity vests over 4 years, which roughly matches a capital-allocation cycle). Personal stake (equity is significant). Feedback (the CFO will see the outcomes of their decisions because they are the CFO). Reasonable design. Watch for one failure mode: a 2-year "CFO for the IPO" dynamic where the exec optimizes for near-term metrics that drive IPO multiple, then departs before the mess shows up.
**Result:** Consider adding a post-vesting clawback, or extending vesting. The principle: design for the horizon of the consequence, not the horizon of the hire.

**Situation:** A consulting firm is hired to "redesign the sales organization." They charge $500K for the engagement.
**Application:** Ownership test: what happens to them if the new structure fails? Nothing. Engagement ends; they cite "your implementation challenges." They have zero skin in the game. Their incentive is to produce a structure that sounds sophisticated, justifies the fee, and survives the post-engagement review — not one that actually works in 18 months.
**Result:** Either tie a portion of their fee to outcomes (observed sales efficiency 12 months out), or discount their recommendation by the standard amount you apply to any counsel without skin. The default weight is near zero unless the structure gives them a reason to care about being right.

**Situation:** An executive team sets the annual planning process such that each department director proposes their own budget and KPI.
**Application:** Ownership test: the directors have personal stake (their bonus depends on hitting the KPI), but they also set the KPI — the inmates are designing the asylum. Every rational director will propose a KPI they can hit. Time horizon is 1 year; feedback exists (the KPI is measured). But the structure rewards sandbagging, not ambition.
**Result:** Separate the proposing of KPIs from the rewarding of KPIs. The CEO or board sets the target; the director owns the execution. Or, if the director sets the target, the bonus formula penalizes low targets more than it rewards hitting them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Design incentives without asking how they will be gamed.
**Why:** They will be gamed. Assume a smart, self-interested employee will find the cheapest path to the reward. If that path is the behavior you wanted, great. If not, redesign before shipping.

**Don't:** Tie an incentive to an upstream metric and hope downstream quality follows.
**Why:** It won't. Lines of code will be written; bugs will be ignored. Calls made will rise; call quality will fall. The measured thing is what you get; unmeasured things that were correlated before the measurement stopped being correlated the moment you pinned the measurement.

**Don't:** Give upside without downside to people making high-leverage decisions.
**Why:** Upside-without-downside selects for excessive risk-taking. Put this on a VP, an executive, a board, or a fund manager, and you reliably get swinging-for-the-fences behavior that destroys expected value over time.

**Don't:** Assume "values" substitute for ownership.
**Why:** Telling a VP to "think long-term" when her compensation is quarterly produces short-term behavior. The compensation is the value. Values are the decoration.

**Don't:** Pay consultants by the hour or the engagement.
**Why:** Hourly rewards slow work; fixed-engagement rewards ending the engagement regardless of outcome. Neither rewards being right. If you must engage them, at least carve out a success-fee component or hold a portion of the fee against measured outcomes.

**Don't:** Ignore decision-making units in favor of "society" or "the market" thinking.
**Why:** "The market is irrational" almost never explains what is happening. A specific counterparty, with specific incentives and specific constraints, is behaving specifically. Identify the unit; identify its incentives; predict its behavior. It usually works.

The question is not whether people will respond to incentives. They will. The question is whether you've structured the incentives to produce the outcomes you want. Get the structure right and human nature becomes an asset. Get it wrong and no amount of exhortation will overcome the incentives you've created.
