# Fear and Confidence

## When to Use

- A founder is staring at a blank document, unable to start.
- A founder keeps drafting and deleting the same opening paragraph, never getting past sentence three.
- A founder is overdue on the all-hands email / board update / fundraising memo and keeps finding reasons to delay.
- A founder is using "professional tone" as the reason they cannot write the way they actually talk.
- A founder is about to write something and opens with "I'm not really a writer, but..."
- A founder is editing in real time as they generate, producing one polished sentence per ten minutes.
- A founder is reaching for elevated language ("transformational," "unprecedented," "remarkable") because the subject feels too serious for plain words.
- A founder is afraid to commit to a position in writing because the position might turn out to be wrong.

## Fails When

- **The problem is technical, not psychological.** Sometimes a founder cannot finish a piece because they don't yet know what they think — that's a writing-to-learn problem, not a fear problem.
- **The user mistakes "be yourself" for "be combative."** Confidence in writing is not aggression. Founders who hear "be confident" and start writing belligerent prose are misreading the principle.
- **The user uses "be authentic" to skip craft.** Authenticity without rewriting is rambling. The fearless first draft still needs revision; courage doesn't replace work.
- **The fear is justified by genuine high-consequence risk.** Some pieces — the regulatory disclosure, the legally-sensitive memo — *should* be over-cautious. Don't urge boldness where the cost of being wrong is real.

## Core Concept

Most founder writing problems are not really writing problems. They are fear problems wearing writing-shaped costumes. The blank document is fear. The four-hour rewrite of the opening paragraph is fear. The reach for elevated language is fear. The "we" that should have been "I" is fear. The "professional tone" that strips voice from the page is fear. The four-day delay on the obvious email is fear. Founders, by the nature of their job, write things that other people will judge them on, often with consequences. The pen does not know this; the founder does, and freezes.

*Fear of writing gets planted in most Americans at an early age, usually at school, and it never entirely goes away. The blank piece of paper or the blank computer screen, waiting to be filled with our wonderful words, can freeze us into not writing any words at all, or writing words that are less than wonderful.*

*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*

The fear has specific shapes for founders. The fear of committing to a position in writing that might later prove wrong. The fear of sounding less smart than the audience expects. The fear of being mocked for trying to sound smart. The fear that "I" is egotistical and "we" is corporate. The fear of being too informal and the fear of being too stiff. The fear, when writing about a hard subject, that the prose has to rise to match the subject's gravity. Each of these fears produces a specific bad writing pattern, and each pattern is the fear made visible.

The first move is to recognise that the fear is universal. *Probably the biggest fear for nonfiction writers is the fear of not being able to bring off their assignment. They are infinitely accountable: to the facts, to the people they interviewed, to the locale of their story and to the events that happened there. They are also accountable to their craft and all its perils of excess and disorder: losing the reader, confusing the reader, boring the reader.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) Founders should know they are not alone in the freeze. The freeze is a sign they are taking the writing seriously. The cost is not that they feel afraid; the cost is that they let the fear edit the page.

The second move is to write about subjects you actually care about. *One way to generate confidence is to write about subjects that interest you and that you care about. Writers who write interestingly tend to be men and women who keep themselves interested. If you write about subjects you think you would enjoy knowing about, your enjoyment will show in what you write.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) For founders, this maps to: write about your actual company, your actual customers, your actual decisions — in the language you actually use. The minute the founder starts writing about "the future of work" or "the SaaS market" in generic terms, they have abandoned the territory where they have authority and credibility, and the fear takes the wheel because the founder is now performing in a domain they don't own.

The third move is the credential. Founders, walking into territory adjacent to their expertise, often feel they don't have the right to write. *I felt that anxiety every time I embark on a new project. I felt it when I went to Bradenton to write my baseball book. Although I've been a baseball fan all my life, I had never done any sports reporting, never interviewed a professional athlete. Strictly, I had no credentials; any of the men I approached with my notebook — managers, coaches, players, umpires, scouts — could have asked, "What else have you written about baseball?" But nobody did. They didn't ask because I had another kind of credential: sincerity. It was obvious to those men that I really wanted to know how they did their work. Remember this when you enter new territory and need a shot of confidence. Your best credential is yourself.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) For a founder, this is the answer to "who am I to write a position piece on industry X?" — sincerity is a credential. Specificity is a credential. Showing up is a credential. Most public writing in any field is by people without traditional credentials; what the credentialed people do better is the work, not the having of the credentials.

The fourth move is the dumb question. *Trust your common sense to figure out what you need to know, and don't be afraid to ask a dumb question. If the expert thinks you're dumb, that's his problem.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) For a founder writing into a new domain — the new market they're entering, the new technology they need to explain, the new audience they're addressing — the fear of looking ignorant produces the over-hedged, jargon-heavy draft that is hardest to read. The fearless version of the same draft asks the question the reader is also asking, in the words the reader would also use, and produces something readable and credible.

The fifth move is the active counterforce: enjoyment. Zinsser is unusual among writers in placing enjoyment at the centre of the practice. *Writing is such lonely work that I try to keep myself cheered up. If something strikes me as funny in the act of writing, I throw it in just to amuse myself. If I think it's funny I assume a few other people will find it funny, and that seems to me to be a good day's work.* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) For founders who write professionally — board updates, all-hands emails, investor letters, recruiting messages — the months and years stack up. The founder who has been miserable while writing every one of these will have produced miserable documents. The founder who finds their own way to enjoy the writing — the small joke, the specific image, the particular fact they like — produces work that is read because the joy is on the page.

There is a related point about audacity. *S. J. Perelman said: "It takes audacity and exuberance and gaiety, and the most important one is audacity." Then he said: "The reader has to feel that the writer is feeling good." That sentence stated the entire case for enjoyment. Then he added: "Even if he isn't."* (*— On Writing Well, "Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence"*) The reader of the founder's writing is reading for two things: information and a sense of the writer's relationship to the material. If the writer feels alive on the page, the reader registers it. If the writer is going through the motions, the reader registers that too. *Writers have to jump-start themselves at the moment of performance, no less than actors and dancers and painters and musicians. You also have to turn on the switch. Nobody is going to do it for you.*

The deepest move is the willingness to be visible. The most common form of founder fear is the impulse to disappear behind the institution. Use "we" instead of "I." Use the corporate voice. Strip the personal. Hide the opinion behind cited facts. The result is the document that no specific human being is responsible for and that no specific human being will believe. *Still, we have become a society fearful of revealing who we are. The institutions that seek our support by sending us their brochures sound remarkably alike, though surely all of them were founded and are still sustained by men and women with different dreams and visions. Where are these people? It's hard to glimpse them among all the impersonal passive sentences that say "initiatives were undertaken" and "priorities have been identified."* (*— On Writing Well, ch. 4*) The founder who writes from the institution is hiding. The founder who writes as themselves — and is willing to be wrong, willing to be quoted, willing to be remembered for specific words — is taking the risk that produces credibility.

There is one more discipline. *Get on the plane.* (*— On Writing Well, "A Writer's Decisions"*) Zinsser's blanket rule for nonfiction work was: when in doubt, go. Take the trip. Make the visit. Show up at the place. Most of the writers who freeze in their offices freeze because they don't have enough specific material — and they don't have enough specific material because they didn't go where the material was. For founders this maps directly: when stuck on a customer-facing piece, talk to a customer. When stuck on a recruiting piece, sit with the team. When stuck on a market piece, go to where the market lives. The fear of the blank page is often the residue of having tried to write from second-hand information. The cure is first-hand experience. *Decide what you want to do. Then decide to do it. Then do it.*

## How to Apply

1. **Write a deliberately bad first draft, fast.** The blank page is the freeze. A bad page is unblocked. The internal editor is not allowed to participate in the first draft. Get the words on the page in any state. *I'm often dismayed by the sludge I see appearing on my screen if I approach writing as a task — the day's work — and not with some enjoyment. My only consolation is that I'll get another shot at those dismal sentences tomorrow and the next day and the day after.*

2. **Allow yourself to be visible.** Use "I." Commit to opinions. Take positions you can be wrong about. The founder who refuses to be specific about what they think is read as untrustworthy by everyone — and is also bored, because they have not allowed themselves to actually say anything.

3. **Don't pre-respond to imagined critics.** Most founder drafts are full of preemptive defences against critics who haven't read the piece yet. "Some may argue that..." "It is important to note that..." "While there are those who feel that..." Cut all of them. Make your claim. The critics will say what they say; you cannot rebut them in advance and the attempt makes the writing weaker.

4. **Refuse the elevated register.** When the subject is grave, the language should be plain. The eulogy that uses small concrete words is more powerful than the eulogy that uses large abstract ones. The layoff email that says "I'm sorry" is more powerful than the one that says "we deeply regret the impact this transition will have." Reach for the small word, especially when the subject is large.

5. **Find the joke.** *If something strikes me as funny in the act of writing, I throw it in just to amuse myself.* For founders this is unfashionable advice; "professional" board updates and investor letters are usually humourless. But the dry wit, the specific affectionate detail, the small dropped joke — these signal a person on the page. They are also the lines readers remember. Don't leave humour out as a gesture of seriousness.

6. **Lean on sincerity as credential.** When writing into adjacent territory, do not pretend to expertise you don't have. Show the work you did. Cite the customers you talked to. Acknowledge the limits of your perspective. Sincerity is read as more credible than fake authority every time.

7. **Get on the plane.** When stuck, go to the source. The customer call you've been putting off. The team meeting you've been observing remotely. The conference you've been skipping. The competitor's product you haven't tried. Most writing-stuck-ness is research-stuck-ness in disguise.

8. **Write toward the question, not away from it.** When a draft starts to feel evasive, the founder is usually circling around the actual claim they don't want to commit to. Identify the claim. Write the sentence. The fear lives in the gap between knowing what you think and being willing to say it. Closing the gap is the whole game.

9. **Read the draft to one specific person you trust.** Not for feedback. For visibility. The act of reading aloud to someone — your co-founder, your partner, a trusted advisor — exposes the moments where you flinched and the moments where you reached for prose that wasn't yours. The other person doesn't need to comment; the act of reading does the work.

10. **Stop polishing as a stalling tactic.** Sometimes the founder is "almost done" with a piece for two weeks because finishing means sending. The piece is done. Send it. The cost of an imperfect document going out is much less than the cost of a perfect document not going out.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has been trying to write the first board update of their Series A for nine days. They keep starting and deleting. Each draft sounds wrong; either too casual or too stiff.

**Application:** The freeze is fear of the new audience. The fix is to write the draft to one specific board member they trust most — a single human, not "the board." The piece written to one person almost always reads better to a group than the piece written to a group. Set a 60-minute timer. Generate the draft in the voice the founder would use to email that one person. Edit later for any adjustments needed for wider audience. Most edits will be minor.

**Result:** Draft completed in 60 minutes. The founder, having broken the freeze, can now revise rather than create. The piece is sent two days later. Future board updates take half the time.

**Situation:** A founder is writing the all-hands email about a major strategic shift. The current draft uses "we" throughout, sounds like marketing, and avoids any first-person account of why the founder personally believes the shift is right.

**Application:** Replace every "we have decided" with "I have decided." Add a paragraph about why the founder personally arrived at the decision — what changed in the last 90 days, what conversation tipped it, what they are betting on. The founder will resist this; "I" feels exposed. Exposure is the point. The team is being asked to follow the founder through a strategic shift; they need to know who is leading them and what they think.

**Result:** Employees register the email as a real communication, not a corporate announcement. The questions that come back in the all-hands meeting are substantive ones. The next strategic shift, when it comes, is easier to communicate because the founder has built the muscle of speaking as themselves.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a public blog post about their views on a contested industry issue. They have written four drafts, each more hedged than the last. The current draft has so many "some might argue" and "it could be said that" qualifiers that it commits to no position.

**Application:** Identify the actual claim. Write the sentence. Defend it cleanly. The fear of being wrong is producing prose that is unreadable and uncite-able. Better to be wrong about a clear claim — and revise publicly later — than to be unreadably hedged about a fuzzy one.

**Result:** The post is shared and quoted. People disagree with the founder, in some cases publicly, which the founder feared. The disagreement turns out to be useful; it produces clarifying conversations. The hedged version would have been read by no one; the clear version is read by everyone in the relevant industry within a week.

**Situation:** A founder needs to write the post-acquisition email to customers. The draft is full of corporate language — "this exciting new chapter," "expanded capabilities," "deepened commitment to our customers." It sounds like a press release.

**Application:** Throw it out. The founder has been gripped by the fear that an acquisition email must "sound corporate." It must not. It must sound like the founder. Rewrite: "We sold the company yesterday. Here's what changes for you, here's what doesn't, and here's what I'm doing next. The short version: nothing changes for the next year. The longer version is below. If anything in this email worries you, please reply — I'll read every one."

**Result:** Customers respond with appreciation rather than anxiety. The acquirer's PR team grumbles about the founder's tone, then adopts it for their own customer communications. The original draft would have generated three weeks of angry support tickets; the rewrite generates two weeks of warmth.

**Situation:** A founder has to write the eulogy at a former employee's memorial service. They have been drafting for two days and reaching for elevated language they would never use in any other context.

**Application:** Plain words. Specific memories. The colleague's actual habits, actual sayings, actual small recurring jokes. *Reach for fresh imagery and avoid phrases that are trite. Writing that will endure tends to consist of words that are short and strong.* (*— On Writing Well, "The Sound of Your Voice"*) The eulogy is not the place to reach for "her mirth illuminated all who encountered her." It is the place to say "She used to send me an email at 5pm every Friday with one specific complaint about the product, and I will miss them."

**Result:** The eulogy lands. The colleague's family thanks the founder afterwards for capturing what was specifically the colleague rather than what is generically said about anyone who has died. The grand version would have been forgettable; the specific version is the one that gets shared.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Disclaim your writing ability before the piece begins.
**Why:** "I'm not a writer, but..." is an evasion. The reader is going to read the piece either way; the disclaimer is the writer asking for forgiveness in advance. Cut it. Just write the piece. If it's good, no disclaimer is needed; if it's bad, no disclaimer will save it.

**Don't:** Use "we" because "I" feels arrogant.
**Why:** The "we" that hides the founder is read as evasion, not modesty. The most successful founder writing is unmistakably first-person. The fear that "I" is egotistical is the same fear that produces the homogenised corporate communications no one believes.

**Don't:** Rise to the gravity of the subject with elevated language.
**Why:** Solemn subjects deserve plain language. Reaching for "remarkable" and "transformational" is an attempt to dignify the subject through prose. It does not work. The reader registers the inflation as a sign that the writer was nervous about the subject.

**Don't:** Pre-respond to imagined critics.
**Why:** The "some may argue" paragraph is the writer wrestling with phantoms. Critics, when they appear, will be specific people with specific objections. You cannot anticipate them; the attempt produces a defensive posture that weakens the original argument.

**Don't:** Confuse "be yourself" with "skip craft."
**Why:** The fearless first draft still needs to be cut, restructured, polished. Authenticity is the starting condition; craft is what turns the authentic draft into a finished piece. Founders who hear "be yourself" and stop revising produce ramble.

**Don't:** Hide behind the language of the audience.
**Why:** Writing to investors in investor jargon, to engineers in engineer jargon, to customers in customer jargon — these are forms of fear, not adaptation. The voice that translates across audiences is the writer's own voice. Train it; do not switch it on every audience.

**Don't:** Wait for confidence to arrive before you write.
**Why:** Confidence is a result, not a prerequisite. The founder who is "waiting until I feel more sure" before sending the email will wait forever. The act of writing produces what writing produces — including a clearer view of what you think — and confidence is downstream of that. Send the draft.

**Don't:** Use polish as a stalling mechanism.
**Why:** When a piece has been "almost ready" for three weeks, the holding pattern is fear, not craft. The founder is afraid to send. Sending is the cure. Set a deadline; meet it; ship.

**Don't:** Outsource your writing to escape the fear.
**Why:** The founder who hands the all-hands email to the chief of staff to draft has not solved the writing problem; they have outsourced the thinking that should have happened in the writing. The result is a fluent communication that the team correctly perceives as not-the-founder. The fear had a job to do — it was forcing the founder to think — and the outsource bypasses the work.
