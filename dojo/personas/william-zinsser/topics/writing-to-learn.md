# Writing to Learn

## When to Use

- A founder is stuck on a strategic question that has resisted weeks of meetings — pricing, positioning, a re-org, a partnership decision, whether to pivot.
- A founder says, with frustration, "I know what I think; I just can't say it."
- A founder is preparing for a hard conversation — a co-founder breakup, a layoff conversation, a board confrontation — and is rehearsing in their head.
- A founder has gathered information (customer interviews, competitor analysis, financial models) and cannot synthesise it.
- A founder needs to explain something complicated to the team — a market shift, a technical decision, a strategy change — and has not yet written it down for themselves.
- A founder is in the same conversation for the fourth time without progress.
- A founder is preparing a board memo or investor letter and the act of drafting forces them to clarify what they actually believe.

## Fails When

- **The question is operational and well-defined.** "Should we use Stripe or Adyen?" is a question that wants research and a decision, not an essay. Writing about it is procrastination.
- **The founder writes to delay deciding.** Some founders use the discipline of writing as an excuse to defer commitments. The essay becomes the thing produced instead of the decision. Notice when "I want to think it through more on paper" is a way of not choosing.
- **The founder writes for an audience instead of for themselves.** As soon as the writing is for investors / employees / board, the function shifts from learning to performance. The clarifying property of writing is lost. Write the private memo first; convert to the public one later.
- **The founder is too tired or rushed to think clearly.** Writing under exhaustion produces fog, not clarity. The discipline assumes you can sustain attention for an hour.

## Core Concept

Most founders treat writing as the last step of the process — what you do once you've decided what to say. Zinsser, after a career as a writing teacher and a separate career as a writer of nonfiction across an unusually wide range of subjects, became convinced of the opposite: writing is what produces the thinking, not what records it. The act of putting one sentence after another, of being forced to commit to specific words in a specific order, is the discipline that turns half-formed beliefs into actual positions.

*Writing is a powerful search mechanism, and one of its satisfactions is to come to terms with your life narrative.*

*— On Writing Well, ch. 24*

The deeper claim, threaded through *Writing to Learn*, is that writing and thinking are not separable activities. *The act of writing and rewriting made them think clearly, organized their ideas, told them what they knew and what they still needed to know, and pushed them to new areas of knowledge.* (*— Writing to Learn, ch. 5*) When you sit down to write a thing, you are forced to make decisions you can avoid in conversation: what is the lead claim, what evidence supports it, in what order, with what qualifications. Speech tolerates fuzziness; writing exposes it. The board member who asks "what's the actual claim?" is the writing on the page asking the same question.

Zinsser describes the experience of discovering this on his own work: *Something happened when I actually started to write. The book took on a life of its own and told me how it wanted to be written. I found myself yanked back to many corners of my past — to long-forgotten people and projects and travels that together taught me much of what I know. The writing of the book proved one of its central points: that we write to find out what we know and what we want to say. I thought of how often as a writer I had made clear to myself some subject I had previously known nothing about by just putting one sentence after another — by reasoning my way in sequential steps to its meaning. I thought of how often the act of writing even the simplest document — a letter, for instance — had clarified my half-formed ideas. Writing and thinking and learning were the same process.* (*— Writing to Learn, Preface*)

For a founder, the implication is operational. The strategic question that has resisted four meetings, six Slack threads, and three whiteboard sessions has not resisted because it is hard. It has resisted because the founder has not yet written about it. Conversation lets you maintain three contradictory positions; writing lets you maintain one. The Sunday morning when you finally sit down and try to write a memo arguing for the pivot is the morning you discover that you actually believe the pivot — or that you discover, fifteen hundred words in, that you were arguing yourself into something you don't believe and the whole question reverses.

This is not a metaphor. It is mechanical. *I had made clear to myself some subject I had previously known nothing about by just putting one sentence after another — by reasoning my way in sequential steps to its meaning.* (*— Writing to Learn, Preface*) Sentence one commits to a claim. Sentence two has to follow. By sentence twenty, you have either constructed an argument that holds together — in which case you now know what you think — or you have hit a contradiction so unavoidable that you have to revise the original claim. Either outcome is progress. Both are unavailable to you in your head.

There is a corollary about teaching. When a founder needs to explain something complicated to the team — a market shift, a strategic pivot, a technical bet — the right path is almost always to write it for themselves first. Not the all-hands deck. The private memo. *We write to find out what we know and what we want to say.* (*— Writing to Learn, Preface*) The deck made before the memo is almost always wrong because the deck is a performance and the memo is the thinking. Founders who skip the memo step give all-hands talks that are charisma without content. Founders who do the memo first give talks that the team remembers because the talks are about something.

There is a discipline here that Zinsser distinguishes from "exploratory" writing — the journal-style free-flow that some teachers favour for self-discovery. *Type A writing — informational writing — is what most people need to get through the day. Its sole purpose is to inform; it has no deeper content that the writer will discover in the act of writing. Type B writing — exploratory writing — is a voyage of discovery into the self. Only by going into uncharted territory can a writer find his potential and his voice and his meaning. Meaning, in fact, doesn't exist until a writer goes looking for it.* (*— Writing to Learn, ch. 4*) Founders need both. The board update is Type A — you know what you think; the work is conveying it cleanly. The strategic pivot memo is Type B — you don't yet know what you think; the work is finding out.

Confusing the two is a common founder error. They sit down to write a strategic memo as if it were a board update — assuming they already know the conclusion — and produce a piece of writing that is competent and shallow. They learned nothing by writing it because they refused to let the writing do the learning. The fix is to begin the strategic memo with a question instead of an answer. "Should we move upmarket?" not "We are moving upmarket because…" The question lets the writing find what you actually believe. The premature answer locks it out.

Zinsser observed this most clearly in education — students who couldn't yet think their way through chemistry or physics turning out to be able to think their way through it the moment they were forced to write about it. *An act of writing is an act of thinking — an organic compound, as the chemists would say. There's little point in having a teacher clean up the messy syntax in a chemistry paper if he can't also clean up the messy chemistry. The indivisibility of language is what gives writing its authority and its majesty.* (*— Writing to Learn, ch. 4*) For a founder, "messy syntax" maps to "I can't write this paragraph cleanly," and "messy chemistry" maps to "I haven't yet decided what I think about the underlying question." The two are the same problem.

There is one final point. Writing exposes you to the people who read it. The founder who writes their own memos has nowhere to hide. The founder who delegates them to the chief of staff has lost the most efficient mechanism available for figuring out what they actually believe — and has acquired a series of memos that read fluently and commit to nothing. Founders should write their own important documents not because the writing will be better (it usually will, but that's secondary). They should write them because the act of writing is the act of thinking, and the thinking is what they are paid for.

## How to Apply

1. **When stuck on a strategic question, write a memo.** Not a deck. Not bullet points. A memo, in prose, with sentences that follow each other in order. The format matters because prose forces the kind of commitment bullet points evade.

2. **Begin with the question, not the answer.** Title the memo with the question you're trying to resolve: "Should we move upmarket in 2026?" Then write your way to an answer. If you start with the answer, you'll write a defence rather than a discovery. The discovery is the value.

3. **Write for an audience of one — yourself.** This is critical. As soon as the audience becomes investors or the board, performance instinct kicks in and the writing becomes wordsmithing rather than thinking. Write the private memo first. You can convert it later. Many of the most useful private memos never become anything else; their value was in the writing.

4. **Don't research first; write what you think you know.** Counterintuitive but important. Research before writing tends to confirm what you walked in believing. Writing first surfaces what you actually believe; research after writing is targeted at the gaps you found. The order matters.

5. **Allow yourself to discover that you were wrong.** *Don't visualize the finished product at the end of your journey; it will look different when you get there. Be ready to be surprised by the crazy, wonderful events that will come dancing out of your past when you stir the pot of memory.* (*— Writing About Your Life, ch. 1*) Writing-to-learn assumes the writer is changeable. If the conclusion at the end of the memo is the conclusion you would have written without doing the work, you didn't actually write to learn — you wrote to perform learning.

6. **Stop when you have an answer, not when you have a length.** A memo that resolves at 600 words is done. Don't pad it to look more serious. Conversely, if the question hasn't resolved at 2,500 words, keep going — the work isn't finished.

7. **Sleep on it before acting on the conclusion.** Writing-to-learn produces conviction. Conviction at the bottom of a freshly-written memo is sometimes false — you have argued yourself into something. The morning after, re-read. If you still believe it, act. If you don't, the gap between the night version and the morning version is the data you needed.

8. **Use writing as the discipline of preparation, not the discipline of communication.** Before a hard conversation — firing someone, asking for a sacrifice from a co-founder, pushing back on the board — write what you intend to say. Not the script. The reasoning. You will discover you don't actually believe a third of your prepared positions. That third would have been the third the conversation broke on.

9. **Write through the same question repeatedly.** A single memo doesn't always resolve a hard question. Sometimes you need three memos across two months, each starting from the same question, watching how your answer evolves. The evolution is the learning. Founders who write quarterly on the same handful of strategic questions develop conviction the rest of us only imitate.

10. **Convert the private memo to the public memo only after the private memo is done.** When you have figured out what you think, write the version for the team or the board. It is now a different task — communication, not learning — and uses different muscles. Don't try to do both at once.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has been arguing with their leadership team for three weeks about whether to raise prices by 30% or by 80%. The 30% camp argues for incrementalism and customer goodwill. The 80% camp argues that the value gap is enormous and they should capture it. Meetings cycle without resolution.

**Application:** The founder takes Saturday morning and writes a 1,500-word memo: "How much should we raise prices, and why?" They start with what they think they believe (40-50%, somewhere in the middle). The first 500 words rehearse what their team has been saying. Around word 700, the act of writing surfaces a fact they had not foregrounded: their best customers are paying 3-5x competitors and they are *still* the cheapest part of their workflow. Around word 1,100, they realise they cannot write a coherent argument for 30%, only for "we're scared." By word 1,400, they have an answer: 80%, with a 90-day grandfather period for top-tier customers. They sleep. Sunday morning they re-read; the conviction holds. Monday they call the leadership team and announce the decision.

**Result:** The decision gets made. The argument that consumed 25 person-hours of meeting time gets resolved in three hours of writing. More importantly, the founder now has the actual reasoning written down — they can paste sections into the customer comms, the board update, the all-hands memo. The writing did three jobs: forced the decision, exposed the real underlying question (price-anchoring vs. value-capture), and produced reusable text.

**Situation:** A founder is preparing the all-hands talk for a major strategic shift. They have a deck. The deck is good. The talk feels like it is going to be hollow. They cannot say why.

**Application:** Stop working on the deck. Open a blank document. Write a 1,000-word memo to no one titled "What is actually changing, and why." The deck was performing the change; the memo has to commit to it. By word 600 the founder discovers that the way they were framing the change in the deck — as natural evolution — is dishonest; this is a real pivot, and pretending otherwise will be felt by the team. The memo gets to the real frame: "Here is what we believed when we started the company. Here is what we now believe. Here is what changes because of the difference."

**Result:** The all-hands talk is rebuilt around the memo's actual frame. The talk lands harder than any previous all-hands. Employees come up afterwards and say "I finally understand what we're doing." The founder learns: the deck was the wrong artifact; the memo was the right one.

**Situation:** A founder is writing a venture capital fundraising memo. They've been told it's a writing task. They treat it as one. They produce a 6-page document that is fluent and forgettable.

**Application:** The memo is fluent because the founder did Type A writing (conveying what they already knew) on a question that wanted Type B (figuring out what the company actually is). Throw it out. Open a new document. Title: "Why is this company a venture-scale opportunity, and why am I the right person to build it?" Spend three hours answering it without reference to standard pitch structure. The writing surfaces the founder's real conviction — a particular technical insight they have under-emphasised, a particular customer relationship that has been doing more strategic work than the deck reflects, a particular weakness that they need to acknowledge instead of hide.

**Result:** The rewritten memo is structurally similar to the first one, but every section now has a centre. Investors who read the second version come back with questions about the company's actual strategy; investors who read the first version came back with vague "interesting" responses. The first memo failed because it was a performance. The second worked because the writing produced the thinking.

**Situation:** A founder is preparing for a hard conversation with their co-founder about role and equity changes. They have run the conversation in their head twenty times. Every time it goes differently. They are not getting closer to clarity.

**Application:** Write the memo. Not "what I will say" — that's a script. Write "What is actually true about how this co-founder partnership has worked, and what should change." Address it to no one; do not show it to anyone. Allow the writing to be honest in ways that conversation has not allowed. The memo will produce three things: a clearer view of the actual gap, a list of items that genuinely need to change versus items that are friction noise, and (often) a softening of position because the founder discovers they are angrier about a specific recent slight than about the structural issue.

**Result:** The conversation, when it happens, is calmer and more specific than the rehearsed versions. It moves to action. The discipline of writing prevented the conversation from being a venting session. The co-founder relationship survives or doesn't, but the decision is now made on real ground.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Outsource the writing of important strategic memos.
**Why:** A chief of staff or strategist can produce a fluent memo. They cannot do the founder's thinking. The memo whose value was in the writing has been replaced with a memo whose value is in the artefact, and the artefact is much less useful than the thinking would have been. Founders who outsource their writing also outsource their conviction.

**Don't:** Confuse "I wrote about this" with "I figured this out."
**Why:** Some writing is performance — the author already knew the conclusion and is producing the document. That writing rarely produces new thinking. Real writing-to-learn requires a question, not a thesis. If you started with the answer, you didn't write to learn; you wrote to display.

**Don't:** Treat journaling as a substitute.
**Why:** Free-flow journaling has its uses but does not impose the structural commitment that produces clarity. Writing-to-learn requires sentences that follow each other, paragraphs that argue, a piece that can be read and tested. Notebooks of fragments do not produce the same discipline.

**Don't:** Write to learn questions that don't deserve it.
**Why:** "Should we use Stripe or Adyen?" is not a writing-to-learn question. Some questions want research and a decision. Reserve the discipline for questions where your view is genuinely unresolved and the resolution will direct real resources.

**Don't:** Use writing-to-learn as a procrastination device.
**Why:** Founders who can't decide sometimes hide in the act of writing about not deciding. The memo never ends. Each draft refines the formulation without committing to the conclusion. If you are on draft three and no closer to action, the writing has stopped serving you.

**Don't:** Skip writing because "I think clearly out loud."
**Why:** No one thinks as clearly out loud as they do on the page. The medium of speech tolerates contradiction, hand-waving, and fluency-disguised-as-thought. The medium of writing does not. The founder who insists on thinking out loud is choosing the medium that flatters them over the medium that exposes them. The exposure is the point.

**Don't:** Try to make the private memo publishable.
**Why:** The discipline that produces clarity is the freedom to write things you would not say in public — to test claims, to be unflattering about colleagues, to admit uncertainties, to argue both sides. As soon as the audience becomes external, those freedoms vanish and the writing reverts to performance. Keep the private memo private. Convert later if you need to.
