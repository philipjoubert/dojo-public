# Writing About Places — The Travel Article

## When to Use

- The founder is writing first-person about a place that is part of the business — a lodge, a camp, a region, a city, a route — and the prose has to do real work for the reader.
- The piece will be read by people who have not been there. The reader's question is *what is it actually like?*, and the lazy answer ("breathtaking," "magical," "an experience of a lifetime") closes the door instead of opening it.
- The writer has just returned from the trip and is about to inflict their enthusiasm on the page. This is the most dangerous moment for any travel-writing draft. Catch it before the trip-report tone hardens.

## Fails When

- **The piece is functional, not evocative.** Logistics, rates, what to pack, GPS coordinates — those are different documents. Don't decorate them.
- **The writer hasn't actually been to the place.** Zinsser's whole travel-writing method is rooted in firsthand observation and locally-sourced quotation. AI-generated travelese is exactly the failure mode he names.
- **The piece is meant for a reader who already knows the place.** Insiders skip the description; they want news. Don't waste their time evoking what they live inside.

## Core Concept

People and places are the twin pillars on which most nonfiction is built. Every human event happens somewhere, and the reader wants to know what that somewhere was like. So you'd think travel writing would be easy. The opposite is true: it produces, more reliably than any other form of nonfiction, the worst writing professional and amateur alike are capable of.

The reason is enthusiasm.

> *Nobody turns so quickly into a bore as a traveler home from his travels. He enjoyed his trip so much that he wants to tell us all about it — and "all" is what we don't want to hear. We only want to hear some.*
> — *On Writing Well*, ch. 13

The traveler-writer is the first writer ever to see this place, think these thoughts, feel these feelings. He is not. He is the hundred-thousandth, and the previous ninety-nine-thousand-nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine wrote it the same way: the storefronts smile, the chimneytops sing, the ruins beckon, the village is nestled, the river is history-haunted, the locals are dappled, the markets bustle, the cuisine is fabled. Travelese has woven itself so tightly into the fabric of place writing that the writer has to make a conscious effort *not* to use it. If a phrase comes to you easily on a travel piece, look at it with deep suspicion.

The other failure mode is softness. "Charming." "Romantic." "Attractive." "Authentic." These mean nothing — or rather, they mean different things to different readers, which amounts to the same thing. *One man's romantic sunrise is another man's hangover.* The reader cannot picture "charming." The reader can picture the demure youth carrying a brass tray on three chains through the chaos of an Istanbul market, never spilling the small glass of red tea in the centre.

The discipline reduces to two principles, one of style and one of substance.

**Style: choose words with unusual care.** Fresh language. No "myriad." No "ilk." No "pristine wilderness" or "bustling souk." The luminous lyrical phrase to describe the wondrous waterfall will at best sound artificial and at worst pompous. Strive for the plain, surprising specific — the word a friend would use telling you about it at the kitchen table.

**Substance: be intensely selective.** *Eliminate every fact that is a known attribute.* Don't tell us shores have rocks, the sea has waves, the sand is white, the elephants are big. Find details that do useful work — that are unusual, colourful, comic, or tell the reader something specific about *this* place that distinguishes it from the seventy other places they've read travel pieces about.

> *Your main task as a travel writer is to find the central idea of the place you're dealing with.*
> — *On Writing Well*, ch. 13

McPhee on Anchorage: *"It is condensed, instant Albuquerque."* Six words. The reader instantly understands the whole place. Pritchett on Turkey: *"There are two breeds in Turkey: those who carry and those who sit."* That single distinction will reorganise the way you see the country forever. Every place has a central idea. Most writers settle for adjectives because they haven't worked hard enough to find it.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse to write the piece until you can finish the sentence "this place is the place where ___."** One distinguishing fact, one central idea, one organising observation. If you can't fill that blank, you are not yet ready to draft. You are still on holiday.

2. **Strike every soft adjective on the first pass.** Spectacular. Breathtaking. Unforgettable. Pristine. Magical. Wondrous. Dappled. Fabled. Authentic. They are reader-repellent. Replace each one with a concrete noun, a specific verb, a particular fact. *Not "the camp was breathtaking" — "the camp had no walls between the beds and the riverbank, and we woke at four to hippos breathing twenty feet from our heads."*

3. **Eliminate every known attribute.** Shores are scattered with rocks. Camps are remote. Guides are knowledgeable. Wildlife is wild. The reader knows. Don't tell them again. Spend that paragraph elsewhere — on the detail that *isn't* a known attribute.

4. **Find the human.** *The strong emotional content of the book was supplied by what I got other people to say. I didn't need to wax emotional or patriotic. Beware of waxing.* Quote the lodge cook. Quote the guide who has worked the same concession for twenty-three years. Quote the elderly American on his fifth safari about why he keeps coming back. *Their* sentences will outweigh anything you could write *about* them.

5. **Beware of waxing.** When you find yourself reaching for "to those who have walked this land, it speaks of something older than memory," stop. Delete the sentence. The reader is allergic. *If you're writing about places that are sacred or meaningful, leave the waxing to someone else.*

6. **Practice locally before you write the marquee piece.** *Go to your local mall, or bowling alley, or day-care center.* Isolate what makes the place distinctive — usually a mixture of the place itself and the regular people who inhabit it. The skill scales: a writer who can find the central idea of his neighbourhood bowling alley will find the central idea of the Okavango.

## Examples

**Situation:** A safari-company founder is drafting the "About the lodge" page for their new camp in the Kafue. The current draft opens: *"Tucked away in a pristine corner of Zambia's untouched wilderness, our intimate lodge offers a truly unforgettable safari experience for the discerning traveller."*

**Application:** Strike every word of that sentence. Every single one is a known attribute or a soft adjective. *Tucked away* — every lodge claims that. *Pristine* — banned. *Untouched* — banned (and not even true; the area has been continuously inhabited for thousands of years). *Intimate* — meaningless; how many beds? *Truly unforgettable* — the *truly* is the tell. *Discerning traveller* — the audience is patronised.

Replace with the central idea. What's the one thing that is not true of every other lodge?

**Result:** *"There are nine beds. The walk between your room and the bar takes you across a strip of grass where, on a long enough timescale, an elephant will eventually walk past. The lodge has been here since 1986. The same head guide has worked it since 1991, and his father worked the previous concession before that. He will tell you what the river is doing, why the hippos are restless this year, and which of his children is currently embarrassing him."*

This is six sentences, no adjectives anyone could squirm at, and a reader can already see the place. The voice is also the founder's, not a marketing department's.

**Situation:** A founder writing a "Letter from the road" essay after visiting three lodges in Namibia for an investor update.

**Application:** Reduce to one sentence what each place is *the place where ___*. Then pick the one with the most charge and start the piece there. Don't try to render all three. Resist the chronological "Day 1 we flew to..."; resist the survey instinct.

**Result:** Open with the specific human moment. *"On the second night at Onguma, the camp manager came to our table at dinner and said, 'I'm sorry — we have to move you.' A leopard had walked through the lounge ten minutes earlier and was now between our cabin and the dining room."* Now the reader is leaning forward. Now the rest of the piece can do its work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with the place itself before placing a person inside it. *"The Okavango Delta, a vast inland river system in northern Botswana, sprawls across..."* — the reader has already left.
**Why:** Description without a human in it is a Wikipedia entry. The reader entered the piece for *experience*, not *geography*. Get a person — guide, fellow traveller, the writer themselves doing one specific concrete thing — into the second sentence.

**Don't:** Stack proper nouns and rare words to signal sophistication. *"Sundowners on the boma overlooking the floodplain, with a chilled glass of South African chenin blanc..."*
**Why:** The reader does not know what a boma is, doesn't have a stake in the chenin, and reads the sentence as the writer auditioning. Replace with a concrete sensory fact a stranger could picture.

**Don't:** Say *"It defies description."*
**Why:** Then why are you writing about it? The phrase is the writer's own confession that they have not done the work. Find the description.

**Don't:** Outsource the emotion to the place. *"The vastness of the African sky humbles the visitor and reminds us all of how small we truly are."*
**Why:** This is *waxing* — Zinsser's specific term for it. The reader is told what to feel, which is exactly the move that will stop them feeling it. *I didn't need to wax emotional or patriotic. Beware of waxing.* Hand the work to a quoted human or a concrete detail and let the reader do their own feeling.

**Don't:** Write a piece that could equally describe any other lodge / city / country with a small find-and-replace.
**Why:** That's the test of whether you've found the central idea of *this* place. If the prose works for the Kafue and also for the Maasai Mara, you haven't found Kafue yet. *Find those distinctive traits.* Until you have, you are decorating, not describing.
