# Firing Well

## When to Use
- You have decided to let someone go (single firing or part of a layoff). The decision is already made. This protocol is about *how to do it well* — not whether to do it.
- A layoff is coming and you need the structure: who tells whom, in what order, what severance, what the all-hands looks like, how the surviving team is cared for.
- You have been avoiding a firing because the conversation feels devastating. The protocol is what makes it survivable for both sides — and the avoidance is costing the team and the person being kept in a role they should not be in.
- You have just done a botched layoff (mass email, Slack, group video) and the team is in trauma. The recovery protocol is in here too — though the cost is higher than if it had been done right the first time.

## Fails When
- **The person learns about it from anyone or anything other than their direct manager in a 1-1.** Group emails, Slack messages, "all-hands" reveals where they hear about it sitting next to colleagues, automated calendar removals — these are the markers of botched layoffs. Once it lands that way, the person feels dehumanised, the press cycle starts, and the surviving team watches and concludes they are next.
- **You let anger talk you out of severance or out of the agent step.** Anger says: *"this person is a liability, why give them a penny extra, why help them."* Anger is wrong. The agent step pays for itself many times over in remaining-team morale, in Glassdoor reviews, in the speed at which your next acceptance lands. Feel the anger, let it pass, then run the protocol.
- **You skip the Heard step because you think it will prolong the meeting.** It won't. Skipping it means the person walks out in pure rage and either shows up on Twitter, in court, or both. Twenty extra minutes spent on Heard saves you twenty weeks of cleanup.
- **You communicate to the wider team before all 1-1s are complete.** The departing person finds out from a group meeting before their manager has spoken to them. This is the single most common mistake in layoffs and it is unforgivable.
- **You don't address the surviving team's emotions afterwards — only the operational facts.** Without 1-1s where each remaining team member is actively heard, the company gets PTSD, morale tanks, and the gains of the leaner org never materialise.

## Core Concept

When you fire someone, put yourself in their shoes. Losing your job is one of the three most traumatic events a person can experience — alongside losing a spouse and losing a home. Your team is watching closely. A vindictive firing makes everyone feel unsafe, performance drops, your best people start interviewing, and the news hits Glassdoor inside a week. A humane firing — one where the person learns from their manager, gets meaningful severance, and gets active help finding their next role — does the opposite: the surviving team feels cared for, recruiting becomes easier, and you can take more risk on people with raw potential because you know the off-ramp is humane.

> "When you do fire someone, put yourself in their shoes. It is a devastating event emotionally. And it is a real setback financially. Your team will also be watching closely to see how you treat ex-employees: a vindictive attitude will make everyone feel unsafe."
> — *Firing Well (updated)*

The protocol has three components, run in strict order: the 1-1 with the person being let go (this is a difficult conversation — read `difficult-conversations.md` first); the company-wide announcement (immediately after the 1-1s, never as the means of delivery); and the surviving-team Heard pass (1-1s within forty-eight hours where each remaining team member is given space to release their fear, anger, and sadness). All three are necessary. Skip any one and the firing becomes a wound the company carries for months.

The single most important framing: **you become the person's agent in finding their next role.** Not a passive "happy to give a reference if asked" — an active agent in the Michael Ovitz sense. You interview them on what they want to do. You write the recommendation. You post on LinkedIn with their permission. You reach out to your network, not to *check the box*, but to actually find them their next great seat. This is what firing well actually means in practice. It is also the move that turns a firing from a one-time loss into something the company gets stronger from.

> "The biggest marker that I've seen between a botched layoff and a successful layoff is — at the moment someone hears that they no longer have a job, did they hear it from their manager in a one-on-one? If that's when they heard it, it'll be okay. But if they heard it in an email, in a group chat, in any kind of thing where they were sitting next to colleagues, hearing it along with other people — it wasn't personalised, it wasn't one-on-one — that is terrible. That's when people get really angry. That's when they start going onto Twitter and going to newspapers, because it feels dehumanising."
> — *Lenny's Podcast, December 2022*

## How to Apply

**Before the conversation.**

1. **Confirm the decision.** Use the decision-vs-implementation lens. Ask: *who do we serve primarily, and what's in their best interest?* If the answer is the customer, and the customer needs only great employees in this seat, the decision is settled. The pain you are feeling is the pain of *implementation* — and you cannot let that talk you out of the right decision. Write the decision down.

2. **Decide severance before the meeting.** Two months minimum, three or more is more realistic. The "two-week notice" model is not the protocol — that's a vindictive minimum dressed up as professional. The right frame is: enough runway that the person can find their next role from a place of stability, not panic. The cost of generous severance is almost always less than the cost of a vindictive firing showing up on Glassdoor or in court.

3. **Check yourself for anger.** You will likely be feeling some. *"This person is a liability. I don't want to give them an extra penny. I want to save those resources for the team that's performing."* Notice the anger. Feel it. Let it pass. Recognise that you have responsibility here — your hiring, onboarding, and management helped create this situation. The right move is not to punish the person; it's to take responsibility, help them land softly, and then fix your hiring/training/management so this doesn't happen again.

4. **Find their passion before the meeting.** Spend ten minutes thinking: *what is this person actually good at and excited about?* The skills they have that didn't fit your role are skills that fit *some* role. You'll need this when you offer to be their agent.

**The 1-1 conversation — synchronous, in person if possible, video if not, never email or Slack.**

Run the difficult-conversations protocol. The script:

1. *"I'd like to talk to you. Is now a good time?"*
2. *"This is going to be a difficult conversation. You will likely feel sadness, anger and fear."* (The warning sentence — without it the amygdala fires twice as hard.)
3. *"I am letting you go."* (Get the punchline out first. No lead-up.)
4. *"This decision is not changeable."* (No lobbying.)
5. *"This is why."* (Two or three sentences. Their brain is in fear; long explanations don't land.)
6. *"Here is what I did to create this situation."* (You hired, you onboarded, you set expectations, you gave or failed to give feedback. Take responsibility. This frame prevents the conversation from becoming an indictment of them.)
7. *"I am going to help you in the following ways. I will be your agent in finding your next job. I am giving you [X] months of severance. Today is your last day [or: until [date], to allow knowledge transfer]. The reason for that is to minimise time lingering in the sadness of saying goodbye."*
8. *"After hearing this, I imagine that you are feeling anger, fear and sadness. Would you be willing to share your thoughts with me?"*
9. **Make them feel Heard.** Whatever they say, run the Heard protocol. *"I think I heard you say…"* If they're toned-down, amp up the actual thought. *"You're feeling pissed off, and you're thinking 'goddammit, I gave three years to this place, I should have seen this coming, screw Matt for not telling me sooner.' Is that close?"* Repeat until they say "no, that's it." This step reduces the emotion enough that they will not act rashly. Skipping it is the single most common reason fired employees end up on Twitter or in court.
10. **Schedule the agent meeting.** *"Tomorrow, or whenever you're ready in the next few days, I'd like an hour with you to interview you on what your next role should look like — not as your manager, as your agent."* Make this real. It is the move that converts the firing from a closed door to an open one.

**The agent step — within 24–72 hours.**

Run a one-hour 1-1 where you are genuinely their agent:

- **Interview them on their dream job.** What do they actually want to do? Match that against their skills and passions. Identify two or three real roles in your network that fit.
- **Write a description of the person and their talents.** With permission, post it on LinkedIn with your endorsement.
- **Send a recommendation message after they pass first-round interviews.** Used at the right moment (after first interview, before final), the endorsement is what closes the role.
- **Stay in touch for a few weeks.** *"How's the search going? Anyone you want me to reach out to?"*

Investment: two to four hours per fired employee over a few weeks. Return: a former employee who becomes a genuine advocate (Mochary's litmus: *"everybody I've let go I continue to be friends with"*), surviving-team trust, easier recruiting, and the courage to take risks on raw-potential hires because the off-ramp is humane.

**The company announcement — immediately after the 1-1s.**

The announcement is what the surviving team needs to metabolise the change. It should happen in the same day as the 1-1s — never before, never via the same channel as the firing itself. Run it as a synchronous all-hands (or a targeted department meeting for smaller layoffs).

Three rules for the announcement:

1. **Do not blame or criticise the person.** Praise their contributions. Take responsibility yourself: *"We didn't evaluate fit well in hiring,"* or *"We didn't onboard them into a clear path."* Take responsibility for what's true.
2. **State what you did to create this situation, and what you're changing so it doesn't happen again.** This is what tells the surviving team that the firing wasn't arbitrary and that you are accountable.
3. **Address fear directly.** The questions in the room (and the ones that will be asked in 1-1s the next day) are: *Is this going to happen to me? Did this person even know they were underperforming? Is the company in trouble?* Answer all three. Be honest. If the answer to "is the company in trouble" is "yes, that's why we cut, and here's the runway we now have" — say that. Vague reassurance reads as evasion.

For a larger layoff: cut deeper than feels comfortable so you only cut once. Two or three rounds of layoffs creates trauma stacked on trauma; people start expecting the next one. One deeper cut, communicated as such, lets the surviving team rebuild trust faster.

**The surviving-team Heard pass — within 48 hours.**

This is the step most leaders skip and the one that determines whether the company performs better or worse after the layoff. Each manager runs a 1-1 with each direct report. The 1-1 has one job: *make them feel heard about the layoff*. Not solve their fears. Not reassure them. Hear them.

The script: *"I'd like to know your thoughts and feelings about the layoff."* They share. Then: *"I think what I'm hearing is — you feel sad because three of your buddies are no longer here, and you feel anger because you think this was bullshit and we should have hired more carefully, and you feel fear because you're not sure if the company is going to implode or if your job is safe. Is that right?"* Run Heard. Go deeper. Reflect their actual thoughts (not the polite tone-down).

Mochary's claim, from running this protocol across dozens of layoffs: *if you skip this step, your company performs better in 60 days. If you run it, your company performs better in two weeks.* The Heard step is what releases the held emotion fast enough that the surviving team gets back to creative work in days instead of months.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup CEO has decided to fire a senior engineer who is technically strong but doesn't fit the team's working style. The CEO has been dreading the conversation for two months.

**Application:** Pre-meeting: confirm decision, set three months severance, check anger, identify the engineer's passion (deep technical work) and two roles in network. The 1-1: warn, deliver, take responsibility (*"I hired you for a coordination-heavy role when your strengths are in deep work — I should have caught that"*), describe severance and agent role, run Heard. Engineer says *"I feel like I was set up to fail."* CEO: *"What I'm hearing is — you're furious, the thought is 'this is bullshit, you hired me into the wrong role, you knew it three months in, and now you're firing me for it instead of moving me sideways.' Close?"* Yes. Deeper. Until "that's it." Agent step: 90-minute interview next day, two warm intros that week. Same-afternoon all-hands. 48-hour Heard pass with surviving team.

**Result:** Engineer lands a senior IC role in four weeks and ends up referring two candidates to the CEO. Surviving morale steadies in two weeks; engineering velocity rises inside sixty days.

---

**Situation:** A company needs to lay off 15% of staff to extend runway. The CEO is debating whether to do it as a single message (faster, "rip the band-aid") or 1-1s with each person (slower, more humane).

**Application:** Protocol is unambiguous — 1-1s, every single one, before the company announcement. Morning: each manager runs back-to-back 1-1s ("Can I talk to you for 15 minutes?"), running the difficult-conversations protocol; agent 1-1 scheduled for the next day. Afternoon: synchronous all-hands; take responsibility, cut deep enough this is the only round, address fears directly. Within 48 hours: every remaining team member gets a Heard pass with their manager. Each manager becomes the agent for their 5–6 laid-off reports; the CEO is not personally agent for 50 people — the management layer is.

**Result:** No Twitter cycle, no press cycle, fair Glassdoor reviews. Within sixty days, the leaner team produces more output than the larger team did — the coordination tax is gone. The CEOs Mochary has run this with report consistently that the company gets *better* after a well-run layoff, not just survives it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Fire by email, Slack, group video, or have someone else do it.
*Why:* The first time the person learns their job is gone must be in a 1-1 with their manager. Anything less is a vindictive act dressed up as efficiency. The surviving team is watching. The press cycle starts. The Heard step becomes impossible. There is no shortcut here that works.

**Don't:** Skip severance to "save resources for the team that's performing."
*Why:* That's anger talking. Severance is not punishment — it is the runway that lets the person land softly and prevents the wound from becoming a lawsuit, a Glassdoor cycle, or a rage-Twitter post. Two months minimum; three or more is realistic. The cost is almost always less than the cost of a vindictive firing.

**Don't:** Skip the agent step because "they didn't perform, why help them?"
*Why:* Same anger. The agent step is the highest-leverage move in the protocol — it converts the firing from a wound into a relationship that often comes back to you (referrals, advocacy, even rehire later). It costs two to four hours of your time. Skipping it is a false economy.

**Don't:** Announce the layoff to the company before all 1-1s are complete.
*Why:* The departing person hears about it sitting next to colleagues, the dehumanisation kicks in, the press cycle starts. Always: 1-1s in the morning, all-hands in the afternoon. Never the other way around, never simultaneous.

**Don't:** Skip the Heard pass on the surviving team.
*Why:* Held emotion in the surviving team turns into PTSD-level disengagement that lasts months. Running the Heard pass within 48 hours releases the emotion fast enough that the team is back to creative work in two weeks. Skipping it means waiting sixty days for the recovery — and during that time you bleed your best people, who interview and leave.

**Don't:** Cut shallow and plan to "see how it goes."
*Why:* Two or three rounds creates trauma stacked on trauma. The surviving team learns to expect the next cut. Cut deep enough that this is the only round, and communicate it as such: *"This is the cut, we're not coming back for more."*

**Don't:** Make excuses in the announcement (*"the market forced our hand," "the board demanded it"*).
*Why:* The team can tell when you're deflecting. Take responsibility for what's true. *"I overhired in 2021 and I'm correcting that now — that's on me"* is the version teams forgive faster.

**Don't:** Stop being their agent after one warm intro.
*Why:* The agent role is a few hours over a few weeks — interview, description, intros, recommendation messages, check-ins. Doing the first 10% and stopping teaches the person the offer was performance.

If you have never run this protocol, role-play it with a partner before the real conversation. Read `difficult-conversations.md` first — the firing protocol sits on top of it. The first time you do it cleanly, you will feel the quieter weight of having done the hard thing well. That feeling is what makes the next firing easier. Leaders who never get good at firing are the ones who never run the protocol — they avoid, then botch, then carry the wound. Leaders who run it cleanly become the ones their teams trust most, paradoxically, because the team has seen how they handle the worst conversation.
