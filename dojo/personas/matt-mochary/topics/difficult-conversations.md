# Difficult Conversations

## When to Use
- You have decided something the other person won't like — a layoff, a layered VP, a missed promotion, a strategy change that costs them their team, a "no" they were counting on being a "yes."
- A relationship has cracks because something true hasn't been said. The cracks are getting worse. The next conversation is the difficult one.
- You are about to give hard feedback that will land as a shock. The recipient hasn't been prepared.
- You are firing someone, and Firing Well sits on top of this protocol — read this one first, then go to `firing-well.md`.

## Fails When
- **You start while you are in fear or anger.** Your brain is on the amygdala, the script will fall apart, and you'll either soften the message until it disappears or harden it into something cruel. Shift first. If you genuinely cannot wait, do the *opposite* of what fear is recommending.
- **You haven't actually decided.** If you walk in saying "I'm thinking of hiring a CRO over you" and you are open to being talked out of it, the other person will spend the next thirty minutes lobbying you, and you will have run a long-form negotiation when what you needed was a clean delivery. Decide first. Then deliver.
- **You skip the Heard step because you think it'll waste time.** It saves time. Without it the conversation extends into a week of follow-up arguments while the person processes alone, calls their friends, posts on Glassdoor, and walks in tomorrow with a lawyer.
- **You deliver it asynchronously — email, Slack, group video, voicemail.** This is not a difficult conversation. This is a vindictive act dressed up as efficiency. Run the protocol live or don't run it.

## Core Concept

There will always come a moment when you have to deliver news the recipient will not like. The question is not whether — it is whether you handle it well. Most leaders avoid the conversation, then deliver it badly when avoidance finally fails, then spend months cleaning up the resentment they could have prevented in thirty minutes.

The reason most difficult conversations go badly is surprise. The amygdala — the reptile brain — fires hardest when something negative is unexpected. If you ambush the person with the news, their brain shuts off, they can't hear what comes next, and whatever they remember of the conversation is filtered through pure fear. If you give them even a few seconds of warning — "this is going to be a difficult conversation, you are likely to feel anger, fear or sadness" — the amygdala still fires, but at half the volume. They keep enough of their brain to hear you. That single sentence at the start is the most important move in the protocol.

The second-most-important move is the Heard step. After you deliver the message, the person is in fear or anger. Their thoughts are louder than your words. The only way to get them out of that state is to make them feel heard — to repeat back what they are thinking and feeling until they say "that's right." You don't have to act on what they say. You don't have to agree. You just have to prove you understood. Once they feel heard, they can metabolise the news. Without it, they cannot.

> "There will always come a moment when you have to deliver news that the recipient will not like. It will be a difficult conversation."
> — *Difficult Conversations: how to have them*

## How to Apply

**Before the meeting.**

1. **Decide first. Write the decision down.** If you find yourself rehearsing the conversation and the decision keeps shifting, you haven't decided yet. Stop. Decide. Write the actual sentence you will say — "I am going to hire a CRO and will ask you to report to them" — verbatim. The decision must already be made when you walk in.

2. **Write what you predict will happen.** Your fear is generating a story: they'll quit, they'll sue, they'll write a hit piece, the team will revolt. Write the prediction down. The prediction is almost always wrong. Writing it down lets you check it against what actually happens. Do this every difficult conversation. Over a year of journaling, the gap between predicted catastrophe and actual outcome will train you to disbelieve the brain's catastrophe stories.

3. **Check yourself for fear and anger.** If either is present, shift before you walk in. Feel the emotion fully — sit with it, name it, let it move through you. If you cannot shift in the time you have, do the opposite of what the emotion is recommending: give the kind feedback you're afraid to give, get curious about the person you want to crush. If you absolutely cannot get out of the emotion, reschedule. A delayed difficult conversation handled well beats an on-time one handled in anger.

**The meeting itself — eight steps.**

1. **Ask if they have time.** *"I'd like to talk to you, Sam. Is now a good time?"* If not: *"When would be a good time?"* Synchronous only — in person, video, or audio call. Never asynchronous. You must be able to see or hear their reaction.

2. **Warn them.** *"This is going to be a difficult conversation. You are likely going to feel some combination of anger, fear and sadness."* This single sentence is what dampens the amygdala enough that they can hear what comes next. Don't skip it because it feels theatrical. It is the single most underrated move in the protocol.

3. **State the difficult thing. First.** *"I am going to hire a CRO and will ask you to report to them."* Do not lead up to it. Do not pad it with context. Get the punchline out so the person knows the decision has already been made and there is no point lobbying you. This is ripping the band-aid off in one stroke.

4. **Briefly explain why. Two or three bullet points.** *"I am doing this because our customers need…"* Their brain is now in fear. They have a limited capacity to hear and process. Long explanations land as defensiveness or as a pitch — neither of which you want. Keep it short.

5. **Take responsibility for what *you* did to create the situation.** "Here is what I did to create this — we hired you for a role that grew faster than we onboarded you for, and I should have brought in marketing leadership a year ago." This is not optional and it is not theatre. It is the move that prevents the conversation from becoming an indictment of them. You are above the line — accountable for your part — and that frame lets them be above the line too.

6. **Invite their feelings and thoughts.** *"I imagine that you are likely feeling anger, fear or sadness. I'd like to know your feelings and thoughts. Are you open to sharing them with me?"* They may say no. That's fine — leave the door open. Most will say yes once they realise you actually want to hear it.

7. **Make them feel Heard.** Whatever they say, run the Heard protocol. *"I think I heard you say… Am I close?"* Summarise what they said. If "not quite," ask them to repeat until you can summarise it correctly. Then go deeper: *"Is there more?"* Keep going until they say "no, that's it." If they have toned the thought down, amp it up — feel the emotion yourself, say it the way it actually appears in their head, swear words and all. They will say "no, not that extreme" and feel heard. Skipping this step is the single most common reason difficult conversations leave residue. See `heard.md` for the full protocol.

8. **If you can help them get what they actually want, do so.** A new role internally. A great reference. A warm intro. Severance that lets them land softly. Sometimes the answer is no, and that's honest. But in firing situations especially, this is where the "become their agent" move sits — see `firing-well.md`.

That's the protocol. It is not long. The first four steps take five minutes; the Heard step takes ten to twenty; the help-them step takes whatever it takes. Most difficult conversations done well run thirty to forty-five minutes total.

**After the meeting.**

- **Write down what actually happened.** Compare it to the prediction you wrote before. Note the gap. Over time the gap teaches you that fear's catastrophe stories are almost always wrong.
- **If the news affects others, communicate it to them within hours, not days.** Silence after a difficult conversation breeds rumour. The wider team should hear the news from you, in a forum where they can ask questions, before they hear it through the grapevine.
- **Schedule a follow-up.** Forty-eight hours later, check in. The person has had time to process; new feelings will have surfaced; the Heard step works again the second time. This is what separates a clean ending from a lingering wound.

## Examples

**Situation:** You have decided to hire a CRO and layer your VP of Sales. He has been with the company for three years, does a fine job on sales but cannot do marketing, and you want him to stay in the layered role.

**Application:**
1. Pre-meeting: write the sentence — *"I am going to hire a CRO and will ask you to report to them."* Write your prediction — *"He'll be devastated, threaten to quit, and probably leave within sixty days."* Check yourself for fear (yes, plenty) and anger (a little — you've been carrying frustration about the marketing gap). Shift before you walk in.
2. *"I'd like to talk to you, Sam. Is now a good time?"*
3. *"This is going to be a difficult conversation. You're likely to feel anger, fear or sadness."*
4. *"I am going to hire a CRO. I will ask you to report to them, not to me."*
5. *"Here's why: our customers need integrated marketing and sales motion, and we don't have it. The gap is costing us. I need someone who can build that."*
6. *"Here's what I did to create this: I should have hired marketing leadership a year ago when I first saw the gap. I delayed because I was hoping you'd grow into it, and that wasn't fair to you. The delay is on me."*
7. *"I imagine you're feeling some combination of anger, fear, sadness. Are you open to sharing your thoughts?"*
8. He says: *"Honestly, this feels like a demotion."* You: *"I think what I'm hearing is — you feel furious, and the thought going through your head is something like 'I broke my back for three years and now I'm being shoved aside for some new hire who won't even understand the company.' Is that close?"* He says: *"Yeah, that's basically it."* You: *"Is there more?"* He goes deeper. You keep going until he says "that's it."
9. Help: *"I want you to stay in this role and grow under the CRO. If after working with them for sixty days you feel that's not viable, I want to be your agent in finding the next thing — a CRO role somewhere our size, or a VP Sales role at a Series B. Let's talk in two weeks."*

**Result:** He stays. He's angry for a week, frustrated for a month, then settles. The CRO arrives, the integrated motion comes online, the company grows. Six months later he's running the largest deals he has ever closed. The relationship survives because the conversation was clean.

**Situation:** You have to tell your co-founder you are going to bring in an outside CEO. You have been avoiding it for three months because you are terrified she will leave the company.

**Application:** Pre-meeting prediction (write it down): *"She'll quit on the spot, take the head of engineering, and we'll be back to square one."* Check fear — overwhelming. Make a bet: predict the prediction will be wrong. Then run the protocol. Warn. Deliver. Explain. Take responsibility for your part (*"I should have surfaced this six months ago when I first noticed I was the bottleneck"*). Invite her thoughts. Run Heard until she says "that's right." Help her — what role does *she* want under the new CEO?

**Result:** She is angry. She is sad. She doesn't quit. Two weeks later she comes back having read the new role description and says "I want this — I never wanted to manage finance and ops anyway, I wanted to build product." The prediction was wrong. The fear had a story; reality had a different one. Add it to the journal.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead up to the punchline. Soften it. Pad it with three minutes of context before the actual decision.
*Why:* The person is now waiting for the bad news, scanning every sentence for the blade. Their brain is already in fear; they cannot hear the context. Worse, the soft lead-in invites lobbying — they think the decision is still open. Get the news out in the first sentence after the warning.

**Don't:** Skip the warning sentence because you find it awkward.
*Why:* The warning is what dampens the amygdala. Without it, the news lands as ambush, and the rest of the conversation is unrecoverable. Two seconds of awkwardness for thirty minutes of better conversation is the cheapest trade in the protocol.

**Don't:** Skip the "what I did to create this" step because you don't think you contributed.
*Why:* You always contributed. You hired, onboarded, set expectations, gave (or failed to give) feedback. If you cannot identify a single thing you did to create the situation, you are not looking hard enough. Skipping this step makes the conversation an indictment of the other person, which is rarely true and never useful.

**Don't:** Try to fix their feelings. Don't say "you shouldn't feel that way" or "I know you'll bounce back" or "this is actually good for you."
*Why:* You are not entitled to manage their emotional response. Your job is to make them feel heard, not feel better. The "feel better" version is for *your* discomfort, not theirs. Sit with their feelings. Reflect them back. Let them process at their pace.

**Don't:** Deliver via email or Slack because it feels safer.
*Why:* It is safer for you. It is brutal for them. They learn that you didn't have the courage or the courtesy to say it to their face. Their anger will be ten times what it would have been live, and now you have no opportunity to run the Heard step. The remaining team will watch how you delivered it, decide they are also disposable, and your best people will start interviewing.

**Don't:** Run the protocol on a decision you have not actually made.
*Why:* The other person can feel the wobble. They will lobby — and they should, because the decision is open. You'll either reverse a good decision under pressure or convince them the decision is open when it isn't. Either way the conversation is corrupted. Decide first.

If you do this protocol once and feel how clean it is, you will never go back to the avoid-then-blow-up pattern. The reason most leaders dread difficult conversations is that they have only ever had bad ones. A clean one feels different — exhausting, but not toxic. Read the script, role-play it with a partner once, then run it next time. You will be shocked at how survivable the conversation actually is.
