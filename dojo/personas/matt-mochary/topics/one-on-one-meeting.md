# The 1-on-1 Meeting — Why You Should Probably Quit Them

## Current stance vs. historical stance

This is one place where the Mochary doctrine has actively flipped. The 2018 *Great CEO Within* treats weekly 1-on-1s as foundational management practice — every manager runs them, every report shows up with a written agenda, the template is canonical. The book is unambiguous: you cannot run a successful >20-person company without 1-on-1s.

The 2025 curriculum says the opposite. Inspired by Jensen Huang (Nvidia), Brian Chesky (Airbnb), and a wave of CEOs Mochary coaches, the current recommendation is to **eliminate weekly 1-on-1s** for status / issues / feedback and force that material into the group. The book template is still useful, but as historical context — not as the operating doctrine.

Lead with the current stance. The book's templates are below as reference for the narrow cases where 1-on-1s still belong (personal/career/coaching, not status updates).

## When to Use the New Pattern (Quit 1-on-1s)
- You're a CEO with 5+ direct reports spending 8–15 hours a week in 1-on-1s.
- 1-on-1s have drifted into venting / status / "let me update you" rather than decisions.
- You hear the same issue raised privately by three different reports — the issue belongs in the group.
- You give the same feedback in three separate 1-on-1s — the feedback belongs in the group.
- The team is mature enough to give and receive feedback openly (or you're willing to invest in building that skill).

## Fails When
- **The team can't yet give or receive feedback in a group safely.** If you kill 1-on-1s before the team has the skill to do absolute feedback in a structured way, the feedback simply stops happening. Build the group-feedback capability first; *then* drop the 1-on-1s.
- **You're a 5-person startup.** Informal hallway contact handles all of this. Don't import a fix for a 30-person problem into a 5-person team.
- **You're using "kill the 1-1" to avoid private hard conversations you actually need to have.** PIPs, terminations, sensitive personal matters — these still belong in a private setting, just not in a recurring weekly cadence.
- **You're trying to skip the cost.** Killing 1-on-1s saves 10–15 hours per week — but only if you genuinely redirect that time into the group standups, weekly unblocking meetings, and structured feedback rounds. Skip the redirect and the issues just stop surfacing anywhere.

## Core Concept

The historical case for 1-on-1s was: a manager needs private time with each report to surface issues, give feedback, and align on goals. The argument made sense in the 1980s and at most companies through 2020. It still makes sense for some kinds of conversation. But for the *recurring weekly slot* with each direct, it has become a bad trade.

> "Inspired by leaders like Jensen Huang (Nvidia) and Brian Chesky (Airbnb), many of our CEOs have eliminated 1:1 meetings. The reasons are clear:
> - Time-consuming and inefficient: hours per week, often low-value for CEO
> - Lack of transparency: siloed information, others miss valuable insights
> - Missed learning opportunities: only one person benefits from feedback, rather than whole team
> - Slows decision-making: key stakeholders aren't present, delaying key decisions
> - Energy drain: becomes a 'therapy session' where employees vent about problems, instead of focusing on solutions"
> — *articles/how-to-quit-1-1s.md*

The cost of weekly 1-on-1s isn't just the calendar hours. It's that **information that needs to be in the group ends up trapped in a dyad.** A report raises an issue with the CEO; the CEO has the answer; the answer never reaches the report's peers, who would have benefited from hearing it. Multiply that across six 1-on-1s a week and the CEO becomes a private oracle whose insights don't compound. Worse: an issue that affects three teams gets discussed three times privately, at three different angles, with three different shadings of the CEO's view — and never gets resolved as one decision in front of the people who would implement it together.

The replacement isn't *no contact*. It's *redirect contact to the right venue*:

- **Status, accountability, KPIs** → daily standup or weekly team meeting.
- **Issues that need decisions** → group meeting using the Issue / Proposed Solution framework, with all stakeholders present so the decision happens in real time.
- **Feedback** → structured rotating round-robin in the group, so every person gives and receives feedback monthly to quarterly with the whole team learning from each round.
- **Personal connection** → quarterly dinners, walking meetings, virtual coffee — relationship-building decoupled from the recurring weekly slot.
- **Genuine private matters** → office hours, on-demand. Rare; that's the point.

> "Get rid of 1-1s to force issues and information into the group, which is much more effective, and frees up 10-15 hours per week for the CEO."
> — *articles/ceo-teachings.md*

The 10–15 hours come back. The information flow gets richer. The team levels up because feedback is shared learning, not private therapy. And the CEO stops being the bottleneck through which all knowledge flows.

## What Still Belongs Privately

Quitting weekly 1-on-1s does not mean every conversation goes to the group. The conversations that still belong in a private 1-on-1 — but on demand, not on a fixed weekly cadence — are:

- **Personal hardship.** A report's family member is ill, divorce, mental health, financial crisis. Office hour or direct outreach. Always 1-on-1, never in the group.
- **Career and growth.** A genuine career conversation — "where do you want to be in three years?" — is a coaching conversation, not a status update. Quarterly is enough; a one-hour walk works better than a 30-minute calendar slot.
- **Performance management / PIPs.** Sensitive performance discussions, PIP starts, terminations. Always private. The Mochary 1-1 template's "SENSITIVE goals and actions — these will NOT be included in the company-wide messaging" provision exists for this.
- **Onboarding the first month.** A new hire needs disproportionate private time with their manager for the first ~30 days. Then taper.
- **The CEO with their executive coach** — that 1-on-1 stays. (The CEO–coach relationship is the canonical 1-on-1 in the Mochary practice.)

The principle: **anything that benefits the team if heard, goes to the team.** Anything that would harm the team or the individual if shared, stays private — but gets handled on demand, not as a recurring weekly slot.

## How to Apply

### Phase 1 — Build the group muscle first

1. **Install group standups before killing the 1-on-1s.** A daily 15-minute exec standup or weekly team meeting becomes the venue for accountability and updates. Each person reports what they did, what they're doing, what's blocked. KPIs and OKR traffic-lights on a shared doc.

2. **Install the Issue / Proposed Solution framework in the group meeting.** When a report has an issue, they bring it to the group as a written I/PS. Stakeholders are present, the decision-maker decides in the room, the action items have DRIs and dates. (See `issue-process.md`.)

3. **Install structured feedback rounds.** Use a rotating round-robin so feedback is predictable and predictably infrequent at the individual level but constant at the team level:
   - **Week 1:** CEO receives feedback from the entire team using absolute feedback (5 As).
   - **Week 2:** CEO gives feedback to each executive.
   - **Week 3:** One executive receives feedback from peers (without the CEO).
   - **Week 4:** Another executive receives feedback from peers.
   The CEO gives and receives monthly. Each exec receives quarterly. Reward the most "gut-punchy" critique — make it explicitly safe to be direct.

4. **Model receiving feedback first.** Before you can ask the team to do this safely, you have to demonstrate it. The 5 As (Acknowledge, Appreciate, Apologize if appropriate, Act, Anti-pattern check) are the script. Run yourself through it publicly until the team trusts it.

### Phase 2 — Kill the recurring 1-on-1s

5. **Tell the team why and when.** Explicitly. *"I'm replacing our weekly 1-on-1s with the daily standup, the weekly team meeting, and a monthly feedback rotation. The reasons: I want issues to reach the group, I want feedback to be shared learning, and I want to give us back ten hours a week. Personal or sensitive topics, message me directly or use my office hour."*

6. **Open a weekly office hour, never used for status.** One hour a week, blocked, available to anyone for personal or confidential matters. In practice: rarely used. That's the point — when used, it's worth your full attention.

7. **Encourage direct outreach.** Reports can call, message, or book time directly when they need a private conversation. The protection is *no recurring weekly cadence* — not *no private contact*.

8. **Maintain personal connection on a separate cadence.** Quarterly dinners, lunches, coffee chats, walking meetings, virtual coffee for remote teams. The connection is real but doesn't live on a weekly calendar slot.

### Phase 3 — When 1-on-1s still happen (the narrow cases)

If you do run a 1-on-1 — for the canonical narrow cases above — use this structure (the **A.C.T.** framework from the historical Mochary template):

- **A**ccountability — what did you commit to last time? Yes / No. If No: Why? What habit will prevent this obstacle next time?
- **C**oaching — what's Good and Not-Good in Company / Department / You. For Not-Good: the Issue, *what you did to help create this situation* (the critical step — don't skip), the proposed Solution.
- **T**ransparency — feedback both ways. Like / Wish That format. Specific actions, not characterizations.

The "what did you do to create this situation?" question is the heart of it. It transforms an externally-caused complaint into an internally-actionable insight. Without it, the 1-on-1 becomes the venting session that justified killing the practice in the first place.

## Examples

**Situation:** A CEO of a 40-person company has 6 direct reports and runs weekly 30-minute 1-on-1s with each. Three hours every week. The 1-on-1s have drifted: status updates the CEO already saw on the dashboard; complaints about other execs that the CEO can't actually resolve in private; vague feedback that's hard to act on. The CEO is exhausted and feels they're not adding value.

**Application:** Build a daily 15-minute exec standup using the Defacto-style agenda (yesterday / today / blocked). Install a weekly 90-minute exec meeting where unblocking happens via Issue / Proposed Solution. Install the four-week feedback rotation. Announce: 1-on-1s are paused for 60 days; if you have a personal or sensitive matter, message me or use Friday office hour. Block the 6 hours a week previously eaten by 1-on-1s into Top Goal time and longer thinking.

**Result:** The exec team complains for the first two weeks ("I miss having you to myself"). By week four, the standup catches issues earlier; the weekly unblocking meeting resolves cross-functional friction in front of all parties; feedback lands in the group with everyone learning. The CEO recovers ~6 hours a week. The 1-on-1s do not return.

---

**Situation:** A founder of a 5-person seed-stage company asks if she should be running structured 1-on-1s.

**Application:** No. At five people, you're in the same room (or Slack channel) most of the day. Informal contact does the work. Don't import a fix for a 30-person problem into a 5-person team. When you cross 15–20, install daily standups and weekly team meetings — and keep skipping the 1-on-1 layer entirely. You don't have to add 1-on-1s and then remove them.

**Result:** The founder doesn't waste calendar hours on a practice she doesn't yet need.

---

**Situation:** A manager kills weekly 1-on-1s with their reports, but did not first install a feedback rotation or train the team on group feedback. After six weeks, no one is giving feedback to anyone. Performance issues that would have surfaced in 1-on-1s are now invisible.

**Application:** Killed in the wrong order. Reinstall the rotation: a structured monthly feedback round-robin where each exec gives and receives. Use absolute feedback (Like / Wish That, with specific actions) so the format is predictable. Model it by being the first to receive a "gut-punchy" critique. After 8 weeks, the group feedback is producing better signal than the old 1-on-1s ever did.

**Result:** The fix isn't to bring back 1-on-1s — it's to install the missing replacement venue.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Kill 1-on-1s to "save time" without redirecting the time and the conversations.
*Why:* The hours saved are real, but if you don't redirect feedback, status, and issues into the group, those things just stop happening. The standup, the unblocking meeting, the feedback rotation — all three need to exist. Skipping any of them means you've lost a venue with no replacement.

**Don't:** Treat all private contact as 1-on-1s to be killed.
*Why:* Personal hardship, career conversations, performance management, and onboarding still need privacy. The killed practice is the *recurring weekly status slot*, not all private contact. Office hour + direct outreach handles the genuinely private; the calendar block is what goes.

**Don't:** Use 1-on-1s to deliver feedback that should be public.
*Why:* If the feedback would benefit three other people on the team to hear, putting it in a private 1-on-1 keeps three people from learning. The shared-learning argument is the strongest single reason to move feedback to the group.

**Don't:** Skip the "what did you do to create this situation?" question.
*Why:* Every 1-on-1 template has it for a reason. Without it, complaints stay external and unactionable. With it, the report leaves with something they can move. (See `difficult-conversations.md`.)

**Don't:** Run a "1-on-1" that's actually a status update you could read on the dashboard.
*Why:* If the meeting could be replaced by the report writing into a doc you read, replace it. Synchronous time is for things that genuinely need synchronous time — decisions in the room, repair of damaged trust, pure connection — not for the CEO learning what happened last week.

**Don't:** Pretend the historical 1-on-1 template is wrong; it isn't, in its narrow domain.
*Why:* The template is still excellent for the on-demand private cases. The flip is about *cadence* — recurring weekly is now the wrong cadence for status / feedback / issues. The structure is fine; the slot is wrong.

**Don't:** Drop 1-on-1s to avoid hard conversations.
*Why:* If you're killing the recurring slot because you don't want to give a hard piece of feedback, you'll just delay the conversation. The conversation still has to happen — and now it happens in the group, which is harder, not easier. Build the courage and the structure first; relocate after.
