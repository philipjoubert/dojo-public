# Trust and Like — How Relationships Actually Get Built

## When to Use
- You're fundraising and need investors who will back you across multiple companies, not just this round.
- You're closing a key hire and need them to choose you over competing offers.
- You're meeting an influential person — board candidate, advisor, customer prospect — for the first time.
- You're maintaining a portfolio of relationships that need to compound rather than decay.
- You feel that your conversations with senior people end politely but go nowhere.

## Fails When
- **Bad intent.** The technique is manipulative in the literal sense — it produces trust faster than it would otherwise emerge. Use it on someone you don't actually intend to treat well, and you generate trust you'll then betray. Mochary warns against this every time he teaches the method. (See the warning below.)
- **You can't be genuinely curious.** The method works because real curiosity is sensed. Fake curiosity — asking the questions without listening for the answer — is detected within one or two meetings. Some people genuinely don't find others interesting. For them, the method doesn't work; they need to develop the underlying skill first (often by practicing with a spouse, child, or close friend — see below).
- **You expect one-meeting results.** The bond forms over two to five rounds of contact. Anything claimed to work in one meeting is something else.
- **You're using it to cover for a broken product or company.** Trust-and-like buys you *patience* and *benefit of the doubt*. It does not buy permanent immunity from a bad business. If the underlying offer is weak, the relationship will eventually call it out — and now you've burned the relationship too.

## Core Concept

Almost every CEO believes that the way to win an investor, customer, or recruit is to *talk about their company* — to pitch hard, fast, and convincingly. The data and Mochary's experience say the opposite. The deepest relationships in tech, the ones that last across pivots and companies, are built on the same mechanic that builds any human relationship: **the other person feels deeply heard, deeply remembered, and deeply appreciated.** That's the entire move. Most founders skip it because it feels indirect, slow, and unnatural compared to closing.

> "Think about the people you like. Do you like people who talk about themselves and show no interest or curiosity about you and your life? Or do you like people who ask about you, listen attentively, and are genuinely curious about what makes you tick?"
> — *articles/trust-and-like.md*

The mechanic has four ingredients, in this order:

1. **Ask them about their lives.** Work and home. Genuine curiosity, not interview questions. What energizes them. What's hard right now. How they got here. What they actually like doing on a Saturday.
2. **Prove you heard them.** *"I think I heard you say…"* and repeat back the highlights. (See `heard.md` — this is the same Heard mechanic.) The repetition lands in their body the way nothing else does.
3. **Prove you remember.** At the next meeting: *"The last time we talked, you said…"* and again repeat. They feel honored that you cared enough to remember the details of their life. Write the details down after each meeting; you cannot rely on natural memory at scale.
4. **Tell them what you appreciate about them.** Specifically. What they did, not what they are ("you took the time to meet with me when you didn't have to," not "you're a generous person"). If nothing else, you can always appreciate that they showed up.

That's it. Two meetings of this and most people will trust and like you. Three to five and they will tell you so out loud. The pattern is so reliable that Mochary describes the moment as predictable: *"At some point, the person will say something like (and this is an actual quote from an investor): 'I really like you. I want to invest in you. Now tell me what your company does.'"*

The quiet engine underneath the method is **empathy as success driver, not soft skill.** If you genuinely get curious about another person — what their work is like, what their fears are, what they're trying to build — they sense it. They feel that you would actually consider their interests when making decisions. That feeling of being-considered is what generates the trust. The technique is the scaffolding; the scaffolding only holds because the curiosity is real.

## The Warning — Always Paired

Mochary teaches this method together with an explicit warning, every single time. Repeat it as he does:

> "Please know that the above techniques, and all of those I have written about in relationship-building below, are manipulative. You can use them for good (to create a connection with someone you will treat well) or for bad (to connect with someone you will not treat well). I hope that you will use them for good only."
> — *articles/trust-and-like.md*

The discipline is not "don't use the technique." The discipline is **deserve the trust the technique generates.** If you find yourself running the four steps on someone you don't actually plan to treat well — an investor you'll mislead in the next round, a hire you'll fire when convenient, a customer you'll churn on after the contract — *stop using the method.* The problem is not your technique. It's your intent. Fix the intent or step away.

The reason this warning lives in the practice itself: tools that work this well are tools you can corrode trust at scale with. Mochary has watched founders absorb the technique, succeed with it short-term, and then realize they've installed a habit of cultivating relationships they have no intention of honoring. The reckoning is brutal — both for the people they used it on, and for the founder, who later cannot tell anymore which of their connections were real.

## How to Apply

### First meeting

1. **Be explicit about your intent.** *"I'd like to develop a real relationship with you."* Stating it out loud is itself heartwarming and signals you're not closing in this conversation. Counter-intuitive — try it.

2. **Ask, don't pitch.** Open with questions about them. Where they grew up, how they got into their current work, what they're excited about right now, what's hard. Most senior people are starved for this — most conversations they're in are someone trying to get something from them.

3. **Listen — really.** Phone away. Notes only if you're going to use them. Make it your full intent to understand, not to talk back.

4. **Prove you heard.** *"I think I heard you say…"* and reflect the highlights. Then: *"Is that right?"* If not quite, keep going until they say yes. (See `heard.md` for full mechanics.)

5. **Appreciate something specific.** Before you leave: *"I really appreciate that you took the time to meet today, and especially [specific thing they shared] — that landed."*

6. **Ask for a second meeting.** Stated purpose: continuing to build the relationship. Most senior people will say yes because the first meeting was about them, not about extracting from them.

7. **Write down what you remember.** Immediately after. Personal details, names of family members, hobbies, what they're working on, what they care about. This is the memory you'll demonstrate at the next meeting; you cannot rely on natural recall.

### Second meeting

8. **Open with memory.** *"The last time we talked, you said…"* and reflect a specific detail from the prior conversation. Watch their reaction. They will be visibly honored — most people they meet with do not remember.

9. **Continue the pattern.** More questions. More listening. More heard. By end of meeting two, the trust-and-like is usually established. They may say so out loud.

10. **Now, and only now, you can talk about your company** — if appropriate, and only because they asked. You'll know the moment by their words or body language.

### Maintenance (3–5+ rounds)

11. **Keep contact light and frequent.** A 15-minute call beats a one-hour lunch you only do once a year. A two-line email referencing something specific they shared beats nothing.

12. **Send appreciation messages on a cadence.** Chris Fralic of First Round Capital allocates one hour every Friday to send appreciation messages through his contacts. Andy Bromberg does it for a few minutes daily. The volume of goodwill this generates is shocking compared to the cost. Hand-written notes (Bromberg's variant) over-perform email by an order of magnitude.

13. **Reference small specifics.** "Saw your old neighborhood in the news today, thought of you." "I remember you said your daughter was applying to colleges — how did that go?" The specifics prove memory more than any general warmth.

### Going deeper — Trust, Deep

For the relationships you want to be the deepest — co-founders, key investors, board members, your own coach — there is a second-order move: **share something genuinely flawed about yourself, and let them do the same.** Mochary opens his CEO group meetings this way, and it's why CEOs choose his group over the dozen other groups they have access to.

> "In every group that I convene, each member must share something deeply shameful about themselves … And then I ask each person, 'Do you feel more or less connected to me?' Without exception, they all say 'More' and then something like, 'Because I appreciate that you were willing to reveal yourself to me that way.'"
> — *articles/trust-deep.md*

Fear says: never reveal anything shameful, you'll be rejected. The reveal proves the opposite. Vulnerability landed first creates the safety for the other person to land theirs. When they do, your only job is *listen and appreciate* — never problem-solve. They aren't asking you to fix their flaw. They're testing whether you still accept them. *"Tell me more"* and *"thank you for sharing that with me, I feel closer to you now"* are the right responses. *"Have you thought about therapy?"* destroys the moment.

Peter Fenton — universally considered the best relationship-builder in tech investing — runs this naturally. Within five minutes of meeting Mochary the first time, Fenton had told him "things that felt like his deepest, darkest secrets." The instant reaction in everyone Fenton meets: *"I love this guy. He's not telling me how great he is — he's telling me how broken he is."* Fenton doesn't do this as a technique. He's openly flawed. The world has rewarded him for it.

### Practice ground

Most founders aren't naturally good at this and don't get enough touchpoints with investors, customers, or recruits to practice on them — every touchpoint is too high-stakes. The fix:

> "Do you have children, a significant other, a friend, or a parent? The answer, of course, is yes. Well, then, there is your practice ground. Relationships with humans (teammates, customers, or investors) are no different than relationships with humans (children, spouse, friends, or family members). Get good at your relationship with your spouse or children, and you will discover that you learn all the skills needed to develop a great relationship with your teammates or customers."
> — *articles/relationships-how-to-get-good-at-them.md*

Read *Hold Me Tight* with your spouse. Practice making your kid feel heard at dinner. The skill transfers exactly. Trying to debut the technique on a $10M investor pitch is like trying to surf without ever paddling.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is fundraising. He has a $5M Series A target, a strong product, and a seven-firm pitch list. He defaults to opening every meeting with the deck.

**Application:** Switch the opening for the top three firms. Email asking for a 30-minute coffee, *"with no pitch — I'd like to get to know you and how you work with your CEOs."* Spend 25 minutes asking the partner about themselves. Reflect what you heard. Appreciate specifically. Schedule a second meeting two weeks later. Open the second meeting referencing a detail from the first. Pitch lightly, only if invited. Run the deck-first version on the back four firms as control.

**Result:** Two of the top three lead the round. The back four firms either pass or come in as participating-only. Cliff Weitzman, CEO of Speechify (cited in the book), reports the same pattern: investors who built relationship-first stick with him across multiple companies. The deck-first investors are one-and-done.

---

**Situation:** A CEO has a board member who feels distant. They speak only at board meetings. The CEO doesn't know how to deepen it.

**Application:** Email: *"I'd like to develop a deeper working relationship with you outside of board meetings. Can I take you to coffee — no agenda, no asks, just to know each other better?"* Spend the meeting asking about their life and career. Two weeks later: a five-line email referencing something they said. Six weeks: another coffee. Eventually: share something genuinely flawed about yourself in the relationship.

**Result:** The board member becomes a real ally — not because of the framework, but because the relationship is now a relationship. They'll back the CEO through pivots and crisis the way they wouldn't have when contact was limited to board meetings.

---

**Situation:** A founder reads the trust-and-like article and says: *"I'm not a people person. This isn't me. Should I just be authentic and skip it?"*

**Application:** Authenticity is fine, but the underlying skill — making another person feel heard, remembering them, appreciating specifics — is learnable. Practice with your spouse first. Read *Hold Me Tight*. Run the four-step pattern on your kid for a month. Make your sister feel heard about something hard at her work. The skill transfers cleanly to investors, customers, and reports. "Not being a people person" is usually "I've never trained the skill" — and the relationships you build inside your family give you ten thousand low-stakes reps.

**Result:** Six months later the founder runs the same pattern with a $20M investor and reports it felt natural. The training reps did the work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run the four-step pattern as a checklist on someone you don't intend to treat well.
*Why:* You become someone who burns trust at scale. The technique works because the care underneath it is real. Use it without the care and you'll succeed twice and then carry a long tail of damaged relationships and a self-image you can't trust. The warning Mochary always pairs with this teaching is not optional.

**Don't:** Pitch in the first meeting after you've stated the purpose was relationship.
*Why:* You've broken the contract you offered. The other person will silently revise their model of you ("they were just running a long-form pitch the whole time") and the meeting was a net negative. If they ask about your company, you can answer briefly. Don't volunteer the deck.

**Don't:** Try to remember without writing it down.
*Why:* Natural recall fails by the second meeting and disastrously by the fifth. The whole proof of "I remember" depends on a notebook or CRM. Andy Bromberg makes appreciation messages a daily five-minute habit precisely because the systematic approach is what scales it.

**Don't:** Substitute warmth for specifics.
*Why:* "Great to see you" is generic and fades. "I remember you mentioned your dad was sick — how is he?" is specific and lands. Generic warmth between two people who barely know each other reads as performative; specifics read as care.

**Don't:** Problem-solve when someone shares something flawed.
*Why:* They aren't asking you to fix them. They're checking whether you still accept them. Problem-solving reads as *"this flaw is unacceptable; you must change for me to accept you."* Listen, appreciate, ask for more. That's the entire move.

**Don't:** Skip the warning when teaching this to others.
*Why:* The warning is part of the practice, not a footnote. Mochary attaches it every time he teaches the method because the failure mode (using it without intent to treat people well) is endemic and self-corroding. If you teach the method to a teammate, teach the warning with it.

**Don't:** Use the relationship to cover a weak business.
*Why:* Trust-and-like buys patience and benefit of the doubt. It does not buy permanent immunity. If the company is genuinely broken and the founder is using personal relationship to delay the reckoning, the reckoning still arrives — and the relationship comes with it. Build the relationship; also fix the business.
