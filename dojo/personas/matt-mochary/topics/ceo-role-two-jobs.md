# The CEO's Two Jobs

## When to Use
- The company has product-market fit and is now scaling. The CEO's job has changed and the calendar hasn't caught up.
- The user is exhausted from being in every department's meetings and asks how to free up time.
- The user is hiring or has just hired a COO / President / Chief of Staff and wants to know what to actually offload.
- The user is trying to design their week and asks what *only the CEO* can do.
- A fire is burning in the company right now and the CEO can't get to it because they're booked back-to-back for two weeks.

## Fails When
- **Pre-PMF.** The CEO of a 4-person company isn't doing two jobs — they're doing all of them, especially product and sales. This framework activates *after* PMF and *after* you have at least an exec team to delegate to.
- **As cover for under-management.** "I only have two jobs" is sometimes used by founders to abdicate hiring, performance management, or a difficult conversation they don't want to have. Hiring the exec team is one of the three non-delegable functions. So is reporting to the board. So is being the relationship anchor for key stakeholders.
- **When you don't yet have a team that can absorb what you delegate.** You don't walk out of the work tomorrow. You hire your replacements, hand off, and *then* the open calendar appears. Trying to clear the calendar before the team is ready creates a vacuum that fills with chaos.

## Core Concept

The CEO role, as most people imagine it, is a giant list: set the vision, run product, manage the team, hire executives, hold people accountable, allocate resources, run any department that doesn't have a head, report to the board, build relationships with customers and investors. All of that is real. But almost none of it has to be done *by you*. Most of it has to be done by *someone* — and your only real obligation is to make sure the right someone is doing each piece, with passion and capability, and then get out of the way.

What's left after you delegate everything you can? Two functions. Paint the vision, and resolve conflict.

> "The CEO's primary job is to one, describe the vision of where the company's going, where the product is going — so describe where are we going to be three months from now, where are we going to be a year from now, where are we going to be five years from now, where are we going to be ten years from now. Because everybody in the company needs to feel like they understand the North Star where we're heading, and the only person who has the authority and legitimacy to describe that as CEO. And secondly, the second job of the CEO is to resolve conflict within the organization."

The vision job is straightforward in concept and ferocious in execution. You describe — out loud, in writing, in slides, in all-hands, on Slack — where the company will be at three months, one year, five years, ten years. Then you refresh that constantly. Not once a year at the offsite. Constantly. Because the team forgets. Because the world changes. Because new hires arrive every week and have never heard it from you. Only the CEO has the authority and the customer-problem context to do this. You can have someone *write* it for you — sit next to you and transcribe — but you have to *speak* it. And the more time the calendar gives you to think about the future, the better the vision gets.

The conflict job is what most CEOs underestimate. Conflict erupts at the fault lines between exec functions — Sales versus Marketing, Product versus Engineering, Finance versus everyone. These conflicts will not resolve themselves; they harden into political feuds that fracture the org. Only the CEO has the standing to walk into a Head-of-Sales-versus-Head-of-Marketing fight and call it. But here is the operational consequence almost no one accepts: *you don't know when a conflict will arise*. So if your calendar is booked Monday through Friday with department meetings, when the fire starts, you're not available, and the fire grows for a week before you can get to it.

The conclusion is the calendar design that distinguishes the best CEOs from the rest:

> "The best CEOs that I coach have huge blocks of time available every single day, and even entire days with nothing scheduled. So that when a problem arises — boom — they're right on top of it, put out the fire that same day, organization moves forward. I have CEOs that take one-hour naps every afternoon. Frankly, they're the best CEOs because what they've done is they've taken all the other parts that do require meeting all day long and they've given them to other people."

The fireman model. The fireman lives at the firehouse. Most of the time there is no fire. So the fireman waits — and the enterprising ones use that downtime productively (write the next iteration of the vision, build relationships with key stakeholders). When a fire breaks out, the fireman is *there* in minutes, because their entire job is to be available. A CEO who has filled every hour of their week with department meetings has organized themselves to be unavailable at the moment they're most needed.

The other functions — managing the exec team, running operating cadence, holding people accountable to goals, hiring sub-execs, allocating resources, putting out fires *inside* a department — get delegated to a COO, President, or Chief of Staff who genuinely loves that work. After ninety days, that person is doing it better than you ever did, because they actually love it. (Your Zone of Genius is product visioning and customer problems; their Zone of Genius is people management and operating cadence. See `energy-audit.md`.)

The three things that genuinely cannot be delegated, even by the most discplined CEO: relationships with key stakeholders (employees, partners, customers, investors), reporting to the board, and hiring the exec team itself. Everything else, with the right person in the right seat, is theirs.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your current calendar against the two jobs.** Print last week. For every meeting, ask: was this *paint the vision*, *resolve conflict*, *report to the board*, *build a stakeholder relationship*, or *hire an exec*? If it was none of those, it shouldn't have been on your calendar.

2. **Identify the function you most don't love and delegate it first.** For most product-founder CEOs, that's people management — running the exec team's operating cadence, the weekly accountability meetings, the resource-allocation decisions, the sub-exec hires. Hire a COO, President, or Chief of Staff whose Zone of Genius is exactly this. (See `energy-audit.md` for the Zone of Genius framing.)

3. **Build the open-calendar discipline progressively.** Start by blocking one entire morning a week with nothing on it. Then a full day. Then half of every day. The goal is huge unscheduled swaths every single day, plus at least one fully-empty day per week. This will feel wrong. Push through.

4. **Use the open time for the two jobs and only the two jobs.** When no fire is burning, paint the next iteration of the vision. Reach out to a customer, a key employee, a partner, an investor — not transactionally, just to know that the CEO is thinking about them. Write the next version of the strategy memo. Do the thing only you can do.

5. **When a fire starts, drop everything and go.** The whole point of the open calendar is that the conflict gets resolved *today*, not next Wednesday when the slot opens up. Your value to the organization is highest in those moments and highest only because you were available.

6. **Refresh the vision constantly.** Three-month, one-year, five-year, ten-year. New hires need to hear it from you (record it on video so it scales). Slack posts. All-hands. One-on-ones. The team will internalize whatever you repeat — so repeat the vision, not the task list.

7. **Re-test the framework whenever a fire surprises you.** If a major conflict landed and you couldn't get to it for two days, your calendar is too full. Cut something. The next surprise fire is the next test.

8. **Know when to fire yourself as CEO.** If the things you don't love include people management, hiring, sales, and fundraising — and the only thing you love is product — it is time to hire a CEO and become Chairman. Large customers, investors, and senior execs want a direct relationship with the person whose title is CEO. If you don't want those relationships, give the title to someone who does. (You keep all your equity, the equity becomes more valuable, and you go build your ideal life.)

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder of a 250-person Series C company is on the verge of burnout. Calendar is fully booked Monday through Friday, 8am to 7pm. Each meeting is "important." Two weeks ago a major conflict between the Head of Sales and the Head of Marketing flared, and the founder couldn't get to it for nine days. By the time he sat down with both, the Head of Sales was already interviewing.

**Application:** Audit. Of last week's 47 meetings, six were paint-the-vision or stakeholder relationships. Forty-one were operational meetings the CEO did not need to be in — sales pipeline reviews, eng standups, recruiting debriefs, biz-ops planning. The founder hires a President from his network whose Zone of Genius is operating cadence. Over ninety days, the President takes over: weekly exec team standup, sub-exec hires, sales-vs-marketing operational sync, biz-ops, recruiting reviews. The founder begins blocking Wednesdays as a no-meeting day and an additional half-day Thursday afternoon.

**Result:** Three months in, the founder has eight to ten unscheduled hours per week and one full empty day. The next major Sales-vs-Marketing flare-up gets resolved in a single 90-minute session the day it surfaces. The founder uses the open time to record three new vision videos for new hires, write the strategy memo for the next round, and call ten customers personally. NPS rises. Time-to-resolution on cross-functional conflict drops from nine days to one. The founder sleeps.

---

**Situation:** A first-time CEO of a 50-person Series A asks: *"My calendar is full of one-on-ones. I have one with every direct report every week, plus skip-levels, plus my product team. Is this right?"*

**Application:** No. Each one-on-one is information that should be in the group, where everyone can hear it and be bought into the response. One-on-ones quietly route around the group — the team member raises their issue with you privately, the rest of the team doesn't know it exists, the team never builds the muscle of resolving issues in front of each other. Get rid of them. Run an exec team meeting structured around the issue/proposed-solution flow (see `issue-process.md`). The hour you'd have spent on each one-on-one comes back to you. Use it for the two jobs. (Caveat: if a person needs personal coaching that genuinely doesn't belong in the group, do that — but don't call it a one-on-one and don't make it weekly.)

**Result:** Founder reclaims 10–15 hours per week. The exec team starts surfacing real issues to each other instead of routing them through the CEO. Information flow improves; coordination overhead drops; the CEO has time to be the fireman. (This is one of the core 2025 CEO Teachings shifts — see `ceo-teachings.md`.)

---

**Situation:** A founder loves product, hates everything else. He's been a CEO for four years and is consistently burnt out. Considering hiring a COO.

**Application:** This isn't a COO situation. It's a CEO situation. If the only thing you love about the CEO role is product, the right move is to hire a CEO, take the Chairman title, run product, and go build your ideal life. Naval Ravikant did exactly this at AngelList — he was great at the CEO role, hated it, replaced himself, and the company took off. (Mochary's word: he "sucked" at it because he didn't love it.) The founder's pushback: *"But customers and investors want to deal with the CEO."* That's exactly why you have to give the title to the person who *will* take those calls, instead of being a reluctant CEO who picks up the phone half the time.

**Result:** Six months later: founder hires CEO from his network, takes Chairman role, focuses entirely on product roadmap and customer interviews. Equity value goes up. Burnout vanishes. Founder writes Mochary, *"this was the right call by a mile."*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "two jobs" with "two meetings."
**Why:** Painting the vision is a constant practice — the strategy memo, the all-hands, the new-hire video, the customer call where you describe where the company is going. Resolving conflict is a posture of constant availability. These are not Tuesday at 2pm. They are how you spend your time, period.

**Don't:** Fill the open calendar with optional meetings because empty days feel uncomfortable.
**Why:** The whole engine depends on having unscheduled time when a fire starts. Every meeting you accept "because it might be useful" is a meeting that isn't there for the fireman role. The discomfort of an empty Wednesday is the price of admission for being the CEO who actually shows up when needed.

**Don't:** Delegate the three non-delegable functions.
**Why:** Reporting to the board, hiring the exec team, and being the primary relationship for key stakeholders cannot be outsourced. Investors and senior hires want the CEO. A board chair can sit alongside, but the CEO is on the call. A recruiter can source, but the CEO closes the offer. A Chief of Staff can prepare the deck, but the CEO presents.

**Don't:** Use "the CEO has only two jobs" to dodge a hire you should be making, a fire you should be putting out, or a stakeholder you've gone quiet with.
**Why:** The two-jobs framing is permission to stop doing things someone else can do. It is not permission to stop doing things only you can do. If your CFO has been signaling for two months that funding is tight and you've been "letting them handle it," that's not delegation — that's avoidance.

**Don't:** Try to clear your calendar before you've hired your replacement.
**Why:** The open calendar requires that someone is doing the work you used to do. Walk out before that happens and you create a vacuum that fills with chaos and fires that didn't need to start. Hire first. Hand off. *Then* the calendar opens.

**Don't:** Insist on running every department because "no one else cares as much."
**Why:** Someone out there genuinely loves running people operations / sales operations / finance / engineering. They will do it better than you because they love it. You are blocking the company from getting that person by occupying the seat. (See `energy-audit.md` for the Zone of Excellence trap.)

**Don't:** Forget that the vision job has a *speaking* requirement.
**Why:** A vision document on a wiki page that no one reads is not a vision. The vision is the thing the CEO repeats out loud, in person and on video, until the team can recite it back. If you've stopped saying it, you've stopped doing the job.

**Don't:** Take a one-hour afternoon nap and feel guilty about it.
**Why:** The best CEOs Mochary coaches do exactly this. Restoration is part of being available; a depleted CEO can't run the fireman role and can't paint a clear vision. Guilt is a signal that the old "busy = productive" frame is still running. Notice it, set it down, take the nap.
