# On Time and Present

## When to Use
- You are the senior person in the room whose habits set the ceiling for everyone else's behaviour.
- You are chronically four-to-seven minutes late to your own meetings and have explained it to yourself as "I'm busy."
- Your meetings produce decisions that mysteriously never become actions.
- You're physically in meetings but mentally on Slack, and you've started to notice your team has copied this behaviour.
- You want a single low-cost discipline that signals respect to customers, investors, recruits, and your own team — without saying anything about respect at all.

## Fails When
- **You optimize for "on time" while remaining absent.** Showing up at exactly 10:00 with your phone in your hand and your head in another conversation is worse than arriving at 10:03 fully present. The goal is both, not one.
- **You make the discipline about you.** It is about the people on the other side of your meetings. The moment you frame it as a personal-productivity practice, it stops working — because you'll cut it whenever it personally inconveniences you.
- **You apply this without also fixing meeting structure.** Being on time to a badly-designed meeting is just a more punctual waste of an hour. Fix the meeting (see `one-on-one-meeting.md` and the Issue/Solution protocol) alongside the discipline of attending it well.

## Core Concept

It is critical to be on time for every appointment you have made, or to let the others involved know you will be late as soon as you realize it. This is common decency, yes — but it has greater significance. There is a person on the other side of your agreement to start the meeting at a specific time. They have stopped what they were working on to attend the meeting on time. If you do not show up on time, they cannot start, but they also cannot leave — because they don't know if you'll appear in the next minute or not. Each minute they are stranded is a minute of productivity you have stolen from them. If they are a customer, an investor, or a recruit, they will not engage with your company. If they report to you, they will keep quiet but resent you. There is no winning scenario when you waste someone's time.

But life happens. Calls run late. Traffic doesn't cooperate. Even with careful planning, it's not possible to be on time for every meeting. The good news is that you don't need to be. **It is only critical to let the other members of the meeting know you will be late as soon as you realize it** — and the realization, plus the message, must happen *before* the meeting starts, ideally before they have to break away from whatever they were doing. The asymmetry is enormous. Showing up two minutes late without a message produces resentment. Showing up two minutes late after a message at T-minus-five-minutes produces gratitude.

In addition to being on time, you must also be **present**. Being present means you are composed, prepared, and focused on the subject matter. It can take a few minutes to get present — prepare the agenda, research the topic and the attendees, get a glass of water, use the restroom between back-to-back meetings. Therefore, plan to arrive at an outside meeting fifteen minutes before it begins. For a meeting in your office, wrap up your current project five to ten minutes before the scheduled meeting time. To make this easy, schedule 25- and 50-minute meetings only — Google Calendar even has an automated setting for this. The buffer gives you five minutes each half-hour and ten minutes each hour to gather yourself.

> "Each minute they are away from their work is a minute of productivity you have stolen from them. This action is not only disrespectful but also counterproductive. If the attendee is a customer, investor, or recruit, they will not engage with your company. If they report to you, they will keep quiet but resent you. There is no winning scenario when you waste someone's time." (*On Time and Present*, Mochary curriculum)

> "When in the meeting, I often see CEOs making the mistake of constantly checking their messages. They cannot get away from being 'on,' if even for a second. This habit is disrespectful, but it also defeats the purpose of the meeting, which is a collaboration with the attendees present. It sends a message that the meeting's content is relatively unimportant. Furthermore, it also breeds a bad habit for the entire company — one that will be hard, if not impossible, to break down the line." (*On Time and Present*, Mochary curriculum)

The phone in the meeting is the most common failure of presence. CEOs check their messages because they cannot get away from being "on" if even for a second. The team copies this. Within a quarter, your meetings have eight people in them and four conversations happening — three of them on Slack. Productivity collapses while looking like activity. **During every meeting, leave your phone in your pocket or face-down.** Inbox Zero discipline outside of meetings makes this possible inside meetings. If the meeting is not efficient enough to deserve your full attention, the answer is not to half-attend; the answer is to redesign the meeting so it is.

### First 2 Minutes of Actions — converting presence into output

Being present is not the deliverable. Being present *so the meeting actually produces action* is the deliverable. A meeting where everyone was on time, fully attentive, and ended with decisions that nobody started — is still a meeting that wasted everyone's time. The First 2 Minutes of Actions framework is what converts presence into momentum.

The principle is borrowed and named: Frank Slootman, in *Amp It Up*, calls it "Bias to Action." In meetings, team members commit to deadlines like *"I'll do this by next week."* Slootman's challenge: *"Why can't you get it done by tomorrow?"* Speeding up actions accelerates the entire company's momentum. Mochary's extension is one step further: **Why not right now?** The hardest part of any task is starting. So begin the first two minutes of every agreed-upon action *during* the meeting itself — reserve the last 10 minutes of every meeting to initiate action steps.

The triage is simple. For each action that comes out of the meeting:

- **Action you can do yourself, takes less than 2 minutes:** do it immediately, in the room.
- **Action you can do yourself, takes more than 2 minutes:** estimate the time, create a calendar event with the action as the title (e.g., *"Write client proposal — 30 mins"*) — block it now.
- **Action involves someone else, you have a meeting with them already:** add it to the agenda for that meeting. Maintain a running notes section listing people you regularly meet with, with discussion points under their names.
- **Action involves someone else, no upcoming meeting, message takes less than 2 minutes:** send the message immediately, in the room.
- **Action involves someone else, no upcoming meeting, message takes more than 2 minutes:** estimate the time (e.g., 15 minutes for a detailed email), block that time on your calendar to complete it.

Why this works: starting makes follow-through easier. Small beginnings produce a snowball effect — the first two minutes of an action create enough momentum that the rest follows almost on its own. The alternative — leaving the meeting with five "I'll do that this week" items — produces the standard outcome where 60% of those items are still not done in three weeks because nobody started.

## How to Apply

1. **Schedule 25- and 50-minute meetings, not 30 and 60.** Use Google Calendar's automated setting. The five-or-ten-minute buffer is what makes presence possible at the next meeting. Without the buffer, you arrive at every meeting half-present from the previous one.

2. **Arrive 15 minutes early to outside meetings, 5–10 minutes early to internal ones.** Use the time to prepare the agenda, research the topic, research the attendees, settle physically.

3. **The moment you realize you'll be late, message — before the meeting starts.** Whatever channel reaches them fastest. The message is a sentence: *"Running 5 late, sorry."* Don't apologize at length; just deliver the information. The cost of the message is 10 seconds; the cost of not sending it is the recipient's stranded time plus their resentment.

4. **Phone face-down or in your pocket. Every meeting. No exceptions.** If a real emergency requires interruption, brief one person to monitor it and pull you out — don't outsource your attention to your own pocket.

5. **In the last 10 minutes of every meeting, run First 2 Minutes of Actions.** Walk down the action list. For each item, apply the triage above: do it now if under 2 minutes; calendar-block it if longer; add to agenda if it belongs in another meeting; message now or schedule the message time. Do not leave the meeting until each action has a real next step, not a vague intention.

6. **Maintain a notes doc per recurring person.** Under each name, keep a running list of "things to discuss next time we meet." When an action surfaces in another meeting that involves them, drop it into their list. The next one-on-one or sync agenda is already half-written.

7. **When you're in the meeting, *be in the meeting*.** This is the soft skill that does the heavy lifting. Eye contact. Listening. Repeating back what people said until they say "that's right" (see `heard.md`). Putting down whatever you were thinking about before the meeting. The other side feels the difference within ninety seconds.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder runs a weekly all-hands. He's three to seven minutes late to it every week because of overrunning customer calls. The all-hands of 35 people sits and waits each Monday. He says he doesn't know how to fix it because the customer calls are real.

**Application:** Two changes. First, the customer calls become 25-minute calls instead of 30, scheduled with the 5-minute Google Calendar buffer — which gets him to the all-hands on time. Second, on the rare week the customer call genuinely overruns, he sends a Slack to the all-hands at 8:55am: *"Running 5 late, please start without me; I'll join when I can."* The team is empowered to begin without him. Net result: the all-hands now starts on time every week. He arrives within five minutes when he's late, which the team is now informed about in advance.

**Result:** Across 35 people, the recovered time over a quarter is roughly 25–30 hours of stranded productivity recovered. More importantly, the team stops resenting the start time and starts engaging earlier, because they're no longer marinating in five minutes of waiting before each meeting begins.

---

**Situation:** A board meeting where the CEO is on her phone for substantial chunks. The board members are senior. They notice. The board chair flags it to her privately afterwards.

**Application:** Phone goes in a drawer or a bag for the entire next board meeting. She tells her assistant before the meeting: "If anything urgent comes up in the next three hours, text \[name\] and pull me out. Otherwise it waits." She arrives 15 minutes early, fully prepared. She doesn't open her laptop except to project the deck. She runs First 2 Minutes of Actions in the last 10 minutes — every action item from the board has an owner, a deadline, and a calendar-blocked starting moment before they all leave the room.

**Result:** The board chair tells her at the end the meeting was the sharpest he'd seen her run. The action items from board meetings, which historically converted to completion at maybe 50%, now convert at 90%+ because each one had its starting two minutes already done.

---

**Situation:** A founder consistently leaves meetings with action items that don't get started for three weeks. He says he's "too busy to actually start them in the meeting."

**Application:** This is exactly the failure mode First 2 Minutes was designed for. The "too busy" is real — but the busy is *because* nothing ever gets started in meetings, so every action item is a future block of work fighting against every other action item for unscheduled time. The triage in the last 10 minutes converts each item: a 90-second message becomes a 90-second message; a 30-minute draft becomes a 30-minute calendar block. The action either happens in the room, or has a real future home — but it never sits on a list called "later."

**Result:** Four weeks of running this, his completion rate on meeting action items goes from roughly 40% to roughly 85%. The remaining 15% turn out, on inspection, to be items that shouldn't have been actions in the first place — which is also useful information.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apologize at length when you're late.
**Why:** Long apologies waste more of the other person's time. The acknowledgement is a sentence: *"Sorry I'm late, the previous call ran. Let's get into it."* The repair is in the next behaviour, not in the apology.

**Don't:** Treat "running 5 minutes late" as a normal recurring state.
**Why:** Five minutes late, every meeting, every week, is not lateness — it's a culture you are personally installing. The team will copy you. Within a quarter, every meeting starts seven minutes late, every meeting overruns by ten, and you've lost 25% of your meeting capacity. Be on time, or actively renegotiate the meeting times to ones you can keep.

**Don't:** Use "I'm checking Slack for an emergency" as cover for checking Slack.
**Why:** The team can tell. They know what your face looks like when you're scanning a feed versus when you're handling a real fire. The pretense is worse than the absence — because now you've also taught the team that you will lie about your attention.

**Don't:** Skip First 2 Minutes "because the meeting already ran long."
**Why:** A meeting that ran long *and* skipped First 2 Minutes is two meetings: the one you just had, and the one you'll need next week to recover the actions that nobody started. Cutting the last 10 minutes is the most expensive shortcut on the calendar. Cut the middle of the meeting if you must; do not cut the action triage.

**Don't:** Schedule back-to-back 60-minute meetings on a packed day.
**Why:** Back-to-back 60s guarantee you are late and unprepared for every meeting after the first. 25/50-minute scheduling is not optional for a CEO; it is what makes presence physically possible.

**Don't:** Confuse "showing up" with "delivering value."
**Why:** Punctuality is the entry ticket to the meeting. Presence is the actual product. First 2 Minutes is what turns the product into momentum. All three are required. Skip any one and the meeting was a worse use of time than not holding it.

**Don't:** Privately exempt yourself from this discipline because you're the senior person.
**Why:** The senior person is exactly who the discipline is most important for. Your habits set the cultural ceiling. A team will not be more punctual or more present than its CEO. If you skip the rules, you are the rules.
