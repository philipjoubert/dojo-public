# Startup = Growth

## When to Use
- Founder is choosing what to work on and can't decide between five options.
- Founder is reporting metrics in a format that obscures growth (total users, cumulative revenue, vanity charts).
- Founder is confused about why their small business isn't getting startup-style attention from investors.
- Founder is spending time on strategy, deals, partnerships — anything that isn't directly moving a weekly number.

## Fails When
- The company genuinely isn't a startup (lifestyle business, consultancy, service firm) — forcing growth framing on it doesn't help.
- Very late stage where growth rate is no longer the single dominant metric — unit economics, retention curves, category position become load-bearing.

## Core Concept

A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary to work on technology, take venture funding, or have some sort of "exit." The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth.

This is a more precise definition than most people use, and it has surprising implications. A barbershop is not a startup no matter how recently it was founded. A barbershop doesn't scale — it serves customers in person, and few will travel far for a haircut. A company that figures out how to cut hair at scale via some new technology might be. The difference is like the difference between a redwood seedling and a bean sprout. They look similar at the earliest stage. They have different destinies. Google is not just a barbershop whose founders were lucky and hard-working. Google was different from the beginning.

To grow rapidly, you need to make something you can sell to a big market. That means (a) make something lots of people want and (b) reach and serve all those people. Most businesses are tightly constrained in (a) or (b). The distinctive feature of successful startups is that they're not. The constraints that limit ordinary companies also protect them — if you open a barbershop you only compete with local barbers; if you start a search engine you compete with the whole world. That's why the ideas remaining for startups tend to be ones where you serve a big market without being killed by competition, usually because you've discovered something everyone else has overlooked.

The growth of a successful startup has three phases. A slow or no-growth period while you figure out what you're doing. A steep ascent when you've figured out how to make something lots of people want and how to reach them. And a slower phase as you bump up against the limits of your market. The S-curve. The phase that defines a startup is the ascent. The slope of that ascent is your growth rate. If there's one number every founder should always know, it's that number.

## How to Apply

1. **Measure weekly. Growth rate, not absolute count.** During YC we measure growth rate per week. 5-7% a week is good. 10% is exceptional. 1% is a sign you haven't figured out what you're doing. "We got 100 new users this month" is not a rate. If you keep getting a constant number of new users every month, your growth rate is decreasing. Rates, not totals.

2. **Pick a target growth rate and hit it every week.** The key word is *just*. If you decide to grow at 7% a week and you hit it, you're successful for that week. There is nothing more you need to do. If you don't, you've failed in the only thing that mattered and should be correspondingly alarmed. This reduces starting a startup to an optimization problem. Programmers know how wonderfully effective that kind of narrow focus can be.

3. **Use growth as the decision compass.** Should I spend two days at that conference? Should I hire another programmer? Should I focus more on marketing? Whatever gets you to your target growth rate is right; whatever doesn't is wrong. This sounds reductive because it is. That's the point. Founders who can't decide what to work on usually can't because they're not using a strong enough filter. Growth is the strongest filter available.

4. **Small variations in rate produce qualitatively different outcomes.**
   - 1% weekly = 1.7x yearly
   - 2% weekly = 2.8x yearly
   - 5% weekly = 12.6x yearly
   - 7% weekly = 33.7x yearly
   - 10% weekly = 142x yearly
   
   Compounding defeats intuition. A startup at $1k/month growing 1% a week is at $1.7k after a year, which is nothing. The same startup at 5% a week is at $12.6k. At 10% a week, $142k. Different companies. Different futures. The rate is the whole game.

5. **If you don't hit the number, investigate — don't just try harder.** Missing a growth target is data. It means something in the engine is broken — maybe the product isn't a must-have for the users you have, maybe the funnel is leaky, maybe the user love isn't deep enough to drive referrals. "Just try harder next week" without diagnosing is obstinacy, not persistence.

6. **Hitting a growth rate can actually discover your startup idea.** If you start with an initial plan and modify it as necessary to keep hitting 10% weekly growth, you may end up with a very different company than you meant to start. That's fine — anything that grows consistently at 10% a week is almost certainly a better idea than you started with. For startups, growth is a constraint much like truth is for a scientist. Feynman's observation: the imagination of nature was greater than the imagination of man. If you just keep following the truth you'll discover cooler things than you could ever have made up. Growth works the same way.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder reports: "Great month! We got 430 new signups." The previous month was 420. The month before was 410.

**Application:** That's not a great month. That's a roughly flat month in growth-rate terms, and flat growth is a signal the engine is broken. Let me rewrite the report. 410 → 420 → 430 is roughly 2.4% month-over-month, which is under 0.6% weekly. That's in the "haven't figured it out yet" zone. You need to do something different. The most likely culprits: (1) you aren't actively recruiting users, you're waiting for them; (2) the users you have aren't telling their friends because you haven't made them love the product hard enough to do that work for you; (3) you're adding features instead of talking to users. Which one is it? Pick one to attack this week, measure what moves, and iterate. Absolute-count growth is how you tell yourself a flat trajectory is going well. Rate is how you tell yourself the truth.

**Result:** Founder switches to weekly reporting, realizes the 430 is actually 5 weeks × ~86/week while year ago was 4 weeks × ~100/week (so rate is declining), commits to 7%/week target, gets back on the curve within six weeks.

---

**Situation:** Founder is debating two ways to spend the next quarter: (1) build three big features customers have requested, (2) raise a Series A. "Which is more important?"

**Application:** The wrong framing. Use the growth compass. Which of those two actions moves the weekly number more? If the three features make existing users love the product more and bring in new ones, they move the number. If the fundraise is a distraction that sucks your attention for three months and delivers money you don't need for another six, it doesn't. You'd be fundraising to look like a real company, not to be one. Now, if the features don't actually move growth — maybe users were requesting polish that doesn't attract new users — then *neither* of these is the right use of the quarter, and you should be asking what actually moves the number. Growth as compass isn't "pick between two plausible actions." It's "drop anything that doesn't point at the number, and find the action that does."

**Result:** Founder postpones Series A, builds the feature that most clearly increases retention, sees growth rate climb from 4% weekly to 8%, and raises three months later on a much better chart.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Report growth as absolute numbers.
**Why:** Totals can grow indefinitely while your rate collapses. "We're up 20k users this year" can describe a company doubling or a company flattening. Only rates show the real trajectory.

**Don't:** Optimize for revenue before growth is real.
**Why:** In the early stage, growth matters more than revenue, because a higher growth rate makes everything else easier — fundraising, recruiting, morale, even product development. Revenue comes from growth if growth is real. Optimizing revenue too early produces local maxima that kill your ability to find the global one.

**Don't:** Confuse a startup with a small business.
**Why:** The economics are different. A barbershop and a search engine obey different laws even if both were founded last month. Treating a small business like a startup (raising money, burning for growth) makes it fragile. Treating a startup like a small business (bootstrapping carefully, waiting for profitability) makes it lose.

**Don't:** Rationalize why the number was low this week.
**Why:** Founders have infinite capacity to invent reasonable stories about why a bad week was actually fine. "Holiday." "Seasonal." "Big enterprise deal took our attention." None of those save you. The reasonable-sounding story is the lie you tell yourself so you don't have to act.

**Don't:** Judge the median startup by the average one's outcomes.
**Why:** The median startup fails. What matters is the average outcome across all startups, dragged up by enormous successes. Judging startups by the median is like judging the fishing industry by the average fish — most of the value is in the outliers. For you specifically, this means: your tolerance for small-probability large-payoff bets should be calibrated to the distribution, not the median.

**Don't:** Let "strategy" and "planning" substitute for hitting the number.
**Why:** Nine times out of ten, sitting around strategizing is just a form of procrastination. Having to hit a growth number every week forces action, and acting versus not acting is the high bit of succeeding.
