# Keep Your Identity Small

## When to Use
- Founder is emotionally stuck on a decision that involves a belief they hold as part of who they are.
- Founder is in a dispute (with a cofounder, with investors, online) where both sides seem unable to update.
- Founder's thinking on a particular topic keeps hitting a wall that looks more like a defended boundary than a reasoned conclusion.
- Founder describes themselves with labels (political, methodological, tribal) and then wonders why they can't think clearly about related topics.
- Founder gets *angry* when someone questions a position rather than *curious*.

## Fails When
- The user is asking about external communications, branding, or positioning — those are public identity questions, not personal thinking questions.
- The question is really about how to choose values for a company — values are a different thing than identities, and the small-identity rule doesn't map cleanly onto them.

## Core Concept

I finally realized one day why politics and religion yield such uniquely useless discussions. It's not that the questions they address don't have answers. Some do. It's that people's opinions about them become part of their identity, and once that happens, it's over. You can't think clearly about anything that has become part of who you are.

This is one of those insights that seems obvious once you hear it but that almost nobody acts on. The more labels you have for yourself, the dumber they make you. Every time you say "I'm a ___," you've just installed a filter that will silently distort your thinking about everything connected to that label. The distortion is invisible from the inside. It feels to you like you're just seeing the world clearly.

That, in a nutshell, is why independent thinking is so hard. Not because the techniques are complex, but because you're fighting an enemy that's evolved to be invisible.

If people can't think clearly about anything that has become part of their identity, then all other things being equal, the best plan is to let as few things into your identity as possible. Most of the really dumb beliefs I've held in my life were ones I held because they'd become part of my identity. I wasn't evaluating them as hypotheses; I was defending them as territory. And I didn't know I was doing it.

Consider how differently people argue about politics versus, say, which way a river flows. A geologist can calmly discuss which way water drains in a particular watershed; if someone presents evidence contradicting their initial guess, they update. But suggest that a political party's position is wrong and watch what happens. They don't evaluate the evidence. They get angry. The identity has been threatened.

Almost anything can become part of your identity if you let it. Your profession, your college, your political party, your nationality, your religion, your diet, the programming language you use, the operating system on your laptop. Each one is a potential blindness generator. I'm not saying you shouldn't have views about these things. You should. But there's a difference between thinking something is true and making it part of who you are. The former lets you update; the latter makes updating feel like dying.

## How to Apply

1. **Use anger as a diagnostic.** When you notice yourself getting angry about an idea, ask whether the anger is really about the idea, or about what accepting it would mean for your identity. That moment of anger is a signal. It tells you exactly where your blind spots are. Anger at an idea is an identity marker. Note it and hold that area suspect in your own thinking.

2. **Distinguish "I think X" from "I am X."** "I think the lean startup method often works" is a belief that can update. "I'm a Lean Startup person" is an identity that will torture reality to stay true. "I think Python is usually the right tool" is a preference. "I'm a Python programmer" is a cap on the languages you'll honestly evaluate.

3. **Prefer meta-identities to particular ones.** Being a scientist is one of the safer identities, because it's a commitment to following evidence rather than to any particular belief. But even "scientist" can corrode if it makes you dismiss ideas from outside your field or defend your published work past its expiration date. The meta-identities — "I follow evidence," "I update when the data changes," "I try to think for myself" — are less restrictive because they don't lock you to a particular conclusion.

4. **Periodically audit your labels.** Every few years, list the labels you claim. For each one, ask: is there any evidence that could make me abandon this label? If the answer is no, the label is not a belief. It's a religion. That may be fine for some labels (family, profession) — but it means you should not expect to think clearly about anything connected to them.

5. **Pick your battles in what to say out loud.** Thinking for yourself does not mean saying everything you think. If you feel you have to say everything you think, it may inhibit you from thinking improper thoughts. Milton's advice applies: *i pensieri stretti & il viso sciolto* — closed thoughts and an open face. Smile at everyone, don't tell them what you're thinking. Argue with idiots and you become one. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. Keep an inner space of free thought, and be strategic about what you express.

6. **Make a few trusted friends with whom you can speak openly.** The people you can say heretical things to without getting jumped on are the most interesting people to know. Seek them out. They need you as much as you need them. This is one of the most underweighted career moves available.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder has strong convictions about a technical stack and is refusing to consider migrating even when the evidence from their team clearly says the stack is slowing them down. They say "I'm a Ruby programmer — that's just who I am."

**Application:** That sentence is the problem. Not the stack choice. Once "Ruby programmer" is your identity, you've installed a filter that will distort every evaluation of every technology you touch. The honest version is "I know Ruby well, it's been the right tool for me, and I want to check whether it's still the right tool for the company." The first version is a defended identity. The second is a belief that can update. Run the honest evaluation — not to conclude one way or another, but to take your identity off the scales so the evaluation is about the company's actual needs, not about who you think you are.

**Result:** Founder runs the evaluation. Confirms Ruby is still the right choice for 60% of the codebase and the wrong choice for a specific new service that needs different properties. Without the identity defense, the decision gets made on evidence, not loyalty.

---

**Situation:** Two cofounders are in a months-long argument about an ethical question related to their product. Both have become rigid. Both are increasingly angry. Neither is updating on the other's arguments.

**Application:** You are both treating this as an identity question, not a reasoning question. The giveaway is the anger. When people argue about a fact — which way the river flows, whether the number is accurate — they don't get angry when contradicted, they get curious. When they get angry, the identity has been threatened. Step back. Each of you write down separately: what is the specific decision the company needs to make, and what evidence would make me change my position? If either of you answers "no evidence would change me," you've revealed that this isn't a belief for you — it's a territorial marker. That's fine for your personal life, but it's fatal as a decision-making input for the company. You either need to find the decision that both of you can live with given the space of positions you hold, or find an outside mediator whose view neither of you has already categorized as hostile. But trying to "win" an identity argument will tear the partnership apart before you resolve the question.

**Result:** Founders adopt the written-evidence frame. One of them has a belief that genuinely no evidence could change; the other has a preference that could move given data. They negotiate around the first person's non-negotiable, find the envelope of workable options, and move on. The partnership survives because they stopped fighting about identity and started bounding the decision.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "keep your identity small" with "have no convictions."
**Why:** The advice is not against strong views. It's against tethering those views to who you are. You can hold a very strong view ("the product must ship before year-end") as a belief you would update given strong counter-evidence, without making it part of your identity. The difference is whether the belief can be killed by data or only by an identity crisis.

**Don't:** Turn "independent thinker" into a new identity.
**Why:** The meta-identity "independent thinker" has its own trap — you start performing independence by disagreeing with the consensus for the sake of disagreeing. True independent thinking has no brand. It's invisible in your behavior because you're updating freely, which means sometimes agreeing with the majority. The "contrarian" as identity is just conformism in reverse.

**Don't:** Use "identity is small" to dodge legitimate values disagreements.
**Why:** There are questions where you should have a firm position — ethics, how you treat people, what you build for. Small identity doesn't mean no values. It means holding beliefs as beliefs, not as fortifications.

**Don't:** Treat the anger signal as a license to shut down the conversation.
**Why:** The anger tells you your identity has been threatened. That's diagnostic for you — an invitation to examine your own defended territory — not a reason to get the other person to stop talking. The most valuable conversations of your life will sometimes be the ones that made you angry.

**Don't:** Express everything you think.
**Why:** Thinking what you want and saying what you want are different. The latter imposes a social cost that often ends up constraining the former — you stop thinking things you can't say. Keep the inner space free. Pick the battles you fight in public. The world is generally wrong about enough things that you'll have plenty of territory to work in even after subtracting the battles you decline.
