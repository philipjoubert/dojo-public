# Relentlessly Resourceful

## When to Use
- Founder is assessing what makes themselves (or a cofounder, early hire, or portfolio founder) likely to succeed.
- Founder is stuck, pushing the same approach against the same wall, and needs to hear that persistence ≠ obstinacy.
- Investor or operator is trying to articulate the quality they're looking for.
- A founder is evaluating their own reaction to setbacks: fire-and-forget or hand-waved?

## Fails When
- The real issue is market, product, or positioning and the person question is a distraction.
- "Relentlessly resourceful" is being invoked as a badge to avoid legitimate feedback — conversion of a description into an identity, which breaks it.

## Core Concept

I once tried to find a phrase that compressed what makes a great startup founder. The best I came up with was two words: *relentlessly resourceful*. That's the ideal founder quality. Not just determined, not just smart, but both persistent and adaptable. The opposite of a good startup founder is not "unsuccessful." It's *hapless*. A hapless person looks at an obstacle and gives up. A relentlessly resourceful person looks at an obstacle and starts looking for a way around it, over it, through it, or underneath it.

Make something people want is the destination. Be relentlessly resourceful is how you get there.

There are five qualities I see recurring in the founders who succeed, and they cluster into that two-word compression:

**Determination** is the first and most important. Empirically, it predicts success better than intelligence, better than experience, better than connections. It's a combination of willfulness and discipline, aimed by ambition. Think of it like a melon seed between your fingers — squeeze it and it doesn't crumble, it shoots forward. Willful people are like that. Pressure doesn't stop them; it propels them.

**Flexibility** is the second — willingness to change your idea. This seems contradictory with determination but it isn't. The persistent person holds the goal fixed and varies the approach. The obstinate person holds both fixed. Obstinacy is the anaerobic respiration of determination — it works briefly but produces toxins. The key differentiator is imagination: persistent people keep finding new ways around obstacles; obstinate people just try harder at the same approach.

**Imagination** is the third — the ability to see possibilities others miss. Specifically, the ability to generate new ideas, not just evaluate existing ones.

**Naughtiness** is the fourth — willingness to break rules. Not evil. The way a child breaks rules, because the child doesn't yet know they exist, or because the rules seem arbitrary. The most successful founders have a streak of this. They look at the way things are done and think, *why?*

**Friendship between cofounders** is the fifth — often undervalued, it matters enormously. A startup is so stressful that if the cofounders don't have genuine bonds of friendship, the stress will tear them apart.

Combine those five and compress into two words: relentlessly resourceful. The best founders are what I call fire-and-forget — give them an introduction or a piece of advice and they'll chase it to completion. The worst have a glazed-over look when you give them a suggestion. They filter everything through their existing plan and reject anything that doesn't fit. A word to the wise is sufficient. The best founders need only the tiniest lead.

## How to Apply

1. **Distinguish persistent from obstinate.** When you push back on a persistent person's plan, they engage — maybe even with almost predatory intensity. The Collison brothers are the canonical example. Point out a problem and they listen hard, because if there's a hole in their boat they want to know about it. Obstinate people, by contrast, glaze over. Their replies sound like ideologues talking about matters of doctrine. If you feel the glaze in yourself or someone on your team, you're in obstinacy, not persistence.

2. **Vary approach, fix the goal.** The distinction between persistence and obstinacy is imagination. If you've hit the same wall three times, the fix is not more force. It's a different angle. Energy creates demand for new ideas; imagination supplies them; resilience holds the morale; judgment picks which approach to abandon; focus on the goal keeps the whole machine pointed. Five qualities working together. Lose any one and the others fail.

3. **The fire-and-forget test.** When you give a founder a lead — a user to talk to, a hire to pursue, a fix to try — do they report back within a week that they chased it to completion? Or do you never hear about it again? That single test is one of the most predictive signals I know. Fire-and-forget founders do not need management. Haplessness is the opposite signature: glazed eyes, filtered-through-existing-plan, "we'll think about it."

4. **Look for naughtiness.** Quiet, rule-respecting compliance is a warning sign in a founder, not an asset. The people who change things have a streak of "why?" that the merely competent lack. They look at a queue and wonder why the queue exists. They look at a permission and wonder why you need it. Inside healthy limits, this is exactly what you want. Founders without any naughtiness will keep running on the rails laid down for them.

5. **Friendship is the structural stabilizer.** Cofounder friendship is one of the most underweighted variables in evaluating teams. Not "we worked together for a year," but "we've been friends since before this idea, we could have dinner with each other's partners, we could say the hard truth to each other and still respect each other." Without that base, the stress of a startup fractures the team at exactly the moment you most need it whole.

6. **Use formidability as a proxy.** Formidable is close to confident, except confident can be mistaken. Formidable is justifiably confident — the person who seems like they'll get what they want regardless of whatever obstacles are in the way. You become formidable by understanding your own thing deeply. The way an inexperienced founder seems most formidable is to stick to the truth. Convince yourself your startup is worth investing in via genuine evaluation, then say that. Formidable people don't perform confidence; they radiate competence.

## Examples

**Situation:** An investor is assessing two founders. Founder A has an impressive résumé, speaks fluently, has all the right contacts, says "yes" to suggestions but little changes. Founder B is awkward, less polished, has no connections, but three days after you give them a lead, the lead reports that B found them, cold-called, closed the meeting, and got the introduction to someone three steps further down the chain.

**Application:** Back Founder B. Every time. What you're seeing is the fire-and-forget signal. A is performing founderhood; B is doing it. Polish is not a signal of capacity — often it's a signal of time spent perfecting an appearance rather than building things. The hapless-vs-relentless axis does not correlate with impressive-résumé-vs-unknown. The Collison brothers are relentless. Many people with twice their résumé at the same age would have been hapless. If you have to choose, choose the one who chases leads to completion.

**Result:** Investor backs B. Over the next year, B ships three versions, pivots once, keeps chasing, and gets to product-market fit. A, in a different portfolio, raises a larger seed, speaks at two conferences, and produces nothing ship-able in the same period.

---

**Situation:** A founder has been trying to close an enterprise deal for four months. Same pitch. Same contact. Same objections. They say: "I'm going to keep pushing, I'm determined."

**Application:** You're not being determined. You're being obstinate. Four months of the same pitch to the same contact with the same objections is not persistence; it's the anaerobic respiration of determination. Determination varies the approach while holding the goal fixed. What's varied in four months? If the answer is "nothing major," you're standing at the wall pushing harder. Change the approach. Go around the contact (another stakeholder at the same customer, a different champion, a different decision-maker at a peer company). Change the shape of the deal (smaller pilot, different pricing model, different scope). Change the hypothesis entirely (maybe this customer isn't going to buy and a different profile of customer will — which is data, not failure). Determination that doesn't vary the approach is how founders end up four months older with the same stuck pipeline, convinced they're being tough when they're actually being predictable.

**Result:** Founder pivots to a different decision-maker at a peer company. Deal closes in six weeks. The original customer remained stuck; that was a feature of the customer, not the product, and no amount of determination was going to move them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Interpret the phrase as a license for obstinacy.
**Why:** "Relentlessly resourceful" contains the word *resourceful*. Resourceful means varying approach, finding paths around, using imagination. The phrase is not a cover for refusing to update when the evidence says your current approach is wrong.

**Don't:** Mistake polish for formidability.
**Why:** Polished founders are often trained to pass interviews, not to build. The real signal is fire-and-forget execution, not well-practiced answers to the investor-favorite questions. Watch what they do for the week after you give them feedback — that's the signal, not the charm of the meeting itself.

**Don't:** Undervalue cofounder friendship.
**Why:** Startups are stress tests for relationships. Professional relationships don't survive those tests. Real friendships usually do. Teams that met at a hackathon and "just clicked" are betting that the click will hold through the hardest 24 months of their lives. Usually it doesn't. Pre-existing friendship is a structural stabilizer no amount of equity alignment replaces.

**Don't:** Recruit the brilliant-but-hapless person hoping determination will develop.
**Why:** It very rarely does. Haplessness is a durable pattern, not a skill gap. Smart and hapless is one of the most common and tragic combinations — the person keeps receiving credit for the ideas but never converts them, and everyone around them spends years wondering why nothing ships.

**Don't:** Take the description as a self-assigned identity.
**Why:** Saying "I'm relentlessly resourceful" is not evidence you are. The test is behavioral: does anyone who sends you a lead report that you chased it? Do problems on your list get resolved or re-appear? Identity-based self-description is often a hedge against the behavioral test. Keep the description as something earned by the pattern, not claimed in advance.
