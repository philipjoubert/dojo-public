# Schlep Blindness

## When to Use
- A founder is looking for ideas and keeps arriving at clever-software-only ideas — recipe sites, aggregators, marketplaces with no commercial friction — even though they sit on top of an industry full of painful unsolved problems.
- A founder dismisses an idea with "I don't want to deal with banks / hospitals / the DMV / legal review / enterprise sales" — the reasoning sounds practical but the dismissal is unconscious, not argued.
- You notice someone complaining for years about a specific painful infrastructure problem they could personally fix, but they keep starting companies that avoid it.
- A founder is considering two ideas — one pleasant-sounding, one with obvious schleps — and the pleasant one has no specific desperate users.

## Fails When
- The founder doesn't yet have enough domain exposure to see painful problems. They need to live in the future first; schlep blindness is about ideas the founder could see but won't, not ideas they haven't been positioned to see.
- The pain is real but no one actually wants the solution. Schlep blindness is the wrong diagnosis when the underlying opportunity doesn't exist — then you're just looking at a hard project with no users.
- The founder is already doing the schlep and suffering. That's a demoralization problem (`how-not-to-die`), not a blind-spot problem.

## Core Concept

There are great startup ideas lying around unexploited right under our noses. One reason we don't see them is a phenomenon I call *schlep blindness*. Schlep was originally a Yiddish word but has passed into general use. It means a tedious, unpleasant task.

No one likes schleps, but hackers especially dislike them. Most hackers who start startups wish they could do it by just writing some clever software, putting it on a server somewhere, and watching the money roll in — without ever having to talk to users, or negotiate with other companies, or deal with other people's broken code. Maybe that's possible. I haven't seen it.

One of the many things we do at YC is teach hackers about the inevitability of schleps. No, you can't start a startup by just writing code. Schleps are not merely inevitable, but pretty much what business consists of. **A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake.** And schleps should be dealt with the same way you'd deal with a cold swimming pool: just jump in. Not that you should seek out unpleasant work per se — but you should never shrink from it if it's on the path to something great.

The most dangerous thing about our dislike of schleps is that much of it is unconscious. **Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps.** That's schlep blindness. The phenomenon isn't limited to startups. Most people don't consciously decide not to be in as good physical shape as Olympic athletes. Their unconscious decides for them, shrinking from the work involved.

The most striking example I know of schlep blindness is Stripe, or rather Stripe's idea. For over a decade, every hacker who'd ever had to process payments online knew how painful the experience was. Thousands of people must have known about this problem. And yet when they started startups, they decided to build recipe sites, or aggregators for local events. Why? Why work on problems few care much about and no one will pay for, when you could fix one of the most important components of the world's infrastructure? Because schlep blindness prevented people from even considering the idea of fixing payments.

Probably no one who applied to YC to work on a recipe site began by asking "should we fix payments, or build a recipe site?" and chose the recipe site. The idea of fixing payments was right there in plain sight. They never saw it. Their unconscious mind shrank from the complications: you'd have to make deals with banks. How do you even do that? Plus you're moving money, so you're going to deal with fraud, and people trying to break into your servers. Plus regulations. It's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site.

**That scariness makes ambitious ideas doubly valuable.** In addition to their intrinsic value, they're like undervalued stocks in the sense that there's less demand for them among founders. If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition, because everyone else will have been frightened off.

## How to Apply

1. **Recognize the feeling, not the thought.** Schlep blindness is not an argument. It's a flinch. When your mind reflexively categorizes an area as "not my kind of problem," watch for the flinch. The flinch is the data. The subsequent reasoning is after-the-fact rationalization — "I'm not a compliance person," "I don't know healthcare," "the sales cycle is too long." Those statements are true of everyone before they start and almost never the real reason they avoid it.

2. **Switch the pronoun — ask what you wish someone else would build for you.** Ignorance is the most reliable antidote to schlep blindness, which is why young founders have an advantage: they make two mistakes that cancel out — they don't know how much they can grow, but they also don't know how much they'll need to. Older founders only make the first mistake. But for any founder, the trick is to take yourself out of the picture. Instead of asking "what problem should *I* solve?" ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?" If someone who had to process payments before Stripe had asked that, Stripe would have been one of the first things they'd have wished for.

3. **Treat the flinch as a lead indicator.** When multiple hackers in a domain complain about the same painful thing for years without anyone building a solution, that is a flag, not a warning. The pain is confirmed. The market's refusal to fix it is the opportunity. The competitor field is thin precisely because the unconscious keeps filtering this idea out of every founder's search.

4. **Don't confuse schlep with scam.** There's a difference between unpleasant-but-on-the-path-to-something-great and unpleasant-and-pointless. Schlep blindness tells you to stop flinching from the first category. It does not mean any painful idea is good. The must-have test still applies: who desperately needs this? If you can name specific humans and the pain is real, the schlep is a moat. If you can't, you're just signing up for a hard life with no reward.

5. **Jump in before you feel ready.** The reason founders grow with the problems is that they have to. But no one can predict, from outside, that they'll grow into it — not even older, more experienced founders. So the specific trap for experienced people is calibrating from the sidelines and concluding they can't do it. Young founders don't do this calculation and are therefore often right despite being less informed.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder with five years of experience as an engineer at a large healthcare company is brainstorming startup ideas. Their list: a recipe app that uses AI to substitute ingredients, a fitness tracker with social features, a marketplace for local handmade goods. They've dismissed healthcare outright ("I've seen enough of that industry, I want to build something fun").

**Application:** You've just described textbook schlep blindness. You spent five years inside an industry where dozens of painful infrastructure problems are obvious to you and invisible to almost every other founder. And the ideas on your list are ones any twenty-two-year-old who's never had a job could have generated. The "I want to build something fun" is not a preference, it's a flinch. Go back and ask yourself the other question: what did you and every other engineer at that healthcare company complain about weekly? What was the thing that, when you had to do it, made you consider quitting? What ten minutes of your week did you spend on some workaround because the tool you actually needed didn't exist? That is the idea. It will sound unpleasant. You'll protest that you don't want to deal with HIPAA or hospitals or integration hell. Yes. That's exactly why no one is building it. That's exactly why it would be valuable.

**Result:** Founder drops the three fun ideas. Six weeks later they start building a tool that removes a specific scheduling headache they and every other engineer in the industry have lived with for years. It involves dealing with hospital IT departments, which is exactly as bad as they feared. Within eighteen months they have contracts at eight hospital systems.

**Situation:** Founder pitches: "We're building a tool that helps creators edit short-form video faster." They're avoiding an adjacent idea they mentioned casually: "Yeah, ad agencies have a nightmare problem with getting clients to approve edits — but I don't want to deal with the agency world, the meetings, the politics."

**Application:** Listen to the two sentences again. One says "creators want this," which is a soft, universal, population-times-percentage market where it's hard to find anyone who desperately needs your v1. The other says "ad agencies have a nightmare problem with a specific workflow." The first is lame camouflaged as fun. The second is a real problem camouflaged as lame. The only reason you're not building the second is the flinch — "I don't want to deal with the agency world." That flinch is the entire reason nobody has built it. Stop and ask the other question: if you could wave a wand and have someone else build this agency-approval tool for you, would you pay for it? Would the ad agencies you know pay for it? If yes, you're in schlep-blindness territory. The flinch is the opportunity.

**Result:** Founder spends two weeks talking to three agency producers. Every one of them lights up at the description. They pivot. The product is uglier, the customer meetings are worse, the sales cycle is longer. The revenue starts month two.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "this sounds hard" with "this is a bad idea."
**Why:** Hard and bad are different axes. Hard-and-good is the best category of idea in the world — you get less competition, the moat is the difficulty itself, and the returns compound because the problem is real. Hard-and-bad is just hard. The filter is still "do specific people desperately need this?" — not "does this sound pleasant to me?"

**Don't:** Use your background as an argument for avoiding the thing your background uniquely positions you to fix.
**Why:** Your background is the only reason you could even see the problem. Everyone else who could build it is elsewhere. The schlep is your edge, not your excuse. If you're the only person in a thousand-mile radius who both understands the pain and could code the fix, the fact that you find it unpleasant is roughly the worst possible reason to pass.

**Don't:** Announce that you're solving the ambitious problem as a marketing hook.
**Why:** The advantage of schlep-blind markets is that your competitors are avoiding them unconsciously. That camouflage is free. If you telegraph that this is the Big Important Problem, you attract attention from people who would otherwise have kept ignoring it. Let it look boring and unglamorous as long as you can. Be the one quietly building the thing everyone else dismisses as painful.

**Don't:** Wait to feel ready before jumping in.
**Why:** You will never feel ready. If you wait, you will calibrate yourself out of the opportunity. Young founders succeed disproportionately at schlep-blind ideas precisely because they don't know enough to talk themselves out of it. If you're older and more experienced, apply the young-founder heuristic deliberately: don't calibrate. Jump.

**Don't:** Mistake "I don't care about this" for "this isn't a good idea."
**Why:** The two feelings are indistinguishable from the inside, but they're very different things. "I don't care about this" is a legitimate reason to pass — if you genuinely don't care, you won't do the decade of work, and there's no amount of market size that makes that worth it. But "this doesn't feel exciting because my mind is shrinking from the work involved" is schlep blindness wearing the costume of preference. Interrogate which one is happening before you trust the feeling.
