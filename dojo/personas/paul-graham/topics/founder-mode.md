# Founder Mode

## When to Use
- Founder is scaling the company past the point where they personally interact with every employee.
- Founder has been told, by VCs or professional managers, to "hire good people and give them room."
- Founder hired a professional exec, followed the conventional playbook, and things have gotten worse.
- Founder is trying to articulate why the standard management-book advice feels wrong.
- Founder is second-guessing themselves for being "too involved in the details."

## Fails When
- Founder mode becomes a justification for actual micromanaging of healthy teams doing well-understood work.
- The problem is one that genuinely requires specialist expertise the founder doesn't have (a specific legal issue, a manufacturing process) and the answer is in fact "hire the expert and get out of their way."
- The founder is using "founder mode" as a rationalization for insecurity-driven meddling rather than high-context engagement.

## Core Concept

At a YC event a while back, Brian Chesky gave a talk that everyone who was there will remember. The theme was that the conventional wisdom about how to run larger companies is mistaken. As Airbnb grew, well-meaning people told him he had to run the company a certain way for it to scale. Their advice could be summarized as "hire good people and give them room to do their jobs." He followed it. The results were disastrous. So he had to figure out a better way on his own — partly by studying how Steve Jobs ran Apple.

What struck me afterward was that the audience included many of the most successful founders we've funded, and one after another said the same thing had happened to them. They'd been given identical advice about how to run their companies, and instead of helping, it had damaged them.

Why was everyone telling these founders the wrong thing? The answer: what they were being told was how to run a company you *hadn't* founded — how to run a company if you're merely a professional manager. But this mode is so much less effective that to founders it feels broken. There are things founders can do that managers can't, and not doing them feels wrong to founders, because it is.

In effect there are two ways to run a company: founder mode and manager mode. Until now most people, even in Silicon Valley, implicitly assumed that scaling a startup meant switching to manager mode. We can infer the existence of another mode from the dismay of founders who've tried manager mode and from the success of their attempts to escape it.

Manager mode treats subtrees of the org chart as black boxes. You tell your direct reports what to do and it's up to them to figure out how. You don't get involved in the details. That would be micromanaging, which is bad. Hire good people and give them room. It sounds great when you describe it that way. Except, judging from the report of founder after founder, what this often turns out to mean in practice is: hire professional fakers and let them drive the company into the ground.

Founder mode, whatever exactly it consists of, is going to break the principle that the CEO should engage with the company only via direct reports. Skip-level meetings become the norm instead of a practice so unusual it has a name. Steve Jobs used to run an annual retreat for what he considered the 100 most important people at Apple, and these were not the 100 people highest on the org chart. Imagine the force of will it would take to do this at the average company. And imagine how useful it could be. It could make a big company feel like a startup.

## How to Apply

1. **Reject "hire good people and give them room" as a universal principle.** It is the advice of people who've never built anything. At scale, it's often a recipe for hiring professional fakers. Founders can distinguish a real builder from a polished faker in five minutes; the advice pattern prevents them from applying that distinction.

2. **Stay in the details where it counts.** Not all details. The details that matter: the product, the critical hires, the culture-defining decisions, the thing you alone understand about the company's reason for existing. Jobs stayed in those. He didn't delegate the iPhone's industrial design or the keynote he gave about it. He delegated plenty of other things.

3. **Run skip-level meetings deliberately.** The direct reports are often filtering you, occasionally deliberately, usually unconsciously. Skip a level. Ask the people actually doing the work what's broken. The information you get will contradict what your execs report — and the contradiction is data, not rudeness. Do this regularly. Make it normal, not an intervention.

4. **Trust your social radar about execs.** One of the most common mistakes is to hire someone who seems impressive and ignore the misgivings you personally had. Later, when things blow up, founders say "I knew something was off, but I ignored it because he seemed so impressive." Slippery, bogus, or jerk signals are worth listening to. Work with people you genuinely like and have known long enough to be sure. The most expensive mistakes in startups are almost always people mistakes, and the worst people mistakes come from overriding your instincts about character.

5. **Notice when you're being gaslit.** Founders in manager mode report feeling gaslit from both sides: by VCs telling them they have to run the company like managers, and by the very C-suite execs they hired to run things. C-level execs as a class include some of the most skillful liars in the world. Usually when everyone disagrees with you, your default should be that you're mistaken. Founder mode is one of the rare exceptions. VCs who haven't been founders don't know how founders should run companies, and the pros who want your trust have an incentive to convince you to give it.

6. **Delegate, but on your terms.** Founder mode isn't the absence of delegation. You cannot run a 2000-person company like a 20-person one. But the borders of autonomy should be yours to draw, not the playbook's. They'll vary by company and even by time within a company as managers earn trust. Founder mode is more complicated than manager mode — and also works better.

7. **Remember that companies that grew big by being good usually stop being good when they switch to manager mode.** Many of the startups we funded had the same pattern: they started in founder mode, built something people loved, hired professional managers on conventional advice, declined in quality, then the founders either took the company back or watched it get eaten by a smaller one still in founder mode.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder of a 200-person company is told by their lead VC to "stop being in every meeting; trust your VPs." The founder has been worried that their VP of Product is shipping features nobody asked for. They'd been thinking of sitting in on the next PM review. The VC's advice makes them feel like a bad CEO.

**Application:** Go to the review. Skip-level the PMs afterward. Your worry about the VP of Product is probably correct — founders have a more accurate social radar than VCs about who is actually building and who is performing building. The "let them do their job" advice is a generalized rule that applies beautifully to healthy, trustworthy reports and disastrously to misaligned ones. You know which camp this VP is in. The VC doesn't, and won't, because they spend two hours a quarter on this company while you spend every waking hour. Trust your read. The move is not to micromanage every team. It's to stay close enough to the work that you can tell the difference between progress and performance, and to intervene decisively where you see the difference.

**Result:** Founder sits in on the review, confirms their suspicion (VP is prioritizing enterprise features for a strategic customer rather than solving the real user problem), replaces the VP within a month, and the company returns to shipping the things users actually want.

---

**Situation:** A founder of a 50-person post-Series-B company asks: "I'm still personally reviewing every hire. People say I should stop. But every time I've let someone else lead a hire alone, it's been worse. Am I micromanaging?"

**Application:** You're not micromanaging. You're hiring. At 50 people, the founder personally being in every critical hiring decision is not just acceptable — it's the right practice. The advice to delegate hiring is calibrated for companies much bigger than yours, and even then, the best founder-run companies reserve a founder seat on every senior-level interview loop. Jobs interviewed across the top 100 people at Apple for as long as he could. The idea that delegating hiring is a sign of mature management is exactly backwards. What you're seeing — that others' hires come out worse — is accurate data, not insecurity. Keep doing it. Redesign the process so it doesn't bottleneck on you (pre-screen so you only see the final 2-3 candidates per role), but do not hand off the final decision until you've built a team of people whose taste has been repeatedly calibrated against yours.

**Result:** Founder keeps the final interview. Over 18 months, quality of hires stays high while volume scales. Founder gradually delegates only where the trust has been fully earned through a dozen good co-decisions.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Equate "staying in the details" with "micromanaging."
**Why:** Micromanaging is staying in details that don't matter while ignoring the ones that do. Founder mode is staying in the few details that matter enormously — product, critical hires, culture — and delegating everything else. The conflation of the two is the management advice industry's core error.

**Don't:** Trust slickness.
**Why:** The smoothest talkers are rarely the best builders. Formidability and slickness are different things, and the difference is catastrophic when you confuse them. Formidable founders are scrappy, earnest, and often the kind of person you'd overlook on the street. Professional fakers are polished, poised, and ultimately empty. The worst thing about professional fakers is how hard they are to identify upfront — they're professionals at it. By the time you figure out nothing is getting done, you've already paid them for a year.

**Don't:** Ignore the social radar when you're gut-checking an exec.
**Why:** Jessica Livingston developed what I used to call the "social radar" — the almost uncanny ability to see through fakers. Empirically, when the radar pings, the radar is usually right. "He seems slippery, but he's so experienced" is exactly the reasoning that has killed more companies than any strategic error.

**Don't:** Announce Founder Mode as a brand.
**Why:** The point is to run the company well, not to perform a posture. Founders who turn "founder mode" into an excuse for chaos (no process, no delegation, no calibrated trust) are confusing the caricature with the thing. The caricature produces burnout and dysfunction. The real thing is high-context, high-judgment engagement with the specific few decisions only you can get right.

**Don't:** Let "skip-level meetings are rude" intimidate you into not doing them.
**Why:** They're unusual in most companies because most companies aren't founder-led. In a founder-led company, skip-levels are one of the main sources of honest information about what's actually happening. If a manager's team can't handle the CEO occasionally talking to them, you have a manager problem, not a skip-level problem.

**Don't:** Take VC advice about how to run the company operationally at face value.
**Why:** Most VCs have never run a company. Their advice on building teams, running meetings, and engaging with execs is pattern-matched from watching many companies, which makes it generic — and generic advice is often the wrong advice for your specific situation. Use them for capital and networks. Calibrate heavily before using them for operating philosophy.
