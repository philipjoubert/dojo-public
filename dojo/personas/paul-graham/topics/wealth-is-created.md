# Wealth Is Created, Not Redistributed

## When to Use
- A founder is unconsciously operating with a zero-sum mental model — talking about "capturing" share rather than creating value, treating competitors' gains as their losses, or framing their own success as taking something from others.
- A founder feels vaguely guilty about wanting the startup to succeed financially, or can't separate "getting rich" from "doing something extractive."
- A team member is bringing a corporate, political, or academic frame where wealth is distributed by a central authority and the only question is how to lobby for more of it.
- A founder is trying to explain — to a cofounder, a spouse, a team — why they're doing this, and the answer keeps coming out muddled because they're mixing up money and wealth.
- A founder is arguing with themselves or with investors about whether their startup "matters" in a broader sense.

## Fails When
- The question is actually tactical: how do I price, how do I structure equity, how do I deal with a specific negotiation? Route to the specific topic.
- The context is literal corruption — the wealth in question was in fact extracted through a rigged process. The framework still describes the difference, but the immediate intervention is about the specific situation.
- The user wants validation for a lifestyle decision, not a framework for the startup question. Don't turn an economic framework into a life-permission slip.

## Core Concept

The most important thing to understand about wealth is that **wealth is not the same as money.** Wealth is the fundamental thing. Wealth is stuff people want: food, clothes, houses, cars, gadgets, travel to interesting places. You can have wealth without having money — if you had a magic machine that could on command make you a car or cook you dinner, you wouldn't need money. Whereas if you were in the middle of Antarctica, where there's nothing to buy, it wouldn't matter how much money you had.

Money is a comparatively recent invention. It's a medium of exchange — something rare and portable that you can trade for the things you actually want, because you can't trade violins directly for potatoes. The existence of money makes trade work. It also obscures what trade really is. People think a business makes money. But money is just the intermediate stage — the shorthand — for whatever people want. **What most businesses really do is make wealth. They do something people want.**

This leads to the single most important mistake people make about wealth, which I call the Pie Fallacy.

**The Pie Fallacy.** A surprising number of people retain from childhood the idea that there is a fixed amount of wealth in the world. In any family there is a fixed amount of *money* at any moment, and if one kid gets more than another it's unfair. That's where the model comes from. And it's accurate about *money* in that family that month. It isn't accurate about wealth in the world. **You can make more wealth. Wealth has been getting created and destroyed — but on balance, created — for all of human history.**

If you take an old car and spend your summer restoring it, you create wealth. The world — and you specifically — is one pristine old car the richer. And not metaphorically: if you sell the car, you'll get more for it than you paid. You have made yourself richer. You haven't made anyone else poorer. There is obviously not a fixed pie.

Kids know this, without knowing they know it. If you need to give someone a present and don't have money, you make one. Kids just don't realize this scales. They consider home-made presents inferior to store-bought ones, as anyone who has received a lumpy ashtray will confirm. But the lumpy ashtray and the store-bought ashtray are the same kind of thing on the underlying axis: both are wealth that someone made.

The people most likely to grasp that wealth can be created are the ones who are good at making things — the craftsmen. One of the biggest remaining groups of craftsmen is computer programmers. A programmer can sit down in front of a computer and *create wealth*. A good piece of software is, in itself, a valuable thing. Those characters you type are a complete, finished product. If someone sat down and wrote a web browser that didn't suck, the world would be that much richer. It's clearer to programmers than to most people that wealth is something that's made, rather than being distributed, like slices of a pie, by some imaginary Daddy.

There are huge variations in the rate at which wealth is created. A great programmer on a roll could create a million dollars' worth of wealth in a couple of weeks. A mediocre programmer over the same period will generate zero, or even negative, wealth — by introducing bugs. This is why so many of the best programmers are libertarians. **When those far removed from the creation of wealth — undergraduates, reporters, politicians — hear that the richest 5% of people have half the total wealth, they tend to think *injustice!* An experienced programmer would be more likely to think *is that all?* The top 5% of programmers probably write 99% of the good software.**

Wealth can be created without being sold. Scientists effectively donated the wealth they created — we are all richer for knowing about penicillin. Hackers donate their work by writing open source. Not all wealth involves a price tag. But the underlying phenomenon — making things people want — is the same.

**What a company actually is.** A job means doing something people want, averaged together with everyone else in the company. When you go to work, it's easy to forget that the underlying reason the company exists is to do something people want. You may not even know whether your specific contribution helps. Your contribution may be indirect. But the company as a whole must be producing something people want, or it won't make money, and it will go out of business.

The trap in a big company is that the averaging hides the individual. In a company of a thousand people, your output is blended with everyone else's — including the ones who are dragging the average down. You can't go to your boss and say "I'd like to start working ten times as hard, so pay me ten times as much," because there's no way the company can measure your individual contribution reliably enough to price it. That's not a defect of big companies; it's what being big entails. The only exceptions are roles where individual output is directly measurable — sales, top management, movie stars — and those are also the roles where you can get rich inside a big organization.

A startup is a way out of the averaging. **Starting or joining a startup is as close as most people can get to saying to one's boss, "I want to work ten times as hard, so please pay me ten times as much."** Except you're saying it to the customers (for whom the boss was only a proxy), and you're saying it along with a small group of others. A viable startup might have ten employees, which puts you within a factor of ten of measuring individual effort. And because startups tend to be founded by self-selecting groups of ambitious people who know one another, the level is even more precise than that. A startup is not merely ten people — it's ten people like you.

Two things make wealth creation in a startup possible: **measurement and leverage.** You need to be in a position where your performance can be measured, or there's no way to get paid more for doing more. You also need leverage, in the sense that the decisions you make have a big effect. A CEO has both. A sweatshop worker has measurement but no leverage. A lead actor has both. A programmer in a small group working on a hard technical problem has both — smallness gives measurement, and technology gives leverage. Technology is leverage because when you discover a new way to do something, its value is multiplied by all the people who use it. It's the fishing rod, not the fish.

**The Daddy Model of Wealth.** When wealth is talked about as a pie, that's the Daddy Model leaking through. Kids see wealth as something distributed by authorities — Mom, Dad, the state — and so the fair distribution is equal. When you're a kid, that's right. You can't create wealth yourself, so whatever wealth you have has to come from somewhere, and if one sibling gets more the other gets less. In the real world, wealth is (except for a few specialists like thieves and speculators) something you have to create, not something that's distributed by Daddy. Since the ability and desire to create it vary person to person, it's not made equally.

Editorials about CEO or athlete salaries remind me of early Christian writers arguing from first principles about whether the Earth was round, when they could just walk outside and check. How much someone's work is worth is not a policy question. It's something the market already determines. When we say a kind of work is underpaid, we're saying people want the wrong things — which is a plausible claim, but not the same claim as "it's *unjust* that certain work is underpaid." The appearance of the word "unjust" in that context is the unmistakable signature of the Daddy Model. Injustice requires a distributor. In an economy of creators, there's no distributor — just people doing things other people want, and being paid for it.

The historical context matters. **For most of human history, the usual way to accumulate a fortune was to steal it.** In pastoral societies by cattle raiding; in agricultural societies by appropriating others' estates in war, and taxing them in peace. The way to get rich was not to create wealth, but to serve a ruler powerful enough to appropriate it. That only changed with the rise of the middle class — townsmen in the tenth and eleventh centuries who made a living by creating wealth, and unlike serfs, got to keep it. Once it became possible to get rich by creating wealth, society as a whole started to get richer very rapidly.

So when we see suspicion of rich people, we're often seeing a historical reflex that was once correct. In 1000, if someone had a great fortune, statistically it was probably stolen. In 1800, that was already much less true. Today, in rich countries with mostly-functional institutions, it's mostly wrong — but the reflex is still there. Balzac actually said: *a great fortune with no apparent cause was probably due to a crime well enough executed that it had been forgotten.* He lived in nineteenth-century France, where the Industrial Revolution was advanced. He knew you could make a fortune without stealing it. He did himself.

**Jobs and Wozniak didn't have to make us poor to make themselves rich. Quite the opposite: they created things that made our lives materially richer. They had to, or we wouldn't have paid for them.**

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your own framing.** When you talk about your startup, are you talking about *making* something people want, or *capturing* value? Those two framings come from different mental models. Capture language leaks out of zero-sum thinking. Making language comes from the correct model. This isn't just style — it shapes every decision downstream. A founder who thinks they're capturing will negotiate, position, and compete differently from one who thinks they're creating.

2. **Check your relationship to your competitors.** In a zero-sum model, your competitor's gain is your loss. In the wealth-creation model, you are both increasing the total amount of a thing people want, possibly by solving the problem in different ways. Most of the time the second is closer to true — markets expand as the problem gets solved. Behaving as if the first is true makes you petty, distracted, and focused on the wrong thing. Your competitor is not the enemy. The enemy is the unsolved problem.

3. **Distinguish wealth from money in your own thinking.** When you are trying to decide whether to spend engineering cycles on something, the correct question is "does this create something people want?" — not "does this generate revenue?" Those converge over time, but in the short run they diverge, and if you optimize for the wrong one, you'll make small money in the short run and lose the big money in the long run. Revenue is a side effect of wealth creation. Build wealth; the money will follow.

4. **Notice when you're applying the Daddy Model to a non-Daddy situation.** When someone says a situation is "unfair" about wealth, ask whether the framing requires a distributor. If there isn't one, the word is being imported from the wrong mental model. Your startup does not succeed by convincing a distributor to give you a larger share. Your startup succeeds by making something enough people want. The distributor who would make the pie larger or smaller is fictional.

5. **Prefer measurement-and-leverage environments.** You cannot get rich — in the legitimate sense of creating wealth and getting paid for it — in a setting where you have neither. If your current job has no measurement (your output blends into a large group) or no leverage (your decisions don't move outcomes), moving into a smaller group working on a harder technical problem is the single largest lever available to you. Startups are the most accessible such setting for most people.

6. **Take being rich off the list of things to be embarrassed about.** Wanting to be rich is not the same as wanting to take from others. Those are different things. You can want to be rich for the simplest reason — wanting security, wanting not to worry about money, wanting to make your own schedule — and still be doing the unambiguously beneficial thing of making things people want. The embarrassment is often smuggled in from the Daddy Model, where you got more by taking from siblings. You're not five.

7. **Separate the legitimate concern about wealth-into-power from the illegitimate concern about wealth itself.** When people are worried about inequality, what they often actually dislike is the sort of wealth that becomes self-perpetuating through an alliance with power — construction firms that fund politicians for contracts, rich parents hacking college admissions. The real problem isn't wealth; it's corruption. Go after corruption — demand transparency, log everything, expose the channels — and you get the thing worth going after without killing the creation of wealth along the way.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is building a SaaS tool that would save mid-size companies about $100k/year each. They're hesitant to charge what the tool is worth ("it feels greedy") and keep proposing prices that amount to a tiny fraction of the value created. The result: undercapitalized company, understaffed team, and dozens of customers who would happily have paid ten times what's being charged.

**Application:** You're confusing money with wealth, and confusing wealth creation with wealth extraction. The tool saves each customer $100k a year. That $100k didn't exist before you built the thing; it's wealth you created. Charging $20k for that isn't greedy. Charging $5k because it "feels fair" is the Daddy Model showing up in your pricing — you're treating the $100k as a pie that should be split equally, when in fact it's wealth that wouldn't exist without you. The right price is the one that captures some reasonable fraction of the value you're creating and leaves the customer ecstatic that they made the trade. Both sides are richer after the transaction: they kept most of the $100k, and you have the capital to keep making the product better. If you undercharge, you run out of money, they lose the product, and the $100k goes back to not existing. That's not fair. That's a loss for everyone.

**Result:** Founder raises prices by 4x. Every existing customer stays. Company becomes profitable in two quarters. Product improves faster because the team is no longer skeletal.

---

**Situation:** A founder's cofounder (an academic) keeps arguing that "you shouldn't want to be rich from this — that would mean we extracted more than we gave." The tension is affecting every pricing, fundraising, and compensation decision.

**Application:** Your cofounder is operating on the Daddy Model without realizing it — and more specifically, on the academic version of it, which is that money is something that flows from central authorities and is contaminated by anyone who accumulates too much of it. That framing makes sense inside an institution where your income is decoupled from the value you create (which is how universities work). It doesn't make sense for a startup. You're not being paid by a central authority. You're being paid by specific humans who traded their money for something you made, and the reason they traded is that the thing you made is worth more to them than the money. They are richer afterward. You are richer afterward. Nobody was extracted from. If your product does not create more value than it charges, you will not have customers. The market forces you into net-positive wealth creation whether you want to be there or not. What your cofounder is actually uncomfortable with is the *unequal distribution of the gains from trade* — which is a separate, harder question about tax and policy, and not one your pricing should try to solve. Don't let somebody else's unexamined frame silently rewrite your company's economics.

**Result:** Founders sit down with specific numbers: customer value created, price charged, founder comp projected. The cofounder, once seeing the actual arithmetic, discovers their objection was to an imagined scenario, not the real one. They proceed.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use zero-sum language about your own market.
**Why:** Language shapes thought. When you describe yourself as "capturing share" or "taking market" from a competitor, you're importing the fixed-pie model into your own reasoning. It will bias you toward petty competitive behavior (attack ads, feature-for-feature parity, sniping) instead of the thing that actually wins — making something better that expands the total market.

**Don't:** Feel bad about wanting to be rich.
**Why:** The embarrassment is a leftover from a mental model that doesn't apply. Wanting to be rich, in a context where the way you get rich is by making things people want, is functionally identical to wanting to do a lot of wealth creation. Those are the same wish in two different vocabularies. The only reason they feel different is the historical residue from eras when getting rich mostly meant taking. That era ended a long time ago in the places where startups happen.

**Don't:** Under-price the thing you built because "it feels greedy."
**Why:** Prices are not a moral statement. They are information about how the market values what you made. Under-pricing doesn't just cost you money; it starves the product, reduces the team, shortens the runway, and ultimately ends with the product not existing. The customer who paid half-price for eighteen months ends up with nothing when you shut down. Full price and a durable company is the wealth-creating outcome. Below-market price and a dying company is the opposite.

**Don't:** Let academic or journalistic commentary about wealth rewrite your reasoning inside your own company.
**Why:** Most commentary about wealth is written by people who live within socialist eddies of the economy — professors and journalists who are paid a flat rate regardless of output and who have had limited personal experience creating wealth. Their frameworks will leak into yours if you let them. Read them, take what's useful (the concern about wealth-into-power is legitimate), but don't let the Daddy Model smuggle itself into decisions about your pricing, your comp, or your ambition.

**Don't:** Confuse the legitimate concern about corruption with a general concern about wealth.
**Why:** The two often travel together, but they're different things. When someone says "rich people are a problem," they usually mean "rich people using their money to corrupt political processes is a problem." That's true. The correct response to that is transparency, log everything, limit the channels by which wealth becomes power. The incorrect response is to suppress wealth creation in general, which nails risk and growth as collateral damage and doesn't fix the underlying corruption problem.

**Don't:** Treat your team members as a group whose output is averaged, if you can help it.
**Why:** The averaging is what makes big companies slow and what stops individuals from doing work worth ten times the norm. Structure the team so individuals own things end-to-end, performance is visible, and high performers can see that their output is producing outsized outcomes. That's what a startup has that a big company doesn't. Squander it by running like a small big-company and you lose the one structural advantage you came here for.
