# Truthseeking Pods and Decision Hygiene

## When to Use

- When you're making consequential decisions and the people closest to you have stopped pushing back. (If your last three big calls all got immediate alignment from the team, the team isn't giving you signal anymore — they're giving you applause.)
- When you're about to set up a structure that's supposed to produce good decisions: a board, an advisory group, a kitchen cabinet, a hiring panel, a strategy review process. Most of these are designed to confirm rather than test, and you can fix that if you're deliberate about it.
- When you find yourself looking for someone who'll tell you you're right rather than someone who'll tell you the truth. (That instinct is normal. It's also exactly when you need a pod.)
- Before any decision review where the outcome is already known. The way you set up the meeting will determine whether you get analysis or rationalization.
- When you're hiring or running interviews and you've noticed that panel discussions converge on the loudest voice's opinion within ten minutes of the candidate leaving.

## Fails When

- **You want a friend, not a stress-test.** Sometimes after a hard loss, what you need is to vent and be heard, not to have your decision-making interrogated. Truthseeking pods work because of explicit opt-in. If you want venting, ask for venting. Don't show up to a pod expecting comfort and then resent them for giving you what they're for.
- **The pod has no charter.** A bunch of smart people in a room without an agreement to truthseek will default to confirmatory thought, just like every other group. The charter — accuracy, accountability, diversity, no shooting messengers — is what makes the pod a pod. Without it, you've just built a fancier echo chamber.
- **The membership is too homogeneous.** A pod of five founders who all went to the same school, raised from the same fund, ran the same playbook will reach consensus quickly and confidently — and will be wrong as a unit. Diversity of viewpoint is what gives the pod its power. Cloning yourself five times produces a louder version of you, not a smarter one.

## Core Concept

You are not going to fix your own biases on your own. I tried for years and I will tell you it doesn't work. Knowing about confirmation bias does not make you immune to confirmation bias. Knowing about self-serving bias does not stop you from doing it. Smart people are actually *worse* at this — Dan Kahan's research, replicated repeatedly, shows that higher cognitive ability correlates with *larger* blind-spot bias, not smaller, because smart people are better at constructing internally consistent stories that protect what they already believe. You are not the exception. I am not the exception. Pete Carroll is not the exception. The fix is not better introspection. The fix is other people.

The structure I use for this is what I call a *truthseeking pod*. It's a small group — three is enough, four or five works, more than seven gets unwieldy — where the membership has explicitly agreed to a charter of accuracy over comfort. They're going to push back on your bad reasoning, you're going to push back on theirs, and none of you are going to take it personally because the agreement was made up front. Eric Seidel did this for me at the start of my career. I went to him moaning about a bad beat in a tournament — pure self-serving fielding, I lost because I was unlucky, can you believe how unlucky I got — and he laid out the whole charter in three sentences. "I don't want to hear it. I'm not trying to hurt your feelings, but if you have a question about a hand, you can ask me about strategy all day long. I just don't think there's much purpose in a poker story if the point is about something you had no control over, like bad luck." That was my first lesson in what a pod actually is: a relationship where the agreement is to be useful, not to be nice.

The charter has three pillars, drawn from research by Philip Tetlock, Jennifer Lerner, and Jonathan Haidt. (1) **Focus on accuracy** over confirmation — the goal is to get closer to what's true, not to feel good. (2) **Accountability**, agreed in advance — members know they'll be answerable to the group for what they believed and what they decided. (3) **Diversity of viewpoint** — the pod has to actually contain people who see things differently, or it just amplifies one perspective. Without all three, what you have is not a pod. It's a club.

Inside the charter, the working norms come from Robert Merton — yes, the magician, before he became one of the most influential sociologists of the twentieth century. Merton wrote a paper in 1942 about the rules a scientific community needed to operate. He gave it the acronym CUDOS: **C**ommunism (data belongs to the group — share everything, especially the inconvenient stuff), **U**niversalism (judge a claim on its merits, not on who said it), **D**isinterestedness (vigilance against your own conflicts of interest), and **O**rganized **S**kepticism (ask why things might not be true, not just why they are). I'd argue Merton's CUDOS is the cleanest list of rules-of-engagement for any decision group anywhere. It maps perfectly onto what good poker pods do, and onto what good corporate strategy groups do, and onto what good boards do. Wherever the rules break down, that's where the pod stops producing better decisions and starts producing groupthink.

Communism — share the data — sounds obvious until you watch yourself violate it. The instinct, when you bring a decision to the group, is to leave out the details that don't flatter you. The hand you lost in some embarrassing way. The hire you didn't really do reference checks on. The customer you angered. Those are *exactly* the details the group needs. The mere fact that you have an urge to omit them is the signal that they're load-bearing. As a rule of thumb: if you're tempted to leave a detail out because you'd have to explain or defend it, that detail is what the group needs to see. The Rashomon Effect — that everyone tells the same story differently — means we cannot trust ourselves to provide a complete account. Be a data sharer. Reward others for being data sharers. Hall of Fame coach John Madden once said he watched Vince Lombardi spend eight hours describing one play — the power sweep — and walked out realizing he didn't actually know anything about football. The level of detail a real expert puts into a single decision-evaluation is shocking to laypeople. That's what the standard looks like.

Universalism — don't shoot the message — is the discipline of evaluating an idea on its merits, regardless of who said it. The classic version: I came into poker with a list of starting hands my brother had written on a napkin in a coffee shop, and I treated that napkin like a holy document. Anyone playing hands off the list was a bad player by definition. So when I saw them win, I dismissed it. When they did things I hadn't seen, I shot the message — bad players, can't possibly be doing anything I should learn from. It cost me roughly the first year of my career to realize that hands off the list could absolutely be played profitably given the right context. The fix was an explicit exercise: when I noticed the impulse to dismiss someone, I made myself find something they did well. Same exercise applies to every founder I've ever worked with — when an investor's note triggers your "they don't get it" reflex, when a junior team member's question triggers your "they don't have context" reflex, that's exactly the moment to go look for what they got right.

Disinterestedness — guard against your conflicts of interest — is the hardest one because everyone has them and almost no one notices. The Harvard sugar-vs-fat researchers in 1967 didn't disclose that the sugar industry was paying them; their conclusion blaming fat shaped American eating for forty years. They probably weren't lying. They probably believed their analysis. They almost certainly didn't notice that their funding was steering their attention. *Your* version of that bias is more subtle: you'll find the analysis that supports the strategy you already pitched the board on more compelling than the analysis that doesn't. The fix is structural. Don't let the listener know the answer when you're asking for input. (See the Semmelweis story below — that's the cautionary tale of what happens when you don't think about the contamination problem.)

Organized skepticism — invite dissent, treat it as cooperative, not confrontational — is the operating posture of the pod. The trick is that real skepticism is gentle. "Are you sure about that?" "Have you thought about it this other way?" "I'm not sure I follow." Those land as collaboration. "You're wrong" lands as combat. Same intellectual content; very different effect. Build the language for dissent into the pod's normal vocabulary so dissent doesn't feel like an attack when it shows up.

The other half of this — the half that keeps the pod from corrupting itself — is what I call **decision hygiene**. Hygiene is the work of preventing belief contamination *during* the analysis, not after. Decision contamination is real. If I tell you I won the hand and then ask you whether I played it well, you will find reasons it was well played. If I tell you I lost and ask the same question, you'll find reasons it was bad. Same hand, same decision, different infection.

The story I use to drive this home is Ignaz Semmelweis. Vienna General Hospital, 1840s. The maternity ward had a horrifying differential mortality rate — the ward run by male doctors had about three times the death rate from puerperal fever compared to the ward run by midwives. Semmelweis noticed that the doctors were coming directly from autopsies to deliveries without washing their hands. He instituted handwashing with chlorinated lime; mortality on his ward dropped to under 2%. He had the answer. The medical establishment rejected it for decades because the *messenger* was abrasive, the *theory* contradicted what doctors believed about themselves, and accepting it meant doctors had been killing women they were trying to save. The information was correct; the context made it untouchable. Semmelweis died in an asylum, beaten by guards, before germ theory was accepted. The lesson is not "Semmelweis was right." The lesson is that *the way information is delivered determines whether it's processed* — and that's a hygiene problem, not an information problem. If you want to give your team useful feedback that doesn't get rejected on the spot, you have to think about the contamination as carefully as Semmelweis should have thought about delivery.

## How to Apply

1. **Recruit your pod deliberately, not by default.** The people who happen to be around you — your cofounders, your direct reports — are not necessarily a good pod. They have aligned interests, shared assumptions, and political stakes in your decisions. A real pod usually pulls in at least one person from outside your direct line of fire — a former boss, a non-portfolio investor, an advisor with a different domain, a peer founder in an adjacent space. Three is a working minimum. Two-to-disagree-and-one-to-referee is the structural test.

2. **Write the charter down.** Sounds bureaucratic, but the act of writing it forces clarity. The charter says: we are committed to accuracy over comfort. We agree to hold each other accountable. We expect dissent and consider it a feature. We don't shoot messengers. When new members join, the charter is the first thing they read. When the group drifts — and it will — the charter is what you point at.

3. **Practice the CUDOS norms in plain language.** You don't have to call them CUDOS to your team. But the four behaviors — share inconvenient details, judge ideas not their source, watch for your own conflicts, treat skepticism as cooperative — should be visibly modeled by you, the leader of the pod. The norms are caught more than taught.

4. **Run decision hygiene reflexively.** When you bring a decision to the pod, *do not reveal the outcome up front* if there is one. Describe the situation, the options, what you knew, what you didn't, what you were leaning toward. Then ask for analysis. Reveal the result only after the group has rendered a verdict on the decision quality. This is the single highest-leverage hygiene move and almost no one does it. (When I do poker workshops, I describe a hand up to the decision point and stop. The room screams at me to tell them how it ended. I tell them: it doesn't matter.)

5. **Don't infect your listeners with your own beliefs.** When you ask for input, don't lead with what you think. Don't lead with what the team thinks. Don't lead with what the board thinks. Get the listener's view *first*, in the cleanest possible form, before contaminating it with everyone else's. This is why hiring panels should write up assessments independently before convening. It's why a 4-person interview team should never debrief together until each member has filed an independent verdict. It's why you should not, in a strategy meeting, say "I think we should do X — what do you all think?" and expect to learn anything. You've already announced the answer the room is supposed to confirm.

6. **Argue the other side.** When two members of the pod disagree, the most powerful move is to have them switch sides and argue each other's position — best version of it, no straw-manning, with the goal of being the most persuasive defender of the view they came in opposing. This breaks confirmation lock. Most disagreements get smaller after this drill, because both people realize there were real arguments on the other side they hadn't taken seriously. The middle is where they end up. Sometimes one position genuinely is stronger and the side-switch reveals it cleanly. Either way, you've learned something the unswapped argument would never have produced.

7. **Lead with assent when communicating outside the pod.** Inside the pod, dissent is the norm. Outside the pod — board, customers, partners, your kid's teacher — most people haven't agreed to the charter. Going in dissenting reads as attack. The technique that works: find the part you actually agree with, name it specifically, and then add. Not "but" — *and*. "I agree with you that the brand needs to evolve, *and* I think the timing might be off." "But" denies what came before. "And" extends it. You're not weaseling; you're just choosing language that keeps the listener available to your point. People listen better when they don't feel they're being countered. (Improvisers know this — "yes, and" is the foundation of every good improv scene. It's also the foundation of every productive conversation outside your pod.)

8. **Build a temporary opt-out for raw moments.** Inside my poker pod, if one of us had just been knocked out of a tournament and was emotionally raw, we could say, "I just need to moan for a minute." That was an explicit, agreed-on temporary departure from the truthseeking norm — and the explicit recognition that we'd come back to it once the rawness passed. You need this. Truthseeking around a flat tire in a rainstorm at midnight is not productive. Truthseeking the next day, after the emotion has cleared, is. Build the temporary venting agreement into the pod, so people don't have to pretend to be more rational than they are in the moment.

9. **Watch for confirmatory drift in the pod itself.** Pods get stale. Members start agreeing too easily, finishing each other's sentences, sharing too many priors. When that happens, refresh — bring in someone with a different background, ask the pod to deliberately argue against itself, run a session where every assertion has to be supported by someone other than the person who first said it. Cass Sunstein's work on federal judicial panels showed that even three randomly assigned judges produced systematically different votes when one of them was from the opposing party. One outsider, structurally embedded, has outsized impact. If your pod has zero outsiders, that's the problem.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder convenes a quarterly strategy review. He opens with a 20-minute presentation laying out the current strategy, the data supporting it, and his proposed adjustments. He asks the room: "What do you all think?"
**Application:** That meeting is structurally guaranteed to produce confirmation. He has just spent twenty minutes telling his direct reports what he believes, marshaled all the evidence in favor of it, and asked them to react. They will react with refinements — they're not going to call him a fool — and the reflection of his strategy will come back at him slightly improved and significantly endorsed. He will mistake this for testing. The fix: he doesn't present first. He sends the data to the team in advance, asks each to write a one-page assessment independently before the meeting, and uses the meeting to surface the divergence among the assessments. *Then* he reveals what he's leaning toward.
**Result:** In the next quarter's review, three of the five independent assessments flagged a market shift his presentation had buried in slide 12. He'd seen it; he'd intentionally minimized it because it complicated the strategy. The independent process caught what the contaminated process couldn't. He shifts the strategy. Six months later, that shift saves the company a quarter of runway.

**Situation:** A founder has lost three customers in a quarter. She walks into the next leadership meeting and announces: "We need to overhaul sales." Five executives nod.
**Application:** This is what a confirmation-machine looks like from the inside. She's announced the answer; the room has heard her tone; the room agrees. The meeting is over before it started. The right move backward: send the three churn cases to each leader independently. Ask each, *before any group discussion*, to write up (a) why they think the customer churned and (b) what, if anything, they'd change about the sales process in response. Then convene. The five answers will be much more divergent than the room would have ever produced together — and the divergence is the data. Some of the leaders will identify customer-success failures. Some will identify positioning issues. One will probably (correctly) identify that one of the three churns was just bad luck and shouldn't be a strategy input at all.
**Result:** "We need to overhaul sales" becomes "we need to fix customer success and adjust positioning, and the third churn was variance." The overhaul that would have cost three months and a leadership transition gets replaced by two targeted fixes. Same data. Different process. Different outcome.

**Situation:** A founder is interviewing a senior hire. The four-person panel debriefs together. Within ten minutes, three of the four are agreeing with the loudest voice's assessment.
**Application:** Standard contamination dynamics. The first person to speak after the candidate leaves anchors the room. Subsequent speakers refine and extend rather than disagree, because disagreeing is socially expensive. This is why panels converge on confident voices. The fix: require independent written assessments from each panelist *before* the debrief. Once written, they're harder to revise socially. The debrief becomes a comparison of pre-existing views rather than a derivation of consensus from scratch.
**Result:** The next hiring round, two of the four written assessments flag a concern the room would have buried — the candidate had a pattern of moving every 18 months. The panel decides to dig in. The reference checks confirm the pattern. The hire doesn't happen. Without the hygiene, this hire would have been made and would have churned 14 months later, costing the company a senior search and a year of misallocated work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Tell your pod the outcome of a decision before they've evaluated it.
**Why:** This is the single most common decision-hygiene violation, and it ruins the analysis on the spot. Once the pod knows whether the bet hit, every subsequent statement they make about the decision quality is contaminated. They will produce a beautiful, internally consistent story about why the decision was good (if it worked) or bad (if it didn't) and almost none of it will be load-bearing. Walk them through the decision *as it was*, get their analysis, *then* tell them what happened. This is uncomfortable. It's also the only way the analysis is worth anything.

**Don't:** Assemble a pod entirely from people who report to you.
**Why:** Direct reports have a structural conflict of interest in disagreeing with you. Their job, their bonus, their political standing all flow through you. Even if they're committed to truthseeking — which most try to be — the pressure to soften disagreement is constant and mostly unconscious. A pod has to include at least one or two people whose careers don't depend on you. Outside advisors, peer founders, former bosses, non-portfolio investors. If everyone in the pod is paid by you, you have a leadership team, not a pod.

**Don't:** Use CUDOS or "truthseeking pod" as jargon to signal sophistication.
**Why:** Performative truthseeking is worse than no truthseeking. If the team starts hearing the founder talk about "Mertonian norms" without actually changing how the meetings run, the vocabulary becomes a shield rather than a tool. The principles don't require the labels. You can run a perfect CUDOS-compliant decision review without ever using the word CUDOS. You can also bury bad confirmation hygiene under a thick layer of CUDOS-jargon and fool yourself that you've done the work. The behaviors are what matter — the labels are scaffolding for your own thinking.

**Don't:** Default the pod's conclusion to "split the difference."
**Why:** When two members of the pod disagree, the temptation is to land in the middle — compromise, average their positions, satisfy both. Sometimes the middle is right. Often the middle is the wrong answer dressed up as diplomacy. If the pod is functioning, one position will sometimes turn out to be substantially stronger after the side-switch exercise. Trust the process to land where the evidence points, not where the social pressure points.

**Don't:** Expect the pod to be the place where you go after a fresh, raw loss.
**Why:** The pod is for analysis when you can think. Right after a really painful loss — a deal blowing up, a hire imploding, a founder you trusted leaving — you cannot think yet. Going to the pod and trying to do CUDOS analysis at 11pm on the day it happened will produce one of two results: (a) you'll bend the analysis to comfort yourself and pollute the pod's norms, or (b) the pod will push hard on a wound and the relationship will scar. Either way, you've burned the very resource you'd need 48 hours later when the analysis would actually be productive. Use the temporary venting opt-out, take a beat, come back when you can be a scout again.

**Don't:** Hire for "culture fit" in ways that select against viewpoint diversity.
**Why:** "Culture fit" is the phrase under which most founders systematically destroy the diversity of viewpoint that would make their decisions better. The interviews feel right because the candidates feel familiar. The team is harmonious because everyone agrees. Then you make a series of bad decisions in lockstep, without internal pushback, and don't notice it until the market tells you. Build the diversity in deliberately at the hiring stage. The cost in cultural smoothness is real; the gain in decision quality is much larger.
