# Resulting — The Cardinal Sin

## When to Use

- Whenever you catch yourself naming "your worst decision of the year" and the example you reach for is actually your worst *outcome* of the year. Almost everyone does this. Almost no one notices.
- When a public figure, a competitor, a hire, a coach, or a former version of yourself is being graded entirely on whether the play worked.
- When a great quarter has the founder rewriting their strategy memo to take credit for the genius they never claimed in advance.
- When a bad quarter has the founder firing the wrong person, killing the right strategy, or spiraling into "I should have known" — when in fact, they shouldn't have, because nobody could have.
- Before any post-mortem. Before any board review. Before any "what did we learn?" conversation. The first job is to separate decision quality from outcome quality. If you skip that step, the rest of the conversation is theater.

## Fails When

- **The outcome data is actually large enough to be signal.** A single hand of poker tells you almost nothing. Ten thousand hands tell you a lot. If your sales team has run the same playbook against 800 prospects and the close rate is 2%, the outcomes ARE the data. Don't hide behind "small sample" when you have a large one.
- **Used to launder a genuinely bad decision.** Sometimes the call was bad AND the result was bad and they were correlated for a reason. The point of refusing to result is not to refuse to ever judge. It's to judge the decision, separately, on its merits.
- **Applied to high-frequency, low-stakes decisions.** If you're optimizing your morning routine, the outcomes are the test. Save the decision-quality vs. outcome-quality framework for the calls where luck has real room to mess with the signal.

## Core Concept

There's a word poker players use that I want every founder to learn: *resulting*. Resulting is the bias of grading the quality of a decision by the quality of its outcome. It is the cardinal sin of decision-making. If you only learn one thing from me, learn this.

Here is the canonical case. Super Bowl XLIX, 2015. Twenty-six seconds left, Seahawks down by four, second-and-goal at the one-yard line, one timeout. Pete Carroll calls a pass. Russell Wilson throws an interception. Patriots win. The next morning, the headlines were unanimous: "Worst Play-Call in NFL History." *USA Today*. *The Washington Post*. The *New Yorker*. Pete Carroll became, overnight, the worst coach in football.

Now do the thought experiment. Imagine the pass is caught for a touchdown. What are the headlines? "Brilliant Call." "Carroll Outsmarts Belichick." "Surprise Play Wins Super Bowl." Same decision. Same process. Same risk profile going in. The only thing that changed is which branch of the future actually happened. Pete Carroll's decision was either great or terrible based on a 2% — at most — interception rate that hit. That's resulting. The play didn't work. Therefore the call was bad. The reasoning runs that fast and that wrong, and almost everyone — pundits, fans, half my consulting clients, you, me on a bad day — does it constantly.

Why is it so seductive? Because outcomes are *visible* and decision processes are not. You can see the interception. You can see the firing didn't fix the company. You can see the Series A you turned down go on to a $4B exit. You cannot see the alternative branches of the tree, the ones that didn't materialize. So the brain does the lazy thing: it reads the outcome backward and constructs a story in which the outcome was inevitable, and therefore the decision was either obviously right or obviously wrong. That backward reading is also called hindsight bias, and it is resulting's twin. They run together. The minute you let one in, the other follows. (See `hindsight-bias.md`.)

What separates a great poker player from a mediocre one is not that they win more outcomes — at the table, the best player in the world still loses 40% of an eight-hour session. What separates them is that they evaluate the *bet*, not the *result*. They know that any decision worth its salt is a probabilistic claim about the future, and that a single iteration of that future tells you almost nothing about whether the claim was good. A 30% bet hits 30% of the time. A 70% bet misses 30% of the time. If you grade yourself on individual outcomes, you'll be wrong about your own quality roughly half the time you bother to check. That's not learning. That's noise being fed into your beliefs.

Here's what makes resulting so destructive for founders: you change your future behavior based on the bad result. The CEO who fires his president, watches the company tank under the next two replacements, and concludes "firing the president was my worst decision of the year." Now he's terrified to make the same call again — even though, when we reconstructed his process, it was a thoughtful, well-evidenced decision. He'd hired an executive coach for the guy. He'd considered splitting the role. He'd benchmarked against comparable companies. The decision was reasonable. The result was bad. He confused the two. And he's about to keep an underperforming exec next time, because he's been trained by his own brain to treat the outcome as proof.

That's the cost. Resulting doesn't just produce bad post-mortems. It produces bad future decisions, by feeding noise back into your belief-updating loop instead of signal. The founder who decides "we should have seen it coming" trains themselves to demand a level of foresight no one has — which means next time, they'll either freeze or pretend to have certainty they don't have. Both are losing strategies.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the bias out loud.** Before you analyze any past decision, say the word: "I want to make sure I'm not resulting here." Naming it is half the battle, because it forces a tiny pause where the lazy outcome-equals-quality reflex would have fired automatically. In a group, give the word a swear-jar status. Anyone who says "great call" or "terrible call" without separating outcome from process owes the team a beat to back it up.

2. **Reconstruct the decision tree as it looked *before* the outcome.** What were the options? What were the probabilities you (or whoever made the call) assigned to each? What was the expected value of the chosen option vs. the alternatives? You're rebuilding the tree the chainsaw of hindsight already cut down. (See `hindsight-bias.md` — this is the "give the chainsaw a rest" move.) If you can't reconstruct the tree, you don't actually know whether the decision was good. You only know what happened.

3. **Run the inverse outcome thought experiment.** "If this exact same decision, with the exact same information, had produced the *opposite* result — would I be calling it brilliant?" Pete Carroll calls the pass; Wilson catches it; Seahawks win the Super Bowl. Are the headlines about a horrible call? No. They're about a brilliant one. Same call. The thought experiment exposes how much of your judgment is being driven by the result rather than the merits. If your evaluation flips when the outcome flips, you're resulting. The decision quality should not be sensitive to which branch of the tree happened to materialize.

4. **Separate the signal-bearing parts from the luck.** Even after you've stopped resulting, there's still real work to do. Some bad outcomes really are caused by bad decisions. Some good outcomes really are caused by good decisions. The discipline isn't "ignore the outcome." It's "evaluate the decision *and* the outcome separately, and only attribute to skill what skill actually explains." This is fielding (see `fielding-outcomes.md`). Resulting is one fielding error — treating outcome as 100% skill. Self-serving bias is the other — treating bad outcomes as 100% luck. Both are wrong, in opposite directions.

5. **Memorialize the pre-decision reasoning in writing.** The single best protection against resulting and hindsight bias is a written record of what you thought *before* you knew the result. Keep a decision journal. Note the options, the probabilities, the expected value, the things that would change your mind. Then when the outcome lands, you have an anchor that pre-dates the fog of hindsight. You can't gaslight yourself with "I knew it all along" if you wrote down what you actually believed. (This is what After-School All-Stars did with their grant pipeline, and what every red-teaming organization does. Memory is malleable. Paper is not.)

6. **In groups, evaluate the decision before revealing the outcome.** When I workshop a poker hand with another pro, I describe the decision up to the point I needed to make and stop. I don't tell them how it turned out — because I know that telling them will infect their analysis. The outcome is contagious. If they know I lost, they'll find reasons the play was bad. If they know I won, they'll find reasons it was good. Same applies in business: when you bring a decision to your truthseeking pod, give them the situation up to the decision point and not a beat further. (See `truthseeking-pods-and-decision-hygiene.md`.)

7. **Redefine winning and losing.** Stop saying "I made the right call" when you mean "the outcome was good." Stop saying "I screwed up" when you mean "the outcome was bad." Try: "I made a 70% bet and it hit" or "I made a 70% bet and it missed." That phrasing keeps the decision and the outcome in separate buckets where they belong. It also has a side benefit: it makes you much more emotionally durable. The 30% miss isn't a failure of judgment; it's the 30% doing what 30% does.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder fires the president of his company after a thoughtful, evidence-driven process — coaching tried, role-splitting considered, benchmarks reviewed. The next two replacements underperform. Sales fall. He shows up to consulting saying "firing the president was my worst decision of the year."
**Application:** Walk through the actual decision tree as it existed at the moment of the call. What were the alternatives? What were their expected outcomes? What information did he have? After reconstruction: the firing was a reasonable bet. The bad result is from the replacement search going wrong, plus market conditions, plus whatever else — none of which were knowable at the firing decision. He had been resulting *and* hindsight-biasing himself for months.
**Result:** He stops calling it his worst decision. More importantly, he doesn't carry the false lesson — "don't fire underperformers" — into the next decision, where it would have cost him a lot more than the original firing did. He drives drunk and got home safely once; he was about to use that as evidence that drunk driving is fine.

**Situation:** Pete Carroll, second-and-goal, one yard line, twenty-six seconds, one timeout, down by four. Calls a pass. Interception. Headlines: "Worst Call in NFL History."
**Application:** Look at the actual probabilities. Out of 66 passes attempted from the opponent's one-yard line that season, zero were intercepted. The historical interception rate from that spot is ~2%. The pass call also bought the Seahawks a third running play that the run-it-twice strategy didn't allow — because an incomplete pass stops the clock, while a stuffed run forces the timeout. The call was, on the math, defensible to brilliant. A 2% event hit. Nothing about the decision quality changed when that 2% event hit.
**Result:** The thing 80% of football pundits called the worst call ever was a perfectly defensible decision that ran into a one-in-fifty outcome. The pundits weren't analyzing the play. They were analyzing whether the play *worked*. That's resulting, on national television.

**Situation:** A founder's growth experiment lands. CAC drops 40%, conversion doubles. The board wants to triple the budget on it next quarter and the founder is ready to sign.
**Application:** Resulting cuts both ways. A great outcome doesn't prove a great decision either. Before tripling, run the same discipline: what did you actually predict before you ran it? What was the probability you assigned? Was this a 70% bet that hit, or a 15% lottery ticket that you're now mistaking for a repeatable engine? The difference matters enormously for the next allocation. If a 15% bet hit, scaling the budget 3x is wildly aggressive. If a 70% bet hit, it might be conservative.
**Result:** The founder pulls up her pre-experiment notes (she keeps a decision journal because of exactly this conversation), discovers she'd estimated maybe 25% chance of the experiment producing meaningful lift, and tempers the rollout accordingly. The big outcome was real — but it was a 25% hit, not a confirmation of a sure thing. She doesn't bet the company on it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "I'm just being a scout / refusing to result" as cover for refusing to take responsibility for a genuinely bad decision.
**Why:** Resulting is one error. Its mirror image — what poker players call self-serving bias — is the other. If every bad outcome is "well, the decision was good, I just got unlucky," you've stopped using outcomes as feedback at all. The discipline isn't "outcomes don't matter." It's "outcomes are noisy signal, not direct evidence — and you have to separate the parts that were luck from the parts that were on you." Some bad outcomes really do trace back to bad decisions. Pretending otherwise is just resulting in reverse.

**Don't:** Apply this framework retroactively only to your losses.
**Why:** The most common pattern is that founders refuse to result on their failures ("I made a great call, the world was just unfair") but happily result on their wins ("I'm a genius, look at the outcome"). That's not a discipline. That's a self-flattering filter. The test is whether you also pull yourself up short after a *win* and ask "wait, was this a 70% bet that hit, or a 20% bet that hit, and how much credit am I giving myself for the difference?" If you only run the framework on losses, you're going to massively overestimate your own skill, scale the wrong things, and walk straight into a bigger loss next quarter.

**Don't:** Run a post-mortem with the outcome announced upfront.
**Why:** The outcome is contagious. The minute the room knows whether the bet hit or missed, every member of the room starts retrofitting their analysis to fit the outcome. You'll get a beautiful, internally consistent story about why the decision was good (if it worked) or bad (if it didn't), and almost none of that story will be load-bearing. It'll be hindsight bias dressed up as analysis. The trick is to walk the room through the decision *as it was* — options, probabilities, information — and only reveal the outcome after you've reached a verdict on the decision itself. This is uncomfortable. It's also the only post-mortem worth running.

**Don't:** Treat "good decision, bad outcome" as a get-out-of-jail-free card.
**Why:** It is occasionally true. It is also occasionally a lie people tell themselves. The way to keep yourself honest is to be willing to write down, *before* you know how things turn out, what process you used, what alternatives you considered, what probabilities you assigned, and what would have changed your mind. If the answer to all those is fuzzy, you don't actually have a "good decision, bad outcome" — you have a hand-wave. The phrase only earns its keep when there's a real decision process behind it.

**Don't:** Stop at "that was resulting" without going on to do the actual analysis.
**Why:** Naming the bias is step one, not step five. Once you've established that the room is judging by outcome rather than process, you still have to do the work of evaluating the process. Otherwise "resulting!" just becomes a rhetorical move that ends the conversation at exactly the moment the conversation was about to get useful. Name it, then keep going.
