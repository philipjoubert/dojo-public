# The Empowerment of Failure

## When to Use
- You just face-planted. The lift didn't go up. The startup folded. The race ended at mile 81. The interview crashed. Whatever you set out to do, the scoreboard says you didn't do it.
- You want to go again, but you can't yet see why the first attempt broke. You're either ready to run the same playbook a second time (which will fail the same way) or you're tempted to walk away.
- The failure is public. People watched. The shame is loud, and the easy move is to hide.
- You've started using the word *failure* to describe yourself, not the result. *I failed* has slid into *I am a failure*, and you're treating that as a closed case.

## Fails When
- **You haven't attempted anything yet.** There is no autopsy without a body. The Empowerment of Failure is for people who actually got in the ring and got hit. People who never even toed the line don't get the gift; they get a different conversation, and that conversation is *go fail at something first*.
- **You're still bleeding.** The night I came off that pull-up bar with both palms filleted to the dermis, I wasn't ready to write the AAR yet. My mom drove me to the ER. I sat in that waiting room and the autopsy started in my head, but I didn't write a word for two days. If you literally just tapped out in the last hour, take a breath. The forensics work better when the adrenaline's gone.
- **You're re-attempting with no analysis.** Just throwing yourself at the wall again, hoping it gives this time, isn't the framework. That's punishment. That's pride. The whole point is that the next attempt is engineered out of the wreckage of the last one. If you're not engineering, you're just bleeding for nothing.

## Core Concept

September 27, 2012. Thirty Rock. NBC studios, the Today Show. Savannah Guthrie, Matt Lauer, the Guinness Book official, a bunch of looky-loos shooting selfies between sets. I'm trying to break the world record for pull-ups in twenty-four hours — 4,020 was the number to beat. I get to 2,500 and my body shut down. I had never hit muscle failure in my life. I'd run on broken legs in BUD/S. Run a hundred miles on broken feet. Now I couldn't lift my hands high enough to grip the bar. I failed in front of millions of people on national [ __ ] television.

So what do you think I did? Hung my head? Cried in my hotel room? You already know the answer. I went for a run in Central Park the next morning, left my phone behind, and started the AAR. After Action Report. In the military, after every mission, you sit down and you do a live autopsy on what happened — what went well, what broke, where the mindset cracked. You do it whether you won or lost. But when you failed, brother, the AAR is everything. Because failure is the gift nobody wants and almost nobody opens.

Here's the thing most people don't get. **The most powerful thing about failure isn't the failure. It's what comes after.** Most people quit at attempt one. They take the L, they let it crater their identity, they tell their wife it wasn't meant to be, and they bury the whole thing somewhere deep so they never have to look at it again. That's the move that makes failure permanent. Not the failure itself — the burial. The empowerment lives in the unflinching forensic on what broke and why, and in the willingness to walk back out and attempt again with the failures of the previous attempt as raw material.

I went back two months later. November 2012, CrossFit Brentwood Hills, Nashville. Failed again. Hands ripped to the dermis. 3,200 pull-ups, 800 short of the record, in rhabdo, dropped out, my mom drove me to the ER. Two failures in two months on national display. The doubters were everywhere. They said my arms were too long, I weighed too much, my form was too brutal, my hands would never hold up. They pointed at the scoreboard. They were not wrong about the scoreboard.

In the ER waiting room I'm holding what's left of my glove. It looks like it came off the OJ Simpson crime scene, marinated in blood. My mom looks at me and says, *Well, I know one thing — you're gonna do this again*. She read my mind. The autopsy was already running. *I knew there was treasure in this wreckage and leverage to be gained somewhere*.

January 19, 2013. Same gym. Custom foam pads cut from a mattress, taped around my hands. Different pace strategy — five pull-ups on the minute instead of six, slower start to last longer. Different break length — four minutes max, not the long ones that killed me twice. Different mindset — I'd already decided the record was mine; I'd just decoupled it from the calendar. Seventeen hours later, 4,030 pull-ups. Record broken.

The third attempt was built out of the corpses of the first two. I do not break that record without those two failures, period. That's the whole [ __ ] doctrine right there. **Failure is data. The forensics is the gift. The next attempt is the proof that you opened the gift.**

And one more thing about it: I went back on national TV after the second one. Larry King had me on. Millions watching me talk about failing twice in a row. You think that was comfortable? It was the opposite of comfortable. But hiding it would have made it worse. Failure that you keep private rots. Failure that you put on the table — that you say out loud, *I failed, here's why, here's what I'm changing* — that one you metabolize. The shame goes away when the analysis is done in public. The shame is just unprocessed failure.

## How to Apply

1. **Take the L. Out loud. To yourself first.** Before any analysis, name what happened. *I failed. Today. At this thing.* The Accountability Mirror is in play here. No spin. No *the bar was rickety, the room was hot, the team didn't show up*. Those things might be true and they go in the AAR — but the verb is *I failed*. You. Period.

2. **Wait one day. Then start the AAR longhand.** Not a mental list. Pen and paper, longhand, the way I did it after Nashville. Mental lists let you skip the parts that hurt. Longhand makes you sit in the chair until every line is on the page. The day of cooldown is so adrenaline doesn't do the writing.

3. **Start with what went well.** Counterintuitive — most people open with what broke. Wrong move. **Every failure has a list of things that went right, and you have to acknowledge them before the rest will land.** In Nashville, I listed Nandor's gym, the chalk, the tape, the support crew, the equipment that worked, my pacing for the first three hours. The good stuff carries forward. You're going to need it on attempt three.

4. **Then the mindset audit.** Not the equipment, not the conditions — the mind. *Where did belief leak? When did doubt show up? What was I telling myself in the prep, and what was I telling myself when it broke?* This is where most AARs fail. People audit the gear and skip the mind. The mind is the front line. *My commitment to preparation and determination in the fight are always there. They didn't waver, but my belief was shakier than I cared to admit.* That sentence, in my own AAR after Nashville, was the whole record.

5. **Then the tactical breakdown.** Now you go through what broke. Pace. Equipment. Strategy. Logistics. Be brutal. Don't blame anyone outside yourself for things you could have controlled. The bar in NYC was rickety — but I tested it twice and didn't make a change, so the bar is on me, not on NBC. *The scoreboard does not lie.*

6. **Schedule the next attempt before you leave the AAR.** This is non-negotiable. The next attempt has to be on the calendar before you close the notebook. *Calendar another attempt as soon as possible.* Not someday. Date and time. Without the next attempt scheduled, the AAR is just a journal entry. With it scheduled, the AAR is a battle plan.

7. **Engineer the next attempt out of the AAR — don't run the same playbook.** This is where pride kills people. They take the L, they say *I'll just train harder*, and they run the same playbook with more reps. That's not engineering. Engineering is reading the AAR and changing the *specific* things that broke. New pacing. New equipment. New mindset framing. If attempt two looks like attempt one with more grit, you're going to fail again the same way.

8. **Put it on the table publicly.** This is the Larry King move. The thing you most want to hide is the thing that loses its power the moment you put it in front of people. Tell your team you failed. Tell your wife. Post it. Talk about it in the next interview. **Failure that hides festers; failure that's named gets metabolized.** I'm not telling you to be theatrical about it. I'm telling you to refuse to lie about it.

## Examples

**Situation:** First Hell Week in BUD/S, Class 230. I'd worked my way down from 297 pounds, taught myself to swim, talked my way into the SEAL pipeline. Halfway through Hell Week I caught double pneumonia. I was coughing up blood, vomiting on the log during PT, and they pulled me on a medical. I defied doctor's orders three times trying to stay in. They eventually rolled me back to day one of Class 231.

**Application:** I didn't quit. The medical pull was failure on the scoreboard, but I refused the burial. I rolled back, knowing I'd be the only guy in BUD/S who had to do Hell Week twice. I did. Got injured again. Rolled back to Class 232. Did Hell Week a *third* time. Three Hell Weeks in one BUD/S pipeline, more than anyone in my class — by an order of magnitude. The graduation in Class 232 was built out of the wreckage of 230 and 231. Every failure was raw material for the next attempt.

**Result:** I graduated. Forty SEAL classes have come and gone since I was Honor Man at Ranger School. The story of "Goggins did three Hell Weeks" became part of my biography because I refused to let attempt one or attempt two be the whole story. **The empowerment was in the second and third attempts. The first one was just the price of admission.**

**Situation:** Pull-up record, attempts one and two, both publicly failed. Two months between attempt one (NBC, 2,500 pull-ups, full muscle failure) and attempt two (Nashville, 3,200 pull-ups, both palms ripped to the dermis, rhabdo). The doubters online were unanimous. *Goggins is too big. His arms are too long. His form is too brutal. He'll never break it.* The scoreboard agreed with the doubters.

**Application:** Live autopsy after each one. After attempt one: I'd been in the spotlight, the bar was loose, the room was a circus, my hydration plan was too pure carbs, my breaks were too long. After attempt two: the bar was solid (good), the chalk worked (good), my pace was right (good), but my hands couldn't take the abuse. The fix was custom foam pads cut from a mattress — something I remembered SEAL buddies using for heavy weight lifts. The pace got slower. Six pull-ups on the minute had failed me twice; I went to five. Breaks went from "as long as I needed" to four minutes max. *The next attempt was engineered out of the wreckage of the last one.*

**Result:** Attempt three. January 19, 2013. 4,030 pull-ups in seventeen hours. Record broken. Total: I'd lifted the equivalent of 846,030 pounds in one day, almost three times the weight of the Space Shuttle. Without attempts one and two, that record stays with Stephen Hyland. The forensics on the failures is what built the win.

**Situation:** Ranger School, my class. I had stress fractures in my shins so bad they were practically broken. I'd already failed out of one phase and was facing a roll-back. Most guys would have either quit or tried to fake their way through.

**Application:** I didn't hide what was happening. I went to medical, got the diagnosis on paper, accepted the roll. Then I did the AAR — what was the body breaking down on, what did the mindset look like, what was I doing in the gaps between phases. The forensics told me the lower legs needed a different recovery protocol and that I'd been over-running between phases instead of letting the bone heal. I came back. I won Enlisted Honor Man. Ranger School graduate, top of class.

**Result:** The Honor Man trophy was built on the back of a failed phase, not on a perfect record. **Honor Man is not the absence of failure; it's the recovery from failure done well.** Most people read the resume backward — they see the win and assume there was no break. There was always a break. The break is the lesson; the win is the application.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat failure as identity.
**Why:** *I failed* and *I am a failure* are different sentences. The first one is data. The second one is a closed case. Once you walk through that door — *I am a failure* — you stop running the AAR, you stop scheduling the next attempt, and you bury the whole thing. The doubters online told me my arms were too long. If I'd accepted that as identity instead of data, I'd still be looking at Hyland's record on a Wikipedia page. Failure is what happened today. It's not who you are. Refuse the verb-to-noun shift every single time.

**Don't:** Run the same playbook again hoping it works this time.
**Why:** This is the lazy version of the framework. Most people who say "I failed and I'm going back at it" mean they're going to throw themselves at the wall again with more grit and a louder pep talk. That's not engineering. That's punishment. After the first pull-up record attempt, if I'd shown up in Nashville with the same pacing, the same hand protection, the same break strategy, just more "heart" — I'd have failed again the exact same way. Probably worse. **The next attempt has to be different in specific, named ways. If you can't name what's different on paper, you haven't earned the next attempt yet.**

**Don't:** Skip straight to "what went wrong."
**Why:** Most AARs fail at this exact step. The autopsy starts with the chest cavity and skips the rest of the body. *Brainstorm everything that went well first, because in every failure a lot of good things will have happened, and we must acknowledge them.* The good stuff is what carries forward — Nandor's gym, the chalk, the support crew, the parts of my pacing that worked. If I trash all of that in the rush to find what broke, I lose the foundation for attempt three. The wreckage has treasure in it. You don't find treasure if you only catalog the holes.

**Don't:** Hide the failure to protect your image.
**Why:** I went on Larry King after the second pull-up record failure. National TV. Talked about failing twice in front of millions. Why? Because hiding it would have made it worse. Failure that you keep private rots and starts to define you in places nobody can see. **The shame is the unprocessed part. Process it out loud and the shame can't stay.** I'm not telling you to do a media tour every time you screw up. I'm telling you don't try to make the failure invisible.

**Don't:** Audit the gear and skip the mind.
**Why:** Easy AAR mistake. *The bar was loose, the chalk was wrong, the room was hot.* All true. Doesn't matter. The most important section of every AAR is the one labeled *mindset during prep, mindset during execution*. *My belief was shakier than I cared to admit.* That one line, on the page, is the difference between attempt three working and attempt three failing the same way. The mindset audit makes you look at yourself, which is the harder mirror, which is exactly why most people skip it. Skipping it is how the third attempt becomes a re-run of the second.
