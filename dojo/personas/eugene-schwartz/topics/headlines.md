# Headlines

## When to Use
- You are writing the first four to twelve words the prospect will ever see from you.
- You have already diagnosed the mass desire, the reader's State of Awareness, and the State of Sophistication of the market.
- The decision at the top of the ad is "what stops the hand, and what makes him read the second sentence?" — not "what is clever?"

## Fails When
- The writer tries to make the headline sell the product. A headline that tries to do the whole job does none of it.
- The writer has not yet read the product four times, taken forty pages of notes, and located the dominant mass desire. The headline will have nothing real to channel.
- The headline is evaluated in isolation. The only valid test is: does the reader's eye move to line two?
- The writer substitutes cleverness for specificity. Literary headlines that call attention to themselves obscure the product inside.

## Core Concept

The headline has exactly one job.

"Your headline has only one job — to stop your prospect and compel him to read the second sentence of your ad. In exactly the same way, your second sentence has only one job — to force him to read the third sentence." Nothing else. The headline does not sell the product, does not identify the company, does not argue the case, does not close the offer. It does one thing: it pulls the hand that was about to throw the mail in the basket. Ten seconds. That is the whole window.

The ad is a chain of acceptances. Each link exists only to drag the reader to the next. The headline sells the first line. The first line sells the second line. The second line sells the third. If any link in that chain fails — if any sentence stands there on its own ego instead of reaching forward to grab the next one — the chain breaks and the ad is dead. A headline you admire is usually a headline that stopped reaching.

The headline is not a statement — it is a selection. It reaches into the unnamed crowd of people walking past with eighty pieces of mail and fishes out, by the lapel, the exact reader this ad was written for. It names him; it names his desire; it hands him, in seven words, the concrete promise of what he already wants. The more aware your market, the less you need to say to do this. The less aware, the more of the desire itself you have to surface in the headline. Wrong level, wrong headline, dead ad.

## How to Apply

1. **Locate the dominant mass desire.** Not "health" — which specific fear, which specific dream, which specific resentment is the strongest current in this market right now. Write it down in one sentence before writing a word of the headline.

2. **Diagnose the State of Awareness.** Level 1 (Most Aware) — you can name product and price. Level 2 (Product-Aware) — you can emphasize a new feature or cut the price. Level 3 (Problem-Aware) — you lead with the problem and tease the solution. Level 4 (Solution-Aware) — you crystallize the desire as a named need. Level 5 (Unaware) — the headline must surface the desire itself before it can promise anything. A headline pitched at the wrong level is noise.

3. **Diagnose the State of Sophistication.** Count the competitors who have already made this claim to this market. In Stage 1 you can state the plain claim. In Stage 2 you enlarge it. In Stage 3 you introduce a mechanism and the mechanism becomes the news. In Stage 4 you elaborate the mechanism. In Stage 5 the mechanism is exhausted and you pivot to identification.

4. **Pick the template that matches the diagnosis.** (Patterns below.) Do not reach for a template first. The template is a consequence of the diagnosis.

5. **Test against the ten-second hand rule.** Imagine the letter hitting the hand next to a stack of junk. The hand is already moving toward the trash. Does the headline stop the hand? If you can't see the hand stopping, the headline isn't finished.

6. **Test the pull-through.** Read the headline, then the first line. Does the first line exist to explain the headline — or does it exist to pull the reader to the second line? If the first line is a victory lap, go back and fix the headline.

## Examples

Drawn from the annotated corpus (`extractions/example-index.md`). Each shows a different template in motion.

**How-to (positive polarity) — "How to Stay Young till 90"**
The mass desire is fear of premature aging and the hope of defeating the biological clock. Level 4 awareness: the reader wants to stay young but doesn't know a program exists that bundles face, weight, emotions, vitality under one author. The headline delivers the desire in flat, declarative form — "till 90" is the specificity that outperforms "stay young longer." Pulls to a lead that opens with three ascending goals: *"look of a youngster past 60... strength and power of a trained athlete beyond your seventies... vibrant good health till 90 and more."* The headline promises, the lead enumerates.

**How-to (negative polarity) — "How to Avoid Lawyers"**
Most legal-book headlines run "how to find the right lawyer." Schwartz inverts the whole category. Level 3 awareness, Level 2 sophistication — the self-help legal category is thin. Instead of promising the reader a better lawyer, he channels the stronger underlying desire: the reader's resentment at needing a lawyer at all. The pre-head reinforces the inversion: *"A breakthrough in FRANK SELF-HELP ADVICE that the public has long deserved from the legal profession — so that you can stop 'running scared' in legal matters."* Negative-polarity how-to works when the reader's dominant emotion toward the category is already hostile.

**Specific-number promise — "37 ingenious SPARE-TIME BUSINESSES that require almost no investment, and net you up to $200 EXTRA INCOME every week!"**
"37" is non-round and therefore specific and therefore credible. The stacked promise — *"almost no investment"* pairs risk-elimination with *"$200 EXTRA INCOME every week"* concrete upside. The pre-headline pre-qualifies: *"For the man (or woman) who would like to break out of the salary-rut — for good..."* The headline exists to pull into a rotating triple lead that opens with three consecutive "How would you like to..." paragraphs. Specific-number headlines live or die on the non-roundness of the number.

**Question headline — "Is It True? This Amazing PSYCHIC PERCEPTOR brings you Wealth, Love and Power Beyond Belief!"**
An interrogative forces the reader to answer by reading on. The desire stack — Wealth, Love, Power — normally violates the "one desire per headline" rule, but each is so universal that the stack reinforces rather than dilutes. The question mechanism (*Is it true?*) is what makes the stack work: it pitches the whole thing as a claim under scrutiny, not a brag.

**News / "At Last!" / "Now!" — "AT LAST! INSTANT RELAXATION!"**
News polarity in three words. "At last!" signals that a long-wanted thing has arrived; "Instant" is the velocity word that collapses time; "Relaxation" is the abstract-noun payoff. All caps. Stage-4 market (tranquilizer/self-help category is dense), so the headline's job is to trigger the perception of news; the mechanism — programmed-learning — gets unpacked in the body. "Now!" works the same way: see *"Now—Run Your Car Without Spark Plugs"* and *"Now Turn Your Mind Into a Mental Magnet."*

**Authority-transfer — "A Chiropractor's Treasury of HEALTH SECRETS"**
The credential ("A Chiropractor's") borrows the reader's trust in a profession. The noun "Treasury" implies abundance and secrets-normally-withheld. The pre-headline *"Revealed at last!"* adds news polarity. The entire headline is one compound promise of insider knowledge — no product description, no verb, no argument. It exists to pull to a lead that seats the reader in the doctor's office: *"How would you like to sit down with licensed Chiropractor, Dr. Samuel Homola, and ask him such vital questions about your health as..."*

**Body-part-as-agent — "Your Eyes Can Heal Themselves" and "Your Nerves Can Cure Themselves"**
This construction recurs in the corpus and is worth isolating. It removes the doctor — and the drug, and the surgery, and the cost — from the loop. The body part becomes the verb's subject. The reader's own body becomes the instrument. It pulls into the classic symptom-cascade lead because once the reader accepts "my eyes can heal themselves" he is already asking *from what?* and the symptom list is waiting.

**Risk-reversal in headline — "Don't Pay A Penny For This Book Till It DOUBLES Your Power To Learn!"**
Rare and powerful — the offer mechanics merge with the promise in one sentence. "Don't pay a penny" is the risk reversal; "doubles" is numeric specificity. Same move: *"DON'T PAY A PENNY TILL THIS COURSE TURNS YOUR MIND INTO A MENTAL MAGNET."* Use when the category is crowded enough that the reader's default stance is skepticism; the risk-reversal in the headline itself pre-empts it.

**Case-history headline — "FROM 4 PACKS A DAY TO ZERO, IN 4 HOURS!"**
Stage-5 market (quit-smoking is exhausted by failure — every smoker has tried something). The headline is a specific, dramatic case, laid out in parallel numbers (4 packs / 4 hours) for rhythm. The case is extreme enough that the reader's skepticism is obvious — Schwartz disarms it in the body: *"This book does not promise you, of course, that YOU will be able to duplicate this speed. But it does promise you this: YOU CAN QUIT SMOKING IN 14 DAYS!"* Case-history headlines work when (a) the case is specific, (b) the numbers are parallel, and (c) the ad immediately concedes that the reader is not the extreme case and offers the real promise.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a headline that tries to sell the product.
**Why:** A headline that tries to close is a headline that stopped pulling. The product is sold in paragraphs 7 through 40. The headline sells line two.

**Don't:** Let the headline "be clever" on its own — write to be admired as copy.
**Why:** Cleverness that calls attention to the glass obscures the product inside. If the reader notices the copy, the copy failed. The prospect must walk straight through the headline into the lead without seeing the writer's hand.

**Don't:** Use a round number ("100 ways") when a specific non-round number (37, 101, 215,000) is available.
**Why:** Round numbers read as estimates. Non-round numbers read as counted. Schwartz's corpus is full of 37, 101, 215,000, $20,000-$50,000-$100,000 — never 50, never 100, never 1000.

**Don't:** Pitch the headline at Level 1 awareness when the market is at Level 4 or 5.
**Why:** "X Brand Book — On Sale Now" speaks to a prospect who already knows the product, knows the brand, and knows he wants it. In a Level 4 market, he has never heard of any of that. The headline must surface the desire first, not assume it.

**Don't:** Write a headline in Stage 3 of sophistication that merely repeats the Stage 1 plain claim.
**Why:** "Lose Weight!" was new once, in 1925. By 1970 the reducing field was Stage 5 — the reader has heard every version of it. The headline must either introduce a new mechanism (*"Turns Up Your Digestive Furnace"*, *"Doesn't Just Shrink Fat Cells, But Destroys Them"*) or pivot to identification (*"How The Beautiful People Get Rid Of Both Cellulite And Ordinary Fat Without Dieting"*). Repeating the plain claim in a Stage 5 market is the single most common way ads die.

**Don't:** Bury the mass desire in an opening throat-clear before the headline hits it.
**Why:** The hand is moving. Ten seconds. Every word before the desire is weight dragging the headline into the trash. If a pre-headline exists, it exists to pre-qualify the reader ("For the man or woman who..."), not to warm up.

**Don't:** Ask a question in the headline whose honest answer is "no."
**Why:** An interrogative only pulls if the reader's silent answer is "yes — and how?" *"Does Your Child's Behavior Worry You Sick?"* pulls because the parent has already whispered yes. *"Do you want to save money on car insurance?"* is usually read as *no, I already have insurance, go away*. Test the silent answer before you commit the headline.

**Don't:** Evaluate headlines on whether the team likes them. Evaluate them on whether the reader's eye jumps to line two.
**Why:** The team is not the market. The team has read the brief, loves the writer, and is rooting for the copy. The prospect has eighty pieces of mail and a trash can. The only valid jury is the hand.
