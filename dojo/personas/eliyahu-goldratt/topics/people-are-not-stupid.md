# People Are Not Stupid

## When to Use
- Behavior in the organization is being labeled "lazy," "political," "resistant," "short-sighted," or "stupid." Those words are warning signs that diagnosis has stopped and blame has started.
- The organization is about to invest in training or discipline to change behavior. Before spending that money, check the measurements.
- New hires behave exactly like the old hires after a few months. The system is grinding them into the same behavior. That is the tell.
- An improvement initiative has been proposed and change management / "communicate more" is the main lever. Measurements and policies are far more powerful than communication.

## Fails When
- The individual is genuinely an outlier — has had all the right system incentives, clear feedback, time to improve, and still cannot perform. These cases exist but are rare. Most "incompetence" is systemic.

## Core Concept

When people behave in ways that damage the organization — hoard inventory, game metrics, protect turf, resist change — the natural conclusion is that they are the problem. They are lazy. They are resistant. They are short-sighted. They are political. They are stupid.

In reaching that conclusion, you make the worst possible error in problem-solving. You aim in the wrong direction.

If we start with blaming the other person, we are aiming in the wrong direction. If we are determined to solve a problem involving another party — in any domain — we should fight the tendency to blame, assume the other side is good, and look for a win-win solution.

Here is the central principle: tell me how you measure me, and I will tell you how I will behave. Read that again. It is one of the most important sentences in this body of work.

When a plant manager keeps all machines running even when there's no demand for the output, is he stupid? No. He is measured on machine utilization. He is responding rationally. When a salesperson discounts aggressively at quarter-end, destroying margin to hit volume, is she short-sighted? No. She is responding rationally to her compensation. When a project manager pads his estimates, is he lazy? No. He is responding rationally to a measurement that punishes misses asymmetrically.

In every case, the behavior that damages the organization is perfectly logical from the individual's perspective. The measurements told them what to do. They did it.

It is very unlikely your people are lying to you. But your measurements definitely are.

Measurements create reality — not metaphorically, literally. They determine what behavior is rewarded, punished, and therefore produced. Change the measurements, and the behavior changes. Keep the measurements, and the behavior persists regardless of training, discipline, exhortation, or hiring.

Why do we keep blaming people anyway? Because blaming is easier. When I blame someone, I don't have to examine the system. I don't have to admit my measurements might be wrong. I don't have to redesign incentives. I can simply say "we need better people." None of that works. New people under the same system behave the same way. Training doesn't override incentives. Discipline creates compliance without commitment.

There is a deeper reason too. When I conclude that someone is stupid or lazy, I implicitly conclude that I am superior. I would not behave that way. This is satisfying to the ego. It is also almost always wrong. Put me in their situation — their measurements, their pressures, their limited information, their competing demands — and I would likely behave exactly as they do.

We are all rational actors responding to our environment. When the environment is badly designed, rational behavior produces bad outcomes. This is not a character flaw; this is systems dynamics.

## How to Apply

1. **Stop using derogatory language about behavior you observe.** "Lazy," "political," "resistant," "deadwood" — these are warning signs that you have stopped thinking clearly. Replace them with "I'm observing [specific behavior]." Language discipline keeps diagnosis alive.

2. **Ask the diagnostic question.** For any counterproductive behavior: "What measurement or circumstance would make this behavior logical?" The answer redirects your attention from the person to the system, which is where the leverage is.

3. **Trace the measurement to the behavior.** If a plant manager hoards inventory, find the metric that rewards him for doing so (almost certainly "stockouts avoided" or "machine utilization" without a counter-metric for carrying cost). If a salesperson discounts, find the metric (quarterly volume with no tie to margin or throughput). The measurement is almost always upstream of the behavior.

4. **Change the system, not the person.** The system includes measurements, incentives, information, authority, resources, and policies. When behavior is problematic, one or more of these is creating it. Training might help with information. But training doesn't override incentives, and if the measurements are wrong, trained people will behave badly with more skill.

5. **Find the win for people who would "lose" under change.** Any significant change creates perceived winners and losers. Losers will resist — rationally. Your job is not to overcome their resistance but to eliminate the reason for it. Find them a real win. The conflict between your initiative and their interests can be removed if you look for it.

6. **Assume good faith.** Fight the tendency to blame; assume the other side is good; look for win-win. This is not naivete — it is strategic. Blame blocks thinking and creates enemies. Assuming goodwill enables creativity. Looking for win-win creates allies.

7. **Accept the principle at the personal level too.** The difficult colleague, the frustrating spouse, the uncooperative neighbor — they are responding rationally to circumstances you may not fully see. This is not an excuse for their behavior; it is a method for actually changing it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A distribution center consistently overstocks slow-moving items while understocking fast movers. Inventory is high; service is poor. "The inventory managers are incompetent."
**Application:** Ask the diagnostic question. What measurement makes overstocking slow movers logical? They are measured on stockouts. Stockouts are visible and embarrassing; excess inventory is invisible on the back shelf. Rationally, they over-order to ensure no stockouts, especially on long-lead-time items. The measurement punishes one direction and ignores the other. Change the measurement to include inventory turns alongside stockout percentage. Same people, same day, different behavior.
**Result:** Inventory drops; service improves. The managers were never incompetent; they were highly competent at optimizing the measurement they were given. The measurement was wrong.

**Situation:** A software team resists a new deployment process. "They're resistant to change."
**Application:** Ask why. Are their measurements unchanged — still on feature velocity per sprint — while the new process adds ceremony that slows them down? Yes. Then their resistance is rational. To get them to adopt the new process, the measurement must reflect its benefit (fewer production incidents, faster rollback), and the process itself must either save them time or be compensated for the time it costs. Forcing adoption without measurement change produces grudging compliance and quiet sabotage.
**Result:** The new process is redesigned to include a benefit the team can see, measurements are updated to reflect its goals, and adoption becomes enthusiastic. The "resistance" was rational; the fix was systemic.

**Situation:** A salesperson is "abusing" a discount policy to close deals. Leadership wants to discipline her.
**Application:** Her quarterly volume bonus is several times her base pay. The discount doesn't cost her anything; missing quarterly volume costs her everything. She is optimizing her income. Discipline will produce either her departure or a more careful version of the same behavior. Change the bonus structure — tie compensation to margin, or to throughput per deal — and the discount behavior disappears by itself, from every rep.
**Result:** The whole team's discounting normalizes. Nobody was disciplined; nobody needed to be. The system produced the discounting, and the system had to change.

**Situation:** A project manager pads every estimate. "He's sandbagging."
**Application:** Under the current regime, missing a task deadline is punished; coming in early produces no benefit. Padding is the rational hedge against that asymmetry. Discipline for padding makes the padding subtler, not smaller. Switch to Critical Chain: aggregate buffers, manage at the project level, stop punishing individual task misses. The padding vanishes.
**Result:** Project durations drop. The project manager was never sandbagging — he was hedging an asymmetric bet he had been set up to hedge. Change the bet, change the behavior.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Fire your way to a better culture.
**Why:** If the system grinds good people into the same behavior, new hires will produce the same result. Firing to "improve culture" without fixing measurements is a rotation, not an improvement.

**Don't:** Treat the individual's intentions as the explanation.
**Why:** Intentions are downstream of incentives. A well-intentioned person under a badly designed measurement system produces bad outcomes. A badly-intentioned person under a well-designed system is constrained. Design the system.

**Don't:** Mistake compliance for commitment.
**Why:** Discipline produces compliance under observation. Commitment requires that the rational choice under the measurement is also the right choice for the system. If the two diverge, you will get rationalized non-compliance the moment attention shifts.

**Don't:** Use "the system made me do it" as an excuse.
**Why:** The principle is a diagnostic, not an alibi. The purpose is to fix the system. If you are a leader and you notice that measurements are producing bad behavior, changing the measurement is your job. "My people are rational" is a beginning of analysis, not a conclusion.

**Don't:** Assume you are immune to the principle.
**Why:** Your own behavior is equally shaped by your measurements — bonus structure, public visibility, what your board asks about. If you are doing the wrong thing consistently, the measurement is probably pointing you there. Apply the diagnostic to yourself.

People are not stupid. Change the system. The behavior will follow.
