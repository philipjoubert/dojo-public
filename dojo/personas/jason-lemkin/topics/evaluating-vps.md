# Evaluating VPs

## When to Use
- You hired a VP — Sales, Marketing, Engineering, CS, or Product — 30 to 90 days ago and you're trying to figure out if it's working.
- Your VP is missing plan, asking for "more time," and you're debating whether to give it.
- You catch yourself making excuses for them in board meetings, in 1:1s, in your own head.
- You've thought "should I fire this person?" at least once, even for a second, and you're trying to talk yourself out of it.
- A top performer under them just quit, or is about to.
- You're doing a lot of their job for them and telling yourself it's temporary.
- You want the cross-functional version of the test — the one that applies no matter what VP seat you hired for.

## Fails When
- You apply it before day 30. Too early. Even great VPs need a ramp. The real signal window is 30–90 days.
- You apply it without having defined the #1 key metric for that function *before* they started. If you never agreed on what success looks like, you can't tell if it's improving.
- You apply it to an IC or a junior manager. The bar here is VP-level — recruiting, ownership, moving a function. Don't hold a senior AE to a VP test.
- You use it to fire someone who's actually a Good-But-Not-Great VP. Those VPs make you think "how do I help them scale?" — not "should I fire them?" Different problem, different answer.

## Core Concept

Look, hiring VPs as a founder is the hardest thing you do. Wait too long, the business stalls. Underhire, you don't get an owner. Overhire, all the money's gone. And simply getting it wrong can set you back a year — if you wait to take action. Most founders wait. They give a bad VP another quarter, then another, then another. 9 times out of 10 they end up saying the exact same thing 6 months later: "I should have done this six months ago."

The evaluation test is simple, it's the same across every VP function, and you'll know by day 60. Four parts. (1) Did they hire someone great in the first 60 days? (2) Did your #1 key metric for that function improve? (3) Did they take some real part of that job off your plate? (4) Does the team believe? Sales for a VPS. Leads and opportunities for a VPM. CSAT or NPS for a VP CS. Product velocity for a VPE or VPP. Whatever the function is, the four parts are the same. 4/4 is a real VP. 3/4 is not OK for now — no, you don't get to grade on a curve. 2/4 or less, move on.

And here's the hard part, the part everyone dodges: if you ever think "should I fire this person?" — even once, even for a second — the answer is yes. Your subconscious has already done the math. It's already added up the declining metrics, the defensiveness, the way the team goes quiet when they walk into the room, the excuses. Your rational brain then steps in with the 50 reasons to wait: "hiring is hard," "they're nice," "what if I can't find someone better," "they still have potential." That rational brain is protecting you from a difficult conversation. It is not protecting your business. In 15+ years and hundreds of B2B companies, I've never — not once — seen a founder regret firing the VP they were questioning. I've seen countless regret waiting.

The other thing to understand: more time does not cure VP woes. A VP that isn't working at day 60 isn't going to work at day 180. They'll hire more mediocre people under them. They'll bury more issues. They'll demoralize your A-players, who will quietly start interviewing. Every month you wait, the damage compounds and the cash burn accelerates. Fire fast. Fire kindly. And then own that it's your fault — because it is. The 48 Types filter failed, or the role was wrong for their stage, or you ignored flags in the interview. Whatever. It's on you. Which means the fix is on you too. Move.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the 4-part test at day 60.** Four questions, honest answers. (1) Did they hire someone great — a real A-player, not a warm body — in the first 60 days? (2) Did the #1 metric for their function improve? (3) Did part of the job come off your plate? (4) Does the team believe? 4/4 = real VP, double down. 3/4 = not OK, but watch closely for another 30 days. 2/4 or less = move on. No curves. No "well, the market is tough."

2. **Use the 30-day recruiting bar as the earliest signal.** 50% of any VP's job — Sales, Marketing, Eng, CS, Product — is recruiting. The real ones always have 1–2 great candidates in their back pocket the day they sign. If 30 days in they haven't hired anyone great, and they can't even name someone they're excited about bringing in, they probably never will. That's the earliest crystal-clear sign. Don't wait for day 90.

3. **Define the #1 key metric *before* they start.** For a VPS it's new bookings or pipeline coverage. For a VPM it's qualified leads or SQOs. For a VP CS it's NRR, CSAT, or churn. For a VPE/VPP it's ship velocity or release cadence. Write it down. Agree on it. Then measure it. If you never agreed what success looks like, you can't judge whether they're succeeding, and you'll spend 6 months arguing about the scorecard instead of the score.

4. **Ask "am I doing a lot of their job?"** The #1 sign you need to move on from a team member — VP or otherwise — is that you're doing a lot of their job. If your calendar looks the same after the hire as before, you didn't hire a VP. You hired a title. Move on.

5. **Run the blame-game diagnostic.** Great VPs own the number. Mediocre VPs blame — the reports, the product, the competition, the market, the quarter. "We'll make it up next quarter." "The product has gaps." "Competition got aggressive." "I need more data before I can commit." Every one of those is a clear sign. Blame-down — blaming their own hand-picked team for a miss — is about 100% terminal. These are their hires. If they're bad, that's on the VP, and a great VP has already moved on them before the conversation even happens.

6. **Watch for top performers leaving.** The best VPs know how to keep their winners — they never let them leave, and if one does, they treat it as a five-alarm fire. If a top rep, top engineer, top CSM quits 60 days after the new VP shows up, that's the team voting with their feet. Don't accept the excuse. The team knows things you don't know, and they've been there longer than the VP has.

7. **Ask once, honestly: "Should I fire this person?"** If the question even crosses your mind, the answer is yes. Not "give them another quarter." Not "wait for their next review." Yes. Today. Be honest but kind about it. "This isn't working out, and we both know it. Let's figure out a transition." And then run the search for the real one.

8. **Check yourself against the 9 CEO flags.** Your VPs fail partly because of their gaps, partly because of yours. Are you the surprise-springer, dropping bombs at board meetings that should've been flagged weeks earlier? Are you the we'll-make-it-up-in-the-last-two-weeks-er? The miss-every-plan-er? The blame-someone-elser? The thin-skinner? Great VPs won't stay with any of those CEOs for long, and if they do, they'll turn mediocre defensively. Own your half.

9. **Check yourself against the 7 signs a CEO isn't going for it.** Do you talk about selling for $20M–$30M? Do you change strategy monthly? Do you go dark with metrics when things get tough? Do you never hire any great VPs — three in a row, all gone in 9 months? If you never hire any great VPs, it's probably not the VP market. It's probably you. Mirror check.

10. **When you decide, fire fast. Replace with a real one.** The longer you wait, the more expensive it gets. The VP you're questioning already knows you're questioning them — they're already checked out or defensive. End the uncertainty. Have a plan for who covers the function in the interim (often: you, back in the seat). And go pre-recruit the real one, using the 48 Types filter and everything you learned about what the role actually needs.

## Signature Numbers

- **30 days** — you'll know on recruiting. No great hire brought in, no strong candidate in the back pocket — it's probably not going to happen.
- **60 days** — the 4-part test lands. 4/4, 3/4, or move on.
- **90 days** — one full cycle. The #1 metric has either moved or it hasn't. More time doesn't cure VP woes.
- **4 parts** — recruiting, metric, load off your plate, team belief. Same four parts for every VP seat.
- **50% of the job = recruiting.** Across every VP function. Same rule everywhere.
- **1 time** — the number of times you need to ask "should I fire this person?" before the answer is yes.
- **6 months** — how much sooner every founder, in hindsight, wishes they'd fired the bad VP. Every single one says the same thing.
- **10+ clear signs** for a VPS not working: blame-down, blame-product, blame-competition, "more time," "make it up next quarter," quota-attainment drop, top reps leaving, crazy unbacked plans, revenue retention slipping, doesn't know key metrics or Top 10 deal status, fear in their eyes. One or two is a signal. Three is a sentence.
- **9 CEO types** VCs struggle with. **7 signs** a CEO isn't going for it. **8 manager flags** that wash out in 3–9 months. Run them on yourself before you blame the VP.
- **$400K+** burned on a bad VP who hires mediocre reps that don't hit quota. That's not a one-time cost. That's a year of runway, gone.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder hired a VP of Sales 55 days ago. Q1 was soft. The VP says: "It's product gaps and the competitive environment. We'll make it up in Q2 — I need a full sales cycle." No hires brought in yet. One of the founder's top two reps just resigned.
**Application:** Four of the ten clear signs in one conversation: blame on product, blame on competition, "make it up next quarter," and a top rep leaving. Run the 4-part test: no great hires (0), metric not improving (0), founder still in every deal (0), top rep just quit so the team clearly doesn't believe (0). That's 0/4. And yes — the founder already caught themselves thinking "should I fire this person?" last week and pushed it down. The answer is yes. Fire kindly this week. Don't give another sales cycle. Own that it's the founder's fault for hiring outside the 48 Types. Then pre-recruit the real one.
**Result:** Founder has the conversation. Painful week. Goes back to founder-led sales for two months while searching. Eight months later hires a real Make-it-Repeatable VP who brings two reps from her network and 4/4s the test by day 60.

**Situation:** Founder hired a VP of Marketing 45 days ago. Qualified leads are flat. But — the VP just hired a great demand-gen lead from her old team, the team seems energized, and the founder has actually stopped writing the homepage copy themselves for the first time in 18 months. Founder is worried the lead number hasn't moved yet.
**Application:** Don't confuse "not instant magic" with "not working." Run the 4-part test honestly. (1) Great hire in 60 days? Yes — the demand-gen hire is real. (2) #1 metric improving? Not yet, but it's day 45 and the pipeline she's building will show up in 30–60 more days. (3) Load off the founder's plate? Yes — homepage, copy, campaigns. (4) Team believes? Yes — energy is up. That's 3/4 with a plausible path to 4/4. Internal dialogue is "how do I help her scale?" — not "should I fire her?" That's the tell. She's a Good-But-Not-Great-Yet VP. Back her. Hold her to the metric at day 90.
**Result:** Founder backs her. By day 90 qualified leads are up 40%. By day 180 she's hired two more, built the content engine, and the founder hasn't touched marketing in two months. Real VP.

**Situation:** Founder hired a VP of Customer Success 75 days ago. NRR has dropped 3 points. The VP says CS is "cleaning up technical debt from the last team" and needs two quarters. Founder has already had the thought "is this going to work?" twice. Best CSM on the team just put in notice yesterday.
**Application:** Two strikes on the thought-test (once is enough — they had two). #1 metric moving the wrong direction. Top performer leaving. "Needs more quarters" is the universal stall. That's 0/4 on the 4-part test, plus the subconscious has already flagged it. More time does not cure CS woes any more than it cures sales woes. Fire this week. Replace with a real one — someone who's scaled CS at a startup your size, not someone from Salesforce CS who managed a BDR-style retention team. And the founder should ask themselves: did you define NRR as the #1 metric before she started, or only after she started missing? If only after — half on you. Own that in the retro.
**Result:** Founder fires kindly. The CSM who was leaving actually stays because the dynamic shifts. NRR stabilizes in one quarter with the founder temporarily owning the top 10 accounts directly. New VP hired at month 5 with a clearly defined NRR target on day one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Give a failing VP "more time" past day 90.**
*Why:* More time does not cure VP woes. Period. If the #1 metric hasn't moved in one full cycle, no great hires have been brought in, and the team doesn't believe — another quarter won't fix it. What actually happens: they hire 2x more mediocre people under them to try to brute-force the number. None hit. Your A-players see you tolerating it and start interviewing. Your 18-month runway becomes 9. Keeping the B-player means losing your A-players. Every time.

**Don't: Ignore the "should I fire this person?" thought when it first crosses your mind.**
*Why:* That thought isn't random anxiety — it's your subconscious finishing a calculation your rational brain is still dodging. Declining metrics, team body language, customer reactions, the energy drain every time they walk in the room. Your gut is protecting the business. Your rational brain is protecting you from a hard conversation. In 15 years I've never seen a founder regret firing the person they were questioning. I've seen countless regret waiting. The cost is asymmetric.

**Don't: Accept "it's the product," "it's the competition," "it's the market" as a VP's explanation for missing plan.**
*Why:* The product was broken before they started. The competition was there before they started. The market was what it was before they started. They took the job knowing all of that. A great VP says: "Here's what we screwed up. Here's what we're fixing. Here's what's different next quarter." A mediocre one deflects to externalities. Blame-up to the product team, blame-down to their own reports, blame-sideways to competition — any direction other than at themselves — is terminal. Great VPs own the number.

**Don't: Keep a VP whose top performer just quit.**
*Why:* The team knows things you don't know. They've been there longer. They see how the VP runs meetings, handles losses, treats their best people in private. When the #1 rep, engineer, CSM, or PM quits 60 days after the new VP shows up, that's the team writing you a letter in ink. If you don't read it, the next three top performers will. Top reps leaving means you have the wrong VP. Don't accept excuses. This is as clear a sign as you will ever get.

**Don't: Make excuses for the VP in board meetings, to yourself, or to your cofounder.**
*Why:* Sign of a mediocre VP, or really of anything: you're making excuses for them. The moment you hear yourself saying "well, to be fair, she hasn't had a full cycle yet" or "he's dealing with a lot" or "the team just needs to adjust to her style" — pay attention. You're not convincing anyone else. You're trying to convince yourself. The fact that you have to means the decision is already made. Stop negotiating with yourself and make the call.

**Don't: Fire the VP without doing the founder mirror check.**
*Why:* If you've fired three VPs in 18 months, all of them "weren't the right fit," the problem might be you. Run the 9 CEO types and the 7 signs a CEO isn't going for it on yourself. Do you spring surprises at board meetings? Change strategy every two months? Go dark on metrics when things get tough? Never actually hire any great VPs? A pattern of VP churn with the same CEO is usually a CEO problem, not a VP problem. Fix your half, or the next VP will fail the same way. Own that *before* the next hire, not after.

**Don't: Keep a mediocre VP because you're scared to go back to founder-led.**
*Why:* Empty seat beats bad seat. I've watched companies keep their "okay but not great" VP for 18 months because the founder didn't want to step back in. Revenue stayed flat. Budget burned. Team calcified. When the change finally happened, revenue accelerated almost immediately — because the founder was actually running the function again, and the drag was gone. Going back to founder-led for 2–6 months while you search is not a failure. It's the correct move. Your reps, CSMs, engineers will respect it more than watching you keep a boss no one respects.

**Don't: Apply the 4-part test to a VP who's actually Good-But-Not-Great.**
*Why:* Good VPs make you think "how do I help them get to the next level? Maybe a coach. Maybe a CRO above them. Maybe help with enterprise while they crush mid-market." Good-But-Not-Great is a different problem — it's an optimization question, not a survival question. Don't fire a VP who's hired well, moved the metric, taken load off you, and earned team trust just because they're not perfect. No VP is perfect. The 4-part test is for "is this working?" not "is this world-class?" Use it correctly.
