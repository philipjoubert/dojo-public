# Burn Rate and Cash

## When to Use
- You just raised and you're deciding how fast to spend it.
- You're past $1m ARR and burn is creeping faster than your gut is tracking.
- You're debating cut vs. keep-investing through a soft quarter.
- Polished CFO or ops models — but you're missing burn plan month after month.
- You have high growth but also high churn and a big burn to match.
- Your board asks about runway and you realize you're not sure of the number.
- You're wondering whether your burn multiple is any good, or how much cash you should be sitting on.

## Fails When
- You're bootstrapped and cash-flow positive. The burn math doesn't apply — manage unit economics instead.
- You use "cut the burn" as the frame when the real problem is a broken sales motion or a product that isn't retaining.
- You cut into the top of the revenue engine — best reps, core product engineers — to hit a burn number. You make the number once and watch the company die anyway.
- You're genuinely 30 days from a wired term sheet with legal diligence done. Sprint through close — with a Plan B.
- You apply SaaS thresholds to D2C or hardware-heavy businesses. Gross margins change the whole calculation.

## Core Concept

Cash is oxygen. You can hide from a lot of things as a founder — a bad VP, a competitor's raise, a lumpy quarter, a roadmap six months behind. You cannot hide from your burn rate. Your cash balance is the most honest number in your business. It doesn't care about your narrative, your pipeline coverage, your operating leverage. It just counts down. When it hits zero, the conversation ends.

Burn doesn't blow up in one dramatic month. It creeps. At $1m–$2m ARR you hire one extra person a month, marketing runs with a 12-month CAC, renewals haven't kicked in — everything sounds reasonable in isolation. Year-end, burn is three or four times where it started. You think 18 months of runway; it's actually nine. Gradually, then suddenly. A high-burn month gets followed by another, not a lower one. Even a slightly too high burn rate gets out of control, because the compounding runs one direction.

The fix is a Burn Rate Budget and a Zero Cash Date. A Burn Rate Budget is a fixed monthly allowance set with the whole management team, reviewed on the first of each month. Zoom ran this way: Eric Yuan's burn was almost exactly equal to his gross profit for three straight years going into IPO. Not a coincidence — he told the team exactly how much they could spend, as a team. Zero Cash Date is the single month, on your current trailing spend, you run out of cash. Every founder, investor, employee should know it. Zero cash date is a fact, not a feeling. If you don't know yours within a week of precision, that's the first problem to fix today.

The danger zone is the 3H: High Growth, High Churn, High Burn — all at once. High churn with no burn, the engine self-perpetuates. High NRR with high burn, your 140%+ NRR pulls you through. The 3H combo is what kills you. Hypergrowth masks the churn for a year or two. Then growth slows, NRR won't save you, and you've got months of runway at a burn rate you built for the old trajectory.

## How to Apply

1. **Compute your Zero Cash Date this week — and share it.** Take average actual cash out for the last three or four months (bank statements, not P&L). Divide cash in the bank by that. Pick the month you hit zero. Tell every investor and your team. Update every 30–60 days.

2. **Hit the cash thresholds by ARR stage.** **$0–$1m:** 24+ months of runway — revenue isn't reliable enough to plan on. **$1m–$10m:** 18 months minimum. Under 12 is the danger zone. **$10m:** at least $5m on the balance sheet. **$24m+:** 50% of ARR in cash; 60–70% if growing 40%+. Below these you underhire and underinvest — that kills momentum more than the cash saves you.

3. **Set a Burn Rate Budget and review it weekly.** Take trailing four months of actual burn, average it, roll forward. Decide the runway you want, add a 3–4 month buffer, solve for the monthly burn. That's the budget. Set per department, publish the team total, review Mondays. Turns every VP from budget-defender into team player, because the allowance is joint.

4. **Know your burn multiple.** Net burn ÷ net new ARR. Early-stage SaaS, 1.0–1.5 is good. Under 1 is great. Over 2 a yellow flag. Over 3 a red flag — you're spending three dollars to produce a dollar of recurring revenue. Cleanest single number for "is our growth worth what we're paying for it?"

5. **Test before you cut or accelerate.** Missed plan last quarter: cut. Don't give the burn "one more quarter." Hit plan with 18+ months of runway: consider acceleration — only where you have proof the spend converts. Under 12 months of runway: doesn't matter if you hit plan. Cut, raise, or both.

6. **Cut in the right order.** First: non-revenue-generating headcount — redundant ops, mid-level managers without a team, zombie projects. Second: vendor and tool spend. Third: comp at the top — senior salaries, executive perks, the founder's own package. Don't start with SDRs, top reps, or core product engineers. That's the revenue engine. Cutting it is cutting the thing you were trying to save.

7. **Cut once, cut deep.** No salary freezes, hiring freezes, 10% trims. Those destroy morale and don't fix the math. Model the org at target burn, build that chart, go there in one motion. The team recovers from one bad day much faster than six months of "when's the next round?"

8. **Approve every check personally until you have real runway.** Every invoice, subscription, reimbursement crosses your desk. Not micromanagement — it's how you develop an instinctive feel for the burn, and catch the $5k/month tool two people use, the duplicate subscription, the unapproved sponsor. The day you delegate is the day burn starts creeping.

9. **Collect 100%+ of MRR in cash each month, ideally 110%.** Plenty of "growing" startups are financing their customers. Renewal invoices 60 days before expiration, payment by contract end, annual prepay on bigger deals when cash is tight. Collections can pull cash forward 20% with zero impact on growth.

10. **Make the CFO's job to lose sleep about burn.** CFO owns cash. Not VP Sales, not VP Product — the CFO. A polished CFO missing burn plan monthly and blaming other departments is a liability. Cash doesn't care about polish. If they can't come to Monday reviews with exact cash in, cash out, and an unflinching zero cash date — micromanage, or replace.

11. **Run an L4M model alongside whatever finance builds.** Average actual revenue and burn for four months, roll forward. Boring. Disagrees with the hockey stick in the board deck. That's the point. Where finance's model and L4M diverge, L4M is usually closer to what's about to happen.

12. **Default alive or default dead — pick one.** Paul Graham's framing. On current cash and burn, are you on a path to profitability? Default alive. If not: default dead. Default dead without a term sheet in hand means crisis mode. Raise now, cut now, or both now — not next quarter.

## Signature Numbers

- **24+ months** runway at $0–$1m ARR. Revenue too unreliable to plan on.
- **18 months** minimum runway at $1m–$10m ARR. Under 12 is the danger zone.
- **$5m on the balance sheet** at $10m ARR. Below that you underinvest.
- **50% of ARR in cash** past $3m–$4m ARR; 60–70% if growing 40%+.
- **$12m in cash** at $24m ARR — the specific floor for that stage.
- **1.0–1.5 burn multiple** — good for early-stage SaaS. Under 1 is great, over 2 a flag, over 3 a problem.
- **$0 net burn** — what Eric Yuan ran at Zoom in 2017 going into IPO, near-identical in 2018 and 2019. Proof a disciplined burn rate budget works at scale.
- **+6 months** on top of whatever runway you think you need. Everyone underestimates time to breakeven or next round.
- **30–40%** of the team — typical headcount cut to meaningfully move burn. "Efficiency" doesn't get you there; layoffs do.
- **3–4 months** minimum buffer in your Burn Rate Budget math.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder at $20m ARR. Grew to $10m on 100% YoY. Now growing 15%. Burn is $2m/month. True NRR has slipped to 80%. Ten months of runway. They want to "push through" until growth returns.
**Application:** Textbook 3H — historically High Growth, High Churn (80% NRR), High Burn. Growth isn't coming back while churn is this high. The burn is funding a broken retention engine, not a growth one. In parallel: attack churn relentlessly — CS into every at-risk account, fix the top three product gaps, re-segment to the ICP that stays. And cut burn in one motion to a rate sustainable on current revenue plus plausible new ARR — probably $700k–$900k/month. Non-revenue headcount first, then vendor spend, then senior comp. Don't touch top reps or core product engineers. You're buying a year to fix retention. If NRR isn't north of 100% in 9 months, you sell.
**Result:** Founders who run this playbook survive. Founders who try to grow out of 3H with burn intact almost always run out of cash before churn fixes itself.

**Situation:** Founder at $7m ARR. Raised $15m Series A six months ago. Burn was $400k/month at close; it's $720k/month now. They "still have 18 months of runway." They want to accelerate marketing into Q4.
**Application:** Runway is closer to 14 months because burn is still growing ~12% a month. Classic spiral. Compute zero cash date on trailing three months of actual cash out (not budget). Set a Burn Rate Budget at $800k/month, cap it, publish. Then: do you have proof marketing spend converts? If LVR is growing 10%+/month and revenue-per-lead is stable, accelerating into proven channels within the cap is defensible. If LVR is flat or close rates sliding, accelerating is just faster burn. "We need to spend to grow" is not evidence.
**Result:** Cap at $800k, acceleration restricted to proven channels, Q4 ends with 16 months of runway instead of 10.

**Situation:** Polished CFO — big-brand resume — has missed burn plan four months in a row. Explanations are always good, numbers are always off. The founder keeps wanting to believe him.
**Application:** Cash doesn't care about polish. A CFO who can't own the burn rate is a liability when cash matters, and cash always matters. Take ownership back for two weeks. Every Monday first thing: actual cash out last week, cash in, net change. Meet with the CFO weekly — exact numbers, no narratives. Inside 30 days he either steps up and starts calling misses in advance, or the gap widens. If he steps up, micromanage until you trust him. If not, replace him.
**Result:** The founder finds out fast — which is the entire point.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Tell yourself "burn will come down with efficiency."**
*Why:* It won't. Efficiency gains get you 5–10%, not the 30–40% cut you need. The only thing that reliably brings burn down is layoffs. "We'll optimize cloud spend" is how you waste three months that could have gone toward a real cut or a real raise. If the math requires a cut, the cut requires firing people.

**Don't: Confuse accrual revenue with cash in the bank.**
*Why:* P&L can look great while cash collapses. Booked $500k in annual contracts? If they pay quarterly, cash hasn't moved. Paid annual software licenses up front? P&L looks worse than actual monthly burn. Manage to the bank balance. Accrual is fine for the board deck — lethal as an operating metric early stage.

**Don't: Credit yourself for a burn multiple under 1 because of one great quarter.**
*Why:* Burn multiple means something over a trailing year, not a quarter. One lumpy deal, one prepay, one delayed hire fakes a great quarter. If trailing-12-month is 0.8 you're a capital-efficiency machine. If just last quarter was 0.8 and the three before were 2.5, you're in trouble and the one number is lying to you.

**Don't: Hide the zero cash date from the team.**
*Why:* Startup employees are adults. They know the company won't last forever on one round. What erodes trust is when the zero cash date leaks — from a board meeting, a rough investor conversation — and the team realizes the founder was hiding it. Share it. Your credibility survives the honest number; it doesn't survive being caught obscuring it.

**Don't: Cut before you've modeled what the cut actually produces.**
*Why:* Founders panic-cut 10% off every department, then realize burn barely moved — because the cost structure was ten big line items, not a hundred small ones. Model the post-cut org explicitly, confirm the burn number you need actually results. Cutting without a target number is pain with no payoff.

**Don't: Put the VP Sales or VP Product in charge of burn.**
*Why:* Those roles should be losing sleep over pipeline, product, and people — not cash. When the CEO makes revenue leaders responsible for burn, they under-hire reps, cut marketing programs that would have worked, and delay product investments that drive retention. Burn is the CFO's job. Growth is theirs.
