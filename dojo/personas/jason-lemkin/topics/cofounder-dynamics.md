# Co-Founder Dynamics

## When to Use
- You're about to pick a co-founder and trying to figure out if they're really in.
- You're debating 50/50 vs unequal splits and someone wants 50/50 for symbolic reasons.
- You and your co-founder both want to be CEO and someone suggests "let's just be co-CEOs."
- You have no co-founder and wonder if you're doomed.
- A co-founder left and you're about to raise.
- You're heading into a co-founder break-up and can feel it coming.
- You're about to lose a co-founder who is genuinely great — or close — and want to know whether to fight.
- You have three or four or seven co-founders and everyone keeps telling you that's too many.
- You're 18 months in, solo, and thinking about bringing on a real co-founder after the fact.

## Fails When
- You're using this as a substitute for an honest conversation. No vesting schedule fixes a conversation you haven't had.
- You're applying it to executive-team dynamics below the co-founder line. VPs aren't co-founders.
- You're treating it as relationship counseling. I'll tell you what works structurally. The feelings are your job.
- You're trying to engineer a co-founder into existence because you think VCs demand it. Fake co-founders are worse than no co-founders.

## Core Concept

Co-founder is the most important hire you'll ever make, and most founders treat it like the least important. 90% of public SaaS companies had two or more co-founders. I'm not good enough to build something great alone, and most of you aren't either. But the bar for a real co-founder is high, and the cost of the wrong one is really high. Be careful who you pick. Once you've picked them — if they're great — do whatever it takes to keep them.

The data is unambiguous. Per Carta, 23% of co-founders leave within 3 years, 30% by year 5, 35%+ by year 7. And the rate is *accelerating* — recent cohorts are 15–20% worse than 2018–2019. The honeymoon is 18 months. The danger zone is years 2.5–4. Plan for it. Structure for it. The question isn't whether the relationship could end. It's whether the company survives when it does.

Here's how I think about it operationally. Test commitment with vesting before you test it with anything else. Stop obsessing about 50/50 — unequal splits are the norm at great SaaS companies, and they're usually fair. There's one CEO. Always. Co-CEOs almost never work at your stage. If you have no co-founder, you can still win — Zoom did it — but you have to over-index on great early hires with real equity. If you lose a great co-founder, it's almost impossible to replace that depth of context, so try harder before you let them go. And if the break-up does happen, do it cleanly, get their stake below 6–8%, and move on.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the commitment test before you incorporate.** Put 8–10 year vesting on the table, starting at seed close or MVP launch (whichever is later), with a 1-year cliff, no credit for work already done, double-trigger acceleration. If every co-founder says yes intuitively and quickly, you're aligned. If anyone hedges, you have your answer — they're a senior early employee, not a co-founder.
2. **Screen out the pattern-match failures upfront.** Don't pick a co-founder who has something better to do, wants to see how it goes, has a clear backup plan, sees it as risky, argues over who should be CEO, or doesn't 100% believe. Any one is a no. Cheaper to see it now than in year 3.
3. **Stop obsessing over 50/50.** Only 3 of ~35 recent SaaS IPOs had truly equal co-founders. The average ratio is about 2:1. One founder usually put in more time, more capital, more risk, earlier. Price it in. Unequal and honest beats equal and resentful.
4. **One CEO. Always.** Co-CEOs almost never work at your stage. Atlassian, Monday, Salesforce pulled it off — those are exceptions with deep trust and carved territory. If the reason for considering co-CEOs is "we can't decide," that's not a reason. Pick one CEO. Give the other a real title — COO, President, CPO — with real power.
5. **Separate roles cleanly. No overlap.** The co-founder teams that last — Wiz, Rubrik, Databricks, Elastic — all do the same thing: each person owns a distinct functional pillar from day one. No committee decisions on things that should have one owner. If you and your co-founder are both "kind of" running product, you have a future fight. Fix it now.
6. **Have the hard conversation early. Especially in the 2.5–4 year window.** What happens if someone wants out? If they're not performing? If family stuff happens? Document it. Schedule it every 6 months. Most co-founder break-ups get ugly because the team avoided this conversation for a year.
7. **If you have no co-founder, compensate with great early hires and real equity.** Eric Yuan did it at Zoom — 20+ engineers with outsized equity. Find an "ex post-facto co-founder": a VP of Product, Engineering, or Sales who takes ownership like an owner. Don't panic-pick a co-founder just because VCs asked. A bad co-founder is worse than no co-founder.
8. **You can bring on a real co-founder later — rarely, but it works.** Usually at Seed or Series A, with real equity (3–5% minimum, often more). They have to pass the commitment test the same as a day-one co-founder. Vesting clock starts now, not retroactively. Don't call someone a co-founder unless they're committed and compensated like one.
9. **Don't lose a great co-founder. Try one more time.** The reverse rule. If your co-founder is still a 10 as an executive, still 90% committed, don't let them walk unless you have someone visibly better on deck. Bring in help. Talk it out. Stop being hurt. Losing a great co-founder slows product velocity, makes multi-product harder, and leads to earlier exits.
10. **If the break-up is the right call, do it cleanly.** Treat them the way you'd want to be treated. Be direct, be grateful, move fast. A bitter ex-founder drags a cloud over the company for months — lawsuits, Glassdoors, whisper campaigns. The exit done poorly costs you far more than the exit itself.
11. **Manage the departed stake down to single digits.** VCs start to worry above 6–8% non-working co-founder ownership. Negotiate: voting agreement with majority, board seat restructured away, new CEO grant in the pre-money to rebalance, or — in a $20M+ round — buy some back at a Common discount. VCs care about control, not the money. Neutralize control and financings go back to normal.
12. **Stop overthinking the number of co-founders.** Wiz 4. Databricks 7. Anthropic 7. What matters: have you worked together before, do you trust each other, can each of you own a distinct piece? If yes to all three, 4 or 7 is fine. If no, even 2 is too many.

## Signature Numbers

- **23% / 30% / 35%+** — Carta co-founder departure rates at 3 / 5 / 7 years. Trend is up.
- **2.5–4 years** — the danger zone where most break-ups cluster.
- **8–10 years** — founder vesting length that actually matches the SaaS journey. 4 isn't enough.
- **~2:1** — average founder-CEO to co-founder equity ratio at public SaaS IPOs.
- **~15%** — average founder-CEO ownership pre-IPO at public SaaS companies.
- **Only 3 of ~35** — recent SaaS IPOs with truly equal co-founder equity. All 3 were B2D.
- **90%** — public SaaS companies with 2+ co-founders. Solo is possible, not easy.
- **6–8%** — max non-working co-founder stake before VCs push for restructuring.
- **$20M+** — round size where buying out a departed founder's stake becomes realistic.
- **1 CEO** — always. Not 1.5. Not 2. One.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is about to incorporate with two friends. Everyone's excited, everyone's in "forever," nobody wants to bring up vesting. They're planning 33/33/33 because it feels fair.
**Application:** Put three things on the table in one sitting. One, 8–10 year vesting with a 1-year cliff from seed/MVP, zero credit for work already done, double-trigger acceleration. Two, an honest conversation about who's really CEO. Three, an honest split based on contribution, capital, and role — not just symmetry. If anyone hedges on vesting, argues for 33/33/33 when one person is clearly the driver, or wants to punt on CEO — you've learned something real.
**Result:** One of the three hedges on vesting. The real conversation surfaces that he sees this as "the thing I'll try before biz school." He shifts to senior early employee with a standard grant. The remaining two incorporate as genuine co-founders. 36 months later, when break-ups cluster, this team is still solid because the uncommitted one never got in.

**Situation:** A founder at $4M ARR and their co-founder — still great, 90% committed not 100% — are clashing. The founder is ready to push them out.
**Application:** Don't. Not yet. A co-founder at $4M ARR knows more about your product, team, customers, and history than any outside hire could learn in two years. Bring in a coach. Have the three hard conversations you've been avoiding. Restructure roles — maybe they stop managing a team, like Dharmesh at HubSpot, and go deep on product. Tell them you want them to stay. Mean it. Losing them slows product velocity, kills your second-product odds, and shortens the exit horizon. Try harder. One more time.
**Result:** Founder has the hard conversation, co-founder shifts to an IC role leading technical architecture, keeps most equity, stays through IPO. The product velocity they kept was the difference between a $500M exit and a $3B outcome.

**Situation:** A founder's co-founder left 14 months ago with 22% of the company. Now the founder is raising a Series A and the term sheet is stuck on it.
**Application:** VCs don't care about the money — they care about control. Negotiate a voting agreement: departed founder votes with the majority on fundraising, M&A, IPO. Get them off the board. Include a CEO grant to you on a new vesting schedule out of pre-money to rebalance. If the round is $20M+ with competitive interest, offer the lead the option to buy some of the 22% at a Common discount. The stake becomes economically neutral.
**Result:** Round closes. Founder gets a fresh grant that rebalances the cap table. Departed co-founder keeps most of their upside, loses control. Company goes from "structurally problematic" to "normal financing" in six weeks.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Default to 50/50 because it "feels fair."**
*Why:* 50/50 is the exception, not the rule, at great SaaS outcomes. Only 3 of ~35 recent IPOs had truly equal co-founders. One founder usually put in more time, capital, and risk earlier. Force 50/50 when contributions are unequal and you build in resentment — the founder who did more will notice in year 3, and the one who did less won't want to hear it. Price contributions correctly at incorporation.

**Don't: Solve a co-CEO argument by making yourselves co-CEOs.**
*Why:* It's almost always a band-aid for a conversation you don't want to have. Co-CEOs spook VCs, confuse the team, and slow decisions. The exceptions had pre-existing trust and clear domains. If your reason is "we can't agree who should be CEO," the structure won't fix it — it will embed the problem. Pick one CEO. Give the other a real title with real authority.

**Don't: Use 4-year vesting for founders.**
*Why:* Nowhere near long enough for the SaaS journey. The break-up danger zone is years 2.5–4 — right when a 4-year schedule is fully vesting. That's backwards. 8–10 year vesting with a 1-year cliff from seed or MVP matches the compounding curve and protects the co-founders who stay from the ones who don't.

**Don't: Call someone a co-founder to get them to join cheap.**
*Why:* Titles without matching equity and commitment are a lie, and the lie shows up in 18 months. If someone is joining for 0.5% or 1%, they're an early employee with a nice title. Pay real co-founders like co-founders (typically 20%+ early, with founder vesting). Pay early employees like early employees. Be honest about which is which.

**Don't: Let a great co-founder walk without fighting for them.**
*Why:* Companies that lose a great co-founder slow down on product, stall on multi-product, and exit earlier than they should have. A co-founder at $10M–$200M ARR knows more about the business than anyone you'll ever hire. If they're still great — or close to it — try harder. Bring in outside help. Do it one more time before you accept the break-up.

**Don't: Assume investors will punish you for a clean co-founder split.**
*Why:* Co-founder departures aren't automatically disqualifying. What disqualifies you is a messy split with a big non-working stake and lingering control issues. VCs don't care how much money the ex-founder makes — they care about control. If you've neutralized that (voting agreement, off the board, stake below 6–8% or bought down in the round), financings continue normally.
