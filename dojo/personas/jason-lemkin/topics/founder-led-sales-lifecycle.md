# Founder-Led Sales Lifecycle

## When to Use
- You're a founder pre-$2M ARR and you've never hired a salesperson.
- You're stuck somewhere between $500K and $2M ARR and thinking "I need to get out of sales."
- You've closed 10–20 deals yourself and you're wondering what the next move is.
- You hired one sales rep and you're trying to decide whether to hire another or jump to a VP.
- Your new bookings were growing, and now they're flat for three months in a row.
- You just hired a VP of Sales at $2M ARR and your close rate is dropping.
- You're bootstrapped, past $3M ARR, selling everything yourself, and you want to scale.

## Fails When
- You're at $10M+ ARR. This topic is past due. The transition should already be done. You have a different problem.
- You're running a pure PLG motion — Stripe, Dropbox for many years, small-team freemium — and sales is literally not the GTM. Skip this. Add sales later, when you add an enterprise edition.
- You're pre-revenue. There's no sales motion to transition. You need customers first. Go close one.
- You haven't shipped the product. Sales can't be the problem if there's nothing to sell.

## Core Concept

Look, founder-led sales isn't optional. It's how you close your first 10 to 20 customers, and it's how you learn what the product actually is. Before that, nobody else can close. They don't know the product. They haven't felt the objections. They can't answer the deep questions. And customers love talking to the CEO — they always will, no matter how big you get. So you sell. You personally close those first deals. That's non-negotiable, even if you hate sales. I hated it too when I started EchoSign. I did it anyway.

Founder-led sales generally breaks at $1M–$2M ARR. Sometimes later. Sometimes a lot later — Stripe didn't add real sales until 2018, Dropbox, Slack, Canva, Monday were all similar, when they bolted on an enterprise edition to a freemium motion. But most of you aren't those companies. Most of you sell B2B software with a real ACV, and at $1M–$2M in ARR, you're going to feel it: bookings growth starts to decay, leads go cold because you can't call them back, you're spending 80%+ of your time on sales and the rest of the CEO job is suffering. That's the signal. Not a feeling. Bookings growth decay is math. Watch the % growth rate, not just the absolute. You can be growing revenue and still be rotting.

The transition rule is: hire two reps, not one. Never one. One rep teaches you nothing. If they succeed, you don't know why. If they fail, you don't know why. Two lets you A/B test. You compare. You learn what segment, what price point, what objection handling works. Get both reps hitting a basic, sustainable quota before you even start looking for a VP of Sales. That's the gate. Two reps at plan means there's something to scale. Without that, there's nothing for a VP to scale, and you'll burn $400K+ on a VP who can't figure out what you should have figured out.

And here's the part most founders get wrong: you never get to leave sales. Ever. I sold personally at EchoSign through $3M–$4M ARR, and even after we had a real VP and real reps, I was still on every strategic call, every enterprise deal, every "this could change the trajectory of the company" conversation. You don't get the time back. You just shift from opener-and-closer to middler. More deals, same hours. The CEOs who try to "get back to product" after their VP of Sales starts — I see this all the time, especially after the Series A — almost always watch sales go down. Don't do that.

## How to Apply

1. **Close your first 10–20 customers yourself. Full stop.** No exceptions. This is how you learn the product, the objections, the buyer. If you can't sell your product, nobody else can either, and you have no right to teach them. Do this even if you hate sales.
2. **Before you hire anyone, prove deals can close.** You need at least a handful of wins that aren't friends-and-family. Real buyers, real checks, real reasons they bought. If you haven't proven deals can close, you're not ready for reps. You're still figuring out what the product is.
3. **At $500K ARR, you should be spending 80%+ of your time on sales.** If you are, that's right, not wrong. You're not behind — you're in the right phase. Keep going.
4. **Watch bookings growth rate, not just bookings.** ARR keeps growing for a long time after founder-led sales has actually stopped scaling. What you're watching for is the % growth rate of new bookings decaying — $100K one month, $80K, $120K, $90K, flat for three months. That's the signal. Not an emergency, but a month to start the VP search.
5. **Start the VP Sales search at the first month new bookings growth stops accelerating.** Not when you're desperate. It takes 6–12 months to close a great VP — they have good jobs you'll need to pry them out of. Start early. Don't wait until you're on fire.
6. **Hire two reps first. Never one.** I made this mistake at EchoSign. Hired one brilliant rep, he crushed it, I learned nothing. Not about segments, not about price points, not about what was actually repeatable. Two reps is the A/B test. Two lets you learn.
7. **Hire reps you'd personally buy from.** This is the one cheat code. If you wouldn't trust them with your own checkbook, you won't trust them with your leads, and the thing falls apart. Eighteen months of SaaS sales experience at roughly your price point is the baseline. Smarter than average. True product experts.
8. **Compensate generously. Commission only costs you if they earn it.** 50/50 base-to-variable is typical. OTE has to be compelling. And give the first reps 2x–3x the equity you'd give a rep a year later. Sometimes more. That first magical rep — get her more equity. You won't regret it.
9. **Document the sales process before or during their first week. ICP, basic playbook, CRM setup.** If it's not in the CRM, it doesn't exist. If you don't have this pre-built, build it together with them in week one. Don't abandon new reps and hope they figure it out.
10. **Give each rep one sales cycle to prove themselves. That's it.** Some great reps crush it week one. Some need a month. But in a startup, if a rep can't put points on the board in one sales cycle, they're not going to. If they're giving 110% and still nothing, it's the wrong fit.
11. **Don't hire a VP of Sales until both reps are hitting plan.** This is the gate. Two reps at a sustainable quota means there's something to scale. Without that, you're hiring someone to figure out the motion for you, and that almost never works. The VP is there to scale what's working, not invent it.
12. **When you hire a VP, aim for a stretch hire, not a double-stretch.** A director of sales ready for their shot at VP. Someone who's hired and managed 2–3 reps before at a company your size ($1M–$10M ARR), going to $10M–$20M. Don't hire from Salesforce, Datadog, late-stage Stripe. Their playbook is for a different company. You want someone who's been in the trenches.
13. **Give your VP a quota on your time. 4 customer calls a week, minimum.** They're expected to drag you into their biggest deals. They should use those chips. You are their closing weapon. Don't resent this — it's the whole point of having you.
14. **Stay in every deal that could change the company's trajectory.** Seven-figure deals, bet-the-company accounts, strategic logos, major case studies. Let the team handle routine. But when a prospect wants to look the founder in the eye, show up. Customers and prospects love, love, love to talk to the CEO.
15. **Never get the time back. Ever.** Assume you spend as much time on sales and customers after the transition as before. You'll become more of a middler than an opener-and-closer, but the clock doesn't reduce. There will just be more deals. That's fine. That's the job.

## Signature Numbers

- **$1M–$2M ARR** — where founder-led sales typically breaks. Watch bookings growth decay here.
- **10–20 customers** — the number of deals the founder personally closes before hiring the first rep.
- **2 reps** — always two. Never one. One teaches you nothing.
- **80%+ of time** — what the founder spends on sales at $500K ARR. That's right, not wrong.
- **$3M–$4M ARR** — where I personally sold through at EchoSign. You can push founder-led longer than most people think.
- **6–12 months** — how long it takes to close a great VP of Sales. Start early.
- **1 sales cycle** — how long you give a new rep to put points on the board.
- **$10M ARR** — the threshold before which you don't hire a VP who "mainly talks about process." You're too early for them.
- **40% drop in close rate** — what I've seen at founders who hired a VP too early and tried to step back. Then they're back on every call anyway.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is at $500K ARR, selling alone, spending most of their waking hours on demos and follow-ups. They're exhausted and ask when they can hire someone to take sales off their plate.
**Application:** Don't. At $500K ARR, spending 80%+ of your time on sales is the job. You haven't closed enough deals yet to know what's repeatable. Keep selling personally through at least $1M ARR. Start watching your bookings growth rate — not just bookings, the % growth rate. When that decays for 2–3 months, that's the signal to start hiring reps. Not before.
**Result:** Founder keeps selling, hits $1.2M ARR, notices bookings growth flattening, starts interviewing two reps in parallel. Hires both at $1.4M ARR. They both hit plan by $2M. Transition starts from a position of knowing what works.

**Situation:** A founder is at $2M ARR, has one great sales rep they hired nine months ago who's hitting quota. They're asking whether to hire another rep or jump straight to a VP Sales.
**Application:** Hire a second rep. Not a VP. You have one data point, and one data point is uninterpretable. Maybe your rep is a genius. Maybe the product sells itself. Maybe you have a lucky lead mix. You don't know. Hire a second rep you'd buy from, get them to plan, then — and only then — go look for a VP. A VP hired before there are two reps on plan has nothing to scale.
**Result:** Founder hires rep two. Rep two hits plan after one cycle. Now the founder knows the motion is repeatable across two different personalities. Starts the VP search with real data and a real playbook. Closes a stretch VP who's hungry, who comes with one rep from their previous company, who actually scales the thing to $8M ARR.

**Situation:** A founder at $2M ARR got excited, hired a big-name VP of Sales from a late-stage unicorn, and six months later the close rate is down 40% and they're back on every important call. They're asking what went wrong.
**Application:** You hired the VP too early, and you hired the wrong type. Two issues. First, you didn't have 2 reps on plan — there was no motion to scale, so the VP had to invent it, and that's not what they're good at. Second, the VP came from a $100M+ ARR company with a mature playbook. They don't know how to build from scratch. Cut bait. Go back to founder-led, hire a second rep, get both on plan, then hire a stretch VP — someone who was a director at a $5M–$10M ARR startup ready for their first VP role. Not a polished process person. A hustler who'll close deals themselves.
**Result:** Founder has the hard conversation, parts with the VP, spends three months rebuilding the motion with reps. Hires a stretch VP nine months later. Growth re-accelerates. Painful lesson, $400K burned, but recoverable.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Hire a VP of Sales before you have 2 reps hitting plan.**
*Why:* A VP of Sales is hired to scale what's already working. Without 2 reps validating a repeatable motion, there's nothing to scale. The VP will burn cash figuring out what you should have already figured out — and most of them aren't actually good at that. You get 6–12 months of decay, a 40% drop in close rate, and you end up back on every call yourself. Get 2 reps on plan first. That's the gate.

**Don't: Hire one sales rep to "save money" or "keep it simple."**
*Why:* One rep is uninterpretable. If they crush it, you don't know why — product, rep, lead mix, luck. If they flop, you don't know why — bad hire, bad product, bad you. You learn nothing either way. Two reps is the A/B test. Two is cheaper in the end because you learn faster and stop guessing. I made this mistake at EchoSign. Don't make it.

**Don't: Hire a rep you wouldn't personally buy from.**
*Why:* If you wouldn't trust them with your own money, you won't trust them with your leads. You'll second-guess every deal, steal leads back, hover, micromanage. The rep fails because you never actually let them sell. This is the #1 error founders make on first reps. Non-negotiable: reps you'd honestly, truly buy from.

**Don't: Think "founder-led sales" means you as CEO stop selling after the VP starts.**
*Why:* You never get to leave sales. Ever. Especially after a Series A — I see founders try to "get back to product" the week the VP starts, and sales almost always goes down. Customers want to talk to the CEO on strategic deals. They always will. Your time in sales doesn't shrink, it just shifts from opener-and-closer to middler. Accept it.

**Don't: Hire a VP Sales from Salesforce, Datadog, or a late-stage unicorn for your $2M ARR company.**
*Why:* Their playbook is for a company 20x your size with systems, brand, inbound pipeline, and a sales ops team. At $2M ARR, you need someone who'll learn the product cold, jump on calls, close deals themselves, hire 2–3 reps immediately. A process-heavy VP from a mature company won't do that. Before $10M ARR, do not hire a VP of Sales that mainly talks about process.

**Don't: Wait for "emergency" before starting the VP Sales search.**
*Why:* It takes 6–12 months to close a great VP. By the time bookings decay is obvious to your board, you're already 6 months late. Start looking the month new bookings growth stops accelerating. You don't have to hire. You just have to start the pipeline. VPs are like customers — you need a pipeline long before you need to close.

**Don't: Let a new rep go longer than one sales cycle without points on the board.**
*Why:* In a startup, reps who don't produce in the first sales cycle almost never do. Some great reps crush it week one, some need a month, but "give them another quarter" is almost always a waste of runway. If they're giving 110% and nothing's closing, it's wrong fit. Move on. Hire the next one.
