# The Psychology of Human Misjudgment: The 25 Standard Causes

## When to Use
- As the checklist on Track Two of any important decision involving people. Not as decoration. As a literal list to run through.
- Diagnosing a disaster. Every famous business disaster can be explained by three or four of these tendencies combining. Use the catalog to reverse-engineer what actually happened.
- Designing systems. You are building a company for creatures who have all twenty-five of these tendencies running in the background. Design accordingly.
- Evaluating your own certainty. When you feel sure, run the list against yourself. You will usually find three or four tendencies loaded in favor of whatever you already believe.

## Fails When
- **Used as academic taxonomy.** The catalog is a working tool. Naming a bias does not neutralize it. If you are not designing around the bias structurally, the naming is theater.
- **Applied only to others.** The people running these tendencies most visibly are almost always the people applying the catalog to everyone around them. You are a human being. You have all twenty-five. Apply first to yourself.
- **Used to explain everything.** Sometimes a decision is bad because the math is bad. Sometimes a founder fails because the market is genuinely small. Don't reach for psychology when rational analysis is sufficient. Both tracks, not just one.

## Core Concept

Here is the thing about the twenty-five tendencies. They are not bugs. They are features. Evolution installed them because they usually work — in the world our ancestors actually lived in, a world of small groups and immediate dangers, these heuristics were survival technology. The problem is that modern life puts us in situations the heuristics did not evolve for, and puts us into groups where our reactions get amplified, and puts us in front of advisors and salespeople and manipulators who have figured out which buttons to push. When the tendencies misfire, and especially when several of them combine in the same direction, you get the lollapalooza effect — cult brainwashing, acquisition fever, corporate fraud, bubble markets, founder catastrophes. Not rare occurrences. The ordinary mechanism of human disaster.

Psychology, as an academic field, studied these biases one at a time in controlled experiments and missed the whole point — which is how they combine. Physics would be ruined if it ignored compound effects. Chemistry would be ruined if it ignored how elements interact. Psychology was ruined, for a long time, by exactly the same mistake. The working version of this list is the combined version. You go down the list and ask which tendencies are operating here, how they are reinforcing each other, and what structural intervention breaks the cascade. That is the only use that matters.

The right culture is one in which no one can make a decision free of the discipline of competent people who know something about it — and none of them is paid to reach the particular conclusion. That sentence, if you take it seriously, disarms roughly three-quarters of the damage these tendencies would otherwise do. The rest you handle by recognizing them and building around them. Here is the catalog.

## The 25 Tendencies

### 1. Reward and Punishment Super-Response Tendency
**What it is:** People respond with intense changes in behavior and cognition to incentives — more intense than almost anyone expects.
**Why it exists:** Evolution favored organisms that responded strongly to rewards and punishments. The ones that didn't got eaten or starved.
**How it causes misjudgment:** People unconsciously game every system to maximize their rewards, and their beliefs shift to match. The surgeon paid per surgery concludes the gallbladder is the source of all evil. Whose bread I eat, his song I sing — and the singer does not know he is singing.
**Antidote:** Audit incentive structures ruthlessly. Fear advice that is especially good for the advisor. Build systems that make bad behavior hard, not just discouraged — Patterson's cash register, not Patterson's honor code.

### 2. Liking/Loving Tendency
**What it is:** Humans are born ready to like and love, and once attachment is formed, it distorts cognition — people ignore faults, favor associated things, and reshape facts to preserve the feeling.
**Why it exists:** Bonding and cooperation required strong attachment mechanisms. Families, mates, allies. The machinery is not optional.
**How it causes misjudgment:** The founder who overweights a trusted employee's judgment. The investor who backs founders he likes against better-looking investments. The hiring process that consistently picks agreeable people over competent ones.
**Antidote:** Franklin's rule, upgraded: "See it like it is and love anyway." When evaluating someone you like, actively search for disconfirming evidence. Don't rely on face-to-face impressions — overweight the track record.

### 3. Disliking/Hating Tendency
**What it is:** The mirror image. Humans are born ready to dislike and hate, and once it's installed, the person ignores virtues, dislikes associated things, and distorts facts to justify the hatred.
**Why it exists:** In-group and out-group distinctions were essential for survival. Suspicion of strangers and competitors was adaptive.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Underestimating a competitor you dislike. Missing what you could learn from a person or faction you've written off. Escalating conflicts because the other side has been miscategorized as uniformly bad.
**Antidote:** Never underestimate a competitor you dislike. Force yourself to find things to respect in people you oppose. When the World Trade Center was destroyed, many Pakistanis concluded Hindus did it and many Muslims concluded Jews did it. Hatred makes fact-finding nearly impossible.

### 4. Doubt-Avoidance Tendency
**What it is:** The brain is built to remove doubt by reaching some decision — any decision. Lingering uncertainty is uncomfortable, so the mind resolves it quickly.
**Why it exists:** A prey animal cannot deliberate in front of a predator. Fast resolution beat careful resolution in the ancestral environment, even when fast was occasionally wrong.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Premature closure on complex decisions. Snap hiring calls. Snap strategy choices. Cult-like conversions triggered by stress and confusion, where the mind grabs whatever answer arrives first.
**Antidote:** Force delay before major decisions. Judges and juries are required to do this for a reason. Wear the "mask" of objectivity before concluding — it actually produces real objectivity.

### 5. Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency
**What it is:** The brain resists changing conclusions, habits, and identities once they are formed. New ideas fail not because they are difficult but because they are inconsistent with old ideas already in place.
**Why it exists:** Mental consistency is efficient — reusing existing programming beats constant updating. Social cooperation also requires predictable behavior.
**How it causes misjudgment:** First conclusions lock in. Public commitments harden into unshakeable positions. The human mind works like a human egg — once one sperm gets in, the shut-off device bars all others. "I wear the chains I forged in life."
**Antidote:** Darwin's method: give priority attention to evidence that disconfirms your favorite ideas. Ask explicitly: "Would I make this same decision today, knowing what I know now?" Watch for tough initiation ceremonies — they lock in commitment to possibly bad ideas.

### 6. Curiosity Tendency
**What it is:** Humans have intense innate curiosity, much stronger than other primates, and culture can amplify it.
**Why it exists:** Exploration and learning produced survival advantages in complex environments. The curious organism finds food, avoids danger, and updates its model of the world.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Rare — curiosity mostly counteracts the other tendencies by driving investigation. Athens produced science and math through curiosity; Rome produced almost nothing of the kind because it focused only on "practical" engineering. Cultures that cultivate curiosity gain enormous advantages.
**Antidote:** None needed — cultivate it. Make time for exploration that isn't obviously practical. Reward people for finding out they were wrong.

### 7. Kantian Fairness Tendency
**What it is:** Humans expect fairness by Kant's categorical imperative — behavior that would work if everyone followed it. When fairness is violated, reactive hostility is intense, often out of proportion to the harm.
**Why it exists:** Fairness norms enable cooperation and trade. Groups with them outcompeted groups without them.
**How it causes misjudgment:** People will punish unfairness at cost to themselves. Perceived unfairness in compensation, recognition, or process produces outsized reactions that wreck teams and relationships.
**Antidote:** Design systems that feel fair, not just systems that are efficient. When you must treat people differently, explain why transparently. Don't underestimate the cost of a small perceived unfairness — it compounds.

### 8. Envy/Jealousy Tendency
**What it is:** Deep, intense envy — especially over scarce or competitive things — that drives hatred and injury. The Ten Commandments forbid coveting your neighbor's donkey for good reason.
**Why it exists:** Competition for mates, food, and status was intense throughout evolution. Envy motivated striving and vigilance against being outcompeted.
**How it causes misjudgment:** University communities go bananas when a colleague earns more. Sibling rivalries persist into boardrooms. Irrational hatred of successful competitors distorts strategy. The world runs on jealousy, not greed, but nobody admits it — which is exactly why it does so much damage.
**Antidote:** Recognize envy as a major driver of human behavior, including your own. Don't trigger it unnecessarily in your compensation or recognition structures. Envy is the one sin you can never possibly have any fun at — refuse it.

### 9. Reciprocation Tendency
**What it is:** Humans reciprocate both favors and disfavors, often automatically and with intensity that exceeds the original act.
**Why it exists:** Reciprocal exchange was the foundation of human cooperation. Those who reciprocated built alliances; those who didn't were excluded.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Free samples work. Concessions in negotiation work. Small uninvited favors produce disproportionate obligations. Retaliation for small slights escalates into feuds that ruin careers and companies.
**Antidote:** Be aware when you're being manipulated through uninvited favors. Ben Franklin's trick: get people to do you small favors — they will like and trust you more. In negotiations, recognize the concession trap.

### 10. Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency
**What it is:** The brain links concepts, emotions, and responses because they occurred together, then lets the association guide future behavior — Napoleon and Hitler both invaded Russia because past success "there" was associated with future success "there." The Persian Messenger Syndrome: messengers bringing bad news get punished for the news.
**Why it exists:** Pattern recognition through association enabled fast learning without deep analysis.
**How it causes misjudgment:** CBS Chairman Paley was famous for hostility to bad-news carriers and lived in a cocoon of unreality. Advertising works by associating products with attractive people. Past wins get attributed to whatever was nearby, including things that won't repeat.
**Antidote:** Welcome bad news fast: "Always tell us the bad news promptly. Only the good news can wait." Consciously separate the message from the messenger. Examine past successes for accidental factors that won't recur.

### 11. Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial
**What it is:** When reality is too painful to bear, the brain distorts facts until they become bearable. Happens unconsciously and automatically.
**Why it exists:** Some protection from overwhelming pain has survival value. Complete mental breakdown serves no evolutionary purpose.
**How it causes misjudgment:** The mother who refuses to believe her son is dead. The addict who believes he's still in respectable condition. The founder who cannot see that his strategy is failing. Reality is denied because reality is unbearable.
**Antidote:** Build relationships with people who will tell you hard truths. Create external feedback mechanisms that force reality through the denial. Regularly ask: "What would I see if this weren't working?"

### 12. Excessive Self-Regard Tendency
**What it is:** People consistently misappraise themselves on the high side — their abilities, their decisions, their possessions, their family, their associates. Ninety percent of Swedish drivers judge themselves above average.
**Why it exists:** Confidence has social and competitive value. Slightly overconfident organisms may have outperformed strictly calibrated ones, on average.
**How it causes misjudgment:** The endowment effect — once you own it, it's worth more to you than you'd pay to acquire it. Cliquish hiring — people hire people like themselves, creating dysfunction chambers. Overweighting your own decisions after you've made them. Even criminals believe either they didn't do it or the circumstances justified it.
**Antidote:** Force yourself to be more objective about yourself, your family, your property, your decisions. Underweight first impressions from interviews — overweight track records. Build cultures that value honest self-criticism over ego protection.

### 13. Over-Optimism Tendency
**What it is:** Even without stress or pain, humans display excess optimism. "What a man wishes, that also will he believe" — Demosthenes, more than two thousand years ago.
**Why it exists:** Optimism enables action in uncertain situations. The pessimist who never tries never succeeds. Evolution may have favored action bias.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Lottery tickets. Founders who believe they will beat base rates that almost nobody beats. Project plans without real contingency. Market-size estimates that overstate by an order of magnitude.
**Antidote:** Learn and use basic probability math. Force base-rate analysis: "Of 100 companies like ours, how many succeeded?" Assign someone to argue the pessimistic case. Add real contingency to plans, not decoration.

### 14. Deprival-Super-Reaction Tendency
**What it is:** Loss hurts more than equivalent gain pleases. Moreover, people react almost as intensely to nearly-obtained rewards being taken away as to actually-possessed rewards being lost.
**Why it exists:** In ancestral environments, losses were often more dangerous than gains were beneficial. Losing your food store meant death; extra food meant temporary surplus.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Loss aversion. Near-miss escalation — slot machines exploit this. Sunk-cost fallacy. Bureaucratic infighting over threatened territory destroys organizations. Ideological entrenchment because changing your mind feels like losing.
**Antidote:** Don't go to open-outcry auctions. Know when to fold. Recognize territorial behavior in your organization. Frame decisions in terms of opportunity cost, not loss avoidance.

### 15. Social-Proof Tendency
**What it is:** Humans automatically think and do what they observe around them. Especially under uncertainty or stress, the crowd wins.
**Why it exists:** Following the crowd was often the right strategy in the ancestral environment. Social learning beat individual trial-and-error.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Conformity to obvious error in Asch experiments. Bystander paralysis. Fads, bubbles, bank runs. Cult conversions. Oil companies that foolishly bought mines because the first one did — followership at the highest levels of business.
**Antidote:** Learn to ignore examples from others when they are wrong. Be especially skeptical when everyone agrees. In emergencies, ignore what others are doing and analyze independently. Correctness is not determined by headcount.

### 16. Contrast-Misreaction Tendency
**What it is:** The nervous system measures contrast, not absolute values. Small changes go unnoticed; relative comparisons distort absolute judgments.
**Why it exists:** Contrast detection was computationally cheap for ancestral brains. Environmental changes were more important than absolute states.
**How it causes misjudgment:** The $1,000 dashboard seems cheap after the $65,000 car. Boiling frog — gradual decline goes unseen until too late. Marrying someone who seems wonderful compared to terrible parents, but is objectively unsatisfactory. Real-estate manipulation — show terrible houses first to make a merely bad one look good.
**Antidote:** Evaluate options in absolute terms, not just relative to what you've just seen. Watch for small-leak patterns over time. Set absolute thresholds before evaluating options. "A small leak will sink a great ship."

### 17. Stress-Influence Tendency
**What it is:** Stress changes cognition and behavior — sometimes improves performance slightly, but heavy stress causes breakdown, makes other biases more powerful, and can permanently alter mental states.
**Why it exists:** Fight-or-flight was adaptive for acute physical threats. Chronic psychological stress is a modern invention the mechanism never evolved to handle.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Decisions made in crisis tend to be worse than the same decisions made the next week. Extreme stress can flip conditioning — Pavlov's flood experiments showed dogs' learned behaviors reversing under sufficient stress. Cult brainwashers use stress deliberately.
**Antidote:** Recognize when you're making decisions under heavy stress and delay them. Build in mandatory waiting periods during crises. Protect your team from unnecessary stress that impairs judgment.

### 18. Availability-Misweighing Tendency
**What it is:** The brain overweights what is easily available — recent, vivid, or frequent information gets more weight than it deserves. An idea or a fact is not worth more merely because it is easily available to you.
**Why it exists:** Working with available information was computationally efficient. The brain can't search all memory for every decision.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Recency bias. Vivid examples outweighing statistical base rates. Neglect of harder-to-find factors that don't produce easy numbers. "When I'm not near the girl I love, I love the girl I'm near."
**Antidote:** Use checklists to ensure all factors are considered, not just available ones. Consciously underweight vivid evidence. Hire skeptical people to argue against incumbent notions. Seek out factors that don't produce easy numbers.

### 19. Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency
**What it is:** All skills attenuate with disuse. The brain prunes circuitry that isn't being used.
**Why it exists:** Maintaining unused skills costs metabolic resources. Efficiency demanded pruning.
**How it causes misjudgment:** The pilot who hasn't practiced emergency procedures fails in the emergency. The executive who hasn't made hard calls in years makes them badly when he has to. Skills crammed briefly are lost quickly; skills raised to fluency are retained longer.
**Antidote:** Practice rarely-used critical skills regularly — aircraft simulators exist for exactly this reason. Raise skills to fluency, not just passing familiarity. Paderewski: one day without practice he noticed; one week and the audience noticed.

### 20. Drug-Misinfluence Tendency
**What it is:** Chemicals — alcohol, drugs — cause severe cognitive dysfunction and moral breakdown. Reward systems built for natural stimuli get hijacked by artificial concentrations.
**Why it exists:** Evolution didn't anticipate concentrated psychoactive substances. The machinery wasn't designed to handle them.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Cognitive impairment during use. Moral breakdown as addiction progresses. Denial about the severity of the problem. Loss of everything that matters, to feed the dependency.
**Antidote:** Stay far away from any conduct likely to drift into chemical dependency. Even a small chance of such great damage should be avoided. Apply asymmetric risk analysis — the downside is catastrophic, irreversible, and common.

### 21. Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency
**What it is:** With age comes natural cognitive decay — differing among individuals but inevitable. Worse, old people often become skilled at disguising their deterioration and may not recognize their own decline.
**Why it exists:** Biology. Maintenance systems eventually fail.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Aging founders and CEOs make worse decisions while believing they're making the same quality of decisions they always did. Retained intensely-practiced old skills mask loss of ability to learn complex new skills.
**Antidote:** Continuous thinking and learning, done with joy, can delay the inevitable — not prevent it. Build succession plans that don't depend on aging leaders retaining full capacity. Create systems that provide honest feedback about performance.

### 22. Authority-Misinfluence Tendency
**What it is:** Humans are programmed to follow leaders and obey authority figures, often automatically and excessively.
**Why it exists:** Dominance hierarchies were the structure of human social life. Following leaders was usually adaptive.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Milgram experiment — ordinary people delivered what they believed were dangerous shocks because an authority figure directed them to. A pilot crashed a plane because he misinterpreted a subtle movement from a general as an instruction. A nurse put ear drops in a patient's rectum because the prescription read "ear." Bad CEOs allowed to remain in power long after they should be removed.
**Antidote:** Be careful whom you appoint to power — they'll be hard to remove. Train people to ignore foolish orders from authority. Create systems where authority can be safely questioned.

### 23. Twaddle Tendency
**What it is:** Humans, as social animals with language, are born to prattle and pour out twaddle that does much damage when serious work is attempted.
**Why it exists:** Social bonding through conversation had survival value. Unfortunately, talking doesn't require having anything useful to say.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Twaddle consumes time that could go to real work. Creates noise that obscures signal. Can sound authoritative while saying nothing. Enables people to occupy positions without contributing. The honeybee, when placed in a situation it can't encode naturally, comes back and does an incoherent dance rather than stay silent. Humans are the same.
**Antidote:** Keep prattling people far away from serious work. The principal job of administration is to keep the people who don't matter from interfering with the work of the people who do. Reward concision.

### 24. Reason-Respecting Tendency
**What it is:** Humans respond much better when given reasons — so strongly that even empty reasons increase compliance.
**Why it exists:** Reasons provide context, enable learning, and allow judgment about applicability. Following reasoned instructions was more adaptive than following arbitrary commands.
**How it causes misjudgment:** People let you cut in line if you say "I need to make some copies" — even though that's not a reason. Claptrap reasoning with the form of reason but no substance. The tendency is exploited by manipulators who know the shape of a reason is enough.
**Antidote:** Always explain why, not just what — the Carl Braun rule at his engineering firm: say who, what, where, when, and why, or you get fired. Be suspicious of reasons that sound good but are actually empty. Why is the Rosetta Stone of mental life.

### 25. Lollapalooza Tendency
**What it is:** Extreme consequences from confluences of psychological tendencies acting in favor of a particular outcome. The most important item on the list, because single biases cause ordinary errors but multiple biases combining cause catastrophes.
**Why it exists:** It's not a separate tendency so much as a structural feature of how all the other tendencies interact. Biases rarely act alone.
**How it causes misjudgment:** Milgram experiment — at least six tendencies acting together. Cult conversions — social proof, authority, commitment, stress, liking. Acquisition fever — social proof, consistency, deprival super-reaction, incentive, over-optimism. Tupperware parties. Corporate frauds like Enron. Every famous disaster in business was a lollapalooza someone failed to count in time.
**Antidote:** Use the list as a literal checklist. When you find four or more tendencies pointing the same way, stop. Mandatory delay, devil's advocate, structural change to the decision process. See `lollapalooza-effect.md` for the full treatment.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the list against every significant decision.** Not metaphorically. Literally — pull up the catalog and go through it. Which of the twenty-five are operating on me right now? Which on the people advising me? Which on the team? Which are combining?

2. **Pay special attention to Reward and Punishment Super-Response and Incentive-Caused Bias first.** These are the biggest single source of misjudgment in business. Audit the incentive structures before you touch anything else.

3. **Apply the list to yourself before you apply it to others.** You have all twenty-five. The people best at this framework are the ones who catch themselves first, not the ones who use it to diagnose their opponents.

4. **When three or more tendencies align on the same side of a decision, treat it as the condition it is — an emergency.** Mandatory delay. Change the room. Bring in a devil's advocate. The mind under multi-bias conditions is not the mind you want to use.

5. **Design systems that work through the tendencies, not against them.** You cannot exhort people out of biases. You can design systems — cash registers, clawbacks, mandatory delays, independent reviewers — that make the biases irrelevant. The engineering approach, not the moral approach.

## Closing: The Lollapalooza Warning

The single most important thing to take from this catalog is that the tendencies combine. One tendency is manageable. You are a human being, you will always have some level of each of these running, and you can mostly function around them. But when several point the same way — four, five, six of them pulling in the same direction on a single decision — you are no longer looking at normal human judgment operating with its usual biases. You are looking at a cascade, and the cascade produces the extreme outcomes that wreck companies and careers and lives.

Every famous business disaster was a lollapalooza. Acquisition fever. IPO fever. Culture-of-personality collapses. Fraud cases like Enron. Market manias. Cult-like product obsessions that burn companies to the ground. In every case, you can go back and name four or more of these tendencies combining in the same direction, and you can name the moment when someone — anyone — could have stopped and counted and called it. And they didn't. Usually because they didn't have the catalog. Sometimes because they had it and failed to use it, which is worse.

The discipline is not theoretical. Mandatory delay when the count hits four. Devil's advocate, paid to kill the deal. Structural change to the room — add people whose incentives differ, remove people whose careers advance only if the decision goes one way. These are not suggestions. They are the only interventions that actually work, because the biases operate below awareness and cannot be defeated by awareness alone. You need the structure.

The right culture is one in which no one can make a decision free of the discipline of competent people who know something about it — and none of them is paid to reach the particular conclusion. Build that, and you have disarmed most of what this catalog predicts. Fail to build it, and the catalog predicts your future.

Tendency is not always destiny. Knowing the tendencies and their antidotes can often help prevent trouble that would otherwise occur. That is the whole project, and it is enough.
