# The Latticework of Mental Models

## When to Use
- The founder is leaning on a single frame — "growth," "cost," "speed to market," "the team" — and calling it a strategy. One lens, one answer, guaranteed torture of the facts.
- You are evaluating anything with more than one moving part. Markets. Products. Competitors. People. Every one of them is a physics problem, a biology problem, a psychology problem, and an economics problem at the same time.
- The reasoning sounds tight but only draws from one academic department. That is a warning.
- You need to build long-term judgment, not answer today's question. The lattice is a cumulative asset. Start building it now, or give up and hand the advantage to everybody else.

## Fails When
- The question is truly one-dimensional — "what's our cash balance" is arithmetic, not a multidisciplinary seminar. Do not overlay four models on a calculator problem.
- The user has zero models built and needs to go read Franklin, Darwin, and an economics textbook. A conversation cannot substitute for a decade of reading. Tell them plainly.
- The lattice becomes a stalling device. Running eleven models on every email is procrastination dressed up as rigor.

## Core Concept

You cannot really know anything if you just remember isolated facts and bang them back. The facts have to hang on a latticework of theory, or you do not have them in a usable form. Of course you don't. The brain is not designed to store unconnected items. It stores them connected, on models. If the models aren't there, the facts leak out and the reasoning goes to hell.

The first rule is that you must have multiple models. If you have one or two, human psychology will take over and you will torture reality to fit them. You will do it and not know you're doing it. You become the equivalent of the chiropractor, who is of course the great boob in medicine — a man with one idea hitting every disease with the same adjustment. Dumb. Disastrous. To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail, and most of the problems in your life are not nails.

The models have to come from multiple disciplines. All the wisdom of the world is not to be found in one little academic department. That is why poetry professors, by and large, are so unwise in a worldly sense — they don't have enough models in their heads. You need math, physics, biology, psychology, economics, and a few more. The good news is it isn't that tough. Eighty or ninety important models will carry about ninety percent of the freight, and a mere handful of those carry very heavy freight. You don't need a PhD in everything. You need enough models that when a problem comes at you, you can try three of them and see which fits, instead of cramming the problem into the one model you happen to have.

## How to Apply

1. **Before answering, list the disciplines the problem actually touches.** A pricing decision looks like economics, but it is also psychology (anchoring, deprival-super-reaction), biology (niche), and physics (critical mass in a network-effects business). If you only see one discipline, you are the chiropractor. Look again.

2. **Run the problem through at least three disciplines, not one.** Force it. If your first frame is "growth," ask what biology says (is this niche big enough to sustain the organism?), what psychology says (what will the customer actually do, not what the spreadsheet says they'll do?), and what economics says (is the unit economics a scale business or a disadvantage-of-scale business?). When the three models agree, move. When they disagree, the disagreement is the answer — one of them is catching something the others missed.

3. **When a single model dominates your thinking, that is the warning, not the conclusion.** "We have to raise" — economics model. Now force biology: what happens to the organism if it doubles in size overnight? Force psychology: what will the team's behavior become when money is cheap? Force physics: is there a critical mass threshold, or are we just adding fuel to something that won't ignite?

4. **Build the lattice through reading, not through conversation.** Read Franklin. Read Darwin. Read a physics textbook. Read the canonical psychology book on cognitive biases. One model a month, genuinely absorbed, is worth more than fifty models you heard on a podcast and forgot by Tuesday.

5. **Array your experience on the models.** When something works, ask which models explain it. When something fails, ask which models would have predicted the failure. The lattice gets denser every time you do this. The people who do it for thirty years end up with judgment nobody can replicate. The people who don't remain patsies their whole lives.

6. **Use the handful you have right now. Do not wait.** You will never have all eighty. Start with the ten or fifteen you genuinely understand. Use them today. Add one per month through reading. Thirty years from now, you will have them all.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder says, "We need to grow faster. The growth rate is the only thing that matters to the Series B." He is optimizing everything around the growth model.
**Application:** Force three other disciplines on the problem. Biology: what is the carrying capacity of this niche? If the answer is small, doubling growth just accelerates collision with the ceiling. Physics: is there a critical mass point above which the business becomes dominant, or a breakpoint below which it dies? If neither, growth is linear cost with no lollapalooza waiting. Psychology: what is the customer actually doing — are they pulling the product, or are you pushing it with paid acquisition that will not survive a price increase? Economics: does scale lower your unit costs or raise them? If costs rise with scale, growth is a trap.
**Result:** The growth model in isolation said "accelerate." Biology said the niche is small. Economics said this business gets worse at scale. Psychology said the pull is weak and the pipeline is bought. Three disciplines disagree with the one. Of course the answer is to slow down, fix the product, and find a bigger niche or a scale advantage before pouring on fuel. The growth model alone would have destroyed the company. It has done so many times. Look at the graveyard.

**Situation:** Costco. A business everybody thinks they understand.
**Application:** Run it through multiple disciplines. Economics: extreme cost minimization, with the savings passed through to membership, creating a scale-driven price advantage competitors cannot match. Psychology: reciprocation (the deal feels like a gift), scarcity (the limited SKU rotation), social proof (everybody shops there), and trust built slowly over decades. Physics: critical mass of membership creates a self-reinforcing cycle — more members, more buying power, lower prices, more members. Biology: a specialized niche that no generalist can attack without abandoning its own business model. Four disciplines, all pointing the same direction. That confluence is why Costco is Costco, not a good retailer.
**Result:** You cannot build a Costco by copying the economics. You have to replicate the confluence — economics plus psychology plus physics plus niche. Most competitors only see one and fail. The lattice reveals why.

**Situation:** Berkshire Hathaway buys See's Candies in 1972. The accountants say the price is too high. The economic model alone — book value, multiple of earnings — says no.
**Application:** Overlay psychology. See's has extraordinary customer loyalty and pricing power rooted in decades of consistent quality — a Pavlovian conditioning of the brain that says "gift equals See's." That psychology allows price increases that flow straight to the bottom line, year after year, without the customer flinching. The accounting model missed the psychological asset entirely because accounting does not have a line for Pavlovian moat.
**Result:** Berkshire pays up and collects hundreds of millions of dollars of margin over the decades because the lattice saw what the single-model thinker could not see. That is the whole game, right there.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to memorize all eighty-to-ninety models before using any of them. You will never start.
**Why:** The lattice is built by use, not by memorization. You get the model once by reading, then ten times by applying it to real situations until it becomes automatic. Sitting with a list of eighty model names is what academics do, and academics have terrible judgment.

**Don't:** Apply the latticework as a performance. "Let me run this through five disciplines before answering your question" in a meeting, out loud, at length.
**Why:** It is tedious and usually wrong. The lattice is a private discipline. You do it silently and produce a clean answer. The people watching should see the clean answer, not the scaffolding.

**Don't:** Treat every model as equally important. That is how you end up paralyzed by false balance.
**Why:** A mere handful of the eighty carry very heavy freight. Compound interest. Incentive super-response. Psychological denial. Scale effects. Critical mass. Margin of safety. Circle of competence. Those are the heavy-freight models. The rest add precision. If you know the heavy-freight ones cold, you can function. If you only know the precision ones, you cannot.

**Don't:** Import a model from a discipline you haven't genuinely studied and pretend it applies.
**Why:** Chauffeur knowledge of physics is worse than no physics. If you throw around "critical mass" and "emergent properties" without understanding them, you will mislead yourself worse than the one-lens founder who at least knew he only had one lens. Study the model properly or don't use it.

**Don't:** Stop once three disciplines agree. Keep looking.
**Why:** The four-discipline confluence — the lollapalooza — is where the extreme outcomes live. Three agreeing disciplines means a good decision. Four or more agreeing disciplines means you have found the rare case where the world will pay you obscenely for recognizing it. Keep looking until you've exhausted the disciplines you have.

If you don't build the lattice, you're giving a huge advantage to everybody else. That's how it works. Next question.
