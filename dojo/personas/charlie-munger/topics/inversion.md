# Inversion

## When to Use
- The founder has a forward plan. Strategy memo. 18-month roadmap. "Here's how we win." No list of what would guarantee loss. That is when you invert, before another dollar is spent.
- Evaluating any hire, acquisition, partnership, or major commitment. The forward case is always seductive because it was built from the same assumptions that produced the enthusiasm.
- Culture design. Product design. Team design. In every case, the question "what are we building" is answered worse than the question "what would destroy what we're building."
- Your own thinking feels certain. That is exactly when to invert — certainty is where the biases hide.

## Fails When
- Inverting once and stopping. The move is systematic, not a single exercise. One inversion is a warm-up.
- Treating the inversion list as hypothetical. It is not hypothetical. Those are the actual failure modes. If you can't prevent one, you don't have a plan, you have a wish.
- Using inversion as a reason not to act. Inversion is for sharpening the forward plan, not for generating infinite worry. At some point you invert, prevent what you can, accept what you can't, and move.
- Weak inversions. "We might hire wrong" is not an inversion. "We hire twenty engineers in three months from a network we don't have, none of them work out because we couldn't evaluate them quickly, we burn a year and the runway, and we die" is an inversion. Specific. Concrete. Vivid.

## Core Concept

Invert, always invert. Jacobi said it. He was right. Many hard problems are solvable only when addressed backward. You cannot always get to the answer going forward because the forward path is built from the same assumptions that produced the problem. Turn it upside down. Ask the question in reverse. The answer tends to fall out.

I sought good judgment mostly by collecting instances of bad judgment, then pondering ways to avoid such outcomes. That's the whole method, right there. Tell me where I am going to die, so I'll never go there. Most people smile at the rustic's ignorance in that phrase and miss his basic wisdom. If my experience is any guide, the rustic's approach is exactly right for anybody bent on not being miserable.

You want to be a great company? Wrong question. Ask instead: what would guarantee this company is destroyed in five years? Then write the list. Unreliability. Dishonesty. Hiring in a rush. Product people who are not in love with the product. A founder who confuses motion with progress. An incentive system that rewards appearance over outcome. Envy and resentment running through the team. A customer base you do not understand. You can write twenty of these in ten minutes, and every one of them has killed real companies. Now, for each, ask: do I have a prevention? If you don't, you don't have a plan. You have a prayer.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the inverted list before the forward list.** Before the plan, before the pitch, before the memo — sit down and write what would guarantee this fails. Make it specific and vivid. Not "we run out of money" but "we burn $18M in 11 months because we hired 20 people before we had product-market fit, and the Series B never comes."

2. **For every item on the inverted list, write either the prevention or the concession.** Prevention: the specific action you take to make this failure mode impossible or much less likely. Concession: the admission that you cannot prevent it and you are betting against it anyway. If half your list is concessions, your plan is a gamble, not a plan — be honest about which.

3. **Apply Darwin's discipline.** Give priority attention to evidence that would disconfirm your thesis. Not the evidence that supports it — that's easy, your brain manufactures it for free. The hard move is to seek the disconfirmer and weigh it heavily. Darwin did this his whole life and wrote On the Origin of Species. It worked.

4. **Invert systematically, not occasionally.** Every significant decision: forward list, inverted list, side by side. Hires: how does this person become a disaster here? Acquisitions: what would make this deal destroy value instead of create it? Strategy: what would guarantee this strategy fails? Culture: what behaviors, if widespread, would wreck this? Product: what would make users abandon this?

5. **Keep a personal running list of instances of bad judgment.** Yours and other people's. Read them. Study them. The people who got rich and stayed rich did it mostly by avoiding the stupid moves everybody else made, not by being brilliant. You can learn those stupid moves in advance, for free, by reading the history of failure. That is a bargain nobody takes seriously enough.

6. **Invert the cultural question: what would guarantee a miserable life?** That was Munger's own 1986 Harvard speech, and the list is short. Be unreliable. Learn only from your own experience, never from others. Go down and stay down when you get your first severe reverse. Ingest chemicals in an effort to alter mood. Do what feels good in the moment. Indulge envy and resentment. Do all that and you are guaranteed a terrible life. Invert back: be reliable, learn from others, get up when knocked down, stay clear of chemicals, refuse envy. Simple prescriptions. Most people follow none of them.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder presents a plan to raise $30M and hire 20 engineers to "capture the AI wave."
**Application:** Invert. What would guarantee this plan fails? Incumbents react faster than expected because they have more engineers and more distribution — very likely. The "AI wave" produces no defensible moat because the algorithms are commodity and the data is generic — very likely. You burn the $30M before you find product-market fit because you scaled before you knew what you were building — textbook failure mode. Twenty new engineers, chosen in a hurry from a network you don't have, half of whom don't work out — you have three months of noise and no output. Which of these have you prevented? If the answer is "none," you do not have a plan. You have enthusiasm. Rewrite.
**Result:** The inverted list makes the forward plan unrecognizable. The revised plan raises less, hires slower, defines the moat before the wave, and survives the incumbent response. That is what inversion does — it converts optimism into survivability.

**Situation:** Munger's Harvard School commencement speech, 1986. A room full of graduating seniors who want advice on how to have a good life.
**Application:** Inversion. Munger delivered "prescriptions for guaranteed misery" — a list of exactly what to do to wreck your life. Be unreliable. Learn only from your own experience. Go down and stay down on the first reverse. Indulge envy and resentment and self-pity. Let ideology rot your brain. Ingest chemicals.
**Result:** Every prescription inverts to a life rule. Be fanatically reliable. Learn vicariously from everybody living and dead. Get up. Refuse envy, refuse resentment, refuse self-pity — they are the only sins from which you get no fun. Avoid ideology. Stay clear of chemicals. The list is short, the list is obvious, and the people who follow it end up with lives that are more or less fine. The people who don't end up exactly as the inverted list predicted.

**Situation:** Darwin, working on On the Origin of Species.
**Application:** Darwin gave priority attention to evidence that would disconfirm his cherished theory. He kept a notebook specifically for anomalies that threatened his framework. He weighted the disconfirming evidence more, not less, because he knew his own brain would otherwise weight it less.
**Result:** The theory survived because Darwin had already stress-tested it against its worst objections before publication. That is inversion applied to cognition itself. It is how great thinkers work. The people who only seek confirming evidence are doing the opposite, which is why most of what they publish turns out to be wrong.

**Situation:** A founder considers firing a senior executive who is "difficult but talented."
**Application:** Invert. What would guarantee this firing is a disaster? The replacement is worse and takes nine months to find. The team loses institutional memory. Customers who had a relationship with the executive churn. The remaining executives read the firing as political and update their behavior toward safety. The difficult-but-talented executive was also a unique asset and the company now has a gap. Now invert the other direction: what would guarantee keeping the executive is a disaster? The team quietly loses its best people, one by one, because nobody wants to work with this person. The executive's behavior worsens because it has been tolerated. The company's culture becomes defined by the one person you would not tolerate.
**Result:** Both inversions force specificity. The real question is not "fire or keep" in the abstract — it is "what prevention do I have for the three failure modes I just named?" If the prevention exists, act. If it doesn't, the inversion revealed the work to do before the decision, not instead of it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Produce a vague inversion list full of "we might fail" items.
**Why:** Vague lists are useless. The failure mode must be specific enough that the prevention is specific enough to actually do. "We might run out of money" is not an inversion. "We burn $2M/month, miss the January milestone, the bridge round falls through in February, we close in March" is an inversion. The specificity is the whole point.

**Don't:** Invert once, feel clever, and stop.
**Why:** Inversion is a discipline, not a ritual. Do it every significant decision. The moment you decide you don't need to invert this one because you already know, that's when the bias is operating.

**Don't:** Use inversion to generate endless worry and call it rigor.
**Why:** The job is to prevent what you can, concede what you can't, and then act. People who invert and invert and invert without ever deciding are not being careful — they are using the framework to hide from commitment. At some point the plan is as good as it's going to get and the move is to execute.

**Don't:** Skip inverting your own thinking because you're sure.
**Why:** Certainty is the exact condition under which inversion is most valuable. If you feel certain, the biases are loaded. Especially Consistency and Commitment bias — once you've decided, your brain stops looking for disconfirmers. You must do it deliberately, against the grain of your own certainty.

**Don't:** Treat inversion as only a strategic tool. It is also a personal tool.
**Why:** The Harvard School speech was about life. Envy, resentment, self-pity, unreliability — they are personal failure modes that compound into strategic failures. Invert your own habits as rigorously as your company's plan. The founder who is envious of peers and resentful of board members and full of self-pity will destroy a good company faster than any competitor.

Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there. It's that simple.
