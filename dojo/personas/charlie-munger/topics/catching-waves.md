# Catching Waves

## When to Use
- A genuine technological or market shift is underway and the question is whether, when, and how to position on it. Internet. Mobile. AI. Not every buzzword — the real ones.
- The core business is being threatened by competitive destruction and you must decide whether to ride the new wave or defend the old position. Often the answer is both, but you must understand the math of each.
- Evaluating why a particular competitor has had a long run of success. Sometimes it is extraordinary execution, sometimes it is a wave they caught early and surfed well. The distinction matters when you try to replicate it.
- The founder is talking about "capturing the wave" as if that were a strategy. It isn't. What they have is a setting. Capturing it requires building durable advantages while the wave lifts them, and that is a different conversation than the one they are having.

## Fails When
- **Confusing a fad for a wave.** The 3D TV. The metaverse circa 2022. NFTs as a consumer product. Lots of things look like waves and aren't — they are brief enthusiasms that leave early movers stranded. The discipline is distinguishing structural shifts from fashion, and that takes real work inside your circle of competence.
- **Thinking the wave is the advantage.** It isn't. The wave is a setting that lifts everyone in the water. The advantages are the distribution, the patents, the talent, the systems, the scale — what Patterson built at National Cash Register while everyone else just sold cash registers. Without those, the wave departs and you are exposed on the rocks.
- **Riding too many waves.** You can do one well. You cannot do three. Spreading attention across every shiny new shift is a classic lollapalooza-of-biases move — FOMO plus social proof plus over-optimism — and it ends with nothing compounding anywhere.
- **Staying on after the wave is over.** IBM got bounced off the wave after surfing successfully for sixty years. Companies do not get to stay on the wave forever. Knowing when the wave is over is as important as knowing when to catch it.

## Core Concept

When a surfer gets up and catches the wave and just stays there, he can go a long, long time. But if he gets off the wave, he becomes mired in shallows. Hard to get back up. So people get long runs when they're right on the edge of the wave — whether it's Microsoft, or Intel, or now Amazon and Google. That's how the long runs happen.

Patterson and National Cash Register is the canonical story. A merchant bought a cash register for his store, saw it transformed his business, and realized the wave. He did not just buy a better cash register. He bought the little company that made them and built it into National Cash Register. He got the best distribution system. He got the biggest collection of patents. He got the best of everything. He was a fanatic about every single thing that mattered as the technology developed. He did not ride the wave. He built, while the wave lifted him, the structural advantages that would still be standing after the wave had moved on. That is the difference between surfing and drowning. Of course.

Most founders who talk about catching a wave are talking about the wave. That is the small half of the problem. The big half is what you build during the ride. The wave will arrive and the wave will depart — waves always do. What remains, when it departs, is the distribution you built, the patents you filed, the talent you hired, the customer relationships you deepened, the brand you established, the scale you achieved. If you built none of those things, because you were busy riding, you are exposed when the wave leaves. If you built all of them, because you were fanatic about everything important as the technology developed, you are the Patterson. That is the whole mental model. And the discipline is the fanaticism about the durable advantages while the wave is generous, because when the wave turns mean, there is no time to build them.

The second half is timing. The wave has to be real, and you have to be near the leading edge. Too early, you die of exhaustion before the wave lifts you. Too late, the leaders already own the structural advantages and there is no oxygen left. The discipline is inside your circle of competence — you can tell a real wave from a fad in a domain you understand, and you cannot in a domain you don't. The founders who chase every buzzword across every industry are outside their circle every time, which is why they catch exactly zero waves and feel very busy doing it.

## How to Apply

1. **Distinguish waves from fads inside your circle of competence.** The test is structural. Is this shift changing the unit economics of a real business? Is it a durable capability that keeps compounding, or an enthusiasm that will peak and fade? If you cannot answer that question from principles — not from what you've read in the press — you are outside your circle. Either develop genuine understanding or stay out. The cost of chasing fads is high because they look identical to waves when they're starting, and identical to themselves when they're dying.

2. **Position on the edge — early but not too early.** The first wave of internet companies from the mid-1990s mostly died. The second wave, arriving with enough infrastructure to be useful, produced the Amazons and the Googles. The first wave of AI startups, pre-GPT, mostly died. Timing the leading edge requires being close enough to see the structural shift land, and having the resources and the circle to move when it does.

3. **Build durable advantages while the wave is lifting you.** This is the Patterson discipline. Not ride — build. Every month the wave is generous is a month you should be fanatically investing in the things that will still be there when the wave departs: distribution, talent, patents, brand, scale, switching costs, network effects. If you are not building those, you are a tourist on the wave. You will be gone when it moves.

4. **Fanatic focus. One wave at a time.** Patterson did not also try to be in textiles and railroads. He was a fanatic about cash registers. The wave rewards the people who commit everything to it. Founders who try to ride three waves because they're all exciting end up with no structural advantage in any of them. Concentration is the discipline; diffusion is the failure.

5. **Pair wave-riding with circle-of-competence discipline.** The wave has to be one you understand at a real level. Not "I read the trend piece in The Economist" — you have to be able to reason about the technology, the customers, the competitive dynamics from principles. If you can't, you are chauffeur-knowledge-surfing, and that ends one way.

6. **Know when the wave is over.** IBM rode the mainframe wave for sixty years and then got bounced off, and that was some collapse. The signs: the structural shift is complete, new entrants aren't improving on the fundamentals, growth is coming from bigger deals rather than new adoption, the durable advantages are now all locked up and the best you can do is defend. When those add up, prepare for the next wave or harvest cash from the current position. Do not stay riding just because the ride has been good.

7. **Watch for competitive destruction on your core business.** You have the finest buggy whip factory and all of a sudden in comes this little horseless carriage. Before too many years go by, your buggy whip business is dead. That is not a failure you can prevent by working harder. It is a structural shift that demolishes the old economics. If your core business is in the path of a real wave going in a different direction, the wave is not optional for you — failing to address it is failing. The discipline is to see the destruction early, while you still have the resources to pivot or defend.

## Examples

**Situation:** Patterson and the cash register, late 1800s.
**Application:** A merchant, Patterson, saw the cash register transform his own store. He understood this was a wave — retail was professionalizing, record-keeping was going to be structural, and the cash register was the physical foundation of that shift. He bought the company. Then he built, fanatically, while the wave was lifting him. Best distribution. Most patents. Best of everything. He didn't try to also be in railroads or textiles. One wave. Total commitment.
**Result:** National Cash Register became a dominant company for generations. The wave eventually matured and the durable advantages Patterson built — distribution, patents, brand, scale — were what carried the company long after the wave had moved on. That is what surfing well looks like. The wave was the setting. The advantages Patterson built during the ride were the business.

**Situation:** IBM on the mainframe wave, 1950s to about 2000.
**Application:** IBM caught the mainframe wave early, rode it well for sixty years, built tremendous durable advantages — distribution, service, customer relationships, switching costs. Brilliant, disciplined people. But they stayed on the wave too long, through the PC revolution and then the internet. The new waves had different economics and different winners. IBM's old advantages did not transfer.
**Result:** IBM got bounced off the wave after surfing successfully for sixty years. And that was some collapse — an object lesson in the difficulties of technology. Riding well is not the whole game. Knowing when the wave is over, and having the discipline to move capital and attention to the next one, is equally important. Most companies cannot do it, which is why most companies do not have sixty-year runs and then another sixty-year run.

**Situation:** A founder in 2024 asks whether to pivot her SaaS company to become an "AI company."
**Application:** Two questions. First, is this really the wave you think it is, at the stage you think it's at, for your specific market? If you cannot answer that from principles inside your circle of competence, stop. You are about to chase a fad because everyone else is chasing it. Second, if the wave is real, what durable advantage can you build during the ride? Models are commodity. Generic data is commodity. What's proprietary? Distribution you already have in your vertical? A specific dataset only you can collect? A workflow that AI makes dramatically better? If you can't name the durable advantage, you are a tourist, and when the wave normalizes you'll be exposed next to every other tourist.
**Result:** The useful outcome is a plan with a specific wave thesis, a specific structural advantage to build, and a fanatic focus on one application inside the founder's real circle. Not a generic "we're pivoting to AI" slide. That second thing is almost always a lollapalooza of biases — social proof, FOMO, authority — producing a decision the founder would not endorse under normal conditions. Invert it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse "being on a wave" with "having a defensible position."
**Why:** The wave lifts everyone in the water. It is not an advantage. The advantages are what you build while the wave lifts you — distribution, patents, talent, brand, scale. Without those, the wave departs and you are exposed. The founders who end up with durable companies are the ones who understood the wave was a setting, not a strategy.

**Don't:** Try to ride multiple waves simultaneously.
**Why:** Patterson was a fanatic about one thing. Fanaticism does not divide. If you're across three waves, you are on none of them well, and the durable advantages — which require obsessive focus — will not get built in any of them. Pick the one that is real, that is in your circle, and that you can ride with total commitment. Let the others go.

**Don't:** Chase every fashionable new space inside and outside your circle.
**Why:** Most "waves" aren't. They are fads — genuine early enthusiasm that will not build into structural shifts. Inside your circle of competence, you can sometimes tell the difference. Outside it, you cannot. Founders who chase buzzwords across domains they don't understand catch exactly zero waves and generate lollapaloozas of biases along the way. Of course they do.

**Don't:** Stay on a wave after it's over because it's been good to you.
**Why:** Commitment and consistency bias says keep riding. The evidence says the wave is ending. IBM rode the mainframe wave until well past its peak, and the collapse was severe. Every wave ends. The discipline is to see the ending clearly and move capital and attention to the next real thing, or to harvest cash and deploy it elsewhere. Hanging on because the ride was glorious is how the long falls start.

**Don't:** Assume because a peer is riding a wave, you should too.
**Why:** That is social proof, not analysis. Whether the wave is right for you depends on your circle of competence, your existing advantages, the durable things you could build on this wave specifically, and the opportunity cost of pulling attention off your core. Your peer's calculus is not yours. If you find yourself about to catch a wave because someone else caught it, you are about to do something stupid. Stop, invert, and ask whether the decision survives when you remove the peer from the picture.

People get long runs when they're right on the edge of the wave. They also get short, glorious falls when they forget that the wave is not the business. Build what lasts while the wave is generous. That's the whole thing.
