# The Three Hypothetical Questions

## When to Use
- The final gut-check before extending an offer, after the formal process is complete.
- Coaching a manager who hires too readily — using the questions to slow them down.
- Diagnosing why your last few hires haven't worked despite passing structured criteria — usually one of the three questions would have caught it.
- Senior or culturally-pivotal hires (manager, head chef, lead engineer, partner-track) where the formal process is necessary but not sufficient.

## Fails When
- **You don't have real reference points to anchor the questions.** The questions need a *specific person you know* who is your judge of character, a *specific rival* whose hire would sting, a *specific discerning customer* whose visit would feel high-stakes. Without those, the test is abstract and useless.
- **You use them to rubber-stamp a decision you've already made.** If you ask the questions to confirm rather than to test, you'll feel positive answers because you want to. The discipline is to ask honestly and abide by what surfaces.
- **You skip the structured process and rely only on the gut test.** The three questions are the *final* filter, not a substitute for trail shifts, references, and rubric-based evaluation. They catch what the structured process misses, not the other way around.

## Core Concept

A great hiring instinct is not random — it is the manager's accumulated pattern recognition firing fast. The three hypothetical questions are a way to *slow that instinct down enough to listen to it*, so you can articulate what your gut is telling you before you act on a polite, surface-level rationalisation.

> "How do I make the subjective objective, and the implicit explicit? One effective way to articulate my gut feelings to others doing the hiring is to teach them how to listen to their own gut feelings."

The three questions, each anchored to a specific named person you know:

**Question 1 — The judge-of-character dinner.** Imagine you invite the candidate to dinner at your home along with one person you trust to be a near-perfect judge of character (Danny's example: his wife Audrey). The three of you eat for two hours. The candidate leaves. As the door closes, what is the *first thing* your character-judge says? *"Hire that person immediately"* or *"What the hell are you thinking?"* For real character judges, there is no grey.

**Question 2 — The rival-company offer.** Imagine you make the offer. The candidate calls back the next day: *"Thanks, but I just got an offer from your fiercest rival, and I'm taking it."* What is your immediate, gut reaction? *"Damn, we lost a great one"* or *"Honestly, that's a relief"*?

> "Sometimes I'll go too far down the road in a hiring situation with someone who isn't quite right for our team. I am still amazed at how often I have felt enormous relief when someone we've actively pursued ends up taking another job."

**Question 3 — The most-discerning-customer visit.** Imagine the most opinionated, weighty-voiced person in your category — a critic, an industry tastemaker, your harshest-loving regular customer — drops in unannounced. There is exactly one table left, and this candidate, just hired, will be their only point of contact. Your reaction: *"Great!"* or *"Oh no"*?

If all three answers are unambiguously positive, hire. If even one is hesitant, fold.

These work because they reveal the *configuration* of feeling, not the surface verdict. The structured process tells you whether the candidate *can* do the job. The three hypotheticals tell you what your accumulated judgment is saying about whether they *should* — using vivid concrete situations to bypass the polite-rationalisation reflex that wrecks late-stage hiring decisions.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick your three named anchors before any candidate enters the room.** Write them down: *who is my Audrey? who is my fiercest rival? who is my most discerning customer/critic?* These are the constants. The hypotheticals don't work with abstract rivals or generic customers.

2. **Ask the questions privately, in writing, *before* the offer-decision meeting.** Each interviewer answers all three in writing (one sentence each, gut answer, no editing). Then the meeting opens with people reading their answers aloud. This prevents social-proof drift in the room — everyone commits before discussing.

3. **Treat any *no* or hesitation as a fold.** All three positive → hire. Any single hesitation → don't. The discipline is in the *and*, not the *or*. Most hiring mistakes go through with a *"I'm a little uneasy about X but everything else is fine."* That uneasy is the answer.

4. **Notice your own bias toward making people comfortable.** Danny's blind spot — the natural inclination to put candidates at ease — is many founders' blind spot. *"It's not my job to soothe prospective employees. It's my job to assess whether they'll be a good fit for our team."* The three hypotheticals counteract this by forcing you to imagine *post-hire* situations, where soothing isn't an option.

5. **Run the questions on existing hires too, periodically.** Ask: *if I were hiring this person today, would all three answers come back positive?* The answer is illuminating, both about the team and about how the bar has (or hasn't) shifted.

6. **Coach managers to use the questions.** They generalise. Any senior decision that depends on judgment — a hire, a partnership, a major contract, an investor — can be filtered through the three vivid hypotheticals adapted to that domain.

7. **Pair with the *whelmer* test** (see `whelmers.md`). The three questions catch the gut-level *should we?* The whelmer test catches the structural *can they become top-three?* Both gates, every time.

## Examples

**Situation: Senior maître d' role, after the formal process. Five rounds of interviews, two trail shifts, references all glowing. The hiring manager is ready to extend. Danny is the final yes.**

**Application:** Danny runs the three questions privately:

1. *Imagine Audrey and I host this person to dinner. Door closes. What does Audrey say?* — He pauses. He realises he is not certain. There's a shrug-shape in the imagined answer.
2. *Candidate calls tomorrow saying our rival made an offer.* — He notices the small flicker of relief.
3. *A senior critic walks in, gets seated by this person.* — There is a tightening in his stomach, not an opening.

Three hesitations. Even though the structured process is clean.

**Result:** Pass. The team protests — the candidate is well-qualified. Danny holds. Three months later the candidate is hired by another restaurant; word filters back about issues that match the original gut signal.

---

**Situation: A line cook hire. Trails went well. Technical chops solid. Danny notices his own desire to "make the candidate feel comfortable" is interfering with assessment.**

> "I'm aware that one of my blind spots when hiring is my natural inclination to make other people feel comfortable. This impulse is so powerful that I tend to have a tough time turning it off when it's inappropriate — for example, in a job interview."

**Application:** He runs the three questions deliberately to override the soothing instinct. The questions force him into post-hire situations where *make-them-comfortable* is irrelevant. The judge-of-character dinner doesn't care if he was nice in the interview. The rival-offer reaction doesn't care. The discerning-critic walk-in doesn't care.

**Result:** The questions reveal a clear fold. Without them, he'd have hired and regretted it.

---

**Situation: A manager position. The leadership team unanimously wants to extend. Three of the four interviewers say *"yes, I think this is the one."* The fourth says *"I'm a little uneasy about how they handled the question about handling conflict."***

**Application:** Run the three questions in writing. The first two come back positive across the team. The third (discerning-customer) is mixed — three positives, one hesitation. The decision rule: any single hesitation, fold. Even though three of four say go.

**Result:** Pass. The cost is a longer search. The benefit is avoiding a bad senior hire. (Senior hire mistakes are 5–10x more expensive than line hire mistakes.)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the questions to overrule a clean structured process when you just *want* to hire.
*Why:* The questions are a *fold gate* — they are designed to catch wrong hires, not to manufacture excitement for marginal ones. If the structured process is positive and the three questions are positive, hire. If structured is positive and questions hesitate, don't. Don't run the questions hoping to talk yourself into someone.

**Don't:** Substitute generic anchors ("a typical critic", "a tough customer").
*Why:* The questions work because the anchors are vivid and specific — *your* Audrey, *your* rival, *your* most opinionated regular. Generic anchors produce generic gut signals which produce useless answers.

**Don't:** Run the questions out loud first in a group meeting.
*Why:* Social proof contaminates gut answers in seconds. The senior person speaks; the rest align. Always commit individually in writing first, then discuss.

**Don't:** Treat the questions as biases to overcome.
*Why:* They are not "irrational gut" to be corrected by structured criteria. They are accumulated pattern recognition that the structured process can't fully capture. Both filters; both real; both load-bearing.

**Don't:** Use the questions for low-stakes hires where the cost of error is small.
*Why:* The questions take effort. For a junior, short-tenure, easily-replaceable role, the structured process plus a trail is enough. Reserve the three questions for hires that will durably shape the team — managers, senior individual contributors, partners, lead chefs, key engineers.
