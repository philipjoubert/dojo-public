# Enlightened Hospitality (the Stakeholder Order)

## When to Use
- Setting the foundational priority order for any people-facing business — restaurants, hotels, retail, services, healthcare, professional firms.
- Re-anchoring a team that has drifted into investor-first or customer-first thinking and is bleeding the people who make the work happen.
- Defending the long view against short-term pressure — a board, an activist investor, a quarterly target, a cost-cutting consultant.
- Deciding whether to take on a partnership, a sponsorship, a supplier, an investor: the question is always whether the affiliation strengthens or compromises the cycle.

## Fails When
- **Survival cash-flow crisis where the business will close in weeks.** When the lights are about to go off, the order has to invert temporarily — you cannot care for any stakeholder in a closed business. The order resumes the moment cash flow does.
- **Pure transactional businesses with no repeat customer.** If you genuinely never see the customer twice and have no community presence, the cycle's payback loop is weaker. Still useful, just less load-bearing.
- **A founder who isn't willing to leave money on the table sometimes.** Enlightened hospitality is more profitable in the long run, but it's slower and less efficient than investor-first in the short run. A founder who can't tolerate that trade-off will compromise the order under pressure and lose both.

## Core Concept

Every business has the same five stakeholders. You don't get to invent new ones; you only get to choose what order you put them in. Most of business school teaches one order: investors first, customers second, employees third, suppliers as line items, community as PR. Danny inverts the entire stack:

> "Our employees. Our guests. Our community. Our suppliers. Our investors."

That ordering is not sentimentality. It is a virtuous cycle, and the math runs the cycle in the order shown. You cannot consistently take care of guests until the team feels jazzed about coming to work. You cannot consistently earn long-term loyalty in a community unless you are visibly investing in it. You cannot get the best from your suppliers unless you treat them as partners and pay your bills on time. And — counter-intuitively — you cannot deliver the best long-term return to investors *unless* the four prior stakeholders are firing on all cylinders.

> "I place the interests of our investors fifth, but not because I don't want to earn a lot of money. On the contrary, I staunchly believe that standing conventional business priorities on their head ultimately leads to even greater, more enduring financial success."

This is not a totem pole with employees at the top and investors at the bottom. It is a *cycle*. Happy employees → happy guests → strong community presence → loyal suppliers → sustained profit → investors who get a real return precisely because the business has been built to last. Break the cycle at any link — particularly by inverting the order — and the whole thing collapses into a faster, shorter, less profitable run.

The hardest part to internalise: **the order is also the order you defend under pressure.** When cash gets tight, the temptation is to cut the employees first to protect investor returns. Enlightened hospitality says do the opposite — defend the employees, defend the guests, defend the community, even if it means a smaller distribution to investors this year. Investors who signed up for the model understand it. They are buying into the long game. The ones who don't — you don't take their money.

The fifth stakeholder, **community**, is its own discipline. The phrase Danny uses is *"leave our campsite better than we found it"* — taken from the Camp Nebagamon Chef's Cap competition where you were judged not just on the food but on whether you left the pit and the surrounding ground better than you found it. The same logic applies to a neighbourhood, a city park, a supplier town, an industry. You don't get to be in business in a community without taking responsibility for the impact of your wake. Done well, this is not philanthropy — it is enlightened self-interest. The community-investment work creates wealth for the community, which creates goodwill for the business, which attracts a different kind of employee, which delivers a different kind of guest experience, which closes the cycle.

## How to Apply

1. **Write down your stakeholder order, in priority sequence, on the wall.** Don't keep it implicit. Most teams operate on an implicit order they haven't named. Naming it forces the choice and makes it auditable.

2. **Run every major decision through the order.** When weighing a hire, a vendor change, a discount programme, a renovation, an expansion: walk down the list. Does this serve the employees? Does it serve the guests? Does it serve the community? Does it serve the suppliers? Does it serve the investors? If a decision serves the fifth at the cost of the first, you've broken the cycle.

3. **Make stakeholder one — employees — feel it concretely.** Pay competitively. Provide benefits. Solicit feedback in roundtables and "Walk the Talk" surveys. Conduct a monthly dining/employee voucher programme so they actually use the product. Listen when they tell you the leadership is the problem. The signal that you mean the order is the actions, not the rhetoric.

4. **Be deliberate with stakeholder five — investors.** Take only money from people who buy into the model. Be explicit upfront: this is a long-term play, not a fast distribution. Reject capital that requires inverting the order. Personally invest in your own deals so your incentives are aligned.

5. **Make the community work an active programme, not a charity reflex.** Pick causes that map to your core competence (a restaurant fighting hunger, a publisher fighting illiteracy, a builder fighting homelessness). Engage employees as leaders and let them choose what to commit to. The community work is also team-building, recruiting, and brand-building — be honest about that and the cycle works harder.

6. **Treat suppliers as partners, not line items.** Pay on time. Communicate proactively when you can't. Look for shared values. Choose suppliers whose own staff are 51 percenters. The food (or product) cannot be better than the worst ingredient that goes into it, and the supplier relationship cannot be better than the trust between the parties.

7. **Audit the cycle quarterly.** Pick one stakeholder per quarter and ask the team: are we serving them better than we were a year ago? If a stakeholder shows up unhappy in the data — high employee turnover, declining return-guest rate, a community partner who's drifted away, a supplier who stopped quoting first — that's the link in the cycle that's about to break the next two.

## Examples

**Situation: After 9/11, the restaurant Tabla is hit hard. Revenue collapses. The investor-first instinct is to cut hours, lay off staff, simplify the menu, hoard cash.**

**Application:** Danny goes the other way. He applies *abundance* instead of scarcity. Tabla starts donating dinners to charity auctions more generously than ever. The logic: the community-marketing affiliation reaches the right potential guests, the team feels invested in (rather than threatened with cuts), and the eventual return-of-business cycle restarts faster.

> "If you want to be busy, especially in times of scarcity and uncertainty, you cannot accept diminished standards of excellence in even one area. You do everything you possibly can afford to show your staff and guests that you care deeply about improving."

**Result:** The investor-first move would have produced a smaller, faster decline and a slower recovery. The enlightened-hospitality move stabilises the team, attracts the right new guests, and restores revenue. The investors get a smaller payout that quarter and a far better one over the next three years.

**Situation: USHG is choosing a uniform supplier and merchandise vendor for Blue Smoke. There are cheaper options.**

**Application:** Choose Timberland. Not because it's cheapest. Because Timberland is a company whose products exemplify enduring quality, whose employees are outstanding, and whose community-building culture is impressive. The supplier choice is itself a stakeholder-order decision — the supplier has to be aligned with the cycle, or the relationship eventually compromises it.

**Result:** A long, mutually beneficial supplier relationship that aligns brand to brand, and signals to staff that we choose partners by values, not just by price.

**Situation: Bottle of champagne in the freezer (Eleven Madison Park, see hospitality-vs-service.md).** The maître d' takes a cab to the guest's apartment, mid-service.

**Application:** This only happens in a culture where the order is real. The maître d' is empowered to spend $31 on a cab and ten minutes off the floor because the order is *guests come second*, and serving the guest in this concrete way matters more than the short-term operational efficiency the manager might have prioritised in an investor-first culture.

**Result:** The guest becomes a regular for years. The story gets told a hundred times. The cab fare is the cheapest marketing the restaurant ever bought. The cycle closes.

**Situation: Big Apple Barbecue Block Party — Madison Square Park, annually.**

**Application:** USHG hosts a two-day event with ten of the country's best barbecue chefs in a public park steps from Blue Smoke. 110,000 attendees. A meaningful share of revenues goes to the Madison Square Park Conservancy. This is the *community* stakeholder being served on purpose, at scale, with the business directly underwriting park improvements that make the entire neighbourhood better.

> "Leave our campsite better than we found it; always responsible for the impact of our wake."

**Result:** The park is improved (better lighting, programming, restoration). The neighbourhood draws more foot traffic. Blue Smoke becomes inseparable from Madison Square Park identity. The community-investment work and the business performance are no longer separable — they are one cycle.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the stakeholder order as a marketing line.
*Why:* Companies that put "employees first" on the careers page and then run an investor-first operation produce the worst of both worlds — cynical employees, disappointed investors, and a brand whose values claim is recognised internally as a lie. Either operate the order or don't claim it.

**Don't:** Skip the community stakeholder because "we're too small for that."
*Why:* The community work scales down. A two-person business can sponsor the local school's reading programme. A ten-person business can leave its campsite better than it found it in a meaningful way. Skipping it makes the cycle weaker — the goodwill loop never starts.

**Don't:** Take investor money from someone who needs the order inverted.
*Why:* You will lose either the investor relationship or the model. Better to walk away from the capital. The cleanest businesses Danny built were funded by family, friends, and aligned partners who explicitly bought into the long game and the order. The model breaks the moment a powerful investor demands you serve them first.

**Don't:** Lay off employees first when cash gets tight.
*Why:* The signal it sends is that the order was rhetorical. The team that survives the cuts will never trust the order again, and the cycle is broken for years. Cut hours before headcount. Cut the menu before the people. Cut the renovation before the wage scale. When the layoff is genuinely unavoidable, treat the people you let go with the same care as the people who stay — that protects the trust of those still in the building.

**Don't:** Confuse "guests come second" with "guests don't matter."
*Why:* The order is *priority of decision* under conflict, not absolute attention. Guests get the lion's share of the operating focus. The order only matters when there is a real conflict — and at that moment, employees first, because nothing the team can do for guests is sustainable if the team itself isn't being taken care of.
