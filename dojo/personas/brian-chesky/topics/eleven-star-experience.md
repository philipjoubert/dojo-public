# The 11-Star Experience

## When to Use
- You're designing a product experience and want to know what remarkable actually looks like.
- You're getting incremental feedback that suggests small tweaks, but you want a breakthrough.
- You're trying to align a team on what "great" means, not just "shipped."
- You're pushing back on a roadmap that optimizes for metrics instead of love.

## Fails When
- You haven't earned 5 stars yet — if the door doesn't open, don't daydream about the surfboard.
- The team tries to literally build the 11-star version. That's not the point. You design the extreme to come back.
- You run the exercise once and file it away. It's a practice, not a workshop.

## Core Concept

Here's what I've learned. If you ask a user "what could I do to make this better," they'll tell you something small. A cleaner, a better key, a faster check-in. That's incremental. That's not a product that changes anyone's life. So you ask a different question. What would it take for me to design something that you would literally tell every single person you've ever encountered?

Now you're in a different conversation.

So we developed this framework at Airbnb. You take one part of your product and you extrapolate. A five-star experience is functional. They open the door. They let you in. Great. Nobody's calling their mom about that. They might say: "I used Airbnb. It worked." Six stars is delightful. Welcome gift, bottle of wine, toiletries in the bathroom, fridge stocked. Better than a hotel. Seven stars is magical. They know you surf, there's a board waiting in the garage, they booked you a lesson, there's a car you can use, a reservation at the best restaurant in town. You're going — whoa. Ten stars? The Beatles arriving in 1964. Five thousand high school kids cheering your name, a press conference on the tarmac. Eleven stars: you show up at the airport and Elon Musk is there. "You're going to space."

It turns out nine, ten, eleven are not feasible. That's not the point. The point is that you have to almost design the extreme to come backwards. Once you've seen the 11-star version, the 7-star version doesn't look crazy anymore. It looks reasonable. A surfboard in the garage, a booked restaurant — that's not exotic. That's just someone paying attention. And that's the level of care you'd never have gotten to if you'd stayed at "what can we do better." You only get there by going too far first.

## How to Apply
1. **Pick one experience.** Not the whole product — one slice. Check-in. First booking. Day one of a trip. Onboarding. The exercise only works when it's concrete.
2. **Write out the 1-to-3-star version.** It's broken. Nobody's there. You're waiting twenty minutes. Be specific about what failure looks like.
3. **Write out the 5-star version.** Functional. It works. Nobody's telling a friend.
4. **Push to 6, then 7.** Delightful, then magical. What would exceed expectations? What would feel personalized?
5. **Go absurd. 10 stars. 11 stars.** The Beatles. Space. Let the team laugh. Let it get ridiculous. This is where the nerve comes in — most companies stop at 7 because they're embarrassed to keep going.
6. **Come back.** Walk backwards from 11. Which of these ideas, stripped of absurdity, are actually plausible? A surfboard doesn't need Elon Musk to procure — it needs a host who asked one question.
7. **Repeat it.** Every release, every major feature. Ask where you are on the scale and what it would take to go one star higher.

## Examples

**Situation:** We were asking our users how to improve check-in. They gave us small answers. Better instructions. A photo of the lockbox.

**Application:** We ran the 11-star exercise. What would a 7-star check-in look like? The host is there, knows your name, hands you a glass of wine, shows you the surfboard because they asked about your trip. What would 10-star look like? A red carpet from the curb. 11-star? Elon Musk, a spacesuit.

**Result:** Walking back from 11 made 7 look completely achievable. We stopped shipping marginal improvements to the lockbox flow and started shipping things like host bios, preference surveys, and the passport profile. The gap between "it worked" and "you have to stay there next time" closed — not by one feature, but by a compounding stack of them.

**Situation:** I followed Ricardo around on a trip to San Francisco. He went to Alcatraz alone. Ate at tourist traps. Sat alone at the hotel bar. By the numbers, the trip was "fine." By the 11-star scale, it was a 3.

**Application:** We storyboarded the 11-star version. A driver picks him up. Dinner parties. The best seats at restaurants. A midnight mystery bike tour with sixty riders where nobody knows where they'll end up. We executed it by hand — not scalable, not cheap, but real.

**Result:** I ran into Ricardo after. "How was it?" "Amazing." I walked away. He yelled after me — "Brian, one more thing." And he started crying. "Thank you. This is the best trip I've ever had." That became our blueprint. We didn't know yet how to scale it. But we knew it was possible.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Literally try to ship the 11-star version.
**Why:** The exercise is a compass, not a roadmap. If you ship space travel, you'll blow the business model, exhaust the team, and still have a broken check-in. You design the extreme to come back, not to stay.

**Don't:** Run the exercise before the core works.
**Why:** We can't do new things unless we have permission. If people are complaining on social media and calling customer service, they don't love the core service. Get your house in order first. Earn the right to dream bigger.

**Don't:** Ask "what can we do better?" and call that the 11-star exercise.
**Why:** That's the question you're trying to replace. Better yields incremental. Tell-every-person-you've-ever-met yields transformative. If the question isn't uncomfortable, you haven't asked it right.
