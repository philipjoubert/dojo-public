# Hiring Like a Detective (and the Scaling Assessment)

## When to Use
- You're about to make a senior hire and you want to get it right the first time.
- You've made a few executive hires that didn't work out and you can't figure out why.
- Someone on your team is good at their job but the people they hire are mediocre.
- You've got a nagging feeling that one of your directs isn't scaling, but they haven't told you so.
- You're closing a candidate who has other offers and you don't want to oversell.

## Fails When
- You apply the presumption of guilt to junior candidates — junior people don't have results yet, so you're looking for energy, learning agility, and passion, not a track record.
- You use references as a checkbox and outsource them to a recruiter — half the signal is in how you ask the question and the follow-ups you push.
- You run the Shackleton close on mediocre candidates to talk yourself into hiring them. The close is a filter, not a persuasion tool.
- You confuse "in the details" of your directs' hires with micromanagement. You're the co-hiring manager. That's the job.

## Core Concept

Hiring is not a sales pipeline. It's a detective novel. And if you treat it like a pipeline, you're going to end up with a company full of mediocre people who interviewed well.

Every potential hire is guilty until proven innocent. It is the opposite of our justice system. Most people interview looking for absence of weaknesses — somebody seems smart, they don't say anything dumb, so you hire them. That's the presumption of innocence, and it's exactly backwards. The proof is they don't work for you. You need evidence to hire them, not evidence to eliminate them. The best people are spiky. They're extraordinary at a few things and they might be pains in the ass to work with. But here's what I've found — the biggest pains in the ass are actually the people who aren't able to do their job.

Start with results, work backwards to people. Most founders start with resumes. They start with brands. "Oh, this person worked at Google." Fine. But what products do I admire? Then who built those products? And who actually built them? You know the number of marketers who said they came up with "Just Do It"? So the best way to find out who actually did it is to ask the people back then who actually did it. It's a detective novel. You're working backwards from the crime scene to find the person whose fingerprints are really on it.

Once you have a candidate, interviews are the weakest signal you'll ever get. Executives have more experience bullshitting you than you have experience detecting their bullshit. It's an asymmetric game — you're a white belt fighting a black belt. They're going to punch you in the face repeatedly. That's why I prioritize references over interviews, and that's why the interview itself has to work differently. Ask them to explain how they did something. Then ask a follow-up. Then ask another one. The key is to ask two follow-ups. You never want the first answer. You always want the third answer. If people don't know what they're talking about, they struggle. The first answer is rehearsed. The second is their framework. The third is where you either get extraordinary specifics — names, dates, tradeoffs, the moment they almost quit — or vague generalities. Vague generalities at the third level mean they weren't in the details. They were managing from above. And a manager who can't describe what they managed is like a cavalry general who can't ride a horse.

And once you've hired, the hiring never ends. You have to keep asking whether the person is still scaling. Because a pitcher never takes themselves off the mound. People are not going to come to you telling you they can't do their job.

## How to Apply

1. **Start with results.** Don't start with resumes. Ask yourself: what products or companies do I admire? Who built them? Who actually built them? That's your candidate pool.
2. **Interview until the recruiting team stages an intervention.** I interviewed the first 400 people at Airbnb. I wish I interviewed the first thousand. Then interview for two more years after the intervention.
3. **Ask the two follow-ups.** In every interview of substance, ask them to explain how they did something, then push two layers deeper. The third answer is the real answer.
4. **Do the references yourself. Never outsource them.** Start every call by saying it's off the record and unattributed. The more unattributed, the more honest.
5. **Ask for specifics.** They said the candidate is amazing — why? What would you point to? No specifics means they don't really know.
6. **Ask the development question.** "What's the one area of development you would give them?" They'll feel they need to say something to seem thoughtful. That something is often the most important signal in the entire reference.
7. **Ask the best-person question.** "Who's the best person you ever worked with?" If they don't name your candidate, the candidate isn't one of the best.
8. **End with: "Who else should I talk to?"** Build a network. Hiring should be more like network building than a sales pipeline.
9. **Qualify your references.** B players say other B players are awesome. Find out how good the references themselves are before you weight what they say about your candidate.
10. **Run the Shackleton test to close.** At the very end, try to talk them out of the job. Tell them the longest hours they've ever had. Higher standards than anywhere else. Day and night. The mediocre people get turned off; the great ones lean in.
11. **Co-hire your directs-to-directs.** If your CFO presents a candidate and they haven't met you, assume they're not good enough. Hire people that don't want to work for your exec — they only want to work for them AND you.
12. **Check the scaling category once a quarter.** Are they behind, on track, or six months ahead? Nine out of ten times the trend never reverses unless you narrow their role.
13. **Use the hire-quality proxy.** Look at who they hire. People never hire people better than them. If their team is mediocre, they're not scaling, no matter how good their own work looks.
14. **Fire faster than feels comfortable.** Everyone says they waited too long to fire someone and never did it too fast. Trust that pattern. Trust your instincts when someone is frustrating you.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is about to hire a CMO from a Fortune 500 company. The resume is excellent — big brand, growing business, ran a $500 million marketing org.
**Application:** Start with results, not the resume. Ask: what marketing work at that company do I actually admire? If nothing comes to mind, that's your answer. Then work backwards: who at that company actually built the thing I admire? It probably isn't the senior executive who took credit for it. Do references with the people two levels down from the candidate — the ones who actually shipped the work. Ask the best-person question. Ask the development question. And remember: someone used to a $500 million budget often can't do anything with $5 million. That's a wrong-stage hire.
**Result:** The founder passes on the CMO and instead finds a director-level marketer from a fast-growing company who built the function from zero. The new hire ships more in six months than the Fortune 500 candidate would have shipped in two years.

**Situation:** Airbnb, early years. I interviewed the first 400 hires personally. It took enormous time. People told me I was being a bottleneck.
**Application:** I kept doing it anyway. I wanted every hire to feel like they were joining something they couldn't join anywhere else. And I wanted to personally calibrate what excellence looked like so that, as the company grew, I had a reference point. My biggest regret is not interviewing the first thousand. Once the recruiting team stages an intervention, interview for two more years after that.
**Result:** The early hires became the cultural core that carried us through ten years of growth and a pandemic. You cannot rebuild that density of conviction later. You have to lay it in at the start.

**Situation:** A CEO has an SVP who is competent and well-liked, but the people she's hired onto her team are uniformly mediocre. Her own metrics look fine.
**Application:** The quality of someone's hires is the proxy you cannot fake. People never hire people better than them. If she can't attract great talent, she's not scaling — no matter how good she looks in her own lane. The trend rarely reverses. You can narrow her role, or you can move her out. Don't wait. You'll look back in six months and wish you'd done it sooner.
**Result:** The CEO moves the SVP to a narrower scope where she thrives, and puts a true A player in the broader role. Within two quarters the team composition flips. A players compound. B players compound differently — they hire lots of C players.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept the first answer in an interview.
**Why:** The first answer is rehearsed. Everyone has a first answer. Without follow-ups you're measuring their preparation, not their competence. The second follow-up is where people without the details start floundering.

**Don't:** Let your executives hire their directs without you.
**Why:** If your executive can hire someone you've never met, they're not reaching high enough. Those people become the company's people the moment that executive leaves — which they will. You'll inherit a team you didn't vet, doing work you didn't shape, going in a direction you didn't set. This happens over and over again.

**Don't:** Sell the candidate a country club.
**Why:** Recruiters want to sell great benefits, great perks, great everything. It creates mismanaged expectations and the wrong people say yes. The great ones want it to be hard. Why do the most fit people in the world want to be Navy SEALs? Because they want it to be hard. Close with Shackleton, not with perks.

**Don't:** Wait for someone who's not scaling to tell you.
**Why:** A pitcher never takes themselves off the mound. People will not come to you telling you they can't do their job. They're not going to take any hints either. You have to see it, name it, and act on it. The compassionate thing is actually to move quickly, not to let them languish while the team suffers around them.
