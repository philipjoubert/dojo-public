# Storyboard and Blueprint — Wrapping Your Arms Around the Company

## When to Use
- Your product has grown organically and nobody on the team can draw the full user journey.
- Teams are shipping individual screens but the end-to-end experience feels disjointed.
- You're about to do a big redesign and you need to understand what you actually have before you touch anything.
- You're trying to reinvent a category and you've run out of ideas from inside the category.

## Fails When
- You do the mapping exercise and then nothing changes. The storyboard is a tool for action. If you don't prioritize and fix, you've just made a beautiful wall chart.
- You copy the surface of an orthogonal industry without understanding the principle underneath. A storyboard on your wall is not Pixar. Walking a trip isn't cinema.
- You produce a map of 150 screens and can't tell which are load-bearing. Everything stays equally important, which means nothing gets fixed.

## Core Concept

To reinvent an industry, you do not take inspiration directly from that industry. You need to look at orthogonal industries. For us, the orthogonal industry to travel was cinema. For hospitality, the inspiration wasn't Marriott or Hilton — it was the French Laundry. For recruiting, it wasn't other recruiters — it was sports agents, or the leaders of Cirque du Soleil, people who live and die on talent.

So I literally hired a storyboard artist from Pixar, and we had him storyboard the perfect Airbnb experience. When we did that, we realized something critical. In a two-hour movie of the trip, only twenty minutes were in the home. There was all this stuff leading up to it — getting to the airport, arriving, going to dinner, exploring. Most of the trip was not in the home. We realized we needed to be in the end-to-end business of travel.

Walt Disney did this in the 1930s. Snow White was the first feature-length animated film, and it was so long he couldn't keep track of it. So he invented the storyboard. We thought — what if we did the same thing for Airbnb?

That led to what we call the Airbnb Blueprint. I asked the team: every single screen a user sees, put it on one wall. It turns out there are 150 screens. Then I said: every user policy, every time someone calls customer service, what policy are they referencing? It turns out there were nearly 70 user policies, some of them 700 pages each. Then every operational touchpoint. Then we went through 20 million customer service calls. Hundreds of thousands of social media posts. Tons of workshops. Our own firsthand experience — because we believe people who make radical products make products for themselves. We call this wrapping your arms around the company.

Based on all that, we created a prioritized map and systematically tried to fix our product. It wasn't glamorous. But it's the work that earned us permission to innovate on top of the core.

And there's a deeper structure underneath the storyboard. Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey. A character starts in the ordinary world, crosses a threshold, faces a challenge, is transformed, and returns changed. This is the narrative of every movie you've ever seen. It turns out it's also the promise of great travel. We applied it to trips. Day one: welcome event, get acclimated. Day two or three: a challenge out of your comfort zone — because if you do not leave your comfort zone, you do not remember the trip. Transformation. Return. The ancient story shape was already there. We just needed to recognize it.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the end-to-end experience you want to understand.** Not one screen. Not one feature. The whole thing, from before they think about your product through after they're done with it.
2. **Find the orthogonal industry.** What industry, completely unrelated to yours, has solved the problem you're trying to solve? Travel → cinema. Hospitality → restaurants. Recruiting → sports agents. Don't copy their surface; learn their principle.
3. **Storyboard the ideal experience.** Hire a storyboard artist if you have to. Draw it frame by frame, scene by scene. You'll discover what's missing. (For Airbnb, we discovered that most of the movie wasn't in the home.)
4. **Put every screen on one wall.** Every screen. Every state. Every empty state. If you can't fit them all on a single wall, you don't understand your product.
5. **Put every policy on the wall.** Every customer-service policy, every host policy, every edge case. Yes, all 70. Yes, some of them are 700 pages. Put them up.
6. **Put every operational touchpoint on the wall.** Email. SMS. Push. Phone. Support. Every contact you have with a user.
7. **Mine the data.** 20 million customer service calls. Social media. Reviews. What are the top categories of complaint, the top categories of delight? Let the users tell you where the gaps are.
8. **Find the Hero's Journey.** Where does the user start? Where do they cross the threshold? Where is their challenge? Where is the transformation? Where do they return? Map the narrative, not just the screens.
9. **Prioritize by impact, not by team ownership.** Resist the urge to let current org structure dictate what gets fixed. The map shows you what the user experiences — fix that order.
10. **Fix systematically.** Not all at once. Not forever. The blueprint is a living artifact. Update it. Walk the wall regularly.

## Examples

**Situation:** By the late 2010s, Airbnb had grown into dozens of divisions, each optimizing their own slice. The end-to-end experience had drifted. Nobody could draw the whole journey. Guests would show up, the key wouldn't work. Photos didn't match reality. And we were out there launching new verticals.

**Application:** We did the storyboard exercise. Hired the Pixar artist. Realized most of the trip wasn't in the home — so we were solving for the wrong 20 minutes. We mapped all 150 screens, all 70 policies, every operational touchpoint. We read through 20 million customer service calls, hundreds of thousands of social media posts. We wrapped our arms around the company.

**Result:** It gave us a prioritized fix list. We stopped launching new verticals and earned permission to innovate by fixing the core. That discipline is what eventually let us launch Rooms, the host passport, Experiences — because the core service was finally good enough that users gave us permission to expand.

**Situation:** We wanted to design what a perfect trip looked like. Metrics couldn't tell us. Surveys couldn't tell us. We needed a real human to walk us through what was wrong and what could be right.

**Application:** We put up anonymous fliers: "seeking a traveler, we'll photograph your trip to San Francisco." A guy named Ricardo from London replied. We sent a photographer to follow him. His trip was awful — Alcatraz alone, tourist-trap restaurants, hotel bar by himself. We brought him back and storyboarded the ideal Hero's Journey for him. Driver at the airport. Dinner parties. Best seats at restaurants. A midnight mystery bike tour with sixty riders where nobody but the leader knows where they'll end up.

**Result:** After the trip, Ricardo walked away, then yelled after me — "Brian, one more thing" — and started crying. "Thank you. This is the best trip I've ever had." We had proof that the storyboard worked. No one had tried to design an end-to-end experience for somebody like they were in a movie before. That became our blueprint for Trips — confident that we knew how to move somebody, even before we knew how to scale it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Map the storyboard and then keep shipping what you were already shipping.
**Why:** Wrapping your arms around the company is only useful if it changes the priority list. If the team walks away from the exercise feeling "enlightened" but the roadmap doesn't move, you've done theater, not work.

**Don't:** Copy the surface of the orthogonal industry.
**Why:** Pixar's storyboard is not magic. The principle is that you have to see the whole arc before you polish the scenes. Copy the principle, not the artifact. Otherwise you get a literal storyboard on a wall that nobody uses.

**Don't:** Try to fix everything at once.
**Why:** 150 screens, 70 policies, 20 million calls — if you try to address all of it simultaneously, nothing gets fixed. The whole point of the blueprint is a prioritized map. Pick the load-bearing items. Fix those. Walk the wall again in six months.
