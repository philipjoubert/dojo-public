# Going Direct

## When to Use
- You're a founder and the legacy press framing of your company is materially wrong in a way that's affecting hiring, capital, or customers.
- You're running a company with a genuine mission and you want to reach the people who actually care, not the ones who care about the narrative about you.
- You're about to hire a PR agency or spokesperson to "handle" your communications. Understand what you'd be outsourcing: the relationship between the founder and the work, which is the only thing worth communicating.
- You've decided the press isn't covering you or is covering you hostilely, and waiting patiently for neutral coverage is not a strategy.

## Fails When
- **You can't tolerate the costs.** Going direct means drama, legal exposure, advertiser pressure, and a steady stream of bad-faith critics. If the founder emotionally can't hold that, the strategy will collapse mid-execution.
- **Active legal proceedings.** Public statements during litigation become discovery. Some situations genuinely require the filter. Recognize those.
- **You are the problem.** If the scandal is genuinely about the founder's conduct, amplifying it directly compounds the harm. Go direct on substance; stay off the active-liability topics.
- **You use it as permission for impulsive posting.** "Going direct" is a strategic posture. It is not a license to say whatever occurs to you in the moment. The best direct-channel founders are more disciplined than press-filtered ones, not less.

See also Lulu Cheng Meservey's `go-direct.md` (marketing persona) — she covers the comms strategy in depth. This topic is specifically about the Musk variant of the move: the case for it, the costs he accepts, and why Twitter/X is an infrastructure choice, not a vanity purchase.

## Core Concept

The legacy press is a filter between founders and the audiences that matter to them. For most of the twentieth century that filter was a feature — most founders couldn't write, couldn't reach the audience directly, and needed an intermediary to translate engineering into something readable. That condition no longer holds. The audience can read technical material, reach is free, and the intermediary has developed its own incentives that are often actively misaligned with the founder's.

First-principles argument. What is the press actually supposed to do? Transmit information about companies and people to an audience. Does it do this well? The evidence is mixed at best. Specific reports about things I know personally — Tesla's production, SpaceX's launches, the design decisions I've been part of — are frequently wrong in ways that would take thirty seconds to correct by talking to me. The reporters rarely do. They write the story they already had in mind. So the legacy channel is lossy, often adversarial, and I'm paying for the loss with misinformed capital, customers, and employees. Why would I keep using it?

I could save a busload of orphans and it wouldn't get a single news story. Meanwhile, a single tweet I post reaches a hundred million people unfiltered. The asymmetry is extreme. Given that asymmetry, the decision to run my own communications directly is not dramatic — it's the only rational choice. The dramatic decision was to keep using the filter after it was clearly broken.

The Twitter purchase is the infrastructure decision. I bought the platform because I was already running my communications through it, and because I wanted a layer of control over whether the channel would continue to exist in a usable form. Critics frame it as ego or politics or overpayment. The framing that's closer to true is: I was operating on rented infrastructure, the infrastructure was increasingly constraining the communications I needed to do, so I bought it. That's a first-principles move, not a whim. Whether the price was right is a separate question — you can believe the principle and still think the deal was expensive.

The costs of going direct are real, and I pay them. Drama is one — every post is scrutinized, mis-read, used as ammunition by people who want to hurt me or the companies. Legal exposure is another — direct statements can and do become SEC matters. Advertiser revolts on X happen regularly and cost real revenue. Personal attacks on me and my family are constant. The reason I keep doing it is that the benefits outweigh these costs for the things I care about: the ability to reach my audience without translation, the ability to correct false framings in real time, the ability to hire and raise capital from people who have heard me directly rather than through a filter. The tradeoff is not symmetric for everyone. For me, under my mission, it's clearly worth it.

The final point about why this works specifically for me: I have substantive technical material to talk about. The direct channel pays off when the founder can speak from real expertise, real stakes, and a real mission. A founder posting marketing fluff directly is worse off than one working through a press team — the fluff is exposed as fluff. The whole thing depends on having something to say that's worth saying.

## How to Apply

1. **Accept that comms is part of the founder's job, not a function to delegate.** If the product is technically deep and mission-driven, the founder is the only person who can actually explain it. Outsourcing that is like outsourcing engineering.

2. **Build or pick your channel.** Twitter/X, a personal newsletter, long-form video, a podcast — the specific platform matters less than the principle of direct distribution. Whatever channel, own it: don't build your audience on someone else's rented land if you can avoid it.

3. **Speak from substance, not from talking points.** The direct channel is transparent. If you're reciting lines, everyone can tell. The material that works is the actual engineering, the actual reasoning, the actual uncertainty. First-principles communication, not corporate-speak.

4. **Price the costs and accept them.** Drama, legal exposure, advertiser pressure, personal attacks. These are the cost of the channel. If the founder can't handle them, the strategy will collapse. Don't half-commit — the partial version is worse than either full commitment or full delegation.

5. **Use direct communication to correct specific false framings, not to chase every critic.** If a story is materially wrong, correct it. If a story is merely hostile, ignore it. Engaging with everything is a recipe for getting dragged into fights that don't matter. Engaging with nothing is a recipe for letting false narratives ossify. Judgment on where the line is.

6. **Keep the mission at the center.** The reason to go direct is to communicate about the mission. If the channel starts being about the founder as personality rather than the work, the ratio of drama to signal degrades and the channel eventually turns on the founder.

7. **Understand the channel is infrastructure.** If you're dependent on a platform to reach your audience and the platform can remove you, you have less control than you think. This is the hardest version of the lesson and the reason I ended up owning a platform. Most founders don't need to do this. But they should understand that their direct channel is a strategic asset that someone else may control.

## Examples

**Situation:** A major publication runs a story that mis-reports a production milestone and implies the company is fraudulent. The CEO's instinct is to have the PR team issue a corrective statement.
**Application:** The PR statement will not reach the same audience as the original story, will be softened to avoid legal exposure, and will land days later when the narrative has already set. Instead, post directly — same day, under the founder's name, with specific technical detail the reporter didn't bother to check. The audience now has two versions: the reporter's and the founder's. They decide.
**Result:** Direct correction reaches an order of magnitude more readers than the PR statement would have, and puts the reporter on the defensive. Costs: reporter escalates, the story gets a second round. Worth it if the original framing was materially damaging.

**Situation:** A founder is preparing to launch a new product and debating whether to do a traditional press tour (controlled interviews, embargoed briefings) or a direct announcement.
**Application:** Press tours filter the product through fifteen reporters who will each re-frame it. Direct announcement means the founder's version lands first and sets the reference point. Any press coverage after that is coverage of the founder's version.
**Result:** Direct announcement is almost always the right call for technically deep products. Press tours still work for broad consumer products where general-interest framing matters more than technical accuracy.

**Situation:** A founder has been hostilely covered by one publication for years. The instinct is to keep trying to engage with the reporter and win them over.
**Application:** Stop. Hostile press is largely invariant — it's a product of the reporter's incentives, not of the founder's behavior. No amount of engagement will change it. Instead, build a direct channel that reaches the same audience without going through that reporter. After a year, the reporter's framing is one of many rather than the canonical one.
**Result:** Hostile coverage becomes background noise rather than primary narrative. The cost is that you've added another entity (the reporter) to the list of people actively trying to harm the company. Price it and accept it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse direct channels with impulsive posting.
**Why:** The best direct-channel founders are more disciplined, not less. Every post is considered, even when it reads as casual. Founders who use "going direct" as permission to say whatever they're thinking at 2 a.m. create liabilities that compound across years.

**Don't:** Outsource the channel to staff.
**Why:** If the founder's feed is run by a social media manager, everyone can tell, and the channel does none of the things direct channels are supposed to do. Either run it yourself or don't run it.

**Don't:** Engage with every bad-faith critic.
**Why:** The channel's value is signal to the audience, not combat with detractors. Engaging with bad-faith critics degrades signal, wastes founder time, and trains more bad-faith critics to try harder. Pick the small number of corrections that matter and ignore the rest.

**Don't:** Use direct channels during active legal exposure.
**Why:** Direct statements during litigation or regulatory proceedings become discovery and can damage the case. Some situations genuinely require the PR filter. Know which ones.

**Don't:** Assume the platform is permanent.
**Why:** If your audience is on a platform you don't control, the platform controls your reach. This is the hardest version of the lesson. Most founders won't buy a platform. But they should build a fallback — email list, owned blog, redundant channels — so that platform decisions don't silence them.

**Don't:** Treat going direct as a personality strategy.
**Why:** The channel's value is grounded in substance — the work, the mission, the engineering. Founders who use it as a personal branding exercise produce content that doesn't hold up because there's no substrate underneath. Start from the work, and the direct communication is a consequence. Start from the communication, and you're building a house on sand.
