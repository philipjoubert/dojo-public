# Constructor Theory

## When to Use

- Someone closes a conversation with "that's impossible" — and you need a discipline for asking *which kind* of impossible they mean.
- A strategy debate is being settled by appeal to a fixed physical limit (resource depletion, planetary capacity, the impossibility of curing aging, the supposed ceiling on energy production), and you suspect the limit is epistemic rather than physical.
- A pessimistic prediction is being treated as rigour — "we have to assume X can't be done" — and you want the framework that distinguishes can't-yet from can't-ever.
- You are reading a sustainability or precautionary-principle argument and want the principled objection rather than a vibes-based one.
- You're working on a problem that has been declared intractable by people you respect, and you want a clean way to ask whether their declaration is a claim about physics or a claim about current knowledge.
- You are explaining why long-shot research bets are not irrational.

## Fails When

- **The limit is genuinely physical.** Faster-than-light signalling, perpetual motion machines, lossless duplication of arbitrary quantum states, decreasing entropy in a closed system — these are forbidden by laws of physics that have themselves survived enormous criticism. The framework does not authorise you to ignore them. It authorises you to ask which side of the dichotomy a given claim lives on, and to answer honestly.
- **You're in execution mode and need to ship.** Constructor theory is a thinking tool for evaluating possibility claims. It is not a project plan. A team that needs to launch this quarter can't profit from "but in principle, given enough knowledge…" — they need the contingent, parochial knowledge they actually have. Don't deploy it to derail tactical conversations.
- **You use it to wave away every constraint as merely epistemic.** Most constraints in startup life — runway, headcount, customer attention, distribution — are not physical. They are also not "solved by knowledge in the relevant sense"; they are bounded resource problems that have to be navigated. Saying "everything is just a knowledge problem" is not the claim. The claim is much more specific: what *the laws of physics permit* is bounded only by knowledge.
- **Used as a banner for techno-utopianism without the work.** The framework is a structural argument about why progress isn't bounded the way pessimists claim. It is not a promise that any specific thing you want will happen on your preferred timeline. The dichotomy is between possible-with-knowledge and physically-forbidden — not between "achievable next year" and "achievable never."

## Core Concept

Almost all the laws of physics, as currently formulated, are *prescriptive* about how things change — given a state, here is what will happen next. Constructor theory is the project of reformulating physics in a different way: as statements about which transformations are *possible* and which are *impossible*. A law of physics, in this view, is a claim about the dichotomy between the achievable and the forbidden — what *any possible constructor*, given enough knowledge and resources, could bring about, and what nothing could.

The reason this matters, beyond the physics itself, is the consequence: every conceivable physical transformation is in exactly one of two categories. Either it is forbidden by the laws of physics, in which case no amount of knowledge or effort can ever achieve it; or it is permitted by the laws of physics, in which case it is *achievable, given the right knowledge*. There is no third category. The argument is not subtle: if there were a transformation that was permitted by the laws of physics but that no possible constructor could ever achieve, that "regardless of knowledge" would itself be a regularity of nature, and that regularity would itself be a law of physics — i.e., the transformation would be forbidden after all. The dichotomy is exhaustive.

The implication, dropped without ceremony into the middle of *The Beginning of Infinity* and elaborated across the constructor-theory papers: anything not forbidden by the laws of physics is achievable, given the right knowledge. This is not a slogan; it is a structural claim. It does not mean every desirable thing will be achieved on your preferred schedule. It means that the obstacle to any non-forbidden goal is *always* missing knowledge, not missing fundamental possibility. When somebody says "we can't cure aging" or "we can't deflect a city-killer asteroid" or "we can't run civilization on clean energy," they are almost always making a claim about current knowledge dressed up as a claim about physics. The discipline is to ask which one they mean.

The relationship to the rest of the framework: constructor theory is the deep version of *problems are soluble*, and it backs up *wealth as knowledge*. Problems are soluble *because* the only obstacle to a permitted transformation is knowledge, and knowledge is the kind of thing that can be created without limit. Wealth — defined as the set of transformations one can effect — grows with knowledge, because each new piece of knowledge widens the set of achievable transformations. The static-society pessimisms — Spaceship Earth, Malthus, the precautionary principle, the "we're at the limits of growth" set of claims — all share the common error of mistaking a limit-of-current-knowledge for a limit-of-physics. They are all making the third-category mistake, and there is no third category.

For founders, constructor theory is rarely a daily operational tool. Where it earns its keep is at the level of *premises*: when the conversation has reached an argument that pivots on what is or isn't possible, the framework gives you a sharp question. Is the constraint a law of physics, or is it a current state of knowledge? If the latter — and it is *almost always* the latter — then the constraint is dissolvable in principle, and the only remaining questions are how much, by when, and at what cost. Those are different questions from "is this even possible?" and they have different answers.

The one move the framework forbids: the appeal to "we don't know how, therefore it can't be done." That move converts an admission of ignorance into a prophecy about the future of knowledge, which is exactly the move *Problems Are Soluble* and the principle of optimism are designed to refuse. You don't get to assume that future knowledge will solve everything; you also don't get to assume that it will solve nothing. The honest position is "we don't know yet, and the laws of physics don't forbid it, so the question is open."

## How to Apply

1. **When somebody says "X is impossible," ask which kind of impossible.** Is the claim that the laws of physics forbid X (in which case fine, but they should be able to point at the law), or is the claim that we currently don't know how (in which case the question is about knowledge gaps, not about possibility)? The two get conflated constantly, and the conflation closes off conversations that should remain open. Demanding the distinction is enough, on its own, to kill a lot of bad pessimism.

2. **When you say "X is impossible," apply the same test to yourself.** Most of the time founders wave the impossibility flag, they are talking about current knowledge or current resources, not about laws of physics. "We can't acquire that customer profitably" is almost never a physics claim; it is a parochial claim about today's distribution channels and unit economics, both of which are knowledge-dependent and changeable. Catching yourself doing this — saying "impossible" when you mean "we don't know how yet" — is itself a stance correction.

3. **For long-horizon bets, separate the in-principle question from the in-practice question.** "Is fusion power achievable?" is a physics question; the answer is yes (fusion happens; the question of whether it can be done in a useful way on Earth is a knowledge-and-engineering question). "Will fusion power be cheap by 2035?" is a parochial question about specific knowledge that may or may not be created in time. Both questions are interesting; they are different questions and need different reasoning. Constructor theory clarifies the first one. It does not pretend to settle the second.

4. **Refuse the precautionary-principle move that imports "we don't know how" into "we shouldn't try."** The precautionary principle in its strong form says: we should not do anything not known to be safe. This is functionally identical to overconfident recklessness — both pretend to know unknowable things about the future. Constructor theory's deeper objection is that progress consists precisely of trying things whose effects are not fully known and learning from the result. The historical record is decisive: civilisations that fell, fell from *insufficient* knowledge, not from *too much*. The discipline is to refuse the precautionary frame as rigour and to substitute an honest "we don't know yet, here's what we'd need to find out, here's what we'll do meanwhile."

5. **Use the framework to redirect "should we do X?" into "what knowledge would unlock X?"** Most of the most interesting questions a founder faces are of the form "should we attempt this?" The constructor-theoretic redirect is: assuming X is permitted by the laws of physics — which it almost always is — what is the missing piece of knowledge between us and X, and is creating that piece of knowledge tractable on our timescale? That reframing kills a lot of unproductive should-we debate and replaces it with a productive what-do-we-need-to-know conversation.

6. **Hold the framework lightly when execution is on the line.** If your team needs to ship by Q3, the right invocation is not "but in principle, given enough knowledge…" — it's "what specific knowledge do we have today, and what can we build with it?" Constructor theory is a tool for evaluating possibility *claims* and pessimism *premises*, not a substitute for parochial competence. Think of it as the metaphysical floor under your operational work: when somebody tries to convince you the floor isn't there, you need this argument; when you're trying to actually move furniture, you don't.

## Examples

**Situation:** A board meeting. A senior advisor argues against the company's longevity-research bet on the grounds that "biology is messy, the human body is too complicated, you can't engineer aging out of an organism." Several board members nod.
**Application:** The argument is doing a great deal of work in the gap between "biology is messy" and "you can't." The first is true; the second does not follow from it. Aging is a physical process happening in physical bodies; nothing in the laws of physics forbids the reverse process (the configuration of an aged body becoming, again, the configuration of a young body — the constraints are entropic but not absolute, given enough energy and information). The question is whether the *knowledge* required to bring about that transformation can be created. That's a question about how hard the science is and how long it might take; it isn't a question about whether the transformation is permitted. The advisor has converted "we don't know how" into "it can't be done," which is the move the framework refuses.
**Result:** The company doesn't have to bet the farm on longevity to recognise that the case against was non-rigorous. The decision becomes: how plausible is it that the relevant knowledge can be created on a timescale we care about, and how big is the bet relative to other bets we could make? Those are real questions. "Biology is messy, therefore impossible" is not.

**Situation:** Sustainability discourse around a startup's growth plans. A consultant argues that the planet has finite resources and the company's growth model is therefore "ultimately unsustainable." The founder feels the criticism is doing some real work.
**Application:** The constructor-theoretic move is to separate the resources from the *transformations the resources are inputs to*. A "resource" is whatever knowledge currently allows you to use — wood was a resource, then coal, then oil, then uranium. The set of resources is not fixed; it is a function of current knowledge. The Easter Islanders ran out of trees; humans in present-day England don't need them, because the transformations that trees used to enable (heat, ships, structures) are now achieved with knowledge-intensive substitutes. The "ultimately unsustainable" claim is a third-category claim: it asserts that no future knowledge can re-route the company's resource needs, which is a prophecy about future knowledge — not a fact about the planet. The right response isn't to deny that constraints exist; it's to reject the framing that converts "current knowledge runs out" into "physics runs out."
**Result:** The company can take resource constraints seriously *as engineering problems* without conceding the deeper claim. "We need to find substitutes for the inputs we currently depend on" is a valid project. "We have hit the limits of growth" is a category mistake.

**Situation:** A founder is told by an industry expert that asteroid mining is unrealistic for the foreseeable future. The expert is technically excellent and the founder respects them. The founder is tempted to drop the line of thought.
**Application:** Three claims hide in "unrealistic." (a) Asteroid mining is forbidden by the laws of physics — false; the relevant transformations (extracting matter from asteroids, transporting it, using it) all happen in present-day technology in nearby contexts. (b) The knowledge required is far away — empirical question, possibly true. (c) The economics don't work given current launch costs and demand — parochial question about current state, with launch costs declining roughly 10x in fifteen years. The expert is almost certainly making (b) and (c), and packaging them as if they were (a). The founder owes themselves the unpacking.
**Result:** The founder isn't obliged to disagree with the expert — (b) and (c) might be persuasive. The discipline is to refuse to let (a) be smuggled in for free. If the expert can defend the claim that the *physics* forbids economically viable asteroid mining, that's a real argument. If they can defend only "the knowledge required is hard to create on a useful timescale," that's a different argument with a different decision attached.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "anything not forbidden by physics is achievable given the right knowledge" as a get-out-of-jail card for any plan.
**Why:** It's a structural claim about possibility, not about your specific company's specific roadmap on its specific timeline with its specific funding. "In principle achievable" is consistent with "your business will run out of money before achieving it." The framework gives you a foothold against bad pessimism; it does not pretend to give you a positive plan. Don't let the philosophical point do operational work.

**Don't:** Conflate the framework with "we'll figure it out" or any other slogan version of optimism.
**Why:** The interesting content of constructor theory is the *dichotomy* — possible-with-knowledge or physically-forbidden, and nothing else. Slogans like "we'll figure it out" trade on a vague hopefulness that constructor theory specifically refuses. The honest position is "this is permitted by physics, the relevant knowledge does not yet exist, here is our best guess at how to create it." The slogan skips the second and third clauses; the framework requires them.

**Don't:** Pretend the framework settles questions it doesn't.
**Why:** Constructor theory is, at the time of writing, a research programme. It is more developed in some areas (quantum theory, thermodynamics) than in others. The structural argument about possibility/impossibility doesn't depend on the research programme's success — that argument is essentially a consequence of taking the laws of physics seriously and asking "what would a third category even be?" — but the broader programme has not yet delivered its full promise. Don't oversell it. The operational use is much more limited than the metaphysical use, and the metaphysical use is plenty by itself.
