# The Landing Page Blueprint

## When to Use
- Building a landing page from scratch.
- Reviewing an existing page to find what's missing. Most broken pages are missing one or two of the ten elements.
- Teaching a team a shared structure so every page hits the same beats.

## Fails When
- **The content warrants a different shape.** Long-copy sales letters, manifesto pages, portfolio pages, and some brand sites play different rules. The blueprint is a default, not a law.
- **You mistake the structure for the substance.** A ten-element page built on generic positioning is still generic. Get the iceberg work right (see iceberg-positioning.md) before slotting elements into the frame.
- **You treat elements as quotas.** If you don't have real social proof yet, don't invent it to fill the slot. Better to leave a slot empty than fill it with wallpaper.

## Core Concept

A landing page is an argument. Structured for scanning. Donald Miller said a caveman should be able to glance at your page and immediately grunt back what you offer. He's right. Five seconds. That's what you get. If someone can't figure out what you do in five seconds, they leave. Doesn't matter how good the product is.

Ten elements. Five above the fold. Five below.

### Above the fold

What's visible before anyone scrolls. This is where you earn attention.

1. **Title.** Explain the value you provide.
2. **Subtitle.** Explain how you create it.
3. **Visual.** Let the user see the product.
4. **Social proof.** Make it believable.
5. **CTA.** Make the next step easy.

Five things. Each one does a specific job.

Take Privy. Title: "How small brands sell more online." Social proof: "18,000+ reviews." Any startup can write that title. But the 18,000 reviews make it believable. The title makes the promise. The social proof backs it up.

For the visual: show your product. Not a stock photo. Not an illustration of people shaking hands. Your actual product in action. Get as close to reality as possible.

For the CTA: "Sign Up" and "Start Trial" are functional. But there are better options. A call-to-value CTA emphasises what the customer gets. "Find my credits" beats "Get started." "See my results" beats "Sign up." An objection-handling CTA adds a few words: "Start free trial — no credit card required." Those last four words do heavy lifting. You can also pair an email capture field with your CTA. One field, one button. Fewer clicks between interest and action. Don't worry about collecting every piece of information upfront. You can do that during onboarding. The landing page's job is to get the first "yes."

In five seconds, customers try to establish whether you can help them. Make their life easy. Clarity over creativity.

### Below the fold

Above the fold you earn attention. Below the fold you earn the sale.

6. **Features and objections.** Make the value concrete.
7. **Social proof.** Inspire action.
8. **FAQ.** Tie up loose ends.
9. **Second CTA.** Repeat the call to action.
10. **Founder's note.** Make yourself memorable.

**Features that handle objections.** Your features section isn't a feature dump. It's an objection-handling machine. Talk to your customers. Group their recurring objections. Use their actual words. If people worry about setup time, show a feature that addresses setup. If they worry about data security, show the security feature. Every feature should answer an unspoken "but what about...?" Riverside promises "podcasts that look and sound amazing." Their first two features below the fold make that promise concrete: studio-quality recording and 4K video. The promise becomes proof. Don't list features for the sake of listing features. Each one should connect back to the headline promise or kill a specific objection.

**Social proof below the fold.** Credibility lives above the fold; inspiration lives below. Use existing customers to bring to life the value you promise. "Get a smile you love" becomes customers smiling. "Email reinvented" becomes customers describing the difference. "How small brands sell more" becomes real sales numbers.

**How to end a landing page.** Most pages just stop. Run out of content and slap on a footer. Three elements end properly:
- **Reiterate value.** Remind people why they're here. "Join hundreds of people doing more with email."
- **One last testimonial.** "I saw immediate results. I got more replies and closed more deals."
- **One last CTA.** Make the button easy. "Add to Gmail with one click."

By this point, you've done the hard selling. The bottom of the page is for people who read everything and need one final push.

**The founder's note.** Leave the customer with a story. Four steps:
1. Put yourself in their shoes.
2. Explain their problem.
3. Take ownership of it.
4. Show the happy ending.

You're walking them down a path they'll want to walk themselves. Sign it with your name. Add your photo. Be a human. People buy from people. Not logos. Not brands. People. (See headers.md for the full founder-story pattern.)

**Removing friction.** Three ways to make people more likely to click.
- **Add a timeframe.** "Get a quote in 2 minutes." Now I know what I'm committing to.
- **Drain objections.** "Fully insured. Licensed and vetted." The thing I was worried about — I'm not anymore.
- **Easy next step.** "Get a fast, free quote." Free. Fast. Two words that remove two barriers.

Lawn Love does all three at once. Timeframe: 2 minutes. Objection: fully insured. Next step: fast, free quote.

**The Blinkist lesson.** Number one objection to signing up for a free trial: "I'll forget to cancel and get billed indefinitely." 99% of sites sweep this under the rug. Blinkist handles it upfront. Maximum transparency. Tells you exactly when you'll be charged and how to cancel. Results: 23% more trial signups. 55% fewer customer complaints. Sincerity sells. The thing you're scared to say is often the thing that converts.

**Exit-intent popups.** Feel desperate. Work anyway. 50% of newsletter signups come from exit-intent popups. Don't apologise for capturing attention you've earned. Timing matters — wait until they're about to leave, not while they're reading. Think of a museum. You don't ask for email addresses at the entrance. You ask on the way out, after they've seen the exhibition.

**The final test.** Your landing page is your sales pitch. Examine each element and ask: would this help me sell if I met the customer in person? If not, remove it. If you don't know, go out and actually sell to customers in person. You'll learn that fancy words and stock photos don't get you far. More importantly, you'll learn the attitude of your customer and the words you need to convince them.

## How to Apply

1. **Above the fold first. Count the five elements.** Title. Subtitle. Visual. Social proof. CTA. If any are missing, add them before anything else. If any are generic, sharpen them.

2. **Title passes the three tests.** Title fails = page fails. Use the header patterns (headers.md) that fit.

3. **Visual shows the product working.** Real screenshot, real photo, real demo. Stock photos signal "we have nothing to show." Cut them.

4. **Above-fold social proof is a number or logo.** Not a long quote. A tight credibility anchor — "18,000 reviews," "trusted by 400 YC companies," "$2.7M in customer savings this year." One line. Above the fold isn't the place for narrative proof.

5. **Above-fold CTA is reward-shaped.** "Find my credits." "See my results." "Start my free trial — no credit card." Adding a timeframe ("in 2 minutes") or de-risk ("no card required") is almost always a win.

6. **Below the fold, each feature kills an objection.** Not a feature list. Talk to customers, identify the three or four recurring objections, build the features section around them. "What if setup is hard?" → setup feature. "What about data security?" → security feature.

7. **Below-fold social proof inspires.** Real customers in their own voices (see social-proof.md). Screenshots, photos, videos, quotes with typos intact. Customer voices doing the selling.

8. **Add an FAQ for the lingering doubts.** Common objections customers voice in sales calls, answered here. Don't hide the hard questions — address them (the Blinkist move).

9. **Close with a three-part ending.** Reiterate value + one last testimonial + one last CTA. Not a footer with links.

10. **Sign the page.** Founder's note with photo and name. People buy from people.

11. **Add an exit-intent popup.** Catchy outcome + specific offer. "Stop killing your plants. Free 10-lesson houseplant parenting course."

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS landing page with "Transform your workflow. Get started today." as the hero, a stock photo of a team, no social proof above the fold, and an 8-item feature list below.
**Application:** Rewrite the title: "Run your standups async in five minutes." Add subtitle: "For distributed eng teams of 5–50." Replace stock photo with a product screenshot showing the standup interface. Add above-fold social proof: "247 eng teams at YC companies use this." CTA: "Try it with your team — free for 14 days, no card required." Below the fold, regroup the 8 features into 3 objection-answering sections. Add raw Slack-message screenshots as social proof. Add an FAQ handling the two biggest objections from sales calls. End with reiterate/testimonial/CTA. Sign with a founder's note from the named founder.
**Result:** Every element earns its place. The page stops looking like a 2014 SaaS template and starts looking like a sales pitch someone would actually sign off on.

**Situation:** A consumer product landing page with two headings ("Our Story" and "Our Products") and one "Buy Now" button.
**Application:** The page is missing 8 of the 10 elements. Above the fold, none of title/subtitle/visual/social proof/CTA meet spec. Rebuild from blueprint — concrete title, specific subtitle, photo of product in use, numeric social proof, reward-shaped CTA. Below fold, introduce features-that-kill-objections, real customer photos, FAQ, second CTA, signed founder's note.
**Result:** The blueprint acts as diagnostic — shows exactly what's missing. Fastest way to rebuild a broken page is to rebuild from ten slots, not to rewrite in place.

**Situation:** A B2B landing page with a 2,000-word manifesto above the fold and everything else below.
**Application:** Respect that the page is doing a manifesto. HEY works this way. But compress the manifesto into a tight hero (title + subtitle) with the rest of the narrative below the fold. Social proof (credibility) still needs to sit in the hero. The manifesto moves to a below-fold section labelled "Why we built this."
**Result:** Readers who want the manifesto find it one scroll down. Readers who want the five-second summary get it instantly. Both audiences are served.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "Sign Up" as your CTA.
**Why:** It describes an action, not a reward. Every other website on earth has a "Sign Up" button. Replace with what the user gets: "Send me Lesson 1." "Find my tax credits." "Start trial — no card required." Reward beats action every time.

**Don't:** List features without connecting each to an objection.
**Why:** A feature list is a spec sheet. An objection-answering feature section is a sales pitch. Same information, completely different effect. If you can't name the objection each feature kills, you haven't done the customer research yet.

**Don't:** Put your testimonials in a carousel.
**Why:** Carousels require a click to see the next testimonial. Users don't click. They scroll past. Put three or four testimonials on the page in sequence. Let them be seen without interaction. Carousels are a common "professional-looking" choice that reduces the effective proof the page delivers.

**Don't:** Hide cancellation terms or refund policies in the footer.
**Why:** The Blinkist lesson. The thing customers are scared about is the thing they want clarified. Putting it upfront increases trust and conversion. Sweeping it under the rug is the default move that costs trial signups.

**Don't:** End the page with a footer and call it done.
**Why:** Pages that just "stop" lose the reader at the exact moment of highest intent (they read to the end — they're interested). A three-part closing (reiterate value + testimonial + CTA) captures that intent. Adding a founder's note creates the human moment that brand copy can't.
