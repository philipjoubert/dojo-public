# Abstract Word Bingo

## When to Use
- Diagnosing why a page feels generic. Nine times out of ten, it's stuffed with abstract words that fail all three tests.
- Training yourself or a team to spot corporate wallpaper in drafts before it ships.
- Running a final pass on copy before publishing — search for the hit-list words and interrogate each one.

## Fails When
- **The abstract word is a deliberate setup.** "Luxury — the kind your grandmother handed down" uses an abstract term before collapsing into concrete specificity. Judge the full line, not the fragment.
- **A specific abstract term has become a specific technical term.** "Ecosystem" is wallpaper when applied to SaaS but might be the correct, specific term in biology. "Solution" is platitude in marketing but the precise word in chemistry. Context-check.
- **You cut the abstract word and leave behind gibberish.** Deleting "seamless" without rewriting the sentence often leaves a broken line. The goal is replacement, not amputation.

## Core Concept

The easiest copy to write is the copy that means nothing.

I made a bingo card. "Seamless transition." "Essential skills to excel." "A better way." "Take it to the next level." "Unlock your potential." Play it at your next marketing meeting. You'll have bingo in ten minutes. Five in a row? That's the whole bottle of pickle juice.

George Orwell called this out in 1946. He described it as "gumming together long strips of words which have already been set in order by somebody else." He compared it to "pre-fabricated words tacked together like sections of a pre-fabricated hen house." Eighty years later, we're still doing it. The difference is now we're spending billions.

**Why people write like this.** Abstract language is easy. No research. No decisions. No commitment. "We deliver innovative solutions to drive growth." Three seconds to write. Means absolutely nothing. Why people do it: it's safe. Vague copy can't be wrong. If you say you're "cutting-edge" or "best-in-class," nobody can prove you aren't. No fact to check. Just word-shaped air. When everyone writes "seamless transition," it feels professional to write "seamless transition." But that feeling is a trap. Freedom Pay — an 18 billion pound company — their copy says "take your payments to the next level." Type that phrase into Google in curly quotes. You'll get hundreds of results. Hundreds of companies. Same six words. The attraction isn't effectiveness. It's convenience.

**The three-test failure.** Abstract language fails all three tests. Every time.

Take "a better way." Can I visualise it? No. Close your eyes. Nothing to see. Can I falsify it? No. Better than what? By what measure? Can nobody else say this? No. Every company in history can claim "a better way." Three nos. You've written rubbish.

Try "seamless transition." Drop it on your foot. You can't. It has no mass. What would prove a transition was seamless? No bar to clear. Can nobody else say it? Every competitor claims it. If you're writing words anyone could say about anything, you're not writing. You're typing.

**The hit list.** Words to interrogate every time they appear:
- **Seamless.** Every company claims it. Emptied of meaning.
- **Innovative.** If everyone is innovative, no one is.
- **Solutions.** As opposed to what? Problems?
- **Leverage.** "We leverage our expertise" means "we use what we know." Just say that.
- **Empower.** Sounds important. Means nothing.
- **Cutting-edge.** The edge of what? Unprovable.
- **Next-level.** What level? Video game language applied to business.
- **World-class.** According to whom?
- **Best-in-class.** Which class? Judged by what?
- **360 view.** Close your eyes. What does that look like? Nothing.
- **Transform.** Into what? By when? Says nothing.
- **Deliver results.** As opposed to delivering no results?
- **Award-winning.** Which award? Who cares?
- **Unlock your potential.** Can't see it. Can't falsify it. Anyone can say it.
- **A better way.** Better than what? By what measure?
- **Take it to the next level.** Hundreds of companies. Same six words.
- **Essential skills to excel.** Durham, Plymouth, and Keele University all wrote this. Word for word.
- **Drive growth.** Sounds busy. Means nothing.
- **Optimise.** Optimise what? For whom? By how much?
- **Scalable.** Everything is scalable if you squint.
- **Streamline.** The corporate word for "make slightly less annoying."
- **Holistic.** A word that makes any sentence immediately suspicious.
- **Synergy.** The original offender.
- **Ecosystem.** You sell software. Not a rainforest.
- **End-to-end.** Which end? What end?

Quick test. Close your eyes. Can you see anything? "360 view of my business." What does that look like? Nothing. These words exist to sound like something. That's their only function.

**When everyone sounds the same.** Compared two mobile network websites once. O2 and Vodafone. Could not tell the difference. Swapped the logos. Still worked. Durham, Plymouth, Keele — three universities, same sentence, word for word. Cambridge is not Cardiff. Edinburgh is not Exeter. But you wouldn't know it from their marketing. Abstract language erases differentiation. Your copy is interchangeable. So you become interchangeable.

**The Google curly-quotes test.** Want to know if your copy is generic? Take your headline. Put it in curly quotes. Type it into Google. If other companies show up using the same phrase, you haven't written anything. You've assembled. "Take your payments to the next level." Hundreds of results. "Point it at the sky. Plug it in." One result. Starlink. One of these is copywriting. The other is wallpaper.

**Why companies write like this.** Abstract copy survives approval processes. When you write something specific — "We respond to support tickets within 4 hours" — someone has to verify that's true. Legal gets involved. Operations weighs in. But "best-in-class customer service"? Nobody can argue. It sails through review. Abstract language is committee-proof. That's its real appeal. Not effectiveness. Deniability. I've watched it happen. "Delivered within 24 hours" becomes "fast delivery" becomes "quick shipping" becomes "convenient options." Each round files down the edges. By launch, abstract mush. The fix isn't better writers. It's braver organisations.

**The real problem.** Abstract language is often a symptom, not a cause. People write "seamless solutions" because they haven't figured out what they're selling. It's a thinking problem, not a word-choice problem. If you know what your product does, for whom, and why it's different, the words come easily. But if you don't? You hide. "Innovative solutions for the modern enterprise." Everyone nods because nobody knows what else to do. The fix isn't wordsmithing. The fix is thinking.

**What abstract language costs you.**
- **Memory.** People don't remember it. "Seamless transition" enters one ear and exits the other.
- **Differentiation.** If everyone can say it, you become invisible.
- **Trust.** Readers sense empty language. Vague copy creates vague trust.
- **Conversion.** Confused people don't buy.

Every abstract word is a missed opportunity to say something real.

## How to Apply

1. **Search your homepage for the hit-list words.** Ctrl+F for: seamless, innovative, solutions, leverage, empower, cutting-edge, next-level, world-class, best-in-class, transform, unlock, drive growth, optimise, streamline, holistic, synergy, ecosystem, end-to-end, 360, deliver results, award-winning, essential, take your X to the next level.

2. **For each hit, ask three questions.**
   - Prove it to who?
   - Compared to what?
   - According to what metric?
   If you can't answer, the word is worthless.

3. **Run the zoom-in.** Abstract word at the top of the page. What do I actually mean? Keep zooming until concrete (see zoom-in.md).

4. **Replace with a number, a customer, a screenshot, or a real verb.**
   - "Fast delivery" → "ships before noon, arrives same day."
   - "Experienced team" → "47 years combined, 200 projects."
   - "High satisfaction" → "94% buy again within 12 months."

5. **Run the Google curly-quotes test on your revised headline.** Hundreds of results means you're still in the parts bin. Keep working until the results thin out to one or zero.

6. **Read aloud. Would you say this at a pub?** (See doorstep-test.md.) If not, delete. The spoken-word test is a cross-check on abstract words — we rarely use them in speech.

7. **Check whether the word is carrying any weight in the sentence.** Often abstract words are filler — delete and the sentence gets better immediately. "Seamless integration with Salesforce" → "Integration with Salesforce" is already cleaner. "Works with Salesforce" is cleaner still.

## Examples

**Situation:** A marketing agency homepage: "We deliver innovative, award-winning, world-class solutions to drive growth for ambitious brands."
**Application:** Bingo. Five hits in one sentence. Interrogate each. "Innovative" — how? "Award-winning" — which award, who cares? "World-class" — according to whom? "Solutions" — deleted, meaningless. "Drive growth" — drive what growth, how measured? Rewrite: "We rewrote three B2B SaaS homepages last quarter. Two doubled their trial-signup rate within six weeks." Specific. Falsifiable. Unsignable by a competitor who doesn't have those results.
**Result:** The five-hit bingo becomes a single specific proof. Same promise, completely different credibility.

**Situation:** A SaaS product: "A seamless, end-to-end platform that empowers modern teams to unlock their full potential."
**Application:** Five bingo hits. Close eyes on each — nothing visible. Replace: "We run your team's standup, async, in five minutes. No meeting. No monologue. No missing anyone." Concrete. Picturable. Nobody else's.
**Result:** The generic sentence was performing the "nothing" all abstract language performs. A rewrite with specific nouns and verbs creates a page that passes the three tests.

**Situation:** A university: "Essential skills to excel."
**Application:** Famous case — Durham, Plymouth, and Keele all wrote this word for word. Zero differentiation. Interrogate: essential skills like what? Python? Public speaking? Financial modelling? Excel at what? Getting a job? Starting a company? Passing the next exam? Rewrite depends on the truthful answer. Possible rewrite for one of them: "We built a job-placement service that places 87% of our graduates within 90 days of finishing. Here's how."
**Result:** A unique, verifiable, specific claim. The original copy was three universities in a parts-bin queue.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Replace abstract words with longer abstract words.
**Why:** "Seamless" becoming "frictionless" is a lateral move, not a fix. Both fail all three tests. Replace with nouns, numbers, or verbs a customer would actually use.

**Don't:** Leave an abstract word just because the team is attached to it.
**Why:** "We've always said 'empower teams'" isn't a reason. Affection for a word is not evidence that it works. If it fails three tests, it fails, regardless of how long it's been in the deck.

**Don't:** Default to "we" and "our" with abstract pronouns.
**Why:** "Our solutions." "Our platform." "Our mission." Abstract-noun phrases starting with "our" are a stylistic tell. Replace "Our platform delivers X" with "You get X." The doorstep-voice rewrite shifts the subject from vague "our" to the reader.

**Don't:** Pretend industry-jargon abstracts are concrete.
**Why:** "Best-in-class observability" is two abstract words stacked. Observability is concrete in a narrow technical sense, but "best-in-class" erases that. "Trace every request from browser to database in under 50ms" is actually concrete. Don't let industry vocabulary smuggle abstraction past the three tests.

**Don't:** Stop at removing the words without fixing the thinking underneath.
**Why:** Abstract copy is usually a symptom. If the rewrite lands on a fact you don't actually have, you've discovered an iceberg problem. Go back to positioning (see iceberg-positioning.md). The goal of abstract-word-bingo isn't to scrub the homepage — it's to find the holes in the thinking the words were hiding.
