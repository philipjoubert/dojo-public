# Individual Over Institution

## When to Use
- Any time a founder is debating "should I put my face on this?" The math is overwhelmingly yes, and the math should be explicit.
- Any company trying to make their corporate social account "quirky" or "have personality" — redirect effort to the actual individuals.
- Founders uncertain about building a personal brand alongside the company brand. The personal brand is a moat the company brand can't build.

## Fails When
- **Regulated industries.** Healthcare, finance, legal products sometimes require the institutional voice for trust and compliance. The founder can still be the face, but within constraints.
- **Safety-sensitive contexts.** High-profile founders in certain domains take real risk putting their face forward. Judge case-by-case.
- **Multi-founder companies where elevation creates internal friction.** Either both founders go direct (Basecamp — Jason Fried and DHH), or neither. Elevating one as "the voice" while sidelining others often backfires.

## Core Concept

People follow people. They don't follow logos.

Quick question. How many brands do you follow on Instagram? Probably a handful. Maybe Nike. Maybe a coffee shop you like. Now — how many people? Cristiano Ronaldo alone has 600 million followers. More than most countries have people.

**Ben Foster vs Watford FC.** Ben Foster, the Watford goalkeeper, started a YouTube channel. Hit 1 million subscribers in 14 months. Watford FC — the actual club he plays for — took 10 years to reach 170,000. Same audience. Same sport. Same city. One is a person. The other is a brand. Ben: budget basically nothing, crew himself. Watford: entire media department, full-time social media managers, decades of brand history, professional equipment. Time to 1M: 14 months. Time to 170K: 10 years. Six times more people in one-tenth of the time. He showed what happens in the dressing room before matches. The weird rituals. The nervous energy. The banter. Talked directly to camera like you were his mate. Watford's content was polished and professional and completely forgettable. Interview clips with corporate backdrops. Highlight reels with licensed music. Professional beats personality, right? Wrong.

**Tesla: zero ad budget.** Tesla has never run a traditional ad. Zero advertising budget. Instead they turned customers into fans. The car makes fart noises. Their CEO makes memes. And simultaneously, they have the best engineers in the world working on the fossil fuel crisis. That juxtaposition is captivating. Competitors spend hundreds of thousands for a 30-second commercial during a basketball game. Tesla sits back and watches fans make YouTube videos for them. "Why I Bought the New Cybertruck" — made by a real person. Far more sincere than any commercial could be. One fan video: 6.9 million views. Even at a tiny conversion rate, that's tens of millions in revenue. From one video Tesla didn't make. The lesson: if the best salespeople are your customers, marketing should be less about attracting eyeballs and more about building relationships. Instead of asking "how many views?" ask "how many fans?"

**Sign your work.** Melissa Ramos has a health website. Her headline: "I help women regulate their hormones for clearer skin, happier periods, ageless beauty, healthier ovaries (okay, AND I talk about poop too.)" That parenthetical. That's a person. No brand guidelines committee would approve "I talk about poop too." But it works because it's real. It's her. Every Marketing Examples newsletter ends the same way: "Over and out." Not clever. Just a signature. But it matters. Most marketing comes from "The Team." Anonymous. Corporate. Forgettable. When you sign your work, you're making a promise. This came from a real person. A person you can reply to.

**Why institutions can't compete.** Corporations have noticed individuals winning. So they've tried to compete. Corporate Twitter accounts trying to be "quirky." Brands trying to have "personality." Almost never works. Personality can't be manufactured by committee. Personality comes from risk. From saying something that might not land. From opinions some people will hate. Corporate accounts can't take those risks. There's always a review process that sands down every edge. What's left is uncanny valley. A brand pretending to be a person. Quirky language with none of the actual quirkiness. Smart companies don't try to compete with individuals. They hire them. You can't fake being a person. You can only be one.

**Basecamp gets this right.** Basecamp — project management software — could be interchangeable with a dozen competitors. But they've got Jason Fried and DHH. Two actual people. With actual opinions. Who write books. Who start arguments on Twitter. You might hate their philosophy — remote work only, small teams, no venture capital. But you know who they are. Compare to any other project management tool. What do they believe? What would their founders argue about? Basecamp spent a few thousand on a book called "Rework." Everyone who's read it knows exactly what they stand for. Individual beats institution. Every time.

**The personal brand moat.** Your personal brand is a moat. Nobody can copy you being you. Companies can copy your features. Your pricing. Your marketing tactics. They can't copy your face. Your voice. Your origin story. Yeezy Dating — a dating site for Kanye West fans in 2017. 45,000 signups. Made about twelve quid. Complete failure by any business measure. But it taught me real marketing. The desperate, figure-it-out-or-die kind. That failure is my credential. Not a degree. Not a corporate job. People trust me because of that failure, not despite it. Because it's real. Because it's specific. I could write down every principle of Marketing Examples. Give it to a competitor. They still couldn't copy it. Because the thing that makes it work is that it's from me. Specific human. Years of consistent content. Thousands of replies.

**The question to ask yourself.** Would someone follow me personally? Or just my company? If they'd only follow your company, you're vulnerable. Companies are interchangeable. Companies get sold. Companies die. If they'd follow you personally, you have something durable. You'll always be you. No matter where you work. If Marketing Examples disappeared tomorrow, would people still want to hear from me? I think so. They're not following a website. They're following a person who happens to run one. Brand assets can be acquired. Replicated. Competed away. Personal brands are you. Nobody can compete away you.

## How to Apply

1. **Put your face on the content.** Not a logo. Your actual face. A real photo. Not a stylised illustration. Real.

2. **Write a one-paragraph origin story.** The failure that led you here. Yeezy Dating. The thing you tried that didn't work. The lesson from the wreckage. Specific human stories create the trust that corporate copy can't.

3. **Sign your work with your name.** Not "The Team." Not "Marketing Team." Your actual name. On newsletters. On blog posts. On emails. On announcements.

4. **Reply to everyone who engages with you.** Twitter replies. Email responses. Comments. The act of replying is the signal that there's a real person on the other end. Auto-responders defeat the purpose.

5. **Take opinions committees would reject.** An opinion some people will disagree with is the currency of personal brands. "Remote work is better." "Founders should own sales." "Most VCs don't add value." Whatever you actually believe. The opinion has to be yours and it has to have an edge.

6. **Let the company reflect the individual.** Basecamp reflects Jason and DHH. Marketing Examples reflects Harry. Your company should reflect you. If you're trying to make a faceless corporate brand while being a specific person, you're fighting both sides of your mouth.

7. **Don't try to make the company account "personal."** The corporate brand and the personal brand are separate. Keep them separate. The company account can share the individual's content, amplify the individual's voice — but it shouldn't try to fake personhood. That way lies uncanny valley.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS company has a dormant corporate Twitter with 500 followers. The founder has a personal account with 20K followers and an engaged audience.
**Application:** Stop investing in the corporate account. Invest everything in the founder's personal account. Let the founder be the voice of the company. Company account retweets, amplifies, and stays low-key — it's the archive, not the voice. New customers come through the founder's content, not through a branded feed.
**Result:** The 20K following is the real asset. Watford-vs-Ben-Foster math applies at company scale: professional brand accounts are slower, costlier, and less trusted than a real person.

**Situation:** A founder's landing page says "We are a team of passionate engineers building the future of finance."
**Application:** "We" is the corporate voice. Rewrite as first person from a specific founder. "I'm Anna. In 2022 I was laid off from a fintech that ran out of runway. I couldn't find a neobank that treated its customers like adults. So I built one. Here's what we're doing —" Add a photo. Sign the page. Founder's note with contact info.
**Result:** The customer now knows who to trust. "We" was plural and anonymous; "I'm Anna" is singular and responsible. Trust follows specificity.

**Situation:** An e-commerce brand is trying to grow social presence and is spending heavily on polished video ads.
**Application:** Switch budget to seeding customers as creators. Tesla's move. Ship the product to 20 real customers with a camera, a prompt, and zero creative direction. Let them make one video each. The best three will outperform the polished agency video. Also: put the founder in front of the camera for a weekly behind-the-scenes channel.
**Result:** Real humans making real videos out-perform corporate-produced content, as Tesla proved at fleet scale. The e-commerce brand gains a flywheel of customer content and a founder presence the category doesn't have.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to make the corporate account "have personality."
**Why:** It can't. The committee review will strip edge. The "quirky brand voice" always reads as corporate cosplay. Put the personality in the individual who actually has one, and keep the corporate account clean and institutional.

**Don't:** Build a personal brand that's disconnected from the company.
**Why:** Harry-the-brand and Marketing-Examples-the-company are aligned. If your personal brand is "AI tips for founders" and your company is a dog-walking service, the audience isn't transferrable. The personal brand should compound into the company, or at least not contradict it.

**Don't:** Sign team-produced work with one person's name dishonestly.
**Why:** If a ghostwriter wrote it, saying "Harry wrote this" corrodes the trust the personal brand depends on. People notice. "With contributions from [team]" is fine. Wholesale ghostwriting under a personal byline is a problem waiting to happen.

**Don't:** Elevate one co-founder above the other silently.
**Why:** Either both are the voice (Basecamp's Jason and DHH model), or one is and the other is explicitly behind-the-scenes by agreement. Ambiguity creates internal friction that shows up externally as mixed signals. Decide together, publicly.

**Don't:** Replace the personal voice with an agency-written personal voice.
**Why:** The moat is *you* being *you*. An agency writing "in your voice" is structurally the committee problem. Reply to emails yourself. Write threads yourself. Choose what to share yourself. The voice is the product.
