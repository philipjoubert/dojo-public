# Talk to the Dog

## When to Use
- Any copy where the reader is a real human making a choice — homepages, landing pages, ads, emails, onboarding, CTAs, founder notes.
- When the substance is right but the copy lands cold — features all correct, value prop clear, nobody cares.
- Rewriting a page that reads like an internal document leaked to the public. "We're excited to announce…" / "Our platform provides…" / "Welcome to the future of…"
- Training a team to stop writing at the customer and start writing to the customer.

## Fails When
- **The dog isn't the problem.** If your iceberg is empty, dog-language doesn't help. Writing warmer sentences about nothing still leaves you with nothing. Fix positioning first (see iceberg-positioning.md). Dog-language is voice; substance is separate.
- **The buyer actually wants the spec.** Some technical buyers want the version number, the uptime SLA, and the regex syntax, not a warm human story. Respect that. The dog's language for a dev-tools buyer IS the spec. Read the customer, not the genre convention.
- **Regulated copy.** Financial products, medical claims, and compliance-heavy pages have mandated language. The dog-test still applies inside those constraints — but the baseline shifts.

## Core Concept

David Ogilvy's rule:

> "Talk to the dog, in the language of the dog, about what matters to the dog's heart."

Three moves in one sentence.

Talk **to** the dog — not at it, not over it, not around it. Not to the dog's owner. Not to your boss. Not to the reviewer who has to approve the copy. To the dog.

In the **language** of the dog — the words it uses. Not yours. Not your industry's. Not the words that sound professional to your marketing team. The words the customer would actually say if it were describing the problem out loud.

About what matters to the **heart** — the thing it actually cares about. Not the thing you wish it cared about. Not the feature you're proud of. The thing that would make it sit up and pay attention.

That's the whole job. Most marketing fails all three.

**Who are you actually writing for?** Go look at your homepage. Who is it written for? Honestly. Is it written for a customer? Or is it written for your CEO, who'll approve the words? For your investors, who'll see it on pitch day? For the analyst who'll quote it in a deck? For your own team, so nobody argues?

Most copy is written for internal audiences that pretend to be external. "We're thrilled to announce our groundbreaking new platform" is what you'd write if the Chairman is in the room. "Stop copy-pasting between tools" is what you'd write if a frustrated operations manager is in the room.

Pick the right room. The room has one person in it. The customer. Everyone else is out.

**The language of the dog.** Your customer has words. Specific ones. The ones they use when they're complaining to a friend. The ones they use in the support ticket. The ones they wrote in the G2 review. Those are the words that land — because they're already in the customer's head. You're not asking them to translate. You're meeting them where they are.

"Email started as a treasure. Now it's a chore." HEY wrote that. It's the customer's language. Nobody opens Gmail and thinks "email lacks a delightful, intentional experience." They think it's a chore. The copy meets them in the word they already use.

"Stop killing your plants." Patch Plants' exit popup. Six words. "Stop killing your plants" is what the customer says out loud when another fiddle-leaf fig goes brown. Not "improve your plant care outcomes." Not "optimise indoor horticulture." Kill. Plants. Six words, exactly the ones in the reader's head.

**The heart of the dog.** Ogilvy's third move — about what matters. This is where most B2B copy dies. The company writes about what matters to the company. Feature shipped. Milestone hit. Award won. Platform extended. Series B closed.

The customer's heart isn't moved by any of that.

What moves it: the thing they lie awake at night worrying about. The frustration they had three hours ago. The fear they won't admit out loud. The status they're quietly trying to achieve. The boss they're trying to impress. The promise they made they're scared they can't keep.

Volvo didn't sell "engineering excellence." They sold: "Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six." The customer's heart cares about the car outlasting them. The feature is just the proof.

Starlink didn't sell "satellite internet connectivity solutions." They sold: "Point it at the sky. Plug it in. Directions work in either order." The customer's heart cares about not being humiliated by the installation manual. The copy is calibrated to that specific fear.

**The CEO's version vs the dog's version.** Here's the test. Take a line of copy. Write two versions.

- The CEO version: what would sound good being read out at an all-hands.
- The dog version: what would sound like one human telling another human the truth.

"We're proud to launch a transformative new platform that empowers teams to unlock unprecedented productivity gains across the entire enterprise." All-hands version. Gets claps. Converts nothing.

"Your team wastes four hours a week in status meetings. Not any more." Dog version. Gets clicks. Converts.

The CEO version exists to be approved. The dog version exists to be read. These are different documents. Do not confuse them.

**Empathy before grammar.** You cannot write for the dog if you don't know the dog. Most "tone of voice" problems are actually empathy problems — the writer hasn't sat with a customer long enough to know what matters to them.

Go sit with one. Read the reviews. Read the support tickets. Listen to the sales calls. Find five phrases they use that you've never used. Those phrases are the language of the dog. Put them on the page.

**Three places to find the dog's language.**

1. **Your CRM and support tickets.** What phrases recur when customers describe the problem they came to you with? Those are the words. Not the words they used after you trained them — the words they came in with.

2. **Reviews and testimonials (unedited).** G2, Trustpilot, App Store reviews, Product Hunt comments. The bits the customer wrote when they were being honest. Not the case study where your marketing team cleaned it up. The raw stuff.

3. **Sales call recordings.** Listen for the sentences customers say back to your rep. "Oh — so it's like a shared inbox but you don't have to forward everything?" That's a positioning line, written by the customer. Steal it word for word.

**The Smalls line.** Dog food. They wrote: "Smalls is cooked gently, like it would be in your own kitchen." Dog's language (kitchen, cooked, gently). Dog's heart (I want to feed my pet the way I'd cook for myself). Compare it to a spec-sheet competitor: "Premium high-protein nutrition for discerning pets." Nobody's heart is in "discerning pets." Nobody cooks in "nutrition." Smalls wins because the line meets the customer where they already are.

**Write for one person. Name them.** When you can't find the dog, invent it. Not "small businesses." One person. Jess, who runs a two-person marketing agency in Leeds, just lost her biggest client, and needs to replace £4,000 of monthly revenue in six weeks. Write to Jess.

"We help small businesses grow" is written for nobody. "You lost a client. You need another one by next quarter. Here's how agencies like yours filled the gap" is written for Jess. Jess reads it. Jess clicks. The hypothetical small business does nothing because it isn't real.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the dog.** Who, specifically, is this copy for? Name them. If your answer is "anyone who" or "SMBs" or "teams who want to scale," you haven't named them yet. Keep zooming in until you have a person with a job, a problem, and a week-to-week frustration.

2. **Write down five phrases they actually use.** Pull from support tickets, reviews, call transcripts, Slack channels, subreddits. Not cleaned up. The raw phrasing. "I'm drowning in tabs." "My Slack never stops." "I spent Saturday reconciling receipts." Those phrases are gold.

3. **Find the heart.** What's the thing they're genuinely worried about? Not the feature-level pain. The underlying fear. Being seen as incompetent. Missing a deadline. Embarrassing themselves in front of the boss. Losing control. Looking cheap. The heart is usually emotional, not functional.

4. **Rewrite the headline to land one of the five phrases near one of the heart-level fears.** If your customer says "I'm drowning in tabs" and their fear is looking disorganised at work, write something closer to that. "Arc keeps your 40 open tabs out of the way. So your boss doesn't have to see your browser." Dog's words. Dog's fear.

5. **Run the audience test on every paragraph.** Ask: who is this paragraph for? If the answer is "our CEO" or "our investors" or "whoever's reviewing this on Monday," delete. Rewrite for the customer. You can write a different document for the CEO. This one is for the dog.

6. **Replace "we" and "our" with "you" and "your" wherever possible.** "Our platform helps your team collaborate" → "Your team stops losing files." The grammatical subject shifts from your company to their world. That shift IS empathy in sentence form.

7. **Read it to a customer.** Not a colleague. A customer. If they smile, lean in, or start saying "yes that's exactly it" — the dog recognised its own language. If they sit politely and nod, it's still CEO copy.

8. **Check every claim against the dog's heart.** For each line, ask: would this matter to Jess? Not "is this technically true." Not "is this brand-on." Would Jess, lying awake at 2am worrying about the client she lost, care about this sentence? If no, cut it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS homepage for a team-inbox product: "We're building the future of collaborative communication with an integrated platform that empowers modern teams to unlock seamless productivity across workflows."
**Application:** Ogilvy three-move test. Who's the dog? A customer support lead drowning in forwarded emails, worried about missing a VIP ticket. What's the dog's language? "Forwarding emails." "Tickets falling through the cracks." "Who replied to this one?" What's the dog's heart? Not missing the important message. Not looking incompetent when a VIP complains on Twitter. Rewrite: "Stop forwarding emails. Your team sees every reply, and no ticket falls through the cracks." Dog's words (forwarding, tickets, falling through cracks). Dog's heart (the fear of missing the important one).
**Result:** The "future of communication" line was written for an investor deck. The rewrite is written for the support lead. The substance didn't change — the audience did.

**Situation:** A fintech landing page for freelancers: "A modern financial platform built for the new economy." The founder insists this is "our differentiator."
**Application:** Neither dog's language nor dog's heart. "New economy" is a CEO word, not a freelancer word. Ask the founder: what does a freelancer say out loud when their tax return is due? "I've got five tabs open, three spreadsheets, and I'm crying into a mug of tea." What's their heart? Not being humiliated by HMRC. Rewrite: "Tax time without the panic. We pull your invoices in, find the deductions, and hand you a return you can file in ten minutes." Freelancer words (tax time, panic, invoices, deductions). Freelancer heart (humiliation of HMRC). Zero "new economy."
**Result:** The "new economy" version makes the founder feel innovative. The freelancer version makes the freelancer buy. Pick the room with one person in it.

**Situation:** A careers page: "Join our mission to transform the industry through innovative, customer-obsessed work that drives impact at scale."
**Application:** Who's the dog? An engineer considering whether to leave their current job. What's their language? "Shipping product." "Good colleagues." "Not being bored." "Getting paid properly." What's their heart? Not wasting another year. Not working for the kind of company they'll be embarrassed about at their next interview. Rewrite: "You'll ship something you can point at on day 90. Your manager writes code. The last four engineers we hired are still here." Engineer words. Engineer heart (tangible work, technical manager, retention as proof of culture).
**Result:** Careers-page mission statements are always written for HR, not engineers. Engineers never read them. Rewriting for the dog gets engineers to read. The "mission" wording didn't move anyone; the three concrete sentences do.

**Situation:** A founder writes: "Excited to announce our Series B. This milestone reflects the incredible journey our team has been on and the trust our investors have placed in us."
**Application:** Who's the dog here? The founder is writing this for three audiences — investors, team, customers — and landing with none of them, because the language is generic congratulations-speak. Split it. If it's for customers, rewrite for customer heart: "We just raised money so we can ship twice as fast. Here's what's going in the product first." If it's for recruits, rewrite for engineer heart: "We can now hire ten more engineers. Come ship with us." If it's for investors, write the investor version separately in the investor update — keep it out of the customer-facing homepage.
**Result:** "Series B" celebrations that live on homepages are CEO-voice pollution. The dog (customer) doesn't care about your Series B. It cares whether your next feature makes its life better. Rewriting for the dog's heart turns a congratulations post into a commitment post.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write for the customer you wish you had.
**Why:** Aspirational copy — "for ambitious, forward-thinking leaders who believe in transformation" — is writing to a phantom. Real customers don't describe themselves that way. Write to the customer you actually have. Their words. Their fears. Not the idealised version you want to attract on a vision board.

**Don't:** Mistake "professional" for "right."
**Why:** Teams push copy toward "professional" under corporate review. Professional is often code for "nobody will complain." Nobody complains because nobody cares. The customer isn't looking for professional. The customer is looking for a human on the other end of the page. (See doorstep-test.md.)

**Don't:** Write for the CEO's approval and call it customer copy.
**Why:** If the CEO reads the page and says "yes, this is the vision" — it's CEO copy. Good for the all-hands, bad for the homepage. Test it against a customer, not an executive.

**Don't:** Steal the customer's words without understanding the context.
**Why:** Grabbing a phrase from one G2 review and pasting it on your homepage without the rest of the review's frustration context can sound off. "I was drowning" works if the page also signals you understand why. Copying phrases out of context produces a collage, not a conversation.

**Don't:** Use empathy as a substitute for substance.
**Why:** You can write the most emotionally attuned copy in the world, but if your product is the same as six competitors, empathy alone won't save you. Dog-language makes a real claim land. It doesn't manufacture a claim. Sort the iceberg first (see iceberg-positioning.md).

**Don't:** Confuse flattery with empathy.
**Why:** "You're brilliant. You're ambitious. You're a leader." That's not talking to the dog — that's patting it. Empathy is knowing what the dog actually fears, not complimenting it into clicking. Customers can smell flattery; it comes off as sales theatre.

**Don't:** Let brand-voice guidelines override the dog.
**Why:** Some brand guidelines mandate an "inspirational, confident, visionary" tone. That tone, applied universally, turns into Troy McClure copy fast (see doorstep-test.md). The tone should bend to the customer's register, not to a document written in a workshop three years ago. If the guidelines fight the dog, the guidelines are wrong for that page.
