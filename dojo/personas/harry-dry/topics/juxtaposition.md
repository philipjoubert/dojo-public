# Juxtaposition

## When to Use
- Any single-pole claim: "we're fast," "we're reliable," "we're simple," "we deliver results." These are invisible until you add a cold tap to the hot tap.
- Pricing copy. Numbers alone don't sell. Numbers next to other numbers do.
- Before/after transformations. Where the customer is now vs. where they could be.
- Category-level claims that are true but generic. Adding the contrast surfaces what's actually different.

## Fails When
- **There is no honest cold tap.** If you're going to compare your price to a competitor's, pick the competitor customers actually consider — not the most expensive one in the market. Cherry-picking erodes trust the first time anyone checks.
- **The cold tap is weaker than the hot tap deserves.** "Most software takes a while to learn. Ours takes less." That's not juxtaposition; that's mush on both sides. The cold tap has to be specific.
- **You keep both poles abstract.** "Before: chaos. After: calm." Both sides failed test 1 (visualise). The magic of juxtaposition is two concrete images side by side, not two abstractions glued together.

## Core Concept

Eugene Schwartz said all writing is just a search for juxtaposition. He was right.

Think about your kitchen sink. Two taps. Hot and cold. You only know the water's hot because of the cold tap. Without the cold, hot means nothing. It's just water.

Copy works the same way. Single-pole statements don't stick. "We're great." "We deliver results." "We're the best." There's nothing to push against. The words float away.

But put two opposites next to each other? Now you've got something.

**Two numbers, one story.** Numbers sitting side by side create instant tension. The gap between them tells the story. Ramp: "Eight. That is how many businesses tried Ramp's corporate card in 2024 and decided it was not for them." Then they give you the other pole: 25,000 said yes. Eight versus 25,000. Now you feel something. The eight failures make the 25,000 wins believable. If they'd just said "25,000 businesses love Ramp," you'd shrug. Admitting the eight is confidence. That's putting your head on the chopping block.

**The VW winter checklist.** Two columns. "How to prepare your car for winter." Left: Ordinary Car. Eight checkbox items — drain radiator, flush thoroughly, check rubber hose, refill with anti-freeze, check heater hose, check heater, check for draughts, change oil. Right: Volkswagen. One checkbox. Change oil. No adjectives. No claims about reliability. Just two lists next to each other. The long list versus the single item does all the work. You understand instantly. The ad never says "simpler" or "more reliable." It doesn't need to. The contrast says it.

**The sugar comparison.** Blume's pumpkin spice latte. They could have said "low sugar." Instead they ran a side-by-side ad. Our Pumpkin Spice Latte: 2g of sugar. The Other Guys: 64g of sugar. 2 versus 64. You don't need the paragraph underneath. The numbers do the arguing. "Low sugar" is single-pole — low compared to what? A glass of water? A Mars bar? 2g versus 64g is two concrete numbers. The cold tap defines the hot tap.

**The Man in the Chair.** McGraw-Hill. A stern businessman. Arms crossed. Seven "I don't know" statements stacked: I don't know you. I don't know your company. I don't know your company's product. I don't know what your company stands for. I don't know your company's customers. I don't know your company's record. I don't know your company's reputation. Then the punchline: "Now, what was it you wanted to sell me?" Every line builds the cold tap. The wall. Seven I-don't-knows stacked up make the gap between stranger and customer feel enormous. That gap IS the argument. It's why you need advertising. The ad doesn't explain that. It makes you feel it.

**Basecamp's price table.** Most pricing pages are single-pole. Here's our price. Here's what you get. Basecamp showed a comparison. Their competitors: $1,188/month, $2,628/month, $3,588/month. Basecamp: $99/month. Same layout as VW. Long list on one side. Short answer on the other. The competitor prices make $99 feel like a steal. Without those numbers next to it, $99 is just a number. With them, it's a bargain.

## How to Apply

1. **Find every single-pole claim on the page.** "We're fast." "We're reliable." "We're easy to use." "We save money." "We're different." Each one is a water tap with no counterpart. They float away on their own.

2. **For each, ask: what's the cold tap?** Fast compared to what? Reliable compared to what? Easy compared to what? Money compared to what? Different from what? The specific comparison is the cold tap.

3. **Frame it concretely.** The cold tap is specific: "most CRMs take three months to set up." The hot tap is specific: "ours takes an afternoon." Now there's tension. Now there's a story. Abstract poles don't work. Concrete poles do.

4. **Pick one of five natural framings.**
   - **Before and after** — where the customer is now vs. where they could be.
   - **Them and us** — what competitors do vs. what you do.
   - **Problem and solution** — what's broken vs. what works.
   - **Two numbers** — big vs. small, many vs. few, $2 vs. $64.
   - **The stacked cold tap** — seven I-don't-knows, then the flip (McGraw-Hill's move).

5. **Let the contrast do the arguing.** When the two poles are specific, you don't need adjectives between them. "Two grams of sugar vs sixty-four grams of sugar" is complete. You don't need "much healthier than the other guys." The numbers did the job.

6. **The cold tap should be honest.** If your price is £99 and your closest honest competitor is £400, use £400. Don't pick an enterprise tier at £4,000 that your customer would never consider. Dishonest cold taps break on the first customer check.

## Examples

**Situation:** A CRM homepage reads "Quick and easy to set up."
**Application:** Single-pole. "Quick" compared to what? "Easy" compared to what? Cold tap: most CRMs take three months and a consultant. New headline: "Most CRMs need three months and a consultant. Ours is live tomorrow, with your own team."
**Result:** "Quick and easy" was invisible. The side-by-side is concrete on both poles — "three months and a consultant" is a picture; "live tomorrow, with your own team" is a picture. Tension does the selling.

**Situation:** A boutique meal-prep service trying to justify a £12 meal.
**Application:** Single-pole price. £12 sounds expensive on its own. Find the cold tap — what's the customer already spending? A £13 Deliveroo with delivery fee on a Wednesday. New line: "£12, delivered Thursday. About what one bad Deliveroo costs on Wednesday." The price has an honest anchor the customer already recognises.
**Result:** Price hasn't changed. Customer's relationship to the price has. The cold tap turned "expensive" into "reasonable" without discounting.

**Situation:** A customer-support tool claims "Faster response times."
**Application:** Both poles are vague. Concrete it on both sides. Industry average response time: 24 hours. Their customers' response time: 4 hours. Line: "Most help-desk tools: 24 hours to first response. Our customers: 4 hours. Same team size."
**Result:** Single-pole "faster" becomes side-by-side numbers. "Same team size" closes the loop — the customer isn't spending more, they're just using better tools.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use juxtaposition to straw-man a competitor.
**Why:** Picking a competitor's worst possible configuration and comparing it to your best makes your claim dishonest the moment a sophisticated buyer checks. Use real, typical configurations of real, typical competitors. The comparison has to survive scrutiny, not just the first read.

**Don't:** Have both poles abstract.
**Why:** "From chaos to calm" is two abstractions. Neither pole can be photographed. Concrete poles: "From 47 scattered spreadsheets to one dashboard." "From six-hour payroll to thirty-minute payroll." If you can't picture both sides, the juxtaposition isn't doing any work.

**Don't:** Over-stack the cold tap.
**Why:** A cold tap can be a long list (McGraw-Hill, VW, Basecamp) or a short one (Ramp — 8 vs 25,000). Pick for your product. A seven-item checklist comparing you to a competitor on seven axes starts to feel adversarial and petty. Two or three comparisons, or one big list with a simple flip, usually lands best.

**Don't:** Skip the juxtaposition because "we don't want to call out competitors."
**Why:** The juxtaposition doesn't have to name a competitor. "Before and after," "problem and solution," "the old way vs the new way" all create tension without naming anyone. DuckDuckGo names Google because it's core to their position. Basecamp names competitors because they're fighting for the same buyers. You can juxtapose against an approach without naming a company.

**Don't:** Add a cold tap that's weaker than the hot tap deserves.
**Why:** If your product really does deliver in 4 hours and the industry is closer to 48 hours (not 24), use 48. Under-selling your own differentiation to sound more modest is a common British-brand failure mode. The cold tap should be the honest worst-plausible alternative, not a charitable one.
