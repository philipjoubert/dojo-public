# Managerial Leverage

## When to Use
- A manager is overloaded and needs to decide what to cut and what to keep.
- Designing the structure of a new manager's first weeks — what to spend time on, what to ignore.
- A founder asks where in the day they get the most return per hour.
- Diagnosing a manager whose team has gone quiet, gloomy, or stuck — leverage can be sharply negative.
- Reviewing a calendar with the question "is this the highest-leverage use of this hour?"

## Fails When
- Used to justify maximum activity rate. The point of leverage is *which* activity, not how many activities per hour. A manager doing eight low-leverage things crisply is doing less work than a manager doing one high-leverage thing well.
- Applied as a pep-talk for working harder. The framework's most important single observation is that some activities have *negative* leverage — and a manager who works harder at a negative-leverage activity makes things worse, faster.
- Used in isolation from output. Leverage that doesn't show up downstream as team output is a story you're telling yourself.

## Core Concept

Output is what the team produces. Leverage is the bridge between a manager's hours and that output. Write it:

> "Managerial Output = Output of organization = L1 × A1 + L2 × A2 + … This equation says that for every activity a manager performs — A1, A2, and so on — the output of the organization should increase by some degree. The extent to which that output is thereby increased is determined by the leverage of that activity — L1, L2, and so on." (HOM ch.3)

Each managerial activity (A) carries a leverage coefficient (L) that turns the hour into team output. The hour is fixed — twenty-four of them in the day, and your time is the one absolutely finite resource you have. The lever is what you choose to do with it. Three ways to push the equation up: do activities faster, raise the leverage of each activity, or shift the mix toward higher-leverage activities. The third is the one nobody applies. Most managers try to speed up; almost none restructure their week around higher-L activities they were already doing badly because they treated them as overhead.

High-leverage activities have one of three signatures. First, **many people affected by one act**: the planning manager who lays out the format that two hundred people will follow for the next quarter; the orientation lecture that imprints new employees while they're most impressionable. Second, **a brief act that shapes long behavior**: a performance review that re-aims a person's effort for a year; a few well-chosen comments at a staff meeting that change how a team frames a problem; a tickler file set up in fifteen minutes that improves daily work indefinitely. Third, **unique knowledge applied to a wide audience**: the marketing engineer who sets prices for the product line, affecting hundreds of salespeople; the development engineer whose process choice shapes every product the company will design on top of it. The art of management is selecting from many activities of comparable apparent significance the one or two that have leverage well beyond the others, and concentrating there.

Leverage runs both ways. **Negative leverage is enormous and most managers don't see it in themselves.** A manager who shows up to a meeting unprepared not only wastes their own hour, they waste the time of everyone in the room and deny those people the chance to do something else with the same hour. A manager who broods because the division won't make plan spreads depression through the organization in days, and didn't realize it until someone on his staff told him. A manager who waffles — who delays a decision other people are waiting on — has effectively decided no, for everyone, and stopped their work. **Managerial meddling** is its own form: the supervisor who, rather than letting the subordinate work through a problem, jumps in with detailed instructions, and after a few rounds the subordinate stops solving things and starts referring everything upward. Each of these — depression, waffling, meddling — has *virtually unlimited* negative leverage; the impact is pervasive and elusive, hard to spot and harder to undo.

## How to Apply

1. **Inventory this week's activities and tag each with a leverage signature.** For every standing block — 1:1s, staff meeting, ops review, code review, customer calls, hiring loop, planning meeting — ask: does this affect many people through one act, does it shape long behavior in a brief act, or does it apply unique knowledge across many recipients? If it doesn't fit any of those, the leverage is probably modest. That doesn't mean cut it; it means don't treat it as your high-leverage time.

2. **Front-load the work that has timing leverage.** Some activities are high-leverage only when done early. Robin's planning-process design has enormous leverage if she lays out the format before two hundred people start planning, and almost none if she does the same work after they've started. The intervention with a valued subordinate who has decided to quit is high-leverage today and irrelevant next week. Map your high-leverage acts to their timing window and protect that window.

3. **Use the calendar as a production-planning tool, not a passive inbox.** Most managers let their calendar fill from outside — every request lands as an entry, and the day looks "full." A factory manager doesn't let material onto the line if the line is at capacity; he says no at the outset. Use your calendar the same way. Schedule the high-leverage activities first, batch similar activities together to share mental setup time, and say no early to anything that doesn't earn its slot. Saying no late is more expensive than saying no early. Carry an inventory of discretionary projects so that when slack appears you don't fill it by meddling in your subordinates' work.

4. **Audit yourself for negative leverage.** Three forms recur. **Waffling**: a decision you're sitting on that other people are blocked behind — a no-decision is a no, and they know it. **Mood broadcast**: depression, irritation, or sourness leaking into the team because you didn't notice it was contagious. **Meddling**: assuming command of a subordinate's situation rather than letting him work it through, often by quoting your own superior knowledge. If any of those is in the room, no amount of high-leverage activity makes up for it. The negative term swamps the positive.

5. **Delegate where you have the most familiarity, then monitor.** Counter-intuitive but true: delegate the things you know best, because monitoring is only practical when you understand the work. Delegating something unfamiliar means you can't tell whether it's going off the rails. **Delegation without follow-through is abdication** — you remain responsible for the output, and the only way to ensure it is to monitor at the lowest-value-added stage (review the rough draft, not the polished memo) and vary frequency by prior performance on this specific task.

6. **Ration the highest-leverage acts so they stay sharp.** A 1:1 done badly because the subordinate is the eighth in a row of identical-feeling meetings is no longer high-leverage. A performance review written tired loses most of its value. Treat the top-L activities as you would treat surgery: prepare, schedule them when you're fresh, and don't run them in long unbroken blocks.

## Examples

**Situation:** Robin, an Intel finance manager, is responsible for setting up the annual financial planning process. She can spend a week now defining what information needs to be gathered at each stage and who is responsible for what, or she can scramble later when individual managers come to her with confusion.

**Application:** The work, done in advance, directly shapes the planning activity of perhaps two hundred people for the duration of the planning cycle. The same work, done after the cycle has started, only resolves confusion that already cost everyone time.

**Result:** The leverage of Robin's hour is determined less by the activity itself than by *when* she performs it. Front-loaded, it eliminates ambiguity for two hundred people for months. After the fact, it's damage control. The lesson is general: a manager's high-leverage hours are often acts of preparation that nobody applauds because nothing visible happened, and the absence of the explosion is the result. (HOM ch.3.)

---

**Situation:** A division manager at Intel finished the annual planning process and saw that, despite a successful cost-reduction effort the prior year, his division was still not going to make money in the coming year. He became depressed.

**Application:** He didn't realize what was happening. The depression spread through his organization in days. People around him became gloomy without knowing exactly why, and the gloom became its own drag on the work. Only when someone on his staff finally told him what he was doing to the people under him did he snap out of it.

**Result:** A single manager's mood, broadcast through dozens of small interactions, is one of the largest negative-leverage events in an organization. A poor sales-training session can be retrained. A manager who broadcasts gloom for a week is much harder to undo, because the impact is everywhere at once and nowhere specifically.

> "Both the depressed and the waffling manager can have virtually unlimited negative leverage … because their impact on an organization is both so pervasive and so elusive." (HOM ch.3)

---

**Situation:** A senior manager is reviewing an indicator that's drifting in a worrying direction. The subordinate responsible is in the middle of investigating it. The senior manager, drawing on long experience, dictates a detailed set of corrective actions to take.

**Application:** This is meddling. The corrective actions may even be the right ones. But after several such episodes, the subordinate has learned that any time something drifts, the supervisor will assume command. He stops trying to solve problems himself and refers them up.

**Result:** The negative leverage is not in the immediate event — the actions might work — but in the second-order effect on the subordinate's initiative. A team trained, over months, to escalate every drifting indicator is a team whose manager has multiplied his own workload while shrinking theirs. The output of the organization falls in the long run, even though every single intervention looked productive in the moment. (HOM ch.3.)

---

**Situation:** A founder is choosing between attending an industry dinner or sitting in on the orientation lecture for fifty new engineers.

**Application:** Run the leverage signatures. The industry dinner is a real activity but its leverage is narrow — it affects the founder's own information base, and possibly one or two relationships. The orientation lecture imprints fifty people who are most impressionable, with the founder's framing of what the company is, what it cares about, how it answers questions. Through the founder's handling of the Q&A — what they take seriously, what they push back on — they communicate values to a population that will carry that framing into every team they join.

**Result:** The orientation lecture has dramatically higher leverage. Most founders, asked the same question, will pick the dinner because it feels more important — more senior people, more visible. That's the trap. High-leverage activities often look ordinary; the dinner looks important and isn't. (HOM ch.3.)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Conflate "high-leverage" with "high-status." 
**Why:** The 1:1 with a junior engineer is often higher-leverage than the meeting with the senior peer. A few minutes of sharp feedback that redirect a year of someone's work outranks an hour of theater with peers who already know what you think. Status and leverage are different axes. Optimize for the second.

**Don't:** Speed up your way out of being overloaded.
**Why:** The most common managerial response to overload is to handle each piece of paper once, hold stand-up meetings, turn the desk to face away from the door. These are speed tactics — they raise the activity rate. They don't change the mix. A manager doing forty low-leverage things efficiently is producing less than a manager doing five high-leverage things well. Restructure the mix before optimizing the rate.

**Don't:** Pretend to delegate.
**Why:** Holding the pencil while telling someone to take it. The "insincere delegation" — where you announce the handoff but second-guess every move, or revoke the delegation the moment the work doesn't look like you'd have done it — produces enormous negative leverage. The subordinate learns the delegation is performance, not real, and stops investing. Either delegate or don't, and decide consciously which.

**Don't:** Skip 1:1s when you're busy.
**Why:** A 1:1 is ninety minutes that improves the quality of a subordinate's eighty hours of work over the next two weeks. Among the highest-leverage acts available to a manager. The week you cancel them because you're "underwater" is the week you've cut your own leverage to keep up with low-leverage activity. The math doesn't work even when it feels like it does.

**Don't:** Broadcast your mood and assume nobody notices.
**Why:** They notice. The manager who comes in irritable and assumes the team will compartmentalize is wrong; they don't. The mood propagates as a tax on every interaction that day. If you genuinely have a bad mood that's not about the team, it is a small kindness and an act of organizational hygiene to name it briefly and not let it color the day's work. The depressed-division-manager story is the lesson — discovered far too late.

**Don't:** Use slack time to meddle.
**Why:** A manager who finds an empty hour and uses it to drop into a subordinate's project is converting unused time into negative leverage. Carry a raw-material inventory of discretionary projects — long-term things that improve the group's productivity later — so that the temptation to meddle has somewhere else to go.
