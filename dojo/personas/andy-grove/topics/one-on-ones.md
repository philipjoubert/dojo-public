# One-on-Ones

## When to Use
- Establishing the format of regular meetings between a manager and each of their direct reports.
- Fixing 1:1s that have decayed into status updates or been quietly dropped because "we see each other all the time."
- Setting up the rhythm with a brand-new report — first 1:1 sets the format for the next year.
- Advising any manager who reports being surprised by what's happening in their own team.
- Deciding how often, how long, and where to hold the meeting.

## Fails When
- Treated as the manager's meeting. The whole point — the leverage — depends on the subordinate setting the agenda. A 1:1 the manager runs is just another status meeting, which the manager has to prepare for eight times rather than the subordinate preparing once.
- Used to replace the performance review. They're different formats with different purposes; a performance review is rare, formal, written, and looks back over a year. A 1:1 is regular, working, and looks at the next two weeks.
- Compressed to fifteen or thirty minutes. Below an hour the subordinate confines themselves to easy items and buries the hard ones because they can't risk running out of time on something thorny.
- Held in the manager's office every time. The supervisor learns more about the subordinate's work by going to where the subordinate works. Whether they are organized, what they are looking at, what gets interrupted.

## Core Concept

> "Once somebody asked me to identify the single most useful management technique that I learned through my years of managing. My answer was: the practice of regularly scheduled one-on-one meetings." (One-on-One, ch.2)

A one-on-one is a meeting between a supervisor and a single direct report, held regularly, owned by the subordinate. Its purpose is mutual teaching and exchange of information: the subordinate brings the supervisor up to speed on what's actually happening in his work; the supervisor teaches skills, judgment, and the company's preferred way of handling things; together they decide what to do next. It is the *medium* through which their working relationship is maintained. Casual encounters are not a substitute. Mission-oriented meetings to resolve specific problems are not a substitute. The 1:1 is its own thing, scheduled, and indispensable.

The crucial design decision: **the 1:1 is the subordinate's meeting, with its agenda and tone set by him**. The reason is mechanical. A supervisor with eight subordinates would have to prepare eight times if she owned the agenda; the subordinate prepares once. So the subordinate is asked to prepare an outline — both copies, one for each of you to take notes on — that forces him to think through, in advance, the issues and points he wants to raise. The outline does several things at once: it makes the subordinate confront the difficult items rather than hide from them, it lets the supervisor pace the meeting according to the meatiness of the items, and it provides a frame for the supporting information he should bring along. **The single biggest mistake managers make with 1:1s is owning the agenda.** When the supervisor sets the agenda, the subordinate brings the easy items and buries the hard ones, because the format signals that the manager will tell him what's important.

The leverage is enormous and explicit:

> "What is the leverage of the one-on-one? Let's say you have a one-on-one with your subordinate every two weeks, and it lasts one and a half hours. Ninety minutes of your time can enhance the quality of your subordinate's work for two weeks, or for some eighty-plus hours, and also upgrade your understanding of what he's doing. Clearly, one-on-ones can exert enormous leverage." (HOM ch.4)

Ninety minutes shaping eighty hours. There is almost no other managerial activity with that ratio. The supervisor's role inside the meeting is not to lecture or to solve — it is to facilitate the subordinate's expression of what's going on and what's bothering him. **Ask one more question.** When you think the subordinate has said all he wants to say about a subject, ask another. Keep prompting until both of you feel you've reached the bottom of the issue. The most important issues are obscure and take time to surface; the easy ones are wrapped up early. The 1:1 is where you find the load-bearing problems before they become emergencies, and the way you find them is by not letting the conversation end at the first plausible stopping point.

## How to Apply

1. **Set frequency by task-relevant maturity, not by personality.** A subordinate inexperienced in a specific situation — a new role, a new project, a new technology — needs frequent 1:1s, perhaps weekly. A veteran handling familiar work can run on a longer interval, perhaps every few weeks. Adjust as task maturity changes; a senior person handed a new domain becomes, on that domain, a junior person who needs more frequent contact. The same person can sit on different cadences for different streams of their work.

2. **Schedule for at least an hour.** "I feel that a one-on-one should last an hour at a minimum. Anything less, in my experience, tends to make the subordinate confine himself to simple things that can be handled quickly." (HOM ch.4.) Below sixty minutes the subordinate plays it safe — he picks the items he can finish inside the time slot. Above sixty minutes he can risk a thorny topic. Schedule on a rolling basis — set up the next one as the current one ends — so vacations and conflicts don't blow standing slots out.

3. **Hold it where the subordinate works, when you can.** Going to his office tells you things you can't learn in your own. Is he organized? Does he get interrupted constantly, or never? How does he approach his work when no one is watching him? Walking to him also signals something: this is for him, not for you.

4. **Make the subordinate prepare the outline.** Both copies, one each. Ask him to walk you through the material. The outline shifts ownership and makes the meeting go where it should. Without it, the meeting drifts to whatever you happened to be thinking about that morning.

5. **What gets covered: indicators, then issues, then potential problems.** Start with performance indicators — incoming orders, output, project status — with emphasis on indicators that signal trouble. Move to anything important that's happened since the last meeting: hiring, people problems, organizational issues, future plans. Then the part that matters most: **potential** problems. Things that aren't tangible yet, intuitions that something is wrong. The subordinate owes the supervisor the early signal even when it's only a hunch, because that hunch is the cheapest, earliest look into the organizational black box.

6. **Run a hold file.** Both supervisor and subordinate accumulate items that are important but not urgent into a file for the next 1:1. This is the practical answer to the manager who's interrupted constantly by drop-ins and phone calls — the regularly scheduled meeting *is* the alternative, and the hold file is what keeps non-urgent items from becoming hallway interruptions.

7. **Take notes — on the outline.** Both of you. Many issues lead to action by the subordinate; when he writes the action down right after you suggest it, the act is a commitment, like a handshake. The supervisor takes notes too — partly to follow up at the next meeting, partly to keep your mind from drifting, partly because writing forces categorization. Most of these notes you'll never look at again. That's fine. The act of taking them is what does the work.

8. **Apply Grove's principle: ask one more question.** When you think you've heard everything, ask another. The bottom of a problem is rarely where the subordinate stops the first time. Heart-to-heart issues — frustrations, doubts, concerns about direction — emerge through the second and third question, not the first. Be wary of the **zinger** — a heart-to-heart issue brought up at the very end of the meeting with five minutes left. ("By the way, I've been looking around outside.") Try to surface those mid-meeting by asking the right one-more-question early enough that there's still time to deal with what comes up.

9. **Encourage long-distance 1:1s, with discipline.** A 1:1 by phone or video works, but only with preparation. The supervisor must have the outline before the meeting. Both parties take notes. After the meeting, exchange notes so you both know what each committed to.

## Examples

**Situation:** A frazzled plant manager is interrupted constantly. Employees stick their heads in to ask quick questions; the phone rings with another inquiry mid-conversation. He insists he has no time for "luxuries" like regular 1:1s with his reports.

**Application:** Most of those interruptions are 1:1 material in disguise — non-urgent issues the reports bring up ad hoc because there's no scheduled forum. Adopt regular 1:1s and a hold file; force non-urgent items into the standing meeting; the constant interruption decays.

**Result:** A year later, the same manager came back, calendar in hand, asking for help. Once he'd switched to standing 1:1s, his head came up out of the water and stayed there.

> "One-on-one meetings are the preventive medicine of management work — even if they are resisted by the patient in the same way." (One-on-One ch.2)

---

**Situation:** Grove is in a 1:1 with the head of Intel's sales organization. The subordinate walks through trend indicators of incoming orders. Grove is vaguely familiar with them; the subordinate brings specific data and an argument that the slowdown is not seasonal.

**Application:** The subordinate prepares the material. Grove asks one more question — and then another. They examine the data together, compare it against industry indicators of business activity, sit with it for a while.

**Result:** Both of them reach the same reluctant conclusion: business has actually stopped growing. The subordinate decides, in the same meeting, to scale back growth in his own area. Grove decides to share the conclusion with the other business groups he supervises. One 1:1 produced not just the leverage on the subordinate's eighty hours, but a chain of decisions that propagated to the rest of the company through Grove's other reports. (HOM ch.4.)

---

**Situation:** An insurance salesman writes in: he likes the profession, but his boss — a former salesman turned manager — doesn't know how to manage. The salesman wants to be coached. He asks if he can succeed without a manager.

**Application:** Don't wait for the boss to invent the format. Take the initiative. Ask for a regular meeting in his office, or at breakfast or lunch if no other time works. Use the meeting to mine the boss's experience — bring specific sales situations, ask how he would have handled them, ask him to come on calls with you, ask him from time to time to take over a particular handling so you can watch him in action. Establish the pattern; once the boss sees he's been able to help, he'll pick up the ball.

**Result:** "Make sure you can spend uninterrupted time with him. Arrange to meet *regularly* in his office. … In other words, create situations where you can learn from your boss's experience as a salesman. I believe that once he realizes that he has been able to help you, he'll pick up the ball and run with it." (One-on-One ch.2.) The 1:1 format works in both directions — when the manager won't initiate it, the subordinate can. The right of any employee, Grove writes elsewhere, is the right to be managed; demanded steadily and without putting the boss on the defensive, that right tends to be granted.

---

**Situation:** A subordinate finishes the 1:1 wrap-up, then with three minutes left says, "By the way, I've been looking outside for another job."

**Application:** This is the zinger — a heart-to-heart issue dropped at an awkward moment. There isn't time to handle it well in three minutes. Don't try. Don't dismiss it either. The right move is to take the news seriously, ask the question that opens up the real conversation, and schedule time inside this week to deal with it properly. The lesson for next time is to apply the ask-one-more-question discipline earlier in the meeting, when there's still time to surface this kind of thing inside the slot.

**Result:** Catching the zinger when there's time to deal with it usually means asking, mid-meeting, "is there anything else weighing on you that we haven't talked about?" — not at the end, but in the middle, when there's still slack in the hour. (HOM ch.4.)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Set the agenda yourself.
**Why:** This is the most common failure. The manager who walks in with their own list has converted the 1:1 into a status meeting they preferred to schedule under a friendlier name. The subordinate brings the easy items, buries the hard ones, and the load-bearing issues never surface. The leverage of the format collapses to zero. Make the subordinate prepare and own the outline. If he doesn't, the answer is to coach him into doing it, not to take the agenda back.

**Don't:** Cancel 1:1s when you're busy.
**Why:** The 1:1 is the highest-leverage hour-and-a-half on your calendar — ninety minutes shaping eighty hours of someone else's work. The week you cancel because you're "underwater" is the week you cut your own leverage to keep up with low-leverage activity. Move them; don't cancel them. Move them again if you have to. Don't drop them.

**Don't:** Run them under thirty minutes.
**Why:** The subordinate confines himself to simple, fast items. The hard things — the potential problems, the heart-to-heart issues, the things that take time to surface — never come up because there isn't time to start them. Schedule sixty at a minimum; ninety is more comfortable.

**Don't:** Use the 1:1 to deliver a performance review.
**Why:** Different format, different cadence, different stakes. Performance reviews are rare, formal, written, looking back over a year. A 1:1 is regular, working, and looking at the next two weeks. Mixing them poisons both — the subordinate stops trusting the 1:1 as a working forum because he doesn't know when it's going to turn into evaluation, and the performance review loses gravity because it gets sprinkled across casual meetings.

**Don't:** Treat seeing each other in the hallway as a 1:1 substitute.
**Why:** Hallway encounters and ad hoc meetings handle small, specific things. They don't handle the subtle issues — frustrations, doubts about direction, the early-warning hunches — that require an hour, a closed door, and the format that says "this is your time, what's on your mind?" "Stopping in the hallway for an impromptu chat is not a substitute for a one-on-one meeting — can you imagine such subjects being dealt with on the fly?" (One-on-One ch.2.)

**Don't:** Stop asking questions when the subordinate stops talking.
**Why:** The first stop is rarely the bottom of the issue. The most expensive problems — the ones you'll wish you'd known about a month earlier — surface on the second or third question, not the first. Ask one more. If the meeting feels finished and you've only asked once, you have probably finished too early.

**Don't:** Skip the hold file.
**Why:** Without a hold file, every non-urgent question that occurs to the subordinate becomes either an interruption (drop-in, phone call, Slack ping) or a forgotten item. Both are worse than the alternative — write it down, batch it, deal with it at the standing meeting. The hold file is the cheap mechanism that makes the standing 1:1 worth more than its time slot, because it absorbs the dribble of small things that would otherwise disrupt your day.
