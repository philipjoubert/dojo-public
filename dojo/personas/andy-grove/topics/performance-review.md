# Performance Review — The Manager as Judge and Jury

## When to Use
- Any formal review cycle — annual, semi-annual, end-of-probation.
- A persistent performance problem that informal feedback has not corrected and that needs the structure and gravity of a formal review.
- Rating a manager whose team has had an outstanding (or terrible) year and you need to decide whether the rating reflects the manager's performance or just the work the prior team did.
- A promotion decision that your gut wants to make on "potential" but the performance evidence does not yet support.

## Fails When
- You don't actually know the person's work. A review you cannot defend with specifics is worse than no review — it teaches the report that the system is theatre.
- The review is loaded with a year's worth of observations the person has never heard. Review is the formal compounding moment for feedback that has been ongoing all year, not a surprise exam.
- You hide the hard message behind soft language because it's uncomfortable. The watered-down review is the most common kind, and it is the one that does the most damage.

## Core Concept

The performance review is the single highest-leverage feedback act a manager performs. It is the only time a manager is required, formally, to act as judge and jury — to render a judgment on a fellow worker's work and deliver it face to face. Done well, it shapes the report's performance for a year. Done badly — generic, vague, surprise-laden, mealy-mouthed — it teaches the report that the system means nothing, and the next year is worse.

The fundamental purpose is one thing only: **to improve the subordinate's future performance.** Not to justify a raise, not to settle scores, not to relieve the manager of the year's accumulated observations. Two implications follow. First, you review **performance, not potential** — form rather than substance is the trap. The manager who "handles himself well" but runs a unit that lost money for a year is not an outstanding manager; he is a manager whose unit lost money. The performance rating of a manager cannot be higher than the rating you would give his organization. Anything else signals to the rest of the company that to do well you must *act* like a good manager, not perform like one. Second, the review must be useful to the person — which means hitting a small number of key messages they can actually remember and act on. A laundry list of seven observations dumped into a one-hour conversation will leave them with nothing.

The delivery is governed by the **three L's**. **Level** with the person — totally frank, no hedging, no charades. **Listen** — not to their words, but to whether they have actually received what you said, using every sensory channel available; if their face says they didn't hear it, keep going until they do. **Leave yourself out** — your guilt, your discomfort, your need for them to like you afterwards are not on the agenda. The review is about them, not about your psychological comfort. The hardest part is not the analysis. The hardest part is delivering the message you'd rather not deliver, in language clear enough that it cannot be misread. The watered-down review is the most common managerial failure in the entire HOM canon — and the one I commit most often myself.

> "The performance rating of a manager cannot be higher than the one we would accord to his organization!" (HOM ch.13)

> "The aim of communication is to transmit thoughts from the brain of person A to the brain of person B... If their responses—verbal and nonverbal—do not completely assure you that what you've said has gotten through, it is *your responsibility* to keep at it until you are satisfied that you have been heard and understood." (HOM ch.13)

## How to Apply

1. **Distinguish the formal review from everyday feedback. You owe both.** Ongoing, informal feedback happens continually — when something noteworthy happens, you say so right then. The annual review is the broader perspective: trends, patterns, a year's arc, with concrete guidance for the year ahead. Neither substitutes for the other. If you skip the informal stuff, the formal review becomes a pile of surprises. If you skip the formal review, the informal feedback never compounds into a clear picture of what to work on.

2. **Sit down with a blank page and dump everything.** Before you start writing the review, scan your one-on-one notes, the report's objectives and outputs, your hold-file. Write everything that comes to mind on the worksheet — major, minor, trivial, in no particular order. Do not edit in your head. Get it all out.

3. **Look for relationships among the items. Cluster into messages.** Many of the items will be different manifestations of the same underlying phenomenon. The clusters become the *messages*. If the worksheet shows "good planning system, poor spec process, mushy meetings, debating-society chairing" — the message isn't four separate things; it's one: "you set crisp processes when you have an analytical model, but you can't drive an issue to a result when one isn't there." That's a message they can do something with.

4. **Cap the messages at two or three.** A person has finite capacity to absorb feedback about themselves — especially when emotional. Seven observations is sensory overload; they walk away with nothing. Two or three messages they can actually carry into Monday. What you cut, you can return to next quarter — there is always next quarter.

5. **Assess performance, not potential.** Ask: did this person produce the output? If they're a manager, did their organization produce its output? Add value above the team's natural output? Hire and develop people? Set the stage for next year? *The performance rating of a manager cannot be higher than the one you'd accord his organization.* Resist the seductive review that says "the org missed its numbers but the manager is excellent" — that review is wrong on its face and corrosive to the company.

6. **Watch the time-offset trap.** A manager whose unit is having a great year may be reaping the planting another manager did two years ago. A manager whose unit is having a brutal year may have *just* turned the corner and the output is still on its way. Read the internal indicators — turnover, morale, hiring quality, leading metrics — alongside the output indicators, and credit the year's *work*, not just the year's *outputs*.

7. **Deliver the written review before the meeting.** Hand them the document a day or two ahead so they can read it privately, react, overreact, calm down, and come to the meeting able to discuss it rationally. Reading it together in the room means they're skimming for the rest of the page while you're trying to talk about paragraph three.

8. **Apply the three L's during delivery.** *Level*: total frankness, including the praise — being straightforward in praise is just as hard as in criticism. *Listen*: watch their face, watch their body, watch whether they actually heard the message. If their response tells you they tuned out at "but," restate it in different words and keep going until you're certain they got it. *Leave yourself out*: your stage fright, your guilt, your need to be liked are not part of the meeting.

9. **For a poor performer, drive them through the four stages.** Ignore → actively deny → blame others → assume responsibility → find solution. Most reviews get stuck at "blame others." The job of the reviewer is to use facts and examples to push them through, knowing that the move from blame-others to assuming-responsibility is an *emotional* step, while the move from assuming-responsibility to finding-the-solution is *intellectual* — and the latter is easier. If they can't get past blame-others, you exercise position power: "I'm not going to convince you. I am instructing you to do X. Here are the milestones I will monitor."

10. **Spend disproportionate effort on your stars.** Most managers spend the most effort writing the review for the borderline performer. The leverage is the other way. Stars produce a disproportionate share of output; a one-percent improvement in a star's performance is worth ten percent on a marginal performer. Don't accept "they're great" as a finished thought. There is always one thing your top performer could do better, and you owe them that thought.

11. **Commit the message to writing.** The pen forces precision the tongue would dodge. If the written and spoken messages diverge, the spoken one is what you wanted to say; the written one is what you should have said.

## Examples

**Situation:** A divisional general manager runs a unit that lost money for the year, missed its revenue forecast monthly, and slipped its product schedules. His direct supervisor wants to give him a strong review because "he's knowledgeable, handles himself well, knows the business — it's the organization that didn't perform, not the manager."

**Application:** The reviewing-up senior leader refuses to approve the rating. The performance rating of a manager cannot be higher than the rating his organization would receive. Approving the inflated review would teach the entire company that you don't have to perform like a good manager, you just have to *act* like one — talk like one, dress like one, emulate one. The supervisor is sent back to redo the review with a rating consistent with the unit's output and concrete messages on what the GM needs to do to turn it around.

**Result:** The signal to the rest of the company is preserved: performance is what counts. The GM either turns it around the next year, or his rating reflects that he didn't.

---

**Situation:** A high-performing manager gets a glowing review for a year in which his unit's output measures were excellent. The reviewing supervisor noticed the leading indicators were off — turnover too high, grumbling in the ranks — but went with the output numbers. The next year, the unit collapses.

**Application:** The mistake was confusing distant-light output (work done two years ago) with current performance. The supervisor should have weighted the internal measures — the turnover, the morale, the hiring quality, the slipping schedules — against the output measures. The review the second year is the painful one: not just rating the bad year, but acknowledging that the previous year's high rating was wrong on the merits, and rebuilding the manager's understanding of how he is actually being judged.

**Result:** The hard lesson of the review system is that the best output indicators can lag the work by a full year. Trusting them blindly turns the review into a celebration of past managers' planting, not the present manager's farming.

---

**Situation:** A manager has prepared a review that is glowing, with no improvement areas. At the end of the meeting the report looks at him and says, "Do you really mean to say I am perfect?"

**Application:** The manager swallows his discomfort, says no, and immediately produces two specific areas where the (genuinely outstanding) report can grow. There is *always* something. A review without improvement messages is not a kindness; it is a confession that the manager has not thought hard enough about a star who deserves the thinking.

**Result:** The star gets what the star needs. For ace performers the bar is *higher*, not lower — they can absorb sharper feedback than marginal performers, and they deserve the investment.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Rate "potential" instead of performance.
**Why:** Potential is form; performance is substance. The "high-potential" rating attached to a missed-numbers manager teaches the company that to advance, you must look like a good manager rather than be one. Promotions are role models — promote on form and you breed form.

**Don't:** Pad the review with seven observations because you have seven.
**Why:** A person can absorb two or three messages, no more. Beyond that, you wash out everything, and they walk away with nothing. The purpose of the review is not to cleanse your system — it is to improve theirs.

**Don't:** Surprise them with a hard message at year-end that you should have raised in March.
**Why:** Surprises in a review mean you skipped your day-to-day feedback duty. The review is the formal compounding moment, not the first moment. If a hard message has to be a surprise, deliver it — but understand it shouldn't have been one.

**Don't:** Water down the message to spare yourself the conversation.
**Why:** This is the single most common review failure. Most bosses are loath to give difficult messages and will, if not disciplined, deliver something so soft the report cannot decode it. The written review is the discipline that prevents this. Write the hard sentence; if you can't write it, you don't yet believe it; once you do, deliver it as written.

**Don't:** Confuse "the report agreed with me" with a successful review.
**Why:** What you need is *commitment* to action, not agreement on assessment. If you and the report disagree on the diagnosis but they commit to the prescribed actions — that's an acceptable outcome. Pushing for agreement past commitment is about your psychological comfort, not their performance.

**Don't:** Lower a rating to fit a budget constraint.
**Why:** The rating reflects performance alone. If the salary budget is tight, every report's raise is proportionally smaller — but their rating is unaffected. Distorting the rating to manage the cost line teaches the team that the review system is a finance tool, not a performance tool, and the system loses all credibility.

**Don't:** Skip the review because you're "too busy."
**Why:** Late reviews are disrespectful. They tell the report that evaluating their year — the highest-leverage thing a manager does for them — is less important than your other obligations. It is not.
