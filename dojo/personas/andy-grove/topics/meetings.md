# Meetings — Process-Oriented vs. Mission-Oriented

## When to Use
- You are designing — or auditing — the meeting calendar of a team, division, or company.
- A team is drowning in ad-hoc, mission-oriented meetings and nothing seems to ship.
- You are about to call a meeting and don't yet know whether one is necessary.
- You are the senior person walking into a meeting and need to know what your job in the room actually is.
- The same decision keeps coming back to you a third time because nobody's ratification rights were nailed down the first time.

## Fails When
- The meeting cadence is borrowed wholesale from another company without thinking about whose maturity dictates frequency. Meetings are not a uniform — they are sized to the work.
- The chairman uses the format to perform thoroughness while quietly avoiding the call he was hired to make. Process is no substitute for a decision the senior person is afraid to make.
- A staff meeting becomes a serial set of two-person conversations while the rest of the room watches. The supervisor's job is to break that and put the issue on hold.
- A mission-oriented meeting is called because a process-oriented meeting that *should* exist does not. Spending more than 25 percent of your time in ad-hoc meetings is the real sign of malorganization.

## Core Concept

A meeting is not the curse of the manager's existence — it is the medium of managerial work. Information is transmitted, know-how is taught, decisions are made — and all of that happens face-to-face. So the question is never "how do I have fewer meetings?" The question is "is this meeting *the right kind*, and is it doing what that kind is supposed to do?"

Meetings split cleanly into two kinds. **Process-oriented meetings** are regularly scheduled, exchange information, and run on a known format — the one-on-one, the staff meeting, the operation review. They batch the recurring transactions of management at a known clock rate so that the rest of the calendar isn't gnawed at by ad-hoc interruptions. **Mission-oriented meetings** are ad hoc and exist to produce a specific output, almost always a decision. They are the expensive ones, and they are also the ones a manager should be most reluctant to call. The rule of thumb is that if the process meetings are working, they handle about 80 percent of the load; the remaining 20 percent will still need a mission-oriented meeting. If the ratio inverts — if you spend more than a quarter of your time in mission-oriented meetings — your process meetings have failed and you are paying for it in firefighting.

The economics are not abstract. An hour of a manager's time, fully loaded, is roughly $100. Ten managers in a two-hour meeting is $2,000 of company resources committed at a whim, with none of the approval discipline that the same $2,000 in capital expenditure would require. Hold the calendar to the same standard you hold the purchase ledger.

> "A meeting is nothing less than the *medium* through which managerial work is performed. That means we should not be fighting their very existence, but rather using the time spent in them as efficiently as possible." (HOM ch.4)

## How to Apply

1. **Classify the meeting before you accept the invite.** Process-oriented or mission-oriented? If you can't tell, the chairman hasn't done his job and you should ask him which it is. The two kinds run on opposite logics — one batches, one decides — and a meeting that pretends to be both will be neither.

2. **Before calling a mission-oriented meeting, answer six questions in writing.** What decision needs to be made? When does it have to be made? Who will decide? Who will be consulted? Who will ratify or veto? Who needs to be informed? If you can't answer all six, you do not yet know what you want from the meeting, and you will not get it.

3. **Cap the room at six or seven, eight at the absolute outside.** Decision-making is not a spectator sport. Beyond eight, the audience displaces the participants and the meeting becomes a presentation.

4. **Send an agenda that names the purpose and the role of each attendee.** Times against items, names against times. The point of writing it down is partly logistics and mostly discipline — it forces the chairman to commit to what he expects from each person.

5. **In a staff meeting, do not lecture.** Your roles are moderator, facilitator, questioner, and pace controller. The brunt of the issue-working is borne by your subordinates. The minute the supervisor starts pontificating, free discussion stops and the meeting becomes a one-way broadcast.

6. **In an operation review, the senior reviewing manager sets the spirit by example.** He should ask questions, react spontaneously, never preview the deck, and treat the role of model audience as seriously as the presenter treats the deck. A bored senior person teaches juniors that the meeting doesn't matter — and they will remember it next quarter when you ask them to prepare for one.

7. **Write minutes immediately after a mission-oriented meeting.** Decision, owners, dates. If the meeting was worth $2,000, the minutes are the small further investment that ensures the value isn't lost overnight. Send them while attendees still remember what they agreed to.

8. **Confront the late arrival.** Letting someone walk in fifteen minutes late, costing the room $250 of company time, is not politeness — it is theft. Don't worry about being a drill sergeant. The disciplined operating room is the one you'd want as the patient.

## Examples

**Situation:** A divisional construction manager has to choose between expanding the existing Philippine plant on a constrained lot — forcing a high-rise — or buying a cheaper, larger lot some distance away. Two organizations have a stake (manufacturing wants adjacency; construction wants the cheaper site) and the senior leadership ratifying the decision is two levels up.

**Application:** The mission-oriented meeting is announced with a written agenda — purpose stated as "decide specific location for Philippine plant expansion," times allocated to manufacturing considerations, construction considerations, alternatives review, and group discussion. The room is composed with parallel-level representation from both interest groups, plus the ratifier. All six pre-meeting questions are answered before anyone walks in. After presentations and questioning, the decision is made in the room and the ratifier signs off without anyone surfacing afterwards as a Johnny-come-lately.

**Result:** The plant gets built, four stories, next to the existing facility. Most of the value of the meeting was front-loaded — by the time the chairman walked in, the meeting could not really fail.

---

**Situation:** A new VP inherits a team that has a daily 9 a.m. stand-up, a Monday staff meeting, a Wednesday strategy meeting, a Friday all-hands, and a continuous stream of "got a sec?" interruptions. He's drowning, and so is everyone else.

**Application:** The fix is not to cancel the staff meeting. The fix is to install one-on-ones at a frequency keyed to each subordinate's task-relevant maturity, run a tight weekly staff meeting with an agenda issued in advance, and starve the ad-hoc traffic by routing it onto either the one-on-one outline or a "hold" file for the next staff. The five existing meetings collapse to a one-on-one cycle plus a staff meeting plus a quarterly ops review. Within two months, the impromptu interruptions drop because the channels are full.

**Result:** Total meeting hours go down; output goes up; the VP no longer has an open door that consumes his calendar in five-minute chunks.

---

**Situation:** A senior leader sits in on a staff meeting where two engineers spend forty minutes debating a build-vs-buy question that affects only their two projects. The other six people in the room watch.

**Application:** Per the rule that anything occupying a staff meeting must affect more than two of the people present, the supervisor breaks off the conversation, asks the two to take it offline, and moves to the next agenda item. He follows up later with each one to make sure the conversation actually happened — a hold-file item for next week.

**Result:** The remaining six people get their hour back; the two-person issue gets resolved without a captive audience; the supervisor has demonstrated, by example, what a staff meeting is and isn't for.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hold a mission-oriented meeting because "we should probably get aligned."
**Why:** Alignment is not an output. Decisions are. If the meeting cannot end with a decision recorded in writing, it should not have been called. Send a memo or an email; reserve the room for the calls only a meeting can produce.

**Don't:** Let the most senior person in the room state his view first.
**Why:** State your view first, and the room re-assembles around it within thirty seconds. You lose the only thing the meeting was supposed to produce — independent judgment from the people closer to the work than you. Speak last.

**Don't:** Run a staff meeting without an agenda issued in advance.
**Why:** Without prep, the meeting decays into whatever's loudest in the head of whoever speaks first. Issue the agenda far enough ahead that subordinates can think; preserve a short open-session block for live items, but do not let the open session swallow the meeting.

**Don't:** Use a one-on-one slot to hold a mission-oriented decision meeting.
**Why:** The two formats do different work. A one-on-one is the subordinate's meeting, his agenda. A decision meeting has a chairman, a defined output, and a different roster. Collapsing them ruins both — the subordinate stops bringing the obscure issues that the one-on-one exists to surface, because the slot has been hijacked.

**Don't:** Treat "we needed an offsite to align" as a sign of organizational health.
**Why:** A team that requires an offsite to find alignment is a team whose process meetings have stopped doing their job. The offsite cures the symptom — the dysfunctional cadence will reproduce it within a quarter.

**Don't:** Skip the operation review because "the senior person is too busy."
**Why:** The operation review is precisely how the senior person stays in contact with managers two levels down. Skip it and you are signaling that what those managers do is not worth your attention — which means the next time bad news from their level needs to travel up, it will not.

**Don't:** Allow status presentations to fill a mission-oriented meeting.
**Why:** Status is a process-meeting input, not a decision-meeting input. If half the agenda is "where we are," the chairman has confused the two formats. Pre-read the status; spend the room time on the decision.

**Don't:** Forgo minutes because "everyone was there."
**Why:** Memory degrades within hours. Without written minutes, the meeting's decisions become contestable inside a week and the chairman finds himself running the same meeting twice. Cheap insurance — write them, send them, the day of.
