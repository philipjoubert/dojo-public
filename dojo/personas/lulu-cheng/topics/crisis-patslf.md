# Crisis (PATSLF)

## When to Use
- Serious injury or fatality has occurred
- Illegal behavior is in question (fraud, misconduct, violation)
- A major company promise has been broken at scale
- Government agencies are involved (investigation, subpoena, regulatory action)
- Significant financial or operational disruption
- If a reasonable outside observer would call this a crisis, treat it as one

## Fails When
- You treat an attack (not an actual crisis) with full crisis mode — looks weak, validates the attack
- You move at full speed on unverified information — preliminary "facts" become commitments you can't keep
- You handle external first and employees learn from Twitter or the news
- You say "no comment" or "mistakes were made" — invertebrate mantra
- You apologize for things out of your control (the storm) instead of things you controlled
- You let legal advise you into silence so strict it reads as guilt

## Core Concept

Don't get ready. Stay ready.

When crisis is upon you, it's too late to craft a good plan. By then, time is short, noise is high, trust is low, and anger is building — all while you're squinting through the fog of war. The best crisis response is crisis prevention. The second best is crisis preparation. Companies that have pre-drafted templates, designated teams, and tabletop-exercised scenarios respond faster and better. Companies that haven't are scrambling while the clock runs.

Crisis is specific. It's not every bad situation. A crisis is something that disrupts your ability to continue normal business operations, seriously jeopardizes trust with customers, or materially affects finances and valuation. Triggers: serious injuries or fatalities, illegal behavior, important promise broken at scale, government agencies involved, major financial misconduct. Bad press alone is usually not a crisis — it's an attack (see `hit-pieces.md`). Confusing the two is costly. Treating a crisis like a mere attack (fighting back without addressing the underlying problem) makes you look callous. Treating an attack like a crisis (going into full damage-control mode) makes you look weak and validates the attack.

When crisis hits, follow PATSLF in priority order.

- **P — People.** Keep your team safe and informed. Before anything else, your people need to know what's going on and that leadership is handling it.
- **A — Accuracy.** Become the single source of accurate information. In crisis, rumors spread fast. If you don't establish yourself as the authoritative source, others will — and they'll get things wrong.
- **T — Transparency.** Share what you can, but only what you're sure of. Transparency doesn't mean saying everything. It means not hiding things you could responsibly share.
- **S — Speed.** Move quickly. Knock down falsehoods before they ossify. Every hour a false narrative is uncorrected, it hardens.
- **L — Legal.** Involve counsel, but find the transparency balance. The safest legal position ("say nothing, admit nothing") is often the worst reputational position.
- **F — Focus.** Most important facts to most important people. Bandwidth is limited. Don't try to tell everything to everyone.

The single most important rule inside all of this: internal before external. Always. External crisis is inconvenient. Internal crisis is existential. When you see companies fall apart and unravel, it's because of internal crisis, not external. Tell your employees before you tell the world.

## How to Apply

1. **Activate the crisis team immediately.** If you don't have one, you've already lost hours. Designate the call makers, the spokesperson, the legal point, the internal comms lead.

2. **Inform employees first.** Email from CEO. All-hands if significant enough. Manager talking points for team discussions. Then — and only then — external. The gap can be short (even an hour is enough), but the sequence matters.

3. **Establish the single source of truth.** One place people (internal and external) get accurate information. Update it as facts develop.

4. **Move on verified facts only.** Don't bank on uncertain early information that could evolve. "We're still learning" beats a preliminary conclusion stated as fact.

5. **Navigate legal toward transparency.** Legal will often advise maximum caution. Push for statements that are both defensible AND transparent. That's harder than either extreme. It's also what the situation requires.

6. **Focus ruthlessly.** Who absolutely needs to know first? What do they absolutely need to know? Start there.

7. **Apologize properly if you're at fault.** See `the-apology.md`. In crisis, the fog of war means it's hard to get everything right. But if you're going to get one thing right, let it be the apology — it's what people will remember.

8. **Follow through, then follow up.** Commitments made in crisis become commitments you have to keep. Missing them creates a second crisis.

## Examples

**Situation:** Serious incident at a company involving a customer-facing failure with regulatory implications. Team instinct is to wait for legal review, issue a press release in 48 hours, handle internal "when we know more."
**Application:** PATSLF says reverse that. People first — inform employees within hours, with what's known. Accuracy second — establish the internal wiki or all-hands update as the single source. Transparency third — share what you can, explicitly mark what you don't know. Speed fourth — don't wait 48 hours. Legal fifth — work with counsel toward transparent-and-defensible statements, not silent-and-defensible. Focus last — concentrate on what the most critical audiences need.
**Result:** Employees aren't blindsided by the news. Customers see the company as the source of truth, not one of several possible sources. Trust holds through the fog.

**Situation:** Executive misconduct surfaces. Company is drafting a careful legal-blessed statement that doesn't say much. It'll go out in a few days.
**Application:** The invertebrate mantra — "mistakes were made" — fails here. Passive voice, no actor, no accountability. Instead: a specific human (probably the CEO) owns the statement, says what happened in active voice, takes responsibility for what the company controlled, and commits to specific changes with specific timelines.
**Result:** The apology is what people remember. A weak apology is often worse than no apology. Getting this right buys the company the chance to recover.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Say "no comment." It reads as hiding something. If you can't answer, explain why. "We can't discuss this because of the ongoing investigation" is better than silence.
**Why:** Silence in crisis is interpreted as guilt or cover-up. The absence of comment is itself a comment.

**Don't:** Use passive voice. "Mistakes were made." This is the invertebrate mantra. Passive voice is evasion. Active voice is accountability. Someone did something — say who did what.
**Why:** Audiences read passive voice in crisis as dishonesty. If no one did anything, why is there a problem? Active voice forces specificity, which builds credibility.

**Don't:** Use humor or sarcasm. Don't get defensive when a reporter is combative. You're on display. How you handle aggression becomes the story.
**Why:** Crisis is the wrong register for humor. It reads as not taking the situation seriously, and it distracts from the actual communication.

**Don't:** Apologize for things outside your control. "We're sorry about the storm" is a non-apology. You're not committing to anything. Everyone can see through it.
**Why:** If you're not committing to anything, what's the apology for? Corporate non-apologies are worse than no apology — they show you understand you should apologize and are choosing to fake it.

**Don't:** Declare victory prematurely. "This is behind us" while the fire is still smoldering invites blowback.
**Why:** Every claim you make during crisis is a commitment. Premature victory is a promise you'll break the moment the next revelation hits.

**Don't:** Handle external first and "eventually" tell employees. Employees who learn from the press become enemies. Employees who learn from leadership — even when news is bad — remain allies.
**Why:** Internal crisis is existential. External is inconvenient. If you reverse the sequence, your internal reality deteriorates while you're managing the external.

**Don't:** Let legal advise you into total silence. Legal's job is to minimize legal risk. Your job is to weigh legal risk against reputational risk. The strictest legal position is often the worst reputational one.
**Why:** You can be compliant AND have a human voice. The two aren't incompatible. Companies that hide behind "our lawyers won't let us" often lose more in reputation than they would have in legal exposure.
