# Under Attack (Hit Pieces)

## When to Use
- You're under attack from a specific person, outlet, or campaign
- A hit piece has dropped
- A viral post is spreading false claims about you
- You've run the "does it matter" diagnostic and the answer is yes, it matters
- The broken nose has just happened and you're deciding whether to reset it now

## Fails When
- This is actually a crisis, not an attack — run PATSLF instead
- You respond emotionally — anger reads as guilt or weakness
- You wait, hoping it blows over — it doesn't, it ossifies
- You over-respond to a minor attack and amplify it
- You apologize for things you didn't do to "de-escalate"
- You attack back in ways you can't document

## Core Concept

Someone is trying to harm your reputation. This isn't a crisis — the underlying business is functioning. This isn't criticism — they're not acting in good faith. This is an attack: a deliberate attempt to damage you. Respond immediately and aggressively. Or don't respond at all. There's no middle ground.

First pass the diagnostic: does it matter? Is it reaching people that matter, AND is it material? If either is no, ignore. Responding to attacks that don't matter validates them. You draw attention to things your audience would never have noticed. You give the attacker the fight they wanted. But if both are yes, the broken nose principle applies. Every hour that a false narrative goes uncorrected is an hour it hardens. Rumors become "common knowledge" shockingly fast. The instinct to wait — let me just maybe it'll just go away by itself — is wrong. It doesn't go away by itself. It ossifies. Palmer Luckey learned this the hard way: "A year of not trimming my hedges turned into a garden of weeds that could never be unpulled."

Categorize the attacker. There are three kinds. **Sustained campaign fighters** are in a long-term fight with you. A competitor who's made attacking you part of their positioning. A journalist who's made you their beat. An activist organization that's targeted your industry. You have to engage them whether you like it or not — if they have an audience, they'll keep repeating false claims until people believe them. The goal isn't to convince them (they're not persuadable) — it's to create a counter-narrative people can find. **Traction gainers** are picking up momentum with an attack but aren't long-term enemies. A random viral post. A one-off journalist story. Quick correction, push back on specifics, provide facts. Before their claim becomes received wisdom. **Useful foils** say the quiet part out loud. They've articulated the opposing argument so clearly that engaging is actually useful — you highlight that this IS the real argument. "Yes, exactly. We do believe that. Here's why."

## How to Apply

1. **Run the "does it matter" diagnostic first.** Reaching people who matter? Material accusation? Both yes → proceed. Either no → ignore.

2. **Categorize the attacker.** Sustained? Traction gainer? Useful foil? Different categories warrant different responses.

3. **Respond now, not later.** Broken nose principle. The cost of imperfect-and-fast is almost always lower than perfect-and-slow.

4. **Be specific.** Don't issue vague denials — vague denials sound like spin. Quote the specific claim. Provide the specific counter-evidence. "They said we did X. Here's the document showing we did Y. Here's the context they omitted."

5. **Make it personal.** Show up yourself. Don't hide behind corporate statements. "Our position is..." sounds like legal-approved corporate speak. "I'm going to explain exactly what happened" sounds like a human with something to say. The attacker made it personal. Respond personally.

6. **Narrow the target.** Apply the pressure equation on offense. Don't complain about "the media" or "critics." That sounds paranoid. This specific person made this specific claim, and here's why that specific claim is wrong. Focused, specific, documented.

7. **Fight stories with stories.** If they're attacking with emotional stories, you need better emotional stories, not statistics. The most powerful statistic is probably not as powerful as the median story. Someone attacks with "here's this poor customer who was screwed over." You respond with "99% of our customers are satisfied." You lose. You need a better story — what actually happened with that customer, and another customer whose life you changed.

8. **Don't apologize for things you didn't do.** If you're being unfairly attacked, do not apologize. "I'm sorry you feel that way" is weak. "We could have done better" when you actually did fine is an admission against interest. Acknowledge feelings if useful. Explain facts. Don't give them the apology they're fishing for.

9. **Think deterrence.** Others are watching. Future attackers are taking notes. A forceful, effective response doesn't just win this fight — it raises the expected cost of attacking you.

## Examples

**Situation:** Palmer Luckey attacked in 2016 over political donations. The Facebook advice: stay quiet, don't engage, let it blow over.
**Application:** He took the advice. Said nothing. A year of silence.
**Result:** "A year of not trimming my hedges turned into a garden of weeds that could never be unpulled. Not in 10 years." The narrative ossified. Ten years of recovery.

**Situation:** A viral tweet claims "Palmer Luckey, who famously supplied [false claim]." Picking up traction, being retweeted by people who matter.
**Application:** Traction gainer category. Not a sustained campaign. Quick, factual correction: "This is not true. Here's what actually happened." Specific, documented, personal.
**Result:** The claim stops spreading before it becomes received wisdom. The cost of pushback was low because the response was precise.

**Situation:** A critic writes a piece saying "The problem with [Company] is that they believe [true thing you actually do believe], which is terrible because [reasons]."
**Application:** Useful foil. They've drawn the battle lines clearly. Engage with them to sharpen your own position: "Yes, exactly. We do believe that. Here's why."
**Result:** The attack gets converted into an argument you want to have, with framing you control.

**Situation:** A journalist attacks Substack with a story about "Substack platforming dangerous voices."
**Application:** Spread the surface area (defense mode). Narrow the specific factual attack if applicable. Don't defend "Substack" as a company. "You're not attacking Substack. You're attacking all independent writers asserting freedom of expression." Specific counter to specific claims, personal voice, coalition activated.
**Result:** The attack dissipates across a larger surface. Independent writers show up. The company isn't defending alone.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Wait. The instinct that it will "blow over" is wrong. A year of silence turns into a decade of pulling weeds. Narratives ossify. Respond now.
**Why:** Broken nose principle. Wait too long and you're choosing between "live with it crooked" or "break it from scratch later." Both are worse than immediate reset.

**Don't:** Respond emotionally. Anger is understandable but counterproductive. An emotional response looks defensive. It gives the attacker satisfaction. It shifts the story from "here's what they did wrong" to "look how upset they are."
**Why:** Cold, precise, factual is the register for response. Emotion is a gift to the attacker.

**Don't:** Over-respond to minor attacks. Bringing overwhelming force to a minor skirmish amplifies the attack. It tells the world this attack really bothered you. It makes the attacker look more important than they are.
**Why:** Match response to threat. Major attacks get major responses. Minor attacks get corrections. Over-response is its own form of losing.

**Don't:** Apologize to de-escalate. "I'm sorry you feel that way" is weak and condescending. "We could have done better" when you actually did fine is an admission against interest.
**Why:** If you didn't do something wrong, apologizing creates a false admission the attacker will quote forever. Acknowledge feelings without conceding fault. Acknowledgment ≠ apology.

**Don't:** Complain broadly about "the media" or "the establishment." That sounds paranoid — tinfoil hat territory.
**Why:** Broad targets mean no one feels personally responsible. Pressure is zero. Specific targets with documented claims create accountability; broad complaints create ridicule.

**Don't:** Bring statistics to a story fight. If someone attacks with "here's the one customer who got screwed," responding with "99% are satisfied" makes you lose.
**Why:** Stories beat statistics. Always. The median story outperforms the most powerful statistic. You need a better story, not better numbers.

**Don't:** Treat every sustained campaign fighter as convincible. Don't treat every traction gainer as an existential threat.
**Why:** Category mismatch means effort mismatch. You'll either waste months trying to convert an opponent or panic about a one-off post that would die on its own with a precise correction.
