# Hook, Story, Medium

## When to Use
- You're planning a content piece and the team is debating platforms first
- You're evaluating whether to accept a podcast invite or media opportunity
- You're about to launch and need to decide where to put your weight
- Someone on the team asks "should we be on TikTok?" and you don't know why they're asking

## Fails When
- The hook is clickbait with no substance — the story doesn't deliver on the hook's promise
- Your medium doesn't fit your story (deep, nuanced argument on a platform built for 15-second clips)
- You have a hook but no clear audience — you're entertaining, not converting
- You optimize for the medium's format instead of what you actually need to say

## Core Concept

Most people plan communications backwards.

They start with: "What podcast can I get on?" or "Should we be on TikTok?" Then they figure out what to say once they've secured the slot. This is wrong. Medium is the last thing you should decide, not the first. The wrong order — medium first, then story, then a hook as afterthought — produces content optimized for the format, not for impact. You end up saying whatever fits the medium rather than using the medium to say what matters.

Reverse it. Hook first. Story second. Medium last. The hook is the most overlooked and highest-leverage piece. Without a hook, nobody pays attention. The story is pointless if nobody's listening. Hooks aren't clickbait — clickbait promises something it doesn't deliver; a hook promises something you actually deliver. Good hooks share characteristics: genuine humor (not "corporate funny"), curiosity (a gap between what someone knows and what they want to know), strong emotion (wow, WTF, that's incredible), or a new angle on something they're already thinking about. The hook just has to answer: "Why should I pay attention to this instead of the thousand other things competing for my attention?"

The medium obsession is often a way of avoiding the hard work of figuring out the hook and story. It's much easier to debate platforms than to figure out what you actually have to say. The medium is a function of where your audience is, what format suits the story, and what the business objective is. But all three of those questions depend on knowing what you're trying to say and why anyone should care. Get that right first, and the medium question answers itself.

## How to Apply

1. **Start with the hook.** Answer: why should anyone care about this instead of the thousand things competing for their attention? Options: humor, curiosity, strong emotion, a new angle on something they're already interested in.

2. **Build the story that delivers.** The hook promises; the story delivers. If the hook says "here's the most counterintuitive thing about AI regulation," the story has to actually contain that thing. No bait and switch.

3. **Match medium to story.** Deep, nuanced arguments need long-form — essay, podcast, video. Quick punchy hooks work on short-form. Match the content to the container, not the other way around.

4. **Match medium to audience.** Your audience isn't everywhere. Don't be on TikTok if they're not. Don't pursue TV if they don't watch TV.

5. **Engineer the conversion path.** What do you want the audience to DO after consuming this? Sign up for the waitlist? Apply for a job? Request a demo? Tell a friend? If there's no "and then what," you're entertaining, not converting.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is about to launch and the team is spending weeks planning which publications to brief, which podcasts to pitch, which events to announce at. The medium planning is meticulous. The hook planning is an afterthought: "Well, we're launching, that's the hook."
**Application:** "Company launches product" isn't a hook. It's a category. Every launch is "company launches product." The team has to answer: what's the angle that makes someone who's never heard of us lean in? Is there a surprise? A specific enemy? A claim nobody else would make?
**Result:** The launch that has a real hook breaks through. The launch that only has a press list gets buried.

**Situation:** Barbie movie marketing. They could have led with "here's the trailer, here's the release date, here's where to watch."
**Application:** Instead, they led with the hook — visual canvases so absurd, meme-worthy, and personalizable that people had to share them. "This Barbie is a lawyer." "This Barbie is an architect." Hook first. Then the story (a movie about identity and womanhood). Then the medium (theatrical release pumped by ambient meme distribution).
**Result:** People weren't promoting Barbie — they were expressing their own identities using Barbie materials. The hook generated the distribution that the mediums couldn't have bought.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Start with "we should be on [hot platform]" and reverse-engineer content to fit. The result is content that fits the format but doesn't serve the objective.
**Why:** The medium doesn't carry signal on its own. It's a vessel. Without a hook and a story, the vessel is empty no matter how well you fit the format.

**Don't:** Confuse clickbait for a hook. Clickbait over-promises and under-delivers. The story never lands because the audience is already betrayed.
**Why:** One clickbait post costs more than a hundred honest ones. You trained the audience to discount you. Trust is the compounding asset. Clickbait spends it for a one-time hit.

**Don't:** Treat "we're launching" as a hook. That's a category, not a hook. Every launch says "we're launching."
**Why:** The attention question is comparative. What makes this moment matter versus every other moment fighting for the same eyeballs? "Launching" doesn't answer that. Specific insight, absurdity, or conflict does.

**Don't:** Skip the conversion step. Attention without conversion is vanity. Remember yesterday when we went viral? High five. That felt great. People move on.
**Why:** The work of the hook and the story is wasted if you haven't engineered what happens next. Attention that goes nowhere dissipates by end of week.

**Don't:** Confuse "medium matters" with "medium is the hardest problem." The hook is the hardest problem. Teams obsess about medium because it feels concrete and debatable. The hook feels fuzzy, so they skip it.
**Why:** You can always find a medium. You can't always find a hook. The hard work is the hook. Do it first.
