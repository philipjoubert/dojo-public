# Value Networks

## When to Use
- An established company is trying to decide whether to chase a technology that seems, at first, to serve a smaller, lower-margin, worse-performing customer than its current base.
- A pattern of failure is repeating in an industry across successive generations of leaders — the same mistake is being made by the third or fourth generation of "best-managed" firms.
- A founder asks whether a disruptive entrant should sell to the incumbent's customers or to different customers.
- A strategist is arguing that "our existing customers want this" is the validation signal for a new product direction.
- An analyst is about to assume that the real test of a disruptive technology is whether the mainstream market would adopt it.

## Fails When
- Applied to a green-field startup with no existing customer base — the pull forces that define value networks don't yet exist for the firm.
- Used fatalistically. Incumbents *can* cross value-network boundaries; the way they do it is almost always by setting up a separate organization inside a separate value network, with its own customers, its own cost structure, and its own P&L. The framework is a diagnostic for why it's hard, not a proof that it's impossible.
- The technology in question is purely sustaining — improving the existing product on existing dimensions for existing customers. Value-network analysis is for transitions that cross customer, cost-structure, and ecosystem boundaries.

## Core Concept

A company does not exist in a vacuum. It exists inside a network of suppliers who sell into its processes, customers who buy its outputs, distributors who carry those outputs to market, cost structures that have been calibrated to produce acceptable margins given those customer prices, and capital markets that judge the company by the metrics of that particular network. That whole ecosystem — the bundle of suppliers, customers, distribution channels, cost structure, and capital expectations — is what we call a *value network*. And once a company is inside one, the network is not a passive backdrop. It pulls.

Here is how the pull works. The customers in a firm's value network constantly demand better versions of the existing product — faster, more capacity, more features, higher performance along the dimensions they already care about. The firm's salespeople hear this demand daily. The firm's product managers respond to it. The firm's engineers are evaluated on how well they deliver it. The firm's profitability depends on the higher margins that come from serving the most sophisticated customers in the network. The firm's stock price is set by analysts who benchmark growth and margins against other firms in the same network. Every one of these inputs — customers, salespeople, engineers, finance, analysts — is telling the firm to invest more heavily in serving the customers it already has, in the way it already serves them. A well-run firm will respond to that signal rationally and repeatedly. That is what being "well-managed" means inside a value network.

A disruptive technology — typically a simpler, smaller, cheaper product that performs worse on the metrics the mainstream network values but offers some combination of convenience, accessibility, or lower cost — does not serve anyone in that network. Mainstream customers reject it because it performs worse on what they care about. Salespeople have no channel through which to sell it. Distributors have no incentive to carry it. The cost structure necessary to make a profit on it is utterly different from the cost structure the firm has painstakingly built. So when the firm's strategists look at the disruptive opportunity and ask "should we pursue this?" the answer they get back through the normal channels is "no — our customers aren't asking for it, we'd make less money per unit, and it doesn't fit our manufacturing or go-to-market model." That answer is not stupidity. It is an accurate reflection of what the value network supports. The firm is trapped by its own coherence. And the disruptive entrant, whose whole existence is in a *different* value network with different customers and a different cost structure, faces no such trap.

The canonical laboratory for this idea was the disk-drive industry between roughly 1975 and 1995. Each generation of disk drive — 14-inch, 8-inch, 5.25-inch, 3.5-inch, and on down — established itself in a different value network. The 14-inch drives served mainframe computers; 8-inch drives served the emerging minicomputer market; 5.25-inch served the early desktop PC; 3.5-inch served portable and laptop computers. At each transition, the existing leaders had the technology — engineers at the mainstream firms frequently built working prototypes of the new form factor, sometimes on bootleg resources, years before commercial products appeared. The leaders just couldn't sell those prototypes through their existing channels, because their existing mainframe customers didn't want an 8-inch drive with 20MB of capacity when they needed 200MB. Between one-half and two-thirds of the established manufacturers failed to introduce models at each generational transition. The new entrants — which were typically spinouts or start-ups whose entire existence was in the new value network — won the emerging market, then rode the sustaining-innovation trajectory of the new drive up into the mainstream until they toppled the incumbents in the old market too.

Notice the structure of the pattern. It's not that disk-drive executives were incompetent. It's that they were responding rationally to the signals their value network was generating. The value network is the incumbent's cage. You cannot simply decide to leave it because an analyst's deck says you should, any more than a fish can decide to live in a different aquarium. The water is what supports you.

## How to Apply

1. **Map the current value network explicitly.** For the firm in question, name: the mainstream customers (who specifically? what size? what margin profile?), the suppliers (who feeds into production, and what are their dependencies on the firm?), the cost structure (what minimum gross margin keeps the salesforce motivated? the plant profitable? the analysts satisfied?), the capital-market expectations (what growth rate and margin profile is priced into the stock?). The answers to these questions define the walls of the cage.

2. **For a proposed new product, ask whether it fits the value network or breaks it.** A sustaining innovation — one that improves performance along the dimensions the mainstream customers already care about — fits. A new technology that would require a different customer (different size, different buying criteria, different price point), a different cost structure (lower margin, different asset intensity), or a different channel (different salesforce, different distributor) breaks the network. Those are two entirely different strategic problems, and treating them the same way is the most common error in innovation strategy.

3. **Do not use "our existing customers say they want it" as the test.** Existing customers live inside the current value network. They will reliably ask for more of what they already buy, delivered better. They are structurally incapable of asking you for a product that serves a different job for a different buyer — they don't know that buyer and wouldn't care if they did. A customer-voice-driven process is an exquisite sustaining-innovation engine and a near-guaranteed disruption blind spot.

4. **If the new product breaks the value network, do not run it inside the mainstream.** The mainstream organization's processes, values, customers, and incentives will starve the project, not from malice but from coherence. Stand up a separate organization inside the target value network — its own P&L, its own customers, its own cost structure, its own people who are evaluated on the metrics that matter in the new network. The disruptive organization should feel as different from the mainstream as if it were a different company.

5. **Watch for the up-market migration.** Disruptive entrants don't stay in their founding value network. Once established, they ride their own sustaining-innovation trajectory up-market, picking off the lower tier of the next-tier customer base first, then the middle, then eventually colliding with the incumbent's core. The time to respond is before this migration reaches the incumbent's mainstream, which means while the disruptor still looks small and unthreatening. Incumbents who wait until the disruptor is obviously a threat are, without exception, too late.

## Examples

**Situation:** In the late 1970s, Seagate and other 5.25-inch disk drive manufacturers emerged as scrappy start-ups serving the new desktop PC market. The established leaders of the disk-drive industry — firms making 8-inch drives for minicomputers — had the engineering talent to produce 5.25-inch drives and, in fact, several of them did build prototypes. Their minicomputer customers, however, didn't want a 5.25-inch drive with 10MB of capacity when they needed 60MB on an 8-inch.

**Application:** The 8-inch incumbents' existing value network — minicomputer OEMs as customers, a cost structure built around those OEMs' prices, and salespeople trained to sell to them — generated a consistent "no" signal for the 5.25-inch project. The resource-allocation process inside each 8-inch firm, properly tuned to the needs of the existing customers, redirected engineering attention toward sustaining improvements of the 8-inch drive, which is what the customers were explicitly asking for. The 5.25-inch start-ups, existing in a completely different value network serving desktop PC makers, faced no such pull. They built the 5.25-inch business in its own ecosystem and then rode its sustaining-innovation curve up until the 5.25-inch was capable enough to serve minicomputers too — at which point the 5.25-inch entrants took over the 8-inch market.

**Result:** Roughly two-thirds of 8-inch manufacturers failed to survive the transition to 5.25-inch. The same pattern — incumbents failing at the next generational transition even though they had the technology — repeated verbatim at the 5.25-inch to 3.5-inch transition, and again at 3.5-inch to 2.5-inch. Four generations of "best-managed" firms, same script. The firms weren't badly managed. They were managed well — inside value networks that happened to be dissolving beneath them.

**Situation:** In the 1960s, integrated steel companies like U.S. Steel and Bethlehem served the high-end sheet-steel market that supplied automobile manufacturers, appliance makers, and construction. Their customers demanded specific alloy compositions, specific surface finishes, and reliable delivery of tens of thousands of tons. The integrated mills' cost structure required all this — the blast furnaces and associated infrastructure cost billions and had to be filled with high-margin orders. Mini-mill entrants like Nucor began at the bottom, melting scrap and producing concrete rebar — the lowest-margin, least-demanding steel product in the industry.

**Application:** When mini-mills took the rebar market, integrated steel makers cheered. Rebar was the least profitable segment in their mix; losing it actually increased integrated-mill margins. The integrated firms' value network — auto and appliance customers, the associated cost structure, and the capital-market expectation of high-margin sheet production — meant that losing rebar to mini-mills felt like good news. Then mini-mills, riding their own sustaining-innovation curve, climbed into angle iron and bar stock. Integrated mills cheered again — angle iron was the next-lowest-margin segment. Then mini-mills reached structural beams, then sheet steel. At every step, incumbent integrated mills were happy to exit the low-end segment where mini-mill competition appeared, because their value network told them that retreating up-market was the margin-accretive move.

**Result:** By the time mini-mills had reached the sheet-steel market in the 1990s, the integrated mills had been pushed into the highest-margin, smallest remaining segments, and the economics of running an integrated mill on a narrowing product line collapsed. Multiple integrated producers went bankrupt. Again, at every step, the integrated-mill managers made the locally rational decision. The value network of integrated steel wouldn't let them fight at the low end; the mini-mill value network wouldn't have let them not.

**Situation:** Xerox in the 1970s and 80s dominated high-volume office copiers sold and leased to Fortune 500 companies through a direct salesforce, with service contracts on machines priced at tens of thousands of dollars. Canon entered the copier market with desktop units priced at a few thousand dollars, sold through dealers to small businesses and individual offices that had previously been too small to buy or lease a Xerox machine.

**Application:** Xerox's value network — large-enterprise customers, direct salesforce compensation tied to lease revenue per placement, a cost structure that required high per-unit prices, and an R&D process tuned to the needs of mainstream customers — generated the consistent signal that serving small businesses was unattractive. Xerox management was not unaware of Canon's tabletop copiers. Xerox built them. What Xerox couldn't do was sell them through a salesforce whose compensation structure would collapse if forced to sell low-priced tabletops instead of leased high-volume machines, and Xerox couldn't afford a separate distribution model at the margins tabletops produced.

**Result:** Canon built the small-business copier market with no resistance from Xerox, then rode that curve up into the large-enterprise market Xerox had owned. Xerox's direct salesforce — once its weapon — became its prison, because retooling it would have destroyed the cash flow that paid for the rest of the company.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume your existing customers would buy a better version of a disruptive product.

**Why:** A disruptive product, almost by definition, performs worse on the dimensions your existing customers care about — that's what makes it disruptive. It exists inside a different value network, with different customers who value different things (typically simplicity, convenience, accessibility, or low cost) and with a different cost structure that permits it to be profitable at lower price points. Your existing customers, embedded in your current value network, are telling you through every sales call and product-management meeting to keep improving the product along the trajectory they already buy from you. If you ask them "would you buy this smaller, cheaper, less capable thing?" they will tell you no, and they will be correct — in their context, they wouldn't. But the answer to that survey question has almost nothing to do with whether the disruptive product is an existential threat. The threat emerges from a different set of customers who don't yet exist as customers of yours, served through a different channel at a different margin, and it only arrives in your market after the new product has ridden its own sustaining-innovation curve up into the range where it can finally serve your mainstream. Running the disruptive idea past your existing customers is not due diligence; it is a ritual of reassurance that produces false negatives every time. The correct test is to ask: who would buy this, if anyone? What cost structure would make it profitable? What value network does it belong in? And is that value network likely to grow into mine?
