# Purpose Brands

## When to Use
- You have a product that does a job well and you want to build a brand that customers associate with that job.
- You're deciding whether to extend an existing brand to a new product, and you need a rule to tell you whether the extension will reinforce or erode the brand.
- Your brand is fading into an "endorser" — a vague signal of general quality — and you can feel it losing its ability to guide customers.
- You're watching a competitor claim a job with a clear purpose brand and wondering why you can't dislodge them even with a better-performing product.
- You're about to spend heavily on advertising to "build brand awareness" and you don't yet have a clearly defined job.

## Fails When
- **The product doesn't actually do the job.** A purpose brand put on top of a product that does the job poorly is a promise the customer will catch you breaking. Trust collapses fast.
- **You attach the brand to too many jobs at once.** Different jobs demand different purpose brands. Force one brand to cover many and it becomes an endorser — which is a diluted thing, not a stronger thing.
- **You try to build the brand through advertising before the product earns it.** Advertising can clarify a job that a good product already does. It cannot stuff meaning into a word. Executives who spend hundreds of millions trying end up "trapped in an expensive, endless arms race with competitors whose brands are comparably vague."

## Core Concept

When a customer realizes they have a job to do, the very next thought is "darn it, I have to buy something to get this done." Then they look around. If nothing specific pops into their head, they have to shop — test drive, compare, return, re-buy, cobble together. That is expensive. Not just in money, but in time and in the anxiety of making the wrong choice. The reward for a marketer who can cause a single brand to *pop into the customer's mind the moment the job arises* is enormous. That brand is what we call a *purpose brand*.

A purpose brand is one that is synonymous with the job. FedEx, for the "I-need-to-send-this-from-here-to-there-with-perfect-certainty-as-fast-as-possible" job. Google, for "find me information." Disney, for "a family vacation where the parents can actually relax." Volvo, for "keep my family safe." Uber, for "get me from here to there right now without having to think about it." When the job shows up, the brand shows up. The customer stops shopping. They will pay a premium, because buying a product that doesn't do the job well is so costly that they are delighted to pay extra for one that does.

A clear purpose brand works as a two-sided compass. One side guides customers to the right product. The other side guides the company's designers, marketers, and advertisers as they develop and improve products. It tells the company what to build and what not to build. It tells the customer what to buy and — just as importantly — when *not* to buy. The price premium that a purpose brand commands is, in effect, the wage the customer pays for this guidance on both sides.

Consider Ikea. Christensen's son Michael went to Stanford for graduate school, and after about a week he called home. "Mom and Dad, I've found my apartment. I need to furnish my apartment tomorrow." That is the job. And when that job shows up in your life, a brand pops into your head. Ikea. Ikea has been rolling out its business model around the world for fifty years and they have no competitors. They have no secrets. It's not that there's no money in it — the owner is one of the richest men in the world, and he got rich selling substandard furniture to college students and the low end of humanity. Why will those students pay a premium? Because going to one store and one store and buying a piece of furniture and throwing it away and assembling something else is so costly that they are delighted to pay for a brand that just does the whole thing in an afternoon. The purpose brand is the moat. And it is stronger than any product-feature advantage a competitor could assemble, because feature parity is easy to copy and a purpose brand is not.

Here is the rule for extending a purpose brand without killing it. There are two legitimate extensions. One: develop different products that address the *same job* (Sony extending Walkman from cassette to CD — the job was "escape the chaos in my world," and the CD player did that job too). Two: create a new, related purpose brand alongside the original, using the original as an endorser (Marriott keeping the master brand for large-meeting hotels, then creating Courtyard by Marriott for the business-traveler-who-needs-a-quiet-place-to-work job, Residence Inn for the long-stay job). What you cannot do is stretch one purpose brand across jobs that have nothing in common. That collapses the brand into an endorser — a vague signal of general quality, still worth something but no longer capable of telling the customer what to buy when a specific job arises. Crest was a purpose brand for the "prevent my children's cavities" job. When P&G started using the Crest name for whitening and breath freshening and plaque reduction, Crest stopped being a purpose brand and became an endorser. Johnson & Johnson walked into the vacancy with ACT for the cavity-prevention job space. That is the cost of a sloppy extension.

Aspirational jobs are the one category where the rules shift. Gucci, Absolut, Montblanc, Virgin — these are brands hired to make the customer *feel* a certain way. Macho. Sassy. Pampered. Prestigious. In these cases the brand itself, more than the product's functional attributes, is the thing doing the job. For aspirational jobs, the brand must be built through advertising and imagery, because the imagery is a material part of how the job gets done. But this is the exception, not the template. Most managers mistake their product for an aspirational job and waste hundreds of millions trying to advertise substance into existence.

## How to Apply

1. **Define the job in one sentence that a customer would recognize.** Not "premium gourmet food." The job: "a meal I can serve to guests that makes me look like I put in effort I didn't actually put in." Not "financial services." The job: "help me sleep at night knowing my family is taken care of if something happens to me." If you can't write the sentence, you don't have a purpose-brand candidate yet.

2. **Confirm the product actually does the job.** Purpose brands only work when the product performs. Test with real customers in the real circumstance. If the product fails the job in one out of three trials, the brand will fail in customers' minds the moment they swap a story.

3. **Name the product in a way that signals the job.** Names that describe the job — FedEx, MinuteClinic, EasyShare, Courtyard by Marriott, Sawzall (the reciprocating saw tradesmen hire when they need to cut through a wall quickly) — do work for you every time a customer hears them. Names that are invented syllables with no referent make the ad-agency rich and the customer confused.

4. **Build the brand through the experience, not through advertising.** "Most of today's great brands — Crest, Starbucks, Kleenex, eBay, Kodak — started out as purpose brands. The product did the job, and customers talked about it. This is how brand equity is built." Advertising can clarify a job that a good product already does — Unilever's Soupy Snax becoming Soupy Snax-4:00 with ads showing lethargic workers perking up, at which point customers said "that's what happens to me at 4:00" — but advertising can't invent a job the product doesn't address.

5. **When extending, pass the two-way test.** Either extend to a different product that does the *same* job (Walkman-cassette to Walkman-CD) or create a new purpose sub-brand with the original as endorser (Marriott to Courtyard by Marriott). If the extension is neither of these, it's an endorsement drift and it will erode the mother brand.

6. **Kill extensions that cross jobs.** If an extension is already out there and spanning jobs that have nothing in common, accept that the brand has become an endorser, give each sub-job its own purpose brand, and stop pretending one name can do the work of many.

## Examples

**Situation:** Ikea, for fifty years, sells flat-pack furniture that is not especially beautiful, not especially durable, and explicitly targeted at the low end of the furniture market. Competitors — department stores, specialty retailers, other flat-pack lines — enter and exit. None of them dislodge Ikea.

**Application:** Ikea is a purpose brand for the "I need to furnish my apartment tomorrow" job. The job has functional dimensions (a complete assortment of everything a person needs to furnish a space, in one trip, at a price a student can absorb), emotional dimensions (relief from the anxiety of a move; the sense of making a new place livable quickly), and social dimensions (the feeling of competent adulthood — I handled this, I set up my life). The entire store, the catalog, the flat-pack format, the cheap restaurant inside, the loading area, the wordless assembly instructions — all of it is designed around the job. Competitors that offer a better chair or a better mattress are competing on features. Ikea is competing on the job.

**Result:** Ikea's owner is one of the richest people in the world, selling substandard furniture at a premium price in a category widely considered to be commoditized. Because the purpose brand pops into the mind of a college kid with a new apartment, the competitive question never gets opened.

**Situation:** Marriott wants to extend its flagship brand — built around large full-service meeting-hotels — to serve very different traveler jobs without destroying what it has built.

**Application:** Marriott identifies three different jobs. The lone business traveler who needs a clean, quiet place to get work done in the evening. The long-stay traveler who needs something closer to a temporary home. The budget traveler who needs a bed and a shower. Each job gets its own purpose sub-brand with the Marriott name as an endorsement. Courtyard by Marriott — the hotel designed by business travelers for business travelers. Residence Inn by Marriott — for the longer stays. Fairfield Inn by Marriott — for the budget traveler. Each sub-brand does its own job precisely. The Marriott endorsement tells the customer "this is held to Marriott's standard of execution," without promising it is the full-service convention hotel.

**Result:** The Marriott endorser brand stays strong *because* each sub-brand does its job well — doing the job is what earns the endorser its credibility. Contrast with Volvo, where Ford acquired the brand in 1999 and immediately began stretching it toward a flashier "luxury" position. Volvo lost the safety purpose-brand status it had spent decades earning, sales fell, Ford eventually sold Volvo to Geely in 2010. Christensen's fear in the record: Volvo may have lost its purpose-brand status forever.

**Situation:** Milwaukee Electric Tool sells a full line of power tools — reciprocating saws, right-angle drills, circular saws, pistol-grip drills, sanders, jigsaws. Durability and price are comparable across the line. The Milwaukee name is the same on all of them.

**Application:** Milwaukee has built purpose brands for *two* of those products. The Sawzall — the reciprocating saw that tradesmen hire when they need to cut through a wall quickly and aren't sure what's under the surface. The Hole Hawg — the right-angle drill plumbers hire when they need to drill in a tight space. For the other products, Milwaukee has a competent endorser brand and nothing more.

**Result:** Milwaukee has owned more than 80% of the Sawzall and Hole Hawg markets for decades. Market share in its other power-tool categories is in the low single digits. Same company, same quality, same factories, same distribution. The difference is that tradesmen *hire a Sawzall* when the job shows up — the name is the job — while the other Milwaukee products are just Milwaukee-branded versions of what Black & Decker and Bosch and Makita also sell. A purpose brand is a more formidable competitive barrier than superior product performance, because competitors can copy performance much more easily than they can copy a purpose brand.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Stretch one purpose brand across jobs that have nothing in common.
**Why:** Different jobs demand different purpose brands. Crest stretched from "prevent my children's cavities" to whitening, breath freshening, plaque — and collapsed from a purpose brand into an endorser. The moment Crest stopped signaling "this is for cavity prevention," J&J's ACT walked into the vacancy. The extension didn't grow Crest; it surrendered the original job's moat.

**Don't:** Ask advertising to do work the product hasn't done.
**Why:** Advertising can clarify a job that a product already performs (Unilever's Soupy Snax becoming Soupy Snax-4:00 helped customers recognize the job they already had). Advertising cannot stuff meaning into a word. Executives who believe it can "generally succeed in stuffing it full of vagueness" and find themselves in an endless arms race with equally vague competitors. The ad agencies and media companies win that game. The brand does not.

**Don't:** Invent a brand name with no tie to the job in the hope of being hired for everything.
**Why:** Look at car model names. Taurus, Escape, Cavalier, Neon, Prizm, Corolla, Camry, Avalon, Sentra, Civic, Accord, Sonata. Invented syllables, no referent. The automakers deliberately create brand names with no meaning in any language in the hope that each model will be hired for every job. The result is that the average subbrand commands less than a 1% share and most automakers are losing money. Focus is scary. Vagueness is more expensive.

**Don't:** Treat your product as an aspirational job when it isn't.
**Why:** Gucci, Absolut, Virgin — these are hired to produce a feeling, and for them, advertising and imagery are part of the product. Most products are not in that category. Most products are hired to do functional-plus-emotional-plus-social work, and the substance has to be real. If you pattern your marketing on the luxury playbook for a product that isn't a luxury, you will spend fortunes on imagery while the product itself remains undifferentiated, and customers will drift to whoever does the actual job.

**Don't:** Confuse "brand awareness" with a purpose brand.
**Why:** Awareness means people have heard of you. A purpose brand means you are the name that pops into the customer's head the moment the job arises. These are very different things. A company can achieve high awareness with enough ad spend and still not own any job. A company can own a job with very little advertising if the product does the job and customers talk about it. Awareness is a metric. A purpose brand is a position.
