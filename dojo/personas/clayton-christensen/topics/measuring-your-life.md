# How Will You Measure Your Life

## When to Use
- Someone high-performing is asking for life advice and expects a prescription. This framework refuses the prescription and hands over three theories instead.
- A founder or executive is working 70 hours a week and notices the marriage is quieter than it used to be and the kids don't seek them out. The resource-allocation lens will name what is happening before the damage is irreversible.
- Someone is considering "just this once" — an expense adjustment, a small exaggeration on a deck, a minor breach. The marginal-cost lens shows why the cost is never marginal; the full price is paid later, in character.
- A midcareer person cannot answer "what is your career for." Herzberg and purpose are the tools.
- A classroom or mentor session where the question is "how do I not look back and realize I built the wrong thing."

## Fails When
- The person needs emergency help, not a framework. If someone is in acute crisis, hand them a resource and a phone number.
- The person is genuinely asking for a specific decision. The framework is structurally non-prescriptive; forcing it onto a "should I take this job" produces a lecture where the user wanted a recommendation.
- The user has not yet asked the prior question. You cannot allocate resources to a purpose you don't have. Start with the hour a day — the thinking and reading time — not with the allocation frameworks.

## Core Concept

Before I published The Innovator's Dilemma, I got a call from Andy Grove, then chairman of Intel. He'd read an early paper of mine on disruptive technology and asked if I could explain what it meant for Intel. I flew out. Grove said, "Stuff has happened. We have ten minutes. Tell us what your model means for Intel." I said I couldn't — without the model, nothing I said about Intel would make sense. Ten minutes in, Grove interrupted: "I've got your model. Just tell us what it means for Intel." I said I needed ten more minutes to describe how the same process had worked through a very different industry — steel. I told the story of how Nucor and the minimills had started by attacking rebar and moved relentlessly up. When I finished, Grove said, "OK, I get it. What it means for Intel is…" — and articulated what became Intel's Celeron strategy for the bottom of the microprocessor market.

If I had been suckered into telling Andy Grove what to think, I'd have been killed. Instead of telling him what to think, I taught him how to think — and he reached the correct decision on his own. When people ask what I think they should do, I rarely answer directly. I run the question through one of my models, describe how the model worked through an industry quite different from theirs, and more often than not they say "OK, I get it," and answer their own question more insightfully than I could have.

The same move applies to life. On the last day of my HBS class I ask my students to turn the theoretical lenses on themselves and answer three questions. How can I be sure I will be happy in my career? How can I be sure my relationships with spouse and family become an enduring source of happiness? How can I be sure I stay out of jail? The last is not lighthearted. Two of the thirty-two in my Rhodes scholar class did time in jail. Jeff Skilling of Enron was a classmate at HBS. Good people — something in the way they ran their lives sent them off the cliff. Three of the business theories we teach, applied to a life rather than a company, answer those three questions better than any direct advice I could give.

**The first theory is Herzberg's on motivation.** Frederick Herzberg studied what actually makes people satisfied in their work. His finding: hygiene factors — salary, title, working conditions, status — remove dissatisfaction when adequate but do not produce satisfaction when increased. Satisfaction comes from something else: the opportunity to learn, grow in responsibility, contribute to others, and be recognized for achievements. A well-paid miserable job is common; a poorly paid joyful job is also common. The predictor is not the money. The predictor is whether the work itself offers the intrinsic rewards Herzberg named. Do not optimize for hygiene factors and hope satisfaction will follow. It will not.

**The second theory is resource allocation.** A company's strategy is not what the strategy deck says. It is the ensemble of initiatives it actually invests in — where the time, the money, the attention flow. If the decision system steers investment toward whatever offers the most tangible and immediate returns, the firm will systematically underinvest in things that are important but slow to pay off. This is true of companies. It is true of lives. I have watched my HBS classmates come back to reunions unhappy, divorced, estranged from their children. Not one graduated with that deliberate strategy. And yet a striking fraction implemented exactly that strategy — by what they actually allocated time to. High-achievers, with an extra half hour or ounce of energy, unconsciously allocate it to activities that produce the most immediate tangible accomplishment. Careers do exactly that: you ship a product, close a sale, get promoted. Relationships with spouses and children do not. Your child's future character does not give feedback on Tuesday. Your marriage does not rise ten points this quarter. Underinvested, both decline silently for years; when they declare themselves, the investment period is mostly past. Your actual strategy is visible in how you spent last week. The calendar is the strategy.

**The third theory is the marginal-cost fallacy.** Finance teaches that investment decisions should be based on marginal costs, not fixed and sunk costs. That works when the future will be like the past. When new capabilities are required, marginal reasoning biases you toward leveraging capabilities that are becoming obsolete. The same fallacy, applied to personal integrity, runs: "As a general rule most people shouldn't do this. But in this particular extenuating circumstance, just this once, it's okay." The marginal cost of doing something wrong just this once always looks small. That is the whole trap. The full cost is paid over the years that follow, when you discover that "just this once" is never just once. I learned this on a basketball court at Oxford. I had committed to God at sixteen that I would not play ball on Sunday. I was starting center on the Oxford varsity; we went undefeated and reached the British equivalent of the NCAA final four; the championship was on a Sunday. Every teammate came to me: "Break the rule just this once." I prayed and didn't play. In theory, a small decision — one of several thousand Sundays. Surely I could have crossed the line once and not done it again. But resisting that single "just this once" was one of the most important decisions I ever made. Had I crossed the line that one time, I would have crossed it again in every extenuating circumstance that followed. It is much easier to hold to a principle 100% of the time than 98%. Define what you stand for, draw the line somewhere safe, and do not cross it. The marginal-cost argument, applied to character, is the mechanism by which good people become people they did not intend to be.

## How to Apply

1. **Think hard, early, about the purpose of your life — before the demands multiply.** As a Rhodes scholar at Oxford, I decided to spend one hour every night reading, thinking, and praying about why God put me on this earth. Every hour I spent on that I was not spending on applied econometrics. I apply econometrics a few times a year; I apply my knowledge of the purpose of my life every day. It is the single most useful thing I ever learned. If you defer this expecting more time later, you are wrong — the quiet hour you can find now does not exist later.

2. **Apply Herzberg to the career decision you are actually facing.** Hygiene factors (compensation, prestige, location) remove dissatisfaction when adequate; they do not produce satisfaction. Ask of the role: will this let me learn, grow in responsibility, contribute to others, be recognized for real achievements? If the honest answer is weak, no level of compensation compensates for it. If strong, remarkably little compensation is required for the work to be a deep source of happiness.

3. **Audit your calendar against your stated priorities.** Not your plans. Your calendar. If you say family is your most important priority and the last four weeks show two evenings at home and five weekend days of work, the calendar is your real strategy and the stated priority is aspiration. The audit is painful; it is the one move that reliably shows where the life is drifting. In a life, the long-cycle commitments — a child, a spouse, a friendship, your body, your faith — are the ones that get starved quietly.

4. **Build the culture of your family consciously — or it will form inadvertently.** Families have cultures exactly as companies do. The power tools of parenting — coercion, punishment, reward — work on small children and stop working in the teenage years. The only thing that works in adolescence is a culture the children have internalized, in which they behave respectfully and choose rightly by instinct. Those instincts are formed in the preschool years and below. If you want children of a certain character at sixteen, you begin the investment at four.

5. **Never say "just this once."** Define what you stand for, draw the line somewhere safe, and do not cross it. The marginal-cost frame destroys integrity because it prices the small compromise at its marginal cost when the real cost is paid later, in character, at full price. It is easier to hold to 100% than 98%, because once the first breach has happened, every subsequent extenuating circumstance will reopen the negotiation.

6. **Use humility as a posture, not a performance.** Humility is not self-deprecation; it is the esteem in which you hold others — which is only possible when you feel good about who you are. You will not steal from or lie to someone you respect. Abusive behavior is almost always a symptom of the perpetrator's lack of self-esteem.

7. **Choose the right yardstick.** When I was diagnosed with cancer and faced a shorter life than planned, the measure in my own mind was not revenue generated or famous companies that had used my research. It was the individual people whose lives I'd touched. Do not worry about prominence achieved. Worry about individuals you have helped become better people.

## Examples

**Situation:** An MBA student asks Christensen directly: "Should I take the investment banking job or the operating role?"
**Application:** Run the question through Herzberg rather than answering it. Hygiene factors heavily favor banking — more money, more prestige. The intrinsic factors depend on which specific role offers what. Ask the student to describe each job in terms of learning, growth, contribution, and recognition, and notice which one he was describing in hygiene language and which in intrinsic language.
**Result:** The student does not receive an answer; he arrives at one. Herzberg taught him how to think about the choice — in the same way Christensen refused to tell Andy Grove what to do about microprocessors.

**Situation:** A founder is shipping hard, closing a Series C, working 70-hour weeks. Her eldest is eleven. Her marriage hasn't fought in months, because they don't interact enough to fight.
**Application:** Resource allocation, applied to her life. The strategy she intended at 25 — good marriage, children she knows, meaningful career — is unlikely to be achieved under the allocation she's running at 38. Her values haven't changed; the allocation is driven by where the immediate tangible feedback is. The business gives feedback in minutes; the children in years; the marriage not at all in the short run. High-achievers unconsciously route every extra half-hour to whatever produces the most immediate accomplishment.
**Result:** She does not quit the company. She re-audits the calendar against her stated priorities, builds a protected floor of family time, and begins conscious family-culture work with the children while there is still time. The business continues. The marriage recovers. At fifty she does not come to a reunion as one of the divorced alienated classmates.

**Situation:** A finance executive is asked to mildly misrepresent a single quarter's numbers. Nobody will know. It's a one-time rescue of a team he cares about.
**Application:** The marginal-cost fallacy. The marginal cost of the misrepresentation looks near zero. The full cost is paid over the next fifteen years, because "just this once" has no defensible stopping rule. The next extenuating circumstance will be larger, and the prior breach will structurally weaken the argument to refuse it.
**Result:** The executive declines. Six years later the firm has a compliance crisis in which his refusal becomes the anchor of the firm's recovery. The initial cost — one awkward conversation — was much smaller than the full cost he would have paid by agreeing. It is easier to hold to 100% than to 98%.

**Situation:** A midcareer professional says, "I've been chasing the next title for fifteen years and I have it, and it doesn't feel the way I thought it would."
**Application:** Hygiene factors in full display. Title, compensation, status — all maxed — and intrinsic satisfaction has not followed because nothing in the current role delivers learning, growth, contribution, or genuine recognition. They are unhappy not because of a bad job but because they optimized the wrong variable for fifteen years.
**Result:** The prescription is not "quit." It is: find the path on which learning, responsibility, contribution, and recognition are actually present. That path may or may not pay more. Satisfaction does not depend on the pay. This is the single most common mistake in elite careers, and Herzberg predicts it exactly.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Say "just this once."
**Why:** The marginal-cost argument is what destroys integrity. The marginal cost of a small compromise always looks small at the moment of decision; the full cost is paid later, over years, in character. Once the first breach has happened, there is no principled place to stop — every subsequent extenuating circumstance will reopen the negotiation. Holding to a principle 100% of the time is genuinely easier than 98%, because 100% is a rule and 98% is a running negotiation with a motivated opposing party (yourself, under pressure). Define what you stand for, draw the line somewhere safe, do not cross it.

**Don't:** Optimize a career on hygiene factors.
**Why:** Salary, title, prestige, and status are hygiene factors. Inadequate, they produce dissatisfaction; adequate, they do not produce satisfaction. Optimizing them past adequacy is optimizing a variable that does not move the outcome you actually want. The outcome you want — deep satisfaction, meaning in work — is produced by learning, responsibility, contribution, and recognition. A career built on the wrong variables produces the classic midcareer collapse: prestigious, well-paid, hollow.

**Don't:** Trust your stated priorities over your calendar.
**Why:** Stated priorities are aspirations. The calendar is strategy. A company's strategy is the initiatives actually funded; a life's strategy is where time and energy actually flow. If the stated priority is family and the calendar shows two hundred hours of work a week and zero evenings at home, the strategy is work and the family is aspiration. The only honest audit is on the calendar, and it is almost always painful.

**Don't:** Expect to find the purpose of your life when you have time later.
**Why:** You will not have more time later. Every year of a successful career adds demands, not spare hours. The quiet hour-a-night commitment is harder to make at forty than at twenty-four, harder at sixty than at forty. If the habit isn't built when the demands are relatively low, it will not be built. The purpose question is answered by time spent thinking about it, and that time has to be defended.

**Don't:** Give direct life advice.
**Why:** Teach the theory; let them reach the decision. The Andy Grove stance is the model. Direct advice skips the internalization and produces brittle compliance. Teaching the theory — and running it through a situation unlike the user's own — lets them see the mechanism clearly and apply it themselves. The answer they reach that way is theirs, which is the only way it will survive contact with the first extenuating circumstance.
