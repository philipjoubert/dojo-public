# Sustaining vs. Disruptive

## When to Use
- An incumbent is trying to decide whether to fight hard, ignore, or isolate an emerging competitor. The right answer depends entirely on which kind of innovation the competitor represents, and the two kinds require opposite responses.
- A startup founder is shaping a business plan and needs to know whether the plan is a sustaining one (and therefore one that established competitors will be powerfully motivated to fight and resourced to win) or a disruptive one (and therefore one that incumbents will be motivated to flee).
- A corporate development group is evaluating a target for acquisition or partnership. Whether the target is sustaining or disruptive determines whether to integrate it tightly or wall it off.
- A board is debating whether a new internal initiative should live inside the core business or in a separate division. Sustaining projects belong inside; disruptive ones almost never do.
- You are reading a market and need a diagnostic that predicts who will win a competitive fight before the fight is over. Predictions based on the size of the companies, or the elegance of the technology, or the cleverness of the management, are frustratingly unreliable. This diagnostic is not.

## Fails When
- The question is about a pure business-model shift that has no obvious performance-dimension axis — the sustaining/disruptive frame can still apply, but you will need to work harder to locate the trajectories.
- The user wants the label to avoid a hard truth. "It's disruptive so we can't fight it" is sometimes a rationalization for abandoning a fight that the incumbent should, in fact, be winning decisively on a sustaining-technology basis.
- The user treats the distinction as a property of the technology or the product rather than of how the strategy is framed. The same internet technology was sustaining for Dell and disruptive for Compaq. Assigning the label to the artifact instead of the strategy produces wrong calls.
- The user uses the label as a finish line. "This is disruptive" is the start of analysis, not the end. The next questions are: disruptive to whom, along which trajectory, and how fast.

## Core Concept

Most new technologies foster improved product performance. Christensen calls these *sustaining technologies*. What they have in common is that they improve the performance of established products along the dimensions of performance that mainstream customers in major markets have historically valued (Innovator's Dilemma, Introduction). Some sustaining innovations are incremental — the fifth blade on a razor, the clearer television picture, better mobile phone reception. Others are radical — the leap from black-and-white to color television, from ferrite heads to thin-film heads on disk drives, from analog to digital telephone networks. It does not matter whether the sustaining innovation is modest or breakthrough; what matters is that it aims at customers who already exist and asks them to pay more for a better product along attributes they already value. Rarely have even the most radically difficult sustaining technologies precipitated the failure of leading firms. The research is stark on this: incumbents almost always win sustaining battles. In Christensen's seminal disk-drive study, only 6 percent of sustaining entrants managed to succeed against incumbents ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", HBR 2015). Corporate giants RCA, General Electric, and AT&T threw massive resources at IBM's mainframe business and failed to make a dent (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). IBM and Kodak failed to outmuscle Xerox in high-speed photocopiers. NCR failed to beat itself to the electronic cash register, yet survived, because the attack on it — the electronic register — was still a sustaining innovation, and NCR's extensive sales force quickly clawed back the share it had briefly lost.

Occasionally, however, *disruptive* innovations emerge: innovations that result in worse product performance, at least in the near-term, along the attributes mainstream customers historically value. Disruptive products are typically cheaper, simpler, smaller, and frequently more convenient to use. They bring to a market a very different value proposition than had been available previously. At first, they are embraced only by fringe customers who either had been priced out of the mainstream (nonconsumers) or were so overserved by the mainstream that "good enough for less" was a welcome trade. Transistors were disruptive relative to vacuum tubes. Honda's Super Cub was disruptive relative to the powerful Harley-Davidson and BMW motorcycles (Innovator's Dilemma ch. 7). Discount retailing was disruptive relative to full-service department stores. Personal computers were disruptive relative to minicomputers, and minicomputers were disruptive relative to mainframes before them. In each instance, the disruptive innovation looked unattractive to the incumbent's best customers, was rejected by the incumbent's internal resource-allocation processes as insufficiently profitable, and was therefore left to entrants to build out. In each instance, the entrants then rode a steeper trajectory of improvement up into the mainstream, where — by the time mainstream customers wanted the new product — the incumbent had been left so far behind that no response was adequate.

The distinction determines the right playbook, and the playbooks are opposites. Against sustaining innovation, the incumbent should fight — hard, fast, resourced to win. Incumbents have the customers, the channels, the manufacturing, the brand, the cost structure tuned to the market, and the asymmetric motivation (they stand to lose their best customers; the entrant stands to win them only by beating the incumbent directly). Against disruptive innovation, the incumbent almost never wins by fighting the entrant head-on inside the existing organization. The existing organization's processes and values — everything that makes it competent against sustaining innovations — become its disabilities against disruptive ones (Innovator's Dilemma, Principle #4). The correct response is to continue strengthening sustaining innovation in the core business while creating a separate organization, under the protection of senior leadership, to pursue the disruptive opportunity with its own cost structure, its own customers, and its own definition of performance ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015; Innovator's Dilemma Principle #1). Disruption works because it is much easier to beat competitors when they are motivated to flee rather than fight (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). That flight is itself generated by the incumbent's correct pursuit of sustaining innovation at the high end; the same thing that keeps the incumbent profitable in the short run prepares the way for its disruption in the long run.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask: which customers is the innovation aimed at?** Sustaining innovations target the most demanding, most profitable customers in the mainstream market. Disruptive innovations target either overshot customers at the low end of the existing market or nonconsumers who were previously locked out. If the innovation's target is an existing, well-served customer, it is almost certainly sustaining.

2. **Ask: along which performance dimension is the innovation better, and how do mainstream customers value that dimension?** Sustaining innovations improve along the dimensions mainstream customers already care about. Disruptive innovations introduce a different bundle of attributes — typically simplicity, convenience, accessibility, price — that existing mainstream customers initially do *not* value, and they sacrifice performance along the dimensions that mainstream customers *do* value. If the new offering is worse along the incumbent's key axis and better along an axis the incumbent thinks irrelevant, that is the disruptive signature.

3. **Ask: how does the incumbent's internal resource-allocation process treat it?** Disruptive innovations get killed or starved inside incumbents because they have lower margins, smaller markets, and less-attractive customers. Sustaining innovations sail through because they promise higher margins and serve known customers. If the question "should we fund this?" is answered positively by the incumbent's normal processes, the innovation is almost certainly sustaining. If the normal processes cannot fund it no matter how hard senior management tries, it is disruptive.

4. **Test: is this idea disruptive relative to *all* the significant incumbents?** The strict rule from Innovator's Solution ch. 2: if your idea appears disruptive to some established companies but might be a sustaining improvement for others, go back to the drawing board. If it is sustaining relative to the business model of a significant incumbent, you are picking a fight you are very unlikely to win. The internet appeared "disruptive" throughout the late 1990s; many internet retailers failed because the internet was sustaining relative to Dell's already-direct business model, and Dell absorbed the opportunity rather than being displaced by it.

5. **Match the response to the kind of innovation.** If it is sustaining and you are the incumbent: fight. Marshal your resources, beat the entrant on their own terms, and you will almost certainly win. If it is disruptive and you are the incumbent: do not dismantle your core business in panic. Continue to strengthen relationships with your most profitable customers via sustaining innovation. Create a *separate* division to pursue the disruptive opportunity — separate customers, separate cost structure, separate success metrics, kept distinct from the core even when the temptation to integrate grows overwhelming. If it is disruptive and you are the entrant: resist the urge to shape your plan into a sustaining attack on the incumbent's best customers. That is a fight you will lose. Shape the plan into a disruptive one aimed at customers the incumbent is motivated to flee.

6. **Remember the relativity.** Disruption is not a property of a technology. The same innovation can be sustaining to one business model and disruptive to another. Evaluate each incumbent separately. Decide on which side of the line the innovation falls from the point of view of the specific company whose strategy you are trying to set.

## Examples

**Situation:** In the 1970s, electronic cash registers begin to appear. National Cash Register (NCR), which dominates the electromechanical cash register market, fails to respond. Its product sales go literally to zero.
**Application:** The instinct is to call this a disruption — look, the incumbent was blindsided! But the diagnostic test fails. Electronic cash registers targeted exactly the same customers as electromechanical registers, served the same job in the same way, and were superior along the dimensions those customers already valued. This was a radical but *sustaining* innovation (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). NCR should have fought — and when NCR finally did introduce its own electronic register, its extensive sales organization quickly captured the same market share it had previously held in the electromechanical realm. It survived on service revenues in the interim because its customers remained its customers. The lesson is not that NCR was brilliant; it is that sustaining attacks favor the incumbent so powerfully that even a temporarily asleep incumbent can recover. If this had been a disruption, recovery would not have been possible.

**Situation:** In 1975, John Bogle launches the first retail index mutual fund. Fidelity's active-management business is enormously profitable. Active-management fees underwrite the entire industry.
**Application:** Is Bogle's index fund a sustaining or disruptive innovation for Fidelity? Apply the tests. It targets customers Fidelity already serves — retail investors. It offers lower prices and a different value proposition (average-return accessibility, not alpha). Along the dimension Fidelity's customers had historically valued — beating the market — it is not merely worse, it explicitly does not even try. It is cheaper, simpler, and aimed at the overshot end of Fidelity's market: customers who had been paying for active management but who were, in fact, not receiving the alpha they were paying for. Disruptive. Fidelity's rational in-house response — to continue offering active management at high margins and dismiss the index fund — exactly maps onto the disruption pattern.
**Result:** Vanguard (Bogle's creation) grew from $11 million to over $3 trillion in assets. The active-management industry capitulated decades later and launched their own index funds. The disruption played out on the predicted timeline: flee, flee, flee, then panic, then surrender. Note the contrast with NCR: where sustaining innovation let an incumbent recover, disruptive innovation made recovery structurally impossible.

**Situation:** Toyota enters the US market with the Corona in the 1960s — a cheap, underpowered subcompact. GM, Ford, and Chrysler dominate the American market with larger, more profitable vehicles.
**Application:** Sustaining or disruptive? Toyota's Corona targeted customers who previously could not afford a new car, or families that wanted a second car. It was inferior along the dimensions American mainstream car buyers valued — size, power, comfort — and better along dimensions they did not previously prioritize — price, fuel economy, simplicity. The Big Three's internal resource allocation could not justify building a competing subcompact; margins were terrible at that end of the market, and the segment looked too small to matter (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). Disruptive.
**Result:** Toyota moved up. Camry, then Lexus. Nissan did the same: Datsun to Infiniti. Honda did the same: CVCC to Acura. The Big Three ran upmarket themselves, into trucks and SUVs, where they remained profitable — but they permanently ceded the passenger-car market. Now Hyundai and Kia are running the same play from below. The disruption is ongoing, generational, and structurally the same each time.

**Situation:** Airbus enters the commercial airframe industry to compete with Boeing. It wants the same customers, building similar aircraft for similar missions.
**Application:** Sustaining or disruptive? Airbus targeted Boeing's exact customers — major airlines — with comparable-performance, comparable-size aircraft. It was a head-on sustaining attack against a dominant incumbent (Innovator's Solution ch. 2). The theory predicts Airbus will find the fight extraordinarily difficult.
**Result:** Airbus did eventually succeed, but only after massive subsidies from European governments — a factor external to the theory that allowed an unusual sustaining success. In contrast, the most profitable growth in the airframe industry in subsequent decades came from Embraer and Bombardier's Canadair, whose regional jets stretched up-market from below. A disruptive strategy. When you can choose, pick the disruptive frame; it does not require subsidies to make the math work.

**Situation:** Apple's iPhone, 2007. Is this sustaining or disruptive?
**Application:** Depends on the comparison. Versus existing smartphones — BlackBerry, Nokia — the iPhone was a sustaining innovation. It targeted the same customers, served the same jobs, was better along the dimensions smartphone customers valued ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?", 2015). Apple had to fight sustaining battles against the incumbents and, for a time, Apple was neck and neck with Samsung and others. But the iPhone's *subsequent* growth is better explained by disruption — not of other smartphones, but of the laptop as the primary device for internet access. Through the app ecosystem, the iPhone pulled internet access out of the laptop value network. That move was disruptive: new customers, new contexts, new performance dimensions.
**Result:** Apple wins in both frames but must be managed differently in each. Product teams inside Apple dedicated to out-innovating Samsung on the smartphone axis are running a sustaining strategy. Teams enabling app developers to build completely new categories of use are running a disruptive one. Both are correct; confusing them would produce the wrong product decisions.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the label as a property of the technology rather than of the strategic framing.
**Why:** The internet was a sustaining innovation for Dell (already selling direct) and a disruptive threat to Compaq (dependent on retail channels). The same technology. Opposite implications. The sustaining/disruptive classification must be made relative to a specific incumbent's business model — not relative to the innovation in isolation. Managers who label the technology and then apply the label to every company in the industry will get the response wrong for half of them.

**Don't:** Use "it's disruptive" as permission to surrender a sustaining fight.
**Why:** Against genuinely sustaining attacks, incumbents have enormous structural advantages — customers, channels, brand, cost structure, and the asymmetric motivation of defending a profit pool the entrant can only access by beating them directly. The 6 percent success rate for sustaining entrants in the disk-drive industry should anchor your expectations. If the innovation is sustaining and you are the incumbent, you should almost certainly fight and you should almost certainly win. Calling it "disruptive" to rationalize retreat is the opposite of the lesson.

**Don't:** Use "it's sustaining" as permission to ignore a disruptive attack because the entrant "isn't really competing with us."
**Why:** Disruptive entrants never start by competing with the incumbent's best customers. They start in the low end or at nonconsumption. Ignoring them on the grounds that "they aren't taking our customers" is precisely how Blockbuster, Kodak, the integrated steel mills, and many others were disrupted. The question is not whether the entrant is taking your customers today; it is whether the entrant is on a trajectory that will, five or ten years from now, produce a product that your customers will want.

**Don't:** Fold a nascent disruptive business back into the mainstream organization because "now it's big enough to integrate."
**Why:** Every company in the history of the disk-drive industry that tried to manage mainstream and disruptive businesses within a single organization failed (Innovator's Dilemma; "Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", HBR 1995). When an independent and mainstream organization are combined in order to share resources, debilitating arguments arise over which group gets what resources and whether or when to cannibalize established products. The autonomous unit must stay autonomous — not only during incubation but into maturity. Fixed-cost sharing sounds efficient and usually destroys the thing that made the new business viable.

**Don't:** Launch a disruptive innovation with the cost structure of a mainstream business.
**Why:** Disruptive innovations serve customers at lower price points and therefore must make money at lower gross margins. Mainstream cost structures — the overhead, the sales apparatus, the quality-control processes — assume high margins and would drown a disruptive product. The autonomous organization's cost structure must be tailored to the low-margin, small-market reality of the disruptive foothold. This is why disruptive businesses inside established firms need to be placed in organizations "where small orders create energy, where fast low-cost forays into ill-defined markets are possible, and where overhead is low enough to permit profit even in emerging markets" ("Disruptive Technologies: Catching the Wave", 1995).

**Don't:** Confuse a "different business model" with automatic disruptiveness.
**Why:** Many business-model changes are sustaining — they make the same value proposition more efficiently for the same customers. The litmus test is the same as always: which customers, along which performance dimensions, with what relationship to the incumbent's existing profit pool. A business model innovation that targets the incumbent's best customers with a better version of what they already buy is sustaining, not disruptive, even if the financial plumbing behind it looks novel.
