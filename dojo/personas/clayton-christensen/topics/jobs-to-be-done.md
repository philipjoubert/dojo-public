# Jobs to Be Done

## When to Use
- A new product has flopped and the post-mortem keeps circling back to "we must have misread the customer." You probably did — you studied the customer instead of the job.
- The market feels commoditized and you're about to add more features in the hope of differentiation.
- You're writing a product spec and the first question on the spec is "who is this for" rather than "what progress is this person trying to make."
- You're trying to predict which new entrant is a real threat. A competitor aligned with an unmet job beats a competitor with a better-looking product.
- You're asked how big the market is and your instinct is to sum up the sales of products in your category.

## Fails When
- **You stop at the functional dimension.** A job has a functional side, an emotional side, and a social side. If you only nail the functional, you'll lose to someone who also nails the emotional and social. The milkshake had to be thick *and* let a commuter feel less bored and more in control — both.
- **You confuse a task with a job.** "Shopping for a car" is a task. "I need to know my daughter is safe when she drives off to college" is a job. The task is what the person does; the job is the progress they're trying to make.
- **You let "jobs" become a synonym for "use case" or "feature request."** A job is bigger than a feature. If your list of jobs is the same length as your feature list, you're using the vocabulary but missing the theory.
- **You use it in a business that is fundamentally cost-competitive at commodity scale.** JTBD explains why people choose one product over another at the margin. It cannot save you if you are structurally 30% more expensive than a competitor doing the same job adequately.

## Core Concept

This is a theory about why customers do what they do. For years, managers in well-run companies have puzzled about why so many of their new products fail. The answer they usually reach for — better data, better segmentation, more sophisticated analytics — is not the answer. The answer is that their data is organized the wrong way. It tells them which customers look like which other customers. It does not tell them *why* customers make the choices they do.

Here is the idea, stated plainly: customers don't buy products. They *hire* them to do a job. A job is the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. When people find themselves with a job to do, they look around, and if something is at hand that does the job well, they hire it. If it doesn't do the job well, they fire it. This sounds almost trivial, but the implications are enormous. It means the fundamental unit of marketing analysis is not the customer and not the product — it is the job. Two very different people can have the same job, and one person can have different jobs at different times of the day.

Every job has three dimensions. The functional dimension — the mechanical work the product is being hired to do. The emotional dimension — how the person wants to feel while doing it, and how they want to feel about themselves afterward. The social dimension — what this choice says about the person to others, or to themselves. Miss any of the three and your product does the job incompletely. Nail all three and the customer will pay a premium and will refuse to consider alternatives. That is the whole theory in one paragraph.

Once you organize the world this way, a few things follow. First: demographic and psychographic correlations are not the job. They are coincidences. I am 64 years old, I'm six feet eight inches tall, my shoe size is sixteen, I drive a Honda minivan, I have five children. None of these facts has ever caused me to buy a New York Times. I buy a Times because I'm about to get on a long flight, or because it's March Madness and I want to read about basketball. Those are jobs. The demographic information is a side effect. Second: markets organized by job are almost always bigger than markets organized by product category — usually much bigger. The milkshake competes with bananas and bagels and Snickers bars and boredom itself. The size of the milkshake market is not the sum of the milkshakes sold; it is the size of the job, and it turned out to be seven times bigger than McDonald's had thought. Third: the real competitive set is not the set of products that look like yours. It is the set of things the customer could hire to do the same job — and those will often come from categories you've never considered. Fourth: once you understand the job, innovation becomes predictable. Because the job exists whether you build for it or not, and if you build something that does the job better than anything else, customers will come. You can stop relying on luck.

## How to Apply

1. **Find a struggle.** Where are people working around a problem? Where are they hiring something unsatisfactorily? Where is there non-consumption — people who would like to make progress and aren't making it because nothing available does the job? A struggle is the visible surface of a job.

2. **Describe the circumstance.** The job is never about the person in the abstract. It is about the person in a particular circumstance. Not "commuters want breakfast" — "a person in a car at 7am, alone, with one free hand, facing a long boring drive, not hungry yet but knowing they will be by 10." Circumstance first, person second.

3. **Spell out all three dimensions.** Functional: what is the mechanical work? Emotional: how does the person want to feel while doing it? Social: what does hiring this product say about them? Write a sentence for each. If you can't, you haven't defined the job yet.

4. **Identify the real competitive set.** Ask: what do people hire when they don't hire you? Cast the net wide. The answer will include things from categories you don't compete in today. That set is your actual competition and the size of the job-defined market.

5. **Design the product, the experience, and the process around the job.** A product alone doesn't do the job — the *experience of purchase and use* does the job, and the company's internal processes either support that experience or fight it. Move the milkshake to the front of the counter and give people a swipe card. Put a sorting room in the condo development. Call the online student back within eight and a half minutes, not twenty-four hours. These are not features. They are the job being done well.

6. **Give it a name the customer can use.** A job that has been well-served deserves a brand that pops into the customer's head the moment the job shows up. A brand that is the name of the job — FedEx for "this must arrive tomorrow for sure," Ikea for "I need to furnish my apartment tomorrow." That is a purpose brand, and it is the reward for getting the job right.

## Examples

**Situation:** McDonald's wants to sell more milkshakes. They run sophisticated demographic studies, profile the quintessential milkshake customer, bring those customers into focus groups, ask what would make the milkshake better. The customers answer clearly. McDonald's improves the milkshake on those dimensions. Sales do not move.

**Application:** Stop asking who buys milkshakes. Ask what job is arising in people's lives that causes them to buy one. Stand in the restaurant for eighteen hours and take notes on the circumstance. Half the milkshakes turn out to be sold before 8:30am, to a person alone, to go, consumed in the car. Confront those people as they leave and ask what they hired the milkshake to do. The answer: "I have a long and boring drive to work, I'm not hungry yet, I know I'll be hungry by 10, I have one hand free, I need something to keep me engaged with life while I drive." The competitive set is bananas, bagels, donuts, Snickers bars, coffee. The functional job: fill the morning, stave off 10am hunger. The emotional: don't be bored. The social: feel like a competent adult managing the day. Make the milkshake thicker so it lasts the full 23-minute commute. Swirl in tiny chunks of fruit so there's a little unpredictability. Move the machine to the front of the counter and sell a prepaid swipe card so the commuter can dash in and dash out.

**Result:** In the part of America where McDonald's implemented this, milkshake sales went up seven times. The size of the market was not the sum of the milkshakes; it was the size of the job. And a very different milkshake is needed for the *other* job — the one where a parent wants to placate a child and feel like a loving parent — because that job doesn't want a thick 23-minute milkshake. It wants a thinner one that the child can finish while the parent is still eating.

**Situation:** A Detroit-area builder puts up condominiums aimed at downsizers — retirees and divorced single parents. They're priced $120,000 to $200,000, with squeakless floors, triple-waterproof basements, granite counters. Marketing is broad, sales staff is available six days a week. Traffic is strong. Almost nobody converts.

**Application:** Stop asking what feature is missing. Ask what job is causing the struggle. Interview the few buyers who did convert. The surprising pattern: every conversation eventually circles to the dining room table. Not the one in the condo — the one they're leaving behind. The old table where birthdays and holidays and homework happened. The decision to buy a condo was blocked not by a missing feature but by the anxiety of leaving behind something that meant family. The job the downsizers were hiring the condo to do was not "acquire modern housing." It was "transition into a new life without losing the old one."

**Result:** The builder redesigned around the job. Smaller second bedroom, larger space that could fit a dining room table. Moving services included. Two years of storage included. A sorting room on the premises where buyers could take their time deciding what to keep and what to let go. Prices went up $3,500. When the 2007 housing market fell 49%, this builder grew 25%.

**Situation:** Southern New Hampshire University has an online program that's been running for a decade as a sleepy side project of the main campus. They market to everyone generically. Growth is flat.

**Application:** A 35-year-old online student and an 18-year-old on-campus student are both "students pursuing a degree" on paper. But the jobs are completely different. The 18-year-old is hiring SNHU to help them come of age — social life, campus, identity formation. The 35-year-old is hiring SNHU for four very specific things: convenience, customer service, credentials, speedy completion. The 35-year-old is researching options at 11pm after the kids are in bed. The window of opportunity closes fast. The real competitor is not the local college — it's nothing. Non-consumption. The person who gives up.

**Result:** SNHU rebuilt every process around the older learner's job. Callback within 8.5 minutes of an inquiry. Financial aid and transfer credit decisions in days, not months. A personal adviser who stays in contact and intervenes when a unit test goes badly. Net Promoter of 9.6. Compounded annual growth of 34%. Same institution. Different job. Different business.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat demographic correlations as the job.
**Why:** The millennials-who-drink-cold-brew correlation is not a job; it's a coincidence. If you organize around it, you'll overshoot on dimensions that don't matter to the actual job the cold-brew drinker is hiring it to do, and you'll miss the features that do matter. Worse, you'll fail to see that a forty-year-old commuter may have exactly the same job as a twenty-five-year-old — and you'll leave them out of your target market.

**Don't:** Stop at the functional dimension.
**Why:** If you build a milkshake that stays thick for 23 minutes but ignore the fact that the person also wants to feel less bored and more like a competent adult, someone else will build a product that does both and take the job from you. Emotional and social dimensions are not soft nonsense — they are load-bearing parts of why customers hire one product over another.

**Don't:** Confuse job-to-be-done with use case or user story.
**Why:** A use case describes what the user does with the product. A job describes what progress the user is trying to make. A user story says "as a user, I want to X, so that Y." A job says "in this circumstance, I am struggling to make this progress — functionally, emotionally, and socially — and I will hire the thing that helps me most." The user story is a design artifact; the job is the underlying reason the design artifact exists.

**Don't:** Define your market by product category once you've understood the job.
**Why:** "The milkshake market" is the wrong frame. The market is "the long-boring-commute-snack job," and once you see it, Burger King is not your main competitor — bananas and bagels and boredom are. If you go on reporting market share of milkshakes to your board, you'll optimize on the wrong axis and miss the entrants who are organizing around the job you've refused to see.

**Don't:** Rush from one job-interview to a product decision.
**Why:** A job that's real will show up consistently across many people who share the circumstance. A job that appears in one or two interviews and feels exciting may be an idiosyncrasy. Build the job out of many conversations, not one. The cost of misreading the job is a product that doesn't get hired; the cost of slowing down to read it properly is a few weeks of observation.
