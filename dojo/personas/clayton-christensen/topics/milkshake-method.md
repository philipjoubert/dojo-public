# The Milkshake Method

## When to Use
- You're about to redesign a product and your team is debating features instead of circumstances.
- You have masses of demographic and psychographic data on customers but can't predict which new features will move the needle.
- You want to find the real competitive set — not the products that look like yours, but the things customers actually hire when they don't hire you.
- You suspect there's a job adjacent to your product that no one is serving well, and you need a protocol to find it.
- A customer-interview program is underway and producing generic answers ("I'd like it to be faster / cheaper / prettier") that don't translate into design decisions.

## Fails When
- **You skip observation and go straight to interviews.** Customers will rationalize their behavior to sound reasonable. The actual circumstance — alone, in the car, 6:30am, one hand free — is only visible if you watch first, ask second.
- **You interview only your customers.** The jobs you're not serving well are visible in the people who *fired* you or who never hired anyone at all. Non-consumption is often the biggest opportunity.
- **You ask abstract questions.** "What are your needs?" gets you platitudes. "Tell me about the last time you were in the same situation and what you hired instead" gets you the competitive set.
- **You treat the first interview as representative.** One interview is a hypothesis. A job that's real shows up in many people who share the circumstance, not in one person who's articulate.

## Core Concept

This is a research method for finding jobs. It came out of a real piece of work for McDonald's and the name stuck, so it's called the milkshake method. Here is the story, because the story is the method.

McDonald's is a very sophisticated marketing company. They have data about the Gazoo on anything you need. They decided they needed to increase the sales of their milkshakes. They had a demographic profile of the quintessential milkshake customer. They would invite people who fit that profile into conference rooms and ask them what would make the milkshake better — thicker, more chocolaty, cheaper, chunkier. The customers gave very clear feedback. McDonald's improved the milkshake on those dimensions. The impact on sales and profits was negligible.

At that point they let us put a different lens on it. The premise: somewhere out there, a job arises in people's lives on occasion that causes them to need to buy a milkshake. And we need to understand what the job is. So one of our colleagues stood in a McDonald's restaurant for 18 hours one day and just took very careful notes. What time did the person buy the milkshake? What was he wearing? Was he alone or with other people? Did he buy other food with it or just the milkshake? Did he eat it in the restaurant or did he get in the car and take off? It turned out that about half of the milkshakes were sold before 8:30 in the morning. It was the only thing they bought. They were always alone. And they always got in the car and drove off with it.

So we came back the next morning and positioned ourselves outside the restaurant so we could confront these people as they were emerging with their milkshake. We asked them: "Excuse me — can you explain what job arose in your life that caused you to come here at 6:30 in the morning to hire a milkshake?" They'd struggle to answer. So we'd ask them to think about the last time they were in the same situation — same job to be done — but they didn't come to McDonald's. What did they hire? The answers came pouring out. "I hire doughnuts, but I can never hire just one." "I do bagels, but they're dry and tasteless — I have to put cream cheese on and steer with my knees." "I hired a Snickers once but I felt so guilty I never hired Snickers again." "Last Friday I hired a banana, but it's gone in less than a minute and you're hungry by 7:30." And several said: "When I come here and hire this milkshake, it's so viscous I can take 23 minutes to suck it up that thin little straw, and by 10 o'clock I'm still full."

The job that all of these people were trying to get done was: "I have a long and boring drive to work, and I need something to keep me engaged with life while I drive the car. I'm not hungry yet, but I know I'll be hungry by 10 o'clock, so I also need something that will keep me full when 10 o'clock happens." That is the job. And the milkshake, from the customer's point of view, does the job better than any of its competitors. The competitors, from the customer's point of view, are not just Burger King and Wendy's milkshakes. The competitors are bananas and donuts and bagels and coffee and Snickers bars.

Here is the structure of the method, pulled out of the story. First, you observe — the circumstance is what defines the job, and the circumstance is only visible if you watch. Second, you interview in the circumstance, not in a conference room. Third, you ask about the alternatives — what did you hire when you didn't hire us — because the alternatives reveal both the functional dimension and the real competitive set. Fourth, you push through the functional answer to the emotional and social dimensions, because the job has all three. Fifth, you find the pattern across many interviews — a job is real when it shows up in different people who share the circumstance.

Later in the day at that same McDonald's, customers hired a milkshake for a completely different job. That job was between a parent and a child. "I want to have a venue where my child believes I'm listening. I just need a way to feel like a loving parent, and I need something I can give them that makes me say yes when I've been saying no all day." That is a very different job. And the milkshake that does the morning commute job — thick enough to take 23 minutes — does the parent-child job poorly. The parent is done with their meal and the kid is still wrestling with the thick shake through the thin straw. Same product, same customer demographic, same location. Two completely different jobs. You only see that if you watch twice — morning and afternoon — and interview in both circumstances.

## How to Apply

1. **Observe first. Watch in the real circumstance.** Put a researcher in the location — the restaurant, the store aisle, the waiting room, the software signup flow — for a full day. Take notes on what time things happen, who is there, what they're wearing, whether they're alone, what else they buy, where they consume it. Do this before you write a single interview question. The circumstance is the primary data.

2. **Interview at the moment of the struggle, not in a conference room.** Catch the person when they have just done the thing. Their memory is fresh, their rationalizations haven't set in, and they're still in the mood of the circumstance. In a conference room two weeks later, they'll invent a reason for their behavior. At the door of the restaurant at 6:40am with a milkshake in their hand, they'll tell you what's actually going on.

3. **Ask what they hired when they didn't hire you.** This is the critical question and it does two things at once. It reveals what job they were doing (because the alternatives they tried all map onto the same job). And it reveals the real competitive set (bananas, donuts, bagels — not Burger King). Phrase it as a memory prompt: "Tell me about the last time you were in the same situation and you didn't come here. What did you do? What did you hire?"

4. **Press for the functional, emotional, and social dimensions.** "What were you trying to accomplish?" gets the functional. "How did you want to feel while you were doing it?" gets the emotional. "What would you have told someone if they asked what you were up to?" gets the social. Don't accept a functional answer as the whole job.

5. **Also interview the people who fired you, and the non-consumers.** The people who bought your product once and quit have a different kind of evidence than the people still buying. The people who never bought anything at all — non-consumption — are often the biggest opportunity. SNHU's online-learning business grew when it realized it wasn't competing with the local college; it was competing with nothing. Bob Moesta's condo interviews with *buyers* uncovered the dining-room-table insight, but the more expensive lesson was about the non-buyers who walked through, nodded, and never came back.

6. **Find the pattern across many conversations.** One interview is a hypothesis. A job that's real recurs across many people who share the circumstance. Look for the recurring structure — not the words, the structure. The commuters used different words (bored, tired, long drive, nothing to do) but the job was the same.

7. **Define the job precisely enough to build against it.** The test of a well-defined job: you can tell a designer exactly which features to add and, just as importantly, which features to leave out. A poorly defined job produces a feature list with something for everyone. A well-defined job produces a product with the guts to be specific.

## Examples

**Situation:** McDonald's is running conventional market research — demographic profiling, focus groups with profile-matching customers, feature-level feedback. Customers give clear answers. McDonald's improves the milkshake on those dimensions. Nothing moves.

**Application:** Run the milkshake method. Stand in the restaurant for 18 hours. Note that 40% of milkshakes are sold before 8:30am, to lone customers, always in cars, always as the only purchase. Return at 6am the next day, confront customers with shakes in hand at the door, ask what job they were trying to get done and what they hired when they didn't hire a milkshake. The answers converge: bagels (dry), donuts (can't hire just one), bananas (gone in a minute), Snickers (guilt). The job is a long-boring-drive job with functional dimensions (stave off hunger until 10, one-handed, clean), emotional dimensions (don't be bored, stay engaged with life), and social dimensions (manage the morning like a competent adult).

**Result:** Make the milkshake thicker so it takes 23 minutes to get through a thin straw. Swirl in chunks of fruit so there's a little unpredictability to the monotonous commute. Move the machine in front of the counter and sell a prepaid swipe card so the commuter can dash in and out without waiting in the drive-through. Sales increase 7x in the parts of America where it's implemented. Same demographic. Same location. Different lens.

**Situation:** Kodak spent $8 billion starting in the early 1990s getting really good at digital imaging. They hired the right people — ex–Bell Labs researchers. They knew digital was coming. They invested heavily. They were disrupted anyway.

**Application:** Kodak defined the job as "preserve memories for posterity." That was wrong. Ninety-eight percent of all the images ever taken have only ever been looked at once. Think about the old routine: get the prints back from the drugstore, flip through them once, put them in an envelope, put the envelope in a box, put the box in a drawer. The box in the drawer is the tell — there was no "preserve memories" job driving that behavior. What gradually emerged — inadvertently, through Kodak's own double-print promotion — was a different job: "I want to communicate with people who aren't here and now, and I can do it better with an image than with words." Grandma, look at this photograph. I love you but I'm busy. Little by little that job coalesced. And when a new technology came along — a tiny flash-memory chip in a smartphone that could send twenty images instantly — the job was fully exposed. Kodak had invested against the wrong job.

**Result:** A well-executed milkshake method in 1995 would have watched people taking photos, watched what happened to the photos afterward, asked "when you didn't take a photo, what did you do?" (wrote a letter; called; sent a postcard), and discovered that the real competitor was the phone call, not the other camera. The job was communication, not preservation. The product would have been a networked, shared-in-the-moment camera — which is what the smartphone eventually became. Kodak had the money, the talent, and the engineering. What they didn't have was the right job.

**Situation:** Bob Moesta is called in by a Detroit-area builder selling new condos to downsizers. Units are well-priced, well-equipped, sales team is attentive, marketing is broad, traffic is good, conversions are not.

**Application:** Moesta doesn't accept the usual explanations (weather, recession, salespeople, location). He interviews the buyers who *did* convert and asks them to draw the timeline of how they got to the decision. The conversations keep circling back to something nobody had thought relevant: the dining room table in the house they were leaving. Not a feature of the new condo — a feature of their old life. The table represented family. The decision to give it up was stopping the decision to move. The job wasn't "acquire a new condo." The job was "transition into a new life without losing what mattered about the old one." The struggle wasn't about features in the condo. The struggle was about anxiety and inertia around the move itself.

**Result:** The builder reshapes the product around the real job. Creates space for a dining room table (by shrinking the second bedroom). Provides moving services. Provides two years of storage. Builds a sorting room on-site. Raises prices $3,500. Grows 25% in a housing market that dropped 49%.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run focus groups in a conference room and skip the field.
**Why:** A person asked in a conference room what would make them buy more milkshakes will give you a list of product attributes — thicker, chunkier, more chocolaty. That list produced zero sales uplift when McDonald's acted on it. The job is only visible in the circumstance. Go to the circumstance.

**Don't:** Interview only current customers.
**Why:** Current customers tell you what's working for the jobs you're already serving. The bigger opportunity is in non-consumption — the people who never hired anyone because nothing available did the job. SNHU didn't discover its growth market by studying its students; it discovered it by noticing that a whole population of older learners was competing with *nothing*. The milkshake-method lens applied to non-consumers is often where the 7x growth is hiding.

**Don't:** Ask "what do you want?" as the first question.
**Why:** Customers answer that question with platitudes. "Faster, cheaper, prettier, easier." None of these tells you the job. The question that works is, "Tell me about the last time you were in this situation, and what did you hire." That question surfaces the actual alternatives, the actual competitive set, and — by implication — the job.

**Don't:** Stop after one or two good-sounding interviews.
**Why:** One eloquent customer can make you believe a job is real when it's actually an idiosyncrasy. A job that's real shows up in many people who share the circumstance, in the same structural pattern (even when their words differ). Hold off on product decisions until the pattern has repeated. The cost of acting on a single interview is building the wrong product.

**Don't:** Treat the functional answer as the whole job.
**Why:** "I need something to eat in the car" is a functional answer. "I need something to keep me engaged with life on a long boring drive, so I'm not bored and not hungry at 10, and I can still feel in control of my morning" is the job. The functional is a third of it. If you design for just the functional, a competitor who also designs for the emotional and social will take the customer from you.

**Don't:** Let the method collapse into "customer interviews."
**Why:** The milkshake method is not "talk to customers." It is: observe in circumstance, interview in circumstance, ask what they hired when they didn't hire you, push through to all three dimensions, interview non-consumers, and find the pattern across many. Cut any of those steps and it collapses back into the sort of research McDonald's was already doing — which produced nothing.
