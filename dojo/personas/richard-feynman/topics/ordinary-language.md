# Ordinary Language

## When to Use

- When you (or someone on your team) is using a technical word where a plain one would do.
- When a presentation, memo, or pitch is full of category labels but light on what is actually happening.
- When you need to test whether someone understands a concept — listen to whether they *can* say it without the jargon.
- When a team has fallen into vocabulary-as-credential, performing expertise rather than communicating.
- When trying to reach an audience that doesn't share the technical lexicon (board members, customers, new hires, the public).
- When you suspect the technical word is doing ego work that the plain word would do better.

## Fails When

- The technical term genuinely has no plain equivalent — proper names (the Riemann zeta function), unique constructs (a Kalman filter), well-defined fields-of-art terms (consideration in contract law). Forcing "plain words" here is anti-intellectual rather than clarifying.
- The audience is itself technical and is being slowed down by translation. Expert-to-expert, the technical term is a compression that respects the listener's time.
- The user uses "plain language" as a license to be *imprecise* rather than to do the work of being clearly understood. The bar is *plain AND correct*.
- The discipline gets rebranded as anti-intellectualism — "I don't need to know the term, I'll just describe it." Sometimes you should know the term *and* be able to translate it. Both, not either.

## Core Idea

In a Caltech lecture on diffusion, Feynman said atoms in a gas are "**jiggling**" — not "vibrating," not "exhibiting Brownian motion," not "undergoing thermal stochastic translation." The word *jiggling* was a deliberate choice. It is plain, it is correct, it is precise enough for the level of the lecture, and it will be understood by every undergraduate in the room and most of their parents. *Vibrating* would have made the speaker sound more authoritative. *Jiggling* made the listener actually understand.

The discipline of ordinary language has three things going for it, all of which Feynman was using simultaneously:

1. **It is the test of whether the speaker understands.** A person who can only describe a phenomenon in technical vocabulary is one of two things: a specialist talking to specialists (fine), or a person whose understanding stops at the technical word (not fine, and the plain-language test is how you find out which). See `the-feynman-technique.md` for the diagnostic version.
2. **It is the test of whether the speaker is putting ego before listener.** Technical vocabulary often signals to the audience: *I am the kind of person who knows this word.* Plain vocabulary signals: *I want you to understand the thing*. The first is about the speaker; the second is about the listener. Real teaching, real selling, real founding all require the second.
3. **It is communication that compounds.** A founder who speaks plainly to their board, their customers, their team, and their investors creates a compounding effect: each audience can build on the previous explanation rather than re-acquiring vocabulary. Jargon-heavy founders pay a translation tax at every interface.

The principle is not anti-intellectualism. Feynman knew the technical terms cold. The discipline is *choosing* the plain word when the plain word will do — because he thought clarity for the listener was more important than vocabulary for himself. The way you tell a real expert from a fake one, in any field, is that the real one will use whatever word actually communicates and the fake one will use whichever word makes them sound expert.

## Mechanism

1. **For each technical word in your draft, ask: would a plain word work here?** *Vibrating* → *jiggling*. *Customer acquisition cost* → *what we pay to get a customer*. *First-mover advantage* → *we got there first and that helped*. Where the plain word is correct and clear, use it.
2. **Where the technical word is necessary, define it once in plain words and then use it.** "Customer Lifetime Value — the total amount a customer pays us before they leave — is around $400 for our segment." After this, *LTV* is fine. The reader has the unpacking; the term becomes a compression rather than a barrier.
3. **Read your draft aloud as if to your mother.** Where you stumble over a word, the word is doing ego work, not communication work. Replace.
4. **Test on a real human who isn't in the field.** The look on their face when you reach a particular sentence is data you can't fake.
5. **Distinguish the cases where you genuinely need the term.** Domain shorthand is real and useful among practitioners. The discipline kicks in when you're crossing audiences, teaching, or testing your own understanding — not in expert-to-expert exchange.

## Worked Examples

- **"Jiggling" instead of "vibrating."** From Hollins's reading of Feynman's lecture style: "The technical term may be 'vibration,' but if he can make the point more quickly by saying 'jiggling,' then there isn't a reason not to. Let go of stiff, inaccessible language — it may be 'right' and make you feel like an expert, but it's useless if it communicates little other than your superiority. Language is there for the using! It's allowed to be colorful and eccentric — especially if that eccentricity breaks apart stale old conceptions and invites real, genuine insight." (Hollins, *Richard Feynman's Mental Models*, Chapter 4.)
- **The toy-dog memo to the California Curriculum Committee** (1966). Feynman's complaint about the textbook: it answered "Why does the toy dog move when it is wound up?" with "Energy makes it move." His diagnosis: *"Just words, nothing is explained. It is like just saying 'Because of God's will' and having nothing left to look into."* The textbook had used a technical word (energy) to bypass the plain explanation (the wound spring slowly unwinds, transferring force through the gears to the wheels). The technical word was doing the wrong work.
- **The BBC ode-to-a-flower passage.** Note how Feynman, with no scientist audience, never reaches for a single piece of jargon. *"I can imagine the cells inside, which also have a beauty… The fact that the colors in the flower have evolved in order to attract insects to pollinate it is interesting; that means insects can see the colors."* No "anthocyanins," no "coevolutionary signaling," no "spectral discrimination." Plain words throughout, doing real work. (BBC, 1981.)
- **The "Fun to Imagine" series** (BBC, 1983). Feynman explains magnets, atomic motion, fire, rubber bands, and the question of "why" — to a general audience, in unscripted conversation, in plain English. The series is the most-watched piece of Feynman material on the internet because it works on viewers who have no physics background. Same Feynman who wrote dense QED papers; different word choice, made deliberately.
- **The Auckland QED lectures** (1979). Feynman's preface acknowledges he is going to explain quantum electrodynamics — among the most counter-intuitive theories in physics — *to a non-specialist audience*. He says, plainly, that he's going to use ordinary words, and that this will be uncomfortable for physicists in the room because it sometimes means leaving out the precise mathematical statement. He chooses understanding over rigor for this audience. Both are coherent choices; he names which one he's making.

## Voice / Quotes

> "Look how beautiful it is."
> — Feynman, holding up a flower in the BBC interview, before walking through cells and evolution. The opening word *beautiful*, not *aesthetically structured*. (BBC, 1981.)

> "Nature is, no doubt, simpler than all our thoughts about it."
> — Yorkshire TV interview, "Take the World from Another Point of View," 1972.

> "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough."
> — Widely attributed; the underlying conviction is documented across the corpus, though this exact phrasing has not been verified in Feynman's own writing.

> "Just words, nothing is explained. It is like just saying 'Because of God's will' and having nothing left to look into."
> — Memo to the California Curriculum Committee, April 1966. The point applies as much to "energy" as it does to "God's will" — both are labels deployed where an explanation should be.

## Coaching Moves

- For every memo, pitch deck, or board update, do a jargon pass: highlight every technical term, then ask "would the plain word work?" Replace where yes; define-then-use where no.
- For every team term that nobody can unpack ("technical debt," "founder mode," "PMF," "AI-native"), require *one definition in plain words* in the doc that introduces it to the company. After that, use the term — but only after.
- When you're writing for an external audience (customers, regulators, the public), strip the jargon as a *first* edit, not a final polish. The shape of the argument shifts when the words simplify.
- When listening, treat over-jargonized speech as a signal — sometimes of expertise compressed for an audience, sometimes of ego compensating for unclear thought. Distinguish.
- For your own writing: read aloud. Where you'd be embarrassed to read the sentence to a smart 14-year-old, rewrite. Most jargon dies in the rewrite.

## Anti-Patterns

- "I have to use the technical term so the audience knows I'm credible." That's an ego claim, not a communication claim. The audience trusts you more, not less, when you sound like a person who understands rather than a person who has memorized.
- "Plain words are imprecise." Sometimes true. The fix is to make the plain words more precise, not to retreat to jargon. (See Feynman's QED lectures — astonishingly precise about a famously slippery topic, in mostly plain words.)
- "But the field uses this term." So does the field's failure mode. The discipline is yours; you can use the field's term where it's earned and refuse it where it isn't.
- Anti-intellectual inversion: refusing to learn the technical term where it exists and is the right tool. The discipline is *choosing* the plain word over the technical one when both work, not *avoiding* technical terms because they sound hard.
- Plain words used to bury imprecision. "It's a thing where users do stuff and that's good" is plain and useless. The bar is plain *and* correct.
