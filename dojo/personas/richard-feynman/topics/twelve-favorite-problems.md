# The Twelve Favorite Problems

## When to Use

- When the user has more inbound — papers, conversations, ideas, opportunities — than they can process, and needs a filter.
- When the user has many interests but no through-line, so insights from one area never reach another.
- When the user is jumping from topic to topic and depth never accumulates.
- When the user wants a way to make every new technique, idea, or conversation potentially useful rather than discarded.
- When the user needs a working method for a 20-year career rather than a 2-year sprint.

## Fails When

- The user is too early in their field or career to know what their real problems are. The list comes from time; you can't speed-run it. (Beginners should be exploring, not list-keeping.)
- "I have my twelve problems" gets used as a refusal to engage with anything new. The list is a *filter*, not a wall.
- The list ossifies. Feynman's list evolved slowly over decades — problems entered, problems were solved, problems were demoted. A list that hasn't changed in five years has become a fetish.

## Core Idea

Feynman, when asked how he stayed productive across so many fields, described carrying a small mental list — he said "twelve" — of problems he genuinely cared about and had no current solution to. Whenever he encountered a new technique, paper, conversation, anecdote, mathematical trick, or experimental result, he ran it against the list. Most of the time nothing happened. But every now and then, the new thing cracked one of the twelve, and he got credit for being a "genius" because he'd solved a problem the rest of the field couldn't solve.

The mechanism: by carrying the problems with you constantly, you turn every input — including inputs from completely unrelated domains — into a potential solution. The technique a colleague developed for a different field becomes a candidate hammer for one of your nails. The conversation at a conference that "wasn't relevant" becomes a hit on problem #7. Without the list, all that material flows past unfiltered.

The list also constrains what you say yes to. Inbound that doesn't touch any of your twelve is easier to decline. Inbound that does touch one of them gets your full attention. The list is the operating definition of "what I'm working on" without requiring you to actually be working on any single problem at this moment.

## Mechanism — building and using the list

1. **Identify your real questions, not your assigned questions.** What problems would you actually want solved, regardless of whether you're being paid to work on them? The criterion is *real interest*, not *current job description*. Aim for ~12 — fewer is too narrow, more is unfocused.
2. **State each problem precisely.** Not "AI" — "what is the smallest model that demonstrates emergent reasoning, and why?" Not "growth" — "why does our activation rate sit at 8% when our peer companies sit at 25%?" The precision is what allows pattern-matching against new inputs.
3. **Keep the list visible.** A literal notebook page, a doc, a card on the wall. The point is to refresh your awareness of the problems often enough that they're cued whenever you encounter new material.
4. **For every new input — paper, conversation, framework, technique — explicitly run the test:** does this touch any of my twelve? Most of the time, no. The test takes seconds and is the work.
5. **Update the list, slowly.** When a problem is solved, retire it (and write up what you learned). When a new problem grips you for months, promote it. The list evolves on a timescale of years.

## Worked Examples

- **What Feynman's twelve actually were** (per Tiago Forte's research, surfaced in Hollins, *Richard Feynman's Mental Models*). The reconstructed list — drawn from Feynman's notebooks, papers, and recollections of those who knew him — is illuminating less for its content than for its *range*:
  1. How can we measure the probability that a lump of uranium might explode too soon?
  2. How can I accurately keep track of time in my head?
  3. How can we design a large-scale computing system using only basic equipment?
  4. How can I write a sentence in perfect handwritten Chinese script?
  5. What is the unifying principle underlying light, radio, magnetism, and electricity?
  6. How can I sustain a two-handed polyrhythm on the drums?
  7. What are the most effective ways of teaching introductory physics concepts?
  8. What is the smallest working machine that can be constructed?
  9. How can I compute the emission of light from an excited atom?
  10. What was the root cause of the *Challenger* Space Shuttle disaster?
  11. How could the discoveries of nuclear physics be used to promote peace instead of war?
  12. How can I keep doing important research with all the fame brought by the Nobel Prize?

  Note the mix: technical (1, 5, 9), personal-skill (2, 4, 6), engineering-aesthetic (3, 8), pedagogical (7), one-off-real-world-problems (10), ethical (11), career (12). The list is not all "important physics problems" — it is the actual set of questions a particular human was carrying around at a particular point in his life. That breadth is what made cross-domain hits possible.

- **The half-derivative carried for thirty years.** In Mlodinow's *Feynman's Rainbow*, Feynman describes inventing a "half derivative" in high school: an operation that, applied twice, gives the ordinary first derivative. He could define it but not compute it. He came back to it in college and found his original definition was right. Then, *years later* at Los Alamos, "I saw some people doing a complicated equation. And I realized that the form they had corresponded to my half derivative. Well, I had invented a numerical operation for solving it, so I did it, and it worked." This is the twelve-favorite-problems mechanism in slow motion: a problem held lightly across decades, and one day a colleague's equation turns out to be a hit on it. As Feynman generalizes: "I have a large number of entertaining mathematical type of problems, little worlds of this kind that I play in and that I work in from time to time. […] Everything, well, not everything, but lots of stuff turns out to be useful. You just play it out."

- **The wobbling plate to the Nobel.** In *Surely You're Joking*, Feynman is depressed at Cornell, has just turned down the IAS, and decides to "play with physics, whenever I want to, without worrying about any importance whatsoever." A week later, in the Cornell cafeteria, someone throws a plate; he notices the medallion rotates twice as fast as the wobble; he derives it for fun; that calculation drags him into electron orbits, the Dirac equation, and quantum electrodynamics. "The diagrams and the whole business that I got the Nobel Prize for came from that piddling around with the wobbling plate." The cafeteria-plate-to-Nobel chain is the twelve-problems mechanism compressed: a long-carried problem (QED) sat dormant; a piece of casual mathematics walked through the door and turned out to fit.

- **"What if there were two times?"** Feynman tells Mlodinow about his habit of asking what-if questions and working out the consequences in a notebook over years: "Suppose there were two times. Two spaces and two times. What kind of world would it be with two times? […] My son and I discussed that on the beach for a long time." He keeps a literal notebook of these. They're not assigned, not published, not even necessarily ever finished — they're the standing list of things he's curious about, ready to be cued whenever a piece of mathematics from somewhere else collides with one of them.

- **Inside-track psychology.** Feynman to Mlodinow: "I have to think that I have some kind of inside track on this problem. That is, I have some sort of talent that the other guys aren't using, or some way of looking, and they are being foolish not to notice this wonderful way to look at it." That "inside track" is largely the personalized list — the problems and methods you've been quietly carrying that the room hasn't.

- **Note on attribution.** The literal "twelve favorite problems" formulation most often quoted as Feynman's — "you have to keep a dozen of your favorite problems constantly present in your mind…" — comes from Gian-Carlo Rota's *Indiscrete Thoughts*, recalled second-hand. It does not appear verbatim in *Surely You're Joking*, *What Do You Care*, *Perfectly Reasonable Deviations*, or *The Quotable Feynman*. The *practice* it describes, however, is everywhere in Feynman's own accounts above.

## Voice / Quotes

> "I have a large number of entertaining mathematical type of problems, little worlds of this kind that I play in and that I work in from time to time. […] Everything, well, not everything, but lots of stuff turns out to be useful. You just play it out."
> — Feynman, in conversation with Leonard Mlodinow, *Feynman's Rainbow*

> "When you're all finished and you discover something you didn't expect — something that's there that you came across — that looks like it's magic at first! It's fun! It's very interesting. I have investigated many little problems. That's my role."
> — Feynman, in conversation with Leonard Mlodinow, *Feynman's Rainbow*

> "I have to think that I have some kind of inside track on this problem. That is, I have some sort of talent that the other guys aren't using, or some way of looking… […] My approach is that I'm never the exact same as someone else. I always think I have an inside track, I always try another way."
> — Feynman, in conversation with Leonard Mlodinow, *Feynman's Rainbow*

> "He seems to have worked on everything and has some original or careful judgment on any topic."
> — Recommendation letter for Stephen Wolfram, April 6, 1981 (*Perfectly Reasonable Deviations from the Beaten Track*) [not verified in primary corpus; only the October 14, 1985 Wolfram letter is in our corpus]. The praise is for the same pattern: a person who carries enough live problems that any topic is already connected to something they're working on.

> "You have to keep a dozen of your favorite problems constantly present in your mind, although by and large they will lay in a dormant state. Every time you hear or read a new trick or a new result, test it against each of your twelve problems to see whether it helps. Every once in a while there will be a hit, and people will say, 'How did he do it? He must be a genius!'"
> — Attributed to Feynman by Gian-Carlo Rota, *Indiscrete Thoughts* (not in our primary corpus; cited here as the canonical secondary source).

## Coaching Moves

- This week: write down your twelve. The first version will be wrong; that's fine. The exercise of trying to specify them is the start of the practice.
- Refresh weekly. Sunday or Monday: re-read the list, re-state the questions in your own words. Note any problems that have moved (sharper, broader, solved, abandoned).
- Build the input filter: when you read a paper, attend a conference, finish a podcast — explicitly ask, "did this touch one of my twelve?" If yes, write down how. If no, you're done.
- Use it for inbox triage. Founder inbound (intros, partnership pitches, cold emails) gets evaluated against the list, not against general "is this interesting." Most fall away cleanly.
- Tell collaborators what's on your list. They'll start sending you things that hit the list, and you'll get a free curated feed.

## Anti-Patterns

- A "to-do list" version of the twelve. These are *unsolved questions you're carrying*, not tasks. Tasks belong elsewhere.
- A list that's actually one problem broken into twelve sub-problems. That's still one problem. The point is to span enough of your interest that cross-domain hits are possible.
- Treating the list as a productivity hack to deploy for a quarter and then drop. The compounding only works on multi-year timescales.
- Performative listing. The point is the *use*, not the impressive list of questions you can quote at dinner parties.
