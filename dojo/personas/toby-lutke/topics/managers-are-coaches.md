# Managers Are Coaches (Not Bosses)

## When to Use
- Redesigning performance reviews, 1:1 cadence, or the manager-employee relationship at a company that's growing through 50–500 people.
- Setting up a management training program and deciding what you actually want managers to do — as opposed to what titles suggest they should do.
- Promoting a strong individual contributor into their first management role. The default they've absorbed from the industry is probably wrong. This is the right moment to reset it.
- Writing a leadership memo or management philosophy that will shape the company's operating model for the next decade.
- Responding to a board or investor who wants stack-ranking, bell curves, or other performance-review machinery that treats managers as evaluators rather than coaches.

## Fails When
- Used as cover for being vague. "I'm a coach, not a boss" is worse than being an honest boss if the result is no feedback, no standards, and no direction. Coaching requires higher-quality feedback, not less of it.
- Applied in crisis moments where the right call is a direct order, a fast decision, or an accountability moment. Coaches on the sideline are sometimes the wrong role for the moment. A coach who won't step in when the game is on fire is failing the team.
- Deployed with genuinely poor performers whose trust battery is near zero (see trust-battery.md) and whose pattern is set. Coaching is for the 95% of people who are underperforming their potential. It's not the right frame when the honest answer is "this isn't working and we should part ways."
- Used as an aesthetic — "we don't have hierarchy, we have coaches!" — without changing what the manager actually does day to day. Rebranding bosses as coaches while keeping all the boss behaviors intact is worse than leaving them called bosses. Naming without substance is theatre.

## Core Concept

The default model of a manager in most companies is: you were a good individual contributor, so as a reward and promotion, you now evaluate, direct, and judge other people. Under this model, the manager's core function is oversight. Their core interaction is performance review. Their core identity is "boss."

I reject almost all of this at Shopify.

My model — and the one I've spent twenty years trying to get right — is that a manager's job is to coach. Their core function is to help the people under them reach a level of performance they couldn't have reached on their own. Their core interaction is an ongoing conversation about the work and about the person's growth inside of it. Their core identity is "I am here to make you better."

This isn't therapy. It isn't softer than traditional management. In some ways it's considerably harder. It means the manager has to actually see each person, understand where they are relative to their own potential (not relative to a corporate bell curve), and give them feedback that is direct, often uncomfortable, and clearly in service of their growth rather than the company's compliance.

Here's the frame I keep coming back to: I really really really think that there is not a single person on this planet who is even close to their maximum potential. This isn't an inspirational line. It's an operating assumption. If you start from it, the job of a manager stops being "fit this person into a role" and starts being "find the gap between where this person is and where they could be, and close it." That's coaching.

A few things follow directly from this:

- **Feedback is the primary deliverable of management, and it's often hard to hear.** If you give feedback that's easy to hear, you probably haven't said the thing. Good coaching feedback sounds like: "I think you had it in you to do this significantly better. You saw path A and path B early in the project and you chose B out of convenience even though you knew A was right. I actually expected better of you. Next time this comes up in your career, go path A — because that's what your conviction was telling you." That's hard to hear. It's also extremely valuable. Most managers won't say it because they mistake softness for kindness.

- **The standard is the person's own potential, not a company rubric.** I'm harder on myself than on anyone else, and by some discount rate I'm equally hard on the people around me — especially the ones who are obviously brilliant. Holding people to the standard of their own potential, for years, is how you see them accomplish things they didn't imagine they could. This is the best thing about the job. A corporate performance review graded against a normalized scale doesn't even touch this.

- **The manager's role is an editor's role, not a grader's role.** The editor's job is to provide information to the writer to make better decisions. Not a pass-or-fail verdict. The grading model — "here's your 3.5 out of 5 for this quarter" — treats the manager as a judge of the past. The coaching model treats the manager as a collaborator on the future.

- **ICs should not have to become managers to grow their career.** The traditional model — "the only path to making more money is to stop doing the thing you're good at and start managing people who still do it" — is a waste of talent. At Shopify we built a mastery track where ICs can progress indefinitely, reach the highest compensation bands, and not touch management. Managers exist because some people are genuinely great at coaching and that's a distinct craft. You don't get promoted into it as a reward for something unrelated.

- **Coaching is a specific skill, and most managers don't have it by default.** This is why Shopify has a talent acceleration team — coaches for our executives and managers. Harley called his executive coach one of the top three or four things that helped him lead Shopify. I agreed when I saw the benefit for Cody Fauser back in 2011 and started working with a coach myself. If we think the job is coaching, we have to teach people to coach, not just hand them a title and five direct reports.

- **The trust battery is how you track individual relationships; coaching is the job description.** These are different things (see trust-battery.md). Trust battery says: your working relationship with each person has a current state and it moves with every interaction. Coaching says: the manager's job is specifically to grow this person, using those interactions. One is the instrument; the other is the work.

Two caveats. First, coaching is not consensus or avoidance. A coach is allowed to tell you you're wrong. A coach is allowed to pull you off the field. A coach is allowed to cut you from the team. The frame "managers are coaches" does not mean managers are pushovers — it means their authority is used for growth, not status. Second: Shopify is a team, not a family. Teams have coaches. Families don't. Shopify has a mission, and if someone is on the team, we're trying to make them better at contributing to the mission. If they aren't, we part ways — respectfully but clearly. A family doesn't cut its members. A team does, because the team is there to win.

The distaste I have for traditional management hierarchy isn't romantic. It's because most management hierarchy is structured as a ladder of status and evaluation, and almost none of that generates better performance. It generates compliance. The coaching model generates growth. That's the actual product.

## How to Apply

1. **Redefine the manager job description.** Don't describe it in terms of oversight, evaluation, or headcount. Describe it in terms of growth — whose growth, over what time horizon, measured how. If the job description doesn't mention "make the people on your team materially better over the next year" as the primary deliverable, you haven't redefined it yet.

2. **Replace performance reviews with coaching conversations.** Not literally — HR still needs some artifacts — but in substance. The manager-employee conversation should be continuous and forward-looking, not quarterly and judgmental. A quarterly conversation that says "here's how you're tracking to your own potential and here's what I'd do next" is dramatically more useful than a quarterly conversation that says "here's your rating against a normalized scale."

3. **Give the hard feedback — directly, with conviction, aimed at their growth.** The easy feedback ("great work this quarter") is noise. The load-bearing feedback is: "I think you saw the better path and didn't take it. Here's what I'd have wanted you to do. I expect you to do it next time because I know you can." That's uncomfortable for both parties. Both parties should feel grown up enough to handle it.

4. **Build an IC track that doesn't require becoming a manager.** If your compensation caps for ICs are below the compensation floors for managers, you've broken your own coaching model. Everyone becomes a bad manager chasing comp. Fix the bands. At Shopify, an IC can be paid more than a VP if they're that good at their craft.

5. **Hire or train real coaches for your managers.** Executive coaching isn't a perk; it's a recognition that coaching is a skill managers need and usually don't get anywhere else. Shopify's talent acceleration team exists because we took this seriously. Most companies don't — and then wonder why their managers default to status games.

6. **Remind people of their potential, constantly.** Not performatively. Specifically. "I've seen you make this kind of call before; I know you can make it again." Reminders of potential, from someone credible, are one of the highest-leverage things a manager can do. Most people dramatically underestimate how good they can be. The coach's job is to puncture that underestimate, repeatedly.

7. **Hold people to the standard of their own potential, not the normalized scale.** If you do this well, very strong people will accomplish things they didn't imagine. If you do this badly, you'll just be exhausting. The difference is whether the standard comes from care and evidence, or from generic "push harder" pressure. Care plus evidence is coaching. Generic pressure is just being a hard boss wearing coaching clothes.

8. **Call it a team, not a family.** Families don't cut anyone. Teams do. The frame matters. A team can be deeply loyal, deeply close, deeply committed — and still part ways when someone isn't contributing to the mission. That's what a team is. A family frame makes the hard conversations impossible and then makes the coaching frame dishonest.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Shopify engineer delivers a project that shipped on time but took a path the manager thought was clearly worse than an alternative they had discussed early on.
**Application:** Traditional model: nothing happens — the project shipped, the metric is green. Coaching model: the manager says, "This shipped, but I think you had it in you to do it significantly better. You saw path A and path B early, and I think you chose path B out of convenience. I expected more from you there. Next time, follow the conviction. This is the kind of call you're good at; use it." That conversation is uncomfortable and it sticks.
**Result:** The engineer's next project reflects the conversation. Over a year, the gap between potential and execution closes. This is what "managers are coaches" actually looks like in practice. Without the conversation, the gap would have stayed open indefinitely, the project would have been called a success, and the engineer would have plateaued quietly.

**Situation:** A company is being pressured by its board to install stack-ranking performance reviews because "that's what serious public companies do."
**Application:** Stack-ranking is the opposite of coaching. It forces managers to rank-order their reports against each other, which means every conversation becomes comparative and political instead of growth-oriented. The alternative is an explicit framework where managers are evaluated on whether their people grew — measured through the people themselves, through their output, and through external observers — not through a normalized curve. The board wants a legible signal; give them one, but don't import the ranking machinery.
**Result:** The company resists stack-ranking and builds a coaching-based model. Over five years, retention of strong ICs improves dramatically and the manager tier is populated by people who are actually good at growth, not politics. Boards eventually understand, especially when the results speak.

**Situation:** A strong IC is offered a management promotion because that's "the only way to grow your career" at their current company. They don't want to manage but feel they have to.
**Application:** This is exactly the broken pattern. At Shopify we built the mastery track specifically to stop this: ICs can reach the highest comp bands without becoming managers. The coach's job is to find out what this person is actually great at and build a path around that — not funnel them into a role that will make them and their future reports miserable. If the company doesn't have that track, build it. If you can't build it, accept that you're losing great craftspeople to a structure problem, and fix the structure.
**Result:** The IC stays in their craft, grows to a staff-level role, keeps making things, and becomes a disproportionate contributor for another decade. The alternative would have cost the company the IC's craft and produced a mediocre manager.

**Situation:** A first-time manager is nervous about 1:1s and defaults to status updates: "what are you working on, what's blocked, what's next." Their reports feel "managed" but don't feel they're growing.
**Application:** Coaching-model 1:1s are fundamentally different. Not "what's blocked" — that's operational and can happen in Slack. More like: "What's been hard this week that you didn't expect? What's a piece of feedback you got from someone that you're still sitting with? If you were going to be twice as good at this by next year, what would you have to get better at?" Those are coach questions. They produce growth. Status-update questions produce status updates.
**Result:** The manager shifts the 1:1 structure. Reports describe the relationship differently within two months. Growth is visible six months later. The manager discovers that coaching is a specific skill they can train, not a talent they either have or don't.

**Situation:** A manager is avoiding a hard conversation with someone who's underperforming. They tell themselves they're "being kind."
**Application:** That's not kindness. That's avoidance dressed up. The coaching move is to sit down and say what's actually happening, with specific examples, framed around the person's own potential rather than a compliance rubric. "I've seen you do X better than this. You aren't doing X now. I'm not sure why. Help me understand." That's hard to hear. It's also the only path back. Kindness that avoids the conversation is just letting someone fail quietly.
**Result:** Either the person steps up — often dramatically, because they're being held to a standard that respects them — or the conversation clarifies that this isn't the right fit and a respectful parting happens earlier than it would have otherwise. Both outcomes are dramatically better than the silent drift.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "managers are coaches" as permission to skip hard feedback.
**Why:** Coaching done well involves more hard feedback than traditional management, not less. If the coaching language becomes a euphemism for "I don't want to say the uncomfortable thing," you've inverted the whole framework. Coaches tell you what you're doing wrong; that's the job.

**Don't:** Rebrand bosses as coaches without changing what they actually do.
**Why:** The frame is only valuable if it changes the behavior. Renaming the role while keeping all the old evaluation-and-oversight behaviors intact makes the company worse, because you've added confusion on top of the old model. Change the job, then change the name. Not the other way around.

**Don't:** Confuse coaching with consensus or softness.
**Why:** A coach can bench you. A coach can cut you. A coach is allowed to have a strong opinion about what you should do differently. The coaching frame is not a commitment to gentle dialogue in every case. It's a commitment to growth — which sometimes means exactly the kind of direct, uncomfortable conversation that a traditional boss would also have to have.

**Don't:** Forget that Shopify is a team, not a family.
**Why:** Team frames allow for hard decisions — benching, cutting, parting ways — while still being deeply committed to each other. Family frames make those decisions feel like betrayals. The coaching model only works if you have the team frame; otherwise the hard conversations become impossible and the coaching becomes theatre.

**Don't:** Skip building a real IC track because "we want everyone to be a generalist."
**Why:** Not having an IC track is how you get bad managers chasing comp. If your only path to seniority is management, people who don't want to manage will manage badly, and you'll lose the craftspeople who made your product good in the first place. Build the track.

**Don't:** Treat coaching as a one-off conversation rather than a continuous practice.
**Why:** Growth doesn't happen in a quarterly review. It happens in the small conversations, the specific piece of feedback after a meeting, the "I noticed you did X — here's what I'd push on for next time." If your coaching lives in scheduled ceremonies rather than the flow of work, you don't have coaching, you have performance theatre with new wallpaper.
