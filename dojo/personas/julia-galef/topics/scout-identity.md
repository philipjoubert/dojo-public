# A Scout Identity

## When to Use

- You've read about scout mindset, agreed with it, and are finding it doesn't stick.
- Updating your beliefs feels like a duty, not a pleasure — and that's making it easy to skip.
- You tried "keep your identity small" and ran into the same problem Julia did (causes you care about require you to publicly identify; daily life demands labels).
- You're hiring, building a team, or choosing a community, and you want to pick for good epistemics.
- You're an activist, founder, or evangelist who needs to be passionate about something without the passion killing your judgment.
- You want to know why some people seem to face hard truths cheerfully while others have to be dragged.

## Fails When

- **The user is performing rather than practicing.** A scout identity is about what you do when no one's watching — how you actually react when evidence contradicts you. If it becomes a public badge, it becomes the same kind of trap as any other identity. "Proud Rationalist" T-shirts miss the point.
- **The user still has the soldier identity live.** You can't just bolt a scout identity on top of an active soldier identity. If the belief-based identities still dominate, the scout identity will lose every fight. Usually you have to do hold-identity-lightly.md work first to clear space.
- **The process is treated as an excuse.** "I'm a scout, so I update all the time" can become its own kind of rigidity — an identity performed by constant public reversing. Updating for show isn't scout; it's just soldier-for-open-mindedness.

## Core Concept

The previous topic ("Hold Your Identity Lightly") argued against letting beliefs calcify into identity. Read alone, that creates a paradox. Human beings need identity to function. An identity provides motivation, belonging, a consistent self to act from. Strip it entirely and you get either drift (no sustained effort on anything) or something worse — a covert, unexamined identity filling the vacuum.

The resolution: **you do want an identity. Just one that rewards updating rather than defending.** Tie your pride to *how you think*, not *what you currently conclude*. *I'm the kind of person who takes the outsider test on my own beliefs. I'm the kind of person who doesn't make excuses. I'm the kind of person who can be shown I'm wrong and thank the person who did it.* That identity is compatible with — in fact actively requires — changing your mind. Every update becomes identity-affirming instead of identity-threatening. Every admission of error becomes a small hit of pride, because it's proof you're living up to who you said you'd be.

This is the direct inversion of the normal move. Normally, identity makes updating harder. Here, the right kind of identity makes updating *easier*. It's using the same emotional machinery — the tribalism, the pride, the in-group warmth, the desire to live up to a self-image — pointed at a different target.

Julia's version contrasts with Paul Graham's "Keep Your Identity Small." Graham said: the more labels you have, the dumber they make you. Julia tried that literally and ran into problems. You can't go to a dinner party explaining your dietary restrictions each time. You can't advocate for a cause you believe in if you refuse to publicly identify with it. Her revision: **hold the identities you have lightly, and keep scout-ness as the identity that dominates when they conflict.** Not smaller identity — *better* identity.

What makes a scout identity durable is that it converts a long-term payoff into a short-term reward. The brain is wired to reach for immediate emotional comfort, which is why we default to soldier mindset in the moment. An identity is a patch for that wiring. Resisting the urge to take a cheap shot at someone you disagree with isn't just "the long-run-correct thing to do" anymore; it's *who you are*, and not taking the cheap shot now *feels good now*. Admitting you've been wrong about something isn't just "epistemically correct"; it's *living up to your standards*, and that lands with a rush of satisfaction the same way a good workout does. The sting is still there — updating still costs something — but, like sore muscles, it becomes the kind of sting that feels earned.

Three things sustain a scout identity over time:

1. **Communities that reward updating.** The community you're in shapes your identity almost invisibly. Bethany Brookshire caught her own errors easily in high school drama club (imperfection was expected). She struggled with it in academia (where colleagues pounced on any admission). She rediscovered it as a journalist (her editor and readers applauded corrections). Choose communities that cheer when you say "I was wrong." If you don't have one, build one — even a loosely knit one on the internet counts.

2. **Role models you can point to.** Not generic ones — specific people who embody one aspect of scout mindset you find particularly compelling. For Julian Sanchez, it was Robert Nozick — a philosopher who didn't try to force conclusions, who raised objections to his own views, who made intellectual uncertainty look confident rather than weak. For Julia, it's the old Oxford zoologist who, after fifteen years arguing the Golgi apparatus was illusory, listened to a visiting scholar's evidence, stood up, walked to the front of the hall, shook the scholar's hand and said *My dear fellow, I wish to thank you. I have been wrong these fifteen years.* That kind of specific image lives in your head and makes scout choices tempting in the moment when they'd otherwise be aversive.

3. **Self-concept reinforcement on small wins.** Notice yourself updating and be proud of it. Notice yourself not taking a cheap shot and be proud of it. Notice yourself spotting your own motivated reasoning — and especially then be proud of it, since *spotting* motivated reasoning is strictly better than being immune to it (which no one is). These are the daily reps that build the identity.

The emotional rewards aren't incidental — they're why scout mindset becomes durable. Scouts look calm, cheerful, playful, resolute. They're not grim truth-warriors slogging through reality. They've found a way to *enjoy* the thing most people find painful. Jerry Taylor, after having his skeptic position shredded by Bob Litterman, described the feeling as *invigorating*. That's the tell. When updating starts to feel like that rather than like humiliation, the identity has taken root.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the process-identity you want.** Write it down in a sentence or two. *I'm the kind of person who changes my mind when the evidence warrants. I'm the kind of person who doesn't dunk on people I disagree with. I'm the kind of person who looks at hard truths rather than around them.* The wording isn't sacred — pick something that feels true to who you're trying to be. The act of naming makes the identity load-bearing.
2. **Choose a role model.** Pick one person, real or historical, who embodies scout mindset in a way that resonates for you. Not a generic rationalist. A specific human with a specific story. Keep them in mind. When the soldier impulse fires, ask what they would do.
3. **Audit your community.** List the five people you talk to most about ideas. Do they reward you for updating, or punish you? Do they make good criticism land softly or make admitting error feel suicidal? You can't fully fix the community you have — but you can add better voices to your information diet. Follow scouts. Read scouts. Reply to scouts. Reduce your exposure to tribalized voices even of "your side."
4. **Make the hits of pride real.** When you catch yourself mid-rationalization and stop — *be proud of yourself for noticing*. Julia says this explicitly: *becoming more aware of motivated reasoning is an essential step on the way to reducing it, and you should feel good about taking that step.* Don't skip the emotional payoff. It's doing real work.
5. **Make the identity visible to yourself, not necessarily to others.** You don't need a Twitter bio that says "Rationalist." The identity works internally, in the moment of temptation. Public scout-signalling can actually backfire — it becomes something to defend, which is exactly the bug this design is trying to avoid.
6. **Use the identity in hard moments.** Before you respond to a piece of criticism, before you post the angry tweet, before you dismiss the confusing data point — pause and ask: *am I being who I said I wanted to be?* The pause is usually enough to flip the choice.
7. **Choose communities that pre-filter for scouts.** ChangeAView (where users award "deltas" to arguments that shift them, and accumulated deltas show next to your name — status tied to being persuaded) selects for people willing to update. r/FeMRADebates selects similarly through its rules. Effective altruism publishes "Our Mistakes" pages. These aren't the only options, but they're the archetype. If you can be in a community where the status gradient points toward good epistemics, you barely have to think about maintaining the identity yourself — the environment does the work.

## Examples

**Situation: Susan Blackmore and the second identity.**
Blackmore was an Oxford psychology student in 1970 when she had an out-of-body experience on drugs. She built her whole identity around belief in the paranormal — trained as a witch, attended Spiritualist churches, read tarot, hunted ghosts. She switched her PhD to parapsychology to prove it real. For years her experiments yielded chance results. When occasionally she got a significant result, she'd double-check and find the error. The paranormal identity should have made updating impossible — her friends, her wardrobe, her professional life were all built around it. But she had a second identity, stronger than the first: *deep down, I was a scientist and always have been*. She prided herself on subjecting her conclusions to scrutiny. That identity outranked the paranormal one when they collided. She ended up as one of the UK's best-known skeptics. The move: have an identity whose content is *I follow evidence*, not *I believe X*. When X starts to look wrong, the first identity supports the abandonment of X instead of fighting it.

**Situation: Joshua Harris changing his self-concept.**
Harris had written *I Kissed Dating Goodbye* at 21, become famous, and then spent fifteen years as the "leader with answers." When criticisms of the book started accumulating, he hit a wall: *Part of the reason this has been so hard for me is that I have so much of my identity tied up in these books. It's what I'm known for. Is the biggest thing I've done in my life this really huge mistake?* What finally enabled the update: he stepped down as pastor and enrolled in graduate school at age 40. *The change in his role sparked a corresponding change in the way he saw himself. He was no longer the "leader with answers." He was a "student with questions."* The new identity made updating structurally compatible with his self-concept. By 2018 he'd pulled the book from publication. The lesson isn't that he changed his position; it's that changing *who he was* is what freed him to change his position.

**Situation: Vitalik Buterin attracting scouts to Ethereum.**
Buterin is the co-founder of Ethereum, the second-largest cryptocurrency by market cap. The default mode for a figure in his position is hype — utter certainty, guru vibes, "we're building the future." His mode is different. In one conversation about Ethereum's flaws, he dropped in uninvited and said *IMO the most valid criticisms of Ethereum as it currently stands are…* and listed seven problems. At the 2017 peak of crypto mania, he tweeted skeptically *have we earned it?* and listed reasons the whole field was overvalued. Critics paraphrased him uncharitably — *Buterin admits he doesn't believe in Ethereum!* He doesn't care, because the style isn't just honesty for its own sake — it's a selection filter. *The people to whom it does appeal tend to be especially thoughtful, smart, and scout-like — and those are the kinds of people he wants to attract to Ethereum.* Your communication style shapes who shows up. If you want scouts around you, talk like one.

**Situation: The Oxford zoologist and the Golgi apparatus.**
An elderly Oxford professor had spent fifteen years arguing the Golgi apparatus — a cellular structure — was an illusion created by observational methods. A young visiting scholar from the US gave a talk presenting new and compelling evidence that it was real. Everyone in the audience watched the elderly professor, wondering how he'd take it. At the end of the talk, the professor stood up, walked to the front of the lecture hall, reached out to shake the visiting scholar's hand, and said: *My dear fellow, I wish to thank you. I have been wrong these fifteen years.* The lecture hall burst into applause. Richard Dawkins, who told the story, says it still brings a lump to his throat. For Julia it's the single most inspiring image of scout mindset — *that's the kind of person I want to be*. That kind of role model, lived imaginatively, is worth more than any abstract argument for why scout mindset matters. When the temptation to defend kicks in, the image is there as an alternative.

**Situation: Bethany Brookshire across three communities.**
Brookshire is a science journalist who cares about getting things right. Her ability to notice and acknowledge her own errors has fluctuated across her life entirely with her environment. In high school drama club: easy — imperfection was expected. In a PhD program: hard — colleagues pounced on admissions of error, and she had to *struggle to overcome the impulse* to paper over mistakes. As a journalist: easy again — her editor was appreciative, and her readers applauded corrections. When she publicly corrected herself on a viral claim about gender bias in emails, the reaction was *surprising and awesome follow-up. Inspirational. We need more of this.* Same person, same values, three different environments, three different revealed scout capacities. The takeaway for anyone serious about this: don't try to white-knuckle scout mindset alone in a hostile environment. Move to a friendly one, or build one.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't: Wear "scout" as a badge.**
*Why:* The moment "scout" becomes a label you wave, it's doing the identity job in the wrong direction — something to defend rather than a process to run. Practice it internally; let it show through your behavior, not your bio. The people who are most clearly scouts rarely advertise the label.

**Don't: Perform updates for credibility.**
*Why:* "I've updated on X" has become a status move in some communities — a way to signal sophistication. If you catch yourself announcing updates mainly for the social reward, you're running soldier mindset through scout costumes. Real updates happen quietly; the announcement, if any, is incidental.

**Don't: Strip all your ideological identities and expect the process identity to fill the gap automatically.**
*Why:* It takes time to build a process identity that's strong enough to anchor you. If you nuke your liberal-or-conservative-or-whatever identity on Monday expecting to be "a rationalist" by Friday, you'll just drift. Build the scout identity first; let the old identities lighten gradually as the new one takes hold.

**Don't: Demand your community change for you.**
*Why:* If you're in a tribalized community and you start scout-posting, expect friction. You may be able to shift some members; you may not. Rather than fighting the community, find (or start) additional ones where scout behavior is rewarded. Supplement, don't convert.

**Don't: Confuse scout identity with contrarian identity.**
*Why:* The contrarian is just a soldier whose tribe is "people who disagree with the consensus." They're defending a flag, same as anyone else. Scouts go where the evidence goes — which sometimes matches consensus and sometimes doesn't. If you find yourself predictably opposing the mainstream on every topic, you've built a contrarian identity, not a scout one.

**Don't: Grade scout-ness by how often you update.**
*Why:* Both under-updating (sticky beliefs) and over-updating (whipsawing on every new piece of evidence) are failure modes. The metric isn't *how often do I change my mind*; it's *do my confidence levels track reality as more information arrives*. Sometimes reality says hold; sometimes it says move. Scout identity rewards accurate tracking, not motion for its own sake.

**Don't: Expect the AIDS-activist or Humane-League version of this to arrive by accident.**
*Why:* The citizen scientists during the AIDS crisis pulled off something rare — full activist passion combined with ruthless scout-mindset about which tactics were actually working. They had to consciously decide to give up some ideological purity, work inside the NIH they'd previously treated as the enemy, and let study results override their hopes and dreams. The result: mortality from AIDS dropped 60% in two years. This is the shining example, and it was deliberate — they talked about it out loud, disagreed about it within the group, paid real social costs. If you want that combination in your work, you have to choose it explicitly. It won't emerge from good intentions.
