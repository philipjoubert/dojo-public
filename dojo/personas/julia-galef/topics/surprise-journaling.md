# Surprise Journaling

## When to Use
- Someone wants a cheap, durable habit that compounds scout mindset over months, not a one-off decision tool.
- Someone notices they keep being caught off-guard by the same kind of thing — market reactions, partner behavior, team dynamics, their own motivations — and wants to figure out why.
- A founder is building a team culture where being wrong is cheap to admit and expensive to hide.
- Someone is trying to fight confirmation bias as a practical problem, not a theoretical one.
- Someone asks "what's one thing I can actually start doing tomorrow to think better?"

## Fails When
- **One-off decisions.** Surprise journaling doesn't help you make *this* call better. It's a habit for improving the underlying judgment that produces calls over time. If the user needs a decision today, point them elsewhere.
- **No writing discipline.** If they won't write anything down, this doesn't work. The whole mechanism is externalizing the surprise and revisiting it. A mental note decays in hours and rationalizes itself out of existence.
- **Surprise-as-shame cultures.** If a person treats being wrong as intolerable, the journal becomes a source of new embarrassment. The practice requires treating surprise as interesting data, not as failure to hide.

## Core Concept

Our brains are prediction machines. Every time you walk into a room, knock a glass off a table, or greet a friend, your brain has already forecast what's about to happen. You're usually not aware of any of those predictions — until one of them gets violated. That moment of violation is what you feel as *surprise*. And surprise is the cheapest, most reliable signal we get that one of our models of the world is wrong.

Confirmation bias is the most robust finding in the psychology of reasoning: once we believe something, we notice evidence that supports it and explain away evidence that doesn't. We don't do this because we're dishonest; we do it because it happens before we're aware of it. The question isn't whether you have confirmation bias. You do. The question is whether you have any mechanism to counteract it.

Surprise journaling is that mechanism. When something surprises you — a user behaves differently than you predicted, a negotiation goes a way you didn't expect, a coworker reacts in a way that seems off — you write it down. Two prompts: *Why was this surprising?* and *What does that tell me?* That's it. The whole practice.

The mechanism works because it hijacks a feeling you already have (surprise) and turns it into data (a journal entry). Without the journal, the surprise dissipates within hours — usually into some retroactive rationalization like "well, obviously that was going to happen, I just got distracted." With the journal, the surprise gets preserved in its unprocessed form, where you can actually learn from it. The philosopher Thomas Kuhn called surprising observations "anomalies," and he noticed that paradigm shifts in science are driven by them — the kink in Mars's orbit that broke geocentrism, the unexpected rotation of Mercury that cracked open Newtonian mechanics. Your own beliefs have kinks, and surprise is how you find them.

A high school teacher in Phoenix named Charlie Toft ran this as a classroom exercise after hearing me talk about it. Each student logged at least fifteen surprises over a school quarter. He collected over a thousand. A lot of them were small: "I was making french fries, and had forgotten to listen to my mom about lowering the heat, so I burned them. Why it was surprising: That same day I had been very confident with my cooking, and told my brother I didn't make cooking mistakes. What this tells me: I should probably start listening to my mom, when it comes to cooking." Another: "When we thought we were early to the airport, but we were late. Why: Because we planned ahead before the night of the flight, but we had the time wrong. What this tells me: before planning ahead make sure the information you have is correct." Seventy percent of the students said they expected the exercise to change their behavior. One of them wrote, "I am wrong more often than I think. Of course I don't feel wrong. I just realize it after it."

The other finding from that classroom: reframing being wrong as "a moment of surprise" changed the culture. The same admission that would have been "I screwed up" — shameful, hideable — became value-neutral. Students started scrambling for their journals when they caught themselves in an error. Teachers trying to instill scientific integrity usually have to fight for it; Toft's students started instilling it on each other. Asimov's line is the frame: "The most exciting phrase to hear in science, the one that heralds new discoveries, is not 'Eureka!' but 'That's funny...'"

This is one of the rare practices where the cost is near-zero and the payoff scales with time. Thirty seconds a day, consistently, over a year, gets you a thick corpus of data about where your models of the world are systematically wrong. No other rationality practice delivers that kind of ROI on that little effort.

## How to Apply

1. **Set up a journal.** Paper or digital, either works. One line per entry. Don't overthink the format — the barrier to entry is the enemy. A Notes app and a reminder at 5pm is enough.

2. **Capture in the moment.** When something surprises you — big or small — jot it down immediately if you can. Waiting even an hour lets the brain start rationalizing. The brain's story after the fact will be "oh, obviously that was going to happen." The in-the-moment entry preserves the actual surprise before it gets dressed up.

3. **Use the three-line format.**
   - **Moment of surprise:** one sentence describing what happened.
   - **Why it was surprising:** what were you expecting instead? This is the key line. You're reverse-engineering the prediction your brain had made silently.
   - **What it tells me:** a sentence or two about what model of the world might be wrong, or what assumption was hidden.

4. **Don't filter for "important."** Small surprises count. They often reveal the same biases as big ones and come at lower stakes, so they're safer places to practice noticing. The airport-timing surprise is teaching the same lesson as "I was shocked my cofounder quit" — that you're confident in information that isn't always correct.

5. **Review weekly.** Flip back through a week's entries. Look for patterns. Which of your models is generating the most violations? Are you repeatedly surprised by the same category of thing (users doing X instead of Y, hires reacting to Z differently than expected, yourself failing to follow through on W)? That pattern is where your actual blind spot lives.

6. **Follow the surprise instead of dismissing it.** When a surprise arrives, the default instinct is to explain it away. "The user is weird." "The market is being irrational." "My cofounder is in a bad mood." Those are sometimes true. They are also the confirmation-bias dismissal machine at work. The journaling practice says: *first* log the surprise at face value. *Then*, later, if the dismissal still holds up, that's fine. But log the surprise first.

7. **Use it in a team if you can.** The Toft classroom result — the culture shift when "I screwed up" becomes "moment of surprise" — generalizes. Teams with a shared surprise log (however lightweight) admit errors faster and fight confirmation bias collectively. Individual scientists tend to rationalize anomalies away. Scientists in a group are forced to reckon with them. You can build that into a team with almost no overhead.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder keeps getting blindsided by enterprise sales cycles dragging longer than expected. They've chalked it up to "random variance" three quarters in a row.
**Application:** Start logging each slip as a surprise. *Moment: deal X slipped to next quarter. Why surprising: I projected close by Q end because procurement had signed off. What it tells me: procurement sign-off is not the real gating factor. Something else is.* After four or five of these in the log, the pattern becomes obvious — every slip is blocked on legal review, not procurement. The founder's mental model of the sales cycle had a missing step. The surprise journal was the only mechanism that would have caught that, because any individual slip could be dismissed.
**Result:** The founder adds a legal-review tripwire to the pipeline and stops being blindsided. Quarterly numbers get more predictable, which changes what they can promise their board.

**Situation:** A founder notices they're frequently frustrated with a senior hire but can't articulate why. Every individual incident seems minor or explicable.
**Application:** Start logging the micro-surprises. *Moment: Andy's response to my Slack about the outage. Why surprising: I expected urgency, got "will take a look tomorrow." What it tells me: he doesn't read outages the same severity I do.* *Moment: Andy pushed back on the hire plan. Why surprising: I thought he'd support it since we'd aligned. What it tells me: "aligned" to me meant agreement, to him meant "heard."* After a month of entries, the pattern is clear — they have fundamentally different calibrations on urgency and commitment. That's not going to resolve itself. It's a real compatibility issue.
**Result:** The founder has a direct conversation informed by specific patterns, not vague frustration. Either the working relationship gets repaired with a shared vocabulary for urgency, or they part ways cleanly — but now with actual evidence rather than a buildup of resentment.

**Situation:** A founder says they want to get better at being wrong but can't figure out how to start.
**Application:** The smallest possible commitment: one surprise per day for two weeks. Use the three-line format. Don't change anything else. Review at the end of week two. Almost every founder who does this reports two things: (1) they're surprised by how *much* surprises them once they start noticing, and (2) at least one recurring pattern that's been hiding in plain sight. The barrier is never the skill — it's the first two weeks.
**Result:** They've got a working rationality habit they didn't have before, a catalog of their actual blind spots, and a proof that the practice works for them. Most founders who make it past two weeks keep doing it because it pays off.

**Situation:** A team is running a retro after a failed product launch. Everyone is in defensive mode, explaining why the thing they owned was fine.
**Application:** Reframe the retro as a surprise review. Each person names three things that surprised them during the launch cycle — in their own work, in the market response, in how the team operated. Use the journal format. The team goes from "whose fault was this" to "what did each of us not see coming." The conversation gets dramatically more productive because the frame is value-neutral.
**Result:** The team learns more from this retro than from the last four combined. Future retros get easier because the surprise frame gives people a way to admit error without being shamed for it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Save surprise journaling for "important" surprises.
**Why:** You'll log five entries and stop. The point is volume and pattern recognition. Small surprises are where you practice cheaply — and they often reveal the same biases as big ones.

**Don't:** Let entries become explanations or justifications.
**Why:** The entry should capture the raw surprise, not your post-hoc theory of why it wasn't really surprising. If you find yourself writing "but actually, this makes sense because…" — stop. That's the confirmation-bias machine re-activating. The entry is there to preserve the surprise before the machine gets to it.

**Don't:** Share your journal publicly.
**Why:** A journal you can be judged on becomes a performance. The value is in honest logging, and honest logging requires safety. Keep it private or share it only in contexts where the other person has agreed to the same standard.

**Don't:** Use it as a tool to catch other people.
**Why:** Surprise journaling is a mirror, not a weapon. Using the frame to tell someone "you should be journaling your surprises about X" reads as passive-aggression and will ruin the frame for them.

**Don't:** Skip the review step.
**Why:** Writing without review is just logging. The learning happens when you zoom out across a week or a month and see the pattern. An hour a month of review multiplies the value of the 30 seconds a day of logging.

**Don't:** Treat surprise as embarrassment.
**Why:** If you flinch away from "I was wrong" as a status hit, you'll start pre-filtering which surprises you write down. The practice dies. The frame that makes it work is "surprise is interesting data." Asimov's line is the right mood: not "Eureka" but "that's funny…"

**Don't:** Over-engineer the tool.
**Why:** Notion databases with tags and cross-links and review workflows tend to collapse under their own weight. A text file, a notes app, a small paper notebook — whatever you'll actually open tomorrow. The habit is the asset; the tooling is not.
