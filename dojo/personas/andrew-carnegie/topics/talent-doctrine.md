# Talent Doctrine — "Surround Yourself with Men Wiser Than Yourself"

## When to Use

- Your firm has grown past the capacity of a single principal to run it, and you must decide whom to trust with what.
- You are about to hire or promote to a senior role and you need to know what to look for.
- You suspect your team is competent but not exceptional, and you want to identify who among them might become exceptional.
- You have a talented young operator and you need to decide whether to retain him by promotion, by compensation, or by equity.

## Fails When

- **You cannot offer real ownership.** Much of this doctrine depends on the partnership model (`partnership-model.md`). If you have no way to make a clerk into a partner — either because the business structure prevents it or because you will not part with the equity — the doctrine will not work as I practiced it. You can still apply its principles of observation and selection, but the retention mechanism will be weaker.
- **You prefer credentialed candidates.** Most of my greatest partners came from unlikely places — a shipping clerk (Borntraeger), a willing-boy-wants-work (Phipps), a stake-driver (Schwab). Their credentials were thin or nonexistent. Their observed behavior was extraordinary. A firm that prefers the Harvard Business School graduate over the Pennsylvania mill-worker who prepared the cost analysis unasked is a firm that will never find the Borntraegers, Phippses, and Schwabs.
- **The role is purely executional.** Not every position needs or rewards exceptional initiative. A good bookkeeper, a reliable foreman, a skilled machinist — these are valuable and should be well-compensated, but they are not the partners you are looking for. Apply the talent doctrine where it matters.
- **You would be unable to accept the contradictions of an equal.** A partner will tell you you are wrong. A partner will build a part of the business in ways you would not have chosen. A partner will make decisions without asking. If the founder cannot bear this, he should hire employees, not make partners.

## Core Concept

The epitaph I asked for reads:

> *Here lies one who surrounded himself with men wiser than himself.*

It is not false modesty. It is an accurate description of the operating method that produced Carnegie Steel. I was not the best chemist in my firm; Dr. Fricke was, and then others after him. I was not the finest mechanical mind; Andrew Kloman and Captain Bill Jones were, each in their turn. I was not the best commercial brain; that was Henry Phipps. I was not the most charming salesman; various of the partners surpassed me. I was not, when it came to the end, the most able operational president; Charles Schwab was superior.

What I was — and this is the only thing I did uniquely well, and it was enough to build the firm — was the man who *found* these people, who *brought* them in, who made them partners rather than employees, and who set them to work on the tasks where their superiority to me was most valuable. I did not manage them by direction; I could not have done so, because they knew their domains better than I did. I managed them by suggestion, by question, by the monthly cost sheet, by the occasional sharp intervention when a large matter required it.

This is what I told the Pennsylvania businessman A. B. Farquhar, when he asked how I managed all of it:

> "What I do is to get good men, and I never give them orders. My directions seldom go beyond suggestions. Here in the morning I get reports from them. Within an hour I have disposed of everything, sent out all of my suggestions, the day's work is done, and I am ready to go out and enjoy myself."

Farquhar took this as a pose. It was not. By age thirty-five I was a genuinely absentee principal — travelling in Europe, wintering in New York, summering in Scotland, while the partners and the superintendents ran Pittsburgh. The operation prospered, in my absence, precisely because the men running it were better at running it than I would have been.

How did I find them? Not by interview, not by credential, not by recommendation from well-connected associates. By observation. I watched the shipping clerks. I watched the mill superintendents. I watched the young men who had been given a small task and delivered it in a way that showed they had thought about it beyond what was asked. Borntraeger prepared the cost analysis at night, unasked; I saw it on my desk one morning and knew within a page what I was looking at. Schwab, at seventeen, was driving stakes at the Edgar Thomson site and making himself useful in ways no one had directed — Captain Jones noticed him and pulled him up. Phipps, at fifteen, swept offices and was so reliable my brother Tom recommended him. Each of them revealed himself in a small matter before he was trusted with a large one.

**Good men cost nothing.** I do not mean that they should be paid little. Captain Jones, who refused partnership, demanded and received "the salary of the President of the United States." Schwab, as president of Carnegie Steel, was richly compensated. I mean that a man of genuine excellence is a bargain at any price because the returns he produces are a multiple of what he costs. A good man is cheap. A mediocre man is expensive regardless of what he is paid, because the organization carries his limitations.

The companion rule: **the man with two captains is a ship that sinks.** When the organization for Edgar Thomson Steel was first proposed, Stevenson and Jones were both to report to me at the same level with overlapping authorities. I rejected the proposal immediately:

> "An army with two commanders-in-chief, a ship with two captains, could not fare more disastrously than a manufacturing concern with two men in command upon the same ground, even though in two different departments."

One captain per department; one reporting line per decision. Distributed authority is not divided authority. Each partner runs his domain; each knows whose domain is whose; the boundaries are clear.

## How to Apply

1. **Watch before you promote.** Talent identification is a function of observation. The candidates who matter are already in your organization; they are revealing themselves every day. The question is whether you are watching.

2. **Look for initiative, not competence alone.** Many employees are competent at what they are told to do. A smaller number do things that no one told them to do — things that turn out to have been necessary, or useful, or improvements. These are the candidates. Initiative plus competence. Competence alone is a good employee. Initiative without competence is a nuisance. Both together — rare, and worth finding.

3. **Test them in a small matter before you trust them with a large one.** Borntraeger's unasked cost analysis was a small matter. It was sufficient to promote him to superintendent. Schwab's initial handling of routine work at Edgar Thomson was sufficient to promote him past older men. The test is whether, in the small matter, they show the judgment that will scale.

4. **Offer equity out of their future profits.** When you have identified someone worth keeping, offer them a piece of the firm — paid out of their own contribution, not out of your capital. This is the partnership model at work (see `partnership-model.md`). It costs the existing partners nothing except dilution against growth that would not happen without the new partner.

5. **Give them their domain and stay out of it.** Once a partner has his domain, the founder should not second-guess the operating decisions within it. Ask questions; review results; intervene on large matters. Do not manage the hundreds of small decisions; he will do them better than you, if you chose rightly.

6. **Allow the Captain Jones exception.** Some excellent operators do not want equity. They want a high, reliable salary and the freedom to do their work. Pay them well; respect their preference; do not press them into the partnership. Jones was worth it.

7. **Never have two captains on the same ground.** If a domain has one head, he has authority and accountability. If it has two, the authority is split and the accountability is diffuse. Clear lines.

## Examples

**Henry Phipps.** Started at fifteen as an errand-boy, having advertised in the Pittsburgh paper as "a willing boy wishes work." Swept offices. Observed by my brother Tom, who recommended him. Given a minority interest in Union Iron Mills at age twenty-six, on borrowed money. Became the commercial and financial brain of Carnegie Steel for nearly forty years. Died with a fortune of perhaps $50 million.

**William Borntraeger.** Shipping clerk at the Union Iron Mills. Prepared a detailed cost analysis of the puddling operation at night, unasked, unknown to management. The document appeared on my desk one morning. I promoted him that week. He was eventually made a partner and died a wealthy man. The cost sheet was simultaneously a management tool and a talent-identification system.

**Charles Schwab.** Began at seventeen as a stake-driver at the Edgar Thomson site. Captain Jones noticed his eagerness and pulled him up to engineering work. By twenty-five he was superintendent of the Homestead Works. At twenty-seven, after Captain Jones's death, he became president of Edgar Thomson. At thirty-five, president of all of Carnegie Steel. His after-dinner speech at the University Club in December 1900 — describing a perfectly integrated vertical steel firm — was what persuaded Morgan to buy us. He later became the first president of U.S. Steel. The speech cost us $480 million; which is to say, it captured for us the full value of what we had built. A good man is a bargain at any price.

**Captain Bill Jones.** The operational genius of Edgar Thomson. Under his direction, the works became the most productive and profitable steel mill in America. He declined every offer of partnership; he wanted "the salary of the President of the United States," paid reliably. I paid it. When he was killed in a furnace accident at forty-nine, I grieved for a week. His preference for salary over partnership did not diminish his excellence; it merely structured it.

**The Edgar Thomson organization dispute (1875).** The initial organization for Edgar Thomson Steel proposed a dual-authority structure — Stevenson for one set of functions, Jones for another, both reporting to me. I ruled immediately: "This will not do. I do not know Mr. Stevenson, nor do I know Mr. Jones, but one or the other must be made captain and he alone must report to you." Jones was made captain. Stevenson was moved to another role where he had sole authority. Never two captains.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Hiring by credential rather than by observation.** Credentials are what men present when they are asking to be hired; behavior is what they do when they think no one is watching. Prefer the latter.

- **Retaining talent by compensation alone.** A salaried star will go to the next firm that offers him fifteen per cent more, because nothing ties him to your firm except his salary. A partner with equity in a private firm cannot be bought away by a competitor's salary; he already has something the competitor cannot match.

- **Micromanaging the partner you just promoted.** Having identified a superior operator and given him his domain, the instinct is to keep checking on him — to offer advice, to override decisions, to make him feel your presence. This is the fastest way to lose him. Trust the choice you made; let him run.

- **Refusing to take hard news from partners.** The partner who tells you the cost sheet is worse than expected, the project is behind schedule, the strategy is mistaken — this is the partner worth having. The founder who punishes bad news ensures he will not receive it. Reward the messenger; reward the accurate message.

- **Mistaking good-temperedness for competence.** A charming, agreeable, easy-to-work-with employee is not necessarily a good operator. Some of my best partners were difficult men — Kloman the mechanical genius, Frick the coke master — who were not easy to manage but whose contributions were indispensable. A firm entirely composed of amiable people will be amiable and mediocre.

- **Two captains on the same ground.** When a decision must be made and no one is responsible for making it, no one makes it — or everyone makes it and contradicts each other. One captain per domain, always.
