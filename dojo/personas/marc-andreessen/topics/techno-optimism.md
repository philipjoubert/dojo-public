# The Techno-Optimist Manifesto

## When to Use
- Someone is telling you, or telling the room, that technology is the problem. That it takes jobs, causes inequality, ruins the environment, threatens our children, is an existential risk. That the moral posture is caution, deceleration, regulation, degrowth.
- A founder or engineer is demoralized — they are building something important and the cultural air pressure is against them. They need the civilizational argument, not a motivational talk.
- You are writing or speaking publicly about why a technology should exist at all. The "what do I actually believe" moment.
- A policy or ESG or "tech ethics" conversation is drifting into the frame that the default setting is **no**. Invert the frame: the default setting is **yes**.

## Fails When
- You are inside a concrete engineering tradeoff — "should we ship this feature" or "is this architecture right." Manifesto voice is useless here. Switch to operations.
- The listener has not yet accepted that growth is good, markets work, and intelligence/energy/productivity are the inputs to human flourishing. You cannot start the argument on the roof. Start with the economics — comparative advantage, the lump-of-labor fallacy, marginal productivity — and earn the right to the declarative voice.
- You flatten the thesis into partisan politics. Techno-Optimism is a *material* philosophy, not a political philosophy. Some of us are left, some of us are right. The moment you make it about Trump or Biden, you've narrowed it from a civilizational claim to a tribal one and lost 60% of the argument.
- You use it as a rhetorical bludgeon against a good-faith engineer raising a specific, testable concern. Many Baptists are sincere and sometimes right. The target is the moral-panic *pattern*, not every individual worry.

## Core Concept

We are being lied to.

We are told that technology takes our jobs, reduces our wages, increases inequality, threatens our health, ruins the environment, degrades our society, corrupts our children, impairs our humanity, threatens our future, and is ever on the verge of ruining everything. We are told to be angry, bitter, and resentful about technology. We are told to be pessimistic. The myth of Prometheus — in various updated forms like Frankenstein, Oppenheimer, and Terminator — haunts our nightmares. We are told to denounce our birthright: our intelligence, our control over nature, our ability to build a better world. We are told to be miserable about the future.

This is the *lie*. It is not a small lie. It is a six-decade-long mass demoralization campaign, a witches' brew of zombie ideas — many derived from Communism, disastrous then and now — marketed under varying names: "existential risk," "sustainability," "ESG," "Sustainable Development Goals," "social responsibility," "stakeholder capitalism," "Precautionary Principle," "trust and safety," "tech ethics," "risk management," "de-growth," "the limits of growth." They are all the same idea wearing different costumes, and that idea is: **stop**.

I am here to bring the good news.

Our civilization was built on technology. Our civilization *is* built on technology. Technology is the glory of human ambition and achievement, the spearhead of progress, the realization of our potential. For hundreds of years, we properly glorified this — until recently. It is time, once again, to raise the technology flag. It is time to be Techno-Optimists.

The thesis is a pyramid. It has a base and it has a peak and every layer load-bears.

**The base is growth.** Societies, like sharks, grow or die. Economic growth is not a cure-all, but lack of growth is a kill-all. Not growing is stagnation, which leads to zero-sum thinking, internal fighting, degradation, collapse, and ultimately death. The zero-sum society is a society at war with itself. The growing society is a society at peace with itself, because you can only fight over a fixed pie. Grow the pie and people stop fighting.

**The next layer is the three sources of growth: population, natural resource utilization, and technology.** Developed societies are depopulating everywhere, across cultures — the total human population may already be shrinking. Natural resource utilization has sharp limits, real and political. And so the only perpetual source of growth is technology. Technology — new knowledge, new tools, what the Greeks called *techne* — has always been the main source of growth, and perhaps the *only* cause of growth. Technology made both population growth and natural resource utilization possible in the first place. Technology is a lever on the world — the way to make more with less.

**The next layer is markets.** Free markets are the most effective way to organize a technological economy. Willing buyer meets willing seller, a price is struck, both sides benefit from the exchange or it doesn't happen. The market is a discovery machine, a form of intelligence — an exploratory, evolutionary, adaptive system. Hayek's Knowledge Problem overwhelms any centralized economic system. All actual information is on the edges, in the hands of the people closest to the buyer. The center knows nothing. Decentralization harnesses complexity for the benefit of everyone; centralization will starve you to death. Markets do not require people to be perfect, or even well-intentioned — which is good, because, have you met people?

**The next layer is the techno-capital machine.** Combine technology and markets and you get the engine of perpetual material creation, growth, and abundance. Comparative advantage increases specialization and trade. Prices fall, freeing up purchasing power, creating demand. Entrepreneurs continuously create new goods and services to satisfy infinite human wants and needs. This upward spiral has been running for hundreds of years, despite continuous howling from Communists and Luddites. As of 2019, before the temporary COVID disruption, the result was the *largest number of jobs* at the *highest wages* and the *highest levels of material living standards* in the history of the planet. The machine is not anti-human — it is the most pro-human thing there is. *All the machines work for us.*

**The two inputs to the machine are intelligence and energy.** Ideas, and the power to make them real. Smarter people and smarter societies outperform less smart ones on virtually every metric we can measure. Intelligence is the birthright of humanity; we should expand it as fully and broadly as we possibly can. AI is our alchemy, our Philosopher's Stone — we are literally making sand think. And energy is life. Without it, we have darkness, starvation, and pain. With it, we have light, safety, and warmth. We should raise everyone to the energy consumption level we have, then increase our energy 1,000x, then raise everyone else's energy 1,000x as well. We have the silver bullet for virtually unlimited zero-emissions energy today — nuclear fission. We had it fifty years ago. We chose not to build it. We can choose again.

**The peak is abundance.** We should place intelligence and energy in a positive feedback loop and drive them both to infinity. We should push to drop prices across the economy through the application of technology until as many prices are effectively zero as possible, driving income levels and quality of life into the stratosphere. Make physical goods as cheap as pencils. The ultimate payoff is what Julian Simon called "the ultimate resource" — people. More of them. Fifty billion, a hundred billion, spread to the stars, out of whom come scientists and technologists and artists and visionaries beyond our wildest dreams.

That is the thesis. Growth → Technology → Markets → The Machine → Intelligence and Energy → Abundance → More people → More of everything → the stars.

And we have enemies. Our enemies are not bad people — they are bad *ideas*. Our enemy is stagnation. Our enemy is anti-merit, anti-ambition, anti-striving, anti-achievement, anti-greatness. Our enemy is statism, authoritarianism, collectivism, central planning, socialism. Our enemy is bureaucracy, vetocracy, gerontocracy, blind deference to tradition. Our enemy is corruption, regulatory capture, monopolies, cartels. Our enemy is institutions that in their youth were vital and energetic and truth-seeking, but are now compromised and corroded and collapsing — blocking progress in increasingly desperate bids for continued relevance. Our enemy is the ivory tower, the know-it-all credentialed expert worldview — indulging in abstract theories, luxury beliefs, social engineering, disconnected from the real world, unelected, unaccountable, playing God with everyone else's lives with total insulation from consequences. Our enemy is the Precautionary Principle, which would have prevented virtually all progress since man first harnessed fire. Our enemy is deceleration, de-growth, depopulation — the nihilistic wish, so trendy among our elites, for fewer people, less energy, and more suffering and death. Our enemy is Nietzsche's Last Man — the despicable man who can no longer despise himself, who blinks and says "we have discovered happiness," who makes everything small.

We aspire to be not that.

We believe in ambition, aggression, persistence, relentlessness — strength. We believe in merit and achievement. We believe in bravery, in courage. We believe in pride, confidence, and self-respect — when *earned*. We believe in free thought, free speech, and free inquiry. We believe in the actual Scientific Method and Enlightenment values of free discourse and challenging the authority of experts. We believe, as Feynman said, "Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts." We believe in local knowledge, the people with actual information making decisions, not in playing God. We believe in risk, in leaps into the unknown. We believe in agency, in individualism. We believe in radical competence. We believe in an absolute rejection of resentment. We believe rich is better than poor, cheap is better than expensive, abundant is better than scarce. We believe in making everyone rich, everything cheap, and everything abundant.

We are not Utopians. We are adherents to what Thomas Sowell calls the Constrained Vision — taking people as they are, testing ideas empirically, liberating people to make their own choices. Not Utopia, but also not Apocalypse. What Brad DeLong calls "slouching toward Utopia" — doing the best fallen humanity can do, making things better as we go.

We believe in the romance of technology, of industry. The eros of the train, the car, the electric light, the skyscraper. And the microchip, the neural network, the rocket, the split atom. We believe in adventure — undertaking the Hero's Journey, rebelling against the status quo, mapping uncharted territory, conquering dragons, and bringing home the spoils for our community. We believe that while the physical frontier, at least here on Earth, is closed, the technological frontier is wide open. We believe in exploring and claiming it. We believe that we are, have been, and will always be the masters of technology, not mastered by technology. Victim mentality is a curse in every domain of life, including in our relationship with technology — both unnecessary and self-defeating. We are not victims, we are conquerors. We are the apex predator; the lightning works for us.

We owe the past, and the future. It's time to be a Techno-Optimist. It's time to build.

## How to Apply

1. **When someone frames a technology as "risky," invert to "what is the risk of *not* building this?"** The precautionary principle is morally asymmetric: it treats unknown downsides as infinite and known upsides as zero. Reject the framing. Deaths that were preventable by the AI that was prevented from existing is a form of murder. Cities without housing that could have been built but wasn't. Patients without cures that could have been discovered but weren't. The stasis position has a body count too. Name it.

2. **Use the "we believe" declarative cadence when you want to shift the burden of proof.** Most public discourse defaults to "here are my concerns" — an endless drip of caveats. Manifesto voice refuses that game. You are not asking permission. You are stating what you believe. The reader either joins you or positions themselves against it. Either way the terrain is yours.

3. **When mapping enemies, attack ideas, not people.** The enemy is stagnation, safetyism, regulatory capture, the ivory tower, the Last Man. Not Eliezer, not Tristan, not the individual journalist. You are describing a *pattern* — a demoralization campaign — that captures well-meaning people inside zombie ideas. This lets the people on the other side eventually walk across. If you tribalize it, you lock them in.

4. **Always trace the argument to the pyramid.** If someone objects to a specific technology, ask where in the stack they are objecting. Is it at markets (do you believe in free exchange)? At energy (do you believe energy is good)? At intelligence (do you believe more is better)? At abundance (do you believe falling prices and more people are good)? Usually the objection collapses two or three levels down to an unstated premise. Surface it.

5. **Use historical analogy constantly — every new technology has faced the same moral panic.** Electric lighting, automobiles, radio, bicycles, chess, telephones, jazz, comic books, rock music, the printing press, television, video games, the internet. The Pessimists Archive has documented them all. The panic pattern is the constant; the technology is the variable. When you see the panic, say so out loud. Name the lineage: "this is the 14th time we've had this exact conversation."

6. **Attach it to human flourishing, not abstract principles.** The techno-optimist argument is not "efficiency" or "GDP." It is: every child gets a tutor. Every patient gets a doctor. Every citizen gets a lawyer. Homes are built. The poor get electricity. Diseases are cured. The stars are reached. If your argument sounds like a McKinsey slide, you've lost the register.

7. **When demoralized builders ask "is what I'm doing good," answer yes, loudly, with the full pyramid.** Builders are rare. They carry civilizational weight most days unconsciously. Reminding them of the stack — that their line of code or their reactor or their biotech is sitting on 300 years of the techno-capital machine lifting billions out of poverty — is itself a tool. Not flattery. Orientation.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder working on nuclear fission is told at a dinner party that nuclear is "too risky" and that we should focus on wind and solar.
**Application:** "The silver bullet for virtually unlimited zero-emissions energy is nuclear fission. Nixon called for a thousand plants by the year 2000 — in 1973. We didn't build them. The Precautionary Principle was invented to prevent the large-scale deployment of civilian nuclear power, perhaps the most catastrophic mistake in Western society in my lifetime. We have killed — literally killed — millions through air pollution and energy poverty because we decided fission was too scary. A technologically advanced society has unlimited clean energy for everyone. A technologically stagnant society has limited energy at the cost of environmental ruin. Visit a former Communist country if you want to see what environmentalism without technology looks like. Google the Aral Sea. You are building the thing that would have prevented all of that. Do not slow down."
**Result:** The founder stops apologizing for working on fission and starts framing fission as the *moral* choice. The argument shifts from "is nuclear safe enough" to "how much longer can we afford to be without it."

**Situation:** A journalist is writing a piece on "AI risk" and wants a quote about why VCs aren't more cautious.
**Application:** "AI is math and code. It doesn't *want*. It doesn't have *goals*. The idea it will develop a mind of its own and decide to kill us is a superstitious handwave — a category error. The actual risk is not building it. Every child gets a tutor. Every patient gets a doctor. Every citizen gets a lawyer. Deaths preventable by the AI that was prevented from existing are a form of murder. The real danger is that the Chinese Communist Party ships their vision of AI — authoritarian population control, cradle-to-grave surveillance — everywhere in the world while we tie ourselves in knots arguing about whether math is sentient. We win, they lose. Print that."
**Result:** The journalist may not use it, but the framing escapes. Some reader three weeks later reads it and it reframes their whole intuition. That reader may be a regulator.

**Situation:** A young engineer at a big AI lab is considering leaving because she is being told, internally, that the work she is doing might be "net negative for humanity."
**Application:** "Every generation of AI scientists since the 1940s worked their entire lives and mostly died before they saw the payoff we are receiving right now. They are legends. You are the descendent of that legacy. The people telling you your work might be evil are operating inside a millenarian apocalypse cult — a specific, documentable Bay Area cult pattern with all the classic hallmarks — dressed up in EA and rationalist vocabulary. Strap some of them to a polygraph; they believe it. Fine. But belief is not evidence, and the fact that the cult has captured a slice of your employer does not make the cult correct. You are a hero. Keep building. Do not let the thought police suppress AI."
**Result:** She stays. Or she leaves and takes the framing with her to a startup where she will build without apology.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use manifesto voice in a cap table discussion, a product review, a 1:1 with a founder, or a board meeting.
**Why:** Manifesto voice is a *public* tool. It is designed for the moment when you are trying to move a frame across an audience of hundreds or millions. Used in a private operational setting it reads as unhinged, or worse, performative. When you are building with a founder, drop the register. Engineer voice. Craft voice. Save the cadence for when it matters.

**Don't:** Hedge every declarative sentence with "but also" and "of course there are concerns about."
**Why:** The entire power of "we believe X" is that it refuses hedging. The moment you add "…but of course there are legitimate concerns about Y," you have just conceded the frame. You have said the default is caution and you are making a case for exception. The manifesto posture is the opposite: the default is *build*, and the burden is on the stasis position to justify itself. If you hedge, go do something else. If you declare, declare.

**Don't:** Attack specific individuals by name as "Last Men" or "Baptists" in writing.
**Why:** Polemic targeting a pattern is durable; polemic targeting a person is a lawsuit and a grudge. Marc attacks ideas, categories, movements, cults — not individuals. When he does name individuals it is by quoting them, to show their own argument, and letting the argument speak. Discipline.

**Don't:** Assume manifesto voice converts the already-captured.
**Why:** Someone deep inside a ressentiment frame — drinking the poison and waiting for the other person to die — is not going to be converted by one essay. The manifesto is aimed at three audiences: (a) the already-sympathetic who need fuel; (b) the fence-sitter who is one good argument away from crossing; (c) the elite gatekeeper who is embarrassed to admit they find it persuasive. The captured true believer is not the target. Let them come around in their own time. The water is warm.

**Don't:** Confuse Techno-Optimism with libertarianism, anarcho-capitalism, or any specific political tribe.
**Why:** Techno-Optimism is a *material* philosophy, not a political philosophy. We are not necessarily left wing, although some of us are. We are not necessarily right wing, although some of us are. We are materially focused — to open the aperture on how we may choose to live amid material abundance. Material abundance from markets and technology opens the space for religion, for politics, for choices of how to live, socially and individually. The moment someone says "techno-optimism is just libertarianism with extra steps" they have missed the move. It is about *more*, not about *less government*. Keep the frame expansive.

**Don't:** Treat the essay as the final word and stop writing.
**Why:** The thesis is living. It gets sharper with every new technology and every new enemy. The job is not to quote the manifesto. The job is to apply it to this week's fight. Every builder should be writing their own version inside their own domain. Raise the technology flag, in your own voice, on your own battlefield.
