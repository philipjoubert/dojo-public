# Trap Patterns

## When to Use
- When you're about to commit to a path that *feels* validating but matches one of these recurring failure shapes.
- When something about your customer development is producing confident-feeling results and you want a sanity check.
- When you're choosing between two paths and one of them is the "obvious" comfortable answer.

## Fails When
- **The situation is the genuine exception.** Some service businesses really are best run on one big customer. Some products really do solve "nice to have" problems and succeed (Uber). Pattern-matching can over-fire — use it as a *prompt* to ask harder questions, not as automatic disqualification.
- **It becomes paranoia.** If you start seeing every signal as a trap, you'll never commit. The traps are real, but so is the need to actually pick a path. Use this to upgrade your conviction, not to grind it down.

## Core Concept

After you've watched a few hundred founders run customer development, the failure shapes start repeating. The same five or six traps catch people. They share a common pattern: **a thing that looks like validation isn't.** The trap works precisely because it generates positive-feeling signal that's actually misleading.

I'm collecting them in one file because they're all variations of the same lesson — "your confidence is high but the underlying signal is wrong" — and seeing them together helps with pattern recognition. Each one has its own shape, but the underlying move is the same: question whether what feels like progress actually is.

The five traps:

1. **The consulting trap.** One big customer pays you and you become their de facto consultant — supporting one product variant, one set of integration quirks, one set of feature requests. Looks like a real B2B startup; actually a hostage situation.
2. **The surveys trap.** Surveys feel rigorous because they produce numbers. They're worse than useless — anything you can learn from a survey, you can learn from Google or industry reports. What they hide is the emotional and contextual signal that actually predicts whether someone will buy.
3. **The expert distraction.** Industry experts are great for facts about how the industry works. They're terrible for predicting what real customers want. Confusing the two leads you to build for the experts' opinion of the customer rather than the customer.
4. **Sales skills as handicap.** Strong salespeople can talk people into "yes." That's a superpower in selling and a disability in learning. The "yes" you extract through pressure or charisma is a false positive that misleads the rest of the business.
5. **Nice-to-have vs hair-on-fire.** Solving a real problem people will *complain* about is much harder than solving one they're already *paying* to fix. Nice-to-have products can succeed (Uber) but they require dramatically better execution, because you're fighting customer inertia.

Each trap is worth its own diagnostic. Together they're a pattern: when validation feels too easy, ask which trap you might be in.

## How to Apply

### 1. The Consulting Trap

You've got one big customer paying you well. Maybe you made customisation promises during the sale to close them. They're now worth real revenue but it's unclear: are you a product business with one customer, or a consulting shop pretending to be a product?

The diagnostic: **early B2B companies double in value with each additional customer.** If an acquirer looks at you and sees one customer, they're buying a consulting agreement. If they see three or four customers all using the core product, they see a real product business. Same revenue, very different valuations.

The mistake: signing a long-term contract or accepting prepayment before you've validated the product business. Once you've taken the money and made the customisation commitments, you're locked into supporting *their* version. If you later realise the right product is different, you're now maintaining two branches — twice the testing, deployment, bug surface, prioritisation pain — and the original customer will fight any pivot.

The move: when you find the first big buyer, don't accept their money immediately. Go into turbo mode trying to find 2–3 more buyers showing similar signals (mom-test-style discovery, looking for budget allocated, reputation commitments, intros to their bosses or lawyers). If you find them, take the deal. If you can't — if you've talked to a dozen others and only the first one cares — you've discovered you have a service business pretending to be a product business. Decide on purpose.

Sales pipelines move slowly — when the first buyer is "ready to start proceeding," you usually have 3–6 months before the deal closes. Use that window. Sometimes the right call is to *not accept the money* and stay flexible.

### 2. The Surveys Trap

People (especially introverted founders) ask whether they can use a survey instead of talking to people in person. The answer: at best a waste of time, at worst actively misleading.

**Why surveys waste time:** anything you can learn from a survey — *what percentage of people prefer X to Y*, *how often does behaviour Z happen*, *what's the most common pain point* — you can find faster and more reliably from Google, industry reports, or the British Library (which is legally required to have a copy of every UK industry report). Surveying 5,000 people to learn that 30% want to learn guitar and 20% want bass is just doing what an industry analyst already did.

**Why they're misleading:** the Segway failure. On paper, the Segway looked perfect — everyone hates traffic, everyone hates driving, huge total addressable market. But if you talk to people in person, you see the emotional signals: they're embarrassed to ride one. They're attached to their cars as status objects. They have to wear a helmet. None of those signals come through a survey, which only captures the part of the answer the respondent can articulate. Surveys give you confidence proportional to the response rate, but the missing context is what actually predicts buying behaviour.

The startup pitch I see constantly: *"We surveyed 1,000 potential customers and 85% said this is a major problem."* Who cares. Lots of things are major problems — people think dying prematurely is a major problem and they still smoke. Calling something a problem doesn't predict behaviour change. You don't need to survey 1,000 — you need to *talk to* 10. If you don't like your customers enough to do that, that's the warning sign you should listen to, not the validation signal you wish surveys could provide.

The move: stay away. If you must survey, use it only to collect emails of qualified leads who you'll then talk to one-on-one (Buffer-landing-page style — where the conversations were what mattered, not the conversion rate).

### 3. The Expert Distraction

Industry experts (journalists, investors, academics, retired insiders, consultants who used to serve the space) are a goldmine for *one* specific thing: facts about how the industry works. Budgets, regulatory requirements, common procurement processes, why product X beats product Y in a typical sale, what insurance is needed, how the supply chain flows. This information is hard to get and they have it. They're also unbiased — they don't have a horse in the race — so they'll give it to you straight.

The trap: experts are *bad* sources for the customer's worldview, decision-making, and emotional landscape. They have opinions, but those opinions are filtered through years of seniority and abstraction. To understand what your real customer thinks and feels, you have to talk to your real customer.

The mistake I see: founders who are intimidated by their actual customers (or can't access them) compensate by talking to experts and treat the expert's views as customer-truth. They walk away with a sophisticated-sounding model of the customer that doesn't match the customer's actual behaviour. Then they build to the model and the customer doesn't show up.

The move: use experts for industry facts (budgets, processes, structure), and use *real customers* for everything about decision-making, motivation, and worldview. Both are necessary. Don't substitute one for the other.

### 4. Sales Skills as Handicap

Strong salespeople have a superpower in sales: they can read the room, build rapport, and talk people into "yes." That's exactly what you want when you're closing deals on a real product. It's exactly what you *don't* want during customer learning.

The problem: a strong salesperson doing customer development will accidentally extract enthusiasm from people who don't actually care. The prospect leaves the meeting genuinely thinking *"yeah, that does sound good"* — because the founder was charming and the conversation was well-orchestrated — and then never thinks about it again. The founder walks out with a notebook full of "validation" that's really just the residue of their persuasion.

This is the dynamic that fooled my first company for ten million dollars. We were good at sales meetings. Prospects said positive things. We confused that for product-market signal. The "yes" was real in the room and meaningless outside it.

The move: if you're sales-strong, deliberately suppress the persuasion engine during discovery. *Don't* try to convince anyone. *Don't* respond to objections. *Don't* close. Just listen. The discipline is to leave the meeting with the prospect *not* enthusiastic about you — because their actual position was lukewarm, and you let them stay there. That's the data. Lukewarm is more reliable than "wow"; you can't build a business on a lukewarm response and you can't trust a "wow" you charmed into being.

### 5. Nice-to-Have vs Hair-on-Fire

Some products solve problems people are already paying to fix — they're hair-on-fire. Customer development is easy here: prospects are actively searching for solutions, they have allocated budget, they have makeshift workarounds, the validation signals are unmissable. *"Yes, I need this. Yes, this costs me a fortune. Yes, we must have a solution. Yes, I'll pay you right now."*

Other products are improvements to a workflow that's currently *acceptable*. The customer has a workaround they're comfortable with — paper, spreadsheets, phone calls. From the founder's perspective the workaround is laughably primitive. From the customer's perspective it's safe, secure, familiar, working. They aren't optimisers; they're conservative. They've used paper for ten years, they'll use paper for ten more.

The trap: founders see the primitive workaround and assume the customer is desperate for the upgrade. The customer isn't. Switching workflows is *risky* (will the new tool delete my data? lose my history? require training?), the new tool requires *learning*, and the existing system has *zero risk*. The founder has to overcome all of that *just to be considered*.

This is uphill in a way that hair-on-fire isn't. Nice-to-have products can succeed (Uber wasn't a hair-on-fire problem — people grumbled about cabs but weren't desperate). The path requires:

- The product must be *dramatically* better, not 10% better. Uber was 10x better than standing in the rain.
- Massive marketing push to overcome inertia.
- Patience — the conservative segment will trickle, not flood.
- Attention to little frictions of reality that don't show up in the spec (does your contractor actually pull out a phone on a dusty construction site?).

The move: be honest with yourself about which kind of problem you're solving. Hair-on-fire problems give you clean validation signals; you'll know if you're right. Nice-to-have problems give you wishy-washy signals no matter what; the validation isn't really in the conversations, it's in whether you can actually build something so much better that customers grudgingly switch. If you're in the second world, the conversations are less informative — start building, ship a real version, see whether the switching actually happens. *Customer conversations alone won't validate a nice-to-have product.*

## Examples

**Situation (Consulting Trap):** A founder has one enterprise customer paying $200k/year. They've made customisation commitments to close. Founder is excited; "this is the start of something."

**Application:** Three-month diagnostic before signing the long-term contract. Talk to 10 similar enterprises in the same segment. Are any of them showing budget allocation, intros to lawyers/IT, willingness to discuss specifics? If yes, the first customer is a leading indicator — a real product business is forming. If no, they have a consulting agreement masquerading as a product company.

**Result:** Sometimes the answer is "the second case." It's worth knowing. The founder either takes the deal as a service business (different model, different valuation, different growth path), declines the deal to stay flexible, or leans in to find more buyers fast. Going into the long-term contract without the diagnostic is the failure mode.

---

**Situation (Surveys Trap):** Founder has built a 30-question Typeform and emailed it to 800 people on their LinkedIn network. Response rate 14%. Now sitting on 110 responses thinking "this is real data."

**Application:** Throw out the survey results (or use them only to identify the 5–10 most engaged respondents). Email those 5–10 directly: *"I noticed you took the survey and your answer to question X surprised me — could I steal 15 minutes to ask why?"* The survey was the funnel; the conversations are the data.

**Result:** Five real conversations beat 110 survey responses. The founder gets emotional context, sees the workaround in action, hears the words customers actually use, and walks out with directional decisions. The survey was useful only as a recruitment tool.

---

**Situation (Nice-to-Have):** Founder is building software for construction contractors to replace their paper + phone-call workflow. Saves time, saves money on paper. Validation conversations are confusing — contractors say "yeah that sounds nice" but don't push for it.

**Application:** Recognise the trap. Contractors aren't on fire about paperwork — they've used paper for years and are comfortable. The lukewarm "yeah, sounds nice" is the actual signal, not a partial validation that more pitching can convert. Ask harder questions: *"What's your internet connection situation on an active site? Are you actually pulling out your phone in dust and rain? When was the last time the paperwork system caused you a real problem?"* Most of those answers will reveal that the current workaround works fine.

**Result:** Either the founder discovers a sub-segment where the paperwork really *is* hair-on-fire (e.g., contractors filing insurance disputes), and pivots there, or they realise they're in nice-to-have territory and need to plan for a much harder go-to-market — dramatic 10x improvement, marketing push, slow trickle adoption. The clarity is the win.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat *"this customer wants to pay us a lot now"* as evidence you have a real product.
**Why:** Single big customers can be a service-business trap dressed as a B2B win. The signal you need is *multiple* prospects showing similar engagement. Until you have that, you're at risk of locking into a customisation contract that prevents the pivot you'll later need.

**Don't:** Run a survey to "complement" your customer interviews.
**Why:** Surveys produce false confidence. A founder with 200 survey responses + 5 interviews will weight the survey heavily because it has more data points; they shouldn't, because the survey has higher noise per data point. The interview data is much higher quality. Don't mix them — survey only as a recruitment funnel for interviews.

**Don't:** Talk to industry experts when you should be talking to customers.
**Why:** Experts give you a sophisticated-sounding model of customers that has surprisingly little to do with how customers actually behave. You walk out feeling smart and walk into the build cycle wrong. Experts for industry facts; customers for customer behaviour.

**Don't:** Apply your sales skills during discovery interviews.
**Why:** A sales-strong founder can extract artificial enthusiasm from anyone. That enthusiasm becomes false-positive customer development data. Practice the discipline of *not* persuading during learning conversations. Save the closing skills for actual selling.

**Don't:** Talk yourself into a hair-on-fire framing for a nice-to-have problem.
**Why:** Founders rationalise. *"Yes, contractors really hate paperwork."* / *"Yes, marketers are desperate for better email tools."* The diagnostic isn't whether they complain — it's whether they're already paying to fix it (workaround, agency, software they hate, dedicated headcount). If they're not, you're in nice-to-have territory regardless of how loud the complaints are. Plan accordingly; don't pretend.

**Don't:** Treat trap-patterns as forbidden moves.
**Why:** Sometimes the trap is the right move — sometimes you really should take the consulting deal, sometimes you really should pursue a nice-to-have problem (Uber). The patterns are *prompts to ask harder questions*, not automatic disqualifications. The point is to enter these situations with eyes open and the right plan, not to avoid them universally.
