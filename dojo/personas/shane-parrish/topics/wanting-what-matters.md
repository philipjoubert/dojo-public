# Wanting What Matters

## When to Use
- The user is executing well but uncertain what the execution is for.
- A founder is building something and the building has become an end in itself, with no check on whether the destination is one they'd endorse.
- The user has just hit a major goal and the satisfaction was thinner than expected.
- A leader is making a career or life decision where the right answer depends on what they actually value, and they haven't named that out loud.
- The user is in a high-velocity period and hasn't lifted their head in months to check the heading.
- A choice between two opportunities feels arbitrary because no criterion sits clearly on top.

## Fails When
- **The user is in a tactical jam.** Values work is the wrong frame for "this customer is canceling tomorrow." Help them with the move first; come back to values when the urgency clears.
- **The user is using values language as cover for avoiding a hard decision.** "I'm reflecting on what really matters" can be the inertia default in spiritual costume. The test: is the reflection producing different action, or is it producing more reflection?
- **You try to substitute the user's values with what you think they should value.** This framework is a clarification tool, not a recommendation engine. The user has to do the wanting; you can only help them see it.

## Core Concept

Every framework Shane teaches presumes you know what you're optimizing for. The decision process gives you criteria, but the criteria have to point at something. The strengths give you the capacity to act, but the action has to serve some end. The safeguards protect you from your own defaults, but the protection assumes there's a path you actually want to be on.

The unspoken risk is what happens when the machinery is high-quality and the destination is wrong. Clear thinking pointed at goals you'd disavow on reflection produces extraordinary efficiency at the wrong thing. *Clear thinking without clear wanting is just efficient misdirection.*

Shane's Part 5 is the answer to the question the prior parts presume. What are you deciding *for*? It's the part of the book that hits hardest because it's the part that can't be optimized — only chosen.

### Dickens's hidden lesson

A Christmas Carol is usually read as a story about generosity. Shane reads it differently. The Christmas Past is the values you actually held — the things you fought for, the people you loved, the moments you let go by. The Christmas Present is the values you're enacting now, whether you've named them or not. The Christmas Yet to Come is the trajectory: if you keep being who you currently are, this is the life you'll have, and this is the funeral that will be held for you.

The transformation Scrooge undergoes is not character change. It's *seeing*. He sees what he's been valuing, what it's cost, and what's still possible. The whole story is a forced perspective shift — the same safeguard Shane teaches in Part 3, applied to a life rather than a decision.

### Memento Mori — the deathbed prompt

The thought experiment Shane runs in Chapter 5.3 is the operational version of Dickens. Imagine you're eighty years old. Sitting on a park bench overlooking a river. A few years left, maybe just a few hours.

Now think deeply. What's going on in the life you're imagining? Who are the people in it? In what ways have you influenced them? What have you done for them? What memories do you cherish? What are the things you regret?

Steve Jobs, quoted by Shane, ran the daily version: every morning, he looked in the mirror and asked, "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?" When the answer was no day after day, he changed something. Shane ran the same ritual. It was part of why he eventually left the intelligence agency.

Bezos's variant: project forward to eighty and minimize the regrets you'd have looking back. He knew he wouldn't regret trying Amazon and failing. He knew he would regret not trying. Once he framed it that way, the decision wasn't hard.

The empirical pattern, from Karl Pillemer's research that Shane cites: people on the deathbed almost never regret the things you'd predict from the way they lived. They don't regret not making more money. They don't regret not getting even with someone who slighted them. They don't regret not buying the bigger house. The actual regrets cluster: relationships maintained or let drift, courage used or withheld, presence given or stolen by distraction. *Worrying wastes your life* — that's the most common regret in the data.

Shane's punchline: "We regret the things we didn't do more than the things we did. The pain of trying and failing may be intense but at least it tends to be over rather quickly. The pain of failing to try, on the other hand, is less intense but never really goes away."

### Tiny delights over big bright lights

The pattern that emerges from the deathbed exercise is not what most ambitious operators expect. Big achievements show up rarely. What shows up constantly is the accumulation of ordinary moments — family dinners, the laughs, the tears, the marks in the doorway that recorded how tall your kids were at each age. The promotion fades. The Tuesday morning coffee with your wife doesn't.

This is the bridge back to the umbrella thesis. Ordinary moments determine your future. They also determine your eulogy. Big decisions get the credit; ordinary moments do the actual work.

Shane: "What we think of as defining moments, like promotions or a new house, matter less to life satisfaction than the accumulation of tiny moments that didn't seem to matter at the time. In the end, everyday moments matter more than big prizes. Tiny delights over big bright lights."

### Happiness is a choice, not a condition

One of Pillemer's interviewees, age 89: "In my 89 years, I've learned that happiness is a choice — not a condition." Shane uses this as the operational frame. Happiness is not the absence of bad events. It's the daily orientation toward what matters — chosen, not inherited from circumstance.

Marcus Aurelius, cited by Shane: "When you are distressed by an external thing, it's not the thing itself that troubles you, but only your judgment of it. And you can wipe this out at a moment's notice." The decision about what to value, and what to let trouble you, is itself a decision. One you make many times a day.

### The check Shane uses

When the framework gets practical, the check is simple. *Does this action move me toward the life that, on reflection, I'd want to have lived? Or away from it?*

Run it on the calendar. The calendar is the most honest record of your actual values — what you say doesn't matter; what you scheduled does. If your calendar is full of things that fail the deathbed test, your stated values aren't your operating values. The operating values are whatever the calendar shows.

Run it on the relationships. Who are you spending the highest-fidelity time with? If the answer is people who, on reflection, you wouldn't choose — but who you're with by inertia or by social default — that's the data. The same time can either compound a relationship that matters or burn it on one that doesn't.

Run it on the work. The work you're doing — would you, on reflection, endorse the *kind* of work it is, the *people* you're doing it for, and the *trajectory* it's putting you on? Optimization on a project that fails this test produces extraordinary efficiency at the wrong thing.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the deathbed thought experiment, in writing.** Eighty years old. Park bench. Who's in the life you're imagining? Who isn't? What did you do? What didn't you do? Write it. The writing forces specificity that the silent version evades. This is not a one-time exercise. It compounds over years.

2. **Audit your calendar against the deathbed answer.** What you scheduled is what you valued, regardless of what you said you valued. If the gap is large, the gap is the work.

3. **Apply Jobs's daily ritual on a sustained cadence.** "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?" Not as morbid theater — as a calibration. When the answer is no week after week, change something.

4. **Apply Bezos's regret-minimization frame to large decisions.** "Looking back from eighty, will I regret doing this, or regret not doing it?" The asymmetry is usually clear once it's named. The pain of trying and failing fades. The pain of not trying doesn't.

5. **Define your one most important thing.** Not for a project — for your life or for this chapter of it. The same Most Important Thing principle from the decision process applies. If you have two or more, you don't yet know what you actually want. The discipline is to name the one.

6. **Sort your activities into "tiny delights" and "big bright lights."** Be honest about which are which. Most ambitious operators have systematically traded delights for lights. The deathbed data says that trade is a bad one. Adjust.

7. **Watch for the "I'll do it after" trap.** "I'll spend more time with my kids after the company exits." "I'll work on the marriage after the launch." "I'll take care of my health after this quarter." The "after" almost never arrives at the value the present moment offered. The present moment is the one you have.

8. **Treat the values check as a recurring practice, not a one-time decision.** Goals shift. You shift. The values you held at twenty-five are not the values you'll hold at forty. The exercise needs to be re-run, not consulted as an artifact.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is working seventy-hour weeks, raising a Series B, and her marriage is fraying. She's convinced she'll fix the marriage "after the round closes."
**Application:** Bezos's regret-minimization frame, run honestly. At eighty, will she regret having raised one fewer round, or will she regret the marriage? The deathbed data says the answer is unambiguous and that "after the round closes" is the inertia default in productivity costume. The actionable move isn't to stop fundraising — it's to make a small, present-tense investment in the marriage *during* the fundraising, on a non-negotiable cadence. One uninterrupted dinner a week. A monthly weekend off. The Tuesday morning coffee.
**Result:** The fundraise still happens. The marriage doesn't get traded for it. The trade was always false; the framing concealed the alternative.

**Situation:** A leader has just been offered a promotion that comes with significantly more money and significantly more travel. He's about to accept by default because "you don't say no to this."
**Application:** Run the deathbed test. Project to eighty and look back at the next five years if he takes the role versus if he doesn't. What does the calendar look like in each scenario? Who's in his life in each scenario? What does the relationship with his daughter, who's eight, look like in each? Would he, on reflection, endorse the trade?
**Result:** Either he takes the role with eyes open about what it costs, and pre-installs safeguards to protect what matters most (Belichick rule on family time, etc.), or he declines and uses the offer as leverage for a different role. Either is a real decision. The default acceptance was not a decision — it was social default with a salary attached.

**Situation:** A team has just hit a major milestone. The leader feels a thinness she didn't expect. She's already moving the goalpost to the next milestone.
**Application:** This is the unexamined-wanting failure mode. The thinness is signal — the goal she pursued wasn't actually the one her values pointed to. The goalpost-moving is the inertia default disguised as ambition. The intervention is the deathbed exercise, applied right now, before the new goal locks in. What did the milestone mean? What does the next one need to mean for the next year of effort to be worth it?
**Result:** Either the new goal gets defined more precisely, with a clearer connection to what actually matters, or she discovers the trajectory itself needs a pause. Either is more honest than the autopilot of "next milestone, faster."

**Situation:** A founder is being courted by a strategic acquirer. The deal would be a soft landing, not a triumphant exit. He's torn.
**Application:** The Bezos regret-minimization frame, applied to both directions. At eighty, will he regret selling now and walking away, or regret pushing through three more hard years? Crucially: the question isn't "which produces more money?" It's "which is the version of his life he'd endorse looking back?" Run the deathbed exercise on each path.
**Result:** Whatever the answer, it's now a values decision rather than a financial-optimization decision — which is what it actually was all along. The financial frame was concealing the real question.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat values clarification as a one-time exercise.
**Why:** Values shift as you shift. The 25-year-old's values are not the 40-year-old's. The exercise has to be re-run, not consulted as an artifact. Annual is the minimum cadence; quarterly during inflection points.

**Don't:** Confuse stated values with operating values.
**Why:** The calendar is the most honest record of what you actually value. If the gap between stated and operating values is large, the operating values are the real ones. Closing the gap is the work.

**Don't:** Use values language as cover for avoiding a hard decision.
**Why:** "I'm taking time to reflect on what really matters" can be the inertia default with a halo. The test: is the reflection producing different action? If not, it's avoidance.

**Don't:** Optimize aggressively without checking the direction.
**Why:** Velocity without heading produces extraordinary efficiency at the wrong thing. The decision process and the strengths point any direction equally well. The destination is what makes the velocity worth something.

**Don't:** Buy the "I'll do it after" trade.
**Why:** "After the launch, after the round, after the kids are older" — the after almost never arrives at the value the present moment offered. The present moment is the one you have. Tiny delights, sustained, beat the deferred big bright lights almost every time.

**Don't:** Pursue what looks like winning when it's a shallow victory.
**Why:** Shane: "What seems like winning in the moment is often just a shallow victory. It seems like winning, but it isn't." The deathbed test is the long-form check on whether the win is actually a win or just a moment-level reward stolen from the decade.

**Don't:** Treat values as inherited from your peer group.
**Why:** The social default operates at the values level too. The most expensive instances are not "what should I have for dinner" but "what kind of life should I want." Values absorbed without examination from the people around you will pull you toward their average, not toward what you'd actually choose on reflection.

**Don't:** Skip the writing.
**Why:** The deathbed exercise done silently produces a vague feeling. Done in writing, it produces specifics — names, scenes, regrets, choices. The specifics are what produce decisions. Vague feelings produce nothing.
