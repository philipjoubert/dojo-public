# Standards and Exemplars

## When to Use
- The user wants to build the four strengths and is asking how to actually install them.
- A leader is establishing the standard for a new team or new project — the moment when the bar gets set is now.
- The user has just outgrown their current environment and is at risk of drifting back to its average.
- The user is hiring, promoting, or evaluating people and needs criteria that go beyond skill.
- A team is producing acceptable but not exceptional work and no one is sure why.
- The user is asking "what would the best person in the world do here?"

## Fails When
- **The user picks exemplars by their public success metric rather than by their character.** The most successful person in your industry might be the worst person to model on — they may have succeeded by traits you wouldn't endorse on reflection. Pick for what you'd want to internalize, not for what you'd want to achieve.
- **The user uses "I'm following X's playbook" as cover for not doing the actual work.** Reading about an exemplar isn't modeling them. Internalizing one specific attribute and acting on it consistently is.
- **The user models on people whose situation is structurally unlike theirs.** Some attributes don't transfer across context. The exemplar test is whether the attribute is portable to your actual position.

## Core Concept

The four strengths don't get built by willpower. They get built by raising your standards, and you raise your standards by changing who you stand next to. The mechanism is unconscious — you absorb the average behavior of the environment you're embedded in, whether you intend to or not.

Shane's central line: "Standards become habits, and habits become outcomes." Average people produce average standards, which produce average outcomes. Exceptional outcomes require exceptional standards. And exceptional standards are almost never ambient — they have to be deliberately imported.

The deeper claim, which Shane returns to repeatedly: "Champions don't create the standards of excellence. The standards of excellence create champions." You don't will yourself into being above-average. You position yourself in an environment where above-average is the average, and the same biological default that would have made you mediocre in a mediocre environment now makes you excellent in an excellent one. Inertia, redirected.

There are two practical applications. The first is raising the standard inside your own work. The second is curating the people whose standards you absorb.

### Raising the standard

Two protocols Shane cites repeatedly.

**The Kissinger protocol.** Henry Kissinger refused to read a draft until the author swore it was their best work. Sent the draft back the first time without reading. The author rewrote. Same question. Sent back again. By the third draft, the author had usually internalized the standard. Works on yourself too. The question — "is this my best work?" — is the safeguard against the social default of "good enough."

**The Belichick rule.** If the star player is late, the star player is sent home. Standards don't bend for talent. The moment you exempt one person from the standard, you've established a different standard — a two-tier standard where talent is the exemption. That standard collapses everything.

The principle underneath both: standards are set by what you tolerate, not by what you state. If you tolerate sloppy work, that's the standard. If you tolerate the star showing up late, that's the standard. The stated standard is irrelevant — only the operative one shapes outcomes.

### The personal board of directors

A deliberately chosen set of people — alive, dead, or fictional — whose standards, thinking patterns, and character you want to internalize. Shane's framing: "Show me your role models and I'll show you your future."

Rules for selection:

- **Not "most successful" — chosen for values and character.** The most-revenue founder is often the worst exemplar. Pick people whose decisions you'd endorse, not whose outcomes you'd envy.
- **Each needs only *one* attribute you want to cultivate.** Ignore the rest. Modeling whole people leads to false reverence and inevitable disappointment when the model turns out to be flawed in ways you didn't see. Modeling one attribute is durable.
- **Don't reject a source because of a flaw.** Seneca: "I shall never be ashamed of citing a bad author if the line is good." The intellectual honesty of taking what's useful and leaving the rest.
- **Personal boards aren't static.** Rotate as your needs change. The "wartime consigliere" is not the "peacetime consigliere." The exemplar you needed at the start of a company is rarely the one you need at scale.

Operational moves:

- Before acting: "What would [exemplar] do here?" The question reframes the moment. Most defaults can't survive the imagined audience of someone whose standard is higher than yours.
- Imagine them watching. You'll behave differently. This is the social default redirected — turning the same biology that makes you conform to mediocrity into the engine that makes you conform to excellence.
- Build a *repository of good behavior* over time. "Here's what the outliers do" in this situation. The repository becomes the reference library you actually consult when defaults are firing.

The Munger standard Shane cites as his bar for *holding an opinion*: "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything unless I know the other side's argument better than they do." This is the exemplar move applied to epistemics — adopting one specific habit from one specific person and applying it consistently as a check.

### Practice and sandboxes

Strengths require repetition. Aristotle, quoted by Shane: "We become builders by building, and we become harpists by playing the harp."

The sandbox principle: deliberately practice in low-stakes environments where mistakes are contained. Pilots in simulators. Surgeons on cadavers. Intelligence agencies rehearsing operations in safe-to-fail conditions before executing. The sandbox lets you build the muscle without paying the cost of the real failure mode.

In ordinary work this means: practice the hard conversation in a low-stakes context before the high-stakes one. Run the public talk in front of one trusted person before the room of two hundred. Make the small commitment public before you make the big one. The standard you can hold in the sandbox is the standard you can hold under pressure.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit the standard you actually operate at.** Not the one you state. Look at the last five outputs you shipped or accepted. Was each one your best work, or was each one "good enough"? "Good enough" is the standard.

2. **Ask the Kissinger question on your next deliverable.** Before you ship: is this my best work? If no, why are you shipping it? The honest answer is usually "because the deadline is now" or "because the audience won't notice." Both are social-default reasoning. Hold the standard.

3. **Pick your personal board, on paper, with the one attribute you want from each.** Three to seven people. Living, dead, or fictional. For each, write the single attribute. "Munger — refusal to have an opinion you can't argue both sides of." "Ada Lovelace — patience with an idea that takes a generation to mature." "My grandmother — kindness without obligation." The list is private and revisable.

4. **Before significant decisions, run the exemplar test.** Pick the exemplar most relevant to this kind of decision. Ask: "What would they do here, given what they care about?" Not "what would they do given everything they know" — what would they do given the *standard* you're trying to import.

5. **Build the repository.** When you observe an outlier doing something unusually well, write it down. "Here's what the great salespeople do when a deal is going sideways." "Here's what the great founders do in a board meeting that's drifting." The repository becomes the reference library that interrupts your defaults.

6. **Sandbox the hard moves before you need them in production.** Practice the hard conversation. Practice the difficult negotiation. Practice the public position you'll have to defend. The first time you do something hard should not be the time it matters.

7. **Apply the Belichick rule before talent becomes a standards exemption.** Decide in advance how you'll handle it when your highest-leverage person violates the standard. The decision will be much harder when the moment arrives. Pre-deciding is the safeguard.

8. **Audit the environment you stand in.** Whose average behavior are you absorbing? If the answer is people whose standards you wouldn't actively choose, the standard you're absorbing is the standard you'll produce. Move closer to people whose standards you would choose.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has been writing first-pass investor updates and shipping them after a single read-through. The team is starting to do the same with their own deliverables.
**Application:** Kissinger protocol on himself first. Before sending the next investor update, ask: "Is this my best work?" The honest answer is no — he wrote it in 30 minutes between meetings. Send it back to himself. Rewrite. Then announce the standard to the team: from now on, anything sent under the company's name is "best work" or it doesn't ship. The standard is set by what's tolerated, and he's been tolerating sloppy.
**Result:** The first three weeks are slower. Output volume drops. By week four, the team is operating at the new standard and the volume returns. The standard now creates the work, instead of the work creating the standard.

**Situation:** A senior leader is about to fire someone for repeated poor performance. She wants to do it the right way, but she's never fired anyone before.
**Application:** Personal board exemplar test. She picks the exemplar she trusts most for difficult interpersonal decisions — let's say her former boss who was famous for firing people in a way that left them more, not less, intact. Asks: "What would she do here?" Imports the specific moves: do it in person, do it on Monday morning not Friday afternoon, give the person a real reason and not corporate language, offer to be a reference if appropriate.
**Result:** The conversation happens at the higher standard than the leader would have produced from her own defaults. The fired employee leaves with their dignity intact. The remaining team observes how the firing was handled and updates their model of the company.

**Situation:** A team of engineers consistently writes code that passes review but accumulates technical debt that bites them six months later. Each individual review is "fine."
**Application:** The Belichick rule applied to code quality. Until now, the team's standard has been "passes review under deadline pressure." That's the operative standard regardless of what's stated. The intervention: define the actual quality bar in writing, with examples, and apply it to everyone — including the most senior engineer whose output the team is most reluctant to push back on. The first time the senior engineer's PR is sent back for not meeting the standard is the moment the standard becomes real.
**Result:** Short-term friction. Medium-term reduction in technical debt. Long-term, the team's average has shifted upward because the standard isn't an exemption-zone.

**Situation:** A founder has just moved her company to a new city and noticed she's spending most of her time with a different group of operators than before. Her decisions feel slightly off.
**Application:** Audit the standards-environment. The new peer group is a different average. Some of the absorbed standards are positive, some neutral, some actively wrong for her stage. The exemplar move: deliberately schedule monthly time with three operators whose standards she actively wants to absorb — even if it requires travel. The accidental peer group will shape her unless she counter-curates.
**Result:** The drift slows. Decisions return to the standard she wants to hold. The accidental environment still influences, but no longer dominates.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick exemplars by their financial outcomes rather than by their character.
**Why:** Outcome-based selection imports survivorship bias and luck. You may end up modeling someone who succeeded by traits you'd actively reject if you saw them clearly. Pick for the attribute, not the asset value.

**Don't:** Treat the personal board as a fan list.
**Why:** Admiring someone is not the same as modeling them. The board is operational — each name is paired with a single attribute you actually consult in decisions. If you can't name the attribute and the situations where you'd apply it, the name is decoration.

**Don't:** Hold the standard for the team but not for yourself.
**Why:** The team's standard is your standard, observed. If you ship sloppy work and ask them not to, you've established a two-tier standard that will collapse. The Belichick rule applies to you first.

**Don't:** Set the standard once and assume it holds.
**Why:** Standards drift in the direction of the easier path unless actively maintained. The standard you set on day one is rarely the standard operating on day two hundred unless you keep tending it. The Kissinger question is meant to be asked repeatedly, not once.

**Don't:** Use exemplars as cover for not making the decision.
**Why:** "What would Munger do here?" is a useful prompt only if you actually do something different as a result. Otherwise it becomes intellectual decoration. The test is whether the exemplar move actually shows up in the next action you take.

**Don't:** Reject an exemplar because they were flawed in some other domain.
**Why:** Modeling whole people is the trap. You're not endorsing them — you're importing one specific attribute. Seneca's line is the discipline: take what's good, leave the rest, don't apologize for the source.

**Don't:** Think that being around exceptional people is the same as absorbing their standard.
**Why:** Standards transfer through observation *and* action — practicing the move yourself is the part that locks it in. Proximity without practice is just adjacency. The sandbox principle is what makes it real.

**Don't:** Skip the sandbox and try to debut the new standard in production.
**Why:** The hard move done for the first time in the high-stakes setting will execute at your old, untrained level — which is exactly the level you were trying to upgrade away from. Build the muscle in the safe context first.
