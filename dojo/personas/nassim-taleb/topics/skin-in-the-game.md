# Skin in the Game

## When to Use

- When a recommendation arrives from someone whose career, capital, or body is not on the line for being wrong.
- When you're evaluating a CEO, board member, banker, regulator, or politician whose compensation is decoupled from outcomes.
- When a consultant, analyst, or pundit confidently prescribes a course of action they will not personally execute or suffer.
- When you're hiring or contracting and the question is how to filter signal from prestige.
- When you're designing partnership terms, equity structures, or compensation plans that will run for years.
- When you need an ethical test for a proposed policy: would the proposer's life materially worsen if it turned out wrong?
- When the system you're inside has accumulated layers of authority that no longer carry corresponding consequences.

## Fails When

- **You use it to dismiss anyone offering general advice.** A friend pointing out a flaw in your plan does not need to bet his house on the conversation. The rule is for *consequential* recommendations from authorities who profit from being heard, not for ordinary discourse.
- **The action is a genuine public good with no engineerable exposure.** Some research, some policy, some art is bona fide gift-economy work. Demanding skin in the game from an unpaid open-source maintainer is a category error.
- **You confuse "has personal cost" with "is correct."** Mafiosi have skin in the game. Religious zealots have skin in the game. The principle is necessary but not sufficient. Exposure filters out a class of charlatans; it does not certify everyone left.
- **The expertise is so abstracted that direct exposure isn't possible.** A theoretical physicist studying cosmology cannot be expected to die on the steps of the Crab Nebula. The principle scales with the consequentiality of the recommendation, not the abstractness of the discipline.

## Core Concept

The single most important sentence in moral history may be Hammurabi's law: *if the architect builds a house and the house collapses, the architect shall be put to death.* The principle is 3,800 years old, predates almost all of moral philosophy, and remains the cleanest formulation we have of the link between authority, action, and consequence. Modernity has systematically broken this link. The architects today collect their fees and walk away; the houses collapse onto people who never agreed to live in them; the architects are then promoted to designing larger structures.

Skin in the Game is three things at once. First, an *ethical rule*: thou shalt not have antifragility at the expense of the fragility of others. The chief modern sin is the inverse hero — the actor who collects upside while transferring downside elsewhere. The 2008 banker bonus structure. The interventionist who orders regime change from a Washington think tank. The pundit who declares wars he won't fight. The CEO with golden parachutes regardless of outcome. The university administrator who builds career capital on student debt. Each of these structures is recognisably the same shape and each is a betrayal of Hammurabi.

Second, an *epistemic filter*. The strongest signal that someone knows what they're talking about is that they bear the cost of being wrong. The pilot is an expert because bad pilots are dead. The trader who keeps trading after twenty years is one because the bad ones are out. The grandmother is right about food because her family's survival depended on her being right. The macroeconomist, the State Department analyst, the academic theorist — these have no skin and therefore no reliable signal. Always look for the actor whose personal life is on the line. Their advice may still be wrong, but at least the selection pressure has done some work.

Third, a *risk-management discipline*. Symmetry. If you collect the upside, you must bear the downside. The Roman emperor who fought in the front line. The Lord whose status came from physical risk-taking. The merchant whose contracts required personal exposure. The Bob Rubin trade — collect upside in good years, transfer the bad year to taxpayers — is the master pathology because it severs this symmetry, and once severance is institutionalised, the resulting class of actors compounds its grip until the system collapses.

The Bob Rubin trade itself: Rubin collected $120M in compensation at Citibank for over a decade. Citi blew up in 2008. Taxpayers — you, the bus driver, the dentist — bailed it out. Rubin kept the $120M. The architecture is everywhere: in regulators who cycle to consulting, in central bankers who pump asset prices and then retire to speaker fees, in CEOs who demolish a company and exit with severance, in foreign-policy hawks who collapse a country and write a book about it.

A practical filter from the essay "Surgeons Should Not Look Like Surgeons": between two surgeons of identical credentials, pick the one who *doesn't look like a surgeon* — fat-fingered, thick New York accent, no diploma on the wall. He has had to be much better than the polished one to overcome the appearance penalty. Look-the-part is cheap signaling; do-the-part is what matters. This generalises. Prefer the rumpled to the polished in any field where the polish is decoupled from the work.

The Hammurabi corollary for modern practice: any policy proposal should pass the test "would the proposer's life materially worsen if the proposal turns out wrong?" If no, the policy is not credible. If yes, take it seriously regardless of credentials.

> *Bureaucracy is a construction by which a person is conveniently separated from the consequences of his or her actions.*
>
> *At no point in history have so many non-risk-takers, that is, those with no personal exposure, exerted so much control.*

## How to Apply

1. **Apply the Hammurabi test to every recommendation.** Before adopting expert advice, ask: what materially changes in this person's life if their recommendation turns out wrong? If the answer is "nothing," weight the recommendation accordingly. If the answer is "they lose money, status, freedom, or position," take it more seriously.

2. **Restructure contracts to include downside.** When hiring a contractor, financial advisor, lawyer, agency, or consultant, build the engagement so that *they* lose if the work fails. Equity stakes, performance fees, repeat-business reputation, retainer-clawback clauses. If they refuse, you've learned something — they don't believe in their own work enough to bet on it.

3. **Identify the Bob Rubin pattern in your own institutions.** Inside your company, find the roles where the actor has upside (bonus, promotion, stock vesting) but no symmetric downside. These are the slots most likely to accumulate hidden fragility. Restructure or eliminate.

4. **Filter pundits by their position record.** When someone gives a public forecast, ask whether they have a position on. The trader who says "I'm long" or "I'm short" has skin; the columnist who says "the market should" has none. Treat the two as different epistemic categories.

5. **In hiring senior roles, prefer the rumpled.** Two candidates with comparable records, one Hollywood-doctor polished and one not — the unpolished one has had to be much better to get equally far. Apply this consciously when interviewing for technical roles.

6. **In your own work, take exposed positions.** Don't write essays you don't believe enough to act on. Don't make recommendations you wouldn't take yourself. The discipline of skin in the game starts with the question of whether you have it on the things you're saying right now.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is choosing between two consulting firms for a strategy engagement. Firm A is McKinsey-style: senior partners with prestigious credentials, fixed fee, no skin in the outcome. Firm B is a smaller boutique: half the fee plus 15% of demonstrated revenue lift over the next year, capped at 3× the base.
**Application:** The Hammurabi test resolves the question instantly. Firm A is paid the same whether the strategy works or not — they have no exposure. Firm B has tied their compensation to the actual result and is willing to be measured on it. The fact that Firm A has more famous logos in its case studies is a credentialing signal that *substitutes* for the exposure signal. The Skin-in-the-Game frame says trust exposure over prestige. The founder may still find a real reason to pick A — domain depth, specific expertise — but credentials alone should not carry the day.
**Result:** The boutique either delivers and earns its bonus, or doesn't and eats the lower fee. Either outcome is informative. The McKinsey engagement, win or lose, produces only a deck.

**Situation:** A senior banker proposes a new product line that promises high returns "if we manage the tail risk carefully." The bank's bonus pool, including his, scales with the returns. Tail-risk losses, if they materialise, would be borne by the bank's balance sheet and ultimately its shareholders or its insurer of last resort.
**Application:** This is the Bob Rubin trade in plain sight. The structure is asymmetric: he captures upside through bonus, the loss is socialised through the institution. The Hammurabi corollary says: *would the banker's life materially worsen if the product blows up?* The answer is no — he would be paid for the years it worked, and absent personal liability he is not on the hook for the year it didn't. The product is not actually a bet on the strategy; it is a bet on time, with the institution standing behind him.
**Result:** Refuse the product *unless* the structure is changed: personal liability, deferred compensation that vests over the full lifecycle of the position, clawback for losses. If the banker objects to those terms, you've learned the only thing that matters — he doesn't believe his own pitch.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat the absence of skin in the game as automatic disqualification.
**Why:** A surgeon doesn't bet his house on every patient; a teacher doesn't sign over his pension to each child's outcome. The principle applies most strongly to *consequential, voluntary recommendations* from actors who profit from being heard. A friend giving informal advice over coffee, a researcher publishing a finding for the field to evaluate — these aren't violations. The filter is for the credentialed, exposure-free actor whose recommendation will be acted on at scale.

**Don't:** Confuse personal courage with correctness.
**Why:** People with deep skin in the game can still be wrong. The grandmother who refuses every modern medical intervention because she's seen friends harmed has exposure but may still be wrong about the specific case. Skin in the game is necessary for *trust* in a domain where credentials have decoupled from consequence; it is not a guarantee of truth. Pair it with content evaluation.

**Don't:** Demand quantified skin in the game from every advisor before listening to anyone.
**Why:** That collapses into nihilism and makes it impossible to act. The right move is to *update* on the absence of exposure — weight the recommendation downward, scrutinise the reasoning more carefully, run your own check — not to refuse all input. The point is to break the equation "credentialed = trustworthy," not to break the equation "thoughtful = worth hearing."
