# Iatrogenics

## When to Use

- When somebody is proposing an intervention in a complex system — a body, an economy, an ecosystem, a market, a foreign country, an organisation — that they do not fully understand.
- When the rallying cry is "we have to do something" and the something is being chosen for visibility rather than effectiveness.
- When a regulation, bailout, or aid programme is being defended on the basis of intentions while the actual outcome is degrading.
- When a parent, manager, or policymaker is reflexively shielding their charges from every small stressor.
- When the proposed benefit is small and specific (we can point to it) and the proposed harm would be diffuse (it requires looking).
- When the proposer of the intervention does not bear the cost if it fails.
- When you want to understand why well-intentioned modern institutions keep producing the opposite of what they intend.

## Fails When

- **The system is genuinely failing.** A bleeding patient needs a tourniquet. A burning building needs water. The frame applies to *complex evolved systems with self-regulation*, not to acute mechanical emergencies. Don't use it to refuse genuine help.
- **The intervention is well-tested with bounded harm.** Some interventions are net-positive and the evidence is robust. Vaccines for major diseases. Antibiotics for bacterial infection. Don't reach for the iatrogenics frame indiscriminately against established medicine or engineering.
- **You use it to refuse every intervention as a matter of principle.** That collapses the framework into reactionary inaction. The diagnostic targets *interventions in complex systems by exposure-free actors*, not all action.
- **The proposer actually does have skin in the game.** A surgeon recommending surgery on the patient she will operate on, with personal liability, is not the structural target. The frame is for the *credentialed exposure-free actor* whose intervention will be executed by others and whose costs will be borne elsewhere.

## Core Concept

*Iatrogenesis* (Greek: "originated by the healer") is harm caused by the very intervention meant to help. Coined in medicine, generalised by Taleb to *every* domain where well-intentioned actors cause net damage by intervening in complex systems they don't understand. The word is precise enough to do work — once you can name the pattern, you stop falling for the rhetorical posture of "but they meant well."

The medical core. For most of human history, doctors killed more patients than they saved. Bloodletting killed George Washington. Lobotomy won a Nobel Prize. Hormone replacement therapy was prescribed to millions of women on the basis of bad observational studies. Statins are prescribed for low-risk patients on the basis of surrogate markers (cholesterol) that don't track the actual outcome (mortality) once you control for everything else. Even today, a meaningful fraction of medical interventions cause more harm than they prevent — the harm is just diffuse and the benefit is specific.

Why intervention skews to harm in complex systems. Most complex systems — the human body, the economy, ecosystems, markets, foreign nations — have self-regulating mechanisms refined over very long evolutionary time. The default outcome of *not intervening* is usually fine — the system handles it. The default outcome of *intervening* is the introduction of effects you didn't model, second-order consequences you didn't anticipate, and disruption of the self-regulation that was already working. The asymmetry: small visible benefit, large invisible harm. The visible side wins political contests; the invisible side wins reality contests, decades later, after the originating actor has moved on.

The rule of thumb is the oldest formal version of via negativa. *First, do no harm.* The Hippocratic Oath — *primum non nocere* — has been honoured in the breach by modern medicine for the last century, and most consequential interventions in modern life betray it. The default action under uncertainty about complex systems should be *no action.*

The generalisations beyond medicine are extensive. *Economic iatrogenics.* Smoothing the business cycle (Bernanke / Greenspan put) suppresses small recessions and produces 2008. Bailouts of failing banks suppress small failures and produce systemic ones. Bailouts of failing carmakers suppress competitive renewal and produce decades-long zombie firms. The "fragilista" central banker who tries to "fine-tune" the economy is iatrogenic. *Foreign-policy iatrogenics.* Regime change in Iraq, Libya, Syria — each justified as "removing a dictator" — produced state collapse, slave markets, civil war, refugee crises, and stronger jihadist movements than existed before. Each intervention made the system worse than the alleged disease. *Educational iatrogenics.* Standardised testing produces students optimised for the test rather than for thinking. Excessive credentialing produces a class of certified non-thinkers. "Pedagogy" replaces apprenticeship with structured nonsense. Children are medicated for "ADHD" because they don't sit quietly through environments that no human evolved to sit quietly through. *Public-health iatrogenics.* Lockdown policy with no honest accounting of the harm done to mental health, education, employment, supply chains. Food guidance that pushed people away from fat and toward seed oils. The opioid crisis produced by the very system meant to manage pain. *Parental iatrogenics.* "Helicopter parenting" sterilises the small stressors that build a robust adult. Removes the antifragile shocks. Produces young adults who cannot handle ordinary disappointment. *Organisational iatrogenics.* "Best-practice" management consultancies that impose generic frameworks on idiosyncratic businesses. ERP migrations that destroy the institutional knowledge they were meant to capture. Culture programmes that demolish the actual culture and replace it with HR-speak.

The detection rule. When evaluating any intervention, ask:

1. What is the *visible* benefit? Usually small, specific, easy to point at.
2. What is the *invisible* harm? Usually large, diffuse, hidden — but discoverable if you ask the right questions.
3. What is the cost of *not intervening*? Usually less than the proponent claims, because the system was self-regulating.
4. Who *bears* the harm if you're wrong? Usually not the intervener — see Bob Rubin trade.

If the visible benefit is small, the invisible harm is potentially large, the do-nothing cost is bounded, and the decider doesn't bear the downside — *don't intervene*. The default is via negativa.

The Soviet-Harvard delusion. The umbrella term for the modern intellectual class's belief that they understand complex systems well enough to redesign them top-down. Soviet central planning and Harvard MBA management consulting are the same disease, just speaking different languages. Both produce iatrogenic harm because both confuse the model for the territory. Both are convinced of their own intelligence in proportion to their distance from any actual feedback. The Soviet-Harvard actor is the architectural cousin of the IYI; the IYI is the producer of the iatrogenic intervention.

> *The tragedy of modernity: as with neurotically overprotective parents, those trying to help are often hurting us the most.*
>
> *Telling people NOT to smoke seems to be the greatest medical contribution of the last 60 years.*

## How to Apply

1. **Run the four-question test before approving any intervention.** Visible benefit, invisible harm, cost of inaction, who bears the cost if wrong? Most proposed interventions fail at least one of the four. The honest accounting changes the conversation.

2. **Demand explicit accounting of invisible harm.** Whenever an intervention is being approved on the strength of a visible benefit, force the proposer to enumerate plausible second-order effects, side effects, and unintended consequences. If they cannot, that absence is itself the diagnostic — they haven't thought about it.

3. **Default to inaction in complex systems.** When the system is complex, self-regulating, and currently functioning, the burden of proof flips: the proposer of the intervention has to demonstrate that the expected benefit exceeds the expected harm by enough to justify the disruption. Most do not.

4. **Identify the Soviet-Harvard pattern in your own organisation.** Find the actors whose role is to redesign systems they have never operated. The McKinsey-style consultant, the central HR programme, the ERP migration team. Ask whether the proposed redesign respects the local knowledge of the people who actually run the system, or whether it imposes a generic framework with no exposure to the consequences.

5. **Apply the framework to parenting and management.** Whenever the instinct is to step in and remove a small stressor, ask whether the stressor is feeding the system's antifragility. Often it is. Suppression of small disappointments is the parental version of suppressing small recessions: it accumulates fragility against the next ordinary shock.

6. **Reverse the rhetoric of "we have to do something."** "We have to do something" is the iatrogenist's slogan. The honest version is "we have to do something *that produces more good than harm*, where someone bears the cost if it doesn't, and where we have engaged with the second-order consequences." Most "we have to do something" pressure does not survive that honest version.

## Examples

**Situation:** A central bank is debating whether to intervene to prevent a moderately stressed regional bank from failing. The intervention is technically simple and would prevent a visible event (the bank's failure). The cost of not intervening is bounded (depositors are insured, the bank's assets are real, market participants would absorb the failure).
**Application:** Run the four questions. Visible benefit: the regional bank doesn't fail. Specific, easy to point at. Invisible harm: every other regional bank now knows that risky positions will be backstopped, behaviour shifts toward more risk, the next failure (when it comes) is larger and harder to contain. Cost of inaction: bounded — depositors paid out, market clears, lessons internalised. Who bears the cost if wrong: not the central bankers, who will be feted regardless of outcome and retire to speaker fees. The intervention scores zero out of four. The honest move is non-intervention.
**Result:** The central bank that intervenes produces 2008 fifteen years later. The central bank that allows the small failure has a sharper banking system that internalises losses without systemic spillover. The latter looks like negligence in the moment and competence in retrospect; the former looks like leadership in the moment and disaster in retrospect.

**Situation:** A new manager is taking over a high-performing team and is being told that the team has "no formal process" for product reviews. The instinct is to install a structured weekly review with a template, a calendar, an output document.
**Application:** The team is currently functioning — that is itself evidence that the do-nothing path is survivable. The visible benefit of the new process: documentation, accountability, "alignment." The invisible harm: the team's actual coordination mechanism (informal Slack threads, lunch conversations, the shared sense of what good looks like) gets displaced by the formal process, the formal process produces theatre rather than the real coordination, the new manager has destroyed the antifragile self-regulation that produced the team's performance and replaced it with bureaucracy. The cost of inaction is zero — the team works. The cost of intervention is borne by the team, not the manager (who looks decisive).
**Result:** The manager who installs the process has a team that produces decks and stops producing product. The manager who sits with the team for three months before deciding what (if anything) to add discovers what's actually load-bearing and intervenes only on the specific places where the informal mechanism is failing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Apply the framework to refuse every intervention.
**Why:** Some interventions are net-positive — vaccines, antibiotics, narrowly scoped engineering fixes, well-tested management interventions on specific failures. The framework targets the *structural pattern* of complex-system intervention by exposure-free actors. Reaching for "iatrogenics" against every proposed change degrades into universal contrarianism.

**Don't:** Confuse "I don't like this intervention" with "this intervention is iatrogenic."
**Why:** Iatrogenics has a specific structure: small visible benefit, large invisible harm, bounded cost of inaction, exposure-free decider. If the structure is present, name the iatrogenics. If it isn't, you don't get to invoke the term just because you dislike the proposal. Maintain the discipline of the diagnostic.

**Don't:** Use the framework as cover for refusing to engage with the actual problem.
**Why:** Sometimes the system really is failing, the do-nothing cost really is unbounded, and the right move is genuine intervention. The framework's job is to weight the priors heavily toward inaction in complex evolved systems with self-regulation. It is not to refuse engagement with the system. The discipline is to do the four-question test honestly, not to use the test as a stop on all action.
