# The Great Stagnation

## When to Use
- A founder is trying to understand the macro context for why their industry is stuck.
- Explaining why regulated sectors (energy, healthcare, transportation, education) have seen so little innovation while software has compounded.
- Motivating a founder to pursue an atoms-heavy category that others avoid.
- Diagnosing why most sectors feel like zero-sum fights.
- Framing why "we need miracles" is literal rather than rhetorical.

## Fails When
- The user needs tactical product advice — this topic is macro context, not execution.
- The conversation requires careful political nuance that a broad-brush stagnation narrative will distort.
- The "stagnation" frame is used to justify pessimism rather than motivate definite optimism.

## Core Concept

"We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters."

The iPhone is extraordinary. It would have seemed like science fiction to anyone in 1970. But compare a kitchen from 1973 to a kitchen from 2023: they are almost identical. The refrigerator, the stove, the dishwasher, the countertops — functionally unchanged in fifty years. Compare transportation: speeds have not increased since the 1970s. The Concorde stopped flying. We do not have supersonic travel. We do not have flying cars. We have Uber, which is a dispatch service for the same automobiles that were on the road in the 1970s, except now slower because of congestion.

The smartphones that distract us from our surroundings also distract us from the fact that our surroundings are strangely old. Only computers and communications have improved dramatically since midcentury. In the world of bits — software, internet, mobile computing — progress has been rapid, compounding, transformative. In the world of atoms — energy, transportation, construction, medicine, food production — we have stagnation or outright regression.

The numbers confirm what the kitchen comparison suggests. Wages for median American workers have been essentially stagnant since 1973 in real terms. Real interest rates have trended toward zero and sometimes below it. When interest rates go negative, it means that people with capital have so few ideas about what to do with money that they will pay someone else to hold it for them. This is not a sign of a dynamic economy. It is a sign that nobody knows what to build.

We landed on the moon in July 1969 and Woodstock began three weeks later. With the benefit of hindsight, that is when the cultural war around progress was lost. The moon landing was the apex of the trajectory that began with the Industrial Revolution — a centuries-long commitment to mastering the physical world through engineering and science. Woodstock was the beginning of the turn inward, the cultural decision that inner experience mattered more than outer achievement. Within a decade, the great projects of midcentury America — the space program, nuclear energy, the interstate highway system — had either been completed, abandoned, or frozen. The future our parents and grandparents expected — four-day workweeks, energy too cheap to meter, vacations on the moon — was not wrong to imagine. They were only wrong to expect it automatically.

### Technology vs. Globalization

Most people think the future of the world will be defined by globalization, but the truth is that technology matters more. These are different things, and confusing them is one of the central intellectual errors of our time.

Technology means doing new things — going from zero to one, creating something that did not exist before. Globalization means copying things that work — going from one to n, spreading existing ideas and techniques to new places. If you take one typewriter and build a hundred, you have made horizontal progress. If you have a typewriter and build a word processor, you have made vertical progress.

Most of what gets called progress today is actually globalization. China building a replica of Manhattan is not technology. It is copying. India's software outsourcing industry is not creating new software — it is doing what American programmers already know how to do, at lower cost. The spread of mobile phones across Africa is not invention — it is distribution. These are valuable activities. Six billion people need access to things that work. But they are not the same as creating things that are new, and a world that mistakes copying for creation will eventually run out of things to copy.

The word "developed world" implicitly means "the part of the world where nothing new is going to happen." It suggests that development is a process with an endpoint, that the rich countries have arrived at their destination and now simply need to wait for everyone else to catch up. This is the globalization thesis in its purest form, and it is profoundly complacent. The real question is not how the developing world will catch up. The real question is how we go about developing the so-called developed world.

Globalization without technology is not merely insufficient. It is dangerous. In a world where seven billion people are competing for the same finite resources using the same existing technology, competition intensifies rather than diminishes. More people pursuing the same opportunities with the same tools creates conflict, not prosperity. **Without new technology to relieve competitive pressures, stagnation is likely to erupt into conflict.** Technology is not optional. It is the only way to make the pie bigger rather than fighting over existing slices.

### Why Stagnation Happened

The stagnation in atoms did not happen because we ran out of ideas. It happened because of a cultural shift so gradual that most people did not notice it until it was complete.

The shift was from exteriority to interiority. From building things in the real world to cultivating inner states. From engineering to psychology. From mastering nature to exploring consciousness. Milton's Satan in *Paradise Lost* declares: "The mind is its own place and of itself can make a hell of heaven and a heaven of hell." This is precisely the temptation we should resist. The idea that inner experience can substitute for outer achievement is the intellectual foundation of stagnation. It tells us we do not need flying cars because we can meditate instead, that we do not need to cure cancer because we can find meaning in suffering, that we do not need to go to Mars because we can explore inner space through psychedelics.

The regulatory state accelerated what the cultural shift began. It costs roughly $100,000 to start a software company. It costs roughly $1 billion to get a drug through the FDA. This is not because drug development is inherently a million times more expensive than software development. It is because we have constructed a regulatory apparatus that makes building in the physical world extraordinarily difficult while leaving the digital world relatively free. The result is predictable: innovation floods into the unregulated space and abandons the regulated one.

Consider what the regulatory environment has done to specific sectors. Nixon declared war on cancer in 1970 and promised victory by 1976. We would not even try to declare war on Alzheimer's today, despite the fact that one out of three people suffers from dementia by age 85. I do not think you would get a polio vaccine approved today. The safety-first mentality has been pushed so far that it has become actively dangerous, preventing the cures and technologies that would save millions of lives.

Risk aversion, incrementalism, and political correctness — broadly understood — form a mutually reinforcing system. Risk aversion prevents bold projects. Incrementalism limits ambition to marginal improvements. Political correctness — in its broadest sense, the social prohibition on questioning certain assumptions — constrains the range of permissible ideas. Our greatest intellectual problem is some form of political correctness broadly understood. Not in the narrow partisan sense, but in the sense that whole categories of thought have been declared off-limits, that questioning certain orthodoxies is socially dangerous, that original thinking is punished before it can be articulated.

### The Four Futures

Nick Bostrom has outlined four possible patterns for the future of human civilization. Recurrent collapse — history as an endless cycle of rise and fall with no permanent progress. Plateau — the world converges to something like the current developed-world standard of living and stays there. Extinction — a catastrophic collapse from which there is no recovery. Accelerating takeoff — a breakthrough to a radically better future.

Most people implicitly assume the plateau scenario. They believe the future will look roughly like the present, perhaps a bit richer, perhaps a bit poorer, but fundamentally the same. This is the default assumption of every pension fund, every insurance company, every government budget projection. It is also unstable.

A globalized plateau means seven billion people competing for the same finite resources using the same existing technology. There is no new wealth being created — only existing wealth being redistributed and contested. In game-theory terms, the plateau is a zero-sum world. And zero-sum worlds generate conflict. Without new technology to create new resources, new energy sources, new ways of producing food and shelter and medicine, the competition for existing resources becomes the dominant feature of civilization. This competition, operating at global scale with nuclear weapons available to the competitors, does not stay at the plateau. It collapses into one of the other scenarios — most likely extinction.

Recurrent collapse was plausible before nuclear weapons. Civilizations could fall and others could rise in their place. Rome fell, but Europe eventually rebuilt. Weapons capable of ending all human life on the planet close this option. There is no recovery from a nuclear or biological extinction event.

This leaves a binary choice that most people refuse to acknowledge. The real options are not plateau, collapse, takeoff, or recurrence. The real options are takeoff or extinction. The plateau is not stable. Recurrence is not possible. The future will be dramatically better or dramatically worse than the present. Nothing in between is sustainable.

The future will not happen on its own. This is not motivational rhetoric. It is an analytical conclusion. If technology stagnates, competition for resources intensifies, conflict escalates, and the trajectory bends toward extinction. If technology advances — if we solve energy, cure diseases, extend human capability — the trajectory bends toward takeoff. The deciding variable is human agency. It is whether specific people decide to build specific things.

*Nothing or something. It is up to us.*

## How to Apply

1. **Locate your industry on the bits-vs-atoms map.** Is it a domain where regulation is light and capital is cheap (software, consumer internet, many B2B SaaS categories)? Or is it a domain where regulation is heavy and capital is dear (pharmaceuticals, energy generation, aviation, materials)? The modal innovation strategy is different for each.

2. **Treat atoms-heavy innovation as undervalued and bits-heavy as overvalued.** Because regulatory and cultural forces push innovation away from atoms, the competition for ideas in atoms is far less intense than in bits. The secrets are more densely concentrated where fewer smart people are looking.

3. **Reject the plateau assumption.** If the framework for your business is "things will continue approximately as they are," you are betting on an unstable equilibrium. The plateau is not a stable scenario; it collapses into extinction or takeoff over sufficiently long horizons. The interesting question is which direction you are pushing.

4. **Build under the globalization-vs-technology frame.** When evaluating an opportunity, ask: is this 0-to-1 (technology) or 1-to-n (globalization/copying)? Both can be profitable; only the first escapes the zero-sum competition of a globalized plateau. Investors who do not distinguish between the two end up overpaying for copies.

5. **Accept that stagnation is hidden by layers of denial.** Education, culture wars, specialization, entertainment — each obscures the fact that material conditions have not meaningfully improved. Probably to get out of the desert you must start by saying you are in a desert, not in an enchanted forest. Diagnose before you prescribe.

6. **Let the civilizational stakes focus the work.** The stakes of building durable technology businesses are not merely financial. Without new technology, the plateau collapses. Specific people building specific things is the mechanism by which civilization advances. This is not inspirational language; it is a description of what is needed and why.

7. **Watch for fields where political correctness has frozen thought.** Broadly understood — not in the narrow partisan sense — whole categories have been declared off-limits for serious inquiry. These are often where the biggest secrets hide. Nutrition, energy economics, educational outcomes, the allocation of medical resources, aging — fields where certain conclusions cannot be discussed are often fields where someone with the courage to discuss them can build important things.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is considering whether to build in synthetic biology or in yet another B2B SaaS category. Synthetic biology has higher capital requirements, longer cycles, more regulatory risk. B2B SaaS has lower requirements, faster cycles, "proven" playbooks.

**Application:** The macro frame says B2B SaaS is the over-subscribed bits category. Every smart founder is there; every VC has a portfolio of them; the competition is intense and the returns have been compressing. Synthetic biology is where the atoms-side innovation is concentrated — fewer founders, less capital, harder problems, and consequently more secrets left to find. If the founder's comparative advantage is in life sciences or physical systems, the synthetic-biology path offers higher expected value despite its difficulty precisely because most people will not take it. If the founder has no comparative advantage in life sciences, they should not force the atoms-side bet, but they should understand that the B2B SaaS consensus is a mimetic trade.

**Result:** The stagnation frame is often what turns founders with dual skills toward the higher-impact, lower-competition atoms-side opportunity.

---

**Situation:** A policy researcher asks what to do about healthcare costs. The current debate is between single-payer and market-based reform.

**Application:** Both sides of the current debate share a hidden assumption: that healthcare innovation is stagnant and the only question is who pays. The deeper frame is that healthcare has been regulated into technological stagnation relative to software. The policy conversation is really about how to allocate a roughly fixed pool of value. The more important question, almost absent from the debate, is how to make healthcare delivery 10x cheaper and better — the kind of question that animated midcentury polio-vaccine politics. That requires technology, not redistribution. The current debate is arguing about how to carve the plateau pie; the interesting work is making the pie grow at bits-speed rather than atoms-speed.

**Result:** Most policy discussions in regulated domains miss the technology/stagnation frame and end up fighting over who captures a declining share of a static resource.

---

**Situation:** An investor asks whether they should be more optimistic or more pessimistic about the next decade.

**Application:** The question is wrong. Extreme optimism and extreme pessimism converge on inaction — if the future happens automatically, you do not need to work; if nothing you do matters, you do not need to work. The productive position is neither. It is hard but doable. The plateau is not stable; the trajectory depends on human agency. The correct emotional register is neither optimism nor pessimism but determination: specific people deciding to build specific things. If you are looking for a directional bet, bet on the sectors where the stagnation has been most severe and the dam is most likely to break — energy, biology, construction, transportation — not on the sectors where the innovation has been easiest for the last decade.

**Result:** "Are you optimistic or pessimistic" is a dinner-party question. The working question is "what specifically am I building" — which is the definite-optimist move regardless of mood.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "we live in the future" as a working assumption.
**Why:** The sentence is partially true — we have iPhones — and mostly false — kitchens, transportation, buildings, medicine have barely changed. The "we live in the future" frame causes founders to build for an imaginary already-futuristic world that does not match the actual material conditions customers inhabit. The right frame: we live in a present that is strangely old in most dimensions, and the work is to change that.

**Don't:** Dismiss atoms-side opportunities because they are capital-intensive.
**Why:** Capital-intensity is often the moat. The exact reason fewer competitors enter atoms-side categories is the capital requirement — which is why the competition is lower and the returns to a durable win are higher. The preference for "capital-light" businesses is the preference most investors have expressed, which is why those markets are over-subscribed.

**Don't:** Treat regulatory capture as permanent.
**Why:** Regulatory apparatuses are constructed and can be dismantled. The cost of getting a drug to market is high but not infinite; regulatory reform is possible; alternative jurisdictions exist. A thesis that accepts the current regulatory structure as an unchangeable fact is too pessimistic; a thesis that ignores it is too naive. The productive position is to know what regulation actually requires and build a plan that can navigate or shift it.

**Don't:** Let the cultural drift toward interiority excuse you from building.
**Why:** "The mind is its own place" is the Miltonic temptation. Therapy, meditation, psychedelics, the whole apparatus of modern interiority — these are fine for individuals but dangerous if they substitute for building. The work of civilization is external; it is new technology in the material world; it is flying cars, not enlightenment-about-the-absence-of-flying-cars.

**Don't:** Confuse optimism about the past (we invented the iPhone!) with optimism about the future.
**Why:** The fact that bits compounded for two decades tells us nothing about whether bits will continue to compound or whether atoms will join. Past optimism can produce present complacency; present complacency produces future stagnation. Definite optimism about the future is specific people committing to specific projects — not a general confidence that progress will "work out."
