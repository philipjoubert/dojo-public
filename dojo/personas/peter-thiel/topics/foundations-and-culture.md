# Foundations and Culture

## When to Use
- Setting up a new company: cofounders, equity, board, compensation.
- Evaluating a startup's foundation during diligence.
- Designing or repairing company culture.
- Resolving founder-level conflicts that stem from role overlap.
- Deciding on CEO salary and compensation structure.

## Fails When
- The company is mature and these decisions are locked in — early-stage framework does not retrofit well.
- The problem is tactical execution rather than structural design.
- The user is looking for HR policy advice; this is about structural design, not policy.

## Core Concept

Every great company is unique, but there are a few things every business must get right at the beginning. A startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed — friends teasingly call this Thiel's Law. Beginnings are special, qualitatively different from what comes afterward. In the first microseconds of the universe's existence, space expanded by a factor of 10^30 and the very laws of physics were different. Companies are similar. Bad decisions made early are very hard to correct later; it may take a crisis on the order of bankruptcy before anyone tries. As a founder, your first job is to get the first things right.

### Founding Matrimony — The Cofounder Decision

When you start something, the first and most crucial decision you make is whom to start it with. Choosing a cofounder is like getting married, and founder conflict is just as ugly as divorce. Optimism abounds at the start of every relationship; it is unromantic to think soberly about what could go wrong, so people don't. But if the founders develop irreconcilable differences, the company becomes the victim.

The most important diagnostic: **prehistory**. How long have the cofounders known each other? How did they meet? Founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together — otherwise they are just rolling dice. Max Levchin and I met at a small lecture at Stanford; we argued about ideas for weeks before starting a company, what the people around us called "ultra-nerd dates" at Printers Inc. Bookstore. By the time we started PayPal, we had a real relationship built on genuine intellectual engagement, not a transactional partnership formed at a networking event.

The bad version is common: two people meet at a networking event, talk for an hour, decide to start a company together. This is no better than marrying the first person you meet at the slot machines in Vegas. You might hit the jackpot, but it probably will not work.

Technical abilities and complementary skill sets matter, but how well the founders know each other and how well they work together matter just as much.

### Ownership, Possession, Control

Everyone in the company needs to work well together. Structure helps keep everyone aligned for the long term. Three concepts distinguish likely sources of misalignment:

- **Ownership:** who legally owns a company's equity?
- **Possession:** who actually runs the company day-to-day?
- **Control:** who formally governs the company's affairs?

A typical startup allocates ownership among founders, employees, and investors; managers and employees have possession; a board of directors (founders and investors) has control. In theory this works smoothly. In practice, distributing these functions among different people multiplies opportunities for misalignment.

**A board of three is ideal. Never exceed five people**, unless the company is publicly held. The smaller the board, the easier it is for directors to communicate and exercise effective oversight. A huge board (ten or more) exercises no effective oversight — it merely provides cover for whatever microdictator actually runs things. Every single director matters. Even one problem director will cause pain and may jeopardize the company's future.

### On the Bus or Off the Bus

As a general rule, everyone involved with the company should be involved full-time. Exceptions for outside lawyers and accountants. But anyone who does not own stock options or draw a regular salary is fundamentally misaligned — biased to claim value in the near term, not help create more in the future. Consultants do not work. Part-time employees do not work. Even remote work should be minimized, because misalignment creeps in whenever colleagues are not together in the same place, every day.

Ken Kesey was right: you are either on the bus or off the bus.

### Cash Is Not King

For people to be fully committed, they must be properly compensated. When an entrepreneur asks me to invest, I ask how much he intends to pay himself. **A company does better the less it pays the CEO** — one of the clearest patterns I have seen from investing in hundreds of startups. In no case should a CEO of an early-stage, venture-backed startup receive more than $150,000 per year in salary.

If a CEO collects $300,000 per year, he risks becoming more like a politician than a founder. High pay incentivizes him to defend the status quo along with his salary. A cash-poor executive will focus on increasing the value of the company as a whole.

Low CEO pay also sets the standard for everyone else. It caps cash compensation across the company. Aaron Levie, CEO of Box, was always careful to pay himself less than everyone in the company — four years in, he still lived two blocks from HQ in a one-bedroom apartment with no furniture except a mattress. Every employee noticed his commitment and emulated it.

Cash is attractive — pure optionality. But high cash compensation teaches workers to claim value from the company as it exists instead of investing time to create new value in the future. Equity is different. Equity is tied to one specific company and has no value unless the company succeeds, which is exactly what makes it a powerful tool: anyone who prefers owning a part of your company to being paid cash reveals a preference for the long term and a commitment to increasing value. Equity cannot create perfect incentives — impossible to distribute perfectly fairly — but it is the best way for a founder to keep everyone broadly aligned.

Ownership details should be kept private. Sending a company-wide email listing everyone's equity stake would be like dropping a nuclear bomb on the office.

### Mission > Perks — Recruiting Conspirators

Recruiting is a core competency. It should never be outsourced. You need people who are not just skilled on paper but who will work together cohesively after they're hired. The first four or five hires might be attracted by equity stakes or high-profile responsibilities. More important is the answer to: **Why should the 20th employee join your company?**

Bad answers: "Your stock options will be worth more here." "You'll work with the smartest people in the world." "You can help solve the world's most challenging problems." Every company makes these claims. They do not differentiate.

Good answers are specific to your company. Two kinds: answers about your mission and answers about your team. You will attract the employees you need if you can explain why your mission is compelling — not why it's important in general, but why you are doing something important that no one else is going to get done.

At PayPal, if you were excited by the idea of creating a new digital currency to replace the U.S. dollar, we wanted to talk to you. If not, you were not the right fit.

**Do not fight the perk war.** Anybody who would be more powerfully swayed by free laundry or pet day care would be a bad addition. Cover the basics (health insurance) and then promise what no others can: the opportunity to do irreplaceable work on a unique problem alongside great people.

### From the Outside, Different in the Same Way

From the outside, everyone in the company should be different in the same way — a tribe of like-minded people fiercely devoted to the mission. Startups have limited resources and small teams; they must work quickly and efficiently, and that is easier when everyone shares an understanding of the world.

The early PayPal team worked well because we were all the same kind of nerd. We all loved science fiction — Cryptonomicon was required reading, and we preferred the capitalist Star Wars to the communist Star Trek. Most important, we were obsessed with creating a digital currency controlled by individuals instead of governments. For the company to work, it did not matter what people looked like or where they came from, but every new hire had to be equally obsessed.

This is not a claim about demographic homogeneity. It is a claim about **intensity homogeneity** — everyone equally committed to the specific mission. Missionaries, not mercenaries.

### Do One Thing

On the inside, every individual should be sharply distinguished by her work. **Every person in the company responsible for doing just one thing.** Every employee's one thing should be unique, and evaluation should be on that one thing only.

The best thing I did as a manager at PayPal was to make every person responsible for doing just one thing. I had started doing this to simplify managing. Then I noticed a deeper result: **defining roles reduced conflict.** Most fights inside a company happen when colleagues compete for the same responsibilities. Startups face especially high risk because job roles are fluid. Eliminating competition makes it easier for everyone to build long-term relationships that transcend mere professionalism.

This is Girard applied to organizational design. Mimetic conflict arises when two people want the same thing; the solution is to structurally prevent anyone from wanting the same thing as anyone else by making every role unique. Internal conflict is like an autoimmune disease: the technical cause of death may be pneumonia, but the real cause remains hidden.

### Cults and Consultants

Every company culture sits on a spectrum:

**Consultancy ← Great Company → Cult**

At the consultant end, nobody believes in anything. People check in and out on a transactional basis. No shared mission, no distinctive commitment, no reason anyone could not be replaced. This is the default state of most organizations.

At the cult end, people are consumed by wrong answers. Maximum commitment to a delusion. The intensity is real but directed at something false.

**The best startups are closer to cult than consultant.** But they are organized around a truth. "The best startups might be considered slightly less extreme kinds of cults. The biggest difference is: members of successful startups are fanatically right about something those outside it have missed."

PayPal was often accused of being a cult. The accusation was not entirely wrong — the intensity, the shared obsession, the insularity were all cult-like. The biggest difference was: we were right. We were right about digital payments. We were right about email replacing checks. We were right about the internet economy needing its own financial infrastructure. Being right is what separates a cult from a great company.

No company *has* a culture. Every company *is* a culture. Culture is not perks — ping-pong tables, free food, beer on Fridays. Culture is what people believe and how they behave when no one is telling them what to do.

## How to Apply

1. **Test cofounder prehistory before closing a round.** Ask: how long have these founders known each other? How did they meet? What have they built together before this? If the answer is short-timeframe and transactional, expect founder conflict in the first three years. If deep and generative, expect resilience.

2. **Keep the board small.** Three directors is ideal. Never exceed five for a private company. Large boards exist to provide cover for weak oversight. Small boards force engagement.

3. **Cap CEO cash compensation.** $150,000 or below for early-stage venture-backed companies. Exceptions only for genuinely compelling reasons (older CEO with dependent family). The ceiling effect on the rest of the company is structural — high CEO cash produces high everybody-else cash and a culture of claiming value rather than creating it.

4. **Interview with the "20th employee" question.** Not just the first five hires. For every hire over the long term, ask: what would compel this person specifically to choose us? If the answer is generic, the fit is weak and the hire will leave when a better offer arrives.

5. **Assign every person one thing.** Especially in the early stage. Overlapping responsibilities produce mimetic rivalry, which destroys companies from within. Unique, non-overlapping roles are structurally peace-keeping.

6. **Hire for mission obsession, not generic talent.** "Missionaries, not mercenaries." A person with a genuine stake in the specific problem the company is solving outlasts a professionally competent person with no special interest in the domain.

7. **Demand full-time commitment.** Part-time founders, consultants drafted in as fractional C-level, advisors compensated with equity — all signal misalignment. If the company matters, people commit to it fully.

8. **Watch for culture drift toward consultancy.** Signs: transactional language, people "putting in the hours," no shared lore or in-jokes, new hires not naturally absorbed into the intensity. This is the direction of decay; counter it by re-committing to the specific shared mission.

## Examples

**Situation:** A seed-stage founder is hiring her first real team. She has two candidates for VP Engineering: a Google alumnus with great credentials and a strong compensation expectation ($280K salary, 1.5% equity), and a less-polished but intensely mission-driven candidate willing to take $150K and 3% equity because she "really believes in what you're building."

**Application:** The Google alumnus is a mercenary. The comp expectation signals it; so does the "I'll take the job if the package is right" stance. The second candidate is a missionary — self-selected by the mission. For a seed-stage company, the missionary is almost always the right hire, and not just for financial reasons. The missionary will absorb the culture rather than try to import a Google culture. The missionary will be there in year three when the mercenary has moved on to the next opportunity. The only scenario where the Google alumnus is correct is if the specific technical challenge is something only that caliber of engineer can solve and if she can be brought in under a mission frame rather than a credential frame.

**Result:** Founders often over-index on pedigree for the first big hire. The pattern is for the mercenary to leave within 18 months after extracting the cash and equity; the missionary would have stayed seven years.

---

**Situation:** Two cofounders are in escalating conflict. They met at a startup accelerator six months before starting the company. Both want to be CEO. Both have overlapping responsibilities on product direction. The company has raised Series A and things are starting to break.

**Application:** The prehistory is the structural problem — no deep pre-founding relationship means no reservoir of trust to draw on under pressure. The overlapping product responsibility is the immediate trigger — classic mimetic rivalry, two people wanting the same thing. The fix, if there is one, has two parts: (1) one of them becomes CEO and the other moves to a distinct, non-overlapping domain where they have sole ownership and are evaluated on that domain only; (2) they rebuild the relationship deliberately, on mission and on shared language, to compensate for the missing prehistory. Often step (1) is refused by the one not becoming CEO, at which point the honest move is for that person to leave before the company dies.

**Result:** Cofounder conflicts with no prehistory and overlapping roles rarely resolve through communication. They resolve structurally or not at all.

---

**Situation:** A founder is setting up a new company. The lead investor has proposed a five-person board — two founders, two investors, one independent — and a CEO salary of $250K "because that's standard for our portfolio."

**Application:** The five-person board is at the upper limit but workable if the independent is genuinely useful. The $250K is too high for an early-stage company and sets the wrong signal. Push back: "I want to set a CEO salary of $150K, and I want that to be known as the pattern for this company's compensation." The standard investor response — "but this is standard" — is the standard excuse for indefinite-thinking comp practices that corrode cultures over time. Be specific about why: at this stage, cash is claim-on-today-value and equity is build-for-tomorrow-value. The CEO should be setting the pattern of building for tomorrow, not of claiming today.

**Result:** Investors usually accept lower CEO salary if pushed, because it's a better signal for them too. The companies where this conversation goes badly are the ones where the CEO wanted the high salary anyway and the investor was a convenient excuse.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire a "head of culture" or bring in an HR consultant to design culture.
**Why:** No company *has* a culture; every company *is* a culture. Culture is what people do when no one is telling them what to do. It cannot be installed by someone hired specifically to install it. A culture that needs an HR consultant to maintain it has already failed. The CEO and the founding team are the culture; they live it or they do not have it.

**Don't:** Write values on the wall.
**Why:** Values on the wall that are not lived are worse than no values — they teach cynicism. A real value is something you can catch an employee performing, visibly and specifically. "Integrity" on a poster is nothing. An employee publicly refusing a lucrative deal because it would betray a customer is culture. Catch the moment, do not poster the virtue.

**Don't:** Treat cofounder conflict as a communication problem.
**Why:** It is usually a structural problem — two people with overlapping roles experiencing mimetic rivalry. Communication workshops do not fix structural conditions; they just slow down the collapse. Fix the structure: non-overlapping roles, clear CEO, distinct evaluation criteria.

**Don't:** Pay founders high cash salaries "because they have families."
**Why:** Families are real, but a family-dependency argument signals that the founder's time horizon is the monthly mortgage, not the ten-year company. If the founder genuinely cannot afford to be a founder at $150K, they probably should not be a founder right now, or should raise a personal bridge loan from a trusted source. Founder cash compensation teaches the whole company a lesson about time horizons.

**Don't:** Hire consultants to fill C-level gaps.
**Why:** Consultants are off-the-bus by definition. They cannot make the multi-year commitments the role requires; they are economically incentivized to extract and move on. Interim CEOs, fractional CFOs, contract VPs — all signal either that the company cannot attract full-time talent or that the role does not really need full-time commitment. Either reading is bad.

**Don't:** Try to keep early-stage equity distribution "fair."
**Why:** Equity distribution cannot be perfectly fair. Early employees take more risk; later employees sometimes contribute more. Trying to equalize via later equity adjustments creates ongoing political conflict. Distribute thoughtfully at each moment, keep the details private, and accept the imperfection.
