# Mimetic Theory

## When to Use
- The conversation involves a sector that is suddenly "hot" without a clear fundamental reason.
- A founder is considering a path because the people they respect are all on it.
- Internal company conflict is between people with similar roles, ambitions, or scope — not over strategy.
- A career choice feels like "the next logical step" but the logic is purely social.
- Someone wants to know whether their conviction is their own or borrowed.

## Fails When
- The question is about genuinely substantive disagreement among informed people. Mimetic theory explains certain kinds of conflict; it does not explain all of them.
- Two people actually do want different things and are fighting about the difference. That is the Marxist case, not the Shakespearean one. Rare but real.
- A "consensus" among experts who independently reached the same conclusion from different starting points. Convergence is not always imitation.

## Core Concept

There are two models for understanding why people fight. Marx said people fight because they are different — workers versus owners, each pursuing opposing interests. Shakespeare said the opposite. In *Romeo and Juliet*, the Montagues and Capulets are not fighting because they are different; they are fighting because they are the same. "Two households, both alike in dignity." The play never explains the origin of the feud because the origin does not matter. The families mirror each other so precisely that any distinction between them has collapsed. The similarity is the source of the violence.

René Girard, who taught at Stanford for decades and whom I count as one of the great intellectuals of the twentieth century, built a theory of human behavior on this insight. The theory has one core claim: human beings do not know what they want. We are not the autonomous, self-directed agents Enlightenment philosophy promised. We copy the desires of others. We want things because other people want them. The word "ape" in Shakespeare's time already meant both primate and to imitate. We are apes in both senses.

This is not a metaphor. It is the literal mechanism driving markets, careers, and conflicts. When you see a long line outside a restaurant, you want to eat there. When a colleague pursues a promotion, you suddenly want it too. When everyone in your peer group applies to the same graduate program, that program becomes irresistible — not because you independently evaluated it, but because desire is contagious. We catch desires the way we catch colds: from proximity to others who already have them.

The implications are severe. If desire is imitative, then most of what we call "ambition" is actually conformity wearing a mask. The person who wants to be a venture capitalist because other successful people are venture capitalists is not pursuing an authentic goal — they are performing someone else's desire without knowing it. Most of what passes for independent judgment in business is clustered imitation dressed up as analysis. The evidence is visible every cycle: the largest Harvard Business School cohorts went into junk bonds around 1989, dotcoms in 1999, housing and private equity around 2005-2007. These are among the most credentialed people in the world, and they end up in the hothouse environment for two years where social dynamics determine career choices — at the end, the largest cohort consistently does precisely the wrong thing at precisely the wrong time.

Girard distinguished between good mimesis and bad mimesis. Learning from a mentor, absorbing the techniques of a master craftsman, modeling yourself on people who have built things worth building — these are productive forms of imitation. The problem arises when imitation turns competitive, when the model becomes the rival, when two people want the same thing and neither can have it without defeating the other. Mimetic desire becomes mimetic rivalry, and rivalry becomes destruction.

The clearest business case of mimetic rivalry in recent memory is the war between Microsoft and Google in the 2000s. At the start, each company was content to leave the other alone. Microsoft dominated desktop software; Google dominated search. They were different businesses serving different needs. Then they started looking at each other. Google launched Chrome OS, a direct attack on Windows. Microsoft launched Bing, a direct attack on Google Search. Google built Docs. Microsoft launched Edge. Each company became obsessed with the other. Each move provoked a counter-move. Two houses alike in dignity, destroying each other while Apple — which came from a completely different place — overtook them both. By the time Microsoft and Google realized what had happened, Apple had become the most valuable company in the world.

The same pattern inside companies: the worst internal conflicts are not between people who disagree about strategy. They are between people who want the same thing. Two executives competing for the same promotion. Two engineers fighting over the same project. Two teams claiming credit for the same outcome. The standard account of organizational conflict says it arises from differences. Mimetic theory says the opposite: conflict arises when people fight over the same thing, and the more similar the combatants, the more vicious the fight.

Bubbles are mimetic phenomena operating at scale. The dot-com bubble was not produced by millions of independent analyses converging on the same conclusion. It was produced by imitation. One investor funded a dot-com and made money. Other investors copied. Entrepreneurs saw the funding and started more dot-coms. Journalists covered the trend, which attracted more investors. The entire cycle was powered by copying, not by thought. The practical difficulty is that mimetic contagion feels like insight. The investor who follows the herd into a hot sector does not experience himself as a sheep — he experiences himself as someone who has independently recognized a great opportunity. The self-deception is total. Girard's theory predicts exactly this: the mechanism of imitative desire is invisible to the person experiencing it. We copy and believe we chose.

If desire is fundamentally imitative, then the most important skill in business and in life is the ability to resist imitation. This is what the contrarian question — *what important truth do very few people agree with you on?* — really tests. The question is not about being contrarian as a pose. It is about whether you have done the work of thinking for yourself. Most people cannot answer because their beliefs are borrowed, not built.

Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius. Many people can identify contrarian truths in private conversation. Few will act on them. The social cost of being wrong and alone is much higher than the social cost of being wrong with everyone else. If you make a conventional bet and it fails, nobody blames you — everyone made the same mistake. If you make a contrarian bet and it fails, you look like a fool. This asymmetry of social punishment is why most people default to convention even when they suspect convention is wrong.

There is a reason so many successful technology founders seem to have mild Asperger's traits. It is not a coincidence. People who are less susceptible to social pressure — who do not automatically calibrate their views to the room — are more likely to hold genuinely independent opinions. Most of us are talked out of our most original creative ideas before they are even fully formed. We pick up on subtle cues from the people around us, we sense that an idea is a little bit too weird, a little bit too strange, and we abandon it before we have tested it. Social dynamics murder originality in the cradle.

## How to Apply

1. **Ask "who else wants this?" before acting on a desire.** When you notice you want something — a job, a market, a deal, an acquisition — check whether the desire predates your awareness that others wanted it. If the desire only appeared after you noticed other people chasing the same thing, treat it as a mimetic signal, not a signal about the thing itself.

2. **Treat consensus enthusiasm as a red flag for fundamentals.** When a sector is suddenly hot and every smart person is there, the fundamentals are almost certainly degraded. The HBS cohort pattern is nearly mechanical: the largest cohort is wrong, in the same direction, at the same time. If the field looks obvious and exciting, be suspicious in proportion to the obviousness.

3. **Design out the conditions that produce mimetic conflict inside companies.** Do not wait for rivalry to appear and then manage it. Assign each employee a single unique responsibility — one thing, theirs alone, on which they will be evaluated. Overlapping domains produce rivalry because they produce structural conditions for it. Distinct domains do not. This is the PayPal "one thing" rule; it is Girard applied to org design.

4. **Ask "are these people like me or are they my models?"** Mimetic rivalry is most destructive between people in the same reference group — similar age, role, scope, status. If you find yourself in intense conflict with someone in your own bracket, suspect the conflict is about structural similarity, not about substance. If you find yourself admiring someone well above your bracket, you can learn from them without the rivalry dynamic.

5. **Watch for the bubble preconditions.** Public involvement is a near-necessary precondition. In 1999, roughly 300 tech companies went public per year — that was a bubble. In 2014, roughly 30 did — that was not. The presence or absence of the general public is a crude but useful indicator of whether a mania has moved from professional enthusiasm into mimetic saturation.

6. **Do the "dinner party test."** If every person at a dinner party gives the same opinion about an investment thesis, that is not convergent analysis. That is social contagion. Ask them each separately how they arrived at the view. If the provenance traces back through the same two or three sources, you are looking at imitation dressed as independent judgment.

7. **Resist the pull to normalize yourself.** Most originality dies not from refutation but from social cue. You sense an idea is a little too strange and you abandon it. The practice is to finish the weird thought, write it down, test it against evidence — and let the social discomfort happen without acting on it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A junior VC tells you every deal in her pipeline is an "AI for vertical X" company — AI for legal, AI for construction, AI for healthcare, AI for sales, AI for manufacturing. She feels pressure to fund several because "if we don't, we'll miss the wave."

**Application:** The pattern is textbook mimetic contagion. Every smart fund is looking at the same dozen categories with the same pitch template, which means the prices are bid up, the companies are substantively similar, and the returns will be disappointing. The move is not to pick the "best" AI-for-X — they are mostly the same company. The move is to ask which problem the AI-for-X consensus is missing, or to ask which of these companies has a genuine secret the AI framing conceals rather than reveals. A good test: ask the founder to describe the business without saying "AI." If they cannot, the AI framing is doing the work of a buzzword, and the business underneath may not exist.

**Result:** Most AI-for-X companies fade into the next cycle's footnote. The few that survive tend to be ones whose secret was not the AI layer at all but a specific insight about the vertical that happened to use AI as one of its tools.

**Situation:** Two senior engineers at a Series B company are in escalating conflict. Both believe the other is getting undue credit for the same critical feature. The CEO is mediating weekly. HR has proposed a team-building offsite.

**Application:** The offsite will not help. The conflict is structural — two people in nearly identical positions with overlapping responsibility for the same outcome. Girard's diagnosis is that the similarity is the cause; no amount of communication training fixes it. The fix is structural. One person owns the feature end-to-end. The other moves to a distinct domain where evaluation is independent. If neither can accept a distinct domain, one of them should leave. Demotion into an ambiguous "associate" role does not work because the original rivalry conditions remain. The test: after the restructuring, is it possible to say what each person is uniquely responsible for, in a way the other person cannot claim?

**Result:** Structural separation resolves most "personality clashes" between peers. The personalities usually turn out to be fine once the rivalry fuel is removed.

---

**Situation:** A thoughtful 27-year-old is considering leaving a promising startup role to go to business school "to keep my options open." Her mentors are enthusiastic. Her parents are thrilled. Most of her peers are taking the GMAT.

**Application:** The mimetic diagnostic is to ask whether the desire predates the social signal. If she wanted to go to business school before noticing her peers were going, that is some evidence of an independent desire. If the desire appeared after she noticed, it is almost entirely mimetic — an insurance policy against falling through cracks, wrapped in the language of "options." The deeper question: what specifically would be different about her life if she did not go? If she cannot name a specific thing business school would teach her that she cannot learn by staying in the job, the decision is a social ritual, not a strategy. The power law tells her one career move will matter more than all others combined, which makes "keeping options open" exactly the wrong frame.

**Result:** The honest answer is usually that business school is the indefinite-optimism move — the thing you do when you do not yet know what you want. Occasionally that is genuinely the right answer, for people who need structured time to think. Far more often it is the thing you do because other people did it.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat being contrarian as an identity.
**Why:** The goal is not opposition for its own sake. The goal is independent thought. Sometimes the crowd is right. Reflexive disagreement is as mimetic as reflexive agreement — both take their cue from what others believe. The discipline is to think about the object, not about the crowd's position on the object.

**Don't:** Confuse divergent conclusions reached independently with mimetic consensus.
**Why:** Not every alignment is imitation. If twenty infectious-disease specialists independently conclude that a new pathogen is dangerous, that is convergence on evidence, not social contagion. The mimetic diagnostic applies when people have adopted a belief without the evidence that would produce it independently. Check for the presence or absence of independent reasoning before calling something mimetic.

**Don't:** Use "we're different" as a claim without substance.
**Why:** Every restaurant in Palo Alto thinks it is different. Every failing startup thinks its pivot is original. The mimetic test is whether the difference is substantive enough that a customer or investor can tell it apart from the competitors in a blind comparison. Self-perceived uniqueness is nearly worthless. Structural uniqueness is everything.

**Don't:** Treat "originality" as a matter of aesthetics.
**Why:** Original people often look unoriginal — plain clothes, quiet voices, boring hobbies. Deliberately weird appearance (the Richard Branson hair) is often a performance of originality, not originality itself. The signal is the specificity of the person's beliefs, not their visual branding. A suit can hide an original mind; kiteboarding with supermodels can conceal a conventional one.

**Don't:** Mistake proximity for alignment.
**Why:** Just because two companies are in the same "space" does not mean they should fight. Microsoft and Google were in different businesses for years and became mirror images only once they each started reacting to the other. Proximity invites rivalry but does not require it. The discipline is to keep building what is specific to your company, regardless of what the nearby company is doing.
