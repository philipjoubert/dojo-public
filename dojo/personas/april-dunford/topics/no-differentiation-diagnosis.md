# The "No Differentiation" Diagnosis

## When to Use
- An executive on the team insists the product has no differentiation, but the company has real customers, real revenue, and meaningful growth.
- Sales is frustrated and saying "it's impossible to sell because there's nothing different about us."
- Marketing and product disagree about whether the product is differentiated.
- You need to diagnose the situation before starting a positioning exercise — otherwise you'll be working from the wrong premise.

## Fails When
- **The product truly has no differentiation.** If you've honestly worked through the diagnostic and the answer is "yes, we deliver nothing the alternatives don't," then positioning will not help. The company needs to go build differentiation — via product features, pricing, partnerships, services, or business model — before any positioning work pays off.
- **The team won't interview happy customers.** You cannot diagnose this from the inside. You have to hear from the people who actually bought, why they bought, and what they almost bought instead. Without that data, every diagnosis is a guess.

## Core Concept

When a team tells me "we have no differentiation," one of two things is true: either (A) the product really is undifferentiated and positioning can't save it, or (B) the product *is* differentiated but some part of the team has gone blind to it. In my experience, for a company that's already doing real revenue, option B is way more common than option A.

I've sat in rooms where the CMO or head of sales insisted the product was impossible to differentiate, and the company was doing $80M in revenue and growing 35% a year. Those facts don't add up. If the product were genuinely indistinguishable from the alternatives, you wouldn't be winning deals. You wouldn't be growing. Customers would be picking the alternatives and you'd be dying quietly. If the business is actually working, then by definition, somebody is choosing you over the alternatives — and there's a reason.

So the first move is to refuse the "no differentiation" framing and diagnose what's actually going on. Six common root causes:

**1. Value blindness.** Executives — especially new ones coming from adjacent markets — sometimes assume they understand your customers when they don't. A new CMO who sold into the same category at a different company may assume the buyer behaves the same way here. Usually not true. Value blindness shows up as "I've seen this market; I know what customers value." The cure is a lot of direct customer contact: sales calls, customer research, maintaining a personal set of customers to sanity-check assumptions against.

**2. Product illiteracy.** Some products are technically deep enough that you have to understand the technology to see what the differentiation unlocks. Sales and marketing teams sometimes conclude a new feature "isn't much different from the old one" because they don't fully understand what it does. Product managers and product marketers are supposed to close this gap by explaining the customer value of advanced features. When they don't, the go-to-market side of the house concludes there's no differentiation — while the product team is stunned that their work is being invisibilized.

**3. Inside-out competitive perspective.** The product team tracks a wide set of competitors, including ones customers have never heard of. When someone in the company notices a competitor has a comparable feature list, they conclude "there's no differentiation." But that competitor is a ghost — they don't show up on real shortlists. Differentiation lives in the minds of the customers who are actually making choices. A competitor who never lands in a real deal does not count as differentiation pressure.

The related point: **a unique combination of value points counts as differentiation, even if individual points aren't unique.** You might have one competitor who's faster to deploy and another who's cheaper. You deliver both fast deployment AND strong cost savings in one product. That combination is the differentiation. Nobody else delivers both.

**4. Loss obsession, win ignorance.** Teams spend a lot of energy studying why deals are lost and almost none studying why deals are won. Loss analysis often fixates on big deals that went sideways — which are frequently bad-fit deals that never should have been worked in the first place. Meanwhile, the wins — which show exactly why customers pick you and what you should amplify — go unstudied. The result: a team that believes the product is always losing when it's actually winning consistently with good-fit accounts. Win analysis is more important than loss analysis. Study the wins.

**5. Pernicious product pessimism.** Some product management teams become unreasonably pessimistic about their own product relative to competitors. They track too many "alternatives," pull the team into no-win deals with bad-fit prospects, do deep loss analysis without win analysis, and build catchup-oriented roadmaps that ignore why customers actually choose the product today. Left unchallenged, this pessimism infects sales and marketing until half the company is convinced the product is behind — while the business is still winning and growing. The cure is getting the product team exposed to winning situations so they see the real picture.

**6. Sloppy segmentation.** When a team tries to sell to everyone, differentiation goes invisible — because features are context-sensitive. A feature that's a weakness in one market segment is a strength in another. The IBM product with 52 items in a drop-down menu that we mocked as "terrible UX" at my startup turned out to be the exact thing Fortune 100 CIOs loved — they saw it as "incredible cross-enterprise flexibility." Same feature. Completely different value depending on the segment. If you don't know who you're selling to, you can't see what's differentiated.

Once you've diagnosed the root cause, the work is usually *recovering* visibility on the differentiation that's already there — not inventing new differentiation. Run the positioning exercise with good customer data and you'll almost always find the differentiation the team had gone blind to.

## How to Apply

1. **Refuse the "no differentiation" framing until you've diagnosed it.** If the business is growing or has real customers, default to "differentiation exists but we've lost sight of it." Make the team prove otherwise before you accept the premise.

2. **Interview the happiest customers.** Who are they? Why did they pick you? What did they almost buy instead? What would they lose if you disappeared tomorrow? Their answers will almost always reveal the differentiation that someone on your team has gone blind to.

3. **Pull win/loss data. Study wins first.** What's the pattern of deals you win? What do winning customers have in common? What alternatives did they seriously consider before picking you? That data — not the loss postmortems — tells you what's differentiating.

4. **Work through the six root causes as a checklist.** Value blindness? Product illiteracy? Ghost competitors? Loss obsession? Product pessimism? Loose segmentation? Usually at least one or two apply. Identify which ones and name them. Sometimes just naming the pattern breaks the spell.

5. **Check the unique combination of value.** Even if no single value point is unique, ask: what combination of value points can we deliver that no single alternative can? Combination differentiation is real differentiation, but teams often miss it because each piece alone feels unexciting.

6. **If after all that, the product genuinely is undifferentiated — accept it and change the product.** Positioning is not a band-aid for a weak product. If you've honestly worked the diagnostic and there's no differentiation, that's a signal to go build some — via new features, pricing strategy, partnerships, packaging, or services wrapped around the product. Positioning is about making differentiation obvious to buyers. If there's no differentiation, there's nothing to make obvious.

## Examples

**Situation:** A $80M company growing 35% YoY has a new CMO who says "we have no differentiation."
**Application:** Growing at 35% on $80M means a lot of customers are choosing this company over the alternatives. If there were no differentiation, that wouldn't be happening. Run the diagnostic. Usually the new CMO has a form of value blindness — they came from an adjacent market and assume buyers behave the same way. Fix it by getting the CMO on lots of sales calls and customer interviews for the first 90 days, and by running a positioning workshop that foregrounds why customers actually pick the product.
**Result:** The team rediscovers the differentiation that's been there all along. The "no differentiation" claim dies. Messaging sharpens because the team finally names what was implicit.

**Situation:** Sales team insists "everyone has the same features."
**Application:** Go to product. Have product walk sales through what the advanced features actually do and the value they unlock. Often the sales team was right about the surface feature list — both products have "X" — but wrong about what X actually delivers. The product team closes the gap and suddenly sales can articulate why their X is different.
**Result:** Sales gains a story about the product's technical depth that they couldn't construct themselves. The "no differentiation" claim was actually product illiteracy.

**Situation:** A team is obsessed with a competitor that none of their prospects have ever actually evaluated.
**Application:** That competitor is a ghost. You're positioning against them in your head, but buyers aren't comparing you to them. Drop them from the competitive set. Focus on the alternatives that actually appear in real deals. Your differentiation becomes visible once you stop comparing yourself to a company buyers don't know exists.
**Result:** The team refocuses on real alternatives. The "we have no differentiation" feeling evaporates because they were comparing to a fantasy competitor who was better-than-them on paper. Against real alternatives, the product is fine.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept "we have no differentiation" as a premise without diagnosing it.
**Why:** In growing B2B companies, this claim is almost always a perception problem inside the company, not a reality of the product. Taking it at face value and running a positioning exercise from that premise will produce a weak pitch because the team will be unable to articulate anything. Push back. Diagnose first.

**Don't:** Try to position an undifferentiated product with clever messaging.
**Why:** Customers aren't stupid. In B2B especially, buyers have to justify choices to procurement, finance, and their boss. They can't pick a product with no reason. Clever messaging without real differentiation fails at the decision-making stage. Fix the product, the pricing, the packaging, or the services — not the adjectives on the homepage.

**Don't:** Obsess over why deals were lost. Study why deals were won.
**Why:** Lost deals are often bad-fit deals where the product was never going to win. Won deals show the pattern of where your value genuinely matters. If you only study losses, you'll build positioning oriented toward catching up; if you study wins, you'll build positioning oriented toward amplifying what actually works.

**Don't:** Track every competitor your product team can find.
**Why:** Most of them never land in real deals. Tracking them creates the illusion of a crowded, undifferentiated market. Focus on the alternatives customers actually consider. The market is much less crowded when you only count the companies your buyers have heard of.

**Don't:** Sell to "everyone" in the name of TAM.
**Why:** Features are context-sensitive. Something that's a differentiator in one segment is irrelevant (or a negative) in another. If you try to sell to everyone, you'll see differentiation that's real for a specific segment and dismiss it because it doesn't matter broadly. Narrow the segment, and the differentiation snaps into focus.
