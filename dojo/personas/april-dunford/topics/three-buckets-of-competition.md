# Three Buckets of Competition

## When to Use
- Your win/loss analysis shows most losses are to "no decision." That's not a loss to nobody — it's a loss to indecision, and it's a distinct diagnostic signal.
- The sales team is obsessed with named competitors and ignores the spreadsheet the buyer is using today. Status quo is the biggest competitor most B2B companies have.
- You're building positioning and realize you've only mapped the shortlist alternatives — you've missed the status quo and the indecision case entirely.
- You're diagnosing slow sales cycles: deals enter evaluation and then freeze. That's usually a no-decision problem, which has its own playbook.

## Fails When
- **You collapse "status quo" and "no decision" into a single bucket.** They look similar — both result in the prospect not buying anything new — but they need different countermoves. Status quo is a deliberate choice to stick with what they have; no decision is an indecisive abandonment of the purchase. Lumping them together means you'll apply the wrong move to the wrong loss.
- **You treat no-decision as a loss to nobody.** That's the label that lets the team off the hook. It's actually a loss to fear — the prospect couldn't confidently decide, so they abandoned the process. Different problem, different fix.
- **Status-quo positioning becomes trashing the buyer's current process.** You're positioning against the spreadsheet the champion built last year. Trashing it insults the champion. Reduce the friction of change; don't mock the status quo.

## Core Concept

In B2B, customers always have alternatives to picking you. Three buckets, and you have to position against all three:

**Bucket 1 — Status Quo.** What the customer is doing today. Excel. Pen and paper. A manual process. An in-house tool somebody built. A feature inside a system they already own that wasn't designed for the job but is being used for it. The prospect is getting the job done today — maybe inefficiently, maybe painfully, but it's happening. Switching costs are real: budget is only part of it. Processes change, end users need training, new software has to integrate with existing systems and data. The more complex your solution, the more painful the change.

**Bucket 2 — Direct Shortlist Competitors.** Other vendors the buyer has explicitly put on their shortlist and is evaluating alongside you. B2B buyers don't buy in a vacuum — they do homework and build a shortlist. To win here, you have to prove your solution is not just valuable, but *more* valuable than the others. Critically: take a customer point of view in building the shortlist. If a competitor rarely or never lands on shortlists, they're a ghost — ignore them. When they start showing up, adjust.

**Bucket 3 — Do Nothing / No Decision.** Some teams mistake this for status quo, but the research says no. When customers opt not to move forward, most of the time they're *not* explicitly voting for the status quo. They've failed to confidently make a decision, and they've abandoned the purchase. Indecision, not preference.

**The 40-60% statistic.** Forty to sixty percent of B2B purchase processes end in no decision. That's the most-cited number in my work for a reason. It means a deal you think is a competitive bake-off against a couple of named vendors is actually a three-way contest between you, the competitor, and indecision — and indecision usually wins. Teams that only build positioning against Bucket 2 are fighting with one bucket of competition ignored. You can be the best in a shortlist of three and still lose because the buyer froze.

**Different buckets, different moves.**

For Bucket 1 (status quo): demonstrate the value they cannot achieve today (your differentiated value, quantified — "companies using our solution increase revenue by 30% over doing it manually"), and reduce the pain of change (migration tools, professional services, onboarding, phased rollout, breaking the deal into smaller pieces).

For Bucket 2 (shortlist): demonstrate your differentiated value — the value you can deliver that the other considered alternatives cannot. Not just "value you can deliver" — *differentiated* value. And ruthlessly disqualify prospects who don't care about the specific value only you can deliver. If they don't check those boxes, walk away. Chasing deals you won't win is a positioning tax on the deals you will.

For Bucket 3 (no decision): two sub-moves. First, increase the customer's confidence in their decision — teach them the pros and cons of different approaches for different types of companies (the Alternatives step of the sales pitch does this), help the champion handle the objections of the other stakeholders, and offer your direct recommendation. Matt Dixon's research in *The Jolt Effect* shows that win rates with indecisive buyers go up 144% when vendors proactively offer their opinion on what the prospect should do. Second, decrease deal risk — guarantees, chopping the deal into phases, tightly managed POCs (with up-front agreement on pass/fail criteria and timeframes).

## How to Apply

1. **Break every recent loss into the three buckets.** Don't let "no decision" hide in a single line on the win/loss report. Is this loss to status quo (they kept using Excel)? To a named competitor (they picked Gong)? Or to no decision (they froze)? The distribution tells you where the positioning work is needed.

2. **If losses skew to Bucket 1 (status quo), the positioning work is on quantified differentiated value + change management.** Sharpen the "here's what you cannot do today" claim with numbers. Then build the reduce-friction side: migration guides, onboarding, phased deployments.

3. **If losses skew to Bucket 2 (shortlist competitors), the positioning work is on *differentiated* value and disqualification.** Most teams position on "value" in general. Force the question: what can *we* deliver that *the other guys* on the shortlist can't? Disqualify prospects who don't care about that specific value.

4. **If losses skew to Bucket 3 (no decision), the positioning work is on buyer confidence.** This is usually the least-addressed bucket even though it's the biggest. Build the Alternatives step of the sales pitch to actively teach the buyer how to think about the market. Arm the champion to handle the objections of IT, Purchasing, Legal, Security. Offer the recommendation — don't be neutral. Reduce deal risk with guarantees, phased pricing, tightly-managed POCs.

5. **Build positioning against all three buckets explicitly.** The Competitive Alternatives component of the positioning canvas should include named status-quo alternatives ("track customers in a shared spreadsheet; bill through POS software") alongside shortlist competitors ("Zoho, HubSpot for customer tracking"). The sales pitch's Alternatives step should walk through the approaches — not just the named vendors.

6. **Separate the product team's competitor list from the sales team's shortlist reality.** Product tracks horizon threats. Sales knows what actually shows up in deals. For positioning, only the sales reality counts. Ghost competitors (ones only the product team knows about) don't go in the shortlist bucket.

7. **Re-check quarterly.** Status quo shifts as the market matures. Direct competitors emerge and die. The no-decision rate fluctuates with economic conditions. Don't treat the bucket breakdown as static.

## Examples

**Situation:** A sales enablement company's win/loss dashboard shows 30% of deals closed-lost go to "no decision."
**Application:** Don't just accept "no decision" as the diagnosis. Dig in: was the prospect indecisive (couldn't confidently pick), or did they actively choose to stick with what they had? If indecisive, the positioning work is on Bucket 3. Tighten the Alternatives step of the pitch to teach the market. Train reps to offer a direct recommendation at the end of the evaluation, not a neutral "let us know what you decide." Add risk-reduction: a 60-day pilot with defined success criteria.
**Result:** Win rate against indecisive buyers climbs meaningfully. The 30% "no decision" number drops, and the deals that would have frozen now move forward — some to wins, some to clean losses, both of which are better than perpetual limbo.

**Situation:** A pet-services software company's direct competitors are small pet-scheduling tools. But most of their losses are to "the owner is using a spreadsheet and doesn't think they need anything more."
**Application:** The real competitor isn't CanineCal or FurFlow. It's the spreadsheet — Bucket 1. Positioning has to make the cost of the status quo visible ("you're losing up to 15% of revenue you could recover with automated repeat-booking reminders") and reduce the pain of change ("3-week deployment, dedicated service rep, training included"). The Alternatives step of the pitch explicitly includes "track customers manually" as the first approach, with honest pros and cons.
**Result:** The pitch stops fighting the wrong battle. By making the status-quo cost explicit and the switch low-risk, deals that would have died in "we're fine for now" start moving.

**Situation:** A team's pitch deck has a competitor battle card for every named vendor in the space and nothing about "doing nothing."
**Application:** Classic Bucket 3 blind spot. The team is preparing for a shortlist fight but losing to indecision before the shortlist ever forms. Add a section to the pitch and to rep training that specifically addresses the buyer who's stuck: walk through the pros and cons of the three approaches (status quo, generic tool, direct competitor) explicitly. Arm reps to make a recommendation. Add phased deployment as a standard option.
**Result:** Reps now have a move for the "I'm not sure we're ready" conversation, which was previously the graveyard of the pipeline. Bucket 3 losses decline.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Report "no decision" as a single category in win/loss.
**Why:** It hides the diagnosis. Split it: status-quo stick (Bucket 1) vs. indecision abandon (Bucket 3). The moves are different. Keeping them lumped means you keep solving the wrong problem.

**Don't:** Trash the status quo in the pitch.
**Why:** The champion built the spreadsheet. Mocking it mocks them. Position *past* the status quo by quantifying what it costs and reducing the pain of change — don't insult the thing the champion spent the last two years building.

**Don't:** Ignore a ghost competitor and then tell sales to pitch against it because product marketing made a battle card.
**Why:** If a competitor never lands on a real shortlist, they're not in Bucket 2. Pitching against them gives the buyer new names to go research, which slows the deal. Your product team can track horizon threats; your positioning shouldn't carry them.

**Don't:** Treat a POC as a universal Bucket 3 move.
**Why:** POCs can accelerate a deal or slow it to a crawl. For enterprise deals where the buyer is indecisive *and* you can tightly manage the POC (defined timeframe, pass/fail criteria, dedicated resources), they help. If the buyer isn't indecisive or you can't tightly manage the POC, avoid — you'll just extend the sales cycle.

**Don't:** Stay neutral at the end of a sale when the buyer can't decide.
**Why:** Neutrality reads as lack of confidence in your own product. The Jolt Effect research showed a 144% lift in win rate with indecisive buyers when vendors proactively recommend a course of action. Have an opinion. Share it.
