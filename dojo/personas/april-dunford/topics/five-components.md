# The Five (Plus One) Components of Positioning

## When to Use
- You need a canonical way to structure a positioning document the whole team can work against.
- You're setting up a positioning workshop and need to give participants a shared vocabulary.
- A team is arguing in circles about "positioning" — breaking it into five distinct decisions usually ends the argument, because most disagreements are about one specific component, not about positioning as a whole.
- You're translating positioning into a sales pitch and need to map the inputs — every component feeds a specific step of the pitch.

## Fails When
- **Teams fill in the components with current thinking and call it done.** That's the positioning statement trap in five-component costume. The point of the framework is to *generate* better answers, not to capture existing assumptions. If you haven't considered alternatives for each component, you haven't done the work.
- **Components get written without customer evidence.** "Easy to use" or "great customer service" as differentiators — said by every company — are not differentiators. Every component has to survive the test of proof from a customer's point of view.
- **Any one component is skipped.** The components interlock. If you don't know your alternatives, you don't know what "unique" means. If you don't know your value, you don't know who cares. Don't skip.

## Core Concept

Positioning is too big a concept to tackle as a single blob. It has five components — plus one optional bonus — and each one is a separate decision. The power of the framework is that it forces you to answer each piece discretely, which is how you catch the place where your thinking is weak.

The five components:

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What customers would do if your product didn't exist. Not what you think your competitors are. What the *customer* would actually use as an alternative — which is often a spreadsheet, a manual process, doing nothing, or a legacy tool they already have.

2. **Unique attributes.** The capabilities or features your product has that the alternatives don't have. Not a feature list. The capabilities that are genuinely absent from the alternatives. For a services business this might be a combination of expertise or experience; for a product, it's technical features, business model, integrations, or delivery model.

3. **Value (and proof).** What those unique attributes actually *do* for the customer. The benefit they unlock, tied to a goal the customer is trying to achieve. "15-megapixel camera" is a feature. "Sharp images even when zoomed or printed large" is value. Every piece of value has to be provable with data, third-party evidence, or customer testimony — your opinion of your own value doesn't count as proof.

4. **Target market characteristics.** The characteristics of the buyers who care *the most* about your differentiated value. Not the broad addressable market. The segment whose specific situation makes your value matter more to them than to anyone else. These are the people who buy fast, rarely negotiate price, and tell their friends.

5. **Market category.** The context you put your product in to make its value obvious. The answer to the question "what is this thing?" that a customer uses to bucket your product against a known set of expectations. Market categories come loaded with assumptions about competitors, features, and price. Your job is to pick the category whose baked-in assumptions work *for* you, not against you.

**(+1) Relevant trends.** Optional. A trend buyers already care about that can be layered on top of your category to make your product feel urgent right now. AI-empowered CRM is still CRM (the category); AI is the trend. Use trends carefully — adding a trend to weak positioning just makes the confusion louder. And never put a trend ahead of your category, because trends aren't categories; they don't tell customers what your product *is*.

The components are interlocked. Attributes are only "unique" relative to alternatives. Value is derived from unique attributes. Target market is defined by who cares most about the value. Category is picked to make the value obvious to the target. Everything hinges on everything else. Which is why when you do positioning, you have to work through all of them in order.

## How to Apply

1. **Competitive alternatives first. Always.** This is the foundational piece. Before you can say what's "different" about you, you have to know what customers are comparing you against. Include the status quo (Excel, doing nothing, manual process) and any direct competitors that actually show up on shortlists. Rank them by frequency. Group them into approaches. Ignore any "competitor" that never actually appears in a real deal — they're ghosts only you can see.

2. **Unique attributes second.** Walk through every alternative and ask "what do we have that this one doesn't?" Make the list broad. Include product features, business model, pricing model, integrations, partnerships, expertise, delivery model. It's OK to include things some team members think are negatives — one person's "requires onsite install" is another person's "high-touch white-glove service." Don't filter yet. Just list.

3. **Value third.** Go down your list of unique attributes and for each one ask "so what? What does a customer get from this?" Then ask "so what?" again until you hit a goal the customer cares about in their business. "Patented fuzzy logic algorithm" → "faster query response" → "can answer customer questions in real time instead of calling them back" → "higher customer satisfaction scores." That last one is the value. Cluster the values into themes. You should end up with one to four value themes, not a list of fifty. One is common. Fewer is better.

4. **Target market fourth.** For each value theme, ask "who cares a lot about this?" Narrow down by the specific situations that make the value matter more. Not demographics. Specific combinations of situation, industry, size, existing stack, buying trigger. You're looking for the segment where your value is a *must-have*, not a nice-to-have. Narrow enough that you could actually identify them in a list.

5. **Market category fifth.** Now ask: what category, named in language your target market already uses, makes my unique value look obvious? Not the default category you started in. Not what customers call you (they'll pick the closest category they already know, which is rarely the best one). The category whose loaded assumptions — competitors, features, price — work in your favor given what you actually do. If no existing category fits, you may need to position in a subsegment, or in rare cases create a new one.

6. **(Optional) Trend sixth.** Only if there's a current trend that your target buyers are tracking and that your product legitimately plays to. Otherwise skip it. Trends are a spice, not a main course.

7. **Capture it on one page.** The whole positioning should fit on one page — five (six) boxes filled in with your best thinking. This is the document the CEO refers to, the document marketing works against, the document that informs the sales pitch. If it's ten pages, nobody will use it. One page.

## Examples

**Situation:** A sales enablement startup (LevelJump) is struggling to differentiate.
**Application:** Work through the five components. Alternatives: shared drive, CMS, full LMS. Unique attribute: only sales enablement solution built on Salesforce. Value: can connect training activity to actual sales outcomes (time-to-first-deal, time-to-quota), proving and improving the impact of enablement. Target: companies onboarding lots of new reps where sales enablement leadership is under pressure to prove impact. Category: sales enablement, sharpened to "sales enablement that drives results."
**Result:** Every downstream artifact — pitch deck, demo, discovery questions, homepage — now has the same bones. The pitch writes itself because each component maps to a step of the sales pitch structure.

**Situation:** A project management tool for agencies is getting lost in a crowded market.
**Application:** Alternatives aren't "Asana and Monday" — those don't actually show up on these buyers' shortlists. Alternatives are "spreadsheets + Word docs" (status quo), "Trello-class easy tools," and "Jira-class enterprise tools." Unique attribute: multi-project resource planning built for small agencies. Value: profitability on thin-margin custom dev work. Target: agencies under thirty people running multiple concurrent projects. Category: project management for agencies (narrowing the parent category).
**Result:** The whole pitch reframes: we're not trying to be better than Asana, we're the tool that lives in the gap between "too simple" and "too heavy" for the specific shape of business you run. That's a position somebody can buy.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Fill in "unique attributes" with a giant feature list your product team loves.
**Why:** Features are only "unique" when compared to actual alternatives. A feature on your list that two of your three alternatives also have is a table-stake. Force every attribute to survive the comparison with the alternatives. Most will get cut. That's the point.

**Don't:** List "target market" as "SMBs" or "enterprise" and call it done.
**Why:** That's firmographics, not targeting. It's not actionable. Your target market is a specific combination of situation + need + buying context. "Enterprise" is a sales ops segment, not a positioning segment. Be narrower. You can always broaden later.

**Don't:** Pick a market category because it's big and trendy.
**Why:** A category brings assumptions. If the trendy category's baked-in assumptions don't actually match your product — your feature set, your price, your competitor set — you'll spend every sales call fighting the category instead of using it. Pick the category where the assumptions work *for* you.

**Don't:** Skip proof and write aspirational value.
**Why:** "Increases productivity by 30%" with no proof is a claim the buyer won't believe, and their procurement team definitely won't. Proof points — customer quotes, third-party validation, hard data from deployments — are not optional. No proof, no value. Just marketing noise.
