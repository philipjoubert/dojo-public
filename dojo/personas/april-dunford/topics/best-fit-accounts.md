# Best-Fit Accounts (and Bad-Fit Accounts)

## When to Use
- You're on Component 4 of the positioning canvas and need to define who the buyers are that specifically care about your differentiated value.
- The team is reaching for "SMBs" or "mid-market" or "enterprise" to describe the target — none of those are targets, they're sales ops segments.
- Sales is spending time on deals that never close. The symptom is almost always a loose definition of best-fit.
- You're translating positioning into marketing — if you can't identify Best-Fit Accounts in a list, you can't target them in campaigns either.

## Fails When
- **The segment you can honestly win is too small.** I call this the "Oh Crap" moment. If your Best-Fit Accounts turn out to be "left-handed car dealership owners in Saskatoon," positioning can't fix it. You need to go back and build more value, or broaden the capabilities of the product, until the segment is big enough to meet your business goals.
- **The team won't exclude customers they've already sold to.** Every B2B company has weird outlier customers. Write them on the board and put an X through them. You're not trying to do positioning that works for every customer you've ever closed — that's not the point.
- **Pre-launch, there's no data.** Best-fit is identified by studying the customers who already love you. If you haven't launched, what you have is a Best-Fit *thesis*, not a Best-Fit segment. Keep it loose until the first wave of customers tells you.

## Core Concept

Best-Fit Accounts are the customers who care *the most* about the differentiated value only you can deliver. Not "customers you could plausibly sell to." Not "the big addressable market." The narrow segment whose specific situation, stack, behavior, or structure makes your value matter more to them than to anyone else.

I identify Best-Fit Accounts the same way every time: I look at the customers who already love the product, and I find the pattern.

**Best-Fit Accounts buy quickly, rarely ask for discounts, and fight to keep the product.** That's the diagnostic. Their other customers think the product is OK for the price and will jump ship if something cheaper shows up. Best-fit customers *loooove* the product, tell their friends, and if you tried to take it away they'd fight you for it. Those are the people the positioning has to resonate with.

And here's the part teams miss: **the segment is narrower than you want it to be.** That's not a bug. That's the point. A broad segment makes generic messaging. A narrow segment makes positioning that feels like it was written for the buyer personally. You can always broaden later once you've won the narrow segment.

**Demographics and firmographics aren't enough.** "Mid-market SaaS companies" is not a target. It's a category Salesforce uses to route inbound leads. An actionable Best-Fit Account definition captures a list of easily identifiable characteristics — environment, behavior, stack, existing process — that make a company care a lot about what you do. Good Best-Fit definitions sound like: "mid-to-large pet services companies with more than 2 employees and a retail presence." You can identify that in a list. You can target that in a campaign. You can qualify against it in discovery.

**The Bad-Fit Account matters too.** Every Best-Fit definition has a matching Bad-Fit definition. For SnoutDesk: Best-Fit is mid-to-large pet services companies with 2+ employees and a retail presence. Bad-Fit is solo practitioners (they can make do with free tools) and online-only businesses (the retail storefront features don't apply to them). Naming Bad-Fit explicitly does two things: it disciplines sales to stop chasing deals they were never going to win, and it sharpens the Best-Fit definition by contrast.

Two tests a Best-Fit Account definition has to pass:
1. **Big enough** to hit your near-term sales targets.
2. **Specific enough** that there are unmet needs common to the segment that your differentiated value maps to.

If it fails test 1, it's too narrow — go broader, or build more value. If it fails test 2, it's too broad — the value will feel generic and you'll get beaten by a competitor who went narrower.

## How to Apply

1. **Start from the happy-customer list, not the TAM slide.** Who actually loves the product? Who bought fast, rarely negotiated, and would fight to keep it? Their characteristics are your data. Don't start from the market you wish you were in.

2. **Look for what's *common* about them, not what's average.** Industry, company size, tech stack, existing process, buying trigger, team structure, role of the champion. You're pattern-matching on the specific situation that makes your value a must-have.

3. **Write the Bad-Fit list at the same time.** Put the outlier customers on the board and cross them out. "Solo practitioners" or "online-only" or "still on Oracle" or "doing this in-house." Naming Bad-Fit stops the "but what about Goldman Sachs that one time?" derailment in its tracks.

4. **Check the definition against the diagnostic.** Do Best-Fit customers buy quickly? Rarely ask for discounts? Fight to keep the product? If not, your definition is still too loose. Narrow it until the three diagnostic signals all show up.

5. **Size the segment against your sales goal.** How many accounts does the definition describe? Can you hit this year's revenue target by selling only to them? If yes, you're good. If no, you haven't hit the "Oh Crap" moment yet — broaden carefully, one characteristic at a time, and re-test.

6. **Make the Champion Persona explicit inside the Best-Fit Account.** Best-Fit Account is the company; champion persona is the person in that company who builds the shortlist and recommends the purchase. The pitch has to resonate for *that person* on the first call. Positioning for a company without naming the champion produces generic messaging.

## Examples

**Situation:** A pet-services software company (SnoutDesk) defines its target as "pet businesses."
**Application:** Too broad. Grooming chains, solo dog walkers, big-box retailers, and online-only pet e-commerce are all "pet businesses" and they have almost nothing in common. Run the diagnostic against the happy-customer list: the loyal ones are mid-to-large pet services companies with more than 2 employees and a retail presence who are focused on revenue growth and improved operations. Solo practitioners and online-only shops are Bad-Fit — they can make do with free tools or don't need the retail features. Now the target is actionable.
**Result:** Marketing can identify these companies in a list. Sales can qualify against the definition on the first call. The differentiated value (growth + profitability via reputation management and dynamic pricing) maps cleanly to what these specific buyers care about most.

**Situation:** A data analytics startup describes its target as "any company with a lot of data."
**Application:** Useless. Loads of companies have large amounts of data and only store it for backup — they don't care about fast analysis. The real question: who cares about getting answers *quickly* from a large amount of data? Narrower: banks with a time-sensitive need to analyze data to catch a potential security threat. Now the target has unmet needs specific to the segment, and your differentiated value (speed at scale) is a must-have, not a nice-to-have.
**Result:** The team stops marketing to "data-rich companies" and starts marketing to a specific slice of financial services where speed on large data is the purchase criterion. Close rates climb because the value is obvious on first contact.

**Situation:** A team lists "enterprise" as their target and wonders why deals stall.
**Application:** "Enterprise" is firmographics, not Best-Fit. Do they buy fast? Rarely ask for discounts? Fight to keep the product? No — they run procurement cycles, negotiate price, and shop renewals. That's not a Best-Fit diagnostic; that's an enterprise procurement process. Strip "enterprise" out of the definition and ask what *situation* inside an enterprise makes the value matter. Usually you'll find the real target is a specific role, a specific existing-system context, and a specific buying trigger — much narrower than "enterprise."
**Result:** Sales stops chasing every Fortune 500 logo and focuses on a narrow situation where the positioning resonates. Conversion on qualified accounts goes up; total pipeline volume goes down. That's the right trade.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Define Best-Fit as "SMBs" or "enterprise" or "mid-market."
**Why:** Those are sales ops labels. They describe billing tiers, not buyer situations. An actionable Best-Fit definition captures situation, behavior, and unmet need — things a sales rep can identify in a discovery call and a marketer can target in a campaign.

**Don't:** Try to position for every customer you've ever closed.
**Why:** Weird outliers will derail the definition. Someone will always protest "but what about that one time..." — that's noise, not signal. Cross the outliers off the board explicitly before you start.

**Don't:** Pick the big market over the winnable one.
**Why:** If you pick "the whole CRM market" over "CRM for investment banks," your positioning has to beat Salesforce on Salesforce's terms. The narrow segment is where your differentiation is actually must-have. Win the narrow segment first; broaden later.

**Don't:** Skip defining Bad-Fit.
**Why:** Without Bad-Fit, sales keeps chasing deals they can't win, marketing keeps broadcasting to people who'll never convert, and the Best-Fit definition drifts. Naming Bad-Fit is a discipline — it forces the team to say out loud who they're walking away from.

**Don't:** Confuse "addressable market" with "Best-Fit Account."
**Why:** TAM is for investors. Best-Fit is for positioning and pitch. TAM says "there are a million pet businesses out there." Best-Fit says "we win against these specific 40,000 of them." Different numbers, different purposes. Don't let the TAM slide corrupt the Best-Fit definition.
