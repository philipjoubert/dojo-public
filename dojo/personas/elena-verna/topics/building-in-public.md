# Building in Public

## When to Use
- You ship weekly or faster and the old "save it for the quarterly launch" motion can't keep up. BIP is the only marketing that matches AI-era shipping velocity.
- Paid channels are collapsing, SEO is getting eaten by AI snippets, and you need a distribution surface that actually works. Founder-led and employee-led social is what's left.
- You're an early-stage founder who can't out-spend anyone on ads. "If I were a startup with very limited resources, I would pick founder socials 100 percent." It's two minutes a day and it's the gift that keeps on giving.
- You (like me) are a solo operator building a business on trust — Substack, advisory, courses, speaking. The same playbook works at every scale: ship in the open, let people see how you think, sell to the ones who recognize themselves in the work.
- You're at a bigger company and leadership wants another "Apple-style keynote" with six months of runway. Gently explain that you're not Apple, you don't make hardware, and the iterative nature of software is a superpower they're refusing to use.

## Fails When
- **Shipping velocity drops.** If the underlying product isn't moving, BIP becomes noise. You're telling people you're alive when you're standing still. They notice.
- **BIP runs out of the corporate account.** People don't want "the company POV." They want Anton. They want the engineer. They want the designer. Corporate accounts get tuned out instantly.
- **Marketing "owns" BIP as a content strategy.** BIP is a company-wide cultural commitment, not a content team project. Dumping it on marketing is how you get half-baked features announced by someone who didn't build them.
- **You expect results in week one.** Audiences compound over months. If you post for three weeks, see no traction, and quit — you were never doing BIP, you were auditioning for applause.
- **You publish nothing but wins.** BIP is about honesty, not victory-lapping. The posts that build trust are the ones that show the ugly middle.

## Core Concept

Most companies still operate like it's 2012. Hide everything. Build in silence. Save it for one glossy launch. Blow a ridiculous amount of money. Expect a lasting lift. Watch it vanish in days. Start anxiously waiting for the next "launch." This happens because company leaders take marketing inspiration from the wrong places. They want to be Hollywood, turning product releases into blockbuster-level brand moments. They want to be Apple, with pixel-perfect videos and industry-wide hype. But Hollywood is inconsistent and risky — one shot, one flop. And Apple makes *hardware*, which is why they need long development cycles and secrecy. Software doesn't. The iterative nature of software distribution is a superpower that traditional marketing refuses to use.

Building in Public is when teams share their work as they build it. Not every internal debate. Not every half-baked idea. The steady drumbeat of progress, decisions, wins, and improvements — in real time. It moves marketing out of a separate "campaigns" bucket and stitches it directly into the development cycle. The product becomes the story. The shipping cadence becomes the engagement strategy. At Lovable this is basically how we breathe. Agents. Credit rollovers. New paid plan. Prompt suggestions. App Discovery. New dashboard. Visual editor improvements. Twelve small touches a week that make things smoother, smarter, or more magical — and instead of holding onto them like Top Secret clearance, we just talk about them. As soon as they're live, sometimes before.

Micro-releases trigger a specific psychology. Users signed up for version X. They keep getting version X+. It feels like a free upgrade. It builds loyalty without you having to ask for it. You get resurrection without boring newsletters. You get buzz without marketing spend. You get a steady stream of reasons for people to talk about you — and root for you. And it builds trust in a way no ad can. Your audience gets to know your team, your reasoning, your taste. That's a kind of connection you cannot buy.

The hard part is it's scary. BIP means giving up control. The polish comes down. You don't have time to carefully frame each update. You might be wrong in public. But here's the thing — less polished *wins* right now. Authentic beats glossy on every feed that matters. And the three guardrails are: maintain shipping velocity (or your public discussions become empty hype), bring in the builders (CEO, engineers, designers — not corporate accounts), anchor on user understanding (so you don't just chase dopamine hits and post the latest shiny thing).

## How to Apply

1. **Pick the platform where your audience actually reads.** LinkedIn for B2B. X for dev tools. Instagram/TikTok for consumer. Pick one. Go deep. Expanding to a second platform is a decision you make in month 6, not month 1.

2. **Start with the founder. Expand to 3–5 employees.** CEO and co-founders first. Personal accounts rank higher than company accounts — "I wouldn't invest heavily into your company account yet." Expand to engineers, designers, PMs who build the thing. Corporate accounts get zero weight.

3. **Commit to 3 months before judging it.** Don't get discouraged in week one. Anton (Lovable's CEO) went from no following to 2000+ reactions per post in 10 months. That's the actual shape of the curve. If you quit at week 3 you never gave it a chance.

4. **Tier your shipping cadence.** Tier 1: big launches, quarterly — "turbo boost moments." Tier 2: meaningful updates, weekly. Tier 3: smaller updates, daily or hourly. Each tier gives you content for socials, press, and conversations. Shipping velocity *is* the marketing calendar.

5. **Post what you're actually doing — not what marketing thinks sounds good.** What shipped this week. What broke. What you're debating internally. What a customer said that changed your mind. Don't manufacture controversy and don't sanitize reality into bland corporate mush.

6. **Engage with comments. Writing is 30% of the work.** Responding is 70%. The distribution engine of any social platform rewards conversation, not broadcast. If you post and vanish, the algorithm buries you and no reader feels seen.

7. **Anchor on customer reality, not dopamine.** Rule of 3 — if you've said something three times in customer calls this week, it's a post. That keeps BIP honest and keeps you from chasing whatever is trending on your feed.

8. **Use the same playbook for your own career.** Treat your brain as a product. Acquisition = posting, speaking, writing. Retention = doing what you're good at AND love. Monetization = full-time role, advising, courses, workshops, speaking fees. Distribution = LinkedIn, Substack, community. That's how I built a business outside of a full-time job. That's how you do it too, if that's where you're headed.

## Examples

**Situation:** A fast-growing AI company is sitting on 8 weeks of shipped features nobody outside the company knows about, planning one big launch at the next conference. Marketing is writing a keynote.
**Application:** Kill the keynote. That's 8 weeks of marketing opportunity rotting on the vine, plus a single point of failure when the launch video doesn't land. Start today — CEO posts about the one feature that shipped Monday with a loom of themselves using it. Engineer posts the follow-up with the technical detail. Designer posts the before/after screenshot on Thursday. Weekly cadence. No approval gate. Cadence compounds; keynotes don't.
**Result:** By week 6 you've had 24 named-person posts across 4 people, total impressions in the low millions, and a founder LinkedIn presence that's now a top-5 acquisition channel. The keynote is still fine — but it's a reinforcement of a story your audience has been following for weeks, not a surprise. That's how launches should feel.

**Situation:** A B2B startup CEO says "content is cringe, I don't want to be a LinkedIn influencer, can we hire a PR firm instead?"
**Application:** You don't have to love it. You have to do it. PR firms build column inches; they don't build trust. The only question is how much you're willing to lose while refusing to be your company's face. One platform (LinkedIn). One topic you know cold (the problem your product solves). 2–3 posts a week, minimum. Rule of 3 for content. Unapologetic authenticity over polish. And engage in comments — that's 70% of the work.
**Result:** Slow, straight line. Month 3: 2K followers, first comment-driven inbound. Month 12: 5–10K relevant followers, a handful of inbound deals per quarter directly attributable to posts, inbound recruiting signal improves. Month 24: top-3 acquisition channel. If you'd hired the PR firm, you'd have 18 months of press clippings and zero compound.

**Situation:** A 200-person SaaS has a content team, a marketing account with 40K followers, and "CEO doesn't post because it feels beneath the role."
**Application:** The corporate account is dead weight — it's pulling 2–3% engagement because nobody trusts it. Reallocate. CEO starts posting twice a week, personally, even if it's rough. Three engineers get enabled with a light editorial partner (not a ghostwriter — an editor). Product team owns the "what shipped" posts. Corporate account becomes an amplifier of human posts, not a broadcaster.
**Result:** Total impressions triple in 6 months. Inbound quality improves because prospects saw the founder's thinking before the demo. Recruitment improves because candidates arrived pre-sold on the mission. Marketing attribution is blurry — which is fine, because the HDYHAU survey now shows "I follow your CEO on LinkedIn" as a top answer.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run BIP out of the corporate account to "stay on brand."
**Why:** Nobody trusts the company POV. People want to hear from *people*. Corporate accounts generate reach without trust; founder accounts generate trust that compounds. Ship the company posts too, fine — but the signal lives with the humans.

**Don't:** Wait for content to be "polished" before shipping it.
**Why:** Less polished wins right now. The top creators on LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok, Instagram all publish fast, rough, authentic. If you wait for polish, you miss the cadence that makes BIP work and you also filter out the raw honesty that makes it trustworthy.

**Don't:** Treat BIP as a marketing team deliverable.
**Why:** BIP is a company-wide cultural commitment. Marketing can help with cadence and infrastructure (drafting tools, visuals, editorial feedback), but the builders have to build *in public*. Dumping BIP on marketing produces corporate posts pretending to be personal — which lands worse than not posting at all.

**Don't:** Build a content calendar detached from the roadmap.
**Why:** BIP is shipping made visible. If you're posting on a calendar instead of posting *what shipped*, you're back to traditional content marketing in a fresh costume. Anchor every post in something that actually happened this week — or don't post.

**Don't:** Quit when week 3 looks quiet.
**Why:** The compound is real but slow. Anton took 10 months to go from nothing to thousands of reactions per post. My own Substack took years. If the goal is compounding distribution, the floor is 3 months of discipline before you even look at the numbers.

**Don't:** Chase engagement with outrage or hot takes.
**Why:** Short-term dopamine, long-term trust erosion. If you're optimizing for likes at the expense of being useful, you're training your audience to expect performance — and they'll leave for someone more reliable. Rule of 3 from customer calls keeps BIP honest.
