# Champion as Internal Salesperson

## When to Use
- You have a PQA account with strong usage, clear team-level adoption, but no economic buyer surfaces through normal prospecting. A human inside the account is raising their hand through product behavior.
- An enthusiastic end-user is DMing support, posting about the product publicly, asking about enterprise contracts they personally can't sign. That's a champion announcing themselves.
- Your AE team is frustrated — "I keep getting leads who can't buy." The problem isn't lead quality; it's that sales is trying to close the wrong persona. Treat the champion as a partner, not a lead.
- You're running the Four W's of PLS and the "Who" question keeps resolving to champion, not buyer. You need the internal-sales play specifically.
- You have executive-access-gated enterprise deals and no way in through cold outreach. The champion is your way in.

## Fails When
- **There's no real champion.** A user with high activity isn't automatically a champion. Champions evangelize — they post, they recommend, they DM support, they attend community events. Pure usage doesn't equal willingness to sell internally. Don't mistake one for the other.
- **The org has heavy procurement gates.** In some enterprises, procurement is walled off from champion influence. Champions don't matter — procurement still vetoes. Map the buying structure first.
- **AE treats the champion like a pitch target.** This is the default sales-rep failure mode. Champions are partners in selling; they are not the purchase decision-makers. If you pitch *them*, you burn the relationship and lose the account.
- **You have no champion enablement assets.** If the champion says "okay, show me your deck so I can pitch internally" and you have nothing usable, you wasted the moment.

## Core Concept

Here's the specific PLS play most teams get wrong. A PQA account has strong end-user traction — volume, velocity, breadth of usage, multiple people actively engaged. But when you prospect externally for the economic buyer, you find nothing. LinkedIn shows a VP who might be the buyer, but cold outreach to them goes nowhere. You try to pitch the end users. They're not buyers. They can't sign a contract. Everyone spins.

This is one of the trickiest sales plays, because sales reps are trained to talk to economic buyers. Their muscle memory is: find the title, pitch the value, handle objections, close. In PLS with no buyer visible, that playbook breaks. The move is an inversion — **partner with the champion, don't sell to them.** Treat them as an internal salesperson. Give them the materials, the data, the pitch. Let them carry the conversation into the rooms you can't enter.

**Who is a champion?** Not just a power user. A champion is a passionate end-user who is already advocating for your product inside their org — they post about it, they invite teammates, they teach their team how to use it, they reach out asking about enterprise plans or security docs they don't personally need. They have already decided, emotionally, that your product should win inside their company. Your job is to arm them.

**Why it works.** The champion has institutional context you will never have. They know who actually signs contracts. They know the politics. They know which competing tool the procurement team keeps pushing. They know the real objections. They know the internal calendar. And crucially, they're *trusted internally* in a way an external AE never can be. A cold email from sales goes to spam; a Slack message from a peer goes to Slack. The champion is the only actor who can bypass every trust barrier that blocks outside vendors.

**The play itself.** Mark the champion clearly in your CRM — a distinct lead type, *not* a PQL. PQLs are people with buying power plus product usage. Champions are advocates without buying power. Sales must *know* the difference, because the wrong playbook gets applied otherwise. Share your sales deck and enable the champion to present it internally. Ask what objections they're hitting, address them in follow-up materials. Offer co-selling: "Would it help if I joined the meeting as your technical resource, and you drove the positioning?" Sometimes yes, sometimes the champion wants to carry it solo.

**What you're giving the champion.** (1) A one-pager — clean, readable, specific to the use case their team is already using. (2) An internal pitch deck — 8–12 slides, customizable, with blanks for internal champions to insert their own team's data. (3) Usage data — "your team has done X with our product, saving Y hours per week, per our instrumentation." Internal ROI stories beat external marketing claims. (4) A security/compliance pack — SOC 2, DPA, pen test summary, privacy policy — stuff that procurement demands and that champions can't chase down themselves. (5) A comparison page for the specific competitor they mentioned. (6) Access to a technical resource (SE or PM) for the buyer's eventual questions.

**What you're NOT doing.** You're not pitching the champion. You're not asking them to schedule a meeting with the buyer and then hijacking it. You're not emailing past them to the exec. You're not promising them a referral bonus for introducing you. All of those moves burn the relationship — the champion signed up to help the product win inside their company, not to be co-opted into your quota system.

**The expansion.** Once the deal closes, the champion becomes a power user and early adopter for new features. They become a customer advisory board candidate. They become a case study. They stay visible on your LinkedIn. Do not forget them the moment the contract is signed. That kind of amnesia is how the next renewal gets lost.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify champions through product behavior and external signal.** In-product: high usage, multiple invites sent, admin/settings activity, frequently helps teammates. External: posts about you on LinkedIn, asks questions in your community, DMs support about enterprise features they don't personally need (SSO, admin controls, security docs, procurement questions). Tag them.

2. **Create a distinct "champion" lead type in your CRM.** Not a PQL. Not an MQL. A champion. This matters because sales will handle the relationship differently — as partnership, not pitch.

3. **Build a Champion Enablement Kit before you need it.** One-pager, internal pitch deck (customizable), security/compliance pack, usage-data template, competitor comparison, case studies, ROI calculator. Version it per segment. Update it quarterly.

4. **Reach out to partner, not pitch.** First message: "Hey, we noticed your team is doing a lot with our product. A lot of folks in your role are trying to roll this out more broadly internally — we built some materials to help. Want to chat through your rollout?" That's partnership language. Not sales language.

5. **Ask what objections they've encountered internally.** The champion has had conversations you'll never see. "What's the biggest pushback you've gotten?" "Who inside your company is skeptical?" "What would their ideal objection resolution look like?" Use answers to build targeted follow-up materials.

6. **Offer co-selling, don't demand it.** "Would it help to have me on the call as your technical resource?" leaves the champion in the driver's seat. Some champions want you there; some want to carry the conversation solo. Both are fine. Respect their judgment about their own org.

7. **Hand the champion everything they need to make the case ROI-provable.** Usage stats specific to their team. Time saved. Outputs produced. Comparison to their old process or the alternative tool. Give them language leaders actually respond to — not marketing copy, internal-memo copy.

8. **Follow up consistently without pestering.** Champions have day jobs. Their internal pitch might take weeks or months. Check in at 2–3 week cadence with a small useful thing each time (new feature their team would like, a case study from a peer company, a useful ROI calculation). Silence erodes; over-pushing burns.

9. **After the close, keep the champion central.** Customer advisory board. Case study. Early access to new features. Public credit where they want it. The champion carried the deal — treat them like it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A B2B PLG product has a PQA account at ~$0 MRR but 15 active users, 3 of whom are repeatedly DMing support asking about SSO and SOC 2. AE has tried to find "the CISO" on LinkedIn and hit nothing.
**Application:** Those 3 users are champions, not leads. Mark them as champion-lead-type in CRM. Reach out to the most active: "Hey, seems like you're trying to roll this out across your team. I'd love to help — we have an internal deck and security pack you can share with your procurement team. Want to hop on a 20-minute call to walk through what you need?" Get on the call. Hand over the enablement kit. Ask them what their buying process looks like. Offer to join their internal meeting if they want, or to answer specific questions asynchronously.
**Result:** Champion sends the deck internally. Comes back two weeks later with 3 specific objections from IT and Finance. AE addresses each with targeted follow-up materials. Champion carries the pitch to the buyer meeting with the AE as the technical backup. Deal closes at $60K ARR in month 4 — a deal that would have been impossible through cold outbound to a buyer nobody could even identify.

**Situation:** A dev-tools company gets constant Slack community activity from an engineer at a Fortune 500. AE has been trying to pitch the engineer for months. No deal.
**Application:** Stop pitching the engineer. Recognize the pattern: sales rep treating a champion like a buyer, champion disengaging. Switch modes. New outreach: "Hey, I realize we've been coming at this wrong. You're clearly a power user, and I'd rather help you make the case inside [company] than keep pitching you. We've built internal-rollout materials specifically for engineers in your situation — can I send them over and talk through what you're running into internally?"
**Result:** Engineer responds warmly — they've been frustrated because they wanted the product at company scale but didn't know how to pitch finance. AE hands over the kit, does a 30-min call on internal-pitch strategy, backs off. Three months later, engineer has gotten the tool approved as a line item in the platform team's budget. AE closes a $120K first-year deal. If the AE had kept pitching, the champion would have burned out and the deal would never have happened.

**Situation:** A founder is about to send "referral bonus" emails to power users offering $500 for "introducing us to your CFO."
**Application:** Don't. That converts a champion relationship into a transactional bounty and burns trust instantly. Champions help you because they want the product to win internally, not because they want $500. Offering money transforms them into brokers, and most brokers disengage because their employer would frown on the arrangement. Replace the referral bonus with the Champion Enablement Kit and a partnership conversation. Champions should be *enabled*, not *hired*.
**Result:** Zero damage to existing champion relationships. The enablement kit drives deals at multiple accounts within a quarter. The referral program, if it ever existed, would have gotten 0 takers and 10 awkward Slack messages.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat champions as PQLs.
**Why:** PQLs are product-qualified *leads* — people with usage signals AND buying power. Champions have usage signals but NOT buying power. Lumping them together breaks the sales motion — AEs will pitch champions, champions will disengage, deals die.

**Don't:** Offer cash referral bonuses to champions for intros.
**Why:** Converts the relationship from mission-aligned to transactional. Most champions disengage because their employer would flag it. You've just monetized a trust relationship and killed it. Enable the champion with materials instead.

**Don't:** Email the buyer past the champion's back.
**Why:** Burns the relationship. The champion finds out (they always do), feels co-opted, and disengages. If you were going to cold-email the buyer anyway, you didn't need the champion. If you *do* need the champion, don't burn them for a shortcut.

**Don't:** Ghost the champion after the deal closes.
**Why:** The champion carried your deal. They expect ongoing partnership, credit, access. If you vanish the moment the PO is signed, you've lost the person who'll champion the renewal, the expansion, and the next five reference calls. Treat them like the asset they are.

**Don't:** Send the same pitch deck you use in cold outbound to the champion.
**Why:** The cold-outbound deck is external-facing marketing. The champion needs internal-facing enablement — ROI specific to their team, objection-handling for their stakeholders, customizable slides for their voice. Same content, different packaging. Skip the customization and you've handed them a generic asset that won't work in their internal meetings.

**Don't:** Ask the champion for an "intro email" to their exec.
**Why:** The generic "can you introduce me to your CFO" ask is the lowest-trust move in the champion playbook. Champions aren't in the business of selling you to their execs cold; they're in the business of rolling out a product they love. Reframe the ask: "What materials can I build that would help you make the case to [role] internally?" That's partnership. The other is a request for free work.
