# Hiring — a company of giants, not dwarfs

## When to Use
- You are hiring a copywriter, art director, account executive, creative director, or research director, and the wrong hire will show up in the work within a month.
- The polished, fluent candidate from central casting is up against the awkward one with the stronger portfolio.
- Someone on your staff has suggested you hire their spouse, their nephew, or the client's son.
- You have reached the level in your career where the people you hire will one day hire people. The quality of that second-order pool is entirely downstream of the first.

## Fails When
- The role does not depend on exceptional talent — filing clerks, receptionists, schedulers. Reliability, courtesy, and discretion matter more than "bigger than you are."
- The local labour market is so thin that the "bigger than you" rule would leave the chair empty for six months.
- You are genuinely untalented yourself. Then the rule produces a company of giants who leave the moment they discover who is signing their pay cheques. Fix yourself first.

## Core Concept

"If each of us hires people who are smaller than we are, we shall become a company of dwarfs. But if each of us hires people who are bigger than we are, we shall become a company of giants." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*, and *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* (the Russian-dolls speech). This is not a morale slogan. It is a mechanical claim about compounding. Each level of management hires the level below. If each level clips itself down to people it can feel comfortable bossing, the average capability of the firm halves at every step. A company of dwarfs is the mathematical consequence, not a moral failure. The correction is to make the discipline explicit and to send every new office head a Matrioshka doll with the motto in the smallest one, so he cannot miss it.

The kind of person you are looking for is very specific. "The most difficult people to find are those who have the capacity to become good copywriters. I have found that they always have well-furnished minds. They give evidence of exceptional curiosity about every subject under the sun. They have an above-average sense of humor. And they have a fanatical interest in the craft of advertising." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Notice the vocabulary: *well-furnished minds, obsessive curiosity, a habit of hard work, the ambition to write better campaigns than anyone has ever written before.* None of these show up on a CV. They show up in conversation, in the six best ads the candidate brings to the interview, in whether he has read any books about advertising, in whether he can quote from the ones he has read, and in whether he has opinions about Bernbach, Burnett, Reeves, Caples, Hopkins. A candidate who treats the craft as a field worth studying is the candidate you want. A candidate who believes he can rely on his intuition is the one who will, one morning, be expected to bet a client's millions on that intuition.

Character matters more than pedigree. "In promoting people to top jobs, we are influenced as much by their *character* as anything else." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. Ogilvy himself started as an Aga Cooker salesman, cooked in the kitchens of the Hotel Majestic in Paris, farmed tobacco in Pennsylvania, worked for George Gallup, and arrived on Madison Avenue at thirty-eight with a curriculum vitae that read "unemployed farmer, former cook and university drop-out." He would not have made it through the HR screen at most agencies he later dominated. The people you are looking for are apt to be unorthodox — "nonconformists, dissenters and rebels" — and unemployable at more conventional firms. "Our business needs massive transfusions of talent… And talent, I believe, is most likely to be found among nonconformists, dissenters, and rebels." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. If your firm cannot tolerate them, your firm cannot hire them, and your firm will remain mediocre.

The final principle is negative: there are categories of people you should never hire, however tempting. "Never hire your friends. I have made this mistake three times, and had to fire all three. They are no longer my friends. Never hire your client's children. If you have to fire them, you may lose the client. This is another mistake I have made. Never hire your own children, or the children of your partners. However able they may be, ambitious people won't stay in outfits which practice nepotism. This is one mistake I did not make; my son is in the real estate business, secure in the knowledge that he owes nothing of his success to his father. And never hire your clients. The qualities which make someone a good client are not the qualities required for success in the agency business." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The rule against nepotism is not squeamishness; it is the observation that "ambitious people won't stay in outfits which practice" it. Every exception you make against this rule repels ten hires you would rather have had.

## How to Apply

1. When you have filled a senior opening, send the new office head a Russian doll. Inside the smallest one, put the motto. If he has the curiosity to keep opening until he finds it, he is the kind of person who should be running an office.
2. In the interview, stop asking about career trajectories and start asking about the craft. What books about advertising has he read lately? Who does he admire? Which of his own ads is his best, and why? A candidate who cannot name six ads he envies and the agencies that made them is a candidate who does not study the craft.
3. Ask for the six best ads and commercials the candidate has ever written. This reveals whether he can recognize a good ad when he sees one, or is only the instrument of a good supervisor.
4. Call on the candidate at home if you can. "Ten minutes after crossing the threshold I can tell whether he has a richly furnished mind, what kind of taste he has, and whether he is happy enough to sustain pressure." — *Confessions*.
5. Check references. "Even today, it is rare for any agency to ask an applicant's former employers what they think of him. I know two men who were hired and fired as Presidents of three agencies — without their references being checked." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
6. Be fanatical about prejudice. "In recruitment and promotion we are fanatical in our hatred for all forms of prejudice." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. The goal is the best writer, not the most familiar face.
7. If you find someone who is better than you are, hire him. "If necessary, pay him more than you pay yourself." — *Principles of Management*.
8. Pay above market. Ogilvy paid peanuts in his early years and did not promote his "monkeys"; he filled senior openings from outside, with partners like Esty Stowell, Jock Elliott, and Andrew Kershaw.
9. Look for the five characteristics Ogilvy wrote down for "Comers": ambition; willingness to work harder than his peers and enjoy it; a brilliant brain, inventive and unorthodox; an engaging personality; demonstrated respect for the creative function.
10. If you have found one of these people, promote him fast enough to keep him. "If you fail to recognize, promote and reward young people of exceptional promise, they will leave you; the loss of an exceptional man can be as damaging as the loss of an account." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
11. Refuse to hire the categories Ogilvy lists as traps: friends, your children, your partners' children, clients' children, current clients, and people successful in other fields without demonstrated interest in advertising.

## Examples

**Situation / Ad:** The "Trumpeter Swans" creative-job-posting ad (the Creative Directors wanted ad that ran in Ogilvy & Mather's own agency campaign).
**Application:** Ogilvy advertised for Creative Directors the same way he advertised for clients — he told the reader exactly what he wanted and exactly what the reader would get. The copy describes "well-furnished minds," "exceptional curiosity about every subject under the sun," "an above-average sense of humor," and "a fanatical interest in the craft of advertising." The ad is Ogilvy's public hiring manifesto; it is also a working example of what he was willing to pay for in print to attract.
**Result:** Demonstrates that hiring is itself an act of marketing. The ads the agency runs for itself will attract exactly the kind of candidate the ads describe. If you run a job listing full of HR boilerplate, you will attract people who respond to HR boilerplate. If you run a job listing in a distinctive voice, you will attract distinctive people.

**Situation / Ad:** Ogilvy hiring the head chef's apprentice from the Hotel Majestic (himself).
**Application:** At thirty-eight, Ogilvy had no college degree, no advertising experience in New York, and a CV that read "unemployed farmer, former cook and university drop-out." Most agencies would not have interviewed him. He founded his own. His path from Majestic kitchen to Aga Cooker salesman to Gallup researcher to Madison Avenue is the shape of a Russian doll: every stage hired the previous stage's giant.
**Result:** Demonstrates that the most productive hires are usually not the ones central casting would have sent. The stewards of convention are not the ones who will produce unconventional work.

**Situation / Ad:** The eighteen-year-old copywriter in Frankfurt.
**Application:** "I used to think that nobody could write good advertising before he was thirty. Then one day, on a visit to Frankfurt, I asked to meet the author of an exceptionally good campaign. She was eighteen." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The rule of thumb (which is where the three-books-rule also lives) is that the signal is the work and the curiosity, not the age or the pedigree.
**Result:** Demonstrates the principle against hiring by credential. The portfolio answers the question that the CV cannot.

**Situation / Ad:** The Russian-dolls board meeting.
**Application:** At a board meeting, every director had a Russian doll at his place. Ogilvy told them, "That's you. Open it." They opened one after another. In the smallest doll, each director found a slip of paper with the motto: "If you always hire people who are smaller than you are, we shall become a company of dwarfs. If, on the other hand, you always hire people who are bigger than you are, we shall become a company of giants." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*.
**Result:** Demonstrates that the rule has to be made unforgettable because it is counter-intuitive to the ego of every manager. Every manager *feels* that hiring someone bigger than he is will shorten his career. The dolls are the antidote to that instinct.

**Situation / Ad:** The three friends Ogilvy had to fire.
**Application:** "Never hire your friends. I have made this mistake three times, and had to fire all three. They are no longer my friends." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The confession matters because it shows the cost of the mistake even when the intentions are good — three friendships forfeited. The firing was correct; the hiring was the error.
**Result:** Demonstrates that the category rule (never hire friends, relatives, clients' children) is not a preference but a scar. If you break it, you pay twice: once in the bad hire and once in the relationship.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire the polished, fluent candidate from central casting because he interviews beautifully.
**Why:** "When I observe these grandees, coldly correct and critically dull, I am reminded of Roy Campbell… 'You praise the firm restraint with which they write / I'm with you there, of course. / They use the snaffle and the curb all right; / But where's the bloody horse?'" — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. The snaffle and the curb you can see in the interview; the horse you see in the work.

**Don't:** Hire people you can afford to bully.
**Why:** That is the definition of hiring dwarfs. It is comfortable. It is also how the firm becomes mediocre one hire at a time.

**Don't:** Hire your friends, your children, your partners' children, or your clients' children.
**Why:** You will have to fire one of them eventually. When you do, you will lose the friendship, alienate the family, or lose the client. Nepotism also poisons the morale of everyone you *didn't* hire for reasons of family. "Ambitious people won't stay in outfits which practice nepotism."

**Don't:** Hire your current clients.
**Why:** The qualities that make someone a good client — trusting the agency, being willing to sign off, having strong opinions about their own category — are not the qualities that make him a good agency person. Ogilvy made this mistake twice.

**Don't:** Hire magazine editors, lawyers, economists, and other "successful in another field" candidates without first testing whether they actually care about advertising.
**Why:** "I have hired a magazine editor, a lawyer and an economist. None of them developed an interest in advertising." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. A distinguished career elsewhere is not an advertising qualification.

**Don't:** Hire without checking references.
**Why:** It is still rare for agencies to ask an applicant's former employers what they thought of him. It is still the cheapest and most informative thing you can do in the hiring process. Do it.

**Don't:** Skip the "what have you read" question because it feels gauche.
**Why:** A candidate who cannot name the books he has read about the craft is a candidate who does not study the craft. "Suppose your gall-bladder has to be removed this evening. Will you choose a surgeon who has read some books on anatomy and knows where to find your gall-bladder, or a surgeon who relies on his intuition? Why should our clients be expected to bet millions of dollars on your intuition?" — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The question is not rude; it is the interview.

**Don't:** Promote from within when no one within is the right size.
**Why:** "Pay peanuts and you get monkeys." — Jimmy Goldsmith, quoted approvingly in *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Ogilvy's early agency filled senior openings from outside precisely because the internal bench had been hired at cut rates. Promoting the available monkey is the short-term convenient move and the long-term cost of the firm.

**Don't:** Refuse to fire the wrong hire because firing is painful.
**Why:** "It is the inescapable duty of management to fire incompetent people." — *Principles of Management*. Keeping the wrong hire demoralizes everyone who can see the work is bad and everyone who is carrying his weight. Ogilvy admitted in private correspondence, "I have always funked firing people who needed to be fired." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*. Do not repeat his failure. Fix it faster.

**Don't:** Avoid hiring unorthodox mavericks because they do not fit the firm's conventions.
**Why:** "The best leaders are apt to be found among those executives who have a strong component of unorthodoxy in their characters." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. The firm that rejects mavericks rejects the people most likely to grow it. Tolerate genius. It is worth the trouble.

**Don't:** Let the first hire into a new office be a hire of convenience.
**Why:** The first hire sets the ceiling. The second hire inherits the ceiling. By the tenth hire, the ceiling is the culture. Every decision to lower the bar compounds. The first hire into an office is the hire you have to be hardest about — because every subsequent hire is a Russian doll of that one.
