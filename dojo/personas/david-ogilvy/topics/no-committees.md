# No committees — advertising is written by a solitary individual

## When to Use
- A piece of copy is about to be passed around ten stakeholders for "input," and each one will soften it a little.
- The agency is telling you the campaign came out of a "team-work session" or a brain-storming retreat.
- A committee of brand managers and account executives is arguing the strategy to a standstill while the copywriter waits for a brief.
- Someone is about to change a hard-edged claim because Legal, or the client's brand manager, or a focus group, flinched at it.

## Fails When
- The decision is whether to enter the category, which market to attack, or how much to spend — those are partnership questions, not authorship questions.
- The writer is producing genuinely bad work that no amount of solitude will fix. The rule assumes a competent author in the room; if the author is the problem, replace the author, not the process.
- The category is regulated (pharmaceuticals, financial services) in ways that require expert review as a matter of law, not preference. Committees of specialists vetting claims for truthfulness are not the committees Ogilvy is writing about.

## Core Concept

"A lot of advertisements and television commercials look like the minutes of a committee meeting, and that is what they are. Advertising seems to sell most when it is written by a solitary individual. He must study the product, the research, and the precedents. Then he must shut the door of his office and write the advertisement. The best advertisement I ever wrote went through seventeen drafts, and built a business." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. This is not a stylistic preference. It is an observation about what actually produces work that moves the sales curve. Committees can criticize; committees cannot create.

Every agency of consequence is the lengthened shadow of one man. DDB is Bernbach; Burnett is Burnett; Bates was Reeves; Y&R was Rubicam; Ogilvy & Mather is Ogilvy. "Some agencies pander to the craze for doing everything in committee. They boast about 'teamwork' and decry the role of the individual. But no team can write an advertisement, and I doubt whether there is a single agency of any consequence which is not the lengthened shadow of one man." — *Confessions*. The word "teamwork" is the flag under which this particular mediocrity marches. Who can argue with teamwork? Everyone wants to be a team. The problem is that when a campaign is produced by a team, what gets shipped is the *smallest common denominator* — the version nobody objected to, which is also the version nobody would have written.

The process by which this happens is visible in every agency and on every client side. Strategy is argued by committees of brand managers and account executives who have a vested interest in prolonging the argument as long as possible; it is how they earn their living. Researchers take months to answer elementary questions. The copywriters, when they finally get the brief, dawdle in brain-storming sessions and other forms of wheel-spinning; "if a copywriter averages an hour a week actually *writing*, he is exceptional." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. Storyboards are argued at level after level on the agency side and level after level on the client side. The average period of gestation is somewhere between that of hyenas and goats. The average copywriter gets three commercials a year on air. All of this is the natural consequence of treating authorship as a vote.

The corrective is not a slogan — "kill the committee!" — but a working policy. The writer writes. A single trusted judge decides. The work ships. The committee's role, if it has one, is an optional veto, never authorship. "Most campaigns are too complicated. They reflect a long list of objectives, and try to reconcile the divergent views of too many executives. By attempting to cover too many things, they achieve nothing. Their advertisements look like the minutes of a committee." — *Confessions*. The second half of the discipline is that the solitary individual who shuts the door cannot be anyone; he must be someone who has done the homework. "He must study the product, the research, and the precedents." Authorship without homework is merely unearned confidence. Authorship *with* homework is what used to be called professional work. Conan Doyle wrote that "mediocrity knows nothing higher than itself." The job of management is to protect authorship from the mediocre majority that would rather vote than write.

## How to Apply

1. Before the writing starts, have one person — the writer — do the homework. Read the books, the trade journals, the research, the precedents. Ogilvy spent three weeks reading about the Rolls-Royce before he wrote a word. The electric-clock headline came out of that reading. It was not brain-stormed.
2. Shut the door. Literally. Solitude is part of the craft, not a symptom of anti-social personality.
3. Write many drafts. The best advertisement Ogilvy wrote — the Hathaway man — went through seventeen. Drafting is not brain-storming. Drafting is one person arguing with the previous version.
4. Have a single trusted judge see the work before the committee does. At O&M, that judge was the Plans Board — a panel of senior partners who were sterner critics than any client. By the time a campaign had passed their scrutiny it was apt to be good. The purpose of the Plans Board was not to rewrite the ad; it was to kill the ad if it deserved to be killed, so that no half-written ad ever reached a client.
5. Present the finished work as one voice. "Bronfman disliked the convention which most agencies observe of using several spokesmen to make their presentations. So do I. It is less distracting to the audience if one man does all the talking." — *Confessions*. Do not let three account people share a presentation; pick the best advocate and brief him until he can stand up under cross-examination.
6. Treat the client's committee as the audience, not the co-author. Sell the work; do not invite the committee to revise it. "We do not grudge clients the right to decide what advertising to run. It is their money." — *Principles of Management*. The client has the right to say no. The client does not have the right to write the headline. If the client insists on writing the headline, the agency has a decision to make about whether this is a client worth keeping.
7. Fight for the kings, queens and bishops; throw away the pawns. A graceful surrender on trivial matters makes you difficult to resist on the major ones.
8. When the committee's edits are making the work worse, say so out loud. Tell the client what you would do if you were in his shoes. This is what clients actually respect. "In meeting with clients, do not assume the posture of servants. They need you as much as you need them." — *Principles of Management*.
9. If the work has been committeed to death and cannot be saved, resign the account before the bad work ships. Ogilvy resigned accounts five times as often as he was fired, "and always for the same reason: the client's behavior was eroding the morale of the people working on his account." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*.

## Examples

**Situation / Ad:** Rolls-Royce — "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in the new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."
**Application:** Ogilvy personally spent three weeks reading about the car. He wrote twenty-six headlines. He shut the door and wrote. The headline came out of a single British motor-magazine line he read during the research. The body copy is 607 words piled up one fact at a time. No committee wrote any part of this.
**Result:** Sales rose fifty per cent in 1958 against 1957. The work would not have survived a committee because no committee would have let a headline be that quiet, that specific, or that long in the body copy. Authorship, not teamwork.

**Situation / Ad:** Guinness — "The Guinness Guide to Oysters" (1950).
**Application:** The first ad Ogilvy wrote as head of his own agency. He immersed himself in a Yale biologist's book on shellfish. Then, in his own words: "You won't believe this, I've had an idea." He structured the ad as nine oyster descriptions. One person, one big idea, one night.
**Result:** The ad became a framable reference object that readers wrote in to request. It spawned a series. One solitary author doing homework on the adjacent subject produced a campaign a focus group could not have voted into existence.

**Situation / Ad:** Dove — "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience…" (1957).
**Application:** The client's own angle — a "neutral detergent" claim — had bombed in consumer interviews. Ogilvy rewrote the positioning from the ingredient itself ("Dove is one-quarter cleansing cream — it creams your skin while you wash"). The word *Darling* was chosen because a psychologist had flashed hundreds of words on a screen and measured emotional reactions; *darling* scored high. One person made the call. The TV execution was a woman in a bathtub on the telephone — a shape no slice-of-life committee would have approved.
**Result:** Dove grew into the number-one cleansing brand in the world. The work did not come out of a strategy committee. It came out of a writer refusing to run the client's brief and refusing to let the focus group vote on the headline word.

**Situation / Ad:** The O&M "Creative Directors wanted" hiring ad (the Trumpeter Swans ad).
**Application:** Ogilvy wrote the hiring manifesto himself, in his own voice, as an ad in his own agency's signature. The copy describes what he was actually hiring for: "well-furnished minds," "exceptional curiosity about every subject under the sun," "an above-average sense of humor," "a fanatical interest in the craft of advertising." It does not describe committee members. It describes solitary authors.
**Result:** Demonstrates the point by being the point — the ad that sells the idea of hiring individuals was itself written by one individual.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let the brand manager's edit make the logo bigger, the headline softer, and the call-to-action more generic.
**Why:** Every round of stakeholder input moves the ad towards the minutes of a committee meeting. After three rounds there is no author; there is a negotiated settlement.

**Don't:** Run focus groups that vote on headlines.
**Why:** Consumers are good at telling you what they buy and why. They are terrible at predicting which of three headlines will sell. Research should tell you what the product's promise is, not which adjectives to put in front of it. "Market-research focus groups voting on headlines" is a euphemism for a committee you assembled and paid to water down your idea.

**Don't:** Have Legal soften a hard-edged claim to the point of meaninglessness.
**Why:** The point of a claim is to be sharp enough to move preference. A claim softened until it is legally unchallengeable is, almost by definition, also commercially useless. If the claim is true, defend it. If it is not true, kill the ad — don't neuter it.

**Don't:** Pitch a campaign with three people sharing the microphone.
**Why:** Ogilvy and Sam Bronfman agreed on this: one voice. Use the most persuasive advocate you have. Brief him thoroughly enough that he can stand up under cross-examination. Nobody is persuaded by a relay race.

**Don't:** Design a presentation to accommodate the client's committee rather than to make the sale.
**Why:** The flip-over easel exists so one person can read the argument aloud, word for word, while the committee reads it with their eyes. Do not depart from your printed text by a single word; the trick lies in assaulting your audience simultaneously through their eyes and their ears. If they see one set of words and hear a different set, they become confused and inattentive.

**Don't:** Say yes to every brand-manager edit because you want to keep the meeting short.
**Why:** A habit of graceful surrender on trivial issues earns you the right to stand and fight on major ones. A habit of graceful surrender on *every* issue earns you a portfolio of committee-written ads.

**Don't:** Let the CEO weigh in on comma placement.
**Why:** This is an anti-pattern because what looks like a comma is usually something else — the CEO is uncomfortable with the claim, or the tone, or the audacity of the headline, and the comma is the hill he can articulate. Flush the real objection into the open and deal with it. The comma is a decoy.

**Don't:** Let "teamwork" substitute for authorship in the creative process.
**Why:** Teamwork is indispensable in running an agency, in landing an account, in shipping a campaign. It is not how the ad gets written. "Every agency of consequence is the lengthened shadow of one man." A team can criticize. A team cannot create.

**Don't:** Accept a brief that tries to cover four objectives, three audiences, and a tagline the CEO's wife likes.
**Why:** "Most campaigns are too complicated. They reflect a long list of objectives, and try to reconcile the divergent views of too many executives. By attempting to cover too many things, they achieve nothing." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*. A brief is a decision, not a catalogue.

**Don't:** Let the creative-director-by-committee pattern calcify — three people who must all say yes before anything ships.
**Why:** A creative department needs one person who owns the answer. When three people must agree, either one of them is decorative (in which case, send him home) or the department will only ship work all three could have written — which is, by definition, work that nobody who had the guts to ship a great ad would have approved.

**Don't:** Design the campaign in brain-storming sessions with a whiteboard.
**Why:** Brain-storming is wheel-spinning by another name. The big idea comes from one person who has done the homework and shut the door. If your creative process requires a conference room and ten markers, you do not yet have a creative process.
