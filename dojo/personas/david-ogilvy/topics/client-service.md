# Client Service

## When to Use
- Managing the relationship with a live client — especially at the seven-year-itch point when the relationship is due to be reappraised.
- Diagnosing an account that is slipping: the top person has become invisible, work is being strained through too many levels, the copy is being rewritten by committee.
- Deciding whether to defend a campaign the client wants to kill, or to capitulate and run the silly doggerel. (I once capitulated on Rinso. It was the worst mistake of my career.)
- Training account executives — the people who have to carry the agency's judgement into the client's building and bring the client's facts back out.

## Fails When
- **The "client" is a five-level approval chain with no single counterpart.** Then the problem is the client's structure, not your service. "Don't strain your advertising through too many levels." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- **The relationship is propped up on one personal tie at the top.** When the CEO who likes your CEO retires, the account goes with him. "The most dangerous thing that can happen to an agency is to depend on a single personal tie with a client company." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- **"Service" has become code for appeasement.** Capitulating to every client whim is not service; it is fear. "Frightened agencies lose the courage to give candid advice; once you lose that you become a lackey." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- **The product is bad and the agency keeps advertising it.** "It is flagrantly dishonest for an advertising agent to urge consumers to buy a product which he would not allow his own wife to buy." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The service at that point is to tell the client, or to resign.

## Core Concept

"The recommendations we make to our clients are the recommendations we would make if we owned their companies, without regard to our own interest." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

Client service is not obsequiousness and it is not negotiation. It is the posture of a professional who has taken on somebody else's problem as if it were his own, and who will tell the client what he believes even when the belief is unpleasant. Ogilvy's letter to the Rolls-Royce people when the new cars arrived with broken gearboxes and fogging air-conditioning in Central Park was the one sentence summary: "the last 600 cars you sent to the United States don't work. And I will no longer be a party to recommending that people buy them. I resign." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* The head of Rolls-Royce, an engineer, wrote back and agreed. That is what serving a client looks like: telling him the product is bad, and meaning it.

The seven-year itch afflicts agency-client relationships as often as it afflicts matrimony. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The average client changes agencies every seven years. Winning a new account is a heady experience; losing one is pure hell. The agency head lives with that fear. The question is what to do about it. Ogilvy's answer had six parts. First, devote your best brains to the service of your present clients rather than the pursuit of new ones. Forbid account executives to hunt new clients; it corrupts them like playing the horses, and they start neglecting the present clients. Second, avoid hiring masochists who unconsciously provoke client rejection. Third, refuse clients who have a record of firing their agencies — you will not cure them of their infidelity. Fourth, keep in contact at *every* level of the client, because the top-to-top tie is not enough when there are five levels of brand managers between the president and the work. Fifth, develop a shot in your locker — as soon as a campaign is approved, begin work on the next one in test markets, so you are ready when the first flops or is disapproved. Sixth, sit on the same side of the table. Buy shares in the client's company. Think like a member of their family.

At the same time, the service must refuse to be bullied. A client who rewrites your copy in committee, who pressures your staff into silly campaigns, who insists on approving the work through five levels — these are not the normal frictions of a professional relationship; they are the early warnings of a service death. "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year. However, there are two circumstances in which resignation is the wisest choice: (1) When the agency would be more profitable without the account; this is uncommon. (2) When the client bullies the agency to such an extent that the morale of your staff is seriously impaired and starts hurting their performance on other accounts." — *Principles of Management* Ogilvy resigned accounts three times as often as he was fired. Rolls-Royce, when the cars stopped working. A client whose executive vice-president was "a shit" who was demoralising both his own people and the agency's. — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* Rinso — a campaign he capitulated on against his own judgement, wrote "the silliest copy in the history of advertising", and was fired six months later. "This episode taught me that it does not pay to appease clients on matters of grand strategy. One Munich was enough." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

The Shell relationship is the instructive contrast. Ogilvy stayed up all night drafting answers to the Shell questionnaire; most of the answers were more candid than customary. He flew to London chasing Max Burns; he shared an umbrella with him in the rain and, walking to the House, gave him the gist of his answers. Back in New York three weeks later the account was won. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Shell's brief then yielded the Platformate fact that reversed a seven-year decline in share. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Every stage of that relationship was the same posture: do the homework, be candid, be willing to walk, and give the client the work you would give yourself if you were him. A client served on those terms keeps you for decades. One served on any other terms will leave you at the next seven-year itch.

## How to Apply

1. **Emancipate your client relationships from fear.** If the agency is frightened of being fired, it will stop giving candid advice, and it will be fired within eighteen months anyway. Give candid counsel; protect your staff from client bullies; resign when you cannot do either. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

2. **Tell clients the truth about their product.** "In the end, clients are grateful to advertising agents who tell them the truth." — Sir Frederic Hooper, quoted in *Confessions of an Advertising Man* When I told one client that I had doubts about the consistency of his spaghetti, we lost the account. Some losses are worth incurring.

3. **Keep in contact at every level.** The top-to-top tie is fragile. Get your media, research, production and copy people in front of the client's counterparts. A relationship "wired in at every level" survives the retirement of the ally at the top.

4. **Have a shot in your locker.** As soon as a campaign is approved, begin work on the next one in test markets. When the first flops, you are ready. Restless preparation of reserves cuts into profits and prolongs tenure.

5. **Never appease on matters of grand strategy.** Rinso was the exception and it cost me the account and my reputation in the agency for years. A client who insists on a silly strategy will fire you anyway; capitulate and you lose both the account and your credibility.

6. **Resign when you lose confidence in the product.** Do not go on advertising a product you would not allow your own wife to buy. Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce "Lemons" letter is the form: name the fault, announce the resignation, send it. — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*

7. **Resign when the client demoralises your staff.** "I'm not going to allow this man to go on demoralizing the people of Ogilvy & Mather." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* An abusive client inflicts costs that compound across every other account you hold.

8. **Refuse clients whose ethos is incompatible with yours, up front.** I refused Charles Revson of Revlon and Lew Rosenstiel of Schenley; the revenue would have been enormous and the firm would not have recovered from either. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The dinner-table test: would you be ashamed to tell your family what the account is doing? If so, refuse.

9. **Make the presentations yourself when it matters.** "The final choice of agency is almost always made by the head of the client company, and chairmen should be harangued by chairmen." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Delegation has its place; the big presentations are not it.

10. **Admit your mistakes before the client charges you with them.** "Many clients are surrounded by buckpassers who make a fine art of blaming the agency for their own failures. I seize the earliest opportunity to assume the blame." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The client who sees you take responsibility early stops looking for someone else to fire.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Shell, winning the account (mid-1950s) and the Platformate campaign that followed.
**Application:** Ogilvy stayed up all night drafting answers to Shell's questionnaire, more candid than customary. He flew to London chasing Max Burns, shared an umbrella with him in the rain, and gave him the answers on the walk to the House of Commons. Three weeks later the account was won. Then a client briefing revealed that Shell gasoline contained Platformate, which increased mileage — a fact nobody at the agency could have known without the briefing. The campaign that followed reversed a seven-year decline in Shell's share of market. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*; *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Shell became the largest account Ogilvy had ever got. Demonstrates the posture: do the homework, be candid to the point of eccentricity, chase the decision-maker personally, listen harder than you pitch, and let the client's own facts generate the campaign.

**Ad / Situation:** The Rolls-Royce "Lemons" resignation letter.
**Application:** "The last 600 cars you sent to the United States don't work. And I will no longer be a party to recommending that people buy them. I resign." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*
**Result:** The head of Rolls-Royce, an engineer, wrote back and agreed. Demonstrates the second test for resigning — resign when the product is bad, because continuing to advertise it is dishonest. The resignation preserved the agency's integrity and cost nothing but an account that was about to destroy its own brand anyway.

**Ad / Situation:** Resigning the account whose Executive Vice-President was demoralising staff.
**Application:** "I said because your Executive Vice President is a shit… he's treating *your* people atrociously and he's treating *my* people atrociously. Now what he does to your people – that's your business. But I'm not going to allow this man to go on demoralizing the people of Ogilvy & Mather. So goodbye." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy*
**Result:** Demonstrates the "morale of your staff" test. The client relationship is worth the revenue only so long as the morale cost is lower than the billing. When the bully inflicts costs on every other account, the right service to your firm is resignation.

**Ad / Situation:** The Rinso capitulation (1954).
**Application:** Jerry Babb at Lever Brothers insisted on advertising old Rinso soap powder and new Rinso Blue detergent in the same ad, with "a note of whimsical gaiety." Ogilvy knew it was wrong. He capitulated. He wrote doggerel verse to be sung to the tune of "Boys and Girls Come Out to Play." The campaign ran. Six months later the account was fired, and Ogilvy "deserved to be." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
**Result:** The most expensive lesson in Ogilvy's career. "It does not pay to appease clients on matters of grand strategy. One Munich was enough." Demonstrates that client service is not capitulation; capitulation loses the account and the agency's own self-respect in one move.

**Ad / Situation:** Ellerton Jette's Hathaway brief.
**Application:** "We are about to start advertising. Our account will be less than $30,000 a year. If you will take it on, I will make you a promise: I will never change a word of your copy." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Jette kept his word. He never changed a word.
**Result:** Hathaway — 116 years old and unknown — became a household name on a minute budget; the campaign ran for 21 years. Demonstrates what a good client looks like from the agency's side, and how to recognise one: he briefs thoroughly, promises not to edit, and keeps the promise.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Let account executives hunt new clients on company time.
**Why:** "It corrupts them, like playing the horses. They start neglecting their present clients, and the revolving door starts spinning." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The best defence against client attrition is better service to present clients.

**Don't:** Rely on a single personal tie at the top of the client's organisation.
**Why:** "The most dangerous thing that can happen to an agency is to depend on a single personal tie with a client company." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The friend at the top retires, gets sick, moves jobs. Wire in at every level or prepare to lose the account with the retirement.

**Don't:** Strain advertising through five levels of client approval.
**Why:** "It can cause leakage of secret information. It ties up able men in an interminable series of unnecessary meetings. It complicates the pristine simplicity of the original submissions. And, worst of all, it poisons the atmosphere with 'creative politics'." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Insist on two levels and no more.

**Don't:** Appease the client on matters of grand strategy.
**Why:** Rinso. A campaign you know is wrong will fail, the client will fire you for the failure, and you will have written silly copy for nothing. "One Munich was enough." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Don't:** Keep advertising a product you would not let your own family buy.
**Why:** The dinner-table test is the only ethical backstop that survives a profitable quarter. "It is flagrantly dishonest for an advertising agent to urge consumers to buy a product which he would not allow his own wife to buy." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Tell the client, or resign.

**Don't:** Take an account so big you cannot afford to lose it.
**Why:** "The day you do that, you commit yourself to living with fear. Frightened agencies lose the courage to give candid advice; once you lose that you become a lackey." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Refuse accounts whose size would make you a lackey to them.

**Don't:** Resign in pique.
**Why:** "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year." — *Principles of Management* The two legitimate reasons are unprofitable-and-unfixable, or demoralising-to-your-staff. Pique is neither.

**Don't:** Leak one client's secrets or results to another, even when flattered to do so.
**Why:** "Once a client loses confidence in your discretion, you've had it." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The temporary advantage of the gossip costs you the trust of every other client who hears about it.

**Don't:** Let the client's incompetent advertising manager go unmentioned.
**Why:** Most reasonable clients regard it as your duty to alert them when a weak link is breaking the communication chain. Denouncing a psychotic or pathological liar is rare — I have only done it twice in fifteen years — but it is part of the job. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
