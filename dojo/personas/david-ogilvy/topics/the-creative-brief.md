# The Creative Brief

## When to Use
- At the start of a new account, a new product, or a relaunch, before a word of copy or a line of layout is attempted.
- When the agency cannot answer, on one page, what the product does, who it is for, and what the single dominant benefit will be.
- When a client is handing work to an agency and has twenty minutes to decide whether to brief them thoroughly or to wing it.
- When a draft keeps coming back off-strategy and the fault lies upstream — in a brief that never decided anything.

## Fails When
- **The brief is written before the homework is done.** A brief is the output of product study, competitive review, and consumer research — not a substitute for them. Skip the homework and the brief is a mood document.
- **The brief dictates the execution.** "Don't keep a dog and bark yourself." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The brief's job is to decide what is to be said and to whom; the copywriter's job is to decide how.
- **Every stakeholder gets a bullet and nothing is chosen.** A brief that lists twelve benefits is not a brief; it is a surrender. Dr. Johnson said, "Promise, large promise is the soul of an advertisement." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* One promise. Not twelve.
- **The brief has been produced to satisfy a process, not to feed a writer.** When I wrote briefs, I wrote them for the single copywriter who was about to shut the door of his office. If the brief cannot be read by that writer and acted on, it has failed.

## Core Concept

"The more your agency knows about your company and your product, the better job it will do for you." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

The brief is an artifact. It is not a meeting, it is not a slide deck, it is not a state of mind. It is the document that tells the solitary copywriter, at the moment he shuts his door, what product he is selling, to whom, with what single dominant benefit, and under what constraints. Before that document exists in writing, the campaign does not exist either. And the document will only be worth writing if three weeks of homework have been done first. When I got Rolls-Royce I spent three weeks reading about the car. When I got Mercedes I sent a team to Stuttgart for three weeks of interviews with engineers. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Out of that homework came the briefs; out of the briefs came the ads. No homework, no brief; no brief, no ad.

What belongs in a brief. First: the product. What does it do, how is it made, what are the facts, what is the price, what are the ingredients? When General Foods hired us for Maxwell House, they sat us at the feet of their experts for day after day of lectures on green coffee, blending, roasting, and pricing. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* That is a briefing. Second: the consumer — not a demographic but a particular person who will read one magazine and consider one purchase. What does she think, what language does she use, what would move her? "Informal conversations with half-a-dozen housewives can sometimes help a copywriter more than formal surveys." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Third: the competitive context — what every competitor has said, and with what success, over the last twenty years. Fourth — and this is the bolt that holds the whole frame together — the single dominant benefit. Not the five things we'd like to say. Not the brand pyramid. One promise, specific, concrete, performable, the one the product can deliver better than any competitor. Dove's was "creams your skin while you wash." Tested against others, it pulled 63 per cent more orders than the next best promise, and it has been the fulcrum of every Dove ad since 1955. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* That is what a brief chooses. Fifth: requirements — the mandatories, the legal, the media, the budget.

What does not belong in the brief. The headline. The layout. The tone. The copywriter's job is not to decode a tone document; it is to receive facts and a promise and then generate the craft. When the brief starts writing the ad, the ad will be the brief and the ad will be a committee minute. "Don't keep a dog and bark yourself. Any fool can write a bad advertisement, but it takes a genius to keep his hands off a good one." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Clients who do this end up with advertising by brief-committee and sales that don't move.

The brief is also where the war with "creativity" is settled in advance. I do not regard advertising as entertainment or an art form, but as a medium of information. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A brief that promises "we need something creative" is a brief that has abandoned the product and is pleading for a rescue by cleverness. The rescue never comes. The only thing that rescues a campaign is a single strong promise about a product the agency has taken the trouble to learn.

## How to Apply

1. **Do the three weeks of homework first.** Study the product. Study the category. Study the consumer. A brief written without this input is a wish list. For Rolls-Royce, three weeks of reading produced a single British motor-magazine sentence — "at 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock" — which became the headline. No reading, no line.

2. **Write the brief in one page.** Five sections: Product (what it does, key facts), Consumer (who buys, in what language, with what worry), Competition (what they claim, what they haven't), Promise (the single dominant benefit — one line, specific, performable), Requirements (mandatories, budget, media, deadline).

3. **Choose one promise and go the whole hog.** "It pays to boil down your strategy to one simple promise and go the whole hog in delivering that promise." — *How to Create Advertising That Sells* If you cannot say the promise in one line, you have not chosen yet.

4. **Test the candidate promise against consumers before you commit.** At the agency we used five research techniques — packaged-product tests, card-preference tests, matched-sample mailings, split-run print tests — to find out which promise pulled hardest. For Dove, "Creams Your Skin While You Wash" pulled 63 per cent more orders than the next best. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

5. **Keep the execution out of the brief.** No headline, no layout, no "visual direction," no "copy tone." The brief names the promise and the constraints. The writer names the craft.

6. **Give the brief to one copywriter and shut the door.** Advertising sells most when it is written by a solitary individual who has studied the product, the research and the precedents. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The brief feeds the writer; the writer shuts the door; the agency defends the result.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958).
**Application:** Three weeks of reading produced a briefing dense with specifics — the 98-point inspection, the seven-hour full-throttle engine run, the Circassian walnut picnic tables, the three-year guarantee. Out of that body of fact came one promise: engineered silence, proved by a single observable consequence. The brief did not write the headline. The brief assembled the facts from which the headline could be written. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Sales rose 50 per cent. The same body of seventeen facts was reused under nine different headlines across the campaign, because the brief — product, promise, audience — was stable enough to carry the brand for a decade.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "one-quarter cleansing cream" positioning (1955 onward).
**Application:** The client's brief arrived with a "neutral detergent for men with dirty hands" angle. Consumer research killed that angle. A review of the ingredient list turned up that Dove contained one-quarter cleansing cream; the research lab confirmed it creamed the skin while you washed. That fact, tested against other candidate promises, beat the next-best by 63 per cent. Only then was the brief rewritten — one promise: "Dove creams your skin while you wash." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Dove became the number-one cleansing brand in the world. Demonstrates that the brief is an output of testing, not a starting assumption. The client's initial brief was wrong; the tested brief carried the brand for thirty years.

**Ad / Situation:** Shell, the Platformate campaign (late 1950s).
**Application:** "A briefing from the client revealed something which came as a surprise to me; that gasoline has several ingredients, including Platformate, which increases mileage." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* One fact, surfaced because the briefer troubled to volunteer it, became the campaign.
**Result:** A seven-year decline in Shell's share of market reversed. Demonstrates that a brief's job is to surface the fact the chemist takes for granted — the single specific that the copywriter would never guess without being told.

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway, "The Man in the Hathaway Shirt" (1951).
**Application:** Ellerton Jette, the client, came with a $30,000 budget and a promise worth its weight in gold: "I will never change a word of your copy." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* That is what a good brief looks like from the client's side. He told us what he wanted (a brand put on the map), gave us the product (shirts he was proud of), and got out of the way of the execution.
**Result:** A 116-year-old unknown shirt-maker became a household name; the campaign ran for 21 years. Demonstrates that the brief is bilateral: the best briefs come from clients who brief thoroughly and then refuse to edit.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a brief that reads like a tone document ("warm, confident, modern, aspirational").
**Why:** Tone is downstream of promise. A brief that names a mood but no benefit has chosen nothing. The writer will either invent a promise you did not sanction, or produce atmosphere without a sale. Both are failures of the brief, not the writer.

**Don't:** Let every department put a requirement in the brief.
**Why:** A brief with twelve "must-mentions" is a brief that has capitulated to the committee before the copy has been written. "Most manufacturers are reluctant to accept any limitation on the image of their brand. They want it to be all things to all people… They generally end up with a brand which has no personality of any kind, a wishy-washy neuter." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Capon briefs produce capon ads.

**Don't:** Specify the headline, the layout, or the visual style in the brief.
**Why:** "Don't keep a dog and bark yourself. Any fool can write a bad advertisement, but it takes a genius to keep his hands off a good one." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The brief decides what; the writer decides how. Cross that line and you are paying an agency to execute your layout instead of to out-think your competitor.

**Don't:** Skip the product study because "we already know the product."
**Why:** What you know is what your last brand manager wrote. What the copywriter needs is the ingredient, the process fact, the price, the thing the chemist has forgotten to mention. Shell's Platformate sat unremarked in briefings for years until somebody asked. The brief's job is to surface these, not to rehearse what everyone already agrees on.

**Don't:** Use the brief as a contract for what cannot be changed.
**Why:** A brief is a working document, updated as research comes in. The Dove brief was rewritten when the original angle tested poorly. A brief frozen at the start of the project is a brief that cannot learn.

**Don't:** Pretend the brief is optional because the agency is "creative."
**Why:** The creative work downstream is only as good as the homework upstream. A brilliant agency given no brief produces brilliant advertising for the wrong product to the wrong buyer. The brief is the pipe through which facts reach the writer; no pipe, no water.
