# Startup Hubs: Location, Ecosystem, and Why SF Still Matters

## When to Use
- A founder is deciding whether to move their startup to the Bay Area (or whichever hub is relevant to their domain).
- A founder is defending staying in their home city and wants a grown-up answer about what they're actually giving up — not a rah-rah SF pitch, and not a nationalistic "your city is fine."
- An ambitious early-career person is trying to figure out where to live for the next several years.
- A founder is trying to understand why their fundraise is harder, their hiring is harder, and their momentum is lower than comparable companies in a hub, when on paper they shouldn't be.

## Fails When
- Non-negotiable personal constraints rule out relocation (kids in school, a spouse's career locked to another city, visa issues that would take a year to resolve). Acknowledge and work with the constraint — don't pretend it doesn't matter, but don't demand they fix what can't be fixed.
- The founder's startup is tied to a specific non-tech industry hub (entertainment → NY/LA, finance → NY/London, government contracting → DC). Those industries have their own geographic logic.
- The real blocker is something else — product, team, runway — and the location question is a distraction from the actual problem.

## Core Concept

If you look at a list of US cities sorted by population, the number of successful startups per capita varies by orders of magnitude. It's as if most places were sprayed with startupicide.

I used to think of it that way — that the average town was a roach motel for startup ambitions: smart, ambitious people went in, but no startups came out. But the framing is wrong. The problem is not that most towns kill startups. **Death is the default for startups. Most towns don't save them. A few places are sprayed with the antidote.** Startups in other places are just doing what startups naturally do: fail. The real question is not what kills them. The real question is: what's *saving* startups in places like Silicon Valley?

There are two components to the antidote, and one underlying cause.

**First component: being in a place where startups are the cool thing to do.** It's quite a leap to start a startup. In most places, if you start one, people treat you as if you're unemployed. In the Valley it's not only real but fashionable. People in the Valley aren't automatically impressed because you're starting a company, but they pay attention. Anyone who's been there any amount of time knows not to default to skepticism no matter how inexperienced you seem or how unpromising your idea sounds, because they've all seen inexperienced founders with unpromising-sounding ideas who a few years later were billionaires.

Having people around you care about what you're doing is an extraordinarily powerful force. Even the most willful people are susceptible to it. It's not that you do whatever a city expects of you — it's that you get discouraged when no one around you cares about the same things you do. There's an imbalance between encouragement and discouragement like the one between gaining and losing money. Most people will work much harder to avoid losing a dollar than to gain one. Similarly, plenty of people are strong enough to resist doing something just because that's what one is supposed to do where they happen to be — but few are strong enough to keep working on something no one around them cares about.

Because ambitions are to some extent incompatible and admiration is a zero-sum game, each city tends to focus on one type of ambition. Cambridge tells you: you should be smarter. New York tells you: you should make more money. The Valley tells you: you should be more powerful — in the sense of how much effect you have on the world. Berkeley, at least the time I lived there, tells you: you should live better. San Francisco's message, at least historically, was the same as Berkeley's, though that has shifted as the center of gravity of the Valley has moved north. Professors in New York and the Bay Area are second-class citizens until they start hedge funds or startups respectively — there's already something else people there admire more, and you can feel that in every conversation.

**Second component: chance meetings with people who can help you.** This is more variable than the background radiation of the first component, but at its strongest it's far stronger. Chance meetings produce miracles to compensate for the disasters that characteristically befall startups. Terrible things happen to startups everywhere; in the Valley, great things also happen to them — so lightning has a sign bit. You start a site for college students and move to Palo Alto for the summer, and on a random suburban street you happen to run into Sean Parker, who understands the domain because he started a similar company, and also knows all the investors, and has advanced views for 2004 on founders retaining control of their companies. You can't say in advance what the miracle will be, or for sure that one will happen. The best you can say is: if you're in a startup hub, unexpected good things will probably happen to you, especially if you deserve them.

Chance meetings play a role like the role relaxation plays in having ideas. You work hard on a problem, can't solve it, go to bed, and think of the answer in the shower. Letting your thoughts drift drops you onto the right path adjacent to the wrong one. Chance meetings let your acquaintance drift in the same way — just the right amount, just far enough to meet someone you had a lot in common with, but not so far that the meeting is random noise.

**The underlying cause of both is the number of startup people around you.** Density produces the chance meetings. Density produces the social norm where founding a startup is a normal thing to do. Density produces enough specialists that your one specific strange need — a database guy, a movie-industry connection, a Sean Parker — will likely be met. The larger the community, the higher the chance that it contains the one person who has the one thing you need most. And once you have enough people working on the same problem, they start to set the norms. The atmosphere encourages you to do something that would otherwise seem too ambitious, instead of pulling you back toward the mean.

The physical infrastructure isn't what makes the Valley work. Weather isn't what makes it work. The university started it, but the reaction has long been self-sustaining. What drives it now is the people. The antidote is people.

**The investor argument.** The second-order effect: hubs have better investors. Silicon Valley investors are noticeably more aggressive than Boston ones. Over and over, west coast investors snatch deals out from under Boston investors who saw the company first and acted too slowly. Boston investors will admit they're more conservative. Some want to believe this comes from the city's prudent Yankee character. Occam's razor suggests the truth is less flattering: **Boston investors are more conservative than Silicon Valley investors for the same reason Chicago investors are more conservative than Boston ones. They don't understand startups as well.** West coast investors aren't bolder because they're irresponsible cowboys. They're the skiers who ski the diamond slopes. Boldness is the essence of venture investing. Empirically, boldness wins. If the aggressive ways of west coast investors were going to come back to bite them, it has been a long time coming.

**The argument from reductio.** You can easily reduce the opposing argument ad absurdum. Few would claim that location doesn't matter at all — that a startup in a small agricultural town wouldn't benefit from moving to a hub. Most people can see that it might help to be in a place with infrastructure for startups, accumulated knowledge about how to make them work, and other people trying to do it. But whatever argument you use to prove that startups don't need to move from London to the Valley would equally well prove that startups don't need to move from smaller towns to London. The difference between cities is a matter of degree. If startups are better off in the Valley than Boston — and nearly everyone who knows agrees they are — then they're better off in the Valley than everywhere else too.

## How to Apply

1. **Be honest that the question is whether to move, not whether it matters.** The question is never *does location matter in some abstract sense*. The question is always: should I, specifically, move my specific startup now? Location matters. The real work is weighing the benefit against the specific cost of your specific move.

2. **Count the legitimate reasons to stay.** There are real ones. A founder with young kids and a working spouse has a cost of moving that a 23-year-old doesn't. Immigration difficulties consume attention the way fundraising does — one Canadian YC startup spent six months trying to move to the US and eventually gave up because they couldn't afford that much time away from their product. If you're in an industry where the real hub is elsewhere (entertainment in LA, a specific biotech cluster in Boston), go there instead. And if a good investor has *committed* — not "seemed interested" — to fund you if you stay put, don't pass up a definite funding offer to chase a general theory.

3. **Don't fool yourself about the soft costs.** The soft cost of not being in a hub is real but easy to discount because it shows up as absence of good things rather than presence of bad ones. Your fundraise takes longer. Your hiring is harder. You don't happen to have a useful coffee with a person who would have changed your trajectory. The disaster doesn't strike; the miracle that would have saved you from a different disaster just never happens. That cost is invisible in the daily experience of running your startup and very visible in the twelve-month retrospective.

4. **If you stay, know what you're compensating for.** Founders in non-hub cities who succeed are almost always exceptionally disciplined. They generate their own morale, because the background radiation is pulling them back toward the mean. Wufoo was based in Tampa and succeeded — but the Wufoos were exceptionally disciplined. If you're staying, you need to be explicitly generating what the hub would have given you ambiently: peers to care about what you're doing, chance exposures to people who can help, and an expectation that running a startup is normal.

5. **Don't read moving as nationalism.** This is about cities, not countries. YC tells US startups in Boston they should move to the Valley. The question is never "is your country good enough?" It's "are you in the highest-density place for the thing you're trying to do?"

6. **Pay attention to the second-order signal: the chance-meeting density.** If you fly into an area and within a day have three unplanned encounters with people working on exactly what you're working on, the hub is doing its job. If six months in a city produces zero such encounters, the density isn't there and won't appear.

7. **Try living in several places when you're young.** You can never tell what message a city sends until you live there, or even whether it still sends one. Tried Florence at 25 thinking it was an art center; turned out to be 450 years too late. Moved to New York; it was exciting for a while, then it turned out I just wasn't like the people there. The message that'll resonate with you is not predictable from outside — but it's unmistakable once you're in it. If you're early in your career and not sure yet what you want, optionality is cheap and lived experience of each place is the only real data.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder in a mid-sized European city is pitching: "We're a software company, we have a great team here, our cost of living is a third of SF, and we work on Slack with investors anyway. Remote is fine. Why would we move?"

**Application:** Every argument you just made is also an argument for never leaving your parents' basement, only more so. Reduce ad absurdum: a startup in a small agricultural town would clearly benefit from moving to your mid-sized city. Everything that made them better off by moving applies even more strongly to the move from your city to a hub. The things you are going to lose by staying are mostly invisible — you won't see the hire you didn't make, the investor who said no because they hadn't run into your partner at dinner, the chance meeting that would have flipped your trajectory. You'll see the costs of moving (rent, visas, disruption) vividly and you'll feel clever for avoiding them. Meanwhile the companies that moved are having a conversation per week that you're not having. If you have a binding reason to stay — a spouse's career, kids in school — fine, that's a real reason, and you can compensate with discipline. But if the reason is "remote works fine," the answer is: it works fine in the sense that your company won't immediately die. It will just grow slower, raise less, and hit a ceiling sooner than you can currently see.

**Result:** Founder relocates two of three cofounders to the Bay for a year. Within six months has made two key hires and closed a round they'd been unable to close for eight months.

---

**Situation:** A 24-year-old with vague ambition and some savings asks where they should live for the next few years. They have no startup idea yet. They're choosing between their hometown (comfortable, cheap), New York (exciting, expensive), and the Bay.

**Application:** Try several. At 24, with no dependents and no specific plan, you have the cheapest possible optionality, and you should use it. Here's the complication: you haven't picked what kind of ambition you have yet, and different cities send different messages. New York will tell you to make money. The Valley will tell you to have effect on the world. Cambridge would tell you to be smarter. You won't know which message resonates until you live there — but the message resonating is how you figure out which kind of ambition you actually have. It sounds evasive but it's not: most ambitious people feel the ambition before they know what to be ambitious about. Going somewhere that takes one kind of ambition seriously helps you find out if that's your flavor. Go. Spend a year in one. Spend a year in another. You'll know. Your hometown, unless it's secretly a center for something, is almost certainly not the place — and staying there because it's cheap will feel thrifty and turn out to be the most expensive choice you made in your twenties.

**Result:** Person spends a year in New York, realizes the money-ambition message doesn't resonate, moves to the Bay, and within two years is cofounding a company with people they met through the density.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Stay put because moving feels like admitting the local ecosystem isn't good enough.
**Why:** This is the nationalist framing. It makes a practical question into an identity question. The right frame is not "is my city good enough?" — it's "is my startup going to do better in a denser place?" The first question invites defensive answers. The second invites real ones.

**Don't:** Move because you feel it's expected, when you have a binding local reason to stay.
**Why:** If you genuinely have a committed investor, a working spouse, or a kid in the middle of the school year, and you move anyway, you'll be distracted, resentful, and worse off than if you'd stayed and compensated with discipline. The Wufoos succeeded in Tampa. It's possible. It requires being exceptional at generating your own morale, but it's possible.

**Don't:** Confuse "we raised from Silicon Valley VCs on Zoom" with "we don't need to be there."
**Why:** You can raise from anywhere. The marginal raise is not what the hub gives you. What the hub gives you is a decade of ambient density — conversations, hires, chance-correlated miracles — that you don't get by quarterly flights out. If the only interaction you want from the hub is the wire transfer, you've priced in only 20% of the value.

**Don't:** Announce loudly that you're staying remote / in your home city as a principled stand.
**Why:** Principles about location are usually post-hoc rationalizations for decisions made for practical reasons. That's fine. But when you frame the practical decision as a principle, you cut off future adjustment. If six months from now the density argument is winning, you should be able to move without losing face. Don't paint yourself into a corner.

**Don't:** Move to a hub and then fail to use it.
**Why:** If you move and keep working in the same closed loop, just now paying three times the rent, you've gotten the cost and none of the benefit. The point of the hub is the density — the chance meetings, the peer exposure, the conversations. Go to the events. Take the coffees. Live walkable. Work from places where other startup people also work. If you're treating the hub like a remote office with better weather, you might as well have stayed home.

**Don't:** Treat "I can't tell what message a city sends from here" as a reason not to go.
**Why:** You can't tell in advance. You can only tell by living there. The information is literally not available from outside, by design. This makes visiting-before-committing seem wise, but a one-week visit gives you almost no signal — the conversations that reveal what a city is actually like happen in the ninety-day to one-year range, not the one-week range. If you have cheap optionality (young, no dependents), go. If you have expensive optionality, do the longest legitimate stint you can before committing.
