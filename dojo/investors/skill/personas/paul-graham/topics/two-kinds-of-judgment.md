# Two Kinds of Judgment

## When to Use
- A founder has been rejected by investors and is in a spiral of "they must see something wrong with us."
- A founder is trying to evaluate whether a specific VC, advisor, or would-be hire is actually a good judge of their business — or just a confident-sounding person whose approval feels significant.
- A founder is deciding how much to weight credentials (elite-school hires, "ex-FAANG", "prior founder of unicorn") in a specific decision.
- A founder is passive in selling themselves because they assume the people doing the selecting are thorough and fair.
- A founder hires a committee to evaluate something critical and the committee produces a bland, safe answer that nobody actually believes.

## Fails When
- The judgment really *is* the end goal — an honest one-on-one review from a trusted mentor, a court case, a formal test where someone is being paid to judge you correctly. Those are the rare cases where taking the judgment seriously is correct.
- The founder is using this reframe to dismiss real, actionable feedback. "It wasn't really about me" is true about most rejections; it is not true about every rejection. If three investors have given the same specific product critique, that critique is data, even if the rejection is a process.
- The problem is general demoralization, not a specific judgment being misread.

## Core Concept

There are two different ways people judge you. Sometimes judging you correctly is the end goal. But there's a second, much more common type of judgment where it isn't. **We tend to regard all judgments of us as the first type. We'd probably be happier if we realized which are and which aren't.**

**Judgment as the end goal** includes court cases, grades in classes, and most competitions. Such judgments can be mistaken, but because the goal is to judge you correctly, there's usually some kind of appeals process. If you feel you've been misjudged, you can protest that you've been treated unfairly. Nearly all the judgments made on children are of this type, so we get into the habit early in life of thinking that all judgments are.

**Judgment as a means to something else** includes college admissions, hiring, investment decisions, and dating. This kind of judgment is not really about you. Put yourself in the position of someone selecting players for a national team. Suppose there are no positions and you have to select 20 players. There will be a few stars who clearly make the team and many who clearly don't. The only place your judgment makes a difference is in the borderline cases. Suppose you screw up and underestimate the 20th best player, so his place is taken by the 21st. You've still picked a good team. If the players have the usual distribution of ability, the 21st will be only slightly worse than the 20th — probably less than the measurement error.

The 20th best player may feel misjudged. But your goal wasn't to provide a service estimating people's ability. It was to pick a team. If the difference between 20 and 21 is less than the measurement error, you've still done that optimally.

**It's a false analogy even to use the word *unfair* to describe this kind of misjudgment.** It's not aimed at producing a correct estimate of any individual; it's aimed at selecting a reasonably optimal set. One thing that leads us astray: the selector seems to be in a position of power. That makes him seem like a judge. If you regard someone judging you as a customer instead of a judge, the expectation of fairness goes away. The author of a good novel wouldn't complain that readers were *unfair* for preferring a potboiler with a racy cover. Stupid, perhaps, but not unfair.

Our early training and our self-centeredness combine to make us believe that every judgment of us is about us. In fact most aren't. **This is a rare case where being less self-centered will make people more confident.** Once you realize how little most people judging you care about judging you accurately — once you realize that because of the normal distribution of most applicant pools, it matters least to judge accurately in precisely the cases where judgment has the most effect — you won't take rejection so personally.

And taking rejection less personally may help you to get rejected less often. If you think someone judging you will work hard to judge you correctly, you can afford to be passive. But the more you realize that most judgments are greatly influenced by random extraneous factors — that most people judging you are more like fickle novel buyers than wise and perceptive magistrates — the more you realize you can do things to influence the outcome.

Most high school students applying to college do it with the usual child's mix of inferiority and self-centeredness: inferiority in assuming admissions committees must be all-seeing; self-centeredness in assuming they care enough about them to dig down and figure out whether they're good. These combine to make applicants passive and hurt when they're rejected. If they realized how quick and impersonal most selection processes are, they'd make more effort to sell themselves, and take the outcome less personally.

**There's a related point about credentials.** Credentials are, functionally, a way of predicting performance. If you could measure actual performance, you wouldn't need them. They evolved because in large organizations individual performance is hard to measure — and the harder performance is to measure, the more important it is to predict it. Large organizations can't measure individuals, so they fall back on credentials as a proxy. A market takes every organization and keeps just the good ones; as organizations get smaller, this approaches taking every person and keeping just the good ones. So all other things being equal, a society of smaller organizations cares less about credentials. In a startup, nobody cares whether you've graduated from college, let alone which one. All they care about is what you can do. Which, in fact, is all that ever should have mattered.

## How to Apply

1. **Classify the judgment.** Before you let a rejection or an approval land emotionally, ask: was judging me correctly the goal, or is this judgment a means to something else? A court verdict on you is a real judgment. An investor pass is a selection decision on their portfolio. A college rejection is a construction of a freshman class. A hiring no is a team-composition decision. In all the second category, the judgment wasn't about producing an accurate estimate of you. Don't treat it as one.

2. **Downgrade rejections accordingly.** The average VC is a rejection machine — one to two deals per year out of five hundred or a thousand plans received. Their "no" has almost no signal about the quality of your company, because their selection process does not and cannot examine your company carefully enough to produce a real verdict. Use specific content of the no (if they articulated a concrete product or market concern, that's data) but don't let the volume of undifferentiated "no"s accumulate as evidence. It isn't evidence.

3. **Stop being passive.** If you wrongly assume the other side is doing careful, fair, thorough work on you, you feel it would be unseemly to push. So you don't. The correct mental model is that they are overloaded, distracted, using crude signals, and biased toward legible proxies. That means you should be actively selling yourself — aggressively, concretely, with specific hooks and demonstrations — because the "judge" is really a fickle customer, not a perceptive magistrate. Selling yourself isn't unseemly. Not selling yourself is the mistake.

4. **Upgrade your own judgment by knowing what type you're making.** When *you* are selecting — hiring, funding, choosing a cofounder — know which kind of judgment you're doing. If you're picking a team, you're selecting an optimal set, not grading each candidate. You will mis-judge some individuals, and that's okay; it isn't "unfair"; and you should spend your precision budget on the decisions that actually move the composition, not on perfectly ranking candidates who are all below the cutoff or all above it.

5. **Don't let credentials do the selecting for you in a small organization.** Credentials are for organizations that can't measure performance. A startup can measure performance directly within weeks of someone joining. Use credentials as a weak prior; use actual work product as the strong signal. A résumé is a compressed, lossy, heavily-filtered proxy written by someone who was not trying to help you. The person's last month of output is the real data.

6. **Be careful whose judgment you weight heavily.** When someone gives you advice, the question isn't "are they impressive?" The question is "are they good at the specific kind of judgment this decision requires, and do they have skin in the outcome?" A partner at a big firm who has never shipped a product can give you a confident, articulate, authoritative opinion on your product. It remains an uninformed opinion. The social pressure to weight it heavily comes from assumptions about credential, not from any actual test of their judgment in your domain.

7. **Watch for advisors whose advice is aimed at *their* judgment rather than *your* outcome.** Some advisors give advice calibrated to how they'll look if it works out and how they'll avoid blame if it doesn't. That produces safe, middle-of-the-road advice that's legible to other advisors and often wrong for you specifically. The advisor whose judgment you want is the one who will make a specific, falsifiable, uncomfortable recommendation — and who is willing to be wrong visibly when they are wrong.

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder has pitched 22 VCs over six weeks. Every one said no. They're spiraling: "Maybe the product isn't good. Maybe I'm not the right person to run this. Maybe we should shut down."

**Application:** You're conflating two kinds of judgment. Twenty-two VC "no"s are not twenty-two verdicts on your company. They are twenty-two selection decisions by people whose job is to fill a very narrow portfolio with companies that match their specific thesis, their fund size, their timing, their existing portfolio's gaps, and a hundred other factors that have nothing to do with whether your company is good. The average VC does one to two deals a year out of hundreds of pitches. They reject almost everyone regardless of quality. That means the rejection carries almost no signal. What carries signal is the *content* of specific rejections. If six of them said the same thing about your metrics, listen to that — but don't let the aggregate sound of twenty-two "no"s become undifferentiated proof of failure. It isn't proof. It's the base rate. Now go back and look at what specific thing the best three of them actually said, and ask whether they're right. The rest is noise.

**Result:** Founder stops treating the aggregate as a verdict. Identifies that three investors raised the same product concern, addresses it, and closes the next round from investors whose thesis actually matches.

---

**Situation:** Founder is evaluating whether to listen to a well-known angel investor who's been on their board and is now arguing strongly that they should hire a COO and "professionalize" the org. The angel has prestige. The founder is uncertain.

**Application:** Two questions before you weight their judgment. First: is this person good at the specific kind of judgment this decision requires? A famous angel investor who has never operated a company at your stage may have excellent judgment about fundraising timing and very mediocre judgment about org design at a 20-person startup. Prestige is not portable across domains. Second: what would they lose if they're wrong? Are they on the hook for the consequences, or will they have moved on emotionally if the COO hire is a disaster? Advice from someone with skin in the outcome is very different from advice from someone whose reputation is unaffected either way. The fact that this person is famous and confident is not evidence that their judgment here is good. It's evidence that they're good at a *different* thing that earned them the fame. Don't let the one overwrite the other. And separately: "professionalize" is one of the most dangerous words in the founder's advisory vocabulary — it is usually the camouflage for manager-mode encroachment. Interrogate the specific thing they want, not the general direction.

**Result:** Founder declines the COO hire, talks to two other founders who've been through the stage, and discovers that what was actually needed was one senior engineering hire. Makes that hire instead. The angel's prestige, it turns out, was earned in a very different context.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Take a rejection as a verdict when the rejection was really a selection.
**Why:** Selection decisions are wrong about individuals on the margin all the time, and they're supposed to be. That isn't unfairness; it's statistics. Treating a selection's "no" as a careful judgment of you personally is a category error that will make you passive and demoralized for no good reason.

**Don't:** Treat a credential as a substitute for looking at someone's work.
**Why:** Credentials are a cheap proxy that exists because large organizations can't measure performance directly. Startups can. The résumé tells you what filter they passed through. The actual work, over the last month, tells you what they can do now. One of these is a strong signal and one is a weak prior. Don't confuse them.

**Don't:** Confuse "I was rejected" with "I should stop trying."
**Why:** The more you realize that most judgments are noisy selections rather than careful verdicts, the more you realize that rejection is a function of volume and fit, not worth. The solution isn't to interpret the rejection differently. The solution is to fix the fit (different investor, different customer, different cofounder) and, in the meantime, make twice as many attempts.

**Don't:** Trust confident advisors on the grounds that they sound confident.
**Why:** Confidence is usually a signal about how someone communicates, not about how well they've reasoned. In selection-type judgments, the person giving advice often doesn't have time or incentive to understand your specific situation carefully. Their confident recommendation is shaped by pattern-matching against the last five companies they saw, not by deep analysis of yours. The advisor you want is the one who will sit with the specifics, produce something uncomfortable and specific, and be visibly wrong when they're wrong.

**Don't:** Build a process that assumes the other side will do careful judgment for you.
**Why:** If your pitch, your application, your cold email assumes the recipient will read carefully, reason generously, and see what's great about you, you'll write it at the wrong altitude. Assume the recipient will spend 45 seconds, will pattern-match, will be distracted, will be slightly hostile. Write for that reality. You're selling to a customer, not petitioning a magistrate. Make the strongest case you have in the first paragraph; don't bury it.

**Don't:** Use "it's just selection, not really about me" as a shield against actual feedback.
**Why:** Most rejections carry no signal. Some do. The specific product concern that three separate investors raised independently is data. If you use the correct general reframe to deflect specific legitimate critiques, you've turned a useful mental model into self-deception. Separate the noise (the aggregate volume of "no") from the signal (what specific people said was wrong) and act on the signal.
