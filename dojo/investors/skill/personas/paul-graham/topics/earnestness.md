# Earnestness (and the Benevolence Advantage)

## When to Use
- Evaluating a founder — you want to distinguish the genuine article from the performer.
- A founder is being tempted to become cynical, mean, or purely strategic in a competitive fight and you want to argue for an alternative.
- A founder is worried that their sincerity is naive or that genuine niceness is a handicap in a tough market.
- Assessing whether a company's unusual success is attributable to something under their skin rather than something on the surface.

## Fails When
- The question is tactical (should I raise? should I fire this VP?) and earnestness is tangential.
- "Earnestness" is being invoked as a moral shield against hard feedback — "I'm being earnest, so you can't critique my approach." That's a misuse.

## Core Concept

There's a word I keep coming back to when I describe the quality that makes the best founders succeed. It's not "smart," though they usually are. It's not "driven," though they certainly are. The word is **earnest**.

Earnestness means both the right motives and maximum effort. It means genuine interest in the problem for its own sake — not because it will make you rich or famous, but because you actually care about it. When founders are both formidable and earnest, they're as close to unstoppable as you get.

Earnestness is the root of being a nerd. It's caring about things that other people don't care about, and caring enough to keep going when the world tells you to stop. It's related to curiosity — earnest people follow their genuine interests rather than calculated career moves. And it's related to persistence — earnest people keep working on problems because they actually want to solve them, not because they're performing effort for an audience.

The reason earnestness is so powerful is that it aligns all the other qualities. Energy, imagination, resilience, good judgment, independent-mindedness — these are all multiplied by earnestness, because when you genuinely care about something you bring your full self to it. When you don't, you bring a performance.

There's a related and often misunderstood point: **being benevolent is a competitive advantage, not just a moral preference.** It struck me once how few of the most successful people I know are mean. There's a reason for this, and it isn't just that nice people attract better employees and partners, though they do.

**Being mean makes you stupid.** That's why I hate fights. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. When you're fighting with someone, all your mental energy goes into the fight, none into the actual work. Startups don't win by attacking. They win by *transcending* — by building something so good that the competition becomes irrelevant.

There's a deeper reason too. In founder mode you're making hundreds of judgment calls a week under uncertainty. If you're mean, you have to spend mental cycles managing the consequences of that meanness — placating people you've offended, watching your back, interpreting every situation through the lens of conflict. If you're benevolent, you can use "just do whatever is best for users" as your decision compass. It's simpler, faster, and produces better results.

Companies that grow big by being nice but try to stay big by being mean eventually pay for it. You get away with it till the underlying conditions change, and then your victims all escape. Being good is not just a moral imperative; it's a competitive strategy in any sufficiently transparent market.

## How to Apply

1. **Test for earnestness by asking about the problem, not the business.** An earnest founder will talk about the user's problem with a level of specific care that is hard to fake. A performing founder will talk about the business opportunity with a level of strategic language that is easy to fake. The difference appears quickly when you press on specifics. Ask: *tell me the exact thing your favorite user does with this every day.* An earnest founder will tell you a story with texture. A performing founder will give you a demographic.

2. **Use benevolence as decision compass.** When you're unsure what to do, do whatever is best for your users. This rule cuts through more strategic debate than any framework I know. It also compounds — in a sufficiently transparent world, you can't seem good without being good, and acting consistently for users builds something no competitor can copy by being cleverer.

3. **Don't fight. Transcend.** If a competitor is eating your lunch, the instinct is to attack them. Don't. Build something so much better that the attack is unnecessary. Fights are not sufficiently general — every minute spent attacking is a minute not spent transcending. Startups don't win by attacking; they win by making the attacking unnecessary. The Ronco principle applies: in a sufficiently connected world, you can't seem good without being good. Transparency means bad behavior gets out.

4. **Distrust your slick-talking candidates.** The smoothest candidates are rarely the best builders. The founders I've seen build the most valuable companies are often the ones you'd overlook on the street. They're scrappy. They're not trying to impress anyone. They're too busy making things. Formidability is not slickness — slick is learned interview behavior; formidable is deep genuine understanding radiating out.

5. **Protect the social radar.** Jessica Livingston had what I used to call the social radar — an almost uncanny ability to see through fakers. If someone seems off to you, they probably are. The best founders I know have an almost allergic reaction to bullshit. Cultivate that sense. The most expensive mistakes in startups are almost always people mistakes, and the most dangerous people mistakes come from ignoring your instincts about character.

6. **Distinguish benevolence from conflict-avoidance.** The advice to be good is not the advice to avoid hard conversations. The opposite — you owe people the honest answer, including the hard one, which is a form of benevolence. Conflict-avoidance looks like niceness from the outside and feels like cowardice from the inside. It isn't what compounds.

7. **Earnestness scales.** Unlike many startup qualities, earnestness is not mostly useful at the larval stage and then obsolete. It compounds. The company that was earnest at 5 people will still be earnest at 500 if the hires are chosen for it. The company that started mean has already infected the culture permanently, and the infection spreads, not weakens, at scale.

## Examples

**Situation:** An investor is evaluating two founder pairs. Pair A is extremely polished — great pitch deck, practiced answers, obvious sales training, smooth. Pair B is awkward, explains their tech in too much detail, laughs at their own joke, admits they don't know the answer to one of your questions and promises to find out. A is asking for a higher valuation.

**Application:** Back B. The polished pair is almost certainly not as formidable as they look. They've invested heavily in performance, which is usually evidence they've invested correspondingly less in the underlying thing. The awkward pair, by contrast, is unpolished because they're spending their time on the substance. Formidability appears as substance radiating, not as practiced smoothness. "I don't know but I'll find out" is one of the most formidable sentences a founder can say; its rarity in high-polish pitches is informative. Follow up with B in a week and ask if they found the answer to the question. If they did — with specificity — you have the signal you needed. If they didn't, downgrade them. But the polished pair, no matter how shiny, is almost certainly the higher-risk bet.

**Result:** Investor backs B. A year later B has shipped two major releases, retained 90% of early customers, and raised a healthy Series A. A has produced a beautiful office, three keynote talks, and not much product.

---

**Situation:** A founder is locked in an aggressive competitive fight. A competitor has been spreading misleading claims about the founder's product. The founder is planning a coordinated press offensive to hit back — ads, a scathing blog post, reaching out to journalists.

**Application:** Don't. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. Every minute of your team's attention on the press offensive is a minute not spent on the thing that actually wins — making your product so good that the competitor's misleading claims stop landing. Fighting in the press is a promise that you and the competitor are the same kind of thing, and you let them set the terms. The alternative: go quiet on them, go loud on the product. Ship three meaningful improvements over the next two months. Let your users' excitement about the improvements drown out the competitor's noise organically. In the long run, the press offensive might win a battle, and definitely loses the war — because the press offensive is a specific one-shot weapon that doesn't compound, and shipping compounds. Transcend. Don't fight. Being good is not just a moral stance; in a transparent market it's the only strategy that scales.

**Result:** Founder cancels the offensive. Ships three strong updates. Competitor's claims become irrelevant as users discover the gap between claim and reality. Founder gets the quiet satisfaction of winning without fighting, which is the only satisfying way to win.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Confuse earnestness with naïveté.
**Why:** Earnest doesn't mean credulous. You can be earnest about the problem, intense about the craft, and simultaneously sharp-eyed about who's trying to take advantage of you. The naïveté confusion is what makes some founders resist earnestness — they think it's incompatible with being worldly. It isn't. It's the opposite of performative, not the opposite of clear-eyed.

**Don't:** Mistake performed niceness for benevolence.
**Why:** Performed niceness is conflict avoidance with a smile. Real benevolence often looks harsher in the moment — it involves telling people the hard truth, firing people when you need to, defending users against well-connected predators. The test is not how pleasant the interaction felt; it's whether the people involved are better off.

**Don't:** Assume earnestness is a handicap in a tough market.
**Why:** The opposite. The toughest markets reward the compound effect of earnestness most, because the dishonest operators burn themselves out while you're still building trust with users, employees, and investors. Short-term, cynicism looks like it wins. Over a decade, it loses almost every time.

**Don't:** Use meanness to "motivate" teams.
**Why:** It produces short-term compliance and long-term brain drain. The best people have other options and will exercise them. You end up with a team you can bully, which is not the team that builds something great. Fear is a terrible substrate for the kind of work that produces superlinear returns.

**Don't:** Attack competitors directly.
**Why:** Every minute attacking is a minute not building. Competitors beat you by being better, not by being louder about you. The fight drains your most creative energy into their most lizard-brained arena. Stop fighting. Ship. The best refutation of a bad competitor is a product they can't match.

**Don't:** Tolerate slippery-seeming advisors or execs because they're impressive on paper.
**Why:** The résumé is lagging. The social-radar signal is leading. If your gut says "slippery, bogus, or jerk" and the résumé says "former VP at impressive-name," trust the gut. Every founder I know who has ignored their gut on a people question later said the same sentence: *I knew there was something off, but I ignored it because he seemed so impressive.*
