# Make Something People Want

## When to Use
- Founder is deep in a business model / pricing discussion before anyone has used the product.
- Founder is debating features, design polish, or architecture before launch.
- Founder has launched and the initial response is tepid.
- A handful of users love the product, the majority are indifferent, and the founder is chasing the indifferent majority.
- Founder is building in secret, certain they'll be copied.

## Fails When
- Product actually does have clear love signal and real growth — the work now is scaling ops, hiring, and fundraising, not more iteration on the core.
- Founder is using "make something people want" as a justification for infinite feature drift without a thesis.

## Core Concept

If I had to pick a motto for Y Combinator again today, "make something people want" is still the one I'd choose. I don't think you can get it down to three words. The phrase emerged about a month after we started YC, and it has turned out to be the most useful single piece of startup advice I know.

The reason it's useful is that it solves, by implication, almost every other problem startups face. If you make something people genuinely want, fundraising becomes dramatically easier because investors can see the growth. Hiring becomes easier because talented people want to work on things that matter. And making money from it is — comparatively — easy. Don't worry too much about the business model at the beginning. Not because money isn't important, but because it's so much easier than making something great.

When you combine "make something people want" with "don't worry too much about the business model," you get something strange: a description of a nonprofit. And indeed the best startups do resemble nonprofits in the early days. They're obsessed with what users need rather than how to extract money from them. Counterintuitively, this turns out to be a competitive advantage, not a pleasant personality trait. Three reasons: morale (caring about users fuels the dark hours when caring about money cannot), attraction (investors and employees rally to startups that look genuinely useful), and the decision compass (when you're not sure what to do, do whatever is best for users — that single rule cuts through enormous strategic complexity).

The test is always the same: do actual humans, right now, desperately want this enough to use a crappy version one? If yes, keep going. If no, iterate until the answer changes, or change what you're building.

## How to Apply

1. **Treat the initial idea as a question, not a blueprint.** When you say "I'm going to build X," it provokes objections. When you say "could one build X? Let's see how far we get," it invites exploration. The main value of your initial idea is that in the process of discovering it's broken, you'll come up with your real idea.

2. **Ship the absolute smallest complete thing.** Build the smallest thing that can be considered a complete application and ship it. Think of version one not as a product launch but as a trick for getting users to start talking to you. If trolls don't make fun of your first version, you waited too long. Airbnb was literally three air mattresses on a floor. Google was a single text box. None looked like what they became.

3. **Launch to start a conversation, not to impress.** The purpose of v1 is to begin a dialogue with users. Ship. Watch. Listen. The feedback you get from engaging directly with your earliest users will be the best you ever get. When you're big enough to need focus groups, you'll wish you could go to users' homes and watch them like you did when there were five.

4. **Focus on the hottest subset.** After launch, a handful of users will love you and everyone else will be indifferent. This is normal and good. The temptation is to try to appeal to the indifferent. Resist it. Go deeper with the users who love you. What specifically do they love? Where can you find more people like them? Ben Silbermann noticed Pinterest's early lovers were interested in design, so he went to a conference of design bloggers and recruited more. He didn't try to make Pinterest appeal to everyone. He found the people who cared and gave them more of what they wanted.

5. **The Woz route.** The very best way to make something people want is to make something you yourself want. Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google, Facebook — all started this way. Their founders weren't trying to start companies. They were building things they personally needed. The Woz route solves the hardest problem in startups — knowing what people want — because you have at least one user whose needs you understand perfectly.

6. **Don't build in secret.** The instinct comes from a misunderstanding of where competitive advantage actually lives. Founders think the idea is the main asset and revealing it lets competitors steal it. In practice, ideas are worth almost nothing. Execution is everything. The risk of someone stealing your idea is negligible compared to the risk of building the wrong thing because you never talked to users. The Segway is the canonical tale of building in secret. Don't be the Segway.

7. **Don't optimize the business model before you have users.** Revenue will come from growth if growth is real. A startup without users has no business model problem — it has a no-users problem. Solve the real problem first.

8. **Use benevolence as decision compass.** When you're not sure what to do, do whatever is best for your users. It cuts through more strategic debate than any framework I know. It also compounds: in a sufficiently transparent world, you can't seem good without being good, and acting for your users consistently builds a brand that can't be bought.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has spent four months in stealth on a tool for teachers. No public presence. Polished design. A forty-page investor deck with pricing tiers, ARR projections, and a CAC/LTV model. They're asking whether to launch now or add two more months of features.

**Application:** Launch Monday. Ugly, minimal, embarrassing. Send it to twenty teachers you have personal contact with. Watch them use it. The investor deck is fiction until a teacher has paid you for the thing. The two months of features you want to add are almost certainly not the right two months — you can't know until users tell you. And your stealth is not protecting an advantage; it's hiding the information you need to build the product correctly. Your version one's purpose is to start a conversation, not to land a launch. Strip it down to the absolute minimum that solves one real thing a teacher actually does daily, and ship it this week.

**Result:** The founder launches with a barely-working tool. Five teachers try it. Three hate it. Two love it desperately and tell all their colleagues. The thing the two love is not the feature the founder was most proud of — it's a small side utility they threw in on the last day. Six months later that side utility is the whole product and the teacher-focused tool has been rebuilt around it.

---

**Situation:** A consumer app has launched. 8,000 signups. 300 are daily actives and extremely engaged. 7,700 are dormant. The team is debating a big marketing push to convert the dormant majority. "That's where our TAM is."

**Application:** Wrong direction. The 300 daily actives are the signal. The 7,700 are noise. You're not going to wake up the 7,700 by marketing harder; you'll just pay to acquire more users who become 97% dormant. Instead: talk to the 300. What do they use the product for? What else is adjacent to that usage? Where do they congregate? What would make them bring their friends? The hottest subset is telling you where the real market is. The indifferent majority is telling you where the ghost market is. Growth comes from doubling down on the first and letting the second fall away.

**Result:** The team ships three features requested by the 300, which causes them to bring in a lookalike cohort. Eighteen months later the product has 200,000 daily actives — all of whom resemble the original 300, none of whom resemble the dormant 7,700 who were never the market.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build in secret.
**Why:** Ideas are worth almost nothing; execution is everything. The risk of being copied is negligible compared to the risk of building the wrong thing because you never talked to users. Dean Kamen's Segway is the canonical cautionary tale — extreme secrecy prevented him from getting the feedback that would have told him people didn't want a $5,000 scooter that made them look silly.

**Don't:** Over-engineer v1.
**Why:** Every extra feature is a feature you'll have to maintain, debug, and explain while also trying to figure out what users actually want. It's not extra credit; it's a lie you have to remember. If trolls don't mock your first version, you waited too long.

**Don't:** Obsess over the business model before you have users.
**Why:** Business model without product-market fit is fiction. The best startups look like nonprofits in the early days. When you have users who love you, monetization is the easier problem. When you don't, the business model is irrelevant.

**Don't:** Try to please the indifferent majority.
**Why:** You'll lose the passionate minority to gain tepid approval from people who will churn. The well, not the crater. Depth beats breadth at the early stage.

**Don't:** Trust friends who say "I could see using that."
**Why:** That's the nice-to-have response, and nice-to-have is functionally the same as not-wanted. What you need to hear is "I would use this tomorrow, even broken, because the status quo is intolerable." If nobody says that, you haven't made something people want yet. Iterate.

**Don't:** Measure the wrong thing.
**Why:** You make what you measure. Measure users happy, not features shipped or lines of code or deck pages. If users are happy, nearly everything else takes care of itself. If they aren't, nothing else saves you.
