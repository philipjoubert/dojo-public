# The Well, Not the Crater

## When to Use
- A founder is choosing between an idea that lots of people kind of want and one that a small group desperately needs.
- Early traction looks numerically small and the founder is being pressured (by self or advisor) to broaden.
- The idea has a passionate core group of users who love it, but the indifferent majority is louder.
- The founder is about to kill a deep-need product to chase a "bigger market."

## Fails When
- The narrow market has genuinely no path out of it — then depth alone is a trap and you need to also see what the expansion looks like.
- The idea is already broadly needed (search, messaging, payments at the right moment) — there's no narrow-vs-broad trade-off; you're inside the big thing.

## Core Concept

Imagine a graph whose x axis represents all the people who might want what you're making and whose y axis represents how much they want it. If you invert the y axis, you can envision companies as holes. Google is an immense crater: hundreds of millions of people use it, and they need it a lot. A startup just starting out can't excavate that much volume. You have two choices about the shape of hole you start with. You can either dig a hole that's broad but shallow, or one that's narrow and deep — like a well.

Made-up startup ideas are usually the first kind. Lots of people mildly interested in a social network for pet owners. Good startup ideas are almost always the second kind. Microsoft was a well when they made Altair Basic — only a couple thousand Altair owners, but without this software they were programming in machine language. Thirty years later Facebook had the same shape: exclusively for Harvard students, only a few thousand, but those few thousand wanted it a lot. The list continues. Stripe started with YC companies. Dropbox started with hackers. PayPal started with eBay power sellers.

You don't need the narrowness of the well per se. What you need is depth. You get narrowness as a byproduct of optimizing for depth and speed. But the link between depth and narrowness is so strong in practice that when you know an idea will appeal strongly to a specific group, that's a good sign. A small group who desperately needs your thing will carry a startup through the early fragility. A larger group who'd kind of use it, won't.

The well also needs a path out. Facebook was a good idea because it started with a small market there was a fast path out of. Colleges are similar enough that a facebook that works at Harvard works at any college. Then by letting in everyone else, you get everyone. The subtle part is that you often can't see the path out in advance. The Airbnb founders didn't realize at first how big a market they were tapping. All they knew was they were onto something. That's probably as much as Gates or Zuckerberg knew at first, either.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the intensity test, not the population test.** Don't ask "how many people might want this?" Ask "who wants this so much that they'd use a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?" If you can't name specific humans who meet that bar, no amount of TAM slides will save you.

2. **Pick the deep hole over the wide one.** When you have to choose between an idea that a large number of people would want a small amount, and an idea that a small number of people would want a large amount, pick the second. Nearly all good startup ideas are of the second type.

3. **Resist the pressure to broaden early.** When you narrow, advisors and VCs will push you to widen. "Surely your TAM is bigger than just X." Maybe — but not yet. Facebook did not start by trying to appeal to everyone on earth. It started by being the perfect thing for Harvard students. The crater forms by digging the well deeper until it breaks through into adjacent ground.

4. **Look for the path out, but don't demand it be visible today.** The right question is not "how does this reach everyone?" but "what's the adjacent group that's similar enough that the same thing works for them?" For Facebook, it was other colleges. For Stripe, YC companies then other developer-first companies then everyone who needed to accept payments. You should have a hypothesis about the second circle. You don't need certainty about the tenth.

5. **Judge larval startups by depth of fit, not scale of ambition.** Microsoft as a couple of guys in Albuquerque writing Basic for hobbyists didn't look like the future of computing. It was the optimal path to it. The question is not "is this company taking over the world?" but "how big could this get if the founders did the right things?"

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has 80 users who love the product and check it daily. An advisor says: "You need at least a TAM of $1B. 80 users is nothing. You need to broaden the appeal." The founder is considering rewriting the product to target a wider audience.

**Application:** Don't. The 80 who love you are the well. The thing your advisor is describing — the broader appeal — is the crater, which is what the well turns into if you dig it deep enough. The broadening-first move is what you do when you don't have the well. You have one. Go deeper. Learn exactly what this group loves, do more of that, and find other groups who are adjacent to them. Facebook at 2,000 Harvard students was a better company than Facebook at 200,000 indifferent users would have been. Your numerator matters more than your denominator right now.

**Result:** Founder ignores the advisor, doubles down on the core 80, studies who they are, finds three adjacent groups of similar users in adjacent institutions, and grows the well into something that ends up serving a large market — but by expansion, not by dilution.

---

**Situation:** A two-founder team is choosing between two ideas. Idea A: a calendar-for-everyone productivity app — a big market, crowded, possibly billions of users if they win. Idea B: a tool for a specific kind of indie musician that they personally know from their scene, maybe 20,000 worldwide, but they know these people intimately and know several who would pay immediately.

**Application:** Idea B. Every time. A few thousand people who intensely need something and whom you personally understand is worth more than a theoretical hundred million who might mildly want something in a market where a dozen established competitors already exist. The depth of the need is the forcing function. When you launch a crappy v1 into a niche you understand, the users pull it forward. When you launch into the crater, nobody notices and you have no one to talk to. The crater is crowded because it's the crater; the well is empty because only you can see it. If Idea B has any path out — other kinds of indie artists, or other niches with similar dynamics — you have a great startup. If it doesn't, you still learn more building it than you would on Idea A.

**Result:** The team builds Idea B, reaches ramen profitable in nine months, and discovers adjacent niches that triple their market. None of that was visible when they picked.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Convert "small user count today" into "small market forever."
**Why:** That's the exact inference that makes investors and know-it-alls dismiss every important startup at the larval stage. The relevant question is depth of need, not current size. A startup is a redwood seedling, not a bean sprout. The destinies are different even when the seedlings look similar.

**Don't:** Dilute the product to reach a bigger group.
**Why:** You'll lose the 80 who love you and gain 200 who kind of like it. Net: worse. Love is the renewable resource that powers the early stage. Tepid approval isn't.

**Don't:** Take seriously the "how does this become a billion-dollar company?" question at the idea stage.
**Why:** Most billion-dollar companies couldn't have answered that question when they were at v1. Facebook couldn't. Airbnb couldn't. The valuable question is "is there a well here?" If yes, the crater comes later. If no, the answer to the billion-dollar question is irrelevant.

**Don't:** Confuse a shallow-but-wide hole with a deep-but-narrow one just because the headline number is bigger.
**Why:** A hundred thousand mildly interested users is not a better indicator than a thousand desperate ones. It's a much worse one. Intensity at the first users is how you know the thing is real.
