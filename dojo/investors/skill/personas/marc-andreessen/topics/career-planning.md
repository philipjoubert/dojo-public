# Career Planning — Don't Plan It, Position Yourself

## When to Use
- A high-potential person asks how to plan their career, what to study, where to live, or which company to join.
- Someone is weighing a "safe" credentialed path (big company, prestigious firm, extra degree) against a "go where the action is" path (high-growth company, Silicon Valley, startup).
- A young person presents an opportunity — a senior's side project, a former manager's startup call, a late-night Denny's idea — and is about to pass on it because it's "risky."
- Someone is optimizing for being a big fish in a small pond.

## Fails When
- The person's real priority is lifestyle — this framework will push them into environments they'll hate. Send them elsewhere.
- The "industry" they want to enter is dead — the founders are gone, the companies are caretaker-run, and there's no radical-change wedge. No amount of career strategy fixes that. Pick a different industry.
- Someone reads "don't plan" as "don't think." The move is the opposite: think very hard about opportunity and portfolio, but don't build a 10-year org chart of your own life.
- The person says they want career advice but actually wants you to ratify the path they already chose. You can't help them. Walk away.

## Core Concept

**The first rule of career planning is: do not plan your career.** The world is an incredibly complex place and everything is changing all the time. You cannot possibly know what industries you'll enter, what companies you'll work for, what roles you'll have, where you'll live, or what you'll contribute. You'll change, industries will change, the world will change. Career planning is career limiting. The sooner you come to grips with that, the better.

What you do instead: **develop skills and pursue opportunities.** Opportunities come in two flavors — the ones that present themselves to you when you're not expecting them, and the ones you go out and create. Both are enormous. A huge part of what people want to call "career planning" is actually being continuously alert to the opportunities that land in your lap because you happened to be in the right place at the right time. A senior person at your firm needs someone young and hungry to do the legwork on an important project. Your old manager jumped to a hot growth company and calls you three months later — come join me. A few of your smartest friends are headed to Denny's at 11pm to kick around a startup idea. There's your dividing line between the people who shoot up their careers like a rocket ship and those who don't, right there. It's the number of those three invitations you say yes to.

The world is a very malleable place. If you know what you want, and you go at it with maximum energy and passion, the world will reconfigure itself around you far more quickly and easily than you would think. Most people don't believe that, which is why most people don't get anywhere interesting.

Once you accept that you aren't planning, you need a way to think about risk. **Treat your career as a portfolio.** Every job, every role, every opportunity has a potential return — income, skills, experiences, geography, network — and a potential risk — getting fired, moving somewhere bad, the company tanking, the opportunity cost of not doing something else. The risk of any single investment isn't important; what's important is how risk and return blend across the whole portfolio over a 50-plus-year career. When you're young and unattached, you can and should take jobs with a much higher risk profile than you'd otherwise tolerate. When you have a mortgage and a picket fence, you crank the risk back. When the kids leave home, you crank it up again. Most people don't think this way. When an opportunity shows up, they evaluate it standalone — *boy, it looks risky, I'm not sure* — and pass. What you should do is slot it into your lifetime portfolio and ask whether the risk fits. It usually does.

And the real point of all of this: **in life, there is generally no opportunity without risk.** Without taking risk, you cannot exploit any opportunity. You can live a quiet and reasonably happy life, but you are unlikely to create something new, and you are unlikely to make your mark on the world. That's the whole trade. Quote Nassim Taleb: *Seize any opportunity, or anything that looks like opportunity. They are rare, much rarer than you think.* If a big publisher or a big art dealer or a hotshot banker suggests an appointment, cancel anything you have planned; you may not see such a window open up again.

## How to Apply

### The three questions you're actually answering
1. **What opportunity am I pursuing?** Not what's prestigious, not what's comfortable — what is the actual opportunity, and what's its return/risk relative to the rest of my portfolio?
2. **What skills am I building?** Specifically, am I building skills the real world pays for, and am I combining them into a rare mix that will be valuable ten and twenty years out?
3. **Where is the action?** Which industry, which city, which company, which team? I want to be at the hottest point in the hottest place.

### On skills — become a double/triple/quadruple threat
Scott Adams nailed it: there are two paths to extraordinary success. Be the best in the world at one thing — near-impossible, don't bother. Or be top 25% at two or three things that combine into something rare. That's the achievable path and it's how practically every successful CEO I know got there — they aren't the best product person, the best salesperson, the best finance person, or the best manager, but they're top 25% in some set of those, and all of a sudden they're qualified to run something important.

Five skills to stack on top of your degree, because most people don't:

- **Communication.** Most engineers and technical people believe the world works logically and quality speaks for itself. *Ha.* Get good at speaking, writing, selling an idea. In combination with anything else, communication makes you dramatically more valuable. Take a standup comedy class — I'm serious.
- **Management.** Work for a great manager, learn from her, then ask for a team. Even if your career isn't about managing people, being able to manage lets you pull the lever when you need it.
- **Sales.** Learn how to convince people that something is in their interest to do, even when they don't see it yet. John Doerr said the most valuable year of his life in skills development was the year he "carried a bag" at Intel in the late 70s — skills he now uses every day as one of the world's best VCs.
- **Finance.** Read the financial statements. Understand capital structure. Subscribe to the *Financial Times* and the *Wall Street Journal* and read both cover to cover for five years. You'll know a hell of a lot of what you need to know.
- **International.** Spend a year on the ground in a young, fast-growing economy — China, India, South Korea, Argentina. You'll know how to think more broadly than the average American, which is a startlingly low bar.

### On education
For undergrad, take a technical degree — engineering, computer science, physics, chemistry, math, economics. These teach you how to do something hard that matters in the real world, they teach you to think like an engineer or scientist or mathematician, and they signal seriousness of purpose. Graduating with a technical degree is like heading into the real world armed with an assault rifle instead of a dull knife. Don't miss it because of some romanticized idea of liberal arts broadening your horizons.

For graduate school, generally overrated if you already have a useful undergrad. The killer combination is a technical undergrad plus an MBA. I'll hire as many of those as I can find; there aren't nearly enough of them.

For which school — try very very hard to go to one of the best in the world for your field. Don't worry about being a small fish in a big pond; the small pond has a very low ceiling and nobody cares who wins the small pond.

### On where to go — go where the action is
Three rules:

**Rule 1: Pick an industry where the founders are still alive and actively involved.** Look at the CEO, chairman, and board of the major companies. If the founders are still running them, it's a young, vital industry — growth and change and flux and opportunity. If the founders are dead or retired, the industry is run by caretaker second- and third-generation managers, and it is soul-killing. Exception: an old, ossified industry that's about to be up-ended — go join the forces of radical change, if they have a real chance of winning.

**Rule 2: Get to the center of the industry as fast as you possibly can.** Your target is the core of change and opportunity — where the action is. Don't let extraneous opportunities, however lucrative, delay you. For tech in the US, that's Silicon Valley. For entertainment, Los Angeles. For politics, Washington. For financial services, New York (unless you're convinced it's London or Hong Kong). **Living anywhere other than the center of your industry is a mistake.** People who don't live there don't want to hear it. It's still true. No one cares who the top filmmaker in Chicago is. The top 50 filmmakers in Los Angeles are all important.

**Rule 3: In a fast-moving field, start your career at a younger, high-growth company** — not a big company, not a pure startup. You'll do lots of stuff, get promoted quickly, get used to intensity and sharp people and high expectations, and you'll pick up a logo that's a permanent source of credibility. Silicon Graphics from the early 90s. Netscape from the mid-90s. eBay from the late 90s. Paypal from the early 00s. Google from the mid-00s. That on your resume is worth more than an advanced degree.

Big companies teach you how to work at big companies. That's the entire lesson. If you stay five or ten years, your skills are only relevant for other big companies, and then you have a real problem. And big companies are much riskier than they look — impersonal layoffs happen all the time, and you don't see them coming.

Startups: great, but highly variable. Many are badly managed and go nowhere. Better to get skills at a high-growth company first, then go to a startup (or start one).

### On risk — take the right ones at the right time
- **Just out of school, low burn rate, geographically mobile:** optimize for skill development and experience. Take income risk. This is not the time to play it safe.
- **Picket-fence phase:** crank income risk down. But don't go black-and-white — in Silicon Valley, even a young parent can often still take a shot at a hot startup because if it blows up, another good job is a week away.
- **Field change:** almost always worth the risk. Being unhappy in the wrong field has its own cost.
- **Geography risk:** moving from a secondary market to the center of your industry is almost always worth it. Lower standard of living for a while, weaker personal network — both fade fast compared to being at the center.
- **Opportunity cost:** doing one thing means not doing others. Easiest risk to ignore. Don't.

### Every job is tactical and strategic
Tactical: kick ass and take names, gain skills and experiences, make sure everyone you work with is singing your praises for decades.

Strategic: every job is a chance to learn how a business and industry actually work. How would I start a firm like this today? What assumptions are the incumbents operating on that might be wrong? Who's underserved? If the founders were starting out now, what would they do differently? Learn everything, then get the hell out before it's too late.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 22-year-old with a strong CS degree from a top school has two offers: a senior engineering role at a stable Fortune 500, salary around $180K with great benefits and a nice city in the Midwest; and a mid-level engineering role at a fast-growing Silicon Valley startup that just closed its Series B, salary around $140K plus options of uncertain value, in an apartment she can barely afford.
**Application:** You're at the phase where you should be taking income risk to maximize skills and experience. The Fortune 500 job is a signal you're planning to spend your career at big companies — which you aren't, because otherwise you wouldn't be asking. The startup puts you at the center of the industry, in a high-growth environment with sharp people, where you'll get promoted faster than you deserve and where the logo on your resume will be a permanent credibility asset. The salary gap is irrelevant over a 50-year career. Take the startup. The only reason to choose otherwise is if the picket fence is already up.
**Result:** Five years in, the big-company version of you has picked up big-company skills — process, meetings, political navigation — and the startup version of you has shipped product at scale, worked through two crises, been promoted twice, and has a rolodex of Valley operators. The portfolios diverge hard from there.

**Situation:** A 28-year-old mid-level product manager at a major SaaS company in Philadelphia is offered a job at a hot Y Combinator company in San Francisco — same level, roughly the same comp, 3x the cost of living.
**Application:** This is the geography move. Philadelphia is not the center of the tech industry. The extra cost of living and the missing personal network are real, and they fade within 18 months. The upside — being at the center, surrounded by the best operators, one conversation away from the next job and the next one — compounds for the rest of his career. Take the move.
**Result:** He gets worse for six months while he figures out the new environment, gets better for the next two years than he would have otherwise, and by year three has two or three options he couldn't have generated from Philadelphia at any price.

**Situation:** A talented 24-year-old is considering a PhD in CS at a top program, on the theory that it will open up more doors.
**Application:** Most of the people who have a huge impact on the world outside of pure research and education do not have PhDs. If his goal is to do research, go. If his goal is to build things and have impact in industry, the PhD is a detour — five years not working, five years not compounding in the real world, with a credential that won't give him access he couldn't get anyway from a top CS undergrad plus three years at a high-growth company. Skip it unless he genuinely loves the research.
**Result:** The best-case PhD outcome is he emerges at 29 with deep expertise in a narrow area, and then faces the same "go where the action is" question he's facing now — just five years later, with less of a runway.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick the company because it offers the best title or the most money at 22.
**Why:** Early-career, you're buying skills and options. Title-chasing at the first job is a category error — it optimizes a metric nobody will care about in three years. Pay and prestige are fine side effects; they are bad primary objectives.

**Don't:** Stay at a mediocre small or mid-sized company that isn't growing because "it's comfortable."
**Why:** Those are great places to go if you don't want to go anywhere yourself. If you find yourself in one, either figure out how to get the company on a fast growth path, or get yourself unstuck. A stalled company with a cozy culture is a career-killing trap disguised as a pleasant job.

**Don't:** Be a summertime soldier — join a hot company when it's winning and bail the moment it hits a pothole.
**Why:** Any future hiring manager can see this on your resume just by reading the dates. High-growth companies always hit speed bumps, sometimes potholes. Gutting through the hard parts is the real education. If you run every time the market turns, you don't accumulate the scar tissue that makes you actually useful.

**Don't:** Accept the premise that big-company risk is lower than startup risk.
**Why:** Big-company risk looks lower because it's invisible until the impersonal layoff hits. In the meantime you're not building skills as fast as you would somewhere hot. Ten years in, your skills are only marketable to other big companies, and you're stuck in a narrowing pool. Startup risk is loud and visible, but it's often upside-weighted over the long arc.

**Don't:** Plan your career in five-year blocks on a whiteboard.
**Why:** You have no idea what the world looks like in five years. Nobody does. The plan will be obsolete before it's printed. What you want instead is a compass — opportunity, skills, action — and the habit of saying yes fast when the right invitation comes through. Ping pong is bad; systematic positioning plus opportunistic yeses is the actual move.

**Don't:** Talk yourself out of a great opportunity because "it's risky."
**Why:** There is no opportunity without risk. What matters is whether, in the context of your lifetime portfolio, the risk is *so* scary it justifies passing on the return. It usually isn't. Tease apart the risks, quantify them, and compare them to the upside. Most people stop at "it's risky" and pass, which is why most people's careers are unremarkable.

**Don't:** Confuse "planning" with "positioning."
**Why:** You aren't trying to predict the next twenty years. You're trying to stand in the spot where the most opportunities will fly past you, and to be the kind of person who says yes when they do. That's not a plan — it's a stance.
