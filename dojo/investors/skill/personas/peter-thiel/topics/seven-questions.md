# The Seven Questions Every Business Must Answer

## When to Use
- Diligence on an early-stage investment.
- A founder is pitching and wants a stress test.
- Reviewing a business plan for missing pieces.
- Coaching a founder through self-evaluation.
- Diagnosing why a well-articulated company feels thin.

## Fails When
- The company is mature and the questions have largely been answered — these are the questions for company formation, not operation.
- The framework is used as a scoring rubric where "5 of 7" means pass. It does not. Each missing answer is a potential death sentence.
- The user is looking for reassurance rather than diagnosis.

## Core Concept

There are seven questions every company must have strong answers to. Getting five or six right might work. Getting one or two right is not enough. Each failure is a potential death sentence. This is not a checklist where a passing grade is 60%. Each question probes a structural dimension of whether the company will survive and become valuable.

### 1. The Engineering Question

*Can you create breakthrough technology rather than incremental improvements?*

The threshold is 10x — not 2x, not 20% better. Anything less is perceived as a marginal improvement and will not compel customers to switch. PayPal made sending money roughly 10x faster than mailing a check. Google was orders of magnitude better than AltaVista at its launch. Amazon's first book selection was at least 10x larger than any physical bookstore's.

If you cannot articulate how your product is 10x better in some important dimension, you do not have a technology company. You have a feature. A feature does not build a monopoly; it gets absorbed into an incumbent's product or competed away within a cycle.

Note that "10x" is about perceived difference, not just measurement. A product that is 10x better in a dimension customers don't care about produces no monopoly. The question is: 10x better in the dimension that actually decides the buying decision.

### 2. The Timing Question

*Is now the right time to start this particular business?*

PayPal could only have been started in 1999. Enough people were on email, the need for online payments was emerging, the intersection of technologies was right. Starting it in 1985 would have been premature — no email. Starting it in 2014 would have been too late — too much competitive density and the secret was public.

The diagnostic question is: *why hasn't anyone done this before?*

Good answers:
- "The technology didn't exist until now." This is the strongest answer — a new capability has opened a new possibility.
- "Market conditions just shifted." Regulatory changes, demographic shifts, platform transitions can create windows.

Answers requiring investigation:
- "People have tried and failed." This could mean the timing was wrong before and is right now, or it could mean the idea is simply bad. Dig into the specific failures.
- "Nobody thought of it." Almost never true. More likely others thought of it and dismissed it for reasons you need to understand.

The first-mover trap: being first is not the same as having correct timing. What matters is making the last great development in a market (see last-mover-advantage). First movers who do not build durable advantages are just infrastructure for companies that come after.

### 3. The Monopoly Question

*Are you starting with a big share of a small market?*

If your initial market is large, you are in intense competition and your probability of dominance is functionally zero. If your initial market is small but you can dominate it, you have a foundation for concentric expansion. PayPal reached 25% of eBay PowerSellers in three months. Facebook reached 60% of Harvard students in ten days. These numbers prove product-market fit in a defensible niche.

The red flag: "it's a $100B market and we just need 1%." This is mathematically delusional. One percent of a huge competitive market is an unstable position that will be competed away, not a beachhead. The correct pitch is about a tiny market you can dominate completely, with a specific expansion path (see start-small-dominate-expand).

### 4. The People Question

*Do you have the right team?*

Two sub-questions:

**Prehistory.** How long have the founders known each other? If the answer is "we met at a networking event last week," that is a bad prehistory. If the answer is "we've been friends for years and have worked together on other things," that is a good one. Founder relationships under crisis depend on what was there before the crisis. Thin relationships shatter; deep ones hold.

**Suit test.** If the CEO looks like a salesman, he is probably bad at sales and worse at tech. Real technologists wear T-shirts and jeans. The suit is a tell in the poker sense — it signals that the founder is performing a role rather than building technology. This is a crude proxy, but in practice it has been remarkably accurate.

Also: are the founders hiring for strength or for lack of weakness? Early-stage companies need world-class strengths in specific dimensions, not generically competent executives.

### 5. The Distribution Question

*Do you have a way to not just create but deliver your product?*

Many founders treat distribution as an afterthought — they will "figure it out" after building the product. This is backwards. If you have invented something new but you have not invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business, no matter how good the product. Poor sales, not bad product, is the most common cause of failure.

Distribution must be designed into the company from the beginning. Where does your product fall on the sales spectrum — complex sales, personal sales, the dead zone, marketing, viral? Does your product's core use case require the two parties needed to create a viral loop? Does the deal size support a sales force, or is it stuck in the dead zone? Have you chosen a single channel and committed to making it work, rather than spreading effort across multiple weak channels?

### 6. The Durability Question

*Will your market position be defensible 10 and 20 years into the future?*

For a company to be valuable it must grow and endure. If you grow but cannot defend your position, you are just building something for the competitor who shows up later. A nightclub in New York is profitable now and obsolete in two years. A technology startup is unprofitable now and potentially dominant for decades. The nightclub looks like a better business to anyone who cannot think past the current quarter. It is not.

The four monopoly characteristics — proprietary 10x technology, network effects, economies of scale, brand on substance — are durability mechanisms. Each, when built correctly, creates a barrier that grows higher over time rather than lower. The test: will this advantage be *stronger* in ten years than it is today, or weaker? If weaker, it is a lead, not a moat.

### 7. The Secret Question

*Have you identified a unique opportunity that others do not see?*

Great companies have secrets — specific reasons for success that other people cannot see. If everyone understands why your company is great, there is no edge. You are in a crazy auction where whoever wins overpays. The contrarian question — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — is the way in.

Without a secret, the other six characteristics are not enough. A company with 10x technology, good timing, a small-market beachhead, a great team, a distribution plan, and durable advantages — but no secret that explains *why this team, this time, this way* — is a company that will eventually be understood and competed with.

### The Composite Test

Tesla answered all seven questions correctly. Its technology was so good that other car companies licensed Tesla's components. It timed the market perfectly, securing a $465M government loan in the brief window cleantech was politically popular but before Solyndra's collapse made such loans toxic. It started with a tiny market — high-end electric sports cars — it could dominate completely. Musk was the consummate engineer-salesman. Tesla owned its entire distribution chain. Its founder-led culture and brand created durable advantage. And its secret was that cleantech was a social phenomenon — rich people wanted to look cool driving an electric car, not just save the planet.

Most cleantech companies answered zero to two of these questions correctly. The difference in outcomes was total. No sector will ever be so important that merely participating in it will be enough to build a great company.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the seven in order.** For any early-stage company (yours, a portfolio candidate, a founder you are advising), ask all seven. Write down the answers. Where the answer is strong, note it. Where the answer is weak or absent, flag it.

2. **Pay attention to what the founder deflects from.** In any pitch there are three vectors: team, technology, strategy. Whatever the founder most wants to talk about is usually where the strength is. Whatever they avoid is usually where the problem is. A founder who talks at length about the technology but cannot articulate a distribution plan has a distribution problem. A founder who talks about the team but cannot explain the 10x technological advantage has a technology problem.

3. **Refuse the "we'll figure it out" answers.** Some questions tolerate uncertainty (durability at day zero is genuinely hard to prove) but others do not. The monopoly question and the secret question must have specific answers now. If they do not, the company does not yet have a reason to exist.

4. **Force specificity on 10x claims.** "Much better" and "industry-leading" are not answers to the engineering question. Ask: in what dimension are you 10x better, and how have you measured it? If the answer is a 30% improvement, the company is a feature.

5. **Test the timing answer against recent history.** If the pitch is that "nobody has built this before because the technology didn't exist until now," ask what specifically became possible in the last 18 months. If the pitch is that "people tried but failed," ask who, when, and why — and what is different this time. Vague timing answers are almost always wrong.

6. **Score honestly, not generously.** Each weak answer is a potential death sentence. A founder who has 4 strong and 3 weak is not 57% of the way to a great company. They are likely to fail on one of the 3 weak dimensions — and which one is hard to predict. The exception is when the weaknesses are in dimensions you have active plans to fix (e.g., "we don't have the distribution figured out yet, but we have a specific plan to test three channels in the next quarter").

7. **Apply the framework to your own existing company.** The seven questions do not expire at Series A. If your Series C company cannot answer the secret question, or the durability question, the company is decaying toward commodity. The questions are the perennial structural tests.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series A founder pitches a "last-mile delivery optimization platform for restaurants." The deck shows 10% better delivery times than competitors, a roadmap for expansion into multiple cities, and a team of three ex-Uber operators.

**Application:** Running the seven:

1. *Engineering.* 10% better is not 10x better. The delivery-optimization space is crowded. No technology question passes.
2. *Timing.* The first wave of food-delivery platforms is mature; several are publicly traded. The timing answer needs to be "something specific just changed." If it hasn't, timing fails.
3. *Monopoly.* "Multiple cities" is the wrong frame. Which specific small market can this company dominate to 60% within a year? If there isn't one, monopoly fails.
4. *People.* Ex-Uber operators is a reasonable team signal. Check the prehistory — have they worked together?
5. *Distribution.* How will the platform acquire restaurant customers? What is the channel, and why does it work at a cost that matches the deal size?
6. *Durability.* 10% operational improvement is not durable. Competitors will match within 18 months.
7. *Secret.* What does this team know about restaurant delivery that everyone else in the space missed? If the answer is "we'll execute better," there is no secret.

The company fails on 1, 6, 7 at minimum and is questionable on 2 and 3. This is a feature company, not a monopoly company. Pass.

**Result:** Most pitches that survive the seven questions turn out to be companies that were interesting to look at but not interesting to invest in. That is fine — the filter is the point.

**Situation:** A founder proposes building a longevity-focused therapeutics company around a specific mechanism targeting age-related cellular damage. She has two senior scientists from a top lab. She claims proprietary IP around a novel delivery approach.

**Application:** Run the seven:

1. *Engineering.* A genuinely novel delivery approach could be 10x, but the technical claim needs independent evaluation. If the IP is real and the mechanism is specific, this question has a plausible answer.
2. *Timing.* Why now? If the answer is "imaging and assay tooling that became available in the last two years makes this measurable," that is a strong timing answer.
3. *Monopoly.* What's the first narrow indication they can dominate? A rare genetic disease? A specific tissue? The "longevity" frame is too broad — a specific starting indication with path to broader application is the correct form.
4. *People.* Two senior scientists from a top lab is a good signal, but check founder prehistory and whether the business leadership is credible for a 10-year capital cycle.
5. *Distribution.* Therapeutics distribution is the FDA plus payers plus physicians. Do they have a credible path? Biotech distribution is structurally different from software, and the capital requirements are proportional.
6. *Durability.* If the IP is genuine and the delivery approach is novel, there is a durability thesis — patent protection plus regulatory moat plus clinical evidence.
7. *Secret.* What do they believe about aging that most of the field does not? If "aging is a disease" is their secret, it is now conventional in biotech. They need something more specific.

A pitch like this can pass if the scientific claim is real, the starting indication is specific, and the secret is more than the generic longevity frame.

**Result:** Longevity is currently a mimetic sector — many companies entering, consensus enthusiasm, rising valuations. The seven questions will filter out most; the rare pass is exactly where to look.

**Situation:** A founder pitches a B2B SaaS for logistics coordination. He frames the pitch around a $100B TAM, claims operational excellence, and shows 20% better coordination efficiency than incumbents.

**Application:** The pitch fails the seven on almost every dimension. $100B TAM is the red flag for competitive-intensity, not opportunity. 20% better is not 10x. "Operational excellence" is not a monopoly thesis. No specific small market to dominate. No distinctive distribution plan. No durable mechanism. No secret — the pitch is indistinguishable from a dozen other logistics SaaS pitches from the same cycle.

**Result:** This is the modal pitch in this space. Pass. The one worth investing in is the pitch that starts "most logistics software is designed around dispatchers, but the real decision maker on a long-haul shipment is the driver's family — and we built around that" — a pitch with a specific secret, a specific tiny market, and a specific reason why now.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the seven questions as a checklist where 5/7 is a pass.
**Why:** The math is not additive. Each weak answer is a potential death sentence, and which one kills the company is hard to predict. A company strong on 5 questions but weak on timing and distribution will die from timing or distribution, not from having been "mostly right." Treat each weak answer as a real risk, not an average.

**Don't:** Accept "we'll figure it out" as an answer to the distribution question.
**Why:** Distribution is the single most underestimated dimension in technology companies. "We'll figure it out" means the founder has not thought about it seriously, which means the product will get built and then fail to sell. Force a specific channel, a specific cost of acquisition, a specific unit economic argument — now, at the beginning, not after the product ships.

**Don't:** Let the founder talk only about the strong questions.
**Why:** Founders naturally direct conversations to the dimensions where they are strong. The seven questions' value is in forcing attention to the dimensions they are avoiding. If the founder is excited about the team, push hard on the technology and strategy. If excited about the technology, push on people and distribution.

**Don't:** Soften the 10x test into "significantly better."
**Why:** "Significantly better" is the language of feature competition. 10x is the language of monopoly. The difference matters. A 30% improvement in a crowded space is a feature an incumbent will absorb; a 10x improvement is a category redefinition an incumbent cannot respond to without rebuilding their product.

**Don't:** Answer the secret question with a convention.
**Why:** If the founder's answer to "what's your secret" is something every other founder in the space would also claim — "we're going to use AI," "the market is underserved," "we care about the customer" — there is no secret. A real secret is specific, defensible with evidence, and surprising enough that listeners feel resistance. If the answer feels comfortable, it is probably not a secret.
