# Passion-Market Fit (Before Product-Market Fit)

## When to Use
- The user is choosing what company to start.
- The user is partway through a company and wondering whether they're on the right thing.
- The user is evaluating a founder (as an investor, advisor, or co-founder) for fit with the market.
- The user has chased hot markets repeatedly and never dug deep.
- The user thinks "product-market fit" is the whole game.

## Fails When
- The user is already in real product-market fit and the question is scale, not fit. Different topic.
- The user is using "I don't feel passion for this" as a rationalization for not executing. Sometimes the passion is already there and the problem is grit, not alignment.

## Core Concept

Everyone talks about product-market fit. Find a product people want. Match it to a market that will pay.

That's necessary. It's not sufficient. There's a layer above it: **passion-market fit. Or more precisely, founder-product-market fit.**

Before you search for product-market fit, make sure you have passion-market fit. It's a long journey.

Building a product is a process, not a discrete action. And the Internet is efficiently arbitraged. Every single simple thing that can be done is being done, or has been done. The lesson of history is that product-market fit is very precise — one wrong tweak or slightly bad timing and you can miss the whole thing.

So the only way you're likely to find product-market fit is if you're almost irrationally obsessed with the market and if you've been working on it for a long time. Where the journey is the reward. Then you're likely to have unique insights in the details, and consistent execution through thick and thin, to actually find fit.

Often, the best companies are ones where the product is an extension of the founder's personality. Which shouldn't be a big surprise, since everyone is passionate about themselves.

## The Founder Must Fit

Product-market fit is a moving target. The market shifts, the technology shifts, the competition shifts, customer expectations shift. Finding fit requires thousands of small adjustments — feature decisions, pricing tweaks, positioning shifts, go-to-market experiments — each one influenced by specific judgment about what the market actually wants right now.

The only way you make thousands of correct micro-decisions is if you have deep understanding of the space, deep passion for the problem, and execution skills.

Deep understanding gives you the taste to know when something's right. Deep passion gives you the willingness to keep adjusting even when the adjustments don't seem to work. Execution skill means you can actually ship the next version before the market moves again.

Miss any one, and you're in trouble:

- **Passionate but can't execute?** You're a dreamer. You'll have lots of ideas and no product.
- **Skilled but not passionate?** You'll quit when it gets hard. It always gets hard.
- **Neither passionate nor skilled?** Why are you doing this?

The best founders have all three.

## The Personality Match

Success hinges on matching founder personality to market requirements.

Craig Newmark was patient and detail-oriented. He was an excellent fit with the requirements of building Craigslist slowly over time. The product could be slow because the market rewarded slowness and discipline — any flashier version of the category died, because the moment it got flashier it competed with sites that could outspend it.

Mark Pincus is aggressive. He tried to compete with Craigslist but didn't have Newmark's patience. Tribe.net failed. The market needed a patient operator, and Pincus couldn't play that way.

But Pincus's aggression made him perfect for social gaming. He wiped out all competition in a fragmented industry. Zynga dominated. Same person, different market — and the fit made the difference.

It's not about what's "good" or "bad." It's about fit. Patient markets need patient founders. Speed markets need aggressive founders. Technical markets need technical founders who care. Distribution markets need distribution-obsessed founders. Customer-service markets need founders who genuinely love hearing from customers. You can't coach past the mismatch.

## Irrational Obsession

Entrepreneurs hunt for "the idea." The bait that traps them for the next five to ten years.

You better love the trap. Because you're going to live in it.

I meet many entrepreneurs who are short-term thinkers. They pick what's hot. They see a market opportunity. They calculate the exit potential. That's fine — it just means this isn't the right idea for them.

Go find the thing you can commit to for ten years. That's how long it takes, minimum, for a good outcome. You have to enjoy the journey because there's no guarantee on the outcome.

The best founders are irrationally obsessed with their market. They'd work on it even without funding. They'd work on it even after they'd already failed at it. It's not a job — it's a calling. They read the trade press on weekends. They argue about the industry at dinner parties. They know the unwritten history of every competitor. They can't stop.

This isn't romance. It's competitive advantage. A founder who's 100% obsessed with the problem will outperform a founder who's 80% interested. Not by 20% — by 10x. Because the 100%-obsessed founder sees things the 80%-interested one literally can't see. The edges of the problem reveal themselves only to someone who cares enough to look for them.

## The Dots Connect Forward

Look at your company as an extension of your life to date. The dots should connect moving forward.

Where did you grow up? What industries do you know intimately? What problems have you personally experienced? What skills have you been building? What communities are you part of?

Your company should leverage all of it. Not fight against it.

If you're starting fresh in an industry you don't know, with problems you've never faced, using skills you don't have, you're at a massive disadvantage. Someone else has the authentic version of what you're faking, and they will win.

This is why the diligence question for founders — yours or your investors' — isn't just "is this a good market" but "is this a good market for you, specifically." An excellent market with poor founder fit is usually worse than a merely good market with excellent founder fit.

## The Test for Fit

You have proprietary insights. Not "I read about this market" insights. "I've lived in this market for ten years" insights. You see things others don't see because you've been there.

You have unfair advantages. Domain expertise. Technical skills. Relationships. Distribution. Something that's hard for others to replicate.

You'd do it for free. Not literally — you need to eat. But the core problem fascinates you. You can't stop thinking about it. Working on it doesn't feel like work.

You're embarrassed by the competition. Not because they're bad people. Because they don't understand the problem as deeply as you do. Because you know you can do it better.

If three or four of those are true, the fit is probably real. If only one is true, you may be in love with an idea, not a market. There's a difference.

## Vision Without Execution

Steve Case said: "Vision without execution is hallucination."

Passion alone isn't enough. Conviction alone isn't enough. You need to actually ship things.

The best founders have three things:

1. **Deep understanding of the space** — proprietary insight from time spent.
2. **Deep passion for the problem** — the kind that survives multiple years of no progress.
3. **Execution skills** — they just get things done, small and large, reliably.

The three traits compound. Understanding without execution produces a think-piece, not a company. Execution without passion runs out of fuel around year three. Passion without execution or understanding produces enthusiastic amateurs with no output. The fit is having all three in one person (or across a tightly aligned co-founder pair).

## The Big-Enough Problem

You have to pick something big to work on. It's hard to commit your life to something small.

It takes the same work to build for a niche market as for a big one. Go after the biggest opportunity you can care about.

Here's the key phrase: **that you can care about.**

Not the biggest opportunity. The biggest opportunity that fits your founder profile. That matches your skills and interests. That you'll be obsessed with for a decade.

Better to dominate a meaningful market than to be irrelevant in a huge one. Better to care enormously about a narrow market than tepidly about a broad one. The market can grow under a founder who cares. A market never grew under a founder who was bored.

## Non-Consensus and Right

The fluffy save-the-world visions sometimes are the founding basis. Sometimes they emerge as you build. Sometimes they're tacked on afterwards for a good story. There's no single formula. If there were, we'd systematize it and the returns would be gone.

Technology is the set of things that don't quite work yet. Entrepreneurship attacks the boundary cases. It advances human knowledge by solving hard problems. By definition, the best ideas are non-consensus. If everyone agreed the idea was good, it would already exist.

You need to be non-consensus *and* right. That's the hard part. Most non-consensus ideas are non-consensus because they're wrong. The few that are non-consensus and right are the startups that matter. Your job is to find one of those, and the best signal that you've found one is that people you respect think it's stupid, *and* you have proprietary insight that makes you confident they're missing something.

## The Hiring Implication

Founder-market fit matters for hiring too.

If you have authentic passion for the problem, passionate people want to work with you. They can smell authenticity. They can smell fake too.

If you're chasing trends, you'll attract mercenaries. People optimizing for their resume. They'll leave when it gets hard.

If you're building your life's work, you'll attract missionaries. People who care about the mission. They'll stay when it gets hard.

This is one of the hidden returns on authentic founder fit — it compounds into who will work with you and who won't. The best early hires have their own options and will pick based on the founder's genuine obsession. Fake obsession can be detected in three conversations.

## The Only Moat

In the end, founder-market fit is the only moat that matters in the early days.

Better funding? Someone can out-raise you.
Better technology? Someone can copy it.
Better talent? Someone can recruit them away.

But no one can copy you. Your unique insight. Your authentic obsession. Your specific combination of skills and experiences.

That's the only sustainable advantage before product-market fit.

Find the problem that only you can solve. Then solve it.

## How to Apply

1. **Before picking the idea, audit yourself.** What have you been obsessed with for years without being paid? What industry do you know too much about, for no good reason? What problems have you personally lived with? Those are your candidate markets.

2. **Kill ideas that don't fit.** The exciting-sounding idea in a market you've never touched is almost certainly a worse bet for you than the less-exciting idea in a market you've lived in for a decade. The market has to match the founder, not just the founder the market.

3. **Test obsession honestly.** Would you still be working on this ten years from now if the outcome was mediocre? If the answer is no, you're going to quit before the real compounding starts. Most founders do.

4. **Commit for ten years.** A good outcome takes a decade, minimum. If you can't see yourself in this problem for ten years, don't start.

5. **Prove unique insight.** Not "I read three Substacks about this." Proprietary views that come from experience. Write them down. If the list is short and generic, your fit isn't deep enough yet — spend more time in the market first.

6. **For investors: evaluate the founder's fit specifically, not just the market.** Two founders, same market, will produce different companies. The one whose personality and background matches the market's demands will win. Spend as much diligence time on fit as on TAM.

7. **Don't hide the narrowness.** If your fit is for a specific vertical, narrow market, or quirky customer type — own it. Don't inflate the TAM. The investors who understand authentic fit will invest on the real story; the ones who don't will pressure you into directions that break the fit.

## Examples

**Situation:** A two-time founder with deep expertise in supply chain logistics is being advised to start an AI company because "AI is hot." He feels he has to, because everyone tells him he's wasting his time otherwise.

**Application:** His specific knowledge is supply chain logistics. His fit is there, not in AI broadly. The right answer is almost certainly an AI-native supply chain company where his operator insight provides the unique insight and the technology serves the problem. A generic "AI company" from him would be a fit mismatch — he'd be a supply chain operator trying to pretend to be an ML researcher, and he'd get outcompeted by founders who actually do have ML expertise.

**Result:** Build something where supply chain is the primary color and AI is the tool. Don't chase the category for its heat. His edge is in the vertical, not in the technology trend.

---

**Situation:** A founder is on year five of her company. It's growing 40% a year, the team is good, but she feels disengaged. She can't bring herself to care about the next feature.

**Application:** Founder-market fit can fail over time — the founder changes, the market changes, the gap opens. Her disengagement is data. The question isn't "how do I motivate myself to keep going." It's: what's actually going on with the fit? Options: (a) she's near burnout and needs a real break, after which the passion returns; (b) the market has evolved toward a shape that doesn't suit her — has shifted from what she loved to something adjacent she doesn't; (c) she's succeeded at the original mission and should either recommission (a bigger ambition) or plan an exit. Each is a different action.

**Result:** Get honest about which of the three it is, and act accordingly. The worst outcome is muscling through disengagement for another two years — that's how companies drift into mediocre exits that hurt the team and the founder both.

---

**Situation:** An investor is deciding between two pitches: one from a world-class ex-McKinsey operator with no domain experience in the target vertical, and one from an obsessed domain expert with weaker execution polish.

**Application:** The pure operator will execute well on the wrong things. The domain expert will execute roughly on the right things, and their specific insights will compound across thousands of small decisions over years. In most cases, bet on the domain expert — but only if they can hire or partner for execution. The best outcome: an authentically fitted founder with a strong operator next to them. The worst: a great operator trying to be the domain expert they're not.

**Result:** Back the domain expert, provided they'll bring on a strong operator co-founder or COO. If they can't or won't, reconsider. A missing operator is fixable; missing domain fit usually isn't.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Pick an idea from a "hot markets" list.
**Why:** That list is your competition's list too. You're entering a crowded field as a latecomer. And if you don't have specific knowledge in the domain, you'll be the worst-positioned competitor in a market that's already being won by better-fitted founders.

**Don't:** Start something because your friends think it's cool.
**Why:** Peer approval is a status signal, not a market signal. Your friends are not your customers. Their enthusiasm will not survive the first product-market fit crisis. Your obsession will. Start based on the obsession, not the applause.

**Don't:** Use "I should be passionate about something more important" as a filter.
**Why:** You can't choose your passion. You discover it. If you're obsessed with something that seems unimportant ("fishing gear for urban commuters"), that's probably where your fit is. Trying to retrofit your passion onto something that sounds impressive usually fails — the authenticity is where the advantage lives.

**Don't:** Pivot the moment the first version doesn't work.
**Why:** Finding product-market fit is a long iterative process. Most great companies went through multiple near-deaths before the fit emerged. If your passion-market fit is real, you'll push through the false starts because you can't imagine stopping. If you're pivoting at the first sign of trouble, the fit probably isn't there — and the pivot is just fleeing.

**Don't:** Fake founder-market fit in your pitch.
**Why:** Good investors can tell. Your lack of domain depth will show in the questions you can't answer, the second-order effects you don't see, the customer conversations you haven't been able to have. And if you do raise, you'll spend years building a company you're not actually positioned to build. The deal works out badly for everyone.

**Don't:** Confuse founder-market fit with being the only expert.
**Why:** You don't have to know more than anyone on earth. You have to know more than enough to see insights others miss and to execute when they can't. Over-indexing on domain credentials alone (e.g., hiring only PhDs for an AI company) can actually hurt you if none of them have founder-level fit with the market.
