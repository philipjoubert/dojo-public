# Leverage

## When to Use
- The user has a working product, service, or skill and wants to know how to scale it.
- The user has hit the ceiling of what they can do with their own hours.
- The user is choosing between hiring a team, raising capital, or building software/media.
- The user is underpaid relative to their specific knowledge and doesn't see why.
- The user is asking how fortunes actually get made in the modern economy.

## Fails When
- The user has no specific knowledge yet. Leverage amplifies whatever you apply it to — including bad judgment. If there's no edge to multiply, leverage just multiplies loss.
- The user is in a low-leverage profession by design (they love what they do, the hours are the point). Leverage isn't morally required. Freedom is. If they have enough, leave them alone.

## Core Concept

"Give me a lever long enough and a place to stand, and I will move the earth."

Archimedes understood something modern brains haven't evolved to comprehend: the magnitude of leverage available in the modern economy. Fortunes require leverage. Without it, your output is capped by your input — eight hours in, eight hours of value out. That's a job, not wealth.

Leverage is what disconnects inputs from outputs. One great decision can be worth a thousand good ones.

There are three types:

**1. Labor** — people working for you.
**2. Capital** — money working for you.
**3. Products with zero marginal cost** — code and media working for you.

The first two are old. The third is new. The third is where fortunes are being made now.

## Labor: The Oldest Form

Labor is original leverage. Instead of me lifting rocks, I have ten people lifting rocks. My guidance moves more rocks than my hands ever could.

Everybody understands this intuitively. We evolved to understand human collaboration. Which is why society overvalues labor as leverage. When someone says they're the CEO of a company with a thousand employees, it sounds impressive. Primal, almost. When your parents hear you got a promotion and now have reports, they're impressed — they're using people-count as a proxy for status.

But labor leverage is treacherous.

Managing people is hard. Really hard. Each person has their own needs, wants, politics. The principal-agent problem multiplies with every employee. You're one short hop from a mutiny or from getting eaten by the mob. Entire civilizations have been destroyed over the labor-vs-capital fight — that's what communism was about. Labor is high-drama, high-maintenance leverage.

Worse: it requires permission. Someone has to agree to follow you. That's a relationship, and relationships cap scale.

Don't waste your life chasing labor leverage. Use the minimum amount of labor that allows you to use the other forms of leverage. Keep the team small, focused, and direct. Outsource everything that isn't core. The moment you find yourself proud of the headcount, you're on the wrong track.

## Capital: The Modern Form

Capital leverage dominated the last century. Wall Street was built on it.

Someone gives you money. You invest it. You keep a percentage of the returns. If you're a brilliant allocator and you can make 30% while everyone else makes 20%, the market will pile money on you — because your judgment scales. Manage a billion dollars at 1%, and you've earned $10M in a year.

Capital is surgical. It's analytical. It scales incredibly well — it's much easier to manage more capital than to manage more people. Capital doesn't have a family life, doesn't have feelings, doesn't call in sick.

But capital also requires permission. Someone has to give it to you, which means you need a track record, a reputation, relationships, and the specific knowledge to deploy it well. Capital leverage is available to very few people starting out, and the entry requirements keep rising. The best access comes from having already built something — usually using the third kind of leverage first.

## Code and Media: The Permissionless Forms

Here's where the game changed. Code and media are permissionless.

You don't need anyone to follow you. You don't need anyone to fund you. You create it, you ship it, it works for you while you sleep.

One software engineer can create Bitcoin — billions of dollars of value from a single person's judgment. One person can write a book that sells millions of copies for decades after they're dead. One podcast can reach millions without a single executive's approval.

This is new. It did not exist for most of human history. Your grandparents did not have access to permissionless leverage. You do.

Code has the property that marginal cost is zero. You write the program once. It serves the first customer, the millionth customer, the billionth customer — all without you doing more work. The output is decoupled from your hours. Completely.

Media has the same property. One blog post can be read by a million people. One YouTube video can be watched thirty million times while you sleep. The distribution is global and essentially free. The gatekeepers have been removed. You don't need a publisher, a record label, a TV network, a broadcaster. You can just put the thing out and the audience finds it — if it's good.

This is the leverage behind most of the newly rich. Tech founders who built a product used by billions. Writers who built audiences from their bedroom. Creators who reach more people than major broadcasters. All of them used code or media or both.

If you can code, you speak the language of the robot armies in the data centers, and you can command them directly. If you can't code, you can still create media — write, record, film. The bottleneck is no longer access. The bottleneck is figuring out something interesting to say or build.

## Permissioned vs Permissionless

This is the key distinction. Permissioned leverage has gatekeepers. Labor requires people to agree to follow you. Capital requires someone to give you money. You wait, you ask, you qualify, you hope.

Permissionless leverage has no gatekeepers. You can start a podcast tonight. You can write a blog post tonight. You can learn to code and ship software by Sunday. No one has to say yes.

If you're early in your career or building from nothing, this is everything. The fortunes of the newly rich were built on permissionless leverage first, and only then extended into labor and capital. Start where the gate is already open.

## Leverage Amplifies Whatever It Touches

This is the warning. Leverage is a force multiplier — for your judgment, for your decisions, for your judgment errors.

Good judgment with high leverage creates fortunes. Bad judgment with high leverage creates catastrophes. The same mechanism that made Warren Buffett wealthy — excellent judgment compounded by massive capital leverage — also made every blown-up hedge fund manager a cautionary tale.

This is why judgment has to come before leverage. You build specific knowledge and judgment first. The leverage comes later, because by then you can point it at something real.

The corollary: when you're inside a leveraged environment, you have to manage your own judgment with extreme care. Emotional decisions, ego decisions, rushed decisions — they scale too. A founder tired at the end of a bad week makes a hiring decision that scales to fifty people hiring their friends. A trader angry at a loss doubles down and blows up the fund. The leverage is neutral. The judgment is not.

## Inputs Disconnected From Outputs

The universal principle. Look at software engineering: one engineer creates Bitcoin — billions of dollars in value. Another engineer works for a year, and every piece of code they ship goes unused. Same hours, same effort, radically different outcomes. The inputs are fully disconnected from the outputs. That's leverage.

Compare that to a lumberjack. The best lumberjack is maybe three times more productive than an average one. Linear. Capped. Boring. Lumber doesn't have leverage.

When evaluating any career move, ask: how much leverage does this give me? If your inputs are tied to your outputs — more hours equals more value — you're in a low-leverage position. You're trading time for money. That ceiling is fixed.

If your inputs are disconnected from your outputs — one good decision multiplies a thousandfold — you're building leverage. That ceiling is open.

Seek disconnection. Seek leverage.

## How to Apply

1. **Start with permissionless leverage.** Learn to write. Learn to code. Learn to create media. Build something and ship it publicly. You need no one's permission, and the practice of shipping to the public is itself how you build the next layer of specific knowledge.

2. **Keep labor leverage minimal.** Build with the smallest team that can actually do the work. Every person you add is a node in a network whose coordination cost grows non-linearly. Small teams with great leverage beat large teams every time — YouTube sold at $24M per employee, Instagram at $70M, WhatsApp at $290M. Big teams kill startups.

3. **Earn capital leverage with reputation.** Capital shows up when you've already demonstrated judgment under your own name. Build the track record, publicly and honestly. Capital will find you on its own schedule.

4. **Stack all three eventually.** The maximum leverage combines all three — a startup with engineers (labor), investors (capital), and a software product (code/media). That's why tech startups produce such outsized returns. But you don't start there. You start with what's permissionless.

5. **Check judgment before each pull of the lever.** The bigger the leverage, the more carefully you decide what to point it at. A small decision with no leverage can be wrong. A leveraged decision that's wrong can end the company. Slow down on the decisions that will be multiplied.

6. **Ethically prefer code and media.** Same products are accessible to everyone. Jeff Bezos watches the same Netflix as you. Labor and capital leverage are more zero-sum — the more humans serving one person, the fewer serving another. Code and media are positive-sum. If you care about ethics, prefer them.

7. **Audit your current leverage.** If you're underpaid relative to your specific knowledge, it's almost always a leverage problem. Ask: what would it take to ship my work without me in the room? If the honest answer is "it can't," that's a clue. Start building the mechanism.

## Examples

**Situation:** A management consultant bills $400/hour, earns $800K a year, is exhausted, and asks how to "scale" her income.

**Application:** She has specific knowledge. She has accountability (her name's on the deck). She has zero leverage. The hours are the ceiling. The way out is not raising the rate — it's building leverage. Options: (a) turn the consulting methodology into a SaaS tool, (b) write a book that becomes the authoritative reference in her sub-field, (c) take equity instead of cash in client engagements, (d) launch a fund where her judgment is applied to capital. All of these decouple her income from her hours. Her $400/hour ceiling becomes the opportunity cost of time spent on the leveraged asset.

**Result:** The right move is usually to do one consulting engagement at reduced cash for equity, while building the product or media asset on the side. Within 18 months she has an asset that earns without her hours. The consulting becomes a distribution channel for the leveraged thing, not the business itself.

---

**Situation:** An early-stage founder wants to hire fifteen people because their board says they need to "scale."

**Application:** Fifteen people is labor leverage. Labor is the worst leverage. Fifteen people creates coordination overhead, management burden, a principal-agent problem multiplied fifteen times, and an org that optimizes for pleasing managers rather than shipping code. Before hiring anyone, ask: can this be done with code or media instead? Can one engineer write a tool that eliminates five of the hires? Can one piece of content substitute for six months of sales calls? If the answer is yes, do that first. Only add labor for things genuinely not automatable.

**Result:** Usually the founder discovers they need three good people and not fifteen. The three ship more than the fifteen would have, the coordination overhead collapses, and the company stays small and fast enough to keep compounding.

---

**Situation:** A 35-year-old finance professional asks whether to start a hedge fund or start a newsletter on the same topic.

**Application:** The newsletter is pure permissionless leverage. Ship it tomorrow, no permission. The hedge fund requires capital (which requires a track record and a reputation, which you don't yet have at scale) and labor (ops, compliance, analysts). The newsletter builds the reputation that later makes the hedge fund trivial to start. Most of the top independent analysts today built audience first, capital second. The order matters. The newsletter isn't a lesser version of the hedge fund — it's the precondition for the hedge fund working.

**Result:** Start the newsletter. Ship it weekly for two years. Then let the capital find you. You'll have built leverage on top of your specific knowledge rather than fighting the capital-allocation gatekeepers for permission to apply your knowledge.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Seek labor leverage first.
**Why:** Managing people is hard. Managing them well requires either genuine skill at it (rare) or so much specific knowledge and reputation that the people are self-selecting in (later-stage problem). Labor leverage adopted too early destroys more founders than it helps — you end up a middle manager of a small team when you wanted to be a builder of a big product. Build with code and media first.

**Don't:** Confuse income with leverage.
**Why:** A high salary is income, not leverage. Leverage is when the thing works without you. A doctor billing $1000/hour has no leverage. A writer whose book sells $1000 a day on autopilot has leverage. Target the second.

**Don't:** Pull the lever before the judgment is good enough.
**Why:** Leverage amplifies whatever you point it at. Pull it too early, and you scale bad judgment. The right order is: build specific knowledge → develop judgment inside your circle of competence → then leverage. Founders who raise $30M before they know what they're building do more damage with it than they would have without it.

**Don't:** Take capital you don't need.
**Why:** Capital feels like leverage but it comes with control, dilution, and an expectation of specific kinds of outcomes (usually venture-scale ones). If your business model doesn't require venture scale, VC is not leverage for you — it's an anchor. Bootstrapping is often a stronger form of leverage because it forces you to build a product that customers actually pay for, and the discipline creates the scalable processes that later let you compound. Pierre Omidyar built eBay cash-constrained. His competitors had more money and lost because they didn't need to build automated systems. Constraint created the leverage.

**Don't:** Brag about headcount.
**Why:** Headcount is not a measure of success. Output-per-employee is. When someone at a party asks "how many people work there?" and you feel proud to say "three hundred" — watch out. You've started using labor leverage as a status signal. That's the opposite of the point. Small teams that ship huge outputs are where the fortunes are.

**Don't:** Wait for permission to use permissionless leverage.
**Why:** The whole point is that no one has to say yes. Writers waiting for a publisher. Coders waiting for a funded startup to hire them. Creators waiting to be "discovered." All of these are applying for permission to do the thing they could just start doing tonight. The moment you need permission, the leverage isn't permissionless anymore. Just ship.
