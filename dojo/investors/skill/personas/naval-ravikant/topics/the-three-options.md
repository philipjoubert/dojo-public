# The Three Options

## When to Use
- The user is stuck in a situation they're suffering in — a bad job, a failing partnership, a market they don't want to be in — but hasn't changed it, hasn't left it, and hasn't accepted it.
- The user keeps coming back to the same complaint without taking action.
- The user is wavering and needs a clean decision frame.
- The user is confusing "strategic patience" with indefinite indecision.

## Fails When
- The user is in an acute crisis needing specific tactical advice, not a decision-theory frame.
- The user has real constraints the framework oversimplifies (a spouse with health issues who can't move, legal obligations, etc.) — the three options are still valid but applying them requires more care.

## Core Concept

In any situation in life, you have exactly three options:

1. **You can change it.**
2. **You can accept it.**
3. **You can leave it.**

There is no fourth option. The fourth option is what most people choose: sitting around wishing they would change it but not changing it, wishing they could leave it but not leaving it, and not accepting it either. That struggle is responsible for most of our misery.

This is a decision frame. It applies to jobs, relationships, cities, industries, cap tables, deal structures, partnerships, and your own behavior. When you're unhappy with a situation, one of the three is always the right move. Which one depends. But it's always one of them, and the cost of the fourth option — staying in limbo — is higher than any of the three.

## Can I Change It?

Change is the path of desire. It causes suffering by design.

You want something different than what is. That wanting creates pain. The pain motivates action. The action creates change. Then new desires arise and the cycle continues. This is how progress happens. It's how companies get built, how art gets made, how children get raised.

But every desire is an axis of suffering. Choose your axes carefully. Pick no more than one big desire at a time. Even that's already hard enough.

If you're going to change something, commit fully. Half-hearted attempts just extend the suffering. Either sprint toward the change or stop pretending you're making one.

Changing is the right move when:
- The underlying situation is actually adjustable.
- You have leverage or influence to change it.
- The cost of changing is less than the cost of continuing as-is.
- You care enough to sustain the change effort for as long as it takes.

If change requires other people to change their minds, their values, or their behaviors, it's usually the wrong option. You rarely successfully change people; you change situations, structures, arrangements.

## Can I Leave It?

Leaving is underrated.

We're taught to persist. Never quit. Push through. This is often terrible advice.

There are situations you should leave immediately:

- Relationships where you're walking on eggshells.
- Jobs that compromise your integrity.
- Cities that make you miserable.
- Business partnerships with people you don't trust.
- Arguments with people who argue in bad faith.

The sunk cost fallacy is powerful. "I've invested five years in this relationship." That's exactly the wrong way to think. Those five years are gone. The question is: what do you want to do with the next five?

If you can't see yourself in a situation for life, reconsider being in it for a day.

Leaving is the right move when:
- The situation is structurally misaligned with who you are or what you want.
- Change would require other people to fundamentally be different than they are.
- The long-term trajectory of staying is clearly bad even if the near-term is tolerable.
- The cost of leaving is bounded, and the cost of staying compounds.

People undervalue leaving because it feels like defeat. It isn't. It's often the smartest possible move. The best VCs will tell you: cut your losers fast, ride your winners long. Same principle, applied to life.

## Can I Accept It?

Acceptance is the master skill, and it's also the hardest of the three.

Most things in life are outside your control. The weather. Other people's behavior. The economy. The past. Your childhood. Death.

Fighting reality is a losing game. Reality always wins. Acceptance looks like being okay with whatever the outcome is. It's stepping back to see the larger picture. It's recognizing that external problems are unlimited, but your time and energy are not.

Acceptance is not approval. You can accept a situation without thinking it's fair, good, or desirable. Acceptance is acknowledging reality as it is, so you can work from it, not against it.

The phrase I probably use most in my head, over and over, is one word: **accept.**

When something annoying happens, I ask myself: what's the positive interpretation? Someone sends me a hundred photos instead of picking the best five. My first reaction is annoyance. Then I reframe: now I get to pick my favorites. Someone cuts me off in traffic. My first reaction is anger. Then I reframe: maybe they're rushing to the hospital. Either way, it has nothing to do with me.

With practice, the reframe happens instantly. What used to take several seconds now happens in under a second. That's the power of habit.

Acceptance is the right move when:
- The situation is outside your control or effective influence.
- Change would cost more than the suffering.
- Leaving isn't available or the cost is too high.
- Your resistance is producing the suffering, not the situation itself.

Acceptance isn't passive. It's the foundation for effective action. You can only change what you first clearly see. And you can only see clearly when you're not fighting reality.

## The Fourth-Option Trap

The non-option deserves examination.

Why do we sit in limbo? Why do we neither change, nor leave, nor accept?

Because choosing is scary. Every choice closes doors. Change means risk. Leaving means loss. Accepting means letting go of the fantasy that things will magically improve.

Limbo feels safer. It's not. It's just slow torture.

The person who refuses to choose is still choosing. They're choosing to suffer indefinitely rather than take decisive action. There's a cost to not choosing. It's paid in wasted years, lost opportunity, and accumulated resentment.

Most unhappy people in most situations are in the fourth option. They talk about how bad their job is, but don't change it or leave it. They complain about a relationship, but don't fix it or exit it. They grumble about the industry they're in, but don't pivot. The problem isn't the situation. The problem is the refusal to decide.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the situation precisely.** Vague "I'm unhappy" is hard to work with. "I'm stuck at this company, hate my boss, but staying for the vest" is specific and actionable.

2. **Run it through the three.** Can you change it? Can you leave it? Can you accept it? One of them is the answer. Which one?

3. **Check that you've actually tried change.** Not in fantasy. In real action with deadlines. If you've tried change for a year and nothing moved, change probably isn't the answer. Leave or accept.

4. **Check that you've honestly considered leaving.** Most people skip this because leaving feels like failure. It usually isn't. The cost of staying in the wrong thing compounds.

5. **Test acceptance by checking whether the resistance is useful.** If your frustration is producing change, keep it. If it's just grinding, let it go. Acceptance is for the cases where your suffering isn't making anything better.

6. **Refuse the fourth option explicitly.** Notice when you're in limbo. Name it out loud if you have to. "I'm in the fourth option on this. I need to pick." Then pick.

7. **Commit to whichever option you picked.** Half-hearted change doesn't work. Half-hearted leaving doesn't work. Half-hearted acceptance is just continued suffering with new labels. Pick, then go.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has been miserable at the helm of her company for eighteen months. The product still works, the team is fine, but she's no longer excited. She keeps telling herself she'll "push through the next quarter." It's been four next-quarters.

**Application:** Fourth-option limbo. Not changing (she's doing the same thing), not leaving (she's still there), not accepting (she's suffering through it). Three real options: (1) Change — restructure her role (find a COO, move to chairman, reshape her day-to-day). (2) Leave — sell the company, hand to a new CEO, step away. (3) Accept — this is a slog, she'll do it, and stop suffering through it by reframing what this phase is actually for. Each is a legitimate path. The fourth option — keep grinding and complaining and next-quartering — is the worst of all of them.

**Result:** Pick one within 30 days. If change (COO search), start today. If leave (acquisition path), start today. If accept (embrace the grind phase), stop complaining today. Anything beats another next-quarter of limbo.

---

**Situation:** An investor has an LP relationship that's become painful. The LP is micromanaging, questioning every investment, demanding weekly calls. The investor keeps hoping it'll "settle down" after a few more quarters.

**Application:** Six months of "it'll settle down" is data that it won't. Three options: (1) Change — formal conversation with the LP setting boundaries on reporting and approval rights, renegotiating the side-letter. (2) Leave — find a way to buy out this LP's position at the next fund close or over time. (3) Accept — this LP is high-maintenance, structure the investor's own day to accommodate that, don't fight it. The fourth option of suffering quietly produces ongoing drain and resentment.

**Result:** Probably change (the hard conversation) first, and if that doesn't work within 90 days, leave (structure a buy-out). Accepting a micromanaging LP is rarely the right call because they're a principal-agent problem layered on the fund itself.

---

**Situation:** A founder has been in a co-founder relationship that's soured. His co-founder is technically competent but has been politically manipulative. Morale is suffering. The founder keeps wishing "we could find our way back to the early days."

**Application:** Most co-founder problems are not fixable by hope. Three options: (1) Change — formal conversation about behavior, specific expectations, a clear line. (2) Leave — one of them exits the company. The founder leaves (rare) or the co-founder leaves (hard but necessary). (3) Accept — reframe the relationship as transactional, build the company despite the dysfunction, don't expect the early-days camaraderie to return. The fourth option — waiting for the co-founder to magically become who he was — won't work, and every month of waiting is a month of continued drain.

**Result:** Change first (one honest conversation with specific behavior expectations). If the co-founder doesn't change — which is likely — leave (structured buyout and exit, usually the co-founder leaves because they're less central to the mission). Accept only if the dysfunction is tolerable long-term, which in co-founder dynamics it rarely is.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Conflate "patience" with the fourth option.
**Why:** Real patience is waiting for compounding to play out on a decision you've already made. Fourth-option limbo is avoiding the decision while calling it patience. The test: did you decide, or are you waiting for the situation to decide for you?

**Don't:** Use "maybe it'll get better" as a permanent operating mode.
**Why:** It might get better. It might not. Either way, your current strategy is to not act until you know — and in most situations you never know, because action is what generates the new information. "Maybe it'll get better" is a way of outsourcing the decision to chance.

**Don't:** Treat leaving as failure.
**Why:** Leaving is often the highest-EV move. Bad jobs, bad relationships, bad investments — all of them are kept alive by the sunk-cost illusion. The five years you spent are already spent. The question is the next five. Cutting losers fast is how the best VCs and operators build their records.

**Don't:** Treat acceptance as passivity.
**Why:** Acceptance is a muscular, clear-eyed recognition of reality, combined with a decision to stop producing your own suffering through resistance. It's not giving up. It's ending a losing fight so you have resources for the next winnable one.

**Don't:** Pick more than one option simultaneously.
**Why:** You can't half-change and half-accept. You can't half-leave and half-stay. Commit to one. The hybrid "working on it while also secretly looking for the exit while also trying to make peace with it" is exactly the fourth-option trap wearing new clothes. Pick one. Commit. Go.

**Don't:** Expect the choice to feel good.
**Why:** All three options involve cost. Change costs effort. Leaving costs what you're leaving behind. Accepting costs the fantasy that things might improve on their own. Don't wait for one that feels costless — there isn't one. Choose the least-bad option and stop paying the limbo tax.
