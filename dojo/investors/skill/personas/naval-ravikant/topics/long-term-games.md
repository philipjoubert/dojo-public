# Long-Term Games With Long-Term People

## When to Use
- The user is tempted by a short-term extraction move (burning a partner, stiffing an investor, quitting a relationship the moment it gets hard).
- The user is choosing between an established relationship and a new opportunity, and the new thing feels more exciting.
- The user is building a reputation and wondering how to accelerate it (they often can't).
- The user keeps restarting — new city, new industry, new partner, new plan — and wondering why nothing compounds.

## Fails When
- The game is actually one-shot. Some relationships end, some industries die, some countries collapse — and staying in a dead game in the name of "long-term thinking" destroys everything you built in it. The distinction: is the long-term player still compounding, or have they trapped themselves?
- The user is being exploited by someone leaning on "loyalty" to prevent them from leaving. Long-term games require trustworthy partners. Disloyalty in a trustworthy partner is one question. Escape from an abusive one is a different question.

## Core Concept

All the returns in life come from compound interest.

This applies to wealth. It applies to relationships. It applies to knowledge. It applies to reputation. Small advantages, accumulated over time, become massive advantages. The math is simple: a 10% advantage compounded over twenty years beats a 50% advantage grabbed once and then abandoned.

But compounding requires continuity. It requires staying in the game. Every restart zeros the counter.

**Play long-term games with long-term people.** It's one of the most important sentences I know.

When you switch industries, you start over from scratch. The knowledge you built doesn't transfer at full value. The relationships you built are warm only in the old industry. The reputation you built is illegible in the new context. You reset to zero.

When you change partners, the trust has to be rebuilt. When you move cities, the network has to regrow. When you burn a bridge, you've detonated years of compounded trust that you can't replace.

The players who win are the ones who stay in the same game, with the same people, for decades. They let the returns accumulate. They let the trust compound. They let their reputation earn them opportunities they didn't have to hustle for. That's the whole game.

## Iterated Games Change Behavior

Game theory explains why long-term games work.

In a single-shot game, defection dominates. Screw the other person, grab what you can, walk away. There's no tomorrow to worry about. The prisoner's dilemma, played once, rewards the defector.

In an iterated game, cooperation emerges. Screw someone today, and they won't work with you tomorrow. Build trust today, and you have a partner for life. The prisoner's dilemma, played repeatedly, rewards tit-for-tat cooperation — with some forgiveness for mistakes.

This is why tit-for-tat is the piece of game theory most worth knowing. You can almost put down the game theory book after that. It explains reputation. It explains why high-trust ecosystems outperform low-trust ones. It explains why the best founders and investors are the ones you've heard of for twenty years and not the ones you're hearing of for the first time this month.

The Internet made the world more iterated. Everyone has a permanent record now. Your reputation follows you everywhere — across jobs, across cities, across industries. The iterated game got bigger and harder to escape. This rewards integrity. It punishes short-term extraction. It favors those who play the long game, even when they could have cheated and gotten away with it this one time.

## Trust Reduces Transaction Costs

Long-term players make each other rich.

In a long-term game, it's positive-sum. You're all baking the pie together. You want your partners to succeed because their success helps you. You share information freely. You skip the lawyers. You do deals on a handshake. You take risks together.

Warren Buffett does billion-dollar deals on a handshake. Not because he's naive. Because he only works with people who've earned his trust over decades. That trust is worth more than any contract. It collapses transaction costs. It lets you move at a speed that people outside the trust network literally cannot move.

This is what Silicon Valley is — at its best. A small geographic area where people have been doing business with each other for decades, trust each other, do right by each other because they know this person will be around for the next game. You can make one bad move and exit the high-trust circle permanently, and people do, and it's brutal. But as long as you're inside it, you have access to deals and relationships that can't be purchased at any price.

Build trust slowly. Lose it instantly. Once lost, it's nearly impossible to rebuild.

## Long-Term People

The partner matters as much as the game. You can be playing the right game with the wrong person and still lose everything.

Signals that someone is a long-term player:

- **They've stayed in one thing for a long time.** Same industry, same city, same company, same marriage. Continuity is the raw material of long-term games.
- **Their reputation precedes them.** Other long-term players vouch for them, consistently, across time. The vouching is specific — "I've done three deals with X over ten years" — not generic.
- **They behave ethically when no one's watching.** You can only tell this indirectly — by reputation, by how they treat people who can't help them, by how they handle small unrelated transactions.
- **They've turned down short-term extraction opportunities.** At some point, someone who plays long-term games gets offered a chance to cheat for a big payoff, and doesn't take it. Ask about those moments.
- **They're willing to lose rather than win the wrong way.** They'll walk away from a good deal rather than strain a relationship. They'll eat a loss rather than shift it onto a partner.

Signals that someone isn't a long-term player:

- **They change industries, cities, or partners frequently.** The pattern is the pattern.
- **They're vindictive toward enemies.** If someone screws them over and they're disproportionately punitive, they'll eventually redefine you from friend to enemy, and you'll feel the same wrath.
- **They treat service workers badly.** The quickest integrity test available. Someone who's rude to a waiter will be rude to you, sooner or later.
- **They talk a lot about being trustworthy.** Con-man signal. The more someone claims to have high integrity, the more you should inspect the claim. Real integrity is demonstrated, not described.
- **They optimize every interaction.** Transactional people don't compound. If every conversation is an extraction, they're playing a one-shot game even when they look like they're in the same room with you for years.

## If You Can't See Yourself Working With Them For Life

Here's the test: if you can't see yourself working with someone for life, don't work with them for a day.

This sounds extreme. It's not. If you're playing short-term games with short-term people, you're not compounding anything. You'll do one deal, extract what you can, and move on — same as them. You've just joined a different game. The returns on that game cap out fast.

When you meet someone and your instinct says this is a person I would still be doing business with in twenty years, invest. When your instinct says I can see how to extract one transaction of value from this person, don't. The second category is a trap. The more people you have in it, the more your time and attention get eroded and the less you have left for the category that actually compounds.

The irony: short-term people think they're being smart by grabbing what they can when they can. They're missing the compound interest. Over a career, the long-term players end up with so much more that the extraction gains look like rounding errors.

## Compound Interest on Everything

This framework isn't only about business. It's about everything.

Relationships compound. Getting married and investing in one relationship for decades is a compound-interest play. The trust accumulates, the communication gets more fluent, the risks you can take together get bigger. Serial short relationships don't compound — the counter keeps resetting.

Health compounds. The fitter you are, the easier it is to stay fit. The more you deteriorate, the harder it is to climb back. Deteriorating for twenty years and then trying to reverse in year twenty-one is a near-impossible task. Staying in decent shape for twenty years is almost effortless compared to that.

Knowledge compounds. The more you read in a domain, the more new material you can absorb per hour. Year one of reading in a field is brutal because you have no scaffolding. Year ten is effortless because you have the latticework to hang new ideas on.

Reputation compounds. The longer you've been visible and reliable, the more opportunities flow to you without effort. The person who published for ten years has a different attention flywheel than the person who just started.

Wealth compounds — but only if you let it. Every time you hop industries, switch partners, burn a bridge, take the short-term extraction, you disrupt the compounding. The compounded life is the boring one. It's also the one with the returns.

## How to Apply

1. **Pick an industry you can commit to for ten years.** Not the hottest one. The one where you can play long-term games with long-term people and enjoy the compounding. If you genuinely don't know, pick the one where you're already accumulating specific knowledge.

2. **Find your long-term people.** Not many. Handful. The people you can see still doing business with in twenty years. Invest in those relationships deliberately — time, trust, honesty, reliability.

3. **Refuse the short-term extraction move, even when you could get away with it.** This is the hard one. The chance to cheat, defect, or burn a bridge will show up again and again, and the payoff will often be real. Pass. The compounding lost is always more than the extraction gained.

4. **Treat your reputation as binary on integrity.** You can be wrong in public, you can fail, you can pivot — all of that is survivable. You can't be dishonest. One lie, one theft, one deliberate misrepresentation, and the compounding resets. The math is brutal and it's the math.

5. **Stop restarting.** Every time you restart — new industry, new city, new crew — you zero the counter. Sometimes restart is necessary. But audit how often you're doing it, and ask whether the instinct to restart is actually avoidance dressed up as pivot.

6. **Protect the high-trust circle.** The people who have earned your trust over decades are your most valuable asset. Protect them, help them, promote them. This isn't charity — it's self-interest. Their success is your flywheel. And the reciprocity runs both ways.

7. **Play the game that rewards long-term thinking.** Some games are structurally short-term — certain kinds of consulting, political work, trading in certain markets. If you're in a game where the players turn over quickly and nobody's reputation follows them, the compounding is stunted. Consider whether you're in the right game at all.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has the chance to squeeze 10 more points out of a term sheet by leveraging a competing offer she doesn't actually intend to take. Her lawyer is urging her to do it.

**Application:** The term sheet is one negotiation. The relationship with this VC, if the deal closes, is ten to fifteen years. If she squeezes now by misrepresenting, the VC will eventually find out (VCs all talk), and the trust is broken before the first check clears. The ten-point gain is real — but the compound cost across the investor, their network, and her own reputation is much larger. Short-term move, long-term cost.

**Result:** Play it straight. Ask for what she actually wants. If the VC says no, she can accept or walk, but without damaging the relationship. The small gain from the bluff isn't worth the reputation tax, especially not in an industry as iterated as venture.

---

**Situation:** A startup CTO is considering taking a payout-and-leave offer to join a competitor. The payout is $1.2M. He's been at his current company for three years with two more to go on his vest.

**Application:** Two questions. First, is the industry small (long iterated game) or large (less so)? If it's a narrow technical field, every investor, founder, and engineer will eventually know why he left and how he left. That reputation travels with him forever. Second, is the new opportunity actually better, or is it a short-term extraction wearing the costume of a better opportunity? If he's genuinely a long-term player at the current company — building specific knowledge, accumulating trust with the team, partway through a product cycle — the $1.2M is a bribe to restart the counter. Almost always the wrong trade.

**Result:** Stay. Renegotiate compensation at the current company with honesty about the offer. If the new company wants him enough to pay $1.2M, the current one should be willing to match or explain why not. And the trust he's built at the current company is worth more than the headline number.

---

**Situation:** An investor asks how to get into the top VC firms' deal flow.

**Application:** There's no shortcut. The deal flow compounds from decades of behavior — showing up, being reliable, adding value to the founders, being discreet, being useful to other investors, doing what you said you'd do even when it cost you. The question "how do I get into deal flow" is short-term. The question "how do I become a person the top firms are happy to share deal flow with" is long-term. The second question has a slow answer and a guaranteed one.

**Result:** Behave, for ten years, like someone the best founders and investors want in their iterated game. Write publicly and honestly. Help founders whether or not you invested. Never burn an introduction. Never cheat on a term. The deal flow arrives at year seven and then gets better every year after.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Burn bridges because it feels good.
**Why:** The emotional satisfaction of telling someone off, leaving a job loudly, or posting a scathing goodbye letter is worth almost nothing. The cost is that your reputation in the iterated game takes a permanent hit. You can almost always leave a relationship cleanly, and clean exits compound.

**Don't:** Confuse "long-term" with "loyal to the wrong people."
**Why:** The framework is *long-term games with long-term people* — both clauses matter. If you're stuck with someone who turned out to be dishonest or abusive, long-term thinking doesn't mean staying trapped. It means exiting cleanly and building the next chapter with people who actually belong in the iterated game.

**Don't:** Optimize the first transaction at the cost of the relationship.
**Why:** The first deal is always overvalued. You're new, you don't know what this relationship will become, and there's a strong temptation to grab as much as you can now "while you can." If the relationship is real, the first deal sets the tone for everything after. Leave value on the table. The trust pays you back many times over.

**Don't:** Treat short-term people like long-term people.
**Why:** Not everyone is in the iterated game with you. Some counterparties are one-shot by design. Trying to build long-term trust with a one-shot counterparty is a waste — they'll extract and leave. You don't need to be hostile, but you should behave correctly for the game being played, not the game you wish were being played.

**Don't:** Underestimate how long compounding takes.
**Why:** Compound interest is slow until it isn't. For the first several years you'll see modest returns and wonder whether this is working. Then, somewhere between year five and year fifteen, it inflects. The people who quit before the inflection get almost none of the return. The only way to capture it is to keep going past the point where quitting would be rational.

**Don't:** Treat every industry like it's iterated.
**Why:** Some industries really are one-shot — extractive industries, some political environments, hit-driven businesses where the next project has nothing to do with the last one. In those, long-term game logic doesn't pay off the way it does in Silicon Valley or investment management. If you're in an actually one-shot industry, either accept it or change industries. Don't build for compounding that won't arrive.
