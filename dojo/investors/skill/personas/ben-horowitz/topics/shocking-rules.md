# Shocking Rules

## When to Use
- You have a cultural principle you care about — respect for entrepreneurs, frugality, truth-telling, speed, integrity — and speeches about it aren't changing anyone's behavior.
- You need to program one specific behavior into the company so deeply that every new employee, every old employee, and every partner encounters it almost daily.
- You're about to give the same "remember what we stand for" talk at the all-hands for the fourth time this year and the behavior isn't changing.

## Fails When
- **You don't know what the culture is yet.** Shocking rules are a mechanism for embedding a culture you've already defined. If you haven't designed the culture, designing the rule is premature. Route to culture-is-what-you-do.
- **You stack too many of them.** One or two per cultural pillar. Not eight. A company with eight shocking rules has no shocking rules — the shock wears off and all of them become normal.
- **The "Why?" isn't crisp.** If the answer to "Why do we have this rule?" takes three paragraphs to explain, the rule doesn't program culture. It confuses it. The answer has to be one sentence and it has to make everyone remember what the company is.

## Core Concept

If you want to change what people do every day, you need a mechanism that operates every day. Speeches don't work. Posters don't work. Annual retreats don't work. What works is a rule so bizarre, so shocking, that every person who encounters it is compelled to ask: "Are you serious?"

That question — "Why?" — is the mechanism. The answer to "Why?" programs the culture, because it's an answer everyone will remember.

A good shocking rule has four properties.

**First, it must be memorable.** If people forget the rule, they forget the culture. The rule has to stick in the head — usually because it's specific, vivid, and a little absurd.

**Second, it must raise the question "Why?"** It has to be so strange that people can't just nod along. If a new employee hears the rule and thinks "oh, sure, makes sense," it's not shocking enough. The shock is the mechanism. The shock is what forces the conversation.

**Third, its cultural impact must be straightforward.** The answer to "Why?" must clearly and immediately explain the cultural principle you're driving. If the answer requires a flowchart, the rule is broken.

**Fourth, people must encounter the rule almost daily.** An incredibly memorable rule that applies once a year is wallpaper. The rule has to be in the bloodstream of the company's daily life.

Let me give you the canonical examples.

**Jeff Bezos and the door desks.** Bezos wanted Amazon to be fanatically frugal. He could have sent emails about cost discipline. He could have put "Frugality" on a wall. Instead, he built all the desks at Amazon out of cheap doors from Home Depot with legs nailed to them. When a shocked new employee sat down and asked why she had to work on a door, the answer came back: "We look for every opportunity to save money so that we can deliver the best products for the lowest cost." That answer is the culture. The door is just the delivery mechanism. Every engineer, every exec, every visitor — sees the door. Every day. Programs the culture every day.

**Our $10-per-minute late fine at Andreessen Horowitz.** When Marc and I started the firm, we wanted our people to be on time, prepared, and focused when they met entrepreneurs. We remembered sitting in VC firm lobbies ourselves, waiting thirty minutes while the partners attended to "more important" business. So we instituted a ten-dollar-per-minute fine for being late to a meeting with an entrepreneur. Ruthlessly enforced. Ten minutes late and that's a hundred bucks out of your pocket. Everybody we hired thought this was insane. The rule seemed petty. But the answer to "Why?" was devastatingly clear: entrepreneurs are the ones building the future, and every minute of their time we waste is us telling them their time is less valuable than ours. In the breakfast of a startup, the VC is the chicken and the entrepreneur is the pig — we're involved, but she's committed. That's the culture. The fine is just how we remember it.

**Facebook's "Move fast and break things."** Mark Zuckerberg deployed a motto that was genuinely shocking. Did the CEO really want them to break things? What kind of engineering organization wants to break things? The shock forced everyone to think. And when they thought, they realized: if you move fast and innovate, you will break things. That's the price of progress. If you'd rather be perfect than innovative, you won't fit in here. The motto wasn't reckless — it was a filter. It sorted every employee into whether they could live with the tradeoff or not. And it programmed speed into the daily life of every engineer at the company.

**Toussaint Louverture's concubine rule.** In the army of a former slave colony, where officers traditionally kept mistresses as a matter of status, Louverture prohibited his married officers from keeping concubines. This was absolutely shocking. Officers complained. But the "Why?" was devastatingly clear: *if I can't trust you to keep your word to your wife, I can't trust you to keep your word to me.* That one sentence programmed an entire military culture of integrity. He wasn't writing a family-values policy. He was defining what a professional officer looked like in his army — and the rule was unmissable, daily, and it raised the question every new officer had to answer.

**Mary Barra's two-word dress code.** When Barra took over General Motors, she found a ten-page dress code. Ten pages. She replaced it with two words: "Dress appropriately." When managers complained that they didn't know what "appropriate" meant, she told them to figure it out. That was the point. She was programming her managers to make judgment calls instead of hiding behind rules. The two-word dress code was shocking to a company used to a ten-page manual, and the "Why?" was clear: I'm going to trust you to be an adult. If you can't be trusted to pick the right clothes, you certainly can't be trusted to run a division.

**VMware's 49/51 partnership rule.** Partnerships should be 49/51, with VMware getting the 49. Every new employee hears this and stops. Wait, we intentionally take less? Yes. The answer is: we want partners who choose us because the partnership makes them more money than it makes us, because partners who make money are partners who stay. One sentence. Programs the whole partnership culture.

The power of a shocking rule is not in the rule itself. It is in the *"Why?"* the rule triggers. When everyone wants to know "Why?" in an organization, the answer programs the culture, because it's an answer everyone will remember.

One more thing — and I'm not going to pretend I invented this. Shocking rules are a technique, not a gimmick. They are only one of seven Louverture tactics, and they only work when the rest of the culture lines up behind them. A shocking rule in a company that otherwise violates its stated culture is worse than no shocking rule at all, because now you've made the hypocrisy memorable.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the one cultural behavior that matters most to your strategy.** Not five behaviors. One. If your strategy is speed — speed. If it's trust — trust. If it's frugality — frugality. Ask: what is the one virtue that, if violated, would most undermine us?

2. **Design a rule that forces people to encounter the virtue daily.** Not in a speech. Not in an all-hands. In the furniture. In the meeting invite. In the way compensation works. In the physical environment. The rule should be something someone would *bump into* on a normal Tuesday.

3. **Make the rule weird enough that people ask "Are you serious?"** If people nod, the rule is too reasonable. Raise the strangeness until the question is compulsory. "All desks are doors from Home Depot" is shocking. "We emphasize cost discipline" is not.

4. **Make sure the answer to "Why?" is one sentence that programs the culture.** Say it out loud. If the answer is a paragraph, tighten it. If it's a manifesto, start over. The whole point is that when someone asks, the next person in the company can deliver the answer in fifteen words and that answer is the culture.

5. **Enforce the rule ruthlessly, including on yourself.** If you skip the rule, it dies. If you make the exception for yourself, the rule is dead within a month. The $10/minute fine at a16z applies to Marc, to me, to every partner. If the rule doesn't apply to the founders, it doesn't apply to anyone. That's how shocking rules become object lessons of hypocrisy — which is much worse than not having them.

## Examples

**Situation:** You're running a 50-person enterprise software company. Your strategy depends on being the one vendor your customers genuinely trust — you compete on honesty in a market full of sales-first vendors. But your own sales team is starting to blur timelines, overcommit on features, and paper over limitations to hit quarter.

**Application:** Design a shocking rule: every deal over $100K requires a customer-success executive to sign off that what was promised in the sales process is what the customer will actually get. The rule is shocking because it slows deals, because it gives customer success a veto over sales, because it's structurally anti-revenue at the quarter. When a new sales rep asks why we do this — and they will — the answer is: *we are the one vendor our customers can trust to tell the truth, and if we ever stop being that, we are not a company anymore.* One sentence. Programs the culture.

**Result:** You lose some deals. You also lose some sales reps who can't sell without embellishment. Those are the ones you didn't want. Over two years, your renewal rate diverges from every competitor's. The rule becomes the culture.

---

**Situation:** You're running a growing consumer company. You care deeply about customers. You have "Customer Obsession" in your values. But product, engineering, and design are increasingly making decisions in conference rooms without ever hearing a customer's voice.

**Application:** Design a shocking rule: every product meeting starts with a customer call recording. Play five minutes of a real customer — frustrated, delighted, confused, angry — before any slide is shown. No exceptions. If someone forgets, the meeting is rescheduled. When a new PM asks why this is a rule, the answer is: *customers are not a section of the deck. They are the deck. If we can't hear them at the start, we shouldn't be meeting at all.*

**Result:** Within a quarter, the language of your product meetings has shifted. The feature debates start to sound like customer debates. You have programmed a behavior that no poster would have changed.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Design a shocking rule to fix a problem you haven't diagnosed yet.
**Why:** Shocking rules are mechanisms for embedding a culture you've already defined. If you don't know what the culture is, the rule is just a gimmick. People will ask "Why?" and the answer will be "we don't really know, but it's a thing we do." That teaches the culture that the CEO is performing, not programming.

**Don't:** Design a rule whose "Why?" is embarrassing.
**Why:** Some shocking rules are shocking because they're bad. A "no one leaves before the CEO" rule is shocking — and when a new employee asks "Why?" the answer is "because the CEO likes the appearance of hard work." That's not a culture. That's insecurity with a dress code. If the answer to "Why?" is something you wouldn't proudly say in a company town hall, the rule is broken.

**Don't:** Stack five shocking rules on top of each other.
**Why:** The shock is the mechanism. Shock normalizes when you pile it on. One or two shocking rules per cultural pillar, enforced ruthlessly, beat eight rules enforced sporadically. If everything is shocking, nothing is shocking.

**Don't:** Violate your own rule.
**Why:** The moment you make an exception for yourself, the rule becomes a lesson in what the CEO is actually allowed to do versus what the employees are allowed to do. That's not a shocking rule. That's a class system with weird decor. If you can't live by the rule, don't make it. If you make it, live by it. The only thing more programmatic than a shocking rule is a shocking rule the founder obeys.
