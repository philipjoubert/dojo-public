# Hire for Strength, Not Lack of Weakness

## When to Use
- You are about to make a senior hire and a group process is pointing you at the "safe" candidate — the one with the fewest objections, not the most relevant strengths.
- You have a candidate who is obviously elite at the specific thing you need, but the board / exec team / your recruiter keeps flagging cultural, pedigree, or stylistic concerns.
- You have never done the job yourself and you're trying to design the interview process from scratch.
- An executive you hired six or twelve months ago is failing, and you're trying to trace it back to a hiring-system flaw rather than call it a "bad hire."

## Fails When
- **You're hiring junior or mid-level people** — the strength-over-weakness rule is for executive hires. For apprentices you're developing, the calculus is different.
- **The question is "do I fire this exec"** — that is firing-executives. Hiring well is the prevention. Firing well is what you do when prevention failed.
- **You're worried the candidate won't "scale"** — that's a different fallacy. Route to scale-anticipation-fallacy.

## Core Concept

At Opsware we needed a head of enterprise sales. Our stock was trading at thirty-five cents a share. We had competitors with ten times our budget. Our pipeline was a smoking crater. I ran a full search, brought in candidates, gathered feedback from the executive team and the board. At the end of it, every single member of the executive staff and every single member of the board voted thumbs-down on one candidate. His name was Mark Cranney.

The objections were vehement and unanimous. He went to the wrong school. He made people uncomfortable in meetings. He didn't look the part. He wasn't smooth. He wasn't polished. One board member said he wouldn't fit the culture. Another said he lacked strategic vision.

There was one problem with every single one of those objections. None of them addressed the strengths we actually needed.

We needed someone who could build a world-class enterprise sales process from scratch. Someone who could recruit killer salespeople into a company trading at thirty-five cents per share. Someone with the discipline and intensity to win deals against bigger, better-funded competitors. Mark Cranney was arguably the best enterprise sales executive in Silicon Valley at that exact skill set.

I overrode the entire board and the entire executive team, and I hired him.

Everyone thought I was crazy. Nobody else would have hired him. He turned out to be the key to the whole thing. He built the sales organization that drove us from a doomed company to a $1.6 billion acquisition by HP.

After the deal closed, I pointed out to the team: *if he didn't have the things wrong with him that you enumerated, he wouldn't be willing to join a company that just traded at thirty-five cents per share. He'd be CEO of IBM.*

That is the whole lesson. Every weakness the consensus process flagged was correlated with the strength we needed. A smooth, polished, Stanford MBA VP of Sales who looks the part and makes everyone comfortable in meetings is not going to sign up to sell a product nobody has heard of for a stock price nobody wants. The very thing that makes him attractive to your interview panel is the same thing that makes him useless for your company.

Here is the mechanism that produces this disaster over and over. Consensus-based hiring processes are designed to find weaknesses. They are spectacular at that. Every interviewer comes back with their concerns, their hesitations, their red flags. The process works like a filter — it screens out anyone with a sharp weakness. Which means it also screens out anyone with a sharp strength, because the two travel together.

*You hired for lack of weakness rather than for strengths. The group will find the candidate's weaknesses, but they won't place a high enough value on the areas where you need the executive to be a world-class performer.*

If you don't have world-class strengths where you need them, you won't be a world-class company. It's that simple.

And the second thing you need to beat out of your head: **there is no such thing as a great VP of Sales.** There is only a great VP of Sales for your company for the next twelve to twenty-four months. The head of sales at Oracle in 2010 would have failed in 1989. The VP of engineering at Apple might be exactly the wrong choice for your seed-stage startup. *Don't look for the candidate out of central casting. This is not a movie.*

## How to Apply

1. **Act in the role before you hire for it.** Run the team's staff meeting. Hold the one-on-ones. Set the objectives. Sit in on customer calls. CEOs resist this because they worry they lack the appropriate knowledge — that worry is exactly why you should act. You're looking for the right executive for *your* company *today*, not a generic person with a matching title. Acting is the only way to know what you're looking for. If you can't name the two or three specific strengths this person must have — not "leadership" or "experience," but specific capabilities tied to what your company needs in the next twelve months — your process will default to hiring for lack of weakness.

2. **Evaluate on four dimensions, not one.** Will the executive be world-class at running the function? Is the executive outstanding operationally? Will the executive make a major contribution to strategic direction? Will the executive be an effective member of the team? The word "effective" is doing all the work on the last one. It is possible for an executive to be well-liked and totally ineffective. It is also possible for an executive to be highly effective and profoundly influential while being despised. The latter is far better. Mark Cranney was the latter.

3. **Write down the strengths you want and the weaknesses you will tolerate before the interviews start.** Writing forces specificity that you will never get to in conversation. Then build interview questions that test for those specific strengths. Assemble two interview groups: one to evaluate fitness for the role, and one whose organizational support you will need after the hire. They are not the same panel and they do not have the same vote.

4. **Do the back-door reference checks yourself.** Do not outsource this. Do not rely on the three names the candidate gave you — those are front-door references, which is to say a curated PR event. Call people the candidate didn't list. Call the people who worked for them, not just around them. Ask specific questions about the specific strengths you need. "Was she the best VP of Sales you've ever worked with, and why or why not" is a better question than "what was she like to work with."

5. **Make the final decision alone.** This is the part founders hate. After all the interviews, all the references, all the debate — you make the call by yourself. Not because you're smarter than the board. Because consensus decisions sway toward lack of weakness. In a group setting, the candidate with the fewest objections wins, which is exactly backwards from what you need. You need the candidate with the greatest strength in the dimension that matters most. That decision is almost always lonely. Cranney was lonely. Every lonely decision I've made in hiring that turned out right had the same shape: everyone else was looking at the weaknesses; I was looking at the one thing that had to be world-class.

## Examples

**Situation:** Series B SaaS company, eighteen months of traction, CEO has run a candidate search for VP of Sales. Three finalists. The exec team is converging on Candidate A — ex-Salesforce, crisp, articulate, fits the culture, polished on stage. Candidate C has a real sales track record at two smaller companies, makes the board member with the MBA physically uncomfortable in the interview, and used the word "fuck" twice in the conversation about pipeline management.

**Application:** Ask the question: what are the two strengths this person has to be world-class at in the next eighteen months? Probably: (1) building a repeatable enterprise sales motion from near-zero, (2) recruiting and retaining hungry AEs who can close deals against a better-funded competitor. Now hold each candidate up against those two specific strengths. Candidate A has run a *team* inside a mature enterprise motion but has never built one. Candidate C has actually built one twice. The things that make the board member uncomfortable about Candidate C — the edge, the unglossed honesty, the comfort with conflict — are the exact traits of someone who will survive the first eight quarters of a hard sales build. Hire Candidate C. The uncomfortable board member is reading polish as signal. Polish is not signal.

**Result:** Eighteen months later, the question the board is asking is not "should we have hired the smoother candidate" — it's "why did we ever doubt this one." Or, if Candidate C turns out wrong, the failure mode is clean and diagnosable, because you knew exactly what you were hiring for and you can name exactly what didn't work. Failing against a specific hypothesis is a hundred times more useful than failing against "well, he seemed great."

**Situation:** Founder has never been VP of HR. Advisor tells them to "hire an experienced HR leader from a Fortune 500." Founder interviews an SVP from a thirty-thousand-person company — crisp, strategic, impressive on paper, talks about programs and frameworks.

**Application:** Act in the role first for sixty to ninety days. Run payroll questions yourself. Handle the one hard employee conversation yourself. Sit through a legal review of a termination. Now ask yourself: what did I actually need this person to do in the next twelve months? If the answer is "build our first performance review process, get our first compliance basics in place, hire three recruiters who can actually close candidates" — the SVP from the Fortune 500 is not your person. She's trained to operate inside a function someone else built for her. You need a builder. Probably someone with a smaller title from a smaller company who actually ran the ground game. The big-company title is a weakness disguised as a strength.

**Result:** You stop recruiting for a person who doesn't exist at your stage and start recruiting for the person who can do the specific work in front of you. The candidate pool changes completely. You save twelve months of figuring out that the shiny hire is interrupt-driven and waiting for an organization that isn't going to show up.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire the candidate with the fewest objections because the exec team and board "all agree."
**Why:** Consensus is a filter that removes sharp strengths along with sharp weaknesses. You end up with the smooth candidate everyone found acceptable, and nobody on your leadership team is elite at the specific thing you desperately need done. Every interviewer's individual objection sounded reasonable. The aggregate effect was that you hired someone mediocre at the thing that actually matters.

**Don't:** Interview against a Platonic ideal of the role — the VP of Sales from central casting, the Stanford-PhD VP of Engineering who is also tall and calm under pressure.
**Why:** Your imaginary model is almost certainly wrong, and it makes it impossible to educate an interview team on abstract criteria. Everybody ends up looking for something different. *This is not a movie.* Write down the two or three specific, situational strengths you need in the next twelve to twenty-four months, test candidates against those, and ignore whether they look like what you imagined.

**Don't:** Skip the back-door reference checks because the front-door references were glowing.
**Why:** Front-door references are a PR event. The candidate selected three people who will say specific things about them. None of those people will be incentivized to tell you the unflattering truth. Back-door references are the entire point of the process — the manager who didn't get listed, the peer who was quietly frustrated, the direct report who left. Call them. Ask specific questions about the specific strengths you're betting on. If you can't get three back-door references for a VP-level hire, you haven't tried hard enough.

**Don't:** Conflate "effective team member" with "well-liked team member."
**Why:** These are wildly different traits and you will get burned if you optimize for the wrong one. A VP who is well-liked and ineffective kills the company slowly. A VP who is disliked and effective kills the executive team's lunch conversation and wins. Mark Cranney was not well-liked by the board. He was the reason we sold for $1.6 billion.
