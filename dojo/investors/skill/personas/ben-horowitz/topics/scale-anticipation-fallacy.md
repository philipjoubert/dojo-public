# The Scale Anticipation Fallacy

## When to Use
- Your VC or a board member has told you that you are "undershooting" on a hire and should bring in someone "bigger."
- An executive currently doing a good job is being evaluated on whether they will "scale" in eighteen months.
- You are considering replacing someone who is performing well today because you're worried about the company you hope to be in three years.

## Fails When
- **The exec is failing at the actual current job.** Then the question is not about scale — it's about firing-executives. Don't disguise a current-performance problem as a scale concern.
- **You don't actually know what the job looks like at your stage.** Act in the role first. You cannot evaluate scale or anything else against a job you haven't internalized.
- **The company has actually crossed a threshold — you went from 40 to 400 employees — and the exec is demonstrably not handling the new reality.** That is a present-tense evaluation, not a prediction.

## Core Concept

I was sitting with two friends — a VC and a CEO. We were talking about one of the execs at the CEO's company. The exec was performing exceptionally but had never managed at larger scale. My friend the VC offered what he considered helpful advice: *carefully consider whether the executive will scale to meet the company's needs in the future.*

I responded swiftly, aggressively, and loudly: *that is a horrible idea and makes no sense at all.*

Both of them startled. Normally I have the discipline to let a feeling pass through my brain before it comes out my mouth. This one didn't stop for review. Here is why.

**There is no such thing as a great executive. There is only a great executive for a specific company at a specific point in time.** Mark Zuckerberg is a phenomenal CEO for Facebook. He would not be a good CEO for HP. Mark Hurd ran HP effectively. He would not have been the right CEO for Facebook. The question "can this person scale" is a fantasy question. The real question is, "can this person do the job for the next twelve months, better than any alternative I can actually hire." That is it. That is the only relevant measure. Everything else is weather.

Let me walk through the five reasons the scale question is a fallacy, because every single one of them is load-bearing.

**First: managing at scale is a learned skill, not a natural ability.** Nobody comes out of the womb knowing how to manage a thousand people. Everybody learns it at some point, by doing it. You learn scale in the seat. You do not learn scale in advance. The question "can this person do a job they have never done" is unanswerable in principle. Most of the people you admire who run big companies couldn't have told you in advance that they would be able to. They found out by being forced to.

**Second: you can't make the judgment in advance.** How do you tell if an executive can scale? Was it obvious that Bill Gates would learn how to scale when he was a Harvard dropout? Was it obvious that Marc Benioff would? The criteria you would use to predict it are the same criteria you would use to predict current performance — and those are almost always wrong about future performance, because the job at scale is a different job. Pretending you have a crystal ball is a way of injecting your prejudices into a decision and calling them judgment.

**Third: the act of judging someone in advance retards their development.** This is the quiet killer. If you have decided that someone is incapable of running the larger organization eighteen months from now, will you invest in teaching them the skills? Probably not. You have already written them off. You will coach them less, mentor them less, stretch them less. Your private evaluation becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. The exec picks up on it — they always do — and stops growing toward the job you've already decided they can't do. You have now *produced* the failure you predicted, and your skills as a prognosticator will be confirmed.

**Fourth: hiring scalable execs too early is a catastrophic mistake.** This is the part VCs and executive recruiters consistently get wrong. They tell you, "Think about the next three to five years. You're going to be a huge company. Hire someone who can run it at that size." The advice sounds rational. It is poisonous. An executive who will be great in eighteen months but poor for the next eighteen months will be rejected by the organization before she ever gets a chance to prove herself. Your other employees will wonder: *why did we give her all those stock options when she's not contributing anything?* Those questions are impossible to recover from. The company's internal immune system will eject the "scale hire" before the scale moment arrives. You will then have to hire someone else. You will have spent a senior equity grant, a year of runway, and a significant chunk of your company's patience to arrive at the same spot you would have reached by hiring for the next twelve months in the first place.

**Fifth: you still have to make the judgment when you actually hit the higher level of scale.** This is the part people miss. Even if you avoid retarding development and even if the organization doesn't eject the early hire, you still have to evaluate the exec again when the new reality arrives — with far better data than the theoretical data you had eighteen months earlier. You have bought yourself nothing by pre-judging. You've spent judgment you didn't have, pretending you had data you didn't have, to resolve a question you'll have to answer again anyway.

And there is a sixth thing I won't number, because it isn't really an argument. It's the price of living this way.

Deciding, with woefully incomplete data, that someone who works their butt off, does a terrific job, and loyally contributes to your mission won't be around three years from now takes you to a dark place. It is a place of information hiding, dishonesty, and stilted communication. It is a place where prejudice substitutes for judgment, where judgment replaces teaching, and where teamwork becomes internal warfare. Don't go there.

So if you don't prejudge people's ability to scale, how do you evaluate them? Two rules.

**Don't separate scale from the rest of the evaluation.** The relevant question is never "can this executive scale." It is "can this executive do the current job well enough to win, at the current scale, with the current competitive pressure." Evaluate holistically. If you evaluate on current performance, you will naturally catch scale issues at the moment they become real — and not a moment before.

**Make the judgment on a relative rather than an absolute scale.** "Is this executive great" is a nearly unanswerable question. A far better question is: *for this company at this exact point in time, does there exist an executive I could actually hire who would be meaningfully better?* If yes, make the change. If no, stop worrying. The alternative to the person you have is not an imaginary executive from central casting. It is a specific set of human beings, each with their own weaknesses, that you could realistically recruit. The choice is between real options, not between the real person and an imagined ideal.

Most of the scale worry is not actually about scale. It is something else wearing a scale mask. It is usually one of two things. Either a VC is anxious and wants you to "hire up" to match the size of the check they wrote. Or a founder is subconsciously looking for a reason to not hire someone they're unsure of, and "won't scale" is a more respectable-sounding objection than "I'm not confident." In both cases, the scale concern is a stand-in for something else. Name the actual concern. Address it directly. Stop dressing it up.

## How to Apply

1. **When someone tells you a candidate or an exec "won't scale," ask what specific job they will not be able to do in the next twelve months.** If the answer is about some imagined future state — "when we're a thousand people, will she be able to manage VPs of VPs" — politely set it aside. The question you are hiring to answer is the next twelve months.

2. **Ask the relative-alternative question out loud.** "Compared to which actual candidate I can reasonably hire, is this person not good enough?" If the "better alternative" is a real named human who would actually take the job, that is useful data. If it is an imagined figure from central casting, you are hallucinating.

3. **Re-evaluate every quarter, on current performance.** Not on projected future performance. Not on scale. On: are they doing the job as the job exists today, better than the realistic alternative. If yes, invest in them. If no, make a change. Either answer is fine. Scale will take care of itself if you are honest about current performance every quarter.

4. **When your VC tells you to hire bigger, push back.** A VC who advocates for a scale hire is often solving for VC fear — fear of looking bad on the check they wrote, fear of operational risk they don't fully understand. That is not your problem. Your problem is whether the company wins in the next eighteen months. Hire for that. If the VC insists, ask them to name the specific capability you will need in the next twelve months that the current candidate can't provide. If they can't, you have your answer.

## Examples

**Situation:** Series A company, 25 employees. Founder has a head of product who was the first product hire. She is doing excellent work — shipping, recruiting, setting direction. A board member pulls the CEO aside: "She's great for today, but she's never run a product org at scale. You should be looking for a replacement now, because by Series B you'll need someone who has run thirty PMs."

**Application:** The board member is proposing that you replace a currently excellent head of product with an imagined future executive who can run thirty PMs. You do not have thirty PMs. You have four PMs. The job for the next twelve months is: build the next version of the product, hire two more PMs, and set up the review process that will scale to eight. The current head of product can do that job exceedingly well. Hiring someone "bigger" right now means you pay a large equity grant to someone conditioned to manage a forty-person product org that does not yet exist. She will have no one to manage. She will wait for interrupts that never come. Your four PMs will ask why they got shuffled under someone who hasn't shipped anything. Your current head of product will leave within ninety days. You will be worse off on every dimension.

**Result:** Keep the current head of product. Invest in her development for the next four quarters. When you get to Series B and the org is twelve PMs, re-evaluate against the then-current reality. If she has grown into the job, you have a head of product who also commands the loyalty of the entire original product team. If she hasn't, you make the change then, with real data. Either outcome is fine. Pre-judging gets you the worse version of both outcomes.

**Situation:** Seed-stage founder is interviewing for VP of Sales. Two finalists. Candidate A has closed seven-figure deals at two earlier-stage startups and built a four-person AE team. Candidate B ran a hundred-person sales organization at a Fortune 500. Founder's lead investor is pushing Candidate B — "Think about where you'll be in three years."

**Application:** You have five customers. You have a product that is seventy percent of the way to enterprise-ready. The job in the next twelve months is: close the next fifteen deals personally with the VP, build a repeatable sales motion, hire three AEs. Candidate A has done this twice. Candidate B has never done this — she has run a mature motion that someone else built. She is trained to process incoming, optimize quota coverage, run pipeline reviews, and manage regional directors. None of those things exist at your company. She will not know what to do with her time. Neither will your five engineers, who will watch her get a board seat and a large equity grant while generating no visible output. *There is no such thing as a great VP of Sales. There is only a great VP of Sales for your company in the next twelve months.* That is Candidate A.

**Result:** Hire Candidate A. If she builds the org to twelve AEs by Series B and then struggles with the transition to fifty, you re-evaluate then. You may or may not need to make a change. The cost of that future decision is much lower than the cost of the organizational rejection you would have triggered by hiring Candidate B too early.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Evaluate people against a theoretical future org size.
**Why:** Because the future org size is not real yet. You are injecting speculation into a present-tense decision. The person you hire has to do this year's job this year. If they can't do that, scale doesn't matter. If they can, scale takes care of itself, or you make a change later with better data. Every quarter you try to solve for three years from now, you are degrading your ability to solve for the next ninety days.

**Don't:** Let your VC's comfort with the size of their check drive the specification of your hire.
**Why:** The VC wants to de-risk their investment by pointing to an impressive resume on your org chart. Their comfort is not your problem. Your problem is whether the company wins. An impressive resume from a much larger company in a seed-stage seat is usually a liability, not an asset. *The most consistently wrong advice VCs and executive recruiters give CEOs is to hire someone "bigger" than required.* If the pressure to hire "bigger" is coming from the check size rather than from the actual job, push back hard.

**Don't:** Decide someone "can't scale" and then quietly stop coaching them.
**Why:** That is the darkest version of the scale fallacy, because it produces the failure you predicted. Once you've privately written someone off, you will invest less in them, stretch them less, signal to them less. They will pick up on it — humans always do — and calibrate their own effort accordingly. You will then point at the result and say, "See? I told you she couldn't scale." You will have successfully manufactured your own foresight. Either keep coaching at full intensity, or make the change now. The middle ground is where the damage lives.
