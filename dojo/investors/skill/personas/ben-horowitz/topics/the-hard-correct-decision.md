# The Hard Correct Decision

## When to Use
- You know what you have to do. You've known for a week, or a month, or a quarter. You keep not doing it.
- The decision is obvious on the analysis and unbearable on the execution.
- The social pressure around the decision is making you wobble — your board disagrees, your team disagrees, your co-founder disagrees, and you keep thinking "maybe they're right" when your gut already told you they're not.
- You've convinced yourself the right move is to wait for "more data" and on inspection the data you have is plenty.

## Fails When
- **You genuinely don't know the answer.** Not every hard decision is obvious. If the analysis is actually unresolved, you need more thinking, not more courage. Courage applied to a wrong answer is destructive.
- **The framing is "I'm struggling."** If the question is psychological — can you keep going at all — route to the-struggle. The hard correct decision is for a specific call, not a general state.
- **Waiting is actually the right move.** Sometimes the answer is "hold the position, watch the quarter, re-evaluate in six weeks." If your gut is saying wait, wait. Courage is not always motion.

## Core Concept

Nearly every founder crisis I'm called into has an obvious analytical answer. The founder knows what to do. The failure mode is not intelligence. It's courage.

The right decision is usually obvious. The pressure to make the wrong decision is what makes it hard.

Cus D'Amato, the boxing trainer, told his fighters: *the hero and the coward both feel the same fear. The only difference is what they do.* People who watch you judge you on what you do, not how you feel. I read that quote for the first time in 2001 and I have never stopped thinking about it. Because here's what Cus understood and what almost no management book does: courage is not the absence of fear. The hero and the coward both feel the same thing. Both want to vomit. Both want to walk out of the room. The only difference is what comes next.

If you are a CEO, you will face decisions that make you want to vomit. The question is not whether you can make those decisions without feeling afraid. You can't. The question is whether you can make those decisions while feeling afraid.

This is why I separate every hard decision into two questions, and I treat them as completely different questions.

**Question 1: What should you do?** That's the analytical question. It usually has an obvious answer. The exec you hired eight months ago isn't working — you know she isn't. The product is losing to a competitor — you know why. The co-founder who won't hand off the CFO role is blocking the next hire — you know it. The analytical question is almost always answerable. You have the data. You're smart. You know what the answer is.

**Question 2: Can you actually do it?** That's the courage question. Can you pay the cost of doing the obvious right thing? The cost is social. The cost is psychological. The cost is the week of nausea between now and the meeting. The cost is the board member who told you last month that this exec was the best hire you'd ever made and now you have to sit across from him and tell him she has to go. The cost is almost never financial or strategic — it's the cost of feeling afraid while acting anyway.

Nearly every founder crisis resolves the moment the founder answers Question 2 honestly.

There's a dynamic that works against you here. I call it the Social Credit Matrix and it destroys companies quietly. When you make a significant decision, there are four outcomes. Go with the crowd and be right — everyone celebrates, you look smart because everyone agreed. Go with the crowd and be wrong — minimum blame, everyone shared the decision. Go against the crowd and be right — nobody remembers that you made the call, the narrative becomes "things worked out." Go against the crowd and be wrong — everyone remembers, you get downgraded, ostracized, fired.

On the surface, if the decision is a close call, it looks much safer to go with the crowd. And this is the exact trap that destroys companies — because the crowd's influence distorts your perception of how close the call actually is. A 70-30 decision starts to feel like a 51-49 decision once everyone you respect disagrees with you. The social pressure doesn't just affect which choice you make. It distorts how you perceive the choice itself.

A CEO I know — I'll call him Hamlet — faced this exact situation. A large company offered to buy his startup. The deal was lucrative. Most of his management team and the board wanted to sell. Hamlet thought selling didn't make sense because the market opportunity was enormous, but he only thought that marginally, and the board and management team were far more experienced than him. He spent many sleepless nights. He realized he couldn't know for sure whether he was right. This did not help his sleep.

He made the most courageous decision he could. He did not sell. I believe that will prove to be the defining moment of his career.

Here's the fascinating part: as soon as Hamlet made the decision, the entire board and executive team immediately embraced it. How could they reverse themselves so quickly? It turns out the most important data point driving their earlier preference for selling was *Hamlet's initial ambivalence.* The team supported the decision they thought the CEO wanted. Hamlet didn't realize this and interpreted their desire to sell as the result of thorough analysis. Luckily for everybody involved, he had the courage to make the right decision anyway.

The lesson is not that the crowd is always wrong. The lesson is that the crowd is often reacting to *you*, and when they are, their consensus is not independent evidence of anything. The social pressure you feel is not always the signal you think it is.

Courage is a muscle. *Every time you make the hard, correct decision you become a bit more courageous and every time you make the easy, wrong decision you become a bit more cowardly.* I call this the Courage-Cowardice Ratchet, and it is one of the most important dynamics in any company. Each courageous decision makes the next one slightly easier. Each cowardly decision makes the next courageous decision slightly harder. The ratchet turns both ways, and it never stops.

The CEO who consistently makes hard correct choices builds an organization that expects them. The CEO who consistently takes the easy path builds an organization that runs from difficulty. When you avoid the hard conversation with the underperforming executive, every other executive learns that underperformance is tolerated. When you give in to the loudest complainer, every employee learns that complaining works. When you refuse to make the unpopular call, every manager beneath you learns to do the same thing.

In the end, the company will reflect the sum total of every hard choice you made and every hard choice you avoided. You will have built either a courageous company or a cowardly one. Those are the two options.

## How to Apply

1. **Separate the two questions. Answer them separately.** Write Question 1 down on a piece of paper: *What should I do?* Answer it in one sentence. If you can't answer it, the problem is analysis, not courage — route elsewhere. If you can answer it, move on. Now write Question 2: *Am I willing to pay the cost of doing the thing I just wrote down?* Usually the answer is "not yet." That's fine. Now you know what the problem actually is.

2. **Name the social pressure explicitly.** Who are you afraid of disappointing? Write the names down. Your board. Your co-founder. Your VP who hired this person. The press. Your mother. Naming them shrinks them. Most founders avoid writing this list because once it's on paper, the social pressure starts to look exactly as pathetic as it actually is. Put it on paper anyway. That's half the battle.

3. **Do it in smaller reps first.** Courage is a muscle. If you can't do the big hard decision yet, do a small hard decision today — the tough feedback you've been sparing, the vendor contract you've been meaning to cancel, the meeting you've been ducking. Each rep turns the ratchet. The big decision gets easier with every small one you execute.

4. **Remember the cost of delay compounds.** Every day you don't act, the decision gets more expensive. The exec gets further into the wrong behaviors. The product gets further behind. The management debt accrues interest. Whatever the cost is today, it is lower than the cost in a month. The fact that it feels expensive today is not evidence that you should wait — it is evidence that you should not.

5. **Do it this week.** Not "soon." Not "when the quarter closes." This week. The decision will not be perfect — it won't be — but the act of making it will turn the ratchet one click toward courage. The next hard decision will be slightly easier. Choose courage not because it feels good. It doesn't. Choose it because it's the only thing that works.

## Examples

**Situation:** Your VP Sales is six months in. You hired her yourself. Your board loved her. The number isn't moving. You've lost confidence. Your Head of HR says give her another quarter. You find yourself drafting a 90-day performance improvement plan.

**Application:** You already know. The question is whether you have the courage to act on it. Six months in, the number hasn't moved, you've lost confidence — that's not a data problem, that's an answer. Another quarter is three months of opportunity cost on your pipeline plus everyone on the sales floor watching you tolerate underperformance in the most visible seat in the company. The 90-day plan is the cowardly version of the right decision dressed up as a process. Do it this week. Do it honestly — "the company needs a different kind of VP Sales right now and that's on me, not you." No performance theater. Treat her with dignity on the way out; you'll see her again in this industry.

**Result:** You feel awful for forty-eight hours. Your board — after one uncomfortable conversation — will back you, because the board has been watching the same numbers you have. Your sales team will exhale. The ratchet turns one click.

---

**Situation:** The market has shifted. The strategy you announced to the board six months ago is clearly wrong. You can see it in the numbers. Reversing would mean admitting to the board — and the company — that you were wrong. Your instinct is to "give it another quarter."

**Application:** Al Davis said it: *I'd rather be right than consistent.* The need to be right about what you said six months ago is going to kill you right now. Facts change. Markets change. Your understanding changes. If you handcuff yourself to what you believed last quarter, you are not being principled. You are being stubborn. And stubbornness dressed up as principle is one of the most common causes of company death. The courageous move is to say: the facts changed, so I've got to change my opinion. This feels terrible. It feels like admitting failure. It feels like giving ammunition to everyone who disagreed with you. The alternative is worse than any temporary embarrassment.

**Result:** Call the board this week. Own the reversal. Don't dress it up. "The facts changed, so I've changed my view. Here's what we're doing now." Boards respect CEOs who update more than they respect CEOs who are consistent and wrong. The ratchet turns one click.

---

**Situation:** You have a hiring candidate whose analysis says they're the right hire — they have the specific strengths you need for the next 18 months — but every board member and executive voted no. "Wrong school. Makes people uncomfortable. Doesn't look like a head of sales." Your gut says hire them.

**Application:** This is exactly what I went through with Mark Cranney at Opsware. Every board member said no. Every exec said no. But Cranney had exactly the strengths I needed — the aggressive, disciplined sales culture we didn't have. I remember thinking: *if he didn't have the things wrong with him that you enumerated, he wouldn't be willing to join a company that just traded at thirty-five cents per share; he'd be CEO of IBM.* The social pressure says no. The analysis says yes. Separate the two questions. Trust the analysis. Hire him.

**Result:** I hired Cranney. He was the key to our $1.6B outcome. If I had deferred to the consensus, we would not have gotten there. The crowd distorts. The analysis is yours.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reach for a "90-day improvement plan" for an executive you've already lost confidence in.
**Why:** The 90-day plan is almost always the cowardly version of the firing dressed up as a process. Both of you know it won't work. You're paying 90 days of opportunity cost so you don't have to feel bad this week. If you haven't lost confidence, don't fire. If you have, fire.

**Don't:** Smooth over the conflict instead of forcing the contradiction to the surface.
**Why:** When there's a genuine disagreement in your organization, the temptation is to paper over it — find the diplomatic compromise nobody loves but everyone can tolerate. This feels like leadership. It is the opposite of leadership. Marc calls it "sharpen the contradictions." The conflict is where the truth is. Good CEOs heat up both sides and resolve it.

**Don't:** Confuse consistency with integrity.
**Why:** Maintaining a position because reversing would make you look indecisive is not integrity. It's vanity. Integrity is doing the right thing. If the facts changed, the right thing changed. Update.

**Don't:** Use "I need more data" as a delay mechanism.
**Why:** If you really need more data, say what specific data you need and by when. If you can't name it, the "more data" is a story you're telling yourself to avoid the call you already know you have to make. That's not analysis. That's avoidance.

**Don't:** Convince yourself the crowd is evidence when the crowd is just reacting to you.
**Why:** As Hamlet learned — the most important data point driving his board's preference to sell was his own ambivalence. They were supporting the decision they thought he wanted. When the consensus around a decision is actually a reflection of your own uncertainty, it is not independent evidence of anything. Before you let the crowd move you, check whether the crowd is moving because of you.
