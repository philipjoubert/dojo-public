# Hiring and Interviewing — One Hour to Predict a Year

## When to Use
- You have an interview slot with a candidate and need to extract maximum information per minute.
- You're designing the interview loop for a role and need to decide what each interviewer probes.
- A recent hire turned out badly and you want to learn the lesson rather than blame the candidate.
- You're about to hire a senior person from outside and want to think clearly about the risk.
- A peer has just told you "I have a great feeling about this one" and you need to convert feeling into evidence.

## Fails When
- The interviewer doesn't know the candidate's prior work well enough to evaluate it. If you can't follow what "I led the platform redesign" actually meant, the answer is decoration.
- The questions list gets used as a checklist with no follow-up. The list is bait; the drilling is the interview.
- The interview is treated as the whole selection process. References are the second of two information channels — the absence of either is a known liability.
- The interviewer talks more than the candidate. Sell the company in the hour and you've learned nothing about them.

## Core Concept

Interviewing is one of two emotionally charged tasks a manager performs — the other being talking a valued employee out of quitting. It has four purposes, in this order: select a good performer; educate the candidate about the company and the work; determine whether a mutual match exists; sell them on the job *if* they belong. Selling comes last and is conditional. If you start selling, you have sacrificed the first three purposes for the fourth — and the fourth only matters when the first three came back yes.

The mathematical problem is brutal. We know how hard it is to assess the actual past performance of subordinates we have worked closely with for years. In the interview we sit somebody down and try to find out, in an hour, how well they will perform in an entirely new environment. If performance appraisal is difficult, interviewing is just about impossible. The risks of failure are high. Careful interviewing doesn't guarantee anything; it merely increases your odds of getting lucky. That is the honest baseline, and the manager who pretends otherwise is the manager who keeps making the same hiring mistake.

The hour demands two disciplines. First, the candidate does roughly 80 percent of the talking, and *what* they talk about is your concern. Your job is to ask "what" and "why" in a way that drives the conversation onto subjects familiar to both of you, where you can evaluate the significance of what they did, not the polish of how they describe it. Second, you steer toward concrete past situations rather than personality reads or hypotheticals. The candidate's account of how they handled a real problem in their last job — what they did, why, what they would do differently — is the closest signal you'll get to how they'll handle real problems in your job. Generalities are the failure mode. The interview is yours to control, and if you don't, you have only yourself to blame.

> "The applicant should do 80 percent of the talking during the interview, and *what* he talks about should be your main concern. But you have a great deal of control here by being an active listener… The interview is yours to control, and if you don't, you have only yourself to blame." (HOM ch.14)

> "If performance appraisal is difficult, interviewing is just about impossible. The fact is, we managers have no choice but to perform the interview, no matter how hard it is. But we must realize that the risks of failure are high." (HOM ch.14)

## How to Apply

1. **Hold the four purposes in mind, in order.** Select the performer. Educate them about the work. Determine the match. Sell, *if* — and only if — the first three came back yes. If you find yourself selling in the first ten minutes, you've inverted the order.

2. **Plan the time budget before the candidate sits down.** You have an hour, maybe two. Roughly 80 percent goes to the candidate talking. Subtract opening and close and you may have forty good interrogating minutes — your only asset. Prepare to interrupt politely when an answer is wandering. "I'd like to change the subject to X" is not rude. Letting a garrulous answer eat ten of your forty minutes is rude — to your own asset.

3. **Drive at concrete past situations, not personality.** The right question is not "are you a self-starter" — it is "tell me about a project where the goal was unclear, what you did in the first week, and what you would do differently if you ran it again." Ask "what" and "why" repeatedly. The answer to "what would you have done differently" is one of the most diagnostic in the kit — it tells you whether they have metabolized the experience or only memorized it.

4. **Use Grove's question set, organized by what each one is *for*.** From a group of Intel managers, the questions that produced the most signal were:

   - *Technical / skills* — "Describe some projects that were highly regarded by your management, especially by the levels above your immediate supervisor." "What are your weaknesses? How are you working to eliminate them?"
   - *What they did with the knowledge* — "What do you consider your most significant achievements? Why were they important to you?" "What do you consider your most significant failures?"
   - *Discrepancies between knowing and doing* — "What did you learn from those failures?" "What problems are you encountering in your current position? How are you going about solving them? What could you have done to prevent them?"
   - *Operational values* — "Why do you think you're ready for this new job?" "Convince me why my company should hire you." "Why do you think an engineer should be chosen for a marketing position?" (vary by role).

   The questions are scaffolding. The drilling is the interview. Each one is the first in a chain — every answer earns a "what specifically" or a "why."

5. **Run a hypothetical in your own world.** I once interviewed a Harvard MBA from food service for a cost-accountant position at Intel. He knew nothing about semiconductors; I knew nothing about finance. I walked him through the manufacturing process step by step and asked him what the finished cost of a wafer would be. He asked questions, thought, reasoned through the basic cost-accounting principles, and arrived at the right answer. Hired — not because he had the answer, but because the exercise demonstrated first-rate problem-solving capacity.

6. **Let them ask you questions, and listen to what they ask.** What a candidate asks tells you what they know about the company, what they care about, and how prepared they are. Signal, not verdict — I once hired a manager whose pre-interview research was the best I'd seen, and he failed badly on the job.

7. **Pair the interview with a serious reference check.** Interview is the candidate's account; references are an independent account. References rarely lie outright, but they routinely fail to volunteer specific critical remarks — "I didn't want to be in his way" is the apology you'll hear after a bad hire blows up. Stay on the phone long enough to build a bond. The last ten minutes of a half-hour call are worth more than the first ten.

8. **If possible, talk with the candidate again after references.** Post-reference second interviews can be tightly focused — addressing exactly the discrepancies the references surfaced.

9. **Cross-reference to TRM before deciding.** A great track record on Task A is not a great track record on Task B. The seasoned sales manager is high-TRM as a sales manager and roughly zero-TRM as a manufacturing manager on day one — same personal maturity, different task. Before extending the offer, ask: what is the task we're hiring this person *for*, and what is their TRM on *that* task? If TRM will be low at start, are you prepared to manage them in structured mode for six to nine months? The hidden hiring failure is rarely talent. It's mismatch between actual TRM on the actual task and the management mode the organization will actually provide.

10. **Be straight, no tricks.** The candidate is a potential employee. They'll leave with a strong set of first impressions, and if those are wrong and you hire them, correcting them takes a long time. Show yourself and your environment as they really are.

## Examples

**Situation:** I interview someone for a high-level position at Intel. I do the work as carefully as I can. I have a very good feel, I think, for the whys and wherefores of the person's skills, past performance, and values. We hire him. From day one he is a disaster.

**Application:** Much humbled, I went back over my interview notes and the reference conversations. To this day I haven't a clue about why I didn't spot the considerable flaws. The lesson isn't "interview harder" — it's "accept the actual probability distribution." Interviewing increases your odds; it doesn't eliminate the risk. The discipline implied isn't heroic technique. It's a reference check that doesn't get skipped, a second interview when references surface a discrepancy, and a quick correction when the hire is wrong rather than rationalizing for six months.

**Result:** Interviewing is a high-risk proposition no matter how good the interviewer. The manager who internalizes that builds the *system* — multiple interviews, references, post-reference follow-up, an honest 90-day review — instead of relying on personal radar.

---

**Situation:** A peer has hired several excellent people for similar roles. You have hired a couple of losers in the same window. You ask: are there certain questions that would help you make better judgments?

**Application:** Focus the selection process on establishing two things only: the candidate's *performance record*, and your ability to *communicate* with each other. The interview is one part of the process — not the whole. Use your questioning to discover exactly what the candidate has done in previous positions, in as much detail as you can understand. Have them elaborate on what they liked and disliked, on what they consider their biggest accomplishment and their biggest failure. Then judge the communication itself — does the candidate give superficial answers, or overwhelm with detail? Either is a signal. Plan to interview more than once and have your assessments validated by your boss and associates.

> "Don't look for magic solutions. Selecting a candidate for a job is one of the most nebulous activities a manager performs. Focus the selection process on establishing only two things: the performance record of the candidate and your ability to communicate effectively with him or her." (One-on-One with Andy Grove)

**Result:** The peer who hires well isn't using a secret question set. They're running the same nebulous process, disciplined enough to hold to past performance and communication, patient enough to do the references seriously. Magic-bullet questions don't exist. Process discipline does.

---

**Situation:** A first-time founder is filling a head-of-marketing role. The strongest candidate has run growth at three startups — strong record, well referenced, and the founder "just clicks" with her in the interview. The hire feels obvious.

**Application:** The chemistry is signal, but it's the wrong signal to weight. Ask: what is the *task* I am hiring her for, and what is her TRM on *that* task? Has she run growth at this stage, in this segment, with this go-to-market motion? If the answer is "growth yes, but never pre-PMF, never B2B, never sales-led" — her TRM on the actual job is medium at best, regardless of seniority. The hire may still be right, but the management plan is structured supervision for the first ninety days, not "she's a veteran, leave her alone." The bad hire isn't the candidate; it's the candidate plus the wrong management mode applied on day one.

**Result:** Same hire, monitoring mode from day one — looks like a bad hire by month three. Same hire, structured mode while she ramps — outperforming by month four. The interview never tells the founder which scenario they're in. Cross-referencing to TRM does.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hire on chemistry or "I had a great feeling in the room."
**Why:** Chemistry is the residue of two people each presenting their best face for an hour. It tells you they can have a pleasant conversation; it does not tell you whether they'll perform under pressure for two years. The fix isn't "ignore your gut" — it's drill into past performance with concrete what/why questions, and write down what specifically they did, not how it felt.

**Don't:** Talk more than the candidate.
**Why:** If they're not doing 80 percent of the talking, you're interviewing yourself. Selling comes fourth, and only after the first three purposes returned yes.

**Don't:** Settle for generalities.
**Why:** "I'm a strong leader" is a sentence, not evidence. The fix is "what specifically." What did you do in the first week? What did you say to the head of engineering? What would you do differently? Drill until the answer has nouns and verbs. If it never does, that *is* the answer.

**Don't:** Skip references because the interview "felt great."
**Why:** Interview and references are two channels into the same question. Either alone is incomplete. References are where the candidate's account gets corroborated or quietly contradicted — and the contradictions are usually buried under polite reluctance. Stay on the phone; build the bond; the last ten minutes are worth more than the first ten.

**Don't:** Use tricks or pressure stunts.
**Why:** Candidates leave with strong first impressions about you and the company. If those are wrong and you hire them, the misimpression takes a long time to correct. Signal comes from straightforward, well-prepared questions, not gamesmanship.

**Don't:** Hire on track record without checking the track-record-to-task match.
**Why:** Past performance predicts future performance only when the past task and the future task are similar. Seasoned sales manager into operations is the canonical case — strong record, near-zero TRM on the new job, supervisor treats them like a veteran, customer pays the tuition. Before extending the offer, write down the task, write down their experience with that task, decide whether the gap is bridgeable.

**Don't:** Rationalize a bad hire for six months because you championed it.
**Why:** Hiring is a probability play. Sometimes the probability loses, even after thorough interviews. Cost of admitting it at month three is far smaller than at month nine. Treat the 90-day window as a real review, not a courtesy.
