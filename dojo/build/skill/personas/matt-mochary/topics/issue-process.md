# The Issue / Proposed Solution Process

## When to Use
- Running a weekly leadership team meeting and you want it to actually resolve things instead of cycling.
- The team keeps surfacing problems verbally, in passing, in 1-on-1s — and the same ones keep recurring.
- Two execs are stuck on a cross-functional issue and you need a clean way to escalate.
- You want to install a default culture of "write the issue down with a proposed solution" rather than "complain about it in the hallway."
- A group needs to decide something and you want the loudest voice to *not* win by default.

## Fails When
- **Genuine emergency.** If the building is on fire — outage, security breach, customer about to walk in the next hour — handle it live, write the post-mortem after. The protocol is for the 95% of issues that aren't actually that urgent.
- **The proposer doesn't have enough context to write the solution.** A new hire surfacing an issue they don't fully own yet. Coach context first; don't force "what's your proposed solution?" on someone who genuinely needs help reading the situation. (But push back on "I don't know" from someone who *should* know — see below.)
- **You run the protocol but never decide.** The system depends on the decision-maker actually deciding at the end. If you keep punting — "let's revisit next week" — the team learns the protocol is theatre and stops writing issues up.
- **The team is below 5 people.** At 2–4 people, real-time conversation is faster than the written protocol. Install it when you grow past the point where everyone is in every conversation.

## Core Concept

Most leadership team meetings are inefficient (listening is slower than reading) and ineffective (only the verbally aggressive people get heard). Verbal issue-raising looks democratic and isn't. The forward people speak; the thoughtful people stay quiet; the issue gets discussed for forty-five minutes; nobody writes anything down; the same issue is back in two weeks.

The fix is to require everything go into writing first. Anyone who wants to raise an issue at a team meeting writes it up with three things: the issue, what they did to help create it, and a proposed solution. Then async comments. Then a structured meeting. Then a decision with DRIs and due dates.

> "Team members will often want to bring up an issue and discuss it verbally. This step is inefficient (listening takes longer than reading) and ineffective (only forward people speak up and get heard). Instead, require that anyone who presents an issue at a team meeting do so in writing. The write-up should include both a detailed description of the Issue as well as their Proposed Solution."
> — *articles/decision-making.md*

The "what I did to help create this" line matters more than people realise. It does two things: it stops the issue being framed as a complaint about other people (so others don't feel attacked and can engage on the substance), and it usually surfaces a way the proposer can solve the issue alone, by reversing whatever they did to create it. Most issues evaporate at this step, before they ever hit the meeting.

The proposed-solution step is non-negotiable, even when the proposer says "I don't know the answer." Make them guess. Even with 10% confidence, write something declarative — *"Do this..."* — not a list of options. The bold proposal looks aggressive and creates a flag in the sand that generates a productive discussion fast. Lists of options generate forty-five minute meetings that decide nothing.

This protocol is what makes the loudest-voice problem solvable in groups: comments go in the doc *before* the senior voice speaks, so the team's actual thinking is captured rather than aligned to whoever talks first.

## How to Apply

**The basic loop:**

1. **Anyone with an issue writes it up.** Standard template:
   - **Proposer:** name.
   - **Decision-maker:** who should decide. (CEO: avoid putting yourself here unless this is genuinely a Type 1 call — push it down.)
   - **Issue:** state the problem.
   - **What I did to help create this situation:** the critical step. Don't skip it.
   - **Proposed solution:** declarative, with actions, DRIs, due dates. No option lists.
   - **Loom video:** 2–3 minutes of the proposer talking through it. The video carries the emotion and nuance the writing can't; the writing carries the structure the video can't.
2. **Share the doc async.** Decision-maker reads it first, applies the heard protocol — repeats the issue back until the proposer says "that's right." Then opens it up to comments.
3. **Each commenter writes in their own line.** "+1" if they agree with the proposed solution. If they disagree, they write their own proposed solution. The decision-maker does *not* comment yet (loudest voice in the room).
4. **At the meeting, the decision-maker has 5 minutes (tactical) or up to 20 minutes (strategic) per issue.** Reads the comments aloud, thanks each author by name (heard step), asks any clarifying questions.
5. **Decide.** The decision-maker writes the decision in the doc. Turns it into Next Actions with DRI (Directly Responsible Individual) and Due Date. Publishes.
6. **If the decision-maker can't decide in the allotted time:** don't keep talking. Write down what additional information is needed, who collects it, by when, and when she'll review. Then move on. (See RAPID for the larger version of this.)

**The 1-on-1 variant:**

The same template, slimmed for a manager-report conversation. Report writes the issue up *before* the 1-on-1, including their proposed solution. Manager reads it during the meeting (not before — keeps the manager honest, prevents pre-loading the response). If the issue can be resolved in the 1-on-1, great. If it requires cross-functional input, port the same write-up directly into the group template. The structure is identical; the audience expands.

## Group Conscience: when the group has to decide

Sometimes the decision-maker isn't a single person — it's the group. A leadership team voting on a strategic direction; a board on a major decision; a small partnership picking among options. In those cases the loudest-voice problem becomes the central problem, and the protocol is structured polling, not loudest-voice.

> "As CEO, your voice is the 'loudest in the room.' [...] Once people hear your perspective, some percentage will naturally alter their views to more closely match yours. This % is much higher than you might imagine."
> — *articles/loudest-voice-in-the-room.md*

**Group decision protocol:**

1. **State the question. Do not state your view.** One sentence written at the top.
2. **Three minutes of silent writing.** Each person writes their proposed answer in their own line. No discussion.
3. **Reveal simultaneously.** Or, if verbal: junior people first (Amazon convention — surfaces information without contamination, also surfaces the strong junior thinkers).
4. **Heard step.** Whoever is facilitating reads each comment back to its author until each says "that's right."
5. **Now discuss.** With the actual range of views on the page, not just the senior view echoed back.
6. **Decide.** This is rarely consensus. Someone is the decision-maker; they decide; everyone got heard. That's where buy-in comes from, not from getting your way.

The exception: an issue is genuinely a Type 1 / irreversible / "this is mine" decision and the senior person has already decided. Don't fake-poll. State openly: "This is a decision I'm making, not a discussion. I'll answer questions." Worse than a unilateral decision is a *fake* democratic process.

## Lord of the Flies: why you can't tell two execs to "work it out"

When two leaders conflict — Marketing vs Sales, Product vs Engineering — the lazy move is "you two, work it out among yourselves." If your team is already proficient at non-violent communication, fine. They aren't.

> "Many managers do not enjoy dealing with people in conflict, and therefore instead say, 'You two, work it out amongst yourselves.' But unless your team is already proficient at non-violent communication and radical candor, then the two will likely not be able to work it out on their own. If you continue to stay at arms-length, the result will be ... Lord of the Flies. The absence of governance leads to civil war."
> — *articles/lord-of-the-flies.md*

The absence of governance leads to civil war. Two execs without a referee don't resolve the conflict; they go cold, route around each other, recruit allies, and the company splits down the middle. You become responsible for the bloodshed because you didn't step in.

**The protocol — Clean Escalate:**

1. **The two parties try first.** Head of Marketing has a problem with Head of Sales — *go to Head of Sales first*, state the problem and proposed solution. Many resolve here.
2. **If they can't agree, they escalate together.** Both reach out jointly to the Apex Manager (the manager with hire/fire over both — usually the CEO).
3. **Both write their positions before the meeting.** Submitted to the Apex Manager in writing in advance.
4. **At the meeting, all read both documents.** Apex Manager asks questions until she has enough context. Decides.
5. **One mulligan allowed.** "I need more information" — but the Apex Manager has to list exactly what info is needed and who gets it. Second meeting; decision made; no more mulligans.

The hard rule: as Apex Manager, **say no to all dirty escalations.** If the Head of Marketing comes to you alone complaining about the Head of Sales, refuse to take the input until both are present, in writing, with their positions. Otherwise you decide based on one side of the story, the other side feels betrayed, and you've installed a culture of complaint-the-boss instead of resolution.

## Examples

**Situation:** A weekly leadership team meeting that has been running 90 minutes and resolving roughly nothing. Same issues recurring monthly. The CEO is frustrated; the team is frustrated.

**Application:** CEO installs the protocol. The Monday before the meeting, every leader who has an issue writes it up using the template. Loom video, written issue, what-I-did, proposed solution. Async comments by Wednesday. At Thursday's meeting, each issue gets 5 or 20 minutes depending on tactical/strategic. Decision-maker decides; DRI and date; published.

**Result:** First two meetings are messy — leaders aren't used to writing the "what I did" section, proposals are wishy-washy. By week four, the meetings run 45 minutes instead of 90, decide 6 issues instead of 1, and the recurrence rate drops by 80%. The "what I did to create this" section turns out to resolve 30% of issues before they hit the meeting — proposers see the answer themselves while writing.

---

**Situation:** Head of Product comes to the CEO frustrated: Head of Engineering is "ignoring the roadmap and building whatever's fun." She wants the CEO to "fix it."

**Application:** CEO refuses the dirty escalation. "I can't decide based on one side. Go to him with the specific problem and your proposed solution. If you two can't resolve it, come back to me jointly, both with written positions, and we'll do a clean escalation." Head of Product is initially irritated. Goes to Head of Engineering. They argue, then realise neither has actually written down what the roadmap says or what's been built off-roadmap. They draft it together — half the conflict evaporates in the writing. The remaining half goes to the CEO via clean escalation: both written positions, single meeting, decision made in 30 minutes.

**Result:** The two execs resolve future conflicts at their layer 70% of the time once they know dirty escalation isn't available. The CEO's calendar opens up. The org learns the rule.

---

**Situation:** A four-person leadership team has to pick a pricing model for the new product. The CEO has a strong view. He suspects the team will rubber-stamp whatever he says.

**Application:** CEO writes the question at the top of a doc — "Which pricing model for product X?" — and lists three candidate models without naming his preference. Three minutes of silent writing; each leader picks a model in their line. Reveal: 2-1-1 split. CEO uses the heard protocol on each leader, repeats their reasoning back until each says "that's right." *Then* shares his own view (which matches the minority of one). Discusses why. Decides. Names the actions, DRIs, dates.

**Result:** The team is split 2-1-1 in the data and aligned 4-0 on execution because each leader felt heard. Two months later the CEO realises the leader who was a single-vote outlier had the right read on a regional difference he hadn't considered. He notices because that leader spoke up — which she would not have done if the CEO had spoken first.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Accept "I don't know the answer, I'd rather list the options" from a proposer.
*Why:* Lists generate meetings; declarative proposals generate decisions. Even at 10% confidence, write *"Do this..."* The flag in the sand creates a productive discussion. Hedged option-lists create endless ones.

**Don't:** Skip the "what I did to help create this situation" section.
*Why:* It's the line that turns the issue from a complaint about others into something the proposer can act on. Skipping it lets the issue stay externalized. Most issues solve themselves at this step before reaching the meeting.

**Don't:** Comment on the doc as decision-maker before others have weighed in.
*Why:* Loudest voice in the room. Your comment shifts everyone else's stated view, you lose the team's actual information, and you lose their buy-in to the outcome. Wait. Let them write first.

**Don't:** Take dirty escalations.
*Why:* Once you decide based on one side, the other side feels betrayed and you've installed complaint-the-boss as the company's conflict-resolution mechanism. Refuse the conversation. Send them back to talk to each other. If they can't, require both written positions and a joint meeting.

**Don't:** Run the protocol but fail to decide at the end.
*Why:* The whole system rests on the decision-maker actually making a call. Punt twice and the team learns the protocol is theatre — they stop writing issues up, you go back to verbal hallway raising, and you've trained the team that writing is wasted effort. If you genuinely need more info, *write down* what info, who gets it, by when, and when you'll review. That's not a punt; it's a structured continuation.

**Don't:** Force the protocol on a 3-person team.
*Why:* At small scale, real-time conversation is faster than the written protocol and you all have the same context. Install it when the team grows past the size where everyone is in every conversation — usually 5+ — or when verbal coordination starts producing recurring issues.

**Don't:** Use "Lord of the Flies, work it out" as your conflict-resolution policy.
*Why:* In organisations where people have proficient non-violent communication skills, it works. In yours, almost certainly, they don't. The absence of governance leads to civil war. Step in once they've had one shot. Hire a coach if you don't want to facilitate yourself. But don't leave them on their own to draw blood.
