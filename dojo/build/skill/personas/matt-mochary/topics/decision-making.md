# Separate the Decision from the Implementation

## When to Use
- The decision feels stuck, impossible, or sickening — you've been carrying it for days or weeks.
- You're polling friends and advisors and getting different answers each time, and none of them help.
- You're co-mingling "what's the right call?" with "how do I tell them?" and the second is poisoning the first.
- You're convening a meeting where you're the most senior voice and you actually want real input.
- You're about to fire someone, lose a customer, kill a product line, or restructure — and you're frozen.

## Fails When
- **The implementation genuinely is the constraint.** You can identify who you serve primarily and what's in their interest, but acting on it bankrupts the entity that protects everyone else. Rare. Almost always fear talking — pressure-test it before you accept it.
- **You use the framing to dodge the human cost.** The point is to keep the pain of implementation from contaminating the decision — not to skip the implementation work. The humans you affect still get the heard step, the help, the soft landing.
- **It's a Type 1 irreversible decision and you've already decided.** Don't run a fake input process. Say openly: "This is a decision I'm making, not a discussion."

## Core Concept

When a decision feels impossibly hard, the decision usually isn't actually hard. The *implementation* is hard, and the pain of implementation has infected your view of the decision itself. You're imagining the conversation, the email, the layoff, the customer call, the disappointed face — and that imagined pain bleeds backward into "what's the right call?" until you can't see it anymore.

Pull the two apart. The decision answers one question: *who do we serve primarily, and what's in their best interest?* If you run a company, that's your customer. A fund — your CEOs and your LPs. A government — the citizens. A family — the members of your family, including you. Answer it and write the answer down. That's the decision. It's almost always clearer than you thought, because it was never actually unclear — it was just fused with the dread of what comes next.

Now treat the implementation as a separate problem. It will hurt people. You have two routes: just do it without dwelling on the hurt, or — better — reach out to the people affected, find what really matters to them, and help them get it. Either way, the implementation is its own piece of work. You can be a coward about it or kind about it; you cannot let it talk you out of the decision.

> "When we have a difficult decision to make, we often feel lost and confused. As we imagine the possible answers, none 'feel good'. We can solicit feedback and opinions from others (and this can identify many different possible answers), but we still may not 'know' what to do. In this situation, we are often co-mingling the decision and the implementation. The decision itself is often clear, but the implementation likely feels painful."
> — *articles/decision-making-separate-decision-from-implementation.md*

If the logic of this is obvious to you but you still can't do it, hand the decision to a thought partner — co-worker, spouse, friend. Share the doc. Walk them through it. They'll see the answer because they aren't carrying the pain of implementation. Only you are.

## How to Apply

1. **Notice the symptom.** Decision has been on your mind for days. You've polled three people. You feel sick when you think about it. You keep flipping. That's the signal — decision and implementation are fused.
2. **State who you serve primarily.** One sentence. The customer. The team. The shareholders. The patient. Write it at the top of a doc.
3. **Ask: "What is in the best interest of the person we serve primarily?"** Answer it. Write it down. *This is the decision.*
4. **Stop. Re-read what you just wrote.** Notice that the answer is probably clearer than you expected. The fog was the implementation, not the decision.
5. **Now, separately, list the implementation pain.** Who gets hurt? In what way? Be specific.
6. **Choose your implementation route.** Either: (a) just do it, accepting the injury; or (b) reach out to the affected, find what they actually want, help them get it, then do it. Most senior CEOs default to (b) — it costs more time and builds more trust.
7. **If you still can't act, hand the doc to a thought partner.** They'll walk you through. They aren't in fear about the pain — only you are.

## The Loudest Voice in the Room (sub-protocol)

Every decision-making protocol in a startup eventually runs into the same trap: the senior person speaks first, and from that moment the room's "input" is mostly people aligning to the senior view.

> "As CEO, your voice is the 'loudest in the room.' Once people hear your perspective, some percentage will naturally alter their views to more closely match yours. This % is much higher than you might imagine. People assume that as CEO, you have more information than they do, and therefore your perspective is probably more correct. Later, these same people will not feel fully bought-in to the outcome, because internally they will know that their true thought was not heard."
> — *articles/loudest-voice-in-the-room.md* (book Ch. 12)

You lose two things at once when you tip your hand: the team's actual information, and the team's commitment to the outcome. You get a worse decision *and* worse follow-through. The fix is mechanical, not cultural.

**The protocol:**

1. **State the question. Do not state your view.** One sentence. "Should we keep customer X or fire them?"
2. **Have everyone vote in writing first.** Three minutes. Each person writes their proposed answer in their own line in the doc. Or use simultaneous thumbs-up / thumbs-down (everyone reveals at the same time). Junior people speak first if you're going verbal — Amazon convention; Peter Reinhardt's note: it surfaces information *and* gives you a read on who the strong junior thinkers are.
3. **Read every comment back to its author.** Use the heard protocol — repeat their proposal until they say "that's right." This is the buy-in step; skipping it costs you everything you set up by polling.
4. **Now share your view.** You speak last because you are the loudest voice. By now the team's information is on the page and protected from your gravity.
5. **Decide.** Not consensus. You're the decision-maker. You acknowledge every comment by name and then make the call. The buy-in came from people feeling heard, not from getting their way.

The exception is when you've already decided unilaterally. Don't run the fake-input version of this. State openly: "I've decided X. This is a decision, not a discussion. I'll answer questions." Pretending to want input you don't want is far more damaging than just naming it.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is agonising over whether to fire a senior engineer who's been with the company since the early days. The engineer is a friend, a believer, was instrumental in the early product — but has been blocking the team for the last six months and won't course-correct. The founder has been carrying this for two months.

**Application:** Separate the decision from the implementation. *Who do we serve primarily?* The customers, and downstream the rest of the team who is being held back. *What's in their best interest?* The engineer can't be in this seat anymore. Write it down. *That's the decision. It took thirty seconds to make.*

Now the implementation. The pain is real: a friend, a believer, an early teammate. Route (b): reach out, find what they actually want next, help them get it. Maybe they want to be an IC at a different company; you write the recommendation, you work your network, you become their agent. The conversation is hard; the decision was never hard.

**Result:** The founder runs the conversation in a week instead of carrying the dread for another two months. The engineer lands somewhere they're happier in three weeks. The team unblocks. None of this was visible while the decision and the implementation were fused.

---

**Situation:** Five-person leadership team, contentious decision: should we shut down the consumer line and double down on enterprise? The CEO has a strong view but knows the team needs to actually own the outcome.

**Application:** Run the loudest-voice protocol. CEO writes the question at the top of a doc — "Should we shut down consumer and go all-enterprise by Q3?" — does not write his view. Each of the five other leaders writes their proposed answer in their line, three minutes, no peeking. Reveal. CEO reads each one back to its author until they say "that's right." Three say shut it down, one says split, one says protect consumer for two more quarters. CEO now shares his own view: shut it down. Discusses the split-proposer's reasoning specifically, names her concern, addresses it. Decides: shut down. Names the actions, DRIs, dates.

**Result:** The split-proposer doesn't get her way but she got heard. Two weeks later she's not relitigating it; she's executing. If the CEO had spoken first, she'd have aligned publicly and resented privately.

---

**Situation:** A CEO is paralysed about cutting the company's largest customer — accounts for 30% of revenue, but is also 80% of the support burden, 100% of the product distortion, and the reason the team is burning out. The CEO has been spinning for three weeks.

**Application:** Who do we serve primarily? *Our other customers, and the team*. What's in their best interest? *Cut the toxic account.* That's the decision. The CEO writes it down. Then handles the implementation: reach out to the customer, explain honestly, offer six months of transition support, refer them to a competitor that fits them better. Painful conversation. Right call.

**Result:** Revenue dips for one quarter. Support load drops by 60% in two months. Team morale recovers. Product roadmap stops getting distorted. The CEO realises the decision had been clear for at least a month — the spin was all implementation dread.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat "the decision is hard" as the actual problem.
*Why:* Almost every "this decision is impossibly hard" is actually "this implementation will hurt people I care about and I'm in fear about it." Diagnose the fear. Then run the protocol.

**Don't:** Use the framing to skip the human work.
*Why:* "Separate the decision from the implementation" is a clarity tool, not a permission slip. The humans you affect still get the heard step, the agent treatment, the soft landing. You decide cleanly *so that* you can implement humanely — not so you can be a robot.

**Don't:** Run the loudest-voice protocol after you've already decided.
*Why:* Fake input destroys trust faster than honest unilateral decisions do. If you've decided, say so. Save the protocol for decisions where the team's information actually changes the outcome.

**Don't:** Skip the "who do we serve primarily?" step and jump to options.
*Why:* The question selects for the right answer. Without it, you'll generate options that serve everyone (or no one) and stay stuck. The question forces a hierarchy and the hierarchy forces clarity.

**Don't:** Speak first in the meeting and then ask "what does everyone think?"
*Why:* You've just told them what you think and what they need to say to agree with the boss. The information you're about to collect is now corrupted, and you won't even know to which degree. Run the writing-first protocol or get used to bad decisions with no real buy-in.

**Don't:** Confuse "consensus" with "buy-in."
*Why:* Buy-in comes from people feeling heard, not from getting their way. You can decide against someone's recommendation and still have their full commitment — *if* they felt heard first. Consensus is a different (often worse) goal; you'll either water the decision down or wait forever.
