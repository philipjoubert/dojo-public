# The Goal Cascade — Vision → OKRs → Weekly Priorities → Daily Top Goal

## How it relates to its source

The **OKR mechanic** (Objectives and Key Results) was developed at **Intel by Andy Grove** and brought to Google by John Doerr; it is documented in Grove's *High Output Management* and Doerr's *Measure What Matters*. Credit Grove for the format. The **quarterly priorities** ("Rocks") pattern that surrounds OKRs comes from **Gino Wickman's EOS (Entrepreneurial Operating System)**, in *Traction*. The idea of carving two hours a day for a single Top Goal is **Greg McKeown's** from *Essentialism*; Mochary cites him by name.

What Mochary contributes is the *unbroken chain* from a written ten-year vision down to *what you are working on at 9:00 a.m. today.* Most companies have one or two of these layers. Mochary's discipline is that all six layers exist, are linked, and are reviewed on a fixed cadence — and that the founder is personally accountable for the chain holding together.

## When to Use
- The company has crossed about ten people and informal "we all know what we're doing" alignment is breaking down.
- A new quarter is starting and you need a planning rhythm that doesn't take three weeks.
- An offsite is on the calendar and you want to leave with goals that the team will actually use.
- You feel busy but not productive — many things are moving but you can't say which strategic outcome any of them serves.
- You have a great vision but the team's day-to-day work has drifted away from it.

## Fails When
- **No company-level OKRs.** A common, lethal mistake. If you skip company OKRs and start at the department level, you create silo-ed fiefdoms that don't trust each other and don't work together. Always company first, then cascade.
- **No 10-year vision.** OKRs without a vision are a treadmill. They keep the company busy but not pointed.
- **Dictated, not negotiated.** If you hand each person their OKRs from above, they don't own them. They'll meet the letter and miss the spirit. Buy-in comes from each person *writing their own* OKRs that align with their team's, which align with the department's, which align with the company's.
- **Too many.** Three Os with three KRs each, per level. More than that and nothing is the priority.
- **No traffic light, no review.** OKRs without weekly Green / Yellow / Red review become decoration. The whole point is to surface the Reds early enough to fix them.
- **OKR creation drags on.** If you let it sprawl across a month, the team disengages. One Admin Week, end-to-end. That's the budget.

## Core Concept

Most founders' relationship to long-term goals is: I have a vision in my head, I have a quarterly board update, I have a calendar full of meetings, and I have a frantic Sunday-night to-do list. There is no chain between these things. The vision doesn't shape the quarter; the quarter doesn't shape the week; the week doesn't shape the morning. Then they wonder why the company feels busy but stuck.

The cascade fixes that. Six layers, each derived from the one above:

1. **10-Year Vision** — what industry do you dominate, who is the customer, what is the moat?
2. **Annual goals** — the big bets for the year that will move you toward the 10-year picture.
3. **Quarterly OKRs** — three Objectives, three Key Results each, at the company level.
4. **Department / team / individual OKRs** — same shape, cascaded so every person can point at how their work feeds the company OKRs.
5. **Weekly priorities** — the highest-value Action you can take in the next week toward each Objective.
6. **Daily Top Goal** — two hours, scheduled early, on your single most important goal. Every workday. Period.

> "In each company, dozens of new challenges arise each week. Some might be critical, but others will be mere distractions, cluttering up your team's time and energy. Commit to a systematic Goal-Tracking System to maintain focus and to prevent the clutter from overwhelming your operation."
> — *the-great-ceo-within.md*, Ch. 19

The chain is what makes it work. A 10-year vision in isolation is a slogan. Quarterly OKRs in isolation are a treadmill. A daily Top Goal in isolation is a productivity hack. *Linked together*, they create a system where the founder can answer, at any moment: *what am I working on right now, why does it matter, which Objective does it feed, and which sentence in the 10-year vision does that Objective ladder up to?* If any link breaks — vision missing, Objectives unrelated to vision, KRs not actually measurable, no weekly priorities, no Top Goal block — the chain stops carrying load.

> "For your quarterly goals, or OKRs (Objectives and Key Results), the target is 3 and 3. Three Objectives, with three Key Results for each Objective. Create these OKRs for the company, then for each department, then for each team, then for each individual. (Warning: A common mistake is to create Department OKRs, without creating Company-level OKRs. This omission leads to silo-ed fiefdoms that do not work together nor trust each other.)"
> — *articles/okr-creation.md*

The Objective is qualitative — the tagline of a movie. *Massively grow revenues. Delight our customers. Create an environment where we are all inspired to do our best work.* It does not need to be measurable. It needs to be inspiring. The Key Results are the measurable evidence: $500K MRR; ten new SDRs hired; Sales Ops person in seat. A third party should be able to read the KR and adjudicate — yes, that happened, or no, it didn't. No fuzziness.

## How to Apply

### Annual layer

1. **Refresh the 10-year vision once a year.** Imagine you are the dominant company in your industry in ten years. Answer four questions in writing: what industry do you dominate? Who is the customer (a real human, not a corporate entity), and what pain do you solve? What is unique about your solution? What asset (human or physical) is your moat? This is your North Star. Refresh annually. Read it monthly.

2. **Set 3 annual goals that ladder to the vision.** What has to be true twelve months from now to be on track for the ten-year picture? These are the bets for the year.

### Quarterly layer (Admin Week)

3. **Run an Admin Week, end-to-end.** OKR creation is distracting. Compress it into one week and do all the quarterly admin in the same week — last quarter's OKR debrief, performance management, recruiting check-in. This is the canonical Mochary timeline:

   - **Monday:** Leadership Team meets for two hours and creates the company OKRs using structured brainstorming. Each member writes their proposal *separately* before discussion. Then merge.
   - **Tuesday:** Each department meets for two hours and creates department OKRs that ladder up. CEO reviews and approves all department OKRs by EOD.
   - **Wednesday:** Each team within each department creates team OKRs. Department head approves by EOD.
   - **Thursday:** Each sub-team. Team head approves.
   - **Friday:** Each individual writes their own OKRs that ladder up. Sub-team head approves.
   - **Following Monday:** Leadership team reviews and CEO approves the full cascade. Announce at All Hands.

4. **Three Objectives, three Key Results.** Per level. Always. More than this and nothing is the priority. Every Key Result must be objectively measurable so a third party could adjudicate it.

5. **Cover the three universal Os at the company level.** Most companies' company-level OKRs map to three universal Objectives:
   - **Profits:** Massively grow revenues while minimizing expense.
   - **Product:** Delight our customers.
   - **People:** Create a positive and transparent environment where we are all inspired to do our best work.
   Use these as scaffolding; let the KRs make them specific.

6. **You are the Decision-Maker, not the consensus-seeker.** Hear everyone out — but you choose the company OKRs. This is not a vote. The Loudest Voice rule still applies though: have everyone write their proposal before you share yours.

7. **Each person writes their own OKRs.** Do not dictate. The buy-in is in the writing. Their team head reviews and approves; the manager doesn't write the report's OKRs for them.

### Weekly layer

8. **Each person picks the highest-value Action per Objective for the week.** "What is the single most valuable action I can take this week toward Objective 1?" That's the week's priority for that O. Three Os, three weekly priorities. They go into the 1-1 / team meeting agenda or the standup.

9. **Traffic-light the OKRs weekly.** Green (on track), Yellow (slightly off), Red (far off). The DRI of each OKR (see `aors-and-dris.md`) updates the status. Reds require a written Issue / Proposed Solution.

### Daily layer

10. **Block two hours every workday for Top Goal work.** Early. Before the inbox opens. No emails, no Slack, no meetings. Only the most important goal you have. McKeown's research is unambiguous: decision and thought-processing energy is highest in the morning. If two hours is too far a leap, start at 30 minutes for a week, then 60, then 90, then 2.

> "In startups, fires never cease to burn. One of the most common complaints I hear from CEOs is that they seem to have infinite things to do on a day-to-day basis, yet weeks will go by, and they don't feel like they have accomplished anything. This dilemma results from getting bogged down with the small immediate things and losing track of the important long-term ones."
> — *articles/top-goal-ceo.md*

11. **Write Actions as inputs, not outputs.** "Close X customer" is an output — the customer decides. "Send a proposal to X customer" is an input — you control it entirely. Inputs always; outputs never. You're never waiting on someone else to take the next step.

12. **Make the next action two minutes or less.** Then do it immediately. That's the Bias to Action. *"Send a proposal"* is not two minutes; *"Schedule two hours to write the proposal"* is. Now the flywheel of momentum has begun.

## Examples

**Situation:** A 30-person Series A company has set department-level OKRs (Sales has revenue OKRs; Engineering has feature OKRs; Marketing has lead OKRs) but no company-level OKRs. After six weeks, Sales is hitting their numbers, Engineering is shipping the roadmap, Marketing is hitting MQL targets — and yet revenue has flatlined.

**Application:** Each department is optimizing its own metric in isolation. Marketing's MQLs are low-quality (they hit the target by buying ad volume); Engineering's features aren't the ones that drive conversion (they shipped the easiest-to-build roadmap items); Sales is closing what they can without the right product or leads. The CEO calls a two-hour meeting and writes three company OKRs: *Massively grow revenues — KR: $X MRR by quarter end. Delight our customers — KR: NPS above Y. People — KR: zero regrettable attrition.* The next day, each department rewrites their OKRs to ladder up. Marketing's MQL target gets a quality gate; Engineering's roadmap pivots to two features that move conversion; Sales's pipeline is built around the new MQL definition.

**Result:** Within six weeks, MRR breaks through. The departments stop succeeding on independent metrics that don't matter and start coordinating around the one that does.

---

**Situation:** A founder has a 90-page deck of strategy. They cannot articulate, in one sentence, what the company is trying to do this quarter.

**Application:** This is what the OKR cascade is designed to fix. Skip the deck for now. Sit down for an hour and write the 10-year vision in four answers. Then write three Objectives for the quarter, with three KRs each. The 90-page deck is mostly defensible — it's just not actionable until the cascade exists.

**Result:** The founder can now answer, in 30 seconds, what the company is trying to do this quarter and why. The deck still exists; it now serves as backing material rather than substituting for direction.

---

**Situation:** A CEO complains they "have no time" — they work 70-hour weeks and feel they accomplish nothing. Their calendar shows back-to-back meetings, mostly status updates. Their inbox runs the day.

**Application:** Implement the daily Top Goal block. Start tomorrow. 8:00–10:00 a.m. Calendar block titled "Top Goal — DO NOT BOOK." The Top Goal for this week: write the new pricing proposal for the board. No email, no Slack. Phone in another room. After two weeks, evaluate. If kept, expand to other workdays. The CEO will resist; the early failure rate is high; the antidote is starting smaller (30 minutes) and ramping.

**Result:** The CEO ships the pricing proposal in two weeks, having tried and failed for two months. More importantly, they feel a different relationship to time — *"I did the most important thing first; the rest of the day is bonus."* This usually generalizes within a quarter.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Set OKRs without first writing a 10-year vision.
*Why:* OKRs that don't feed a vision become quarterly busywork. The vision is what tells you which Objectives are worth pursuing this quarter and which are merely urgent.

**Don't:** Skip the company-level OKRs and start at the department level.
*Why:* You will create silo-ed fiefdoms that compete instead of coordinating. The company OKRs are the integration point.

**Don't:** Dictate individual OKRs top-down.
*Why:* People execute on what they wrote, not what they were handed. The cascade flows in approval (manager approves report's OKRs) but the *writing* flows bottom-up within the constraint of the layer above. Hand someone their OKRs and you've bought letter-of-the-law compliance, not buy-in.

**Don't:** Set more than three Os.
*Why:* Three is already a lot. Four is a wish list. Seven is decoration. If everything is a priority, nothing is. Cut.

**Don't:** Set Key Results that aren't measurable by a third party.
*Why:* "Improve customer happiness" is not a KR; it's an aspiration. "NPS above 60" is a KR. If you can't write a number or a yes/no condition, it's not done.

**Don't:** Spread OKR creation over three or four weeks.
*Why:* The team disengages and the urgency dies. One Admin Week, beginning to end, including the previous quarter's debrief. That's the budget.

**Don't:** Treat the weekly traffic-light review as optional.
*Why:* OKRs without weekly review become wallpaper. The whole point is to surface the Reds early. A Red OKR with no Issue / Proposed Solution from the DRI is the same as no OKR at all.

**Don't:** Confuse the Top Goal block with "deep work" generally.
*Why:* Deep work means "I won't be interrupted." Top Goal means "I am working on *the single most important thing.*" You can do uninterrupted email triage; that's not Top Goal time. Pick the one thing that, if it advanced, would matter most, and *only* that.

**Don't:** Write next actions as outputs.
*Why:* "Close the deal" depends on the customer. "Send the proposal" depends on you. Outputs leave you waiting; inputs let you generate momentum every day. If the next step takes two minutes, do it now and the flywheel has already started.
