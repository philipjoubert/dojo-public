# Top Goal

## How it relates to its source

The Top Goal framework is **Greg McKeown's** — from his book *Essentialism: The Disciplined Pursuit of Less*. McKeown's one-line summary, when Mochary asked him directly, was: *"Schedule two hours each day to work on your Top Goal only. And do this every single workday. Period."* Credit McKeown for the practice. Mochary's contribution is the operational shape — where it sits in the daily layer of the goal cascade (annual → quarterly OKRs → weekly priorities → daily Top Goal block), the ramp-down protocol for founders who can't start at two hours, and the investor-specific 30-minute variant for people whose primary job is meeting new companies all day.

## When to Use
- The end of the week arrives and you cannot point to a single thing you moved forward that mattered.
- The fires are real, but they are *always* real, and you've used them as the reason to never touch the long-term work for six straight weeks.
- You have a clear quarterly priority — the Top Goal — and zero protected calendar time for it.
- You're a CEO, founder, or investor whose calendar gets eaten by other people's agendas if you don't actively defend it.

## Fails When
- **You don't actually have a Top Goal yet.** If you can't name the one thing that, if achieved this quarter, would matter most — Top Goal time becomes wandering. Fix the goal cascade first.
- **You schedule the block but answer Slack during it.** The block only works because of the no-distractions rule. The instant you let in messages, you've turned it into another reactive hour with a different name.
- **You schedule it for 4pm.** Everything that could press on your day will have pressed by then. The block has to be early or it doesn't survive.
- **You treat it as the only fix.** The block lifts the long-term work above the noise; it does not delete the noise. You still need to delegate, kill meetings, and do the energy audit. Top Goal is necessary, not sufficient.

## Core Concept

In startups, fires never cease to burn. The most common complaint I hear from CEOs is the same complaint, every time: *"I have infinite things to do every day, and weeks go by, and I don't feel like I've accomplished anything."* This is not a time-management problem. It is a layering problem. The small immediate things have buried the important long-term ones, and the small things will keep doing this forever unless you put a block in the calendar that the small things are not allowed to touch.

The Top Goal is the daily layer of a cascade. Annual vision sits at the top. Quarterly OKRs sit underneath the annual vision. Weekly priorities derive from the quarterly OKRs. And the Top Goal — the single most important thing you can move forward today, in service of the quarterly priority — sits at the daily layer. Each layer hands work down to the next; the Top Goal block is where that handed-down work actually gets done. Without the daily block, the cascade exists on paper and never reaches your hands.

The mechanic is brutally simple. Schedule two hours each workday to work on your Top Goal only. Earlier in the day, the better — both because the small fires haven't yet pressed for your attention, and because there is strong, research-backed evidence that you have more decision and thought-processing energy early when your brain is fresh and well-rested. Take advantage of the time when your brain performs at its best by doing the essential things first. During the block: no email, no Slack, no texts, no calls, no meetings. Only the Top Goal.

> "In startups, fires never cease to burn. One of the most common complaints I hear from CEOs is that they seem to have infinite things to do on a day-to-day basis, yet weeks will go by, and they don't feel like they have accomplished anything." (*Top Goal*, Mochary curriculum)

> "Greg McKeown, who wrote a phenomenal book on productivity called Essentialism, boils this down to one key concept: Schedule two hours each day to work on your Top Goal only. And do this every single workday. Period." (*Top Goal*, Mochary curriculum)

## How to Apply

1. **Name the Top Goal explicitly.** Look at your quarterly priority. Ask: what is the single highest-leverage thing I could push forward this week, this day, that would move that priority? Write it down in one sentence. If you can't write it in one sentence, you don't yet know what it is.

2. **Block two hours on tomorrow's calendar — early.** Title the calendar event "Top Goal: \<the one sentence\>". Put it in your first or second hour of the workday. The earlier the slot, the more likely it survives.

3. **Pre-commit to the no-distraction rule.** Phone in another room. Slack and email closed. Calendar declined for that block. If someone *needs* you in those two hours, the answer is they need you at 11am, not 9am — almost always true, almost always survivable.

4. **Show up and only work on the Top Goal.** No "quick check on the inbox first." No "let me just answer this one Slack." The block is a load-bearing wall; one small breach takes the whole wall down because next week you'll breach it twice, and the week after, you won't bother scheduling it.

5. **At the end of the block, capture what you did and what's next.** Write the next concrete step for tomorrow's block. The setup for tomorrow happens at the end of today, while the work is fresh — not at 9am tomorrow when you're trying to start.

6. **Repeat every workday for a quarter.** This is not a thing you try for a week. The compounding only shows up around weeks 4–6. The first week feels like nothing has changed. By week six, you will look at what you've built and you'll wonder why you ever lived any other way.

7. **Re-derive the Top Goal weekly.** Sunday evening or Monday morning, look at your weekly priorities (which derived from the quarterly OKRs). Pick the Top Goal for the week. Each day that week, the daily Top Goal block points at the weekly Top Goal. The cascade is what makes the block point in the right direction.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series B CEO is convinced she has no time for strategic work. Her calendar is full Monday through Friday, 8am to 8pm, with one-on-ones, exec syncs, customer calls, recruiting interviews, and reactive fires. She has a quarterly OKR around launching a new product line and zero progress on it.

**Application:** We block 8am–10am every workday on her calendar, titled "Top Goal: write the product launch narrative." We move two existing 8am one-on-ones to later slots — they survive the move; nobody objects. Her phone goes in a drawer at 8am. Slack and email stay closed until 10am. The first three days she physically twitches at the silence; she keeps reaching for the phone. By day five, she's drafting. By the end of week two, she has a complete narrative document she'd been telling herself for two months she didn't have time to write.

**Result:** The quarterly OKR ships on time. The other meetings, the ones she "couldn't possibly move," turned out to be entirely movable. The thing that wasn't movable was the standing belief that the day was already full.

---

**Situation:** A solo VC partner says he can't do Top Goal because his job is meeting new companies and that takes most of the day.

**Application:** Investors get the 30-minute variant, not two hours. McKeown's recommendation is two hours per day; for an investor, whose primary job is meeting with new companies for many hours, thirty minutes is more realistic. Block 30 minutes first thing each morning. Title it: "Top Goal: \<one sentence\>." For a VC, the Top Goal is usually the deep memo, the portfolio thesis, the strategic write-up — the work that gets crushed by the meeting calendar otherwise. Same no-distraction rule, smaller block.

**Result:** Thirty minutes a day for a quarter is roughly 30 hours of deep work that otherwise wouldn't have happened. That's a thesis, a memo, a strategic plan — the things investors think they have no time to write because the day is meetings.

---

**Situation:** A founder tries Top Goal, lasts four days, and quits because two hours feels impossible.

**Application:** Two hours was too great a leap. Start over with 30 minutes for tomorrow morning. If that goes well, schedule 30 minutes every weekday morning for a week. If that goes well, increase to one hour the following week. Keep ramping until you find the right level — knowing the target is two hours, but the floor is wherever you'll actually show up. A 30-minute block you keep for six months is worth infinitely more than a two-hour block you abandon in week one.

**Result:** The ramp-down works because the discipline being installed is *showing up at the same time every workday with no distractions*. Once that habit is real, the duration grows almost on its own.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Schedule Top Goal at 4pm "after the day calms down."
**Why:** The day does not calm down. The day produces new fires until you stop responding to them. By 4pm your brain is depleted from decision-load, and the fires that ate the day will have eaten your willingness to defend the block. Early or it dies.

**Don't:** Use the block to "catch up on email."
**Why:** Email is the noise the block was built to escape. Catching up on email during Top Goal time is the most precise way to defeat the framework while telling yourself you used it. If email is the issue, the answer is Inbox Zero discipline — separately, in a different block.

**Don't:** Make the Top Goal vague — "work on strategy," "think about the company."
**Why:** Vague goals do not survive contact with a blank document. You'll spend the block re-deciding what you're working on, which means you spent the block on the meta-question instead of the work. The Top Goal is one specific deliverable: "draft the launch narrative," "redesign the org chart for the engineering team," "write the next investor update." Concrete or it doesn't activate.

**Don't:** Skip the block when the day is "an emergency."
**Why:** Every day in a startup is an emergency. The whole point of the block is that it survives emergencies, because the long-term work is what *prevents* the next quarter's emergencies. Skipping it once teaches you the block is optional. Two skips and it's gone.

**Don't:** Confuse Top Goal with your task list.
**Why:** Your task list has thirty things. Top Goal has one. The task list is reactive surface; Top Goal is strategic depth. If you treat the block as "the time when I burn down the task list faster," you've just made yourself more efficient at the small immediate things — which was the problem, not the solution.

**Don't:** Announce Top Goal to your team without first protecting it on your own calendar.
**Why:** If you tell the team "I'm doing Top Goal 9–11" and then move it to Tuesday because of an investor call, the team learns the block is decorative. Lock it in for yourself for two weeks first. *Then* the team will see it's real and stop scheduling against it.

**Don't:** Treat Top Goal as a substitute for fixing what's draining your calendar.
**Why:** Top Goal lifts the strategic work above the noise. It does not reduce the noise. If the rest of your day is being consumed by work that should be delegated, killed, or restructured, you also need to do an energy audit (see `energy-audit.md`) — and probably reduce the number of one-on-ones forcing issues into the group instead. Top Goal is one piece of the calendar protocol, not the whole thing.
