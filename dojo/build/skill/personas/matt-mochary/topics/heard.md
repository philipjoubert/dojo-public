# Heard — How to Make People Feel It

## How it relates to its source

The technique — repeat back what someone said until they say *"that's right"* — has three precedents Mochary cites directly: Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* ("seek first to understand"), Marshall Rosenberg's *Nonviolent Communication*, and Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* (the *"that's right"* moment is straight from Voss's FBI hostage-negotiation work).

Mochary's framing is relational rather than tactical: this is the gating step before *any* difficult conversation, feedback, or conflict resolution — not a negotiation trick but the basic operating discipline of any relationship that lasts more than a transaction. His distinctive contribution is integrating the move into the daily operating system of a CEO — one-on-ones, conflict resolution, layoffs, board feedback, and the Love Meeting with a spouse.

## When to Use
- Before any difficult conversation — feedback, layoff, repricing, reorganization, declining a request, telling someone they're being layered.
- Before any attempt to resolve interpersonal conflict between two people who distrust or dislike each other.
- When you can see someone repeating themselves — they're repeating because they don't believe you heard them.
- When you've explained something three times and the other person "isn't getting it" — flip it. You're failing to listen. Use heard to discover what's actually keeping them from agreeing.
- As a daily practice with your spouse or partner (the Love Meeting — see sub-section).
- Receiving feedback (the Acknowledge step in the 5 A's).

## Fails When
- **The user is performing the technique without genuine interest.** People feel the difference between *"I'm doing the heard thing"* and *I am genuinely curious about what you're experiencing.* The first is theater; people sense it within a sentence and shut down further. Heard works because it proves real care. It only works as long as the care is real.
- **The situation requires immediate action.** If something is on fire — literal or figurative — handle the fire first, then make people feel heard about the experience of the fire. Don't run the heard protocol in the middle of a crisis.
- **Heard becomes a substitute for the hard message.** *"I just want them to feel heard"* can be a euphemism for *I don't want to actually deliver the difficult news.* Heard is the gate to the hard conversation, not a replacement for it. After they feel heard, you still deliver the message.
- **The user gets triggered mid-listen.** The whole technique collapses if you become defensive, angry, or sad while listening. Prepare for the trigger before you start. (See step 1.)

## Core Concept

When people have a feeling or thought, they want to feel heard. If they don't, they will feel resentment. Almost every interpersonal conflict — co-founder, exec, customer, spouse — comes from one or both sides not feeling heard. The fix is laughably simple: repeat back what they said until they say *"that's right."*

You don't have to agree with them. You just have to prove you understood them.

> "When people distrust or dislike each other, usually, it is because they don't feel heard. For me to respect you, I don't need for you to agree with me. But I do need for you to listen to what I have to say... There is a simple fix. You only need to prove to me that you have 'heard' me. And to do that, you only need to repeat back what I've said in summary form by saying 'I think I heard you say...' until I say, 'That's right!' Then I will feel heard. I will now be open to hearing what you have to say." (`articles/conflict-resolution-aka-relationship-building.md`)

This is the single most powerful tool Mochary has found for creating trust and connection. The strange part: it's more impactful than acting on the feedback or solving the problem. People don't need you to fix it. They need you to genuinely understand it. Once they have that, many problems dissolve on their own. The ones that don't, they're now open to your solution — because you've proven they're not talking to a wall.

The application is precise. The exact words matter. *"I think I heard you say..."* (not *"What you're saying is..."* — that's correcting). *"Is that right?"* (not *"Did I get it?"* — too vague). *"Is there more?"* — the most underused phrase in the protocol; there's almost always more, and going deeper is what lands the *"that's right"*.

## How to Apply

1. **Prepare yourself for the trigger.** Before any conversation where you'll need to make someone feel heard, especially when they're upset with you, do this: tell yourself that fear, anger, or sadness may come, and remind yourself that those emotions will give you bad advice (see `fear-and-anger.md`). Imagine a glass case around the person — what they say does not leave the glass and does not hit you. This is the critical step. If you get triggered while listening, you will fail to make them feel heard, and you'll likely do the opposite — interrupt, defend, escalate.

2. **Ask them to share — and then actually be present.** *"What's on your mind? I want to hear it."* Put away your phone, your laptop, your notes. Look at them. Make it your entire intention to listen to understand, not to plan your reply.

3. **Reflect back what they said.** When they pause, say: *"I think I heard you say..."* and summarize. Then: *"Is that right?"*

   - If they say *"yes"* — you can stop, or go deeper.
   - If they say *"not quite"* — ask them to repeat, refine your summary, ask again.

4. **Go deeper with *"Is there more?"*** Once they've confirmed your summary, ask: *"Is there more?"* There almost always is. People front-load the polite version of their concern. The real one comes second or third. Keep asking until they say *"No, that's it."* When the person is allowed to go all the way down to the core issue, they often have an *aha* moment that resolves the issue on its own — without you having to do anything else.

5. **For advanced practice: name the emotion underneath.** Don't just reflect the words — reflect the feeling. Get into their head. Imagine the version of their thought that they didn't say out loud — the one with swear words, the one that's harder than what they actually said. *"I imagine you might be thinking something like: 'goddammit, why do I always have to chase this? Why can't he just respect my time?'"* Amp it up rather than tone it down. They will likely say *"well, it's not that extreme"* — but they will feel deeply heard, because you went to where they were actually living.

   This works because they were softening their thought when they said it (they feared you'd react badly). When you reflect back the un-softened version and don't react badly, they get two things: they feel heard *and* they see it's safe to bring you the real version next time.

6. **For someone sharing pain (not feedback): don't repeat back, imagine their feeling.** When the conversation isn't about you — they're going through something hard in their life — the move is different. Don't summarize their words. Say: *"I imagine you are feeling..."* and try to honestly imagine what they're feeling and thinking. Then: *"Is that right?"* If yes, *"Is there more?"* or better: *"There's more. Tell me more."* Because there is almost always more.

7. **Confirm at the end.** *"Do you feel heard?"* If yes, you're done. If they hesitate or say no, ask what's missing and repeat. Don't move to your message until the answer is a clean *yes*.

8. **Then deliver your message.** Heard is the gate. Once they feel heard, they are open to you in a way they were not before. *Now* is when feedback lands, when the difficult news can be received without rupture, when the strategic decision can be explained without triggering a quit. Don't waste the opening — you earned it.

9. **For the reverse case — when you don't feel heard:** ask directly. *"To make sure I'm communicating well, can you please repeat back what you heard me say?"*

### Sub-section: The Love Meeting (with a spouse or partner)

People who feel emotions strongly need a regular release. One of the most effective releases is to speak aloud for a few minutes about whatever is causing fear, anger, or sadness — and have someone fully present, listening, not interrupting.

Most people benefit from this release after a long day of life — work, kids, the news. If you have a spouse or partner, one of the greatest gifts you can give them is to be the listener for that release.

> "I call such a listening meeting a Love Meeting. My wife and I do it regularly. And it magically creates a deep connection between us." (`articles/love-meeting.md`)

The protocol:

1. **Offer to listen.** *"Do you want to talk for a bit? I'll just listen."* Wait for them to accept.

2. **Be fully present.** Phone away. Laptop closed. Look at them. Breathe. The presence is what makes it work — partial listening doesn't.

3. **Do not interject.** No suggestions. No advice. If you give advice, you kill the stream of consciousness and end the release. The talker doesn't want a solution; they want to be heard.

4. **Reflect emotion physically.** When they express frustration, feel it and let your face show it. Same for sadness, anger, fear. They see you receiving it and feel even more heard.

5. **Trust the time will be finite.** *What if they keep going for hours?* They won't. Most releases run ten to fifteen minutes the first time, shorter as it becomes regular.

When this is a regular practice it produces a deep sense of connection that almost nothing else does. The act of being present and listening *is* the love. (For a vivid demonstration of what *not* to do, watch the two-minute video *"Nail in the Head"*.)

## Examples

**Situation:** Founder needs to tell her VP of Sales that she's hiring a CRO and the VP will be layered. The VP is loyal, has been there four years, and will likely be devastated. Founder wants him to stay in his current role under the new CRO.

**Application:** This is a difficult conversation, layered with the heard protocol. (See also `fear-and-anger.md` step about doing-the-opposite of what fear recommends, and the Difficult Conversations protocol.) Schedule a synchronous meeting (in person or video — you need to see his face). Open: *"This is going to be a difficult conversation. You may feel some combination of anger, fear, or sadness."* Then deliver the punchline first, no lead-up: *"I'm going to hire a CRO and ask you to report to them."* Brief why (two or three bullets). Then: *"I imagine you're likely feeling anger, fear, or sadness. I'd like to know your feelings and thoughts. Are you open to sharing them with me?"*

Now apply heard fully. He might say *"I feel like you don't trust me to scale the team."* You: *"I think I heard you say you feel I don't trust you to scale this — that bringing in someone above you is me losing confidence in your ability. Is that right?"* He confirms or refines. *"Is there more?"* He says: *"Yeah — and frankly I feel kind of insulted that you didn't ask me first."* You reflect that back. *"Is there more?"* Probably. Keep going until *"No, that's it."*

**Result:** He doesn't quit on the spot. He may quit later anyway, but he won't quit *because of how the conversation was handled.* The relationship survives because he was heard. If you'd skipped the heard step and gone straight to logical justification of the org change, he'd have heard it as dismissal and the quit-on-the-spot probability goes up dramatically.

---

**Situation:** A customer left a furious one-star review online claiming the product is broken and the support team is incompetent. Founder wants to respond. The brain is producing several drafts that are sharp and defensive.

**Application:** Don't respond from anger (see `fear-and-anger.md`). Wait. Then write the response with heard as the spine: *"I think I heard you say [specific issue]. I imagine you're feeling [specific frustration — try to amp it slightly: 'completely sick of being passed around, like nobody is actually listening to what's broken']. Is that right? Tell me more — what else are we missing?"* Then, only after they confirm, address the substantive issue.

**Result:** The customer often responds with substantially more useful information than the review contained — because they now feel safe enough to share the actual story. Many of these reviews convert to five-stars when the customer feels heard. The ones that don't, you've still learned more about the actual problem than you would have from a defensive reply.

---

**Situation:** Co-founder conflict, six months in, both partners blaming each other and increasingly frosty in meetings. Founder wants to "fix it" by having dinner and "talking it out."

**Application:** Skip the casual dinner. Run the structured conflict resolution protocol. Each writes thoughts in five categories — Anger, Fear, Sadness, Joy, Excitement — for the other. The lower-power person reads Joy and Excitement first (recipient says *"thank you"*). Then the recipient picks the heaviest Anger or Fear item and asks the other to read it aloud. *Now apply heard fully.* Repeat back. *"Is that right?"* *"Is there more?"* All the way down. Then switch. Only after both feel fully heard do they negotiate impeccable agreements (precisely defined, written, initialed).

**Result:** The conflict resolves not because either won the argument but because both feel deeply heard for the first time in months. Often there's an *aha* moment — *"oh, that's what's actually been bothering you?"* — and the surface conflict was never about what they thought.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Go in defensive.
*Why:* If you get triggered while listening, you will interrupt, you will explain, you will defend — and the other person will feel unheard *more*, not less. Use the glass-case visualization before you start. If you can't stay un-triggered, stop the conversation, shift first (see `fear-and-anger.md`), and come back.

**Don't:** Tone down what they said.
*Why:* People soften their thought when speaking to you because they fear your reaction. Reflect the softened version and they feel managed, not heard. Amp it up slightly. They'll often say *"well, it's not quite that extreme"* — but they'll feel heard, and they'll see it's safe to bring the un-softened version next time.

**Don't:** Give advice or solutions before they say *"that's right."*
*Why:* Advice before they feel heard registers as dismissal — *you don't actually understand my problem; you're just trying to make it go away.* Even if your advice is correct, it won't be received. Solutions are downstream of *"that's right."*

**Don't:** Skip *"Is there more?"*
*Why:* The first thing people say is the polite version. The second thing is the real version. The third thing is sometimes the actual core of the issue. Stopping after the first round leaves most of the value on the table — and often leaves the actual issue un-surfaced, which means it'll come back.

**Don't:** Use heard to manipulate.
*Why:* The technique works because it proves genuine care. The moment you use it on someone you do not actually intend to treat well, you become the kind of person who burns trust at scale. Mochary is explicit about this every time he teaches the tool: it is manipulative. Use it for good only. The discipline is to deserve the trust the technique generates.

**Don't:** Treat heard as the whole conversation.
*Why:* Heard is the gate, not the destination. After they feel heard, you still need to deliver the actual message — the feedback, the decision, the news. People who run heard endlessly without ever getting to the substantive content frustrate everyone, including the person being heard. The structure is: shift, hear, deliver.

**Don't:** Run heard remotely if you can avoid it.
*Why:* The protocol works best in person, second-best on video. Audio-only loses the facial reflection. Text loses everything. Layoffs, terminations, hard feedback — synchronous and visual or it doesn't count.

---

If the full protocol feels like too much, start with one move: the next time someone is repeating themselves to you, stop them and say, *"I want to make sure I heard you. I think you said..."* and summarize. Watch what happens. The repetition stops. That single loop, run a hundred times across a year, rebuilds trust in relationships you didn't realize you were losing.
