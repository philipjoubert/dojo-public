# The Anti-Sell

## When to Use
- The first interview, after the first 5–10 minutes when you have an intuitive hit that you like the candidate. Run the anti-sell *next*, before any further sell.
- Any role where the day-to-day reality is meaningfully harder, faster, or weirder than what the candidate is reading on the careers page.
- Senior hires whose acceptance cost is a relocation, a pay cut, or a spouse making peace with a startup salary.
- Pre-offer validation: when you are about to make a verbal offer, run a structured Top-Grading reference call instead of a backchannel ping.

## Fails When
- **You skip the "do I like them?" gate and run the anti-sell on every candidate.** Anti-sell is for candidates you already want. Used on people you don't, it just wastes everyone's time and broadcasts a culture of negativity.
- **You confuse the anti-sell with bragging about how brutal you are.** "We work 80 hours" said with chest-pounding signals dysfunction, not honesty. The anti-sell is calm, factual, specific.
- **You substitute backchannel gossip for direct references.** A friend-of-a-friend who saw the candidate from across the office is not a manager. Backchannel signal is mostly noise; do the structured reference call.

## Core Concept

When you hire, the temptation is to sell. You highlight the mission, the trajectory, the team, the comp, the office. The candidate hears a version of the company that doesn't actually exist — an idealized one. They take the job. Six months in, they discover the high-pressure days, the long hours, the founder who runs product directly, the customer call at 11pm, the pivot that just blew up their roadmap. They are dissatisfied. You are dissatisfied. Both of you spend three to six months unwinding a hire that should never have happened.

The anti-sell inverts this. In the first interview, after you've gotten the intuitive hit that you like the person, you stop selling and start describing — calmly, specifically — the worst parts of the job and the company. The hours that are real. The pressure that is real. The aspects of the culture a polite candidate would consider off-putting. You let the candidate sit with that picture. About 98% of the people you'd otherwise have spent weeks interviewing will quietly self-select out. The remaining 2% will lean in. Those are the candidates who arrive committed because they chose the reality, not the brochure.

> "Present the reality. During the interview, openly describe the most challenging and potentially off-putting aspects of the job and company culture. This approach will deter the majority (98%) of candidates who aren't a fit for the actual work environment. The few candidates who remain (about 2%) are those genuinely excited about and prepared for the reality of the job."

The anti-sell sits inside a larger hiring discipline: be efficient with people you won't hire, be lavish with people you will. Every minute you spend with a candidate you're not going to hire is a minute you're not spending with a candidate you should be onboarding, training, and falling in love with. Speed signals love. Slowness signals hesitation. The candidates you actually want are reading the speed of your process as a vote of conviction — and the anti-sell, deployed early, gets you to clarity faster than anyone else can.

The complement to the anti-sell is the structured reference call. Backchanneling — asking a friend-of-a-friend who happens to have worked at the same company — is the lazy default. It almost never reaches someone who actually managed or worked next to the candidate; it routes the same five people in your network around the same shallow gossip. The disciplined alternative is the Top-Grading interview from the book *Who* by Geoff Smart and Randy Street: walk the candidate through their job history from school onwards, collect the names and spellings of every direct manager, and after a verbal offer ("contingent on reference interviews") you call those managers — not the references the candidate hand-picked. Three to seven of them. The candidate does the work to make those managers available. If they can't, that's the signal.

## How to Apply

1. **Open the first interview by asking about the candidate.** Five to ten minutes. Their story, what they're working on now, what's driving them. You're listening for the intuitive hit — do you actually want this person on the team? If no, end the interview kindly and quickly. If yes, switch modes.

2. **Run the anti-sell before any further selling.** Calmly, factually, describe the hardest realities of the role and the company. Not as a stress test. As information. Examples: "We work in person five days a week. The CEO runs product and you'll be in friction with him directly. We have shipped three pivots in the last eighteen months and there will likely be a fourth. Our customers email us on Sundays and we answer." Whatever is actually true and would surprise the candidate.

3. **Watch what happens.** A candidate who is wrong for the role visibly deflates or starts negotiating around the realities ("Would there be flexibility on the in-person?"). A candidate who is right for the role leans in, asks sharper questions about the realities, and gets *more* energized, not less. The "hell yes" after the anti-sell is the only "yes" worth pursuing.

4. **Move fast on the candidates who pass the filter.** A full day of onsite interviews — the next available Friday, ideally — and end-of-day decision. Speed signals conviction. The longer your process, the more you tell them, *we don't have conviction about you*.

5. **At verbal-offer stage, conduct the Top-Grading interview.** A second 20–30 minute conversation walking the candidate's job history from school onwards. For each job, get the name and spelling of their direct manager. Collect at least seven names. If they haven't had seven managers, get peers from one or more jobs.

6. **Make the verbal offer contingent on reference interviews.** Phrase: *"I really like you. I'd like to hire you. I'm going to make you a verbal offer now that is contingent on successful reference interviews. If I made you the following offer, would you accept?"* Then ask the candidate to connect you to three to seven of the managers *you* picked from their list — not the ones they would have hand-picked.

7. **The candidate gets the references on the phone, not you.** If they can't get three former managers to take a ten-minute call, that is itself the reference. A red flag the size of the building.

8. **Anti-sell the spouse too, when there is one.** The biggest blocker to a yes from a senior candidate is the spouse's fear of saying no. Ask the candidate to introduce you. Repeat back what the spouse is afraid of until they say "that's right." You do not have to solve the fears — you just have to prove you heard them.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is hiring a Head of Sales for an early-stage B2B startup. The role requires the new hire to take customer calls personally for the first six months because there is no junior team to delegate to yet, and the CEO will sit in on every call. The founder has been pitching the role as "build the sales function from scratch" — true, but the *building* part is preceded by months of grinding individual contributor work. Every senior candidate they've hired has quit inside ninety days because they expected to manage from day one.

**Application:** Next interview. After the opening five minutes, the founder shifts. *"I want to be specific about what this role actually looks like. For the first six months, you are not managing anyone. You are personally taking discovery calls, demos, and closes. I will be on those calls — not as a coach, but because I run product and need the customer signal directly. The first hire under you doesn't happen until we hit a revenue threshold I can't yet predict. The build comes later. The first six months is grind."*

**Result:** The candidate the founder was leaning toward visibly cools. *"Six months IC? I thought we'd be building the team in the first quarter."* The interview ends warmly. Two weeks later a different candidate — older, has done the IC-then-build motion before — runs through the same anti-sell and says *"that's exactly the role I want."* Hire closes in two weeks. Sticks.

---

**Situation:** A CEO is about to make an offer to a senior product hire. A board member has offered to backchannel — *"I know someone who worked at her last company, I'll text him."* The CEO is tempted to short-circuit references this way.

**Application:** The CEO declines the backchannel and runs the Top-Grading instead. Twenty minutes walking the candidate's job history. Seven manager names collected. Verbal offer made: *"I'd like to hire you, contingent on reference interviews. If I made you the following offer, would you accept?"* The candidate says yes. The CEO picks four of the seven managers — including one from a job the candidate didn't volunteer to talk about — and asks the candidate to connect them. Calls happen the following week. Two of the four managers are unguardedly enthusiastic, one is mixed and surfaces a real pattern of conflict-avoidance, one calls the candidate "best hire I ever made."

**Result:** The mixed reference is the signal that mattered. The CEO designs the onboarding and the first ninety-day plan around the conflict-avoidance pattern, names it openly with the candidate at offer ceremony, and gets ahead of what would otherwise have been a six-month problem. The board member's backchannel — "I heard she was great" — would not have surfaced any of this. Hire goes well.

**Situation:** A candidate has accepted a verbal offer at half their previous comp, with the equity making up the difference. The candidate's spouse is anxious. The CEO has felt this kind of anxiety derail two prior offers.

**Application:** *"I know you're excited about this company, but I imagine your spouse is feeling fear and has many concerns. I'd like for him to have those concerns addressed directly and feel that he has a direct line of communication with me. Would you be willing to introduce me?"* Call happens two days later. The CEO listens, repeats back the spouse's concerns — the kids' school, the mortgage, the 50% comp drop — until the spouse says "that's right." Doesn't try to solve any of them. Just proves he heard.

**Result:** Spouse, end of call: *"In all of our years together, no boss of my spouse has ever reached out to me like this."* Candidate signs the offer the next day. The spouse becomes an advocate, not a brake. (See `heard.md` for why this works.)

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Anti-sell every candidate you meet to filter the funnel.
**Why:** The anti-sell is for candidates you already want. Used as a generic filter, it's just a long-winded way to be unkind. Spend the first five to ten minutes evaluating whether you want them. *Then* run the anti-sell, and only on the ones you do.

**Don't:** Frame the anti-sell as a stress test or a toughness exam.
**Why:** "Can you handle this?" said with bravado is hazing. The anti-sell is calm, factual, and self-deprecating about the company's actual messiness, not a flex about how hard the team works. Done with bravado, you select for self-flagellators, not high performers who choose hard work because it pays off for them.

**Don't:** Skip the anti-sell because you're afraid of losing the candidate.
**Why:** A candidate you lose to the anti-sell is a candidate who would have washed out at month four. You haven't saved them or yourself anything by hiding the realities — you've just moved the cost forward and added a severance package to it. Lose them at the offer stage, not after onboarding.

**Don't:** Run a backchannel reference instead of a structured one.
**Why:** Backchannels reach indirect contacts who almost never worked with the candidate as manager or peer. The signal is gossip, not data. A structured Top-Grading reference call with three to seven direct managers selected by *you* (not cherry-picked by the candidate) is the only reference process that predicts performance.

**Don't:** Drag the process out because "we want to be sure."
**Why:** A two-month interview process tells the candidate you have no conviction about them. The best candidates have other offers. Speed — first call, full-day onsite the next available Friday, verbal offer that evening, references the following week — closes the candidates everyone else is also chasing. *Each day of delay sends the message, "We don't have conviction about you."*

**Don't:** Treat references as a formality after you've decided.
**Why:** References are where the predictive information lives. Interview performance is not predictive of job performance — there are plenty of people who are good at interviewing and bad at the job. The only reliable predictor is how a person has performed before. The Top-Grading interview and the calls that follow it are where you do the actual evaluation. The interviews themselves are mostly about whether you like the person enough to want references.

**Don't:** Hire someone whose former managers won't take your call.
**Why:** If a candidate cannot get three former managers to spend ten minutes on a call about them, that is the reference. The signal is total, and it is bad. Pass.

**Don't:** Ignore the spouse.
**Why:** A candidate's no almost always comes through the spouse first. The candidate is in love with the company; the spouse is sitting with the math. Reach the spouse early, run Heard on whatever they're afraid of, and the candidate's yes becomes a household yes — which is the only yes that holds.
