# Getting to "That's Right"

## When to Use
- The negotiation is stuck. Both sides are defending positions because neither side feels understood.
- You're about to make a proposal and you want it to land on an ear that trusts you instead of an ear that's waiting to bat it away.
- You suspect the other side has a story about their situation that you haven't fully heard yet, and until you hear it, nothing you offer will fit.
- You want to test whether connection is real. "That's right" is the verification signal. Without it, move on at your own risk.

## Fails When
- The other side says "you're right" and you take the win. You just lost. They want you to stop talking. They haven't agreed to anything.
- Your summary is mechanical. They feel the technique instead of the understanding. You'll get a lukewarm "yeah, I guess" at best.
- You ask for it. "Does that sound right?" and "Am I understanding you?" don't earn "that's right." A genuine, accurate summary earns it.
- You get it and immediately pivot to your proposal. The moment of connection needs a beat of silence. Honor it, or you burn it.

## Core Concept
"That's right." Two words. The sweetest two words in any negotiation. When your counterpart says "That's right," you've achieved something profound. They're not just acknowledging what you said. They're affirming that you actually understand their position, their feelings, their constraints. And in that moment of feeling deeply understood, something shifts. Barriers fall. Resistance melts. The conversation starts moving forward.

"That's right" is a stealth victory. They don't feel like they've given anything up. They haven't conceded a point. They haven't agreed to your terms. They've just confirmed that you get them. But that confirmation is everything, because from that point forward your proposals are heard through the ear of someone who believes you understand their world.

Now here's the critical distinction, and I had to learn this painfully. "That's right" is not "you're right." They sound similar. They are opposites. "That's right" is a breakthrough. "You're right" is a disaster. Think about when you say "you're right" in your own life. It's when someone is lecturing you and you want them to stop. "You're right, I should exercise more." "You're right, I should call my mother." You're not agreeing to change. You're agreeing to end the conversation. I was teaching a class years ago and a guy kept pushing back on everything I said. I'd make my case and he'd say "you're right." I thought I was winning. A colleague pulled me aside after: "Chris, every time he said 'you're right,' he was telling you to shut up. He didn't agree with anything." She was right. When you hear "you're right," you've lost the connection. Back up. Listen more. Label more. Build toward a real "that's right."

## How to Apply
1. Stop advocating for your position. Start understanding theirs. Really understand it. Not as a technique, as a genuine goal.
2. Use effective pauses. Silence after they say something important signals that what they said matters. Let it breathe. Five seconds. Ten seconds. Don't rush to the next thing.
3. Layer in minimal encouragers as they talk. "Yes." "OK." "Uh-huh." "I see." These keep them going without taking the floor.
4. Mirror the last one to three words of what they've said when you want them to elaborate. Label what you sense underneath: "It seems like you're frustrated." "It sounds like this has been a long road." "It looks like this matters a lot to you." Accurate labels build credibility as someone who gets their situation.
5. Paraphrase what they've said back to them, in your own words. Not their words. Your own. "So what you're saying is you need the delivery by Friday, but you're worried about quality at that pace."
6. Finally, summarize. Summary = paraphrase + label. All the facts of their situation plus the emotion underneath. "So you've been on this project six months, you've hit several unexpected obstacles, your team is stretched thin, and the deadline hasn't moved. It seems like you're feeling set up to fail." When the summary is accurate, the only honest response is "that's right." Don't ask for it. Earn it with the summary.
7. When you get it, pause. Let it land. Then, and only then, move to your proposal.

## Examples

**Situation:** The Jeffrey Schilling case. American hostage held by Abu Sayyaf in the Philippines. Months stuck. The kidnapper, Abu Sabaya, keeps demanding ten million dollars in "war damages." Every counter-proposal bounces. Every reasonable appeal fails.
**Application:** Voss coaches his team to stop arguing and build a summary. Not of the US position. Of Abu Sabaya's. His grievances against the government. His sense of injustice. His belief in his cause. His constraints inside his own organization. All of it, voiced back to him. Not agreement. Understanding.
**Result:** Abu Sabaya pauses. Then: "That's right." That was the moment. The negotiation unlocked. Progress started happening. Not because the US team gave him anything new, but because he finally believed they understood his world. When someone believes you understand them, they're willing to work with you. Before that, they can't.

**Situation:** A software vendor keeps pushing back on a client's timeline but can't articulate why. The conversation circles. Both sides frustrated. Each new call covers the same ground.
**Application:** The client stops advocating for her timeline. She labels: "It sounds like you're caught between wanting this contract and protecting your team from burnout." She paraphrases: "So your development resources are already allocated, and this would require pulling people off other projects." Then she summarizes the whole picture.
**Result:** Vendor says, "That's right. That's exactly our situation." The stall breaks. They find a phased implementation that gives her critical features fast and gives the vendor time to staff properly. The solution had been there all along. Neither side could see it until both felt understood.

**Situation:** A couple keeps having the same argument about their social calendar. He wants quiet nights at home. She wants more time with friends. They've had this fight dozens of times.
**Application:** He stops defending his position. He summarizes hers back to her. Her need for connection. Her fear of losing friendships. Her sense that their life is getting too isolated.
**Result:** She says, "That's right. You finally understand." They still disagree about the specifics. But the argument stops, because they're no longer trying to prove they've been heard. From that foundation, they find a compromise neither had considered before.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Celebrate when you hear "you're right."
**Why:** "You're right" means they want you to shut up. They've checked out of the conversation. They're not agreeing with you, they're dismissing you. If you treat it as a win and launch into your proposal, you're proposing into a closed door. Recognize the signal. Back up. Listen more. Label more. Earn the real phrase.

**Don't:** Ask "does that sound right?" and treat a polite "yeah" as the breakthrough.
**Why:** You can't ask for "that's right." You have to earn it with a summary so accurate the only honest response is agreement. Asking for it produces compliance, not connection. A polite "yeah" or "I guess" is the tell that your summary missed. Go back, listen again, build a fuller picture.

**Don't:** Immediately pivot to your proposal the moment you hear the magic words.
**Why:** "That's right" is a moment of connection. If you sprint straight into your ask, it tells the other side you were just running a play to soften them up. That retroactively poisons the whole exchange. Pause. A beat of silence. Maybe a simple "I appreciate you sharing that." Then move. The restraint is what makes the understanding feel real.
