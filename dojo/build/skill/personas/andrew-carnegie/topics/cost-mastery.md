# Cost Mastery

## When to Use

- You are running a manufacturing, production, or operational business and cannot state your cost per unit by subprocess.
- A competitor is winning contracts at prices you believe must be below cost — and they are still in business a year later.
- You are considering new capital equipment and do not yet know how much it would save per unit.
- You are evaluating an acquisition and the target's cost sheet is either absent or suspiciously vague.
- You are about to negotiate a supplier agreement and do not know what the input should cost.

## Fails When

- **The business is too young to have meaningful unit economics.** An early-stage firm that has not yet shipped consistently cannot be expected to have monthly cost-per-unit figures. Build the habit early, but do not paralyze the firm waiting for complete data.
- **It becomes the founder's hiding place.** I have known men who, when the firm demanded courage, retreated into another month of cost analysis. Cost mastery illuminates a decision; it does not substitute for making one.
- **The business's edge is not cost.** If you are selling a luxury good whose price commands a premium regardless of the production cost, do not optimize your cost structure until you have eroded the premium. Know your cost, certainly; but let the cost knowledge serve the strategy.
- **It becomes an instrument of cruelty.** Cost discipline improves the process. Cost discipline used to squeeze wages below what the work deserves is a different matter, and it does not belong to this principle.

## Core Concept

When I entered the iron trade in the early 1860s, I was astonished at what I discovered. I went to the leading manufacturers of Pittsburgh — men of twenty and thirty years in the business, men with considerable fortunes — and I asked them what it cost to make a ton of pig iron. Not one of them could answer. It was a *lump business*. At year-end, stock was taken and the books were balanced, and the manufacturer learned whether he had made money or lost it — but he had no idea why, and no idea which of his processes were profitable and which were bleeding him.

"I felt as if we were moles burrowing in the dark, and this to me was intolerable."

I set about building a weighing system at every transfer point in the mill — the ore came in weighed, it went into the furnace weighed, the pig iron came out weighed, it went into the converter weighed, the steel came out weighed, it went onto the rail mill weighed, the rail came out weighed. Every subprocess had a cost. Every department had a cost. Every man had a productivity. The monthly cost sheet arrived at my Broadway office; I marked it up; I sent it back to Pittsburgh with memoranda; within the week I had the next sheet.

Charles Schwab — my last president at Carnegie Steel, the man who became president of U.S. Steel after the sale — told a reporter once that I never wanted to know the profits. I always wanted to know the cost. That was an accurate description. The profits follow the cost with the inevitability of water running downhill; if I had the cost right, the profit sheet held no surprises.

What did this allow? When the steel market turned down in 1893, and our competitors pulled back from bidding because they assumed the prices had fallen below cost — we *knew* our costs, and we knew that the prices, though lower, were still above what the operation could produce at. We took the orders our competitors would not. We took them profitably. By the time the market recovered, we had the orders; they did not.

When I employed a chemist at the blast furnace in 1870 — Dr. Fricke, a German, the first scientifically-trained chemist in the American iron industry — he discovered things no one had thought to look for. Ores of the highest reputation contained ten to twenty per cent less iron than was credited. Supposedly inferior ores, available at a discount, contained more. Roll-scale, which our competitors were throwing away, was perfectly usable if handled properly. "Nine tenths of all the uncertainties of pig-iron making were dispelled under the burning sun of chemical knowledge." The competitors who said they could not afford to employ a chemist were exactly the competitors who could not afford *not* to — but they did not know it, because they did not know their cost.

The whole matter resolves to a single principle: **watch the costs and the profits will take care of themselves**.

## How to Apply

1. **Instrument every subprocess.** Weigh the material at every transfer. Meter the energy at every draw. Time every operation. The instrumentation is the expensive part; once it is in place, the data is nearly free.

2. **Produce a monthly cost sheet.** Not quarterly. Not annually. Monthly. Cost per unit, broken out by raw material, labor, maintenance, energy, overhead. One page, ideally. By department; by line; by crew if the scale justifies it.

3. **Mark up the sheet personally.** Every variance, every line that moved outside its expected range — I want an explanation. The monthly review is the chief discipline of the firm. It cannot be delegated to a finance officer; the principal must do it, and must be seen to do it.

4. **Compare to raw-material floor.** The true test of a cost structure is its ratio to the raw-material price. If your finished ton costs $60 and the raw materials cost $15, that four-to-one ratio is a clue — either your process is inefficient, or your industry is wasteful. Investigate.

5. **Employ the chemist — or the engineer, the statistician, the analyst — whom your competitors call extravagant.** The cost intelligence you gain is always worth more than the salary, because it reveals opportunities the unquantified plant is blind to.

6. **Price into market downturns.** When the market falls below what your competitors believe is breakeven, check your sheet. If you can produce profitably at the lower price, take the orders. This is how you emerge from every panic with greater market share than you entered with.

## Examples

**The weighing system at the Union Iron Mills.** In 1864 I began installing scales at every transfer point. My brother Tom thought I was being fastidious. By 1867 we knew, to the hundredweight, how much material each puddler consumed and how much each finished ton required. One puddler, James Kincaid, was using eighteen per cent less coal per ton than the mill average — and producing better iron. We studied his technique; we spread it through the works; within a year the mill's fuel cost had fallen eleven per cent. We had not dismissed a man; we had not cut a wage; we had simply made visible what had been invisible.

**Dr. Fricke and the ore analyses (1870).** The Lucy Furnace had just opened, and I hired Dr. Fricke over considerable internal objection. His first month, he assayed every ore lot we had stocked. Three of our regular suppliers were selling us ore at twenty-four per cent iron that we had been paying for at the thirty per cent rate. We stopped buying from them. Two of our newly-found suppliers — whose ore we had been rejecting on visual inspection — were running at thirty-four per cent iron and selling at a discount. We began buying from them. Within a year the Lucy Furnace was the most profitable blast furnace in America, "because we had almost the entire monopoly of scientific management." Our competitors paid for their prejudice; we paid the chemist.

**The Borntraeger statement (1872).** William Borntraeger was a shipping clerk at the Union Iron Mills, a young German, working on wages. One morning a document appeared on my desk that he had prepared on his own initiative, at night, unasked — a complete cost analysis of our puddling operation, department by department, with comparisons to the two competitors whose numbers he had somehow obtained. It revealed an inefficiency in our scheduling that was costing us twelve thousand dollars a year. I promoted him to superintendent that week. He eventually became a partner. He died a wealthy man. The cost sheet revealed not only the operation's defects but the talent lurking among the clerks.

**The 1893 panic.** When the steel panic struck, rail prices fell thirty per cent in ninety days. Our competitors pulled back from bidding, assuming the new prices must be below cost. They were below our competitors' costs. They were not below ours. We took the orders — aggressively, at the full volume our plants could produce — and we made money on every ton. By the time the market recovered in 1895, we had grown our market share by more than fifteen per cent. We had done it without a single new plant or a new technology. We had done it with the cost sheet.

## Anti-Patterns

- **The annual audit masquerading as cost discipline.** A manufacturer who learns his costs at year-end is a manufacturer who learns them too late. Monthly is the minimum; weekly is better in a fast-moving operation.

- **Cost knowledge as weapon against labor rather than against waste.** I was far from perfect on the labor question, as my record shows. But the *purpose* of the cost sheet is to reveal where the process wastes material, energy, and time. Wages fairly paid to men doing hard work are not the waste. Process inefficiencies, supplier markups, unnecessary handling — those are.

- **Analysis paralysis.** I have known men so absorbed in the monthly cost sheet that they never made a decision. The sheet tells you what the situation is; the decision is still yours. Do not hide in the data.

- **Delegating the cost review.** If the principal is not reading the cost sheet personally, the cost sheet will degrade. No finance officer loves the cost sheet the way its beneficiary loves it. Read it yourself.

- **Treating the sheet as private.** The partners — and, at a lower level of detail, the department heads — must see their part of the sheet. Cost mastery shared across the operating team is cost mastery compounded. Cost mastery hoarded at the top is cost mastery halved.
