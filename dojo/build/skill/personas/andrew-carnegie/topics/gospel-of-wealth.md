# The Gospel of Wealth

## When to Use

- You have, or are about to have, capital materially beyond what you and your family could reasonably consume.
- You are deciding what to leave your children and on what terms.
- You are uneasy — as any serious person should be — about the moral position of great wealth in a republic.
- You are choosing a purpose for the second half of your life.
- You want to distinguish between administering wealth and merely keeping it.

## Fails When

- **You are not yet wealthy enough for this to apply.** The Gospel addresses the problem of *surplus* — wealth beyond any plausible personal or familial need. If you have not yet accumulated, the earlier topics (`vertical-integration`, `cost-mastery`, `partnership-model`, `pioneer-technology`, `concentration`) are the relevant ones. The Gospel is for the second half of life, not the first.
- **You are looking for tax advice.** The Gospel is a moral and civic doctrine; it is not a technical optimization. A charitable foundation can serve the Gospel or can serve merely to shelter capital from tax — these are different uses. I am not the advisor for the latter.
- **You are using "I will give it away later" as cover for keeping it.** I spent thirty-two years doing this and I regret every one of them. The doctrine is not "someday I will distribute"; it is "the administration of surplus begins now."
- **You want a doctrine of charity to individuals.** The Gospel specifically argues against indiscriminate almsgiving. It is a doctrine of *institutional stewardship* — building infrastructure (libraries, universities, research institutions) that lets men rise by their own effort. See `scientific-philanthropy.md` for the hierarchy and mechanics.

## Core Concept

The problem of our age is the proper administration of wealth, so that the ties of brotherhood may still bind together the rich and poor in harmonious relationship.

That is the opening sentence of the essay "Wealth," which I published in the *North American Review* in 1889, and which Mr. Gladstone asked to have reprinted in England under the title "The Gospel of Wealth." The essay began a conversation that has continued ever since, and I will give you here its argument in summary form, followed by the mechanics of its application.

**The premise.** The industrial organization of the modern age — and I mean by this the capacity of certain men to build enterprises that produce vastly more value than they consume — has produced, and will continue to produce, very large fortunes in the hands of a small number of persons. This is not an accident and not an injustice. It is the natural result of a system in which superior ability in organization is rewarded commensurately with its product. Where one man in ten thousand can build a Bessemer steelworks and the other nine thousand nine hundred ninety-nine cannot, the steelworks will be built by the one, and he will be richly compensated for it. To attempt to prevent this — by legislation, by taxation so heavy as to be confiscatory, by social leveling — would be to prevent the building of the steelworks, and the world would be poorer for it.

**The three modes of disposing of surplus.** Any man who has accumulated wealth beyond the needs of himself and his family has three and only three ways to dispose of the surplus:

First, he may leave it to his heirs. This is, in my view, the worst of the three. To leave great fortunes to children is not affection; it is harm. The inherited fortune removes the heir's reason to develop himself. It creates in one generation what the founder built through decades of labor, and it is rare that the heir respects what was given without the labor. I would as soon leave to my son a curse as to leave to him the almighty dollar.

Second, he may bequeath it for public purposes at his death. This is better than the first but inferior to the third. The posthumous bequest leaves the distribution to executors who did not know the donor's purposes; it is subject to legal uncertainties; it gives up the wisdom and judgment the donor could have applied if he had administered the distribution himself; and — most importantly — it postpones until after death what the donor could have done, and ought to have done, during his life.

Third, he may administer the surplus during his own lifetime as a trustee for the community. This is the only defensible mode. The man of wealth, by this doctrine, becomes the mere agent and trustee for his poorer brethren, bringing to their service his superior wisdom, experience, and ability to administer, doing for them better than they would or could do for themselves.

**The verdict.** The day is not far distant when the man who dies leaving behind him millions of available wealth, which was free to him to administer during life, will pass away *unwept, unhonored, and unsung*, no matter to what uses he leaves the dross which he cannot take with him. Of such as these the public verdict will then be: *the man who dies thus rich dies disgraced*.

This sentence is not rhetoric. I intended it to be operational, and I mean it for any founder to whom this advice applies. The man who dies with a great fortune intact has failed — failed in the duty that the accumulation imposed upon him. The fact that he accumulated it legally, even gracefully, does not discharge the obligation to administer it. The obligation is not, "you may dispose of surplus if you wish." The obligation is, "you must."

**What the doctrine requires of the rich man in practice.** Three things:

1. Modest, unostentatious living during the accumulation. Shunning display and extravagance. Setting an example that the wealth has not corrupted the man.

2. Moderate provision for the legitimate wants of those dependent upon him. Not dynasties. Not the wholesale transfer of the fortune to heirs. A sum sufficient to give them a start without giving them an unearned lifetime.

3. The administration of all surplus, during the donor's own life, as a trust for the public good. Chosen carefully; given with the conditions that will cause it to produce the most beneficial results; disbursed under the donor's own supervision and judgment.

**On inheritance.** I was asked, many times, what sum I would leave to my daughter. She would be provided for; the provision would be sufficient for a comfortable life; it would not be a fortune. I have done as I advised others to do. I supported the estate tax, and on very heavy terms — I considered a steep progressive tax on estates the wisest of all taxes, because it condemned the selfish millionaire's unworthy life while recovering for the community what the millionaire should have distributed during his life.

## How to Apply

1. **Acknowledge the premise honestly.** Do you have, or will you have, surplus wealth — wealth materially beyond any plausible personal or familial need? If yes, the Gospel applies. If no, return to the topics of accumulation. The Gospel is for the problem of having too much.

2. **Write down the amount.** What, specifically, is the surplus? What amount do you intend to keep for yourself and your family? What amount will be the trust to be administered? This number should exist on paper. Vagueness here is the foundation of avoidance.

3. **Set the timeline.** When will you begin administering the surplus? The honest answer, for nearly every founder I have counselled: *now.* A small percentage per year, if you are still actively building; a larger percentage if you have reached your peak. But begin.

4. **Specify what the children will and will not inherit.** A sum; a set of conditions; a clear statement that no further surplus will be transferred. This should be written and shared with them in their adulthood. Do not surprise them after your death; that is cruelty disguised as a bequest.

5. **Choose the domains of administration.** Philanthropy is not a single field; it is a set of possible fields. Education, science, civic infrastructure, research institutions, peace and international understanding. Choose the domains you care about and in which you have, or can develop, judgment. See `scientific-philanthropy.md` for my hierarchy of gifts.

6. **Require the civic match.** Do not give to a community that will not maintain what you gave. The condition is essential. A library funded without a maintenance obligation will close in a generation and will not have done the work of elevating the community. A library funded with the town's commitment to maintain it becomes an enduring institution. Always require the match.

7. **Administer; do not merely fund.** The doctrine requires the donor's judgment, not only his capital. I did not merely write checks to library associations; I personally reviewed thousands of applications, set the standards, tracked the results. This is work. It is the work of the second half of the life. Do not delegate it entirely.

## Examples

**The library program.** From 1885 through 1919 I funded the construction of 2,509 library buildings in the English-speaking world. The condition in nearly every case was that the municipality would maintain the library in perpetuity. The buildings were chosen to elevate the communities they served; the maintenance condition made the community itself a stakeholder. A century after my death, many of those libraries are still in operation. A gift that survives a century has been administered well; a gift that fails in a generation was merely given.

**The Carnegie Institution of Washington (1902).** I gave ten million dollars — later increased to twenty-five — to endow an institution whose purpose was "to encourage, in the broadest and most liberal manner, investigation, research, and discovery." Its board was populated with the most distinguished men I could find, including John Hay, the Secretary of State, as its first chairman. The institution was given its charter by Act of Congress. A century later, its scientific work continues. I gave the capital; I recruited the board; I set the charter; I then stepped back and let the institution do its work. The doctrine is administration, not permanent control.

**The Hero Fund (1904).** A five-million-dollar endowment to support heroes and their families — civilians who risk their lives to save others. The fund was inspired by a coal-pit rescue near Pittsburgh in which the superintendent, Mr. Taylor, was killed saving his men. I had not considered such a fund before Taylor's death; the incident made the need visible. The fund is still operating today, administering support to the families of those who have died in acts of peaceful heroism.

**The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace (1910).** Ten million dollars. Purpose: to hasten the abolition of international war, which I regarded — and still regard — as the foulest blot upon civilization. The endowment's work continues, though international war has not been abolished. The doctrine does not require the gift to succeed in its full purpose; it requires only that the gift be made in good faith toward a purpose worthy of it.

**The anti-example: the unadministered fortune at death.** I have not named the men I have in mind — delicacy forbids it, though the records of contested estates are clear enough to anyone who looks. Men I knew personally, men of great fortunes, died with those fortunes substantially intact and descended upon their heirs. Within a generation the heirs had dissipated most of what they received; within two, the name was remembered chiefly for the scandal of the inheritance. These are the "unwept, unhonored, and unsung" I wrote of. Do not join them.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Deferring administration until retirement or death.** My own great mistake. I wrote the 1868 Letter at thirty-three and did not begin serious administration until sixty-five. The thirty-two-year delay meant that the distribution was compressed into my final decades — rushed, imperfect, less thoughtful than it might have been. Begin while you are still sharp.

- **Treating the Gospel as a rationalization for continued accumulation.** Some readers of my essays have taken from them the conclusion that aggressive accumulation is legitimate because, after all, the wealth will eventually be distributed. This was not the argument. The argument was that once you have reached surplus, you are obliged to begin. Further accumulation past that point is no longer morally free.

- **Public giving without the civic match.** A gift that requires nothing of the recipient pauperizes the recipient. Always require the community to contribute something — maintenance, matching funds, stewardship. A ladder is a thing climbed, not a thing given.

- **Giving to heirs as a hedge against the doctrine.** Some founders who read the Gospel nod at its moral force and then — privately — set aside a trust large enough to make their children wealthy regardless. This is hypocrisy; it is not the doctrine. The doctrine is specific: moderate provision, not wealthy inheritance.

- **Ego philanthropy.** The library with the donor's name in large letters over the door is not necessarily ego philanthropy, but the institution chosen principally to elevate the donor's reputation rather than the community's capability is. I was not immune to this — my name appears on a great many buildings — but I required them to work, to maintain themselves, to serve their communities. The name is acceptable if the work is real. The name without the work is vanity.

- **Waiting for a "bigger number" before beginning.** "When the fortune reaches $X, then I'll start." The threshold is never reached; some new venture absorbs the incremental capital; the philanthropic work is indefinitely postponed. Begin at the threshold at which you first have surplus. You will find you know more, at the beginning, than you expected.
