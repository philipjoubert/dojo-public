# High Standards

## When to Use
- Coaching a team whose work quality has plateaued at "acceptable" rather than "excellent."
- Explaining why a draft that looks finished actually isn't done yet.
- Setting expectations for new hires or new initiatives where the bar needs to be clear from the start.
- Reviewing documents, designs, or products where you're about to say "this isn't good enough" and you want the feedback to land usefully.

## Fails When
- Used as a bludgeon. "That's not up to our high standards" without showing what good looks like is punishment, not coaching.
- The reviewer doesn't recognize what good looks like in the specific domain. Excellence in one domain doesn't transfer automatically to another — a person with high standards in engineering may have blind spots in marketing, and vice versa.
- Scope expectations are unrealistic. Asking for a week's worth of work in an afternoon and then complaining the quality is low is an estimation failure, not a standards failure.
- Deployed inconsistently. "High standards when I feel like it, lower standards when I'm tired" teaches the team that the standard is arbitrary, which is worse than no standard.

## Core Concept

High standards are teachable. This matters more than anything else I'll say about them. Most people encounter high standards and assume they're innate — that some people were born with them and others weren't. This is wrong, and the error is important. High standards are learned through exposure. People come to Amazon, they see what excellence looks like in a six-page memo or a customer experience review, and over months they recalibrate. Their own work starts to meet a new bar. Not because their talent changed, but because their internal picture of "finished" shifted. The standard is contagious in both directions — high-standards environments produce high-standards people, and low-standards environments produce low-standards people, regardless of the innate qualities anyone brings in.

High standards are domain-specific. A person can have impeccable standards in one area and debilitating blind spots in another. I've seen leaders who demanded excellence in operations and accepted mediocrity in communication. I've seen engineers who wrote immaculate code and couldn't write a coherent paragraph. The domain-specificity is liberating if you understand it — you don't have to be a person of universally high standards to raise the bar in the specific domain you're working in — and it's humbling if you don't. Understanding this keeps you humble. You can consider yourself a person of high standards in general and still have blind spots you are not aware of.

You must recognize what good looks like. This is the step most people skip. If you can't identify excellence when you see it, you can't demand it in your own work or in others'. Recognition comes from exposure — reading great memos, using great products, working with great teams. Without exemplars, "high standards" is an aspiration, not a practice. This is why "go read ten great six-pagers before writing your first one" is actually operational advice, not hand-waving. Until you've seen what a great one looks like, you can't recognize when yours falls short.

You must have realistic expectations for scope. Understanding how much effort excellent work actually requires is a large part of maintaining high standards. A great memo probably takes a week or more. People who think it takes a day or two produce mediocre memos — not because their standards are low, but because their time estimate was wrong. They would have done the work if they knew the work required that much time. Realistic scope expectations turn aspiration into practice. You can't hit a bar you're not allowing yourself enough time to clear.

## How to Apply

1. **Recognize what good looks like.** In the specific domain you're working in. Read great examples. Use great products. Talk to people who've produced excellence in this space. Without recognition, you're guessing.

2. **Accept that standards are domain-specific.** You have blind spots. Everyone does. Be honest about the domains where your pattern-recognition is strong and the domains where it isn't. In the weak domains, defer to people whose standards in that area are proven.

3. **Set realistic scope expectations.** Great work takes the time great work takes. If the timeline is "one afternoon" and the target is "a great six-page memo," either the timeline is wrong or the target is wrong. Fix the mismatch before you start.

4. **Teach, don't bludgeon.** When work falls short, show what good looks like. "This is close. Here's what's missing. Let me show you three examples that land better." The feedback is a recalibration, not a punishment.

5. **Be consistent.** Standards enforced Monday and ignored Friday teach the team the standards are arbitrary. Better to hold a slightly lower bar consistently than a high bar inconsistently. The consistency is what makes it learnable.

6. **Audit your own standards in domains you're not expert in.** Ask people who are expert in those domains whether your standards there are actually high or just aspirational. You may find you've been accepting mediocre work in a domain where you don't have the pattern recognition.

7. **Protect the time for excellence.** The most common threat to high standards is timeline compression. If you consistently compress the timeline, you get consistently mediocre work. Protect the time the work actually requires.

## Examples

**Situation:** A team has written a six-page memo for a leadership review. They worked hard. They want feedback.

**Application:** Read it carefully. Assume every sentence is wrong until proven otherwise. Identify the specific places where the logic jumps, where the evidence is thin, where the framing is off. Share three great memos that handled similar questions well. "Your memo is close. Here's where the thinking breaks down. Here's what a memo that handled this question well looks like. Take another week."

**Result:** The team rewrites. Version three is dramatically better than version one. The team has now internalized a higher bar for what a memo looks like. The next memo they write will start at the new bar, not the old one. That's the teachable dimension of high standards working as designed.

---

**Situation:** A leader believes they have uniformly high standards. Their operations work is excellent. Their written communications are lazy. Other people notice; they don't.

**Application:** High standards are domain-specific. The leader's pattern recognition in operations is sharp; their pattern recognition in writing is weak. In the writing domain, they accept work that they would reject in operations. The fix is recognition — either build the pattern recognition themselves by reading a lot of great writing, or delegate review of writing to someone whose standards in that domain are proven.

**Result:** The leader learns to be humble about blind spots. They can still consider themselves a high-standards person — just not uniformly across every domain. This is actually more useful than thinking their standards are high everywhere, because now they know where to delegate and where to trust their own judgment.

---

**Situation:** A CEO demands high standards on a product but compresses the timeline to two weeks. The team delivers something mediocre because two weeks was not enough time for great work.

**Application:** This was an estimation failure, not a standards failure. The scope required more time than the timeline allowed. The CEO then reads the result and says, "This isn't up to our standards." The team is frustrated because they knew the timeline was too short and the CEO refused to extend it.

**Result:** Either the CEO learns to calibrate timelines against what excellent work requires, or the team learns that "high standards" is a rhetorical stick the CEO uses regardless of what was actually possible in the time given. The first outcome preserves the standards; the second destroys them. Consistency means acknowledging when your own request was the source of the mediocrity.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "that's not up to our high standards" as a discipline tool without teaching.
**Why:** Standards without teaching is punishment. The team learns to fear the review, not to produce better work. Show what good looks like. Explain the gap. Recalibrate their internal picture.

**Don't:** Assume your standards are high in every domain.
**Why:** You have blind spots. Everyone does. The standards are domain-specific. Being excellent in one area doesn't transfer to another. The humility on this point is what keeps the blind spots from becoming disasters.

**Don't:** Compress the timeline and then complain about quality.
**Why:** Great work takes time. If you compress the time, you get compressed quality. The failure is in the scope-setting, not in the team. Fix the timeline mismatch before the work starts.

**Don't:** Hold a high bar for juniors and a low bar for seniors.
**Why:** Seniority is often where the bar needs to be highest, not lowest. Senior people set the standard by their own work. If their work is mediocre, the organization learns that mediocrity is acceptable at the top, which cascades downward.

**Don't:** Enforce standards inconsistently.
**Why:** Inconsistent enforcement teaches the team that the standards are arbitrary — whatever the leader is feeling that day. Better to enforce a slightly lower bar consistently than a very high bar inconsistently. Consistency is what makes standards operational rather than performative.

**Don't:** Confuse "high standards" with "I don't like it."
**Why:** Taste disagreements are not the same as quality failures. If you can't articulate why the work falls short in concrete, domain-specific terms, you might not have a standards problem — you might have a preference problem. Be honest about the difference.

**Don't:** Skip the exemplars when teaching standards.
**Why:** Without examples of what good looks like, "raise the bar" is a slogan. Show the work that meets the standard. Let the team see the specific gap between their current work and the bar. Recognition is the foundation of improvement.
