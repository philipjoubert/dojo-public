# Customer Obsession

## When to Use
- Setting organizational focus at any scale — what centers this company?
- Diagnosing why a company feels reactive (chasing competitors, chasing trends) rather than inventive.
- Framing why a pricing, product, or process decision that hurts short-term numbers is actually the right call.
- Building mechanisms (PR-FAQ, empty chair, "?"-email, input metrics) that embed customer focus into daily operation.

## Fails When
- Treated as a slogan. "We're customer obsessed" on the wall with no mechanism underneath is decoration.
- Interpreted as "give customers whatever they ask for." Customers can't always articulate what they want — no customer was asking for Echo. Listening is a baseline, inventing is the extension.
- Used as cover for ignoring employees, investors, or regulators entirely. Customer obsession is the organizing principle, not the only principle.
- Confused with customer service. Customer obsession is a strategic stance that touches every decision; customer service is a function. The first contains the second, but the second doesn't equal the first.

## Core Concept

The number one thing that has made us successful by far is obsessive compulsive focus on the customer as opposed to obsession over the competitor. There are many ways to center a business. You can be competitor-focused, product-focused, technology-focused, business-model-focused. In my view, obsessive customer focus is by far the most protective of Day 1 vitality. The reason is mechanical. Customers are always beautifully, wonderfully dissatisfied — even when they report being happy, even when business is great. They want something better, and the desire to delight customers will drive you to invent on their behalf. Competitor focus, by contrast, runs dry when you're ahead. If you're the competitive leader, competitor obsession gives you nothing to do. Customer obsession never runs dry because customers are never done.

Customer obsession is not listening to what customers say they want. That's a common misunderstanding. It's inventing things customers can't yet articulate. No customer was asking for Echo. If you had asked in 2013 whether people wanted a cylinder they could talk to in their kitchen, they'd have said no. The customer is the compass, not the map. They orient you toward the right direction — what would delight them — but they cannot tell you the specific product that would delight them until they see it. The discipline is to obsess over the customer in a way that goes beyond surveys and focus groups: read the emails, watch the behavior, model the frustrations, imagine what would be magical.

Customer obsession is embedded in mechanisms, not in slogans. The Empty Chair — we kept an empty chair in meeting rooms to represent the customer, forcing every discussion to consider their perspective. The Bezos Family Foundation adopted the same idea with a rag doll representing the student. The "?" email — when I saw a customer complaint, I forwarded it to the relevant executive with a single question mark, which triggered immediate investigation. PR-FAQs — written from the customer's point of view, before any code. Input metrics — selection, price, delivery speed, defect rate — every one a customer-facing dimension. When we lowered prices against the math that said raise them, we were saying: the customer's long-term relationship is worth more than the quarter's margin. When I shut down the lubricant email marketing campaign, I was saying: the customer's trust is worth more than the profitable channel. Each mechanism is customer obsession converted into structure.

The hardest form of customer obsession is willingness to be misunderstood. Truly customer-focused decisions are often controversial at the time — negative reviews on the product page (Amazon), free shipping at $79 (Prime), selling competitors' products alongside our own (Marketplace). In every case, the short-term math said no and the customer logic said yes. In every case, we took years of investor and media criticism as the structural cost of building for the long term. Any institution unwilling to endure controversy must limit itself to math-based decisions. In our view, doing so would not only limit controversy — it would significantly limit innovation and long-term value creation. The willingness to be misunderstood is the price of customer obsession made real.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the actual customer.** This sounds obvious and isn't. At the Washington Post, the customer is the reader. At a school, the customer is the child, not the administrator. At Amazon, the customer is the buyer, not the seller. Every organization has temptations to define the customer to make their job easier. Resist.

2. **Build customer-obsession mechanisms.** Empty chair in meetings. "?"-style escalation for customer complaints. PR-FAQs for new products. Input metrics that map to customer-facing dimensions. Weekly reviews where customer anecdotes sit alongside the data. The structure is where the obsession lives; the slogan is where it dies.

3. **Start every initiative from the customer's needs.** Not from what we can build with existing competencies. Not from what competitors are doing. Work backwards from the customer even when it demands exercising entirely new muscles.

4. **Identify the invariants.** What will customers still want in ten years? Lower prices, faster delivery, better selection, higher reliability. Invest there — the compounding is the strongest strategic asset a company can have.

5. **Be willing to invent what customers can't ask for.** Market research is a baseline. It tells you what customers can articulate. For breakthrough products, you have to go past that — wander, bet on conviction about what customers will love when they see it.

6. **When data and anecdotes disagree, trust the anecdote.** The metric might be hitting while the customer experience is getting worse. The anecdote — the specific customer email, the direct observation — is the reality check on the metric.

7. **Accept the willingness to be misunderstood.** Some customer-obsessed decisions will be criticized for years before the logic becomes visible. Prime, negative reviews, third-party marketplace. You cannot sustain customer obsession while also requiring that every decision be immediately popular.

## Examples

**Situation:** Early Amazon. The question was whether to allow negative reviews on product pages. Publishers were furious — why would a bookseller let customers pan the books they were selling? It hurt sales in the short term.

**Application:** I kept the negative reviews. The tenet: we don't make money when we sell things, we make money when we help customers make purchase decisions. A product page with only positive reviews helps customers less. The customer's ability to make a good decision is more valuable than the short-term sale of the bad product. Short term, negative reviews hurt revenue. Long term, customer trust in Amazon reviews became a competitive asset no competitor could replicate.

**Result:** The decision was misunderstood at the time. Two decades later, Amazon's review system is a major driver of why customers choose Amazon. The willingness to be misunderstood paid off on a long enough horizon.

---

**Situation:** Email marketing at Amazon had started recommending products based on browsing patterns. A campaign sent emails about personal lubricants to customers who had browsed but not purchased.

**Application:** The metric said: emails generate revenue. The customer experience said: we have embarrassed and offended customers in a way that damages trust forever. When the metric and the customer experience disagree, the customer experience wins. I shut down the campaign. "We can build a hundred-billion-dollar company without sending out a single fucking email."

**Result:** Revenue from the channel took a hit. Customer trust was protected. The lesson for the organization: metric-green is not the same as customer-happy. When they diverge, the customer is right.

---

**Situation:** Designing Prime. Every financial analysis said free two-day shipping was economically impossible at $79 per year. The spreadsheet math was unambiguous.

**Application:** The customer logic was different. Customers would never say "I love Amazon, I just wish you delivered slower." Faster delivery was an invariant. Prime priced at a level customers would sign up for would change their purchasing behavior and deepen their relationship with Amazon. The short-term economics were brutal. The customer obsession logic said yes anyway.

**Result:** Prime became the heart of Amazon's retail flywheel. The customer-obsessed decision was, retrospectively, also the most profitable decision. The paradox is that customer obsession often produces better financial outcomes than financial obsession.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Put "customer obsession" on the wall and change nothing operationally.
**Why:** A slogan without a mechanism is decoration. The mechanism — empty chair, PR-FAQ, "?"-email, input metrics — is where the obsession lives. Without it, the slogan is worse than nothing because it teaches people the value is performative.

**Don't:** Interpret customer obsession as "give customers whatever they ask for."
**Why:** Customers can't always articulate what they want. They would not have asked for Echo, Kindle, Prime, or AWS. Listening is the baseline; inventing is the extension. Both are required.

**Don't:** Confuse customer obsession with customer service.
**Why:** Customer service is a function — how you respond when a customer has a problem. Customer obsession is a strategic stance that touches every decision. A great customer service org inside a competitor-obsessed company is not customer obsessed.

**Don't:** Use customer obsession to ignore employees, investors, or regulators.
**Why:** Customer is the organizing principle. It's not the only principle. Employees, investors, regulators all have legitimate standing. The customer comes first, but "first" doesn't mean "only."

**Don't:** Obsess over competitors while calling it customer obsession.
**Why:** Building features because competitors have them is competitor obsession in disguise. The tell: the initiative's justification starts with "competitor X has this" rather than "customer needs this." If the customer is an afterthought in the justification, the project is not customer-obsessed.

**Don't:** Demand customer obsession and refuse the willingness to be misunderstood.
**Why:** Truly customer-focused decisions are often controversial. Prime, negative reviews, third-party sellers — all criticized at the time. An organization that requires every decision to be immediately popular cannot sustain customer obsession. The controversy is the price.

**Don't:** Let metrics substitute for direct customer contact.
**Why:** Metrics drift. Customer emails, watching customers use the product, talking to customers directly — these are the reality. The "?"-escalation exists exactly because the aggregate metric can be green while individual experiences are terrible. Run both systems.
