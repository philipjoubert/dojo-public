# Volunteer Employees

## When to Use
- Setting the tone in new-hire orientations — making explicit what the company owes the employee, not just what the employee owes the company.
- Coaching managers on how to think about the relationship with their teams.
- Designing retention strategy in a tight labour market where comp parity is table stakes and the differentiator is *why someone wants to work here specifically*.
- Reframing the founder's mental model from *"we hire people to do work"* to *"talented people choose to share their gifts with us, and we owe them a reason."*

## Fails When
- **Used as a substitute for competitive pay.** The framework only works when comp and benefits are at least competitive. The volunteer framing is about the *additional* obligations on top of fair pay, not a replacement for it. Underpaying while invoking *higher purpose* is exploitation dressed as values.
- **Used to make leaving feel like betrayal.** The volunteer framing names the company's obligations — *we owe them a reason to stay* — not the employee's debt of loyalty. If a manager invokes *"but you volunteered to be on our team"* as a guilt trip when someone wants to leave, they've corrupted the framework.
- **Applied without follow-through.** Telling employees they're volunteers and then treating them like disposable line items is the worst of both worlds — you've made an explicit promise and broken it.

## Core Concept

Most companies operate on the implicit assumption that the employee owes the company. The employee was hired, the employee gets paid, therefore the employee should be grateful and should perform. This produces a transactional posture from leadership and a transactional response from staff.

The volunteer reframe inverts this. Anyone qualified to work for you could work for any of dozens or hundreds of comparable employers. They chose you. The choice is the employee's, not the company's — comp is a precondition, not the relationship. **The company therefore owes the employee much more than just a paycheck in return for that choice.**

> "I learned such a crucial lesson, which is that if someone's volunteering, the only way to motivate them is to have a higher purpose. We're all doing this because we agree with this bigger idea. And so basically took that idea to work with me when I first became a restaurateur, and again, everybody was getting paid, but at least half the people were older than I was, and I was learning what it was like to be a leader, what it was like to be the boss, and I basically, to this day, treat all of our employees as if they are volunteers, which is not in the real sense, you're gonna get paid, but if you're working for me, it means you're probably good enough to have gotten another 25 job offers at least, and so as far as I'm concerned, you're volunteering to share your gifts with us. I better give you a higher purpose and reason for wanting to be here."

The framework was forged in 1980, when Danny was the Cook County field coordinator for John Anderson's independent presidential campaign — managing actual volunteers, where there was no compensation lever to pull and no discipline lever beyond *the cause*. He learned that volunteer leadership is structurally different from paid management: you cannot motivate with money you don't have, you cannot discipline with pay you can't dock, you cannot threaten with a job they don't actually need. The only currency is the *why*.

Danny brought that framing back into the restaurant business and never let it go. Even though every staff member is paid, the operating posture is volunteer-management:

> "I'm aware that you're all here, on the most basic level, to pay the rent. Just as you need a job, I need people to take orders accurately, and to cook wonderful food."
>
> "Then I remind them that if they're as talented at what they do as we believe they are, they could have gotten a job at any of 200 other very good restaurants for the same pay. 'You could all be doing what you do anywhere else,' I say. 'But you chose to be with us. You have volunteered to be on our team, and we owe it to you to provide you with much more than just a paycheck in return.'"

What does the company owe in return for the choice? **A higher purpose.** Mutual respect and trust. The chance to learn from excellent colleagues. Work where the contributions actually matter. A culture worth showing up to. Recognition of the choice as a real choice, not as something the company is owed.

This is how stakeholder priority one (employees first — see `enlightened-hospitality-stakeholder-order.md`) gets operationalised at the relationship level. The order says employees come first; the volunteer framing is the *posture* that makes the order real. *We owe them* is the operating mental model, not *they owe us*.

## How to Apply

1. **Pay competitively first.** The framework requires this as a precondition. The volunteer framing on top of substandard pay is hollow. The framing earns its weight only when the financial table stakes are met or exceeded.

2. **State the framing explicitly to every new hire.** In the orientation, the new-hire meeting, the first one-on-one. *"You could be doing what you do anywhere else. You chose us. We owe you something beyond a paycheck."* The framing has to be named or it doesn't operate.

3. **Articulate the higher purpose in concrete, current terms.** The *why* of working here can't be a mission statement on a wall. It needs to be specific: *we're trying to be the best in the city at this; we treat people the way we wish we were treated; we're building an institution, not a quarterly result.* The team needs to be able to repeat the *why* in their own words.

4. **Make the relational obligations operational.** Mutual respect and trust shows up in roundtables where employees give feedback, in the dining/employee voucher programme so they actually use the product, in the *Walk the Talk* survey where they grade leadership, in the manager who asks how their day is and means it. The framing without the operational follow-through is empty.

5. **Treat departures as the system working as designed.** When someone leaves, the volunteer framing says *they had the right to leave the moment they walked in; thank you for the time you chose to give us*. Do not make leaving feel like betrayal. The team watches how departures are handled — punitive farewells teach everyone that the volunteer framing was rhetorical.

6. **Re-recruit the existing team constantly.** The volunteer framing implies that retention is not automatic — it has to be earned every day. Treat the existing team as if they could be recruited away tomorrow (because they could). Spend management attention on understanding what each person came for and whether they're still getting it.

7. **Coach managers in volunteer-leadership posture.** A manager whose mental model is *"my reports owe me X"* will produce transactional teams. A manager whose model is *"I owe my reports the conditions to do their best work"* will produce engaged ones. Train this explicitly.

## Examples

**Situation: 1980 — Cook County field coordinator for John Anderson's independent campaign. Pay: $214/week. Reports: 100% volunteers, most older than 22-year-old Danny.**

> "I didn't have any way to motivate them with money. Couldn't give them a raise, couldn't dock them their pay, so I learned such a crucial lesson, which is that if someone's volunteering, the only way to motivate them is to have a higher purpose."

**Application:** This is where the framework formed. With no compensation lever, the only way to lead was through purpose. The lesson generalised: even when you *do* have the compensation lever, treating the team as volunteers (in posture, not in pay) produces dramatically better engagement than transactional management.

**Result:** Danny brought the volunteer framing into Union Square Cafe in 1985 and every business since. The framing became the operating mental model that made the stakeholder order real.

---

**Situation: Every USHG new-hire orientation, every four weeks, conducted personally by Danny.**

> "Meeting with all our new hires — as I continue to do once every four weeks — often makes me think of the way champagne houses make nonvintage, or multivintage, champagne... I tell new hires right off the bat that for their salary review, 51 percent of any raise or bonus is set by how they're faring at the emotional skills necessary to do their job well, and 49 percent is tied to technical performance."

**Application:** The new-hire orientation is where the volunteer framing gets stated, the higher purpose gets articulated, and the company's obligations get named. *You chose us. Here is what we owe you in return: respect, trust, the chance to work alongside excellent colleagues, recognition that your contributions matter.* The orientation is the framework's installation moment.

**Result:** New hires understand from day one that the relationship is mutual, that the company has obligations, and that the work is connected to a *why* larger than the paycheck. Retention reflects this — USHG's tenure averages are far above the restaurant industry norm.

---

**Situation: A long-tenured server announces they're leaving for a different opportunity.**

**Application:** The volunteer framing dictates the response. *Thank you for the time you chose to give us. We're better for it. You always had the right to leave; we appreciate the years you stayed.* No guilt trips, no "after everything we did for you," no resentment. The departure is treated as the volunteer framework operating as designed.

**Result:** The leaving employee becomes a brand ambassador for the company forever. They tell everyone about the company. They send candidates. They sometimes come back. *"We have a pretty small industry and invariably if you're someone who left our business on your terms or on our terms, you're probably gonna be serving me in a restaurant sometime in New York. And I feel really good that I never mind seeing these people."*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the volunteer framing while underpaying.
*Why:* The framework requires competitive comp as the precondition. *"You volunteered, so you should be grateful even though you're underpaid"* is exploitation in values clothing. Pay first; volunteer-frame on top.

**Don't:** Use the framing to make leaving feel like betrayal.
*Why:* The framework names the company's obligations, not the employee's debt. The right to leave is implicit from day one. Manipulating that right with guilt corrupts the framework and damages the recruiter pipeline (because departing employees tell other prospects about the experience).

**Don't:** Confuse the framing with not enforcing standards.
*Why:* Volunteer-leadership is *not* permissive leadership. The standards are still high; the *constant gentle pressure* (see `constant-gentle-pressure.md`) still applies; the saltshaker still gets re-centred. The framing changes the *posture* of the relationship, not the rigour of the work.

**Don't:** Articulate the higher purpose as a marketing slogan.
*Why:* *"We're passionate about excellence"* on the careers page is too generic to motivate a volunteer. The *why* has to be specific, current, and connected to the actual work. A slogan substitutes for the framework; it doesn't operationalise it.

**Don't:** Skip the new-hire conversation about the framing.
*Why:* The framing has to be named to operate. Most companies assume the higher purpose transmits by osmosis. It doesn't. Name it explicitly in the orientation and repeat it in pre-shifts, all-hands, and one-on-ones.

**Don't:** Treat the team as volunteers and then ignore them in the stakeholder priority.
*Why:* The volunteer framing is the relational posture; the stakeholder order (see `enlightened-hospitality-stakeholder-order.md`) is the structural commitment. Together they work. Either alone is incoherent — a volunteer framing without employees-first prioritisation is theatre; an employees-first prioritisation without the volunteer posture is paternalism.
