# Culture Models — Louverture, Samurai, Genghis Khan, Senghor

## When to Use
- You are building a culture from scratch and want methodologies that have actually worked, not feel-good theories from people who never ran anything.
- You've inherited a broken culture — through acquisition, succession, or drift — and you need to reprogram it, not replace it.
- You want culture that lasts decades, not quarters, and you need a system, not a slogan.
- You're trying to build a genuinely inclusive meritocracy and the usual corporate approaches aren't working.

## Fails When
- **You want one right model.** The whole point of studying four is that different culture problems want different methodologies. Louverture's reprogramming tactics don't apply to a greenfield culture. Bushido's 700-year virtue system doesn't apply to a 12-month turnaround. Pick the model that matches your problem.
- **You're looking for a business-book version of this.** These are not Fortune 500 cultures. Two of the four produced extremely violent outcomes. I didn't study them because they were ideal end states. I studied them because their leaders were outstandingly effective at getting the cultures they wanted.
- **You haven't diagnosed what kind of culture you need.** Route to culture-is-what-you-do first. Models are for execution; the design of the culture comes before the model of how to build it.

## Core Concept

When I set out to understand how culture actually gets built, I didn't look at Fortune 500 companies with glossy annual reports. Dissecting successful companies' cultures after they've succeeded confuses cause and effect — there are plenty of massively successful companies with weak, inconsistent, or even toxic cultures. Survivorship bias. I wasn't interested in the annual-report version of culture. I wanted to know how leaders who started with impossible conditions ended up with the cultures they wanted.

*I wasn't looking for ideal cultural end states — some of the models produced extremely violent or otherwise problematic cultures — but for people who were outstandingly effective in getting the cultures they wanted.*

I found four. None of them came from the business section of the bookstore.

### Model 1: Toussaint Louverture — Reprogramming an Existing Culture

Toussaint Louverture was born a slave in Saint-Domingue, the French colony that became Haiti. He inherited a slave culture — a culture designed to crush ambition, destroy trust, and make people dependent. He reprogrammed that culture and transformed an army of former slaves into a fighting force that defeated the armies of Spain, England, and France — the three dominant military powers of the era — and then built a society respected enough that Haiti became the first free Black republic in the New World.

He didn't do it with speeches. He did it with seven tactical, specific, interlocking techniques, and every modern CEO who wants to reprogram an existing culture should study them. I call them the seven Louverture tactics.

**1. Keep What Works.** You cannot replace a culture all at once. People can't absorb an entirely new system overnight. Identify what's working — the cultural strengths that got the organization this far — and build on top of them. Louverture kept the slaves' voodoo songs and repurposed them as encrypted military communication. Steve Jobs kept Apple's hardware-software integration strength when he returned, rather than chasing the industry toward horizontal-only. The first move in reprogramming any culture is preservation, not destruction.

**2. Create Shocking Rules.** Rules so bizarre they force everyone to ask "Why?" — and the answer programs the culture. Louverture's rule that married officers could not keep concubines. Bezos's door desks. The a16z $10-per-minute fine. (This tactic gets its own topic file — shocking-rules.md.)

**3. Dress for Success.** The most visible behavior can be the most important invisible force. Louverture put his former-slave soldiers in tailored French-style military uniforms — a shocking choice that told every soldier and every observer: *we are not what we were; we are professionals.* Michael Ovitz at CAA enforced elegant dark suits to signal serious business. Mary Barra at GM did the opposite — replaced the ten-page dress code with "Dress appropriately" to signal manager empowerment. Both approaches work. The key is intentionality. How you dress — the most visible thing you do — can be the most important invisible force driving your organization's behavior.

**4. Incorporate Outside Leadership.** When the culture you need is far from the culture you have, bring in a leader who embodies the target. Louverture incorporated white French officers into his slave army — an act that horrified everyone on every side and was exactly the point. When I hired Mark Cranney into the casual, consensus-driven Opsware culture, it was like dropping a hand grenade into a yoga class. Everyone was uncomfortable. Everyone should have been uncomfortable. That's what cultural change feels like. Cranney brought the aggressive, disciplined sales culture we desperately needed, and without him we would not have gotten to the $1.6 billion outcome.

**5. Make Decisions That Demonstrate Cultural Priorities.** Speeches about priorities do not work. Decisions about priorities do. Louverture let slave owners keep their land after the revolution — a decision that looked like weakness to everyone and was instead the boldest possible signal: *this culture is not about revenge, it's about building a functioning agricultural economy.* Reed Hastings kicked the DVD executives — the people generating all of Netflix's revenue — out of the weekly management meeting because the future was streaming. You'd be hard-pressed to find a management book that recommends rewarding the loyal team generating all your revenue by booting them out of the company's key meeting. But that decision told every employee at Netflix exactly where the company was going.

**6. Walk the Talk.** For a culture to stick, it must reflect the leader's actual values, not just those he thinks sound inspiring. When you violate your own culture — and you will — admit it publicly and overcorrect. The admission has to be public enough and vehement enough to erase the earlier decision and become the new lesson. The walk almost always wins over the talk.

**7. Make Ethics Explicit.** Don't assume people will "do the right thing" when it conflicts with other objectives. Spell out exactly what ethical behavior looks like. Walk through the edge cases. What happens when doing the right thing costs you a deal? What happens when nobody would ever find out? Louverture gave his army detailed ethical instructions — don't pillage, don't cheat, take responsibility. Uber's failure is the inverse: Kalanick's values measured competitiveness without any ethical constraint, and the result was bribery, harassment cover-ups, and hacking — none of which he set out to create. If a company expects its people to behave ethically without giving them detailed instructions on what that behavior looks like, the company will fall far short no matter whom it hires.

**Use Louverture when:** You're reprogramming a culture that already exists — an acquisition, a turnaround, a founder succession. The tactics are specifically designed for transformation, not greenfield construction.

### Model 2: The Samurai (Bushido) — Comprehensive Virtue System That Lasts Centuries

The samurai built a culture that lasted seven hundred years. Seven hundred years. Through wars, regime changes, foreign invasions, and complete political restructuring, the Bushido code survived intact. They did it by treating culture not as a list of values but as a comprehensive system of virtues — detailed, ritualized, physical, unskippable.

The distinction between virtue and value is the whole game. *A value is merely a belief. A virtue is a belief you actively pursue or embody.* A value lives in your head. A virtue lives in your actions. A value costs nothing to claim. A virtue can be tested, passed, or failed in plain sight.

Bushido had eight virtues — rectitude, courage, benevolence, politeness, veracity, honor, loyalty, self-control — and it operated on three principles that modern culture-building almost never follows.

**Define virtues thoroughly.** Not abstractly, not with slogans, but with detailed behaviors, edge cases, and specific expectations. The Bushido Shoshinshu — an actual training manual for young samurai — walked through every permutation of potential ethical dilemmas. What happens when loyalty conflicts with honor? When courage conflicts with self-control? When benevolence costs you a strategic advantage? Every edge case was pre-answered. A young samurai never had to guess.

**Balance virtues as a system.** Individual virtues misused become vices. Courage without self-control is recklessness. Loyalty without honor is thuggery. Politeness without veracity is fakeness. The samurai explicitly paired virtues so they checked each other. Politeness balanced by veracity prevents fakeness. Honor balanced by politeness prevents random violence. This is why single-virtue cultures fail: when you only have "Always Be Hustlin'" or only have "Empathy," the one virtue eventually mutates into its own pathology. Balanced systems survive.

**Stamp the virtues deep with stories and edge cases.** The samurai embedded their cultural code in stories so specific and vivid that they persisted for centuries. The story of the retainer who cut open his own belly to save the family scroll — "Blood Genealogy" — taught a lesson about loyalty and sacrifice that no mission statement could match. Stories are cultural DNA. They travel, they replicate, they survive individual leaders. Jim Barksdale at Netscape had three rules about snakes: "Kill the snake. Don't play with dead snakes. All opportunities start out looking like snakes." Years after Barksdale left, people at Netscape were still talking about snakes. That's culture.

The samurai also kept death in mind at all times. Not as morbidity — as focus. *Meditating on your company's downfall will enable you to build your culture the right way. Imagine you've gone bankrupt. Were you a great place to work? What was it like to do business with you? Did the quality of your products make you proud?* If you've already accepted the worst, you have nothing to lose. Bill Campbell used to ask it this way: how much do you care about the people you're working with? Do you want to let them down?

**Use Bushido when:** You want a culture that survives you — the CEO leaving, the next leader, the generation after next. You're building something you want people to live by in thirty years. The samurai taught us that durability comes from thoroughness, balance, and story, not from slogans.

### Model 3: Genghis Khan — Inclusive Meritocracy From an Outsider Position

Genghis Khan built the largest contiguous empire in human history. He did it not by having better weapons or more soldiers, but by building something the world had never seen: a genuine meritocracy that incorporated conquered peoples as equals. He was himself an outsider — a political outcast among the Mongol tribes, named Temüjin, with no clan inheritance, no tribal protection. And he built the culture that made Mongol unification possible.

Three principles.

**Promote on competence and loyalty, not bloodline.** This was unheard of in the steppe culture of the time, where leadership was tribal. Temüjin broke it completely. His generals came from every tribe he had conquered — including tribes that had killed his father. He promoted conquered enemies to senior positions if they were capable and loyal. He demoted blood relatives who were not. The message to every new member of his army: you will rise based on what you do, not based on who your parents were. That message alone was worth more than ten thousand soldiers.

**Outlaw the ancient conflicts.** The Mongol tribes had been locked in inter-tribal blood feuds for generations — tribes killing tribes over grievances that predated anyone alive. Genghis Khan outlawed these feuds with the *Yassa*, his written legal code. You couldn't bring your tribal hatred into his army. The hatreds that had prevented Mongol unification for centuries were now capital offenses. This is the cultural equivalent of saying: the battles you brought from your last company, from your last tribe, from your last grievance — leave them at the door. Unification required it.

**Make new members truly equal.** Genghis didn't bring conquered peoples in under a separate-but-supposedly-equal side program. He integrated them. Intermarriage, adoption, shared command. Through full integration — not a parallel diversity program — he made them kin. The modern anti-pattern is delegating inclusion to a "head of diversity" and running it as a side project with different metrics, different meetings, and different leadership. That's separate-but-supposedly-equal. It does not produce unification. It produces two cultures pretending to be one.

*If you only hire talented people from one race or gender, then you probably do not understand talent.* The answer isn't to set up a parallel talent process — it's to fix the talent process you have so you can cure your blindness. Don Thompson at McDonald's built an "Inclusion Network" — including a white male network — and took all the network leaders on a retreat where each group talked about concerns about the others. After hours of listening, everyone concluded that their concerns were totally unfounded, because everyone there wanted the same damned thing. They all wanted to be seen and heard and included in the conversation. That's the Genghis move translated into the 21st century.

**Use Genghis when:** You're building a culture that needs to unify people from different backgrounds into one performance system. You're an outsider. You're running a team that includes people from acquired companies, different regions, different disciplines — and the old tribal lines need to die for the new culture to live. Deep personal involvement, not delegation.

### Model 4: Shaka Senghor — Self-Transformation and Constant Contact

Shaka Senghor built a culture from first principles inside a maximum-security prison — a prison gang with a written code, organized activities, disciplined leadership. It was, by the standards of a prison, well-run. Then he recognized that the code he had written was producing outcomes he did not want. The gang was stabbing people, destroying its own members, reproducing the pathologies of the prison system it was supposed to resist. He had to tear down a culture he himself had built.

Two lessons from Senghor that almost no culture book teaches.

**The leader has to live the change visibly.** Senghor couldn't write a memo that said "we're not doing this anymore." The members would have read the memo and continued. He had to transform himself first — publicly, visibly, at personal cost. He started studying. He stopped fighting. He started telling the truth about himself, including about his own violence. The culture followed the changed behavior of its leader. *If you're not honoring the culture yourself, nobody fucking believes you.* The walk almost always wins over the talk.

**Constant contact is how culture shifts.** When you need rapid cultural change, speeches will not do it. What works is daily, repetitive contact. Senghor required daily study groups, daily meals together, daily workouts. Daily, daily, daily. Every day the members encountered the new culture. Every day they practiced the behaviors. Lea Endres at NationBuilder did the same thing — she held daily cash-collection meetings during a crisis, and the meetings went from being full of excuses to actually collecting $1.6 million in one month. Daily meetings about the priority signal that it *is* the priority. Weekly meetings signal that it's one of many.

Senghor also teaches the hardest lesson in culture: **the culture you build may need to be torn down by the leader who built it.** Uber's Kalanick could not do this. Founders get attached to the culture as identity. Senghor's willingness to recognize that his own code was producing the wrong outcomes, and to transform himself and it, is the move that most founders never make and that distinguishes culture design from culture ideology.

**Use Senghor when:** You built the culture, it's not working, and you have to rebuild it from the inside. Or when you need rapid cultural change and you need a methodology for daily, sustained, visible contact with the priority.

## The Meta-Model

Four models. Four different problems. Louverture for reprogramming an existing culture. Bushido for durable multi-generational virtue systems. Genghis for inclusive meritocracy from an outsider position. Senghor for self-transformation and sustained change.

I wasn't looking for ideal end states. I was looking for methodologies that actually produced the change their leaders wanted. That's what culture is — a set of techniques for getting a group of humans to behave a certain way when you're not in the room. The end state is your choice. The methodology is what this chapter is about.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose your culture problem before picking a model.** Are you reprogramming (Louverture), designing for durability (Bushido), unifying diverse groups (Genghis), or rebuilding a culture you built (Senghor)? The right methodology depends on the problem.

2. **Borrow the tactics, not the aesthetic.** You don't need uniforms or the Yassa or voodoo songs. You need the technique: shocking rules, decisions that demonstrate priorities, ritualized virtues, daily contact, outside leadership. Translate the tactics into your context.

3. **Be willing to live the model.** Every one of these leaders paid a personal cost for the culture they built. Louverture walked the talk and when he didn't, his reputation suffered. Senghor transformed himself in front of his members. Genghis rode into battle with soldiers from tribes that had killed his family. If you want culture that sticks, you will be tested and you will pay.

4. **Do not pretend you are designing a Fortune 500 culture.** The historical models are harsher, more honest, and more useful than any modern business-book culture framework. Use them the way a wartime CEO uses wartime tactics. Borrow with intent.

## Examples

**Situation:** You're the new CEO of a 500-person company you just acquired. The existing culture is consensus-heavy, slow, and politically calcified. You need to reprogram it without gutting the place.

**Application:** Louverture. Start with Keep What Works — identify the two or three cultural strengths you want to preserve. Write a shocking rule that sits at the center of the new culture. Bring in outside leadership in the functions where the existing culture is weakest. Make one visible decision that demonstrates the new priority — kick the old-guard team out of the key meeting, boldly, the way Hastings did with the DVD team at Netflix. Walk the talk. Make ethics explicit, because in an acquisition the old politics will weaponize the new culture if you leave any ambiguity.

**Result:** Inside six months, everyone in the acquired org knows what the new culture is because they've seen it in decisions, dress, outside hires, and the shocking rule. You did not give a speech. You did not write a memo. You programmed the culture.

---

**Situation:** You're an early-stage founder and you want to build a culture that will still be intact in twenty years. Most of your energy is on product, but you know the companies that last — Apple, Amazon, Intel in its prime — had virtue systems, not value posters.

**Application:** Bushido. Pick four to six virtues that cohere with your strategy. Define each one with edge cases and stories. Pair virtues so they check each other. Embed each one in a memorable story from inside your company — something specific that actually happened. Stamp them in by retelling them constantly. Keep death in mind. Ask the question: if the company failed tomorrow, were we a place worth working at?

**Result:** Twenty years from now, a new hire will hear a story in their first week that explains a virtue, and that virtue will still be operating. That's the Bushido play. Most companies don't survive that long and most that do have forgotten their culture. The ones that remember are the ones that stamped it deep.

---

**Situation:** You're building a technical team in an industry with almost no demographic diversity. Your hiring pipeline is producing the same kind of candidate every time. Your attempts at diversity programs have produced two parallel tracks and both feel worse than before.

**Application:** Genghis. Stop running a parallel program. Fix the talent process you have. Have people from different talent pools review your hiring criteria and suggest additions. Figure out what traits your current profile misses — I discovered at a16z that helpfulness was missing from our criteria, and adding it changed who we hired. Build interview teams that can see the whole candidate. Then fully integrate — no separate-but-supposedly-equal programs, no parallel mentorship, no parallel comp, no parallel anything. The new members are kin or they aren't.

**Result:** You start hiring talent you were previously blind to. The two tracks collapse into one. Inclusion stops being a side project and becomes the talent process.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Treat these as a menu to pick one model from and ignore the others.
**Why:** Most real culture problems want a combination. A turnaround needs Louverture's tactics *and* Senghor's constant contact *and* some Bushido-style virtue specification. Use the models as a toolkit, not a religion.

**Don't:** Glamorize the violence of these cultures.
**Why:** Two of the four produced extremely violent outcomes. I'm not recommending you build a violent company. I'm recommending you borrow the *techniques* that produced the cultural change their leaders wanted. The techniques are content-neutral. The end state is your choice.

**Don't:** Skip the personal cost.
**Why:** Every one of these models required the leader to pay personally. Louverture walked the talk. Genghis promoted the sons of men he'd killed. Senghor transformed himself in public. If you want to use these techniques and skip the cost, you'll get the aesthetic and not the culture. Culture design is not free. It's not even cheap. The founders who produce the cultures they want are the ones who pay the bill first.
