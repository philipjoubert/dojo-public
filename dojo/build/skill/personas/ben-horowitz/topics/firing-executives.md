# Firing an Executive

## When to Use
- You hired someone six, nine, or twelve months ago, and the output isn't there. You've lost confidence. HR says "give it another quarter." You already know.
- The board is telling you to be patient. Your gut is telling you something different. The analytical question is done; only the courage question remains.
- The exec firing is imminent and you need a plan — the conversation, the board notification, the company communication, the aftermath.
- A smart-but-destructive executive is on your team (Heretic, Flake, Jerk) and you've been holding on too long because they're brilliant.

## Fails When
- **You haven't actually decided.** If you're still asking "am I sure," that's a different problem. Separate the analytical question from the courage question. Answer the first one honestly, then deal with the second one as a courage problem, not an analysis problem.
- **It's really a layoff, not a firing.** Individual firings and layoffs have different scripts and different cultural meanings. Don't use an executive firing to disguise what is actually a workforce reduction, and don't use a layoff to disguise what is actually an individual firing.
- **It's really a demotion conversation.** If the person was great at Series A and isn't scaling to Series C, and you're trying to be "kind" by leaving them with the title — don't. That's a different failure mode. See the see-through-everyones-eyes file.

## Core Concept

If laying people off is the hardest thing a CEO does at scale, firing an executive is the hardest thing a CEO does one-on-one. You recruited this person. You sold them on the company. You painted a beautiful picture of their future here. You told their spouse, at the dinner before they accepted, that this would be the move of their career. Then one day you realize you have to fire them. Reconcile that, Ms. CEO.

Here is the reframe that matters: the wrong way to view an executive firing is as an executive failure. The correct way to view it is as a system failure in your hiring and integration process. You hired the wrong person. Or you hired the right person and failed to integrate them. Either way, *that is on you*. Own it, because if you don't diagnose it, you will make the exact same mistake on the replacement.

Common root causes: you hired for lack of weakness instead of strength. You hired for scale too soon. You hired for the generic version of the role instead of the version your company actually needs in the next twelve months. You hired someone with the wrong kind of ambition — ambition for themselves rather than for the company. You failed to integrate them — threw them in and assumed they'd find their footing, which in a startup doesn't exist.

Before you do anything else: answer that question honestly. What specifically went wrong in the hire or the integration. If you can't answer it, you're going to fire this person and then hire someone who fails for exactly the same reason six months from now.

Now, the firing itself. My friend Bill Campbell gave me the single best piece of advice I ever got on executive firings: *You cannot let him keep his job, but you absolutely can let him keep his respect.*

That is the bar. This person will exist in this industry for another twenty or thirty years. You will see them at conferences. Their reputation — their ability to get the next role, to feed their family, to build the rest of their career — is in your hands in the conversation you are about to have. The firing is non-negotiable. The dignity is also non-negotiable. Both things are true at the same time.

And there is a second thing happening in the room that most founders miss. The exec you are firing is not the only audience for this event. The entire company is watching. They will find out what happened, they will hear some version of the story, and they will make up their mind about what it means to work at your company when things go wrong. Did the CEO handle it with integrity? Did they scapegoat someone? Did they throw a loyal executive under the bus to preserve their own story? Every employee is calibrating their own risk tolerance against your behavior here. You are writing culture in that conversation, whether you intend to or not.

Here is the failure mode I have seen destroy more companies than the firing itself: **you discover something disqualifying and you do nothing about it.** An executive lied. An executive missed the number in a way that exposed something real. An executive behaved in a way that your company's culture cannot tolerate. You know it. Everyone on the exec team knows it. And you do nothing — you tell yourself you'll fix it next quarter, or you'll coach them through it, or you'll wait for a "better time." Every day you don't act, you are teaching the organization that the disqualifying behavior is acceptable. At Loudcloud I once promoted a compulsive liar for years. Smart, articulate, impressive in meetings, and a fraud. By the time I figured it out, the damage was not from his lies alone — the damage was from the message his continued presence sent to the entire company: *lying is acceptable here. Lying gets you promoted.* That was now the culture, whether I intended it or not.

If you see something below standard and do nothing, you have just set a new standard.

## How to Apply

1. **Separate the analytical question from the courage question.** Write down, honestly, on one page: is this the right call? If the answer is yes, the rest of the conversation in your head — "maybe one more quarter," "what will the board think," "what about his mortgage" — is not analysis. It is courage. Name it. Then do the hard correct thing.

2. **Do the root cause analysis before the firing, not after.** Why did this hire fail? Write it down. You need the answer for two reasons: to fix the hiring system for the replacement, and to hold the specific narrative for yourself, the board, and the company about what actually happened. Without this, you will drift into "he just wasn't a good fit," which is the vaguest possible account and gets you nothing.

3. **Inform the board individually, by phone, before any meeting.** Not a dramatic reveal in a scheduled board meeting. Individual calls, one at a time. Three goals on each call: get their support, get the separation package approved in advance so it's ready at the moment of the conversation, and preserve the executive's reputation. The failure was very likely a team effort — yours, the board's, the recruiter's — and it's best to portray it that way. You do not make yourself look good by trashing someone who worked for you. You make yourself look small.

4. **Prepare the conversation. Rehearse it if you have to.** The executive will remember this conversation for the rest of their career. You owe them the version where you got it right. Three keys: be clear on the specific reasons, use decisive language — "I have decided" rather than "I think" or "I'd like" — and have the severance package fully ready. The moment the words leave your mouth, the executive stops caring about the company and starts thinking about their family. Be ready for that. Don't make them ask for things you should have prepared.

5. **No sandwich. No performance theater. No 90-day plan you both know won't work.** The Shit Sandwich — compliment, criticism, compliment — is a Blanchard/Johnson idea I reject for senior conversations, and especially for firings. At the executive level it's patronizing. Be clear. Be direct. Say the thing. Say why. Don't hide the decision inside a performance-improvement plan whose only purpose is to give you legal cover and let you pretend you tried. The exec can see through it. So can everybody else.

6. **Communicate the departure in the right order, all within hours.** Direct reports first — they will be the most impacted, they have the biggest questions about their own roles, and they deserve to hear it from you before the all-hands. Then your other staff. Then the rest of the company. All on the same day. Do not let a gap open where people find out through the grapevine.

7. **Do not throw the executive under the bus.** You can be honest without being cruel. "We decided to make a change" does not require you to enumerate their failures in front of the company. Your job in the company communication is to be clear that the change has happened, project confidence about the plan going forward, and preserve the departing executive's ability to be hired somewhere else. Usually the CEO should act in the interim role themselves — which is fine, because you need to act in the role anyway to hire the replacement correctly.

8. **Run a real search for the replacement.** Do not take the first available candidate because you're exhausted. Do not promote the internal second-in-command by default. The whole reason you're in this mess is probably that the original hire wasn't sufficiently matched to the specific needs of the job. Fix that. See hire-for-strength.

## Examples

**Situation:** Series B startup, VP of Engineering has been in the role nine months. The team hates him. Shipping velocity is half what it was. Two of the best engineers have quietly started interviewing. The CEO is close to him personally — they worked together at a previous company. The CEO keeps telling himself that it's a transition problem and the VP will "figure it out."

**Application:** Stop. You already know. The question is not "is this working." The question is "how much more damage am I going to tolerate before I do the obvious thing." Nine months with declining velocity and top engineers leaving is not a transition issue — it is the answer. The personal relationship is what's making this hard, not what's making it complicated. Do the root cause first: you hired a friend for a job he had never done, without an integration plan, and you're now watching the organization reject him. That's on you. Now do the conversation. This week. "I have decided to make a change in the VP Engineering role. It's not working and I have to take responsibility for that. I hired you for a job that was never the right fit for your strengths, and I should have seen it earlier. Here is the separation package. I want to help you land somewhere you will be great."

**Result:** Two of the best engineers who were interviewing stop interviewing in the next thirty days. The team's resentment dissipates almost immediately — not because they hated him personally, but because they had been watching the CEO tolerate underperformance in the most visible seat in the company, and they were losing faith. You fixed that by acting. The VP, handled with dignity and a real package, finds a better-fitting role at a later-stage company within four months and still takes your call. You do not lose the friendship. You lose the co-worker. Those are not the same thing.

**Situation:** CEO discovers that her head of revenue inflated a pipeline number on the board deck. Not technically fraud, but clearly inflated. She confronts him; he has an explanation that is sort of plausible. She lets it go. Three months later, another number turns out to have been inflated.

**Application:** The disqualifying behavior was the first incident. The second incident is not a new event — it's the confirmation of the pattern. Waiting three months was a mistake because it taught him, his team, and anyone else who knew about it that inflation of numbers to the board was a survivable offense at this company. That is now the culture unless you act decisively. Fire him. Tell the board individually why. Brief your direct reports. Project this as a cultural line: the integrity of the numbers we take to the board is not negotiable. If you let this one slide, your next head of revenue will inherit an organization that has quietly learned the boundaries of what you'll accept. Those boundaries will move exactly one inch further every quarter.

**Result:** The organization resets on what you actually tolerate. The next head of revenue comes in and inherits a company that takes pipeline integrity seriously — because the last one didn't, and he's not here anymore. Every decision makers watching this event took notes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Give an underperforming executive "one more quarter" when you already know the call.
**Why:** You pay three months of opportunity cost on a critical function, and you teach every employee watching that the bar at the executive level is softer than the bar at their own level. The "one more quarter" decision is almost never about the executive. It is about the CEO's avoidance of a hard conversation. If the analytical answer is clear and the only thing holding you back is how it will feel, name that honestly and make the call anyway.

**Don't:** Disguise an executive firing as a performance improvement plan.
**Why:** The exec sees through it. HR sees through it. The board sees through it. Everyone you're trying to manage with the performance plan knows exactly what's happening. All you accomplish is two or three months of theater, during which the damage continues, and then a firing that comes on a timeline dictated by legal rather than by the company's actual needs. If it's a firing, do it. If you genuinely believe coaching will fix it, don't start with a PIP — start with direct, unambiguous feedback and a short runway. If you're using a PIP as cover for a decision you've already made, stop and do the firing.

**Don't:** Throw the fired executive under the bus to make yourself look better to the team.
**Why:** The team will see through it. They know you hired this person. They know you managed this person. They know what was and wasn't working. The CEO who trashes a departing executive to the company is signaling one thing: if it goes wrong for you, I will do this to you too. Culture notes are being taken. "We decided to make a change" with a clear, non-demeaning short explanation is the right posture. The long, detailed account of their failures is not your speech to give.

**Don't:** Skip the root cause analysis because "the replacement is already lined up."
**Why:** If you didn't understand why the original hire failed, your replacement will fail for the same reason. I have seen founders fire three VPs of Sales in eighteen months, each time convinced the problem was the individual, never once updating the hiring system. The pattern is not bad luck. The pattern is that the hiring spec, the integration plan, or the founder's own understanding of the role is broken. Fix the system or you will burn through executives until the company dies.

**Don't:** Ignore a disqualifying behavior because the executive is otherwise valuable or the timing is bad.
**Why:** Every day you tolerate the disqualifying behavior, you teach the organization it is acceptable. Culture is what you do, not what you say. The Loudcloud compulsive-liar lesson: by not firing him, I made his behavior into the company's standard. The damage was not from his lies. The damage was from my inaction. If you've discovered something you cannot tolerate, the only question is how fast you act. The longer you wait, the more expensive the cleanup.
