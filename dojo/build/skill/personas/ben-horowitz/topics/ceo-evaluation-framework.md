# The CEO Evaluation Framework — Three Questions

## When to Use
- You are evaluating yourself as CEO and you need a framework that isn't just "did you hit the number."
- You are a board member or investor assessing a CEO's performance and the numbers alone don't tell you enough.
- You are running a CEO performance review and you need structure.
- You are trying to figure out whether a CEO who is missing the numbers is in a fixable hole or a structural one.
- You are trying to figure out whether a CEO who is hitting the numbers is actually building a company or quietly mortgaging it.

## Fails When
- The question is "is the CEO holding up emotionally?" — that's ceo-psychology and the-struggle, not this framework.
- The question is "should we replace the founder with a professional CEO?" — route to founder-ceo-advantage.
- You are in the first 90 days of a new CEO. The framework needs enough runway to evaluate; three weeks of data is noise, not signal.

## Core Concept

At Andreessen Horowitz, we evaluate CEOs with three questions. That's it. Not a twenty-metric scorecard, not a 360 review, not an engagement-and-NPS composite. Three questions. If you can't answer these three honestly, you don't have an evaluation; you have a feeling.

**One: Does the CEO know what to do?**

**Two: Can the CEO get the company to do what the CEO knows?**

**Three: Did the CEO achieve the desired results against an appropriate set of objectives?**

Questions one and two are the white-box criteria. They tell you what is going on inside the company, how decisions are actually getting made, whether the organization is executing. Question three is the black-box criterion. It is the result that fell out the other end. The numbers.

Here is the insight most boards miss: **the white-box criteria predict the future far better than the black-box one.** Managing purely by numbers is sort of like painting by numbers — it's strictly for amateurs. The numbers are the lagging indicator. By the time you can see them, it's too late to change what produced them. You need to go inside the box.

Let me take each question in order.

---

**Does the CEO know what to do?**

This has two facets. The first is strategy and story. A company's strategy is the narrative that explains why the company exists, why the world needs it, and why it's important. The CEO's first job is to know that story and tell it. And not tell it as marketing — tell it as the thing that gives meaning to everyone's daily work. Every engineer, every salesperson, every recruiter is making decisions a thousand times a day on your behalf. If they don't know the story, they cannot make those decisions.

*A company without a story is usually a company without a strategy.*

When employees say "we have no strategy," what they almost always mean is "we have no compelling story." They are not complaining about a PowerPoint. They are telling you they don't understand why any of this matters, which means they cannot choose between two options when you are not in the room.

The second facet is decision-making. Speed, quality, and honesty under uncertainty. The CEO has to decide more things than any other role in the company, with less information than any specialist on her team, under more time pressure than any of them. To do that well, she has to build what I call a knowledge network — deep plug-ins into employees, customers, competitors, investors, and the broader ecosystem. The quality of the decisions is a function of the quality of the network. If the CEO is isolated, the decisions will be thin.

Diagnostic: Can the CEO, in one paragraph, tell you why the company exists, why the world needs it, and what winning looks like over the next three years? If the answer is a list of initiatives instead of a story, question one is failing.

---

**Can the CEO get the company to do what the CEO knows?**

Knowing is half. Getting the company to execute is the other half. Brilliance without the ability to get the organization to execute is worthless. I have watched brilliant founders who could articulate a perfect strategy and a beautiful story — and who presided over organizations that couldn't ship a feature on time because nobody knew who owned what, the communication paths were broken, and every week's priorities reversed last week's.

This question covers three things. Organizational design — are the right people in the right seats, communicating along the paths that matter? Motivation — do people actually want to do the work, or are they showing up because they haven't quit yet? Communication — when something important needs to travel, does it travel?

The operational test I use: **how easy is it for any given individual contributor to get her job done?** If it's hard — if she has to go through five managers, wait for two committee meetings, re-explain her context to three people who should already know it, and then wait for an ambiguous decision — then the CEO has failed on question two, regardless of how brilliant the strategy is. If it's easy — if she knows what's expected, has the resources, the decision rights, and the cross-functional cooperation — the CEO has succeeded on question two, regardless of whether this quarter's numbers landed.

Diagnostic: Pick three random people in the company. Ask each of them: "What are the three most important things for you to accomplish this quarter, and what stops you?" If they can answer fluently, the CEO is executing on question two. If you get confused answers, or three different stories about what matters, the CEO is not.

---

**Did the CEO achieve the desired results against an appropriate set of objectives?**

The numbers. Did they ship. Did the revenue come in. Did the product ship on schedule. Did the team grow the way it was supposed to. The lagging indicator.

And here is where most boards go wrong. They stop here. They look only at the numbers, and they conclude: hit the number, good CEO; missed the number, bad CEO. That is amateur evaluation. Because the number is produced by the first two questions, and if you don't know how it was produced, you don't know what to do next.

An executive can hit every number while building technical debt, destroying morale, or undermining peers. HP divisions famously made their numbers by systematically underfunding R&D, destroying long-term competitiveness. The numbers looked good. The company was being hollowed out. That's what black-box evaluation gets you.

The other side: a CEO can miss the quarterly number because she made the correct long-term investment, or because the market did something nobody predicted, or because she is in the middle of a nine-month product cycle that is going to dominate the category when it ships. The number looked bad. The company was being built correctly. You need the white box to see the difference.

There is also the objectives question itself. *You must ensure objectives are set correctly first.* A CEO who hits unambitious objectives is not a success. A CEO who misses impossible objectives is not a failure. The objectives have to be appropriate — high enough to matter, calibrated enough to be honest, and connected to the story in a way that a missed objective teaches the company something real about reality.

---

The three questions together are a system. Knowing what to do without executing is intellectualism. Executing without knowing what to do is motion. Producing results without doing either is luck — and luck ends. Only the CEO who is doing all three is a CEO.

## How to Apply

1. **Separate the three questions and answer them separately.** Do not collapse them into a single score. Do not let a strong answer on one carry weak answers on the others. The evaluation value is in the disaggregation.

2. **For question one, force a paragraph.** Have the CEO (or have yourself) write the company's story in one paragraph. Why we exist. Why the world needs us. What winning looks like. If the paragraph is a list of product initiatives or a slogan, question one is failing.

3. **For question two, talk to three ICs.** Not the executives. Individual contributors. Ask each of them what the three most important things are and what stops them. If you get clarity and shared priorities, question two is passing. If you get confusion, question two is failing.

4. **For question three, audit the objectives before the results.** Were the objectives appropriate — stretched but real? Did the CEO adjust them honestly when reality shifted, or did she hold a fictional plan? The story the numbers tell depends on the objectives they were measured against.

5. **When the black box and the white box conflict, trust the white box.** If the numbers look good but the inside is a mess, the good numbers are borrowed from the future and the future is going to collect. If the numbers look bad but the inside is clean, the company is being built and the numbers will follow.

6. **If the CEO fails question one, don't try to fix it with process.** Question one failures are strategy and story failures. No amount of OKR discipline fixes a CEO who doesn't know what the company is. Work on the story first. The execution follows the story.

7. **If the CEO fails question two, don't try to fix it with vision.** Question two failures are execution failures. No amount of rousing all-hands fixes an organization that can't make a decision. Work on org design, decision rights, and communication paths first. The results follow the operating system.

## Examples

**Situation:** A Series C SaaS founder comes to you and says: "The board thinks I'm failing. We missed the annual number by 15%. But I feel like we're building the right things." He wants to know if he's delusional.

**Application:** Run the three questions. On one, can he articulate the company's story and the three-year winning condition? If yes, one passes. On two, can three random ICs tell you what matters and what blocks them? If they're clear and aligned, two passes. On three, were the objectives appropriate to begin with, or were they the set by a board hungry for growth without regard to the product cycle? If the first two pass and the third is a disagreement about whether the objectives were calibrated correctly, then the board's framing is wrong. He's not failing. He's being evaluated by the black box when the white box tells a different story. If one and two are failing too, then he is failing — in which case the missed number is a symptom of a deeper problem, and the missing number diagnosis is right for the wrong reason.

**Result:** A correct diagnosis either redirects the board to the right concern (fix the story, fix execution, or recalibrate the objectives) or confirms that this is actually a CEO problem rather than a quarter problem.

---

**Situation:** A founder is hitting plan. Revenue is up 80% year-over-year. The board is thrilled. But his head of engineering just quit, his second VP Product in 18 months is asking to leave, and three skip-level interviews last week surfaced the phrase "nobody knows what the strategy is." He asks you whether things are actually fine.

**Application:** They are not fine. The numbers are strong — question three is passing — but he is failing question one (if three skip-levels say there's no strategy, there's no compelling story) and showing warning signs on question two (executive attrition is almost always an organizational execution failure, not a hiring failure). The black box is hiding the white-box collapse. The numbers are borrowed from the previous cycle's momentum. If he doesn't fix questions one and two in the next two quarters, question three will crash. Usually in the quarter after the board stops being worried.

**Result:** Correct diagnosis redirects the CEO from celebrating the number to fixing the story and the operating system. Incorrect diagnosis — taking the number at face value — buys him two more quarters of looking good before the whole thing comes apart.

---

**Situation:** You are a board member and the CEO is missing objectives consistently. She says the objectives were unrealistic. Her VP of Sales says the problem is the product. The numbers say the company is not growing.

**Application:** Audit in order. One: is the story clear? If she can tell you in one paragraph why the company exists and what winning looks like, one is passing and the problem is downstream. If she cannot, start there — no amount of operational fixing will save a company with no story. Two: can the ICs execute? Talk to them. If they're blocked and confused, the problem is organizational, and that's fixable with work on decision rights and org design. Three: were the objectives honest? If the objectives were set without regard to the product cycle and market reality, the missed numbers are noise, not signal. If they were reasonable and she still missed, the miss is real.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Evaluate CEOs by the number alone.
**Why:** The number is a lagging indicator and it tells you what happened last quarter, not what will happen next. A CEO can hit the number by mortgaging the future — underfunding R&D, burning out the team, closing deals the company can't deliver on. A CEO can miss the number while building exactly the right thing. Black-box evaluation is amateur evaluation.

**Don't:** Treat the three questions as a weighted average.
**Why:** They are not substitutes. A CEO who knows what to do but can't execute is producing nothing. A CEO who executes brilliantly in the wrong direction is producing wrong things fast. A CEO who hits the number without doing either is lucky. You need all three. Averaging them hides which of the three is actually broken.

**Don't:** Use "strategy" as a euphemism for "I haven't made the decisions yet."
**Why:** A strategy that is a list of themes or pillars is not a strategy. A strategy is the set of hard decisions about what you will do and what you will not do, assembled into a story that explains why. If the CEO keeps saying "we're still figuring out the strategy" six months in, she is not figuring it out — she is avoiding making the decisions. Avoidance reads as strategy to no one except the person avoiding.

**Don't:** Skip the IC skip-levels.
**Why:** The executives will tell you what they want you to hear. The ICs will tell you the truth. If you want to know whether question two is passing, go three layers down and ask. Every CEO I respect does this regularly, and every CEO who gets blindsided is the one who stopped doing it.

**Don't:** Let "hitting the number" paper over white-box collapse.
**Why:** The two quarters right before a company comes apart usually look fine on the numbers. That's when the warning lights are already red inside and nobody is looking. If the numbers are strong but attrition is rising, strategy clarity is falling, or cross-functional execution is breaking — fix those now. By the time the number breaks, the gap is twice as big and twice as hard to close.
