# CEO Psychology

## When to Use
- The founder's ability to manage their own mind is becoming the ceiling on the company. Strategy and product are not the bottleneck. The person in the chair is.
- The founder is discovering that being a good CEO requires actions that feel wrong — giving negative feedback, firing friends, making unpopular calls — and is asking whether that unnaturalness is a sign of doing it wrong.
- The founder is feeling the 22-out-of-100 mean on the CEO test and thinks their score is uniquely low because nobody told them the mean is 22.
- The founder is running scenarios at 3am and wants to know if that's normal, and what to do with it.

## Fails When
- **There is a concrete decision at hand** — fire the VP, ship the product, draft the layoff. Don't answer the decision with a meditation on psychology. Answer the decision.
- **The founder is in acute meltdown** — load the-struggle first. This topic is about the operating system over time; that topic is about the emergency.
- **Psychology talk is being used to avoid a decision.** "I need to work on my mindset before I fire him" is often a delay tactic dressed as self-improvement. Route to the-hard-correct-decision.

## Core Concept

*By far the most difficult skill I learned as CEO was the ability to manage my own psychology. Organizational design, process design, metrics, hiring and firing were all relatively straightforward skills to master compared to keeping my mind in check. Very few people talk about it. It's like the fight club of management.*

The first rule of the CEO psychological meltdown is don't talk about the psychological meltdown. I lived that rule for years at Loudcloud and Opsware. Lying awake at night running scenarios — if we lose the EDS deal, we have four months of runway; if we lose EDS and Deutsche Telekom, seven weeks; if our stock drops below a dollar we get delisted and our debt covenants trigger — and then getting up, putting on a clean shirt, and walking into the office like everything was fine. It wasn't fine. "Fine" was a foreign country. I thought my job was to be the steady hand, the one person who never showed fear. So I swallowed it all. That was wrong. Not entirely wrong — there are things a CEO cannot share with the company — but wrong enough that it nearly destroyed me.

Here is why managing your psychology is so hard. Being CEO requires a lot of unnatural motion. Boxing trainers teach you that to go backward, you have to pick up your back foot first. It's completely counterintuitive. Doing what feels natural — picking up your front foot — gets you knocked cold. Running a company is the same. *From an evolutionary standpoint, it is natural to do things that make people like you. Yet to be a good CEO, in order to be liked in the long run, you must do many things that will upset people in the short run.* Giving negative feedback feels unnatural. Firing a friend feels unnatural. Making an unpopular decision feels unnatural. You will want to avoid all of them. If you do, the company dies politely.

The foundational unnatural skill is giving feedback constantly — on everything. Every forecast, every plan, every presentation, every comment in a meeting. *Have an opinion on absolutely everything.* Be authentic, come from the right place (wanting them to succeed), don't get personal, don't clown people in front of their peers, tailor your style to the person, be direct but not mean. When you give feedback on everything, two things happen. One: feedback stops being personal. It becomes the operating register of the company, not a crisis event. Two: people get comfortable discussing bad news, because the conversation is already happening constantly. *Bad news travels fast and good news travels slowly* — that is the sign of a high-quality culture.

On top of the unnatural motion, there's the 22-out-of-100 problem. *If CEOs were graded on a curve, the mean on the test would be 22 out of 100.* That is psychologically devastating for straight-A students, and almost every CEO is a straight-A student. At any size, your company will do things so bad you never imagined you'd be associated with that kind of incompetence. Watching people fritter away money, waste each other's time, do sloppy work — that will make you feel sick. And it's your fault. Every problem in the company is your fault. You don't get to blame the prior regime, because you are the prior regime. The specific cruelty of the job is that nobody tells you the mean is 22. So you assume you are uniquely bad, when in fact you are normally bad. That is the job.

Given that pressure, CEOs typically make one of two mistakes. One: they take everything too personally, urgently try to fix every issue, and either terrorize the team or make themselves sick. Two: they take nothing personally enough, rationalize away the problems, feel better while the company turns to crap. The ideal state — *urgent yet not insane, moving aggressively and decisively without feeling emotionally culpable* — is a third state that almost nobody achieves naturally. Getting there requires a specific understanding: your emotions about the company are a systematically biased signal. Not useless. But noise that has to be filtered through reason before it can be trusted. The CEO's perception of reality is darker than actual reality, because nobody takes the losses as hard as the person most responsible. What feels like a death sentence is usually a WFIO — *We're Fucked, It's Over,* pronounced "whiff-ee-yo" — and every company goes through at least two and up to five of them. I went through at least a dozen at Opsware. In all cases, WFIOs feel much worse than they are — especially for the CEO.

And here is the hardest part. My single biggest personal improvement as CEO came on the day I stopped blowing sunshine. My brother-in-law Cartheu was a telephone lineman at AT&T. I was visiting him one day and an executive walked by. I asked if he knew him. *"Yeah, I know Fred. He comes by about once a quarter to blow a little sunshine up my ass."* That sentence hit me like a truck. Because I had been doing the exact same thing to my own company. Projecting positive demeanor. Shielding employees from bad news. I was being Cartheu's Fred. The sunshine approach destroys trust because people can see reality for themselves, prevents smart people from working on hard problems because they don't know the problems exist, and creates a culture where bad news gets buried — which is the exact opposite of what a company needs to survive.

## How to Apply

1. **Make some CEO friends.** Not drinking buddies. Not golf partners. People who have run companies. The knowledge gap between a CEO and a non-CEO is vast enough that non-CEO advice on the hardest decisions is almost never useful. Find someone who has survived the same darkness — not for their advice, because they can't give you a recipe, but for the psychological comfort of knowing someone else has lived through it. Bill Campbell was a huge help to me, and interestingly the useful part wasn't his Intuit success — it was his disastrous run at Go Corporation and his most traumatic days at Intuit, like laying off a third of the company. Failure survived is more valuable than success achieved.

2. **Get it out of your head and onto paper.** When I had to explain to Bill and the rest of my board that as a public company I thought we should sell all our customers, all our revenue, and change our entire business, it was messing with my mind. I wrote a detailed decision memo — the logic, the assumptions, the alternatives. The process of writing separated me from my own psychology and let me make the decision swiftly. Writing forces clarity. The argument in your head goes in circles forever, accumulating emotional weight each lap. On paper the circular reasoning becomes visible. The weak links show. You can see which assumptions are driving fear and which are driving analysis. The act of translating psychology into prose is an act of purification.

3. **Focus on the road, not the wall.** Racecar drivers, first lesson: going around a 200-mph curve, do not look at the wall. Look at the road. If you look at the wall, you drive into it. If you look at the road, you follow the road. Running a company is the same. There are always a thousand things that can sink the ship. If you focus on them, you drive yourself nuts and probably capsize. Focus on where you're going, not what you hope to avoid. This does not mean ignore problems. It means don't let the problems become the center of your mental universe. Turn attention to things you can fix. One problem solved creates a small burst of confidence, which produces better thinking, which produces the next problem solved. The positive spiral is as real as the negative one.

4. **Don't take it personally.** Every CEO makes thousands of mistakes. Evaluating yourself and giving yourself an F doesn't help. You still have to walk into the office tomorrow. The ideal is urgent but not insane — act on the problem, don't demonize yourself about the problem. The mean on the test is 22. You are normally bad. That is the job. Keep going.

5. **Don't quit.** I have seen CEOs try to cope with the stress by drinking heavily, checking out, and quitting. In every case, the CEO has a marvelous rationalization why it was okay for him to punk out. None of them will ever be great CEOs. *Great CEOs face the pain. They deal with the sleepless nights, the cold sweat, and what my friend the great Alfred Chuang calls "the torture."* Whenever I meet a successful CEO, I ask them how they did it. The mediocre ones point to a brilliant strategic move. The great ones, remarkably consistently, say one sentence: *"I didn't quit."*

## Examples

**Situation:** A first-time CEO has been in the seat for eighteen months. She has not slept a full night in six. She tells you she is paralyzed in one-on-ones — she can see her direct reports are underperforming but she cannot bring herself to give the negative feedback. She thinks this means she is not cut out to be a CEO.

**Application:** What you are describing is the foundational unnatural motion. Giving negative feedback is evolutionarily wrong — it is the opposite of the thing your social brain was built to do. Every CEO feels this. The ones who don't feel it are sociopaths, which is a worse problem. The fact that it feels wrong is not a sign you should not be CEO. It is a sign you are doing the job. What you have to do is build the reflex by doing it constantly, on everything, on small things first. Have an opinion on the slide deck. Have an opinion on the forecast. Have an opinion on the email. Give your reaction immediately, authentically, without making it a ritual event. Two things will happen. First — feedback will stop feeling like a crisis, because it will become the operating register. Second — when the big hard feedback comes, it will arrive in a channel that's already been lubricated by a thousand small instances, and it will land cleanly instead of breaking the relationship. You are not the problem. Silence is the problem. Open the channel.

**Result:** Six weeks later she reports that feedback no longer feels like an event. She has also discovered that two of her direct reports are significantly better performers than she thought, because she had been reading their underperformance as a permanent state rather than one they could have corrected if she'd told them.

**Situation:** A CEO writes at 2am. He has been running the same scenario for nine consecutive nights — a specific customer renewal, combined with a specific hire, combined with a specific fundraise. He cannot make the scenario stop. He has not told his co-founder, his board, or his wife how bad it feels. He asks what to do.

**Application:** Three moves, tonight. First — open a document and write the scenario down in full. Not a plan. A description. What is the worst case. What is the best case. What are the three decisions you would make in each. What are you most afraid of. What evidence would change your mind. When you put the loop on paper, the loop stops being a loop, because prose does not permit circular reasoning the way thought does. Second — tomorrow, tell one person who has been in this seat before. Not your wife, who loves you but cannot give you the thing you need. Not a drinking buddy. A CEO who has survived their own version of this. Not for advice. For the knowledge that you are not the only person who has been in the dark room. Third — tomorrow morning at the office, pick the smallest of the three problems and solve it. Not all of it. Just the piece you can actually move today. The positive spiral is as real as the negative spiral. One problem solved creates a small burst of confidence, which produces better thinking, which produces the next problem solved. The point is not to fix everything by Friday. The point is to break the loop.

**Result:** He writes the memo. Sleeps four hours — better than the zero he was running on. Emails a CEO friend in the morning who tells him, in ninety seconds, a story about a nearly identical scenario in her own company that she survived, and the loop releases enough that he can make the three calls he needed to make that day. None of the underlying problems are solved by the end of the week. But he is operating again, which is the prerequisite for solving any of them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Blow sunshine at your team to protect their morale.
**Why:** They can see through it. They always can. You destroy trust, you prevent the best brains in the building from working on the real problems (because they don't know the problems exist), and you create a culture where bad news gets buried — which is the exact opposite of what a company needs to survive. A company that discusses its problems freely and openly can solve them quickly. A company that covers them up frustrates everyone involved, and then loses.

**Don't:** Obey the toxic maxim "don't bring me a problem without bringing me a solution."
**Why:** It kills information flow. An engineer who spots a flaw in how the product is being marketed cannot fix it — that's not her domain. Do you want her to bury the information because she doesn't have the fix? The maxim sounds disciplined and is actually lethal. Treat problems brought to you as gifts. When someone surfaces one, be ecstatic. *Isn't it great we found out before it killed us?* Bad news is kimchi — the deeper you bury it, the hotter it gets.

**Don't:** Cope by drinking heavily, checking out, or fantasizing about quitting.
**Why:** Every CEO who has quit had a beautiful rationalization why it was okay. None of them became great CEOs. The cost of quitting is not that you leave the company. The cost is that you never learn the one skill — staying in the chair through the pain — that the job is actually testing you on. *Great CEOs face the pain.* That's the whole sentence. Find the friend, write the memo, focus on the road, and stay in the chair.

**Don't:** Assume that because the decision feels hard, your analysis must be wrong.
**Why:** The hardness of a decision is not analytical signal. It is social and psychological pressure. Nearly every founder crisis is solved when you separate the analytical question (what is the right call?) from the courage question (do I have the strength to make it?). Most of the time the first question has an obvious answer, and the paralysis is really about the second one. The 22-out-of-100 mean is the mean. If the decision were easy, someone else would already have made it.
