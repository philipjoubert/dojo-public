# Think Different — Storytelling Through Values, Not Features

## When to Use
- You're rebuilding a brand that's been neglected, and everyone on the marketing team is debating taglines.
- You're launching a company and the pitch feels like a spec sheet.
- You're drafting an internal speech to the company about who you are and what you stand for.
- The team is losing faith and you need to remind them — and yourselves — why this company exists.
- Your agency came back with three campaign concepts and all three lead with the product.
- You've just said no to a "We're back" kind of slogan and you're trying to articulate what to do instead.

## Fails When
- Your company doesn't live the values you're writing about. Customers can tell. The gap between claimed values and shipped behavior is the real story, and it's not flattering.
- You're in a pure procurement segment where the buyer's job depends on unit-economics justification. Values still exist there, but they're woven into proof, not leading the ad.
- You reach for the template — honoring "rebels" and "misfits" — as an aesthetic, with no underlying belief about what your company is for. The audience reads that as cosplay and it damages the brand more than doing nothing.

## Core Concept

To me, marketing is about values. This is a very complicated world. It's a very noisy world. And we're not going to get a chance to get people to remember much about us. No company is. So we have to be really clear on what we want them to know about us.

When I came back to Apple in 1997, the brand had clearly suffered from neglect. Apple is one of the half-dozen best brands in the whole world — right up there with Nike, Disney, Coke, Sony. But even a great brand needs investment and caring if it's going to retain its relevance and vitality. And the way to do that is not to talk about speeds and feeds. Not to talk about MIPS and megahertz. Not to talk about why we're better than Windows.

Customers don't care.

The best example of all, and one of the greatest jobs of marketing the universe has ever seen, is Nike. Nike sells a commodity — sneakers. And yet when you think of Nike, you feel something different than a shoe company. In their ads they don't talk about the product. They don't talk about air soles or why they're better than Reebok's air soles. They honor great athletes, and they honor great athletics. That's who they are. That's what they stand for.

The dairy industry spent twenty years telling you milk was good for you. Sales flat. Then they tried "Got Milk" — which doesn't even talk about the product, it talks about the *absence* of the product. Sales went through the roof. That's what great marketing does. It doesn't argue for the product. It changes how you feel about your life with or without it.

So when I got back to Apple, we fired the previous agency — they'd been running a four-year competition between twenty-three agencies to pick one. I blew that up and hired Chiat\Day, the agency I'd worked with on the 1984 ad. We worked for about eight weeks. The question we asked was: *our customers want to know who is Apple, and what is it that we stand for? Where do we fit in this world?*

What we're about isn't making boxes for people to get their jobs done, although we do that well. Apple is about something more than that. Apple at its core, its core value, is that we believe that people with passion can change the world for the better. And that the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do.

So our first brand marketing campaign in years was going to honor those people — Einstein, Gandhi, Edison, Ali, Dylan. The misfits, the rebels, the troublemakers. The round pegs in the square holes. Some of them were living, some were not. But the ones who weren't — if they'd ever used a computer, it would have been a Mac.

The theme of the campaign was *Think Different*.

And notice what it wasn't. It wasn't "Apple has new products." It wasn't "Apple is faster than Windows." It wasn't even "Apple is back." We weren't back yet. The company was months from bankruptcy. We hadn't shipped the iMac. The products that would eventually validate the campaign didn't exist.

But the values existed. And if we could tell people clearly what we believed — *before* the products came back — we could buy ourselves the permission to keep trying. The ad didn't sell a Mac that quarter. It rebuilt the *right to matter*.

### The Framework: Belief → Honor → Absence of Boast

Look at the structure of the best brand storytelling and you'll see it almost always goes the same way:

1. **Start with what you believe.** Not what you do. Not what you sell. What you *believe* about the world and the people in it. "We believe people with passion can change the world." "We honor great athletes." "We believe in craftsmanship." It has to be short, true, and sayable internally without embarrassment. If your team cringes when they say it out loud, it isn't real yet.

2. **Honor someone outside yourself.** The best brand campaigns are not about the brand — they are tributes. Nike honors athletes. Apple honored the rebels and misfits. The milk campaign honored the small domestic moments. The customer sees the honor and recognizes themselves in it. *We're going to honor those people. We're not going to be arrogant about it and say we're one of them. We're going to pay tribute.*

3. **Use absence when you can.** Got Milk doesn't show milk being drunk; it shows the moment you don't have it. Think Different didn't show a single Apple product. Absence creates longing; presence invites comparison. The product becomes *implicit* in the story you're telling about the person.

4. **Keep the continuity across decades.** A brand that reinvents its values every few years has no values — it has rotations. The brands that last find a core belief and deepen it. Nike has honored athletes for forty years. Apple has honored the people who think differently from before 1997 and still does. Continuity is a value.

### Why This Matters for the Company, Not Just the Customer

Think Different was aimed at the world, but it also did internal work. Apple in 1997 was demoralized — best engineers had left, the product line was confused, the brand was damaged. The people still at Apple had forgotten who they were. The ad reminded *them* what they were building for. Every product decision after that — the iMac's translucent plastic, the retail stores, the iPod, the iPhone — got measured against the belief the ad articulated. The marketing didn't just tell the world who Apple was. It told Apple who Apple was. That made every downstream decision sharper.

That's the deeper use of brand storytelling. Externally it earns attention. Internally it aligns the thousand small decisions that actually make or break the products. A company that knows what it believes ships sharper work. A company that doesn't ships features.

## How to Apply

1. **Answer three questions, in order, before you write a word of copy.**
   - What do we believe? (One sentence. No product in the sentence.)
   - Who do we honor? (The customer, the craftsman, the creator — someone outside the brand.)
   - What is our life about *without* this? (The absence frame.)
   If you can't answer these, you're not ready to market. You're ready to describe features.

2. **Say the belief out loud, internally, before you externalize it.** If your execs can't say "we believe X" in a town hall without wincing, the belief isn't real. Fix it internally first. External storytelling that exceeds internal conviction reads as fraud.

3. **Refuse the comparison deck.** When the team brings you "we beat competitor X on 14 of 20 features," send it back. That's the voice of second place. The question isn't whether you beat them on specs. The question is what do you *believe* that they don't.

4. **Honor someone.** The ad should make the viewer feel seen. Nike puts the athlete at the center. Apple put the misfits at the center. The product is implicit, behind the person. If the ad makes the viewer feel small, it's boast. If it makes them feel recognized, it's tribute.

5. **Test the "nothing" version.** If you stripped out the product entirely, would the ad still carry meaning? Great brand ads survive that test. Think Different doesn't show a product. Got Milk shows the absence. The 1984 ad barely mentions the Macintosh. If your ad collapses without the product on screen, it's a feature demo, not a brand story.

6. **Commit for years, not quarters.** The belief doesn't change every season. Deepen it. The executions change — new people to honor, new moments to capture — but the core is stable. A brand that reinvents itself every two years has no brand.

7. **Use it as an internal compass.** After the campaign ships, put the belief on the wall. Refer to it in product reviews. "Does this decision match what we said we believe?" If the product sells out the belief, the belief loses meaning and the brand starts to die. Brand is a promise kept — in the work, not just the copy.

## Examples

**Situation:** Apple 1997. Three months from bankruptcy. Product line confused. Best engineers have left. Brand clearly neglected.
**Application:** Fire the previous agency. Hire Chiat\Day. Spend eight weeks asking "who is Apple, what do we stand for, where do we fit?" Write one sentence: "We believe people with passion can change the world for the better." Build a campaign that honors those people — Einstein, Gandhi, Edison, Ali, Dylan. No Apple product in the ad. No specs. No "we're back." Just the belief and the tribute.
**Result:** The campaign doesn't sell a Mac that quarter. But it re-grants Apple the permission to matter. Over the following decade, every major Apple decision — the iMac design, retail, iPod, iTunes, iPhone — flows from the value the ad articulated. The marketing didn't save Apple. It framed what Apple was rebuilding *toward*, so the products could eventually save the company.

**Situation:** The dairy industry has spent twenty years saying "milk is good for you." Sales flat. Customers have learned to tune it out.
**Application:** Stop arguing the nutritional case. Show the absence. A person at a pivotal moment — peanut butter sandwich, cookie, dry mouth — with no milk. The ad ends with the question. No argument made. No fact stated.
**Result:** Sales climb meaningfully. Not because the spec message got stronger, but because the emotional frame changed. The customer stopped being a patient receiving a health lesson and became a hero in a small domestic story. The product is implicit, present through its absence.

**Situation:** A B2B startup's marketing leads with feature-parity charts against the incumbent. Their product is genuinely better on craft — but charts don't show craft. The sales team keeps losing to the larger competitor.
**Application:** Stop the charts. Answer: what do you actually believe? "We believe customer support shouldn't be an afterthought." "We believe software should feel made, not assembled." Lead the marketing with the belief. Show customers being treated with that care. Let the specs be implicit proof.
**Result:** The conversation shifts. Craft-obsessed prospects self-select. You lose some procurement-only deals you were losing anyway. You keep customers whose values match — and those customers are worth 3x in retention and expansion. The incumbent can't follow you onto the ground of care because they're too big to be careful.

**Situation:** A founder is about to launch a new product. The launch deck leads with the architecture diagram and the benchmark numbers.
**Application:** Rip up the first ten slides. Write one sentence: *what do we believe about the person we're building this for?* Rebuild the deck around that belief. The architecture shows up in slide 30, as proof, not as lead.
**Result:** The launch feels like a company introducing itself, not a feature announcing itself. Press covers it differently — as a perspective, not a product. Users who resonate with the belief self-select in.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Claim values your company doesn't live.
**Why:** Customers catch the gap. If you claim craftsmanship and ship sloppy software, the gap is the story and the story isn't flattering. Marketing can point at real values; it cannot manufacture absent ones.

**Don't:** Honor your own company in the ad.
**Why:** Boasting makes customers defensive. Honoring somebody else — the customer, the craftsman, the athlete, the rebel — makes them feel seen. Nike doesn't put Nike at the center. Apple didn't put Apple at the center. The brand earns standing by giving it away.

**Don't:** Lead with "we're back" or "new era" messaging while the company is still broken.
**Why:** The audience isn't stupid. If the products aren't back, the campaign that says you are back reads as denial. Better to say what you *believe* — which can be true even before the products prove it — and let the products catch up.

**Don't:** Change the belief every campaign cycle.
**Why:** Brands that rotate through taglines every two years are telling the world they don't know what they are. The strongest brands deepen one belief across decades. New executions, same core. Continuity is a value in itself.

**Don't:** Over-explain the belief.
**Why:** "We believe X" is a sentence. If you need three paragraphs to explain what you believe, you don't believe it yet. The test of a real belief is that it fits on a T-shirt and the T-shirt doesn't feel embarrassing.

**Don't:** Hand the brief to the agency with specs and ask them to make it feel emotional.
**Why:** Good agencies can't manufacture belief out of a product brief. Hand them the belief — or work with them to find it — and then let them build the execution. If you can't write the belief yourself, the ads won't land no matter how good the creative shop is.
