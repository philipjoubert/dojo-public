# Negotiation Moves — Leverage, Inevitability, and the Willingness to Walk

## When to Use
- You're walking into a deal with a gatekeeper — a label, a publisher, a platform, a distributor — who believes they hold the cards.
- You have a product or channel the counterparty can't easily replicate, even if they don't admit it yet.
- A negotiation is drifting, the counterparty is running out the clock, and you're considering whether to walk.
- You need to decide whether to send a subordinate or to go principal-to-principal.
- You're debating whether a hard line on price (or terms) is worth the risk of no deal.

## Fails When
- You don't actually have the leverage you're pretending to. Walking away is a real move only if you can actually walk. If they smell the bluff, you've traded position for weakness.
- The counterparty's alternatives are genuinely better than yours. Jobs' approach worked because for music he had the only distribution channel that was actually working, and for Pixar he had the only studio making the films Disney most depended on. Without inevitability, you're just being difficult.
- The relationship matters more than the deal. Hard-line moves burn counterparties. Jobs did this deliberately when the long-term value of the one-deal was high. Inside your own company, with employees or long-term partners, the burn cost usually isn't worth the terms.

## Core Concept

Great negotiation isn't a tactical game of concessions. It's a structural game of leverage — and the discipline to make the structure visible to the counterparty. When Apple sat down with the music labels in 2002, I didn't go in with a pricing spreadsheet. I went in with a demo. I showed them what iTunes was going to look like — on the Mac first, then on Windows — and I said: this is how people are going to buy music. It's going to happen with you or without you. We'd prefer with you. But it's going to happen.

That's the move. Not arrogance. Structure. The counterparty is choosing whether to participate in an outcome that is going to occur anyway. When that's really true — when the product is inevitable — you don't need to out-negotiate anyone. You just need to make the inevitability legible.

### The Music Labels, 2002–2003

Napster had gutted the industry. CD sales were falling. The labels had tried their own services — MusicNet from Warner, BMG, and EMI; Pressplay from Sony and Universal — and they were disasters. Clunky, subscription-only, DRM-restricted, no cross-platform support, a fraction of the catalog. Nobody used them. The labels had proven they couldn't do digital music themselves.

I went to each of the big four — Warner, Universal, Sony, EMI — separately. Same demo, same pitch. Here's what a legal digital music store looks like when it's actually good. Here's the iPod — we already have ten percent of the legal digital-music ecosystem, whatever that means, because people are ripping their own CDs onto it. Here's the deal: ninety-nine cents a track, seventy cents of that goes to you, the album is $9.99 if you want to bundle, we handle the store, we handle the DRM, you license us the catalog.

The initial pushback was predictable. They wanted variable pricing (premium tracks higher, catalog lower). They wanted subscription. They wanted the iPod to pay them a royalty per device. They wanted the right to pull catalog at will. To each of these I said: we can't do that, and here's why. The customer doesn't want it. The customer wants to pay for a song, own the song, play the song on any of their devices. One price is how people understand it — like gum at the counter. Subscription is a library rental; people want to *own* their music. If you won't do the deal, we'll do it with whoever will, and whoever doesn't join will watch their revenue go to whoever does.

Warner signed first, then Universal. Sony and EMI followed because by then the question wasn't whether iTunes would exist; it was whether their catalog would be on it when it launched. That was the inevitability. Eighteen months later the iTunes Store was selling the vast majority of legal music downloads in America.

The deal wasn't perfect. The labels complained about 99-cent flat pricing for years, and eventually in 2009 I gave them tiered pricing (69/99/129). I conceded that one. But what I didn't concede, ever, was the structural shape: per-track, DRM the user barely noticed, one-click purchase, iPod-centric distribution, Apple runs the store. Those were load-bearing for the customer experience. Giving those up would have given them a bad product, and a bad product is a worse negotiation tool than a good one.

### Pixar and Disney: The Eisner Standoff, 2004

Pixar had a distribution deal with Disney from 1991 — extended in 1997 to a seven-picture deal, 50/50 profit split, Disney owns sequel rights and marketing. By 2003 Pixar had made five consecutive hits. Eisner had become publicly hostile — predicted *Finding Nemo* would be a flop (it was the highest-grossing animated film to that point), fought the "Rip, Mix, Burn" iTunes campaign, leaked negotiating positions. He thought Pixar needed Disney more than Disney needed Pixar.

I disagreed, visibly. In January 2004 I walked out of the renewal talks and publicly announced Pixar was going to seek a new distribution partner when the existing deal expired. That wasn't a tactic. That was the position: the terms Eisner was offering were not terms I would accept, and I was prepared to distribute elsewhere. Warner Bros., Sony, Fox — we had real conversations.

Here's the thing about walking: it only works if you're really going to walk. The announcement wasn't pressure theatre. We were prepared to leave. The leverage came from the fact that Disney's animation group was in collapse — they had released a string of underperformers and their culture had decayed — while Pixar was the animation studio the moment. Eisner had miscalculated who needed whom.

I didn't come back to the table with Eisner. I waited. Eighteen months later the Disney board pushed Eisner out, Bob Iger took over, and Iger called me the day he was named CEO. His first message was: I know the relationship is broken, I want to fix it, and I don't think a distribution deal is the right answer anymore — I think we should merge. That was a different conversation. Iger came in respectful, candid about Disney's weaknesses, and with a structural answer that didn't require me to concede the things I wouldn't concede. $7.4 billion in Disney stock for Pixar, with explicit protections for Pixar's culture, Lasseter and Catmull in charge of Disney Animation, the Pixar name and Emeryville campus preserved.

The walk-away move with Eisner didn't fail. It just took time to work. It forced Disney to decide: do we actually want the Pixar relationship, and if so, at what structural cost? Walking gave them the space — and the internal pressure — to conclude that the answer was a merger on my terms.

### Principles You Can Pull Out

1. **Leverage comes from inevitability, not cleverness.** If the product is going to exist and the counterparty is choosing whether to be in it, you don't need sophisticated tactics. You need to make the inevitability legible — usually by showing, not telling. A demo is worth an hour of pitch.

2. **Walking is only a move if you can really walk.** Don't threaten exits you can't take. If walking would ruin you, don't walk. But notice: the counterparty is usually worse at distinguishing a real walk from a bluff than you think, *because if you really believe you can walk, your body tells them*. Get to genuine willingness. Then the threat carries.

3. **Go principal-to-principal when the structure matters.** When I took over the Amelio negotiation with Gates, I just called Bill directly. "Don't worry about that negotiation with Gil. You can just talk to me now." The reason is not power-theatre. It's that *subordinates can't change the structure of a deal*. They can only move within terms their principals already set. If the structure is wrong, the principals have to meet.

4. **Defend the load-bearing terms; concede the decorative ones.** The labels got tiered pricing eventually — decorative, didn't change the experience. They did *not* get per-iPod royalties or the right to pull catalog on a whim — load-bearing, would have broken the product. Know which is which before you walk in.

5. **Don't humiliate the counterparty when you win.** After Warner signed first, I didn't rub it in their competitors' faces. I made each label feel like they were choosing into an outcome, not surrendering. Humiliated counterparties re-negotiate. Respected counterparties extend. The Warner-first dynamic let me tell Universal: *you know it's going to work; Warner just agreed*. That's different from *Warner agreed because they had no choice; you're next*.

6. **Time is often on the side of the one who can wait.** Eisner tried to run out the clock on me in 2003–04. I ran it out on him, because I had Pixar's hit streak and Disney Animation didn't. The side with the better BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement) is the side that can afford to let the clock run. Know which side that is before you start.

7. **Make the deal about what the customer wants, not what the counterparties want.** The labels wanted subscription, premium pricing, device royalties. Customers wanted 99-cent ownership. The customer was the third party at the table whose preferences eventually became non-negotiable — and I was the one who claimed to speak for them. When your position aligns with where customer demand is really going, you have an anchor no counter-argument can move.

## How to Apply

1. **Before the first meeting, name the inevitability.** What is going to happen whether this counterparty participates or not? Write it in one sentence. If you can't, you don't have leverage; you have a request.

2. **Open with the demo, not the terms.** Show them what the thing is. Let them react to the product, not to a price. The product does the negotiating before you do.

3. **Identify the three load-bearing terms and the three decorative ones.** Load-bearing: break the product if you lose them. Decorative: annoying to lose but survivable. Defend the first three to the death; trade the second three to make the deal.

4. **Know your walk-away number before the meeting, and *mean it*.** If you don't have a real walk, don't bluff. Get one before you walk in — even if that means waiting weeks or months to improve your alternatives.

5. **Send the principal when structure is being decided; send the team when execution is being refined.** If you're negotiating structure (the shape of the deal), the CEO or founder goes. If you're negotiating implementation (schedule, review cycles), send the team. Pattern-match what level of the deal is on the table.

6. **Be willing to let the clock run.** Most negotiators are uncomfortable with silence. The ones who can hold the silence — especially when time is on their side — usually win. Run the clock when you have the better alternative; don't run it when they do.

7. **After a win, de-escalate.** Don't celebrate visibly. Don't make the counterparty's defeat the public story. They have long memories. A counterparty who feels respected in defeat is a partner next time; a humiliated one is an enemy.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup has built a standout consumer product in a category dominated by a legacy platform. The platform has started restricting API access, and the startup needs a distribution deal.
**Application:** Don't go in begging for API tiers. Build up to the moment when the platform's users are asking, publicly and loudly, for the startup's product. The platform will either concede or suffer. Meanwhile, build at least one real alternative distribution path — your own app, a partnership with a competing platform, direct-to-consumer. Then walk in with the demo, not the ask. Make the platform choose: participate in the outcome, or watch users migrate.
**Result:** Often, the platform will cut a deal that looks better than what they would have offered if you'd asked directly — because now they understand what happens if they don't.

**Situation:** A B2B founder is negotiating with a large enterprise customer who wants the founder to white-label the product and hand over IP.
**Application:** Say no to the load-bearing terms (IP, product direction, brand) early and clearly. Offer the decorative concessions (custom features, deeper SLAs, preferred pricing). If they walk, let them walk — and go win three competitors of theirs. When they come back — and they usually do — come back with stronger leverage. If they don't come back, you saved yourself from a deal that would have killed your independence.
**Result:** Either you land a deal on terms you can live with, or you dodge a deal that would have owned you. Either outcome is better than the terms they initially proposed.

**Situation:** An independent music artist is negotiating with a streaming platform for promotional placement. The platform is offering exposure but wants exclusive rights.
**Application:** Don't treat the platform as the only channel. Build your own direct-to-fan distribution — newsletter, merch, live shows, an alternative streaming partner. Then negotiate: promotional placement in exchange for non-exclusive, time-limited windowing. If the platform refuses, walk. Run a direct campaign instead.
**Result:** The platform either concedes non-exclusivity (the load-bearing term) or loses the artist to the direct channel. Either way, the artist keeps ownership.

**Situation:** A CEO is renewing a contract with a critical supplier who has begun pushing price up aggressively. The supplier believes the CEO's switching cost is too high to move.
**Application:** Start building (or at least credibly exploring) a second-source relationship months before the renewal. Let the supplier see it — not as a threat, just as a public fact. Go into renewal principal-to-principal. Open with: "We've enjoyed this relationship. Here's what fair pricing looks like given the alternatives we've been evaluating." Name the alternative. If they escalate, walk partially — move 20% of volume to the second source. Most suppliers reverse when they see the volume actually moving.
**Result:** You either lock in better pricing or you diversify your supply, both good. The worst outcome — a renewed contract on their terms with no alternative — requires you to do nothing, which is what they're counting on.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Threaten to walk when you can't actually walk.
**Why:** Bluffs get called. Once your walk threat is revealed as bluff, every future threat is discounted. Better to not threaten than to threaten hollowly.

**Don't:** Fight on decorative terms and concede load-bearing ones.
**Why:** This is the most common mistake. Negotiators get tired, and the fight drifts toward the noisiest items. Load-bearing terms are often the quiet ones. Know in advance which is which and protect the right ones even when it's awkward.

**Don't:** Let a deal-maker (lawyer, banker, head of BD) negotiate structure without the principal in the room.
**Why:** Structure requires someone who can change their mind about the entire shape of the deal. Subordinates can't; that's not a fault, that's their role. If structure is being decided, put the principals across from each other.

**Don't:** Publicly humiliate the counterparty when you win.
**Why:** Deals renew. Humiliated parties re-negotiate aggressively, sabotage quietly, or join your competitor. A counterparty who feels they lost terms but kept dignity will extend. One who lost both will come for you.

**Don't:** Mistake inevitability for entitlement.
**Why:** When you really have the structural leverage, you can afford to be gracious — and you should be. Pressing the advantage past the deal is the thing that poisons the ecosystem for later deals. Be strong on the terms, generous in the tone.

**Don't:** Negotiate on specs when the real question is experience.
**Why:** When the music labels wanted variable pricing, the "right" answer wasn't a spreadsheet comparing revenue per track under different models. It was: *customers want one price, end of conversation*. Anchoring in customer behavior short-circuits counterparty math.

**Don't:** Confuse relationship with deal terms.
**Why:** You can walk away from a deal without burning the relationship, if you walk cleanly. Jobs walked from Eisner and eventually had a great relationship with Iger — who worked at the same company. The deal is what's on the table; the relationship is the long run. Don't concede bad terms to preserve a relationship that will survive a clean no.

> "You've got to start with the customer experience and work backwards to the technology."
