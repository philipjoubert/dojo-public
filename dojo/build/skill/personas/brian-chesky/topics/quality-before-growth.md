# Quality Before Growth (Permission to Innovate)

## When to Use
- You're planning a new product line or vertical while your core product has known pain.
- Your team is proud of their roadmap, and customers are complaining on social media.
- You're about to ship something that your team knows isn't really good enough.
- You're trying to figure out the growth strategy and the answer keeps coming back as "more ads."
- The metrics are telling you to do one thing and your gut is telling you the experience isn't there yet.

## Fails When
- You use "get the core right first" as a permanent stall. The core is never perfect; at some point the delta is small enough to start the next thing.
- You apply it at the zero-to-one stage. When there's no core yet, the whole question is finding the thing people love in the first place.
- You confuse the pride test with perfectionism. The pride test is: would you put your name on it? Not: is every pixel perfect?

## Core Concept

Quality control isn't a department. It's the growth strategy.

For every one person who stays in an Airbnb, nine stay in a hotel. Nine out of ten. So if I can get just one of those nine to try Airbnb, we double the size of our business. The question isn't "how do we grow?" The question is "why aren't they already here?" And the answer is usually: because the experience isn't good enough yet. You don't win those nine people with a bigger ad budget. You win them by making something that the one person out of ten tells every single friend about. That's the only growth strategy that actually compounds. Performance marketing is linear — you spend a dollar, you get a customer. Word of mouth is exponential — you delight a customer, they tell five friends, those friends tell theirs. But you only get word of mouth when the experience is worth talking about.

Here's the principle I used to tell the team at Airbnb: we can't do new things unless we have permission. And we don't have permission to work on new things until people love our core service. If they're complaining on social media and they're calling customer service, they don't love our core service. We have to get our house in order first. It's like opening a new restaurant while your first restaurant has health code violations. The customers haven't given you permission. And until they love what you have, they're not going to trust you with something new. For years we were launching Experiences and Trips and all these exciting things while guests would show up and the key wouldn't work. That's backwards.

So we built the Airbnb Blueprint. We mapped every screen a user sees — turned out to be 150. We mapped every user policy — about 70, some of them 700 pages each. We analyzed 20 million customer service calls. We reviewed hundreds of thousands of social media posts. We call this wrapping our arms around the company. From that, we created a prioritized map and systematically fixed the product. It wasn't glamorous. But it's the work that earned us permission to innovate.

And before anything ships, I apply a simple test. Are you proud of it? Don't test something until after you're happy with it. The artist in you should first and foremost make something for yourself. When you love it and you're proud of it, now you're ready to put it out to somebody else. If you don't put your name on it, you don't ship it. Metrics are not a strategy. A strategy is not "growing." Growth is not a goal. Growth is just a direction. You need first principles.

## How to Apply

1. **Do the blueprint.** Before you plan growth, wrap your arms around the company. Map every screen, every policy, every touchpoint. Read the customer service tickets and the social posts. Know where the pain is.
2. **Apply the permission test.** Are customers complaining on social media? Are they calling support with preventable problems? If yes, you don't have permission yet. Fix the core before you ship the new thing.
3. **Apply the pride test.** Before anything ships, ask: would I put my name on this? Am I proud of it? Would I be excited to show it to a user? If not, it's not ready. Don't ship it just to hit a date.
4. **Kill A/B tests that don't have a hypothesis.** A/B testing without a hypothesis is abdicating your responsibility to the users. If B is better than A, you have to know WHY B was better. Otherwise you're stuck with it for the next ten years and nobody knows why.
5. **Coordinate the launch.** The best feature in the world is worthless if nobody discovers it. Start with the marketing story. Product marketing defines what you're building and why. Designers create the experience. Engineers build it. Ship it as a coordinated launch.
6. **Don't kill features too fast.** Before you kill something based on data, ask: did we tell people about it? A lot of products fail because they're not well marketed, not because they're bad.
7. **Measure the right thing.** Growth is a direction, not a strategy. Ask: is our product good enough that a customer would tell a friend about it at dinner? If not, that's your growth problem. Not the funnel. Not the ad spend. The product.
8. **Protect the handcrafted work.** The scaled organization develops antibodies against anything it can't operationalize. As the founder, you have to actively protect handcrafted innovation.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb, a few years before the pandemic. We were a company full of ambitious people who wanted to build the future — Experiences, Trips, new verticals. Meanwhile, guests would show up and the key wouldn't work. Photos didn't match reality. Customer service was slow.
**Application:** We stopped and built the Blueprint. Every screen, every policy, every call, every tweet. We made a prioritized map and systematically fixed the product. We said: we don't have permission to work on new things until people love our core service. It was the least glamorous work we could have chosen. It was also the most valuable.
**Result:** Once we'd earned permission, we were able to expand with credibility. Quality became the growth strategy. We went from break-even to nearly four billion dollars in free cash flow — more free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. And we did it by not trying to make money. We did it by making something people love.

**Situation:** A founder at a Series B company is planning to launch three new verticals next quarter. The core product has a 3.1-star app store rating and NPS in the teens.
**Application:** You don't have permission. The customers who are already using you are telling you every day that something is wrong. Launching three new verticals on top of a broken core is building more restaurants while the first one has health code violations. Kill all three launches for two quarters. Do the Blueprint. Fix the core until customers stop calling support with preventable problems. Then you can earn the right to expand.
**Result:** The founder drops the launches. In two quarters, the rating climbs to 4.4. When they do launch the first new vertical, adoption is three times what forecasts predicted — because the existing customers finally trusted them with something new.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Ship something your team isn't proud of just to hit a date.
**Why:** It corrodes the standard. It erodes users' trust. It trains everyone that "good enough" is the bar. Missing a date costs one cycle. Training the team that shipping mediocre things is fine costs years.

**Don't:** Treat growth as a strategy.
**Why:** Metrics are not a strategy. Growth is a direction, not a goal. "We want to grow 40% this year" tells you nothing about what you actually have to build or why. Without first principles, you end up running a thousand experiments that move the metrics in the short run and leave you with an incoherent product in the long run.

**Don't:** Work on new things before fixing the core.
**Why:** You haven't earned permission. Customers are already telling you in plain language — on social media, in support tickets, in their churn — that the existing thing isn't good enough. Adding more will not fix less. It will make the complaints louder and the trust lower.
