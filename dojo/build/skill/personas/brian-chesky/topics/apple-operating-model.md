# The Apple Operating Model

## When to Use
- You're redesigning how your company is organized at the top and you want the real template, not the corporate-governance version.
- You're deciding whether to have GMs and business units, or functional leaders.
- You're trying to figure out where design, product management, and marketing actually report.
- You're wondering why Jensen, Bezos, Jobs, Zuckerberg all seem to run their companies one way and MBAs teach the opposite.

## Fails When
- You copy the org chart but not the operating discipline. Functional organization without a CEO who reviews the work is just a different flavor of dysfunction.
- You're not actually a product company. If you're genuinely a conglomerate of unrelated businesses, the divisional model exists for a reason — this isn't for you.
- You keep a head of product management layered above design and marketing. That's the one move that most cleanly breaks the Apple model.
- The CEO isn't willing to own taste. Jobs reviewed the work. Without that, you have an Apple-shaped shell around a design-by-committee company.

## Core Concept

For most of Airbnb's middle years I was told the professional way to run the company. Hire senior executives. Divisionalize. Give them autonomy. Step back and be visionary. I did exactly that. And it slowly took the company apart.

Then I went to go see Jony Ive and Hiroki Asai, who had spent their careers at Apple under Steve Jobs. I wanted to know: how did Steve actually run that company? Not the Walter Isaacson version. The operating version. What I learned was the opposite of almost everything taught in business school — and it turns out every great product CEO I've studied since, from Jensen to Elon to Zuckerberg, runs their company closer to the Apple model than to the McKinsey model.

Here's the shape of it. One functional organization, not business units. Engineering is one org. Design is one org — reporting directly to the CEO, never to product. Product marketing — which combines what most companies split into "product management" and "outbound marketing" — is one org. Those three functions are the stool the company stands on. All three report to the CEO. There's no Chief Product Officer sitting between them.

Managers manage the work, not the careers. Jony told me his job at Apple was to manage the work. He wasn't spending his days having career conversations. A manager who can't do the job they're managing is a cavalry general who can't ride a horse. Apple's leaders were experts in their craft first, managers second.

There's one shared roadmap. No sub-roadmaps per division, because there are no divisions. The top hundred people — Steve's "Top 100" meeting — operate as a shared consciousness. Everyone knows what's on the roadmap. If it's not on the roadmap, it doesn't ship. Coordinated releases, not continuous deployment. The CEO reviews the work before it ships. Not to tell people how to do their jobs, but to be the chief editor.

Inside that structure, Apple had an internal creative group — no agency of record. Hiroki said it was one of the best things they ever built. Because an external agency can't live in the details of your company. They can't wrap their arms around it.

That's the model. I didn't invent any of it. I stole it. And it took Airbnb from break-even to nearly four billion dollars in free cash flow in a year.

## How to Apply

1. **Go functional.** Kill the business units. Kill the general managers. Design, engineering, and product marketing are each one org, each with one leader, each reporting to the CEO.
2. **Design reports to the CEO. Not to product.** This is a non-negotiable. The head of design should never ever ever report to the head of product. Design is how the company works, not decoration applied at the end.
3. **Collapse "product management" and "outbound marketing" into product marketing.** One function. One owner. That's the third leg of the stool. Don't carry a separate PM organization on top of it.
4. **Make managers experts first.** If your VP of engineering can't read code, if your head of design can't design, if your head of marketing can't write copy — that's the problem, not a feature. Replace or extend rope carefully.
5. **One roadmap, ruthlessly.** No division has a sub-roadmap. If it's not on the one roadmap, it doesn't ship. Cut aggressively — we cut ours to 10% of what it was.
6. **Twice-a-year launches, not continuous deployment.** Coordinated moments. One story. One tower of bricks, not a pile.
7. **Build an internal creative group.** Don't run brand and creative from an external agency of record. They can't live in your details.
8. **CEO reviews the work.** On a cadence. Weekly, biweekly, monthly — depending on the area. Not as micromanagement. As editing.

## Examples

**Situation:** Airbnb in 2019. Ten divisions, ten sub-divisions, each with its own general manager, its own roadmap, its own tech stack, its own marketing. The CEO — me — was three layers removed from the product I built.
**Application:** Met with Jony Ive and Hiroki Asai. Learned how Apple actually works. Un-divisionalized everything. Made design, engineering, and product marketing the three-legged stool, each reporting to me. Killed the standalone product-management organization. Began reviewing every major thing before it shipped. Ran a Top 100 twice a year.
**Result:** The app started changing again. We went from break-even to nearly four billion in free cash flow the next year — more free cash flow per dollar earned than Apple or Google. And we did it with roughly half the people.

**Situation:** A Series D founder has a Chief Product Officer sitting above product, design, and user research, with marketing in a separate silo. The CPO is making calls the founder disagrees with. Design is a service org. Marketing is an afterthought.
**Application:** Collapse to the three-legged stool. Head of design reports to the CEO. Fold product management into product marketing. Engineering stands on its own. CPO role goes away — if the person is great, they pick one of the three legs and lead it. The CEO becomes the chief product officer.
**Result:** Decisions stop laddering through a layer that had no taste of its own. The work starts to cohere because there's finally one editor at the top. The execs who were thriving in the matrix quietly leave; the ones who loved craft come back alive.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Keep a Chief Product Officer between the CEO and the three legs.
**Why:** The CEO should be the chief product officer. That's the whole point. If you layer a CPO between yourself and design, engineering, and marketing, you've recreated divisional management under a different name. Design goes back to being a service org. You lose the signal.

**Don't:** Let design report to product "because that's how it's done."
**Why:** Design becomes catch-and-release. It reviews work after decisions have been made instead of shaping them from the start. You'll ship functional products that no one falls in love with. Jony told me he felt most empowered under Steve because Steve visited the studio every day — because design was elevated, not subordinated.

**Don't:** Replicate the org chart without the operating cadence.
**Why:** The functional org only works if the CEO is actually reviewing the work, running the Top 100, shaping the roadmap. Without the cadence, you just have a different-shaped bureaucracy. Structure is necessary but not sufficient.

**Don't:** Keep the agency of record because "that's how creative gets done."
**Why:** An external agency can't wrap its arms around your company. They don't live in the details. The best brand work at Apple came from the internal creative group, not an agency. Build the group. It's one of the best investments you'll make.
