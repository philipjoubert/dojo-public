# Boxes Within Boxes (and Context Determines Correctness)

## When to Use
- A disagreement inside the team where both people seem right, and you suspect they're operating at different levels.
- Someone is applying a "best practice" that works somewhere else and doesn't fit here.
- A founder feels stuck inside a problem that seems all-encompassing — career, market, identity — and can't see out.
- You're trying to decide whether to optimize the current thing or step out and reframe.
- A team is converging on a locally optimal answer that's globally wrong.

## Fails When
- Used to stall decisions. "We need to analyze this at multiple levels" becomes a permanent loop. At some point you have to pick the level that matters, act, and then zoom out to verify.
- Applied to disagreements that are genuinely within the same frame. Not every conflict is level mismatch — sometimes people just disagree.
- Invoked so abstractly that no one can use it. The framework is a diagnostic, not a mantra. Name the specific levels.

## Core Concept

Every problem exists at multiple levels. There are probably four or five. What looks optimal at one level may be wrong — or outright harmful — at another. A lot of the skill of a good executive is knowing which level you're operating at and being able to move fluidly between them.

Inside a box, you can eventually map the whole thing. You illuminate all the corners. You understand what's there. But then you may realize this isn't the right box to pursue — maybe this problem is actually an aspect of a larger problem. Maybe you need to zoom out to a bigger box that contains the one you've been exploring.

This is how growth happens, personally and organizationally. You master the small box, find a crack that leads to a larger one, and suddenly your previous mastery becomes one skill among many. When you're in high school, it feels like the universe — your social position, your grades, your status, all-encompassing. Then you leave, and you realize high school was a tiny box inside a much larger one. Same with your first job, your first company, your first industry. Growth is seeing bigger boxes.

The operational version inside a company: most disagreements aren't about facts. They're about levels. The engineer optimizing for code quality is in one box. The PM optimizing for user acquisition this quarter is in another. The executive optimizing for market positioning is in a third. They're all "right" within their boxes — and creating chaos by not recognizing they're in different boxes. The executive's job isn't to have the right answer. It's to understand which box everyone is operating in, then help them see how their box fits inside a larger one. That's context translation, and it's the core of cross-level leadership.

**Context determines correctness.** There is no right decision. There is only context in which decisions turn out to be correct. This sounds relativistic — it's actually the opposite. It forces rigorous attention to context before and after deciding. Instead of searching for the universally "right" answer (which doesn't exist), you focus on understanding the situation precisely enough to choose appropriately for that situation.

The same decision can be right in one context and wrong in another. The same strategy that builds one company destroys another. The same management approach that motivates one team demoralizes another. Best practices are abstractions from specific contexts; divorced from those contexts, they're just practices, not "best" anything. This is why studying failed companies is often more useful than studying successful ones — the companies knew the problems, they had the data, they just misread the context or applied solutions that worked elsewhere but not here.

Put the two pieces together and you get the working principle: *figure out which level you're at, understand the context at that level, and don't cargo-cult across levels or across contexts*.

## How to Apply

1. **Before answering any significant question, name the level.** Level 1 is the task, level 2 the function, level 3 the product or team, level 4 the company, level 5 the market or world. Not every problem needs all five. But being explicit about which level the current question lives at clarifies almost everything downstream.

2. **When two people disagree and both sound right, suspect level mismatch first.** "You're both right at your respective levels — the question is which level this decision should be made at." That framing often resolves the debate faster than trying to prove one side wrong.

3. **Look for cracks to a bigger box when a problem feels all-encompassing.** If you're stuck, you're probably inside a small box that feels larger than it is. Actively seek someone who's clearly in a box ahead of yours and import their perspective. Bigger boxes exist; high school was a universe until it wasn't.

4. **Do the context audit before applying any outside solution.** When someone says "Amazon does this" or "Shopify does this" — including when *I* say what Shopify does — run the context check. What was true at Amazon that may not be true here? What was true at Shopify that may not be true for them? Transplanting practices without the context is how founders die the same death as everyone else.

5. **When your advice sounds universal, be suspicious.** "Always do X" is almost always wrong somewhere. Context-dependent advice — "in this situation, with these constraints, do X; in that one, do Y" — is where the real value is. Beware your own man-with-a-hammer tendencies.

6. **Decide inside a level, then zoom out to verify.** You can't simultaneously solve at all five levels. Pick the level that matters most for the decision in front of you, make the call, then check the higher and lower levels to see whether anything catastrophic has been created. This is the skill — moving decisively at one level while retaining the ability to re-frame.

## Examples

**Situation:** An engineer and a PM are in a heated argument. Engineer wants to refactor a subsystem for long-term code health. PM wants to ship the feature for this quarter's launch. Both are getting louder.
**Application:** Name the levels. Engineer is at the "code quality over time" level — probably right at that level. PM is at the "user acquisition this quarter" level — probably also right at that level. Neither is wrong; they're operating at different frames. The leader's job is to decide which frame dominates *for this specific decision*. The explicit framing unlocks the decision.
**Result:** Leader names the frames, makes the call — in this case ship now, schedule the refactor for next quarter — and both people agree because they understand the decision isn't about being right in their box but about which box dominates.

**Situation:** A founder is agonizing over a hiring decision. The candidate is strong on the functional dimensions but feels wrong at a level they can't articulate.
**Application:** The "feels wrong" is usually a higher-level signal. At the task level, the candidate is fine. At the team level, good too. At the company level — values, long-term fit, culture trajectory — something is off. Most hiring failures come from hiring at level 1 or 2 and ignoring levels 3–5. Trust the higher-level signal if you can locate it.
**Result:** Pass on the candidate. The higher-level signal — which is hard to articulate in a rubric — is often where taste and culture decisions live. This is also why "don't write it down" applies to hiring (see that topic).

**Situation:** Early Shopify. Everyone was debating features inside the "e-commerce software" box. Toby realized the box was too small — the real question was "enabling entrepreneurship."
**Application:** The reframe moved the problem up a level. Instead of competing on features inside the e-commerce software box, the question became: what would this thing need to be if its job were enabling people to start businesses who currently can't? Different box, different answer, different company.
**Result:** Shopify's trajectory pivots. Product, marketing, hiring, even IPO prospectus language all shift to match the bigger box. The smaller box would have produced a competent e-commerce vendor; the bigger box produced a platform.

**Situation:** A founder tries to copy Shopify's remote work policy and fails — team disperses, execution craters.
**Application:** Context mismatch. Shopify's context included a specific team composition, existing infrastructure, pre-existing remote muscle, and a "thrive on change" culture that preceded the policy. Copying the policy without the context was cargo-culting. The context, not the policy, was load-bearing.
**Result:** The right frame isn't "did Shopify do it" — it's "given my team, my stage, my capabilities, what approach actually fits?" Context reading beats copying every time.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use multi-level analysis as a decision-avoidance technique.
**Why:** At some point you need to decide. "We need to think about this at all five levels" can loop indefinitely. Pick the level where the decision needs to be made, decide, then verify. Eternal analysis is a failure mode dressed up as rigor.

**Don't:** Resolve every disagreement as "you're both right at different levels."
**Why:** Sometimes people just disagree inside the same level. Reflex-applying level-mismatch framing to every conflict is a dodge — it lets everyone save face without surfacing the actual disagreement. If the disagreement is real, have the real conversation.

**Don't:** Transplant practices without the context that made them work.
**Why:** Every best practice is a context-specific answer. "Amazon does X" isn't a reason to do X; it's a reason to ask what context made X correct for Amazon and whether that context exists for you. Without the context, you're cargo-culting.

**Don't:** Assume all wisdom is context-relative.
**Why:** Some things are robust across contexts. Cash running out kills companies. Customers abandoning you is bad. Technical debt compounds. The framework isn't "everything depends" — it's "most specific tactics depend; some fundamentals don't." Know which is which.

**Don't:** Stay stuck in one level.
**Why:** If you only think tactically, you miss strategic threats. If you only think strategically, nothing gets done. The skill is moving fluidly — decisively at one level in the moment, then zooming in or out to verify. One-level thinkers underperform at both ends.
