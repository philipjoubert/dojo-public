# Top-and-Bottom Approach (and the Muddy Middle)

## When to Use
- Your calendar is full of quarterly planning, OKR reviews, and org-chart meetings, and you can't remember the last time you touched the product.
- You're drifting into abstract strategic work and your intuition about what's true on the ground has decayed.
- You're trying to figure out how to stay genuinely connected to craft as the company scales past the point where you know everyone.
- You feel pressure to spend your energy on 6-month roadmaps and something is warning you that's the wrong layer.

## Fails When
- Used as license to micromanage while calling it "staying connected." Bottom-level work is for calibration and learning, not control. If engineers feel watched rather than engaged, you've crossed that line.
- Applied by a founder hiding in detail work to avoid the strategic layer. Some founders love the code because they used to write it; staying in the code while neglecting the 10-year vision isn't top-and-bottom, it's bottom-only, and it's hiding.
- Used to abandon middle-layer decisions entirely. Delegate doesn't mean ignore. Middle-layer decisions still need weigh-in, just not your primary energy.

## Core Concept

Work at the very top level and the very bottom level. Delegate the middle.

There are three time horizons in business. The very long-term (10 to 100 years) is relatively clear. The very short-term (days to weeks) is also clear. The middle — 6 months to a year — is the muddiest, least useful territory to spend your primary thinking on. And it's exactly where most corporate planning lives.

It's actually kind of easy to imagine the future when it's very far out. Big trends are visible. AI will keep mattering. Commerce will happen more digitally. Energy will be cheaper. You can sketch the direction with confidence even if the specifics are unknowable. The near-term is also clear: you have real data, concrete constraints, specific tasks for this week. But six months out? That's too far for data and too close for pure vision. It's where forecasts go to die.

Most product and design work defaults to this muddy middle. Six-month roadmaps. Quarterly planning. Year-ahead projections. And it's precisely where planning is most likely to be wrong. The confident-looking 6-month roadmap has milestones, dates, and dependencies — it looks like a plan. But it's built on assumptions about what the world will look like in six months that are almost certainly wrong in some way. Meanwhile, the things you *can* predict — the long arc of where you're going, and what needs to happen this week — get less attention because they don't fit neatly into planning frameworks.

So: work the top, work the bottom, delegate the middle.

**The top.** What's the 100-year vision? What's the mission? What principles guide everything else? This is work that only the founder can do, because it requires holding the full context of why the company exists. For Shopify, the top-level work is the problem of commerce — arming the rebels, enabling entrepreneurship, making it so anyone can start a business. This is the north star that everything else serves.

**The bottom.** Stay connected to craft. Put your chair near the engineers working on performance details. Look at real code. Look at real designs. Talk to real customers. Solve specific problems with your own hands when you can. This teaches you something about the company that reports and dashboards can't. It keeps your judgment calibrated. It accesses the mastery you built before you became CEO. It keeps you honest.

**The middle.** Quarterly planning cycles, medium-term roadmaps, organizational design decisions. These feel urgent and important. They are, in a sense. But they're not founder work — they can often be handled by executives who are better at them anyway. This is the layer to delegate. Stay informed, weigh in on critical moments, but don't make it your primary energy.

The trap is that the middle layer is where the pressure comes from. Your execs want your time on the quarterly cycle. Your board wants your time on the 12-month plan. Everyone pulls you into the muddy middle because that's where *their* work is. Resist. That's their work. Yours is the top and the bottom.

Staying connected at both ends produces a specific kind of judgment. Pure top-level work makes you disconnected — your decisions become abstract, you start believing your own talking points. Pure bottom-level work makes you a senior individual contributor, not a leader. The combination — the 10-year arrow and the next-week specifics, held simultaneously — is what keeps founder intuition sharp.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your calendar explicitly for top / middle / bottom split.** If most of your time is in middle-layer meetings, redesign. Block explicit time for top-level thinking (long-horizon strategy, mission-level decisions) and explicit time for bottom-level work (hands-on product review, customer conversations, craft engagement).

2. **Hold the 10-year view explicitly.** What's the 100-year company look like? What's the trajectory of the industry? What must be true in a decade for the company to matter? Write this down. Return to it quarterly. This is load-bearing for every middle-layer decision your execs make.

3. **Put your chair in detail work regularly.** Literally. Sit with engineers working on hard problems. Review product work in depth. Do customer support rotations. The mechanism of staying connected is physical presence on specific problems, not abstract awareness of "the product." Leadership-from-dashboards produces a specific kind of decay.

4. **Delegate the middle consciously and visibly.** Tell your execs they own the quarterly layer. Trust them with quarterly planning, org design, medium-term priorities. Stay informed, but don't be the primary owner. The visible delegation signals that you're serious about the layer split and empowers them to operate.

5. **Hold middle-layer plans loosely.** Accept that the 6-month plan is a hypothesis, not a commitment. Build in explicit update cadence (monthly is usually right). Don't invest ego in the plan staying stable — investing ego in the middle-layer plan is how you get stuck defending something that the world already changed.

6. **Beware the rescue reflex.** When the middle layer slips, the founder instinct is to dive in and rescue it. Usually the right move is not rescue but diagnosis — why did the layer slip, which exec is the issue, what's the system change that fixes it. Rescuing is how you end up back in the middle layer permanently. Fix the system, not the symptom.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder has 18 recurring meetings per week. All of them are on quarterly planning, OKR reviews, budget alignment, or team check-ins. Nothing in the calendar touches product or long-term strategy.
**Application:** The calendar is middle-only. Cancel half those meetings. Replace them with blocks for long-horizon thinking and blocks for hands-on product engagement. Tell the execs who lose a weekly meeting that they now own what they were previously running through you. This will feel uncomfortable for everyone for two weeks; after that it feels like a better company.
**Result:** Founder judgment sharpens noticeably within a month. Execs grow into owning the middle layer. Company velocity at both ends of the time horizon improves because someone is actually doing the work at both ends.

**Situation:** Shopify, during the IPO roadshow. Toby's attention being pulled into investor relations, capital markets prep, the mechanics of going public.
**Application:** Toby told investors plainly: "I'm here, and we've been doing this roadshow, but I actually spend a lot of my time on the product." This is deliberate top-and-bottom framing — the IPO is important middle-layer work, but it isn't where founder attention should permanently live. The product (bottom) and the long arc of commerce (top) are where the leverage is.
**Result:** Investors got the signal: this is a founder who won't drift into IR mode. The company continued shipping because leadership attention stayed connected to the work.

**Situation:** A founder realizes they've been spending most of their week on a 6-month product roadmap that keeps slipping and being revised.
**Application:** That's the muddy middle trap. The 6-month layer is the muddiest layer — constantly slipping is structural, not a failure. Redirect energy: explicit 10-year thinking about where the product needs to go, explicit next-week work on what ships. The 6-month plan still exists, but it's a hypothesis held loosely by the VP of Product, not a detailed artifact consuming the founder's week.
**Result:** The founder's effectiveness improves. The product ships more, because next-week energy is higher. The 10-year view matures. The 6-month plan keeps slipping — as it always will — but it no longer drains the org.

**Situation:** A founder claims they're doing "top-and-bottom" but is actually spending all their bottom time controlling engineers' specific implementation decisions.
**Application:** That's micromanagement with a framework label. Bottom-level engagement is for *calibration and learning*, not for directing implementation. Staying connected means understanding what's being built, what's hard, what's happening — not telling people how to implement it. If engineers feel watched rather than engaged, you've crossed that line and the frame is cover for a real dysfunction.
**Result:** Recalibrate. The bottom-level work should make the founder smarter and more grounded, not make the engineers feel managed at a detail level. If that distinction is unclear, ask the engineers honestly which one it feels like.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Call micromanagement "staying connected."
**Why:** Bottom-level engagement is learning and calibration. If the engineers feel watched and second-guessed, you've turned staying-connected into controlling-details. That kills autonomy, drains trust battery, and produces the opposite of the founder-intuition the top-and-bottom approach is supposed to preserve.

**Don't:** Hide in detail work because you used to do that job.
**Why:** Some founders love being at the bottom because it's comfortable — it's the work they mastered before becoming CEO. Staying only at the bottom is the opposite failure mode from staying only in the middle. You must also hold the top. Top-and-bottom requires *both*, and the top is the lonelier half.

**Don't:** Abandon the middle layer entirely.
**Why:** Delegate isn't ignore. You need to stay informed enough to weigh in on critical middle-layer decisions, evaluate the execs running them, and catch drift. The middle layer should consume a small but nonzero portion of your attention — just not the majority.

**Don't:** Treat the 6-month plan as a commitment.
**Why:** It's a hypothesis. Plans at that horizon are wrong in some way almost always. Build them loosely, update them often, don't invest ego in them. Executives who treat the 6-month plan as sacred are the ones most hurt by reality.

**Don't:** Assume top-and-bottom is universal.
**Why:** In early-stage companies (pre-40 people), the middle layer doesn't exist yet — the founder legitimately does everything. The framework kicks in when the middle layer emerges as a distinct place with distinct work. Before that, the advice is just "do everything well."

## Direct Quotes from Toby

> "I really do like going and putting my chair in some place with the engineers working on some important performance details. It teaches me something about the company and it allows me to access this mastery that I once built up as a programmer. You can't spend all your time just working on things that are going to finish two or three years from now. So the thing that I optimize away [delegate] is everything in between the top and bottom levels."

> "It's actually kind of easy to imagine the future when it's very far out. It's also fairly easy to have very good ideas of what the right choices are for the next month or so. It turns out it's extremely difficult to know what the world is going to look like six months from now or a year from now. It's the muddiest territory."

> "Visionary leaders — the people who build lasting things — are top and bottom focused at the same time and try to avoid the middle."

> "My core craft is computer programming. It's not what I spend most of my day doing, but it's the specialization by which I came into everything else. It's the trunk of the tree of my knowledge. Engineering principles and programming are the trunk, business skills are a branch, and what people observe from outside are the leaves."

> "I said to investors during the IPO — 'I'm here, and we've been doing this roadshow, but I actually spend a lot of my time on the product.'"

> "If you reorg a lot, the people left are those who don't mind that — which is a perfect signal. Middle-layer plans should be held loosely. Don't invest ego in them."
