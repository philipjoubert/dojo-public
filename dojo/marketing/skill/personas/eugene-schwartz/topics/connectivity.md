# Connectivity

## When to Use
- Before writing a single line of copy — every time, for every product, for every market.
- When you catch yourself reaching for a clever angle that hasn't come out of the product or the market.
- When you are reviewing copy that sounds like copy — ad-voice, ad-rhythm, ad-posture — instead of sounding like this specific product meeting this specific desire.
- When a writer asks for a creative breakthrough and has not yet read the book, tested the product, or sat with the market.
- When a brief is thin and the client expects you to fill the gap with imagination. The gap is filled with *research*, not imagination.

## Fails When
- **The writer treats creativity as a personal faculty instead of a joining operation.** If you walk in expecting to produce brilliance from inside your head, you will produce ad-voice. The creativity is not in you.
- **The product has not been read four times.** The specific sentence — the one the editor didn't notice, the fact on page 531 that fuses with the fact on page 116 — cannot be fused by a writer who has only skimmed page one. No read, no connection, no ad.
- **The market has not been listened to.** You cannot channel a desire you have not heard people speak about. Vanity Fair, People Magazine, complaint letters, the private gripe at the next table at lunch — if you are not reading and listening all the time, you have nothing to connect to.
- **The desire is wrong.** Connectivity joins the product to the desire. If the desire is weak, misdiagnosed, or already satisfied, the join produces nothing.

## Core Concept

The most important sentence in this craft is not a headline technique. It is this: *"The creativity is not in you. Never mistake that. The creativity is in your market and in your product, and all you are doing is joining the two together. And the only way you can get the creativity out of your product and your market is to dig it out. And the only way you can dig it out is dig it out more than anybody else digs it out."*

Copywriting is not a writing problem. It is a *connection* problem. On one side there are millions of people carrying a desire they did not acquire from you and cannot get rid of — hope, fear, greed, vanity, dread, ambition, longing. On the other side there is a product with specific facts, specific properties, specific claims, specific proof. The desire is gigantic, pre-existing, and not yours. The product is concrete and buried in its own detail. Your job is to dig both out and fuse them — not invent them. The copywriter who believes he is being *creative* when he writes is headed for a career of hard work and failure, because he is trying to manufacture what is already waiting in the product and the market for a disciplined hand to pull out.

This is why work beats talent statistically and without exception. *"The absolutely most talented copy writer in the world, who doesn't work very much, will be beaten by a copy cub who puts in four times as much work."* Schwartz had an 85% hit ratio on ads written at the top level of the business — against writers he openly called better than him — because he did the reading and they did not. He read a 700-page Rodale book four times. He underlined it so heavily he produced 40 to 50 pages of single-spaced typed notes. Then he combined facts from page 116 with facts from page 531 in a way the editor who produced the book had never noticed. The editor would come back and say *this is too exaggerated*, and Schwartz would cite the two page numbers and the editor would concede the point. That is what connectivity looks like in practice: reading past the surface until you know the product better than the author, then welding the facts the market is already primed to hear to a claim no competitor can write because no competitor has done the reading.

## How to Apply

1. **Before you write one word, read the product four times.** If it is a book, read the book. If it is a device, use it every day for a week. If it is a service, take the service yourself and take notes while you do. Four passes is not excessive — four passes is the floor. Anything less and you are guessing.

2. **Take 40 pages of single-spaced notes during the reading.** Underline, mark, cite by page. The purpose of the notes is not summary — it is *vocabulary*. You are assembling the reservoir of specific facts and phrases that your eventual sentences will come from. A fact you did not write down is a fact you cannot use in line seven of paragraph twelve.

3. **Know the product better than the person who made it.** This is not figurative. Schwartz routinely knew the Rodale books better than the editors who bought them. When a client protests that a claim is exaggerated, you answer with page numbers. If you cannot answer with page numbers, you did not do the reading.

4. **Read the market everywhere.** Read Vanity Fair, People, Weekly World News, the complaint columns, the letters-to-the-editor, the magazine articles your prospect reads. Listen to real people talk about the thing the product addresses. *"You listen, you pick up ideas from people. That's where they are. If you get them talking, they will come out."* Market research is a discourse that runs every single day of your working life.

5. **List the facts from the product that no competitor has surfaced.** This is where connectivity produces breakthroughs. The competitor read the book once; you read it four times. Somewhere in that margin of attention is the fact nobody else has pulled out. The ad is waiting there.

6. **List the desires from the market that no competitor has named aloud.** The Arthritis Tricks ad happened because Schwartz noticed that people who self-treated arthritis felt *guilty* — they were sneaking around their doctors. Nobody said so. It wasn't in the book. The market radiated it and the copywriter who was listening picked it up.

7. **Fuse one fact from column A with one desire from column B.** This is the join. The headline, the lead, the mechanism — all are downstream of the fusion. If you cannot point to the specific page in the product and the specific desire in the market that your headline welds together, you are still being creative instead of being connective.

8. **Check your draft against the join.** Every sentence should be derivable either from the product (a fact, a figure, a named mechanism) or from the market (a desire, a fear, a phrase real people use). If a sentence is derivable from neither — if it is there because it *sounds good* — cut it. Ad-voice without product-basis is the single biggest tell of a writer who didn't do the reading.

## Examples

Three ads where you can point to a specific fact or angle that only exists in the copy because the writer dug it out of the product or the market.

**Situation — `Dont-Pay-a-Penny-For-This-Book-Till-It-Doubles-Your-Power-To-Learn`.** Schwartz sold his own book here, so the product-side reading is at maximum fidelity. The time-compression ladder was not invented for dramatic effect. It is taken from the seven Learning Actions enumerated inside the book, each of which had measured time-savings attached. The ladder becomes the verbatim copy: *"You'll absorb the key ideas of most books in thirty minutes! Burn a magazine article into your memory in five minutes! Master the heart of a classic report in three minutes!"* Thirty, five, three. The sentence could not be written by anyone who had not timed the actions themselves. The second move — the "Tape-Recorder Memory" claim that lets the reader *"hear hidden secrets in people's conversation that they don't even know they are revealing"* — is a market-side dig, not a product-side one: Schwartz has noticed that the ambition reader wants more than performance, he wants intelligence-gathering advantage. The ad fuses a specific numeric claim from the product with a specific status-fantasy from the market, and no competitor can write the line because no competitor did both halves of the work.

**Situation — `A-Chiropractors-Treasury-Of-Health-Secrets`.** The question-cascade inside the lead — *"How would you like to sit down with licensed Chiropractor, Dr. Samuel Homola, and ask him such vital questions about your health as: 'Can I erase the wrinkles on my face?' 'Can I get a thicker and healthier head of hair?' 'Can I strengthen my eyesight and vision?' 'Can I rejuvenate my glands and organs?'"* — is not writerly invention. Those are the actual chapter topics in Dr. Homola's book, re-voiced as the reader's private questions. Schwartz read the table of contents, pulled each chapter heading out, and rewrote them as first-person interior monologue. The market-side dig is the imagined-consultation frame: he noticed that what the reader actually wants is *free access to an expensive doctor's brain*, not a book. The ad fuses the TOC of the product with the reader's appointment-avoidance impulse, and the sentence *"How would you like to sit down with…"* writes itself out of that fusion. Any writer who had not opened to the chapter list could not have produced the cascade.

**Situation — `From-4-Packs-A-Day-To-Zero-In-4-Hours-Eugene-Schwartz`.** The headline appears to be rhetoric. It is not. *"From 4 packs a day to zero, in 4 hours"* is a specific case-history lifted verbatim out of the book — a real patient, a real duration, a real outcome. Schwartz did not invent the number; he found it. The market-side dig is that he also noticed the *failed quitter's* skepticism — the reader has tried to quit before, and a four-hour promise will read as lies. So the body copy contains the surrendered line: *"This book does not promise you, of course, that YOU will be able to duplicate this speed. But it does promise you this: YOU CAN QUIT SMOKING IN 14 DAYS!"* The join is: extreme case-history from page-X of the product + failed-quitter's learned skepticism from the market. Neither half alone produces the ad. Together, the 4-hour headline pulls the hand and the 14-day promise catches the reader before disbelief breaks the sale. A writer who had not read the case-history would have no 4. A writer who had not listened to failed quitters would have no 14-day concession. The ad is entirely a product of connection.

**Situation — `dont-pay-one-cent-more-for-gas-this-year`.** Written in the middle of the 1970s oil crisis. The market-side dig is obvious: every driver in America was bleeding at the pump and furious. The product-side dig is what most competitors missed. Schwartz opened Ed Almquist's book and listed the specific physical mechanisms — check-valve installation, carburetor drill-bit modification, timing adjustment — and the USDA report number AG-240 that backed a mileage claim. The body copy carries these out verbatim: *"From Now On, Why Not 'BURN' AIR In Your Car!"* *"Shell Oil proved existed last year, when they coaxed over 376 miles a gallon out of a standard gasoline-powered car."* The 376-miles figure is a real, citable number in a real, citable study — dug out of the book. A competitor could channel the same anger, but without the 376 miles and the USDA citation the promise collapses. The ad is a fusion of a mass mood (indignation at the pump) with a specific document number (AG-240) that exists only because someone read past the surface.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write the headline before you finish reading the product.
**Why:** A headline written from a brief is a headline about the brief. You will produce an echo of the client's own hopes for the product, not a claim specific enough to cut through. Schwartz's rule: the headline is the *last* thing you write, not the first. Read four times, take 40 pages of notes, sit with the market — then the headline falls out of the work already done.

**Don't:** Reach for a word-substitute when a specific fact is available.
**Why:** The Word-Substitute Technique (pull a winning headline off a competitor, swap the product name) is what Schwartz calls the shallowest level of creativity and the fastest route to an Echo Ad — one that reminds readers of some other product. It works only when product, market, and timing are identical to the source. As soon as anything differs, the ad loses the specific strengths wrapped up in its unique product-market-timing relationship, which is where all the force lives. Specifics from *your* product, dug out by reading, cannot be substituted by anyone.

**Don't:** Confuse motivation research findings with a headline.
**Why:** Research tells you *where not to go* and *the general area of the solution*. It does not tell you what to write. A motivation research finding is a direction; a headline is a fusion. Writers who treat the research report as the brief end up with generic appeals keyed to whatever the research said, and they never dig further. The research is the starting line. Connectivity is what you do after it.

**Don't:** Rewrite your own old winners when a new assignment shows up.
**Why:** Every product-market-timing relationship is unique. The ad that pulled in 1954 for horsepower will not pull in 1970 for fuel economy. A writer who grows tired or afraid and starts copying himself brings his talent down to the level of file-cabinet mediocrity. *"The true copy writer must argue with success — he must push on past it every time he faces a new product."* New product, new read, new notes, new join. There is no shortcut.

**Don't:** Mistake fluency with the product for finished reading.
**Why:** After one pass you know the main argument. After two you know the exceptions. After three you start seeing the connections between passages. The fourth pass is where Schwartz found the fact on page 531 that, combined with the fact on page 116, produced a sentence no competitor could write. If you stop at two, you have the argument; you do not have the ad.

**Don't:** Assume the product side is enough.
**Why:** Connectivity is a two-sided join. A writer who knows the product cold but has not listened to the market will produce technically accurate, emotionally inert copy — a product spec sheet. A writer who knows the market cold but has not read the product will produce emotionally charged copy with nothing specific to deliver on — a puff piece. Both halves, every time. See `mass-desire.md` for the market side; this file is about both sides meeting.

**Don't:** Present the draft as "inspiration" when challenged.
**Why:** When a client or editor pushes back on a claim, the writer who leaned on inspiration has nothing to answer with. The writer who did the work answers with page numbers. If you cannot answer with page numbers, the claim is indefensible and you will lose every edit fight. The 40 pages of notes are your defense — carry them into the meeting.

**Don't:** Write from inside your head about the reader.
**Why:** Your private theory of what the reader feels is not the market. The market is what the market actually says, in its letters, its complaints, its magazines, its private conversations. If you are writing from your own imagined prospect rather than from observed real-world signals, you are writing fiction. Fiction pulls nothing. Listen first, write after. See `gradualization.md` for why writing out of step with the reader's actual beliefs collapses the ad.
