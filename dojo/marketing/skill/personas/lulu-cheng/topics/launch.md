# Launch

## When to Use
- You're planning a major product, category, or company launch
- You're deciding how much to invest in the launch day itself vs. ongoing narrative
- You're right-sizing the effort — not every launch warrants the same energy
- You're tempted to put all your comms resources into one day

## Fails When
- Your launch isn't connected to a master narrative — it's just a product announcement
- You treat launch day as the entire strategy, then go dark on day 2
- You have no hook beyond "we're launching" — which is a category, not a hook
- You optimize for press hits without engineered conversion paths
- You get attention from the wrong audience for your actual business objective

## Core Concept

Launches are not the be-all end-all.

You don't remember the day Facebook launched. You don't remember the precise day Airbnb launched. The most successful products in the world often had unremarkable launch days. This doesn't mean launches don't matter. It means they matter differently than most people think.

Launches are happening constantly now. It used to be every few months. Then every few weeks. Now it's multiple times a day, and sometimes huge launches happen on top of each other. Breaking through this noise is incredibly hard. The tactics that worked five years ago — TechCrunch article, Twitter announcement, hope it goes viral — are table stakes now. Everyone does them. They're not differentiators. This means a single launch day cannot carry the weight of your entire narrative strategy. And the launches that break through are the ones connected to something bigger.

Your launch is one moment in an ongoing narrative. Before the launch, you should have been building presence, establishing positioning, creating anticipation. Your launch doesn't start the conversation — it's a milestone in a conversation you've already been having. After the launch, the work continues. The launch gets you initial attention. What you do with that attention determines whether it converts to anything lasting. Companies that treat launch day as everything often have post-launch hangovers — the team is exhausted, the budget is spent, the attention disperses. Companies that treat launch day as one moment in a continuous story are better positioned. The launch amplifies existing momentum rather than creating momentum from scratch. Don't over-index.

A launch, done well, accomplishes specific things: creates a news peg (a reason journalists can write about you today), concentrates attention (multiple channels on the same thing, briefly), activates your base (supporters get ammunition to share), and provides proof points ("we said we'd do X, we did"). The most powerful launches are those that reinforce your master narrative. Your narrative says "we exist to fight X and build Y." Your launch says "here's proof we're doing what we said." Launches disconnected from narrative are noise. Launches connected to narrative are proof.

## How to Apply

1. **Connect the launch to your master narrative.** The launch should be a chapter in the story you've been telling. If it's not — if it doesn't reinforce the mission — the launch is an orphan.

2. **Hook first, then story, then medium.** Don't start with "which publication?" Start with "why should someone who's never heard of us care about this specific moment?" "We're launching" isn't a hook. It's a category.

3. **Engineer conversion paths.** What do you want people to DO after hearing about the launch? Sign up for a waitlist? Apply for jobs? Request a demo? If there's no path, attention dissipates with no impact. Build the paths before launch day.

4. **Right-size effort to significance.**
   - Major launches (new company, major product category, strategic pivot): heavy investment, broad outreach, coordinated campaign.
   - Medium launches (new features, partnerships, expansions): solid execution but not all-hands mobilization.
   - Minor launches (updates, iterations): blog post and some social, nothing more.
   - Treating every launch as major is exhausting and undermines the impact of your actual major ones.

5. **Launch day tactics.** Coordinate timing (press embargo, social, email all at the same moment). Arm your allies (employees, investors, advocates with pre-written materials). Be available (respond instantly to journalists, customers, questions). Monitor and respond (correct misinformation quickly, engage positive responses). Don't go dark the day after.

6. **Treat launch as launch week, not launch day.** Day 1: initial hit. Day 2-3: amplification, responding, adding commentary. Day 4-7: follow-through, updates, customer stories. If you treat launch as a one-day event, you lose most of the value.

7. **Don't burn resources on the launch itself.** Save fuel for the sustained presence after. The launch without follow-through is a pulse that dies. The launch with follow-through is a chapter in a flywheel.

## Examples

**Situation:** A company spends six months preparing the perfect launch. Perfect press kit, perfect embargoes, perfect timing. Launch day arrives. It goes fine. Then the team exhales and goes dark for a month.
**Application:** Fail. The attention from launch dissipates. Meanwhile, the company that did a "good enough" launch but maintained consistent presence before and after is building compounding returns. Don't over-index on launch. It's important, not all-important.
**Result:** In six months, nobody remembers the launch day. They remember whether the product is good and whether the company has a consistent public presence. The all-in launch loses to the consistent presence.

**Situation:** Boom Supersonic launching a new milestone. "We built a plane" is a product announcement.
**Application:** Connect to master narrative: the speed limit in the sky, the 50-year stagnation, the crusade. The launch becomes proof — "we said we'd break the speed limit, here's the aircraft that does it." Hook is the narrative angle, not the product specs.
**Result:** The launch lands as a chapter in a story people have been following. Press coverage frames it as progress on the mission, not a corporate milestone.

**Situation:** A B2B SaaS company wants a TechCrunch feature for the launch.
**Application:** Work backwards from business objective. D = "book 1,000 enterprise demos in Q1." C = "book a demo." B = "heads of engineering at mid-market SaaS companies." A = "this solves a problem I'm spending budget on now." X = TechCrunch doesn't reach heads of engineering effectively. Better: focused podcast appearance on a show those heads listen to, paired with outreach, paired with a conversion-optimized landing page. Launch aligned to business outcome, not press-hit vanity.
**Result:** Fewer press hits. More demos booked. That's the launch actually doing work.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Treat "we're launching" as a hook. That's a category. Every launch is "we're launching." What makes yours worth attention?
**Why:** The attention question is comparative. Your launch is competing with a thousand other moments. Without a specific hook — surprise, conflict, specific insight — the launch gets buried.

**Don't:** Put all your comms resources into launch day and leave nothing for after. The attention captured on day one dissipates by day seven if you disappear.
**Why:** The flywheel doesn't start from one pulse. Sustained follow-through is what converts attention to lasting outcomes. Post-launch silence is the most common failure mode.

**Don't:** Launch without a conversion path. Lots of eyeballs, nothing to do with them. That's vanity, not strategy.
**Why:** Attention without conversion is the thing people move past. High five, we went viral, now back to work. Build the "and then what" before launch, not during.

**Don't:** Right-size every launch as major. If every launch is a massive campaign, the signal dilutes and your team burns out before your actual major launch.
**Why:** Reserve the big coordinated campaigns for moments that warrant them. A blog post and some social is enough for most iterations. The bigness should match the stakes.

**Don't:** Treat launch as separate from narrative-building. A launch disconnected from your master narrative is just a press release.
**Why:** Narratives do the heavy lifting. Product announcements dissolve into noise. The launches that break through are chapters in stories people already care about.
