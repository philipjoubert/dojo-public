# Go Direct

## When to Use
- You're a founder building reputation from scratch
- You're deciding whether to invest in traditional PR or build your own platform
- Your narrative is being shaped by journalists, competitors, or Twitter randos
- You're about to hire an agency to "handle comms"
- You're in a fight and the instinct is to hide behind a corporate statement

## Fails When
- Founder is genuinely the problem (active scandal, misconduct) — going direct broadcasts the liability
- You're a public company in an earnings blackout — securities law isn't optional
- Founder cannot sustain the cadence — one post a quarter isn't a Rostra, it's a ghost town
- Founder is using "going direct" as a synonym for "impulsive posting" without strategy

## Core Concept

Traditional PR is dead.

The model that built careers for a generation — win over ten gatekeepers, let them broadcast to millions — has collapsed. Trust in media is at historic lows. The distribution bottleneck is gone. You don't need the New York Times to reach millions of people. You can do it yourself, from your phone, in minutes.

"Corporate communications" itself is now an oxymoron. Nothing meaningful can be communicated by a faceless committee. If press releases read like they were written by a baker's dozen of middle managers, that's because they were. Their only discernible purpose is to avoid upsetting anyone and jeopardizing the future job prospects of those middle managers. The resulting stories are bland and generic, with passion reduced to pablum.

Going direct means the founder — or whoever holds the secret knowledge upon which the enterprise is built — speaks to the audience without intermediaries. No handlers. No agencies filtering the message. No spokespeople reciting approved talking points like they're putting fries in the bag. The best spokesperson is not the one with the most polish or the longest tenure. It's the person who can answer, from firsthand experience, why this matters. When Brian Armstrong took Coinbase public during a regulatory war, he didn't hide behind lawyers. He showed up, wrote directly to employees, investors, and customers, and named the enemy. That's going direct.

## How to Apply

1. **Accept that comms is the founder's job.** You would never outsource your product to someone who doesn't understand it. You would never let a committee of middle managers design your core technology. The story IS the product when it comes to convincing others to support the mission. Outsourcing comms is as bad as outsourcing code.

2. **Build your own Rostra.** In ancient Rome, the Rostra was a platform in the Forum from which orators addressed the public. Every founder needs their own. Twitter, Substack, a podcast, or all three — the platform matters less than the principle. You own the relationship with your audience. You can reach them without asking permission.

3. **Speak with conviction, not corporate hedging.** Generic statements are invisible. Taking actual positions — positions some people will disagree with — is what makes you visible.

4. **Show up consistently.** A founder who has been visibly direct for two years has a platform. A founder who's been silent has nothing. Starting from zero is possible, but it's expensive.

5. **Never go silent.** If you don't tell your story, someone else tells it for you. They get it wrong. They frame it their way, with their agenda, to serve their interests. And narratives ossify. A wrong story that goes unchallenged becomes "true" through repetition.

## Examples

**Situation:** Coinbase under sustained SEC attack, 2023. The conventional playbook said: lawyers-only, minimal public engagement, quiet lobbying.
**Application:** Brian Armstrong went direct. Wrote long posts naming the specific regulators, the specific actions, the specific failures. Put his face on it. Made the case to crypto users, employees, and investors at the same time.
**Result:** Coinbase built a defensible public narrative the SEC had to answer to. Won key regulatory fights. Turned a defensive posture into a rallying flag for the industry.

**Situation:** Palmer Luckey attacked in 2016 over political donations. Facebook advisors told him to stay quiet, don't engage, let it blow over.
**Application:** He followed the Facebook strategy. Said nothing. Hoped it would pass.
**Result:** "A year of not trimming my hedges turned into a garden of weeds that could never be unpulled. Not in 10 years." He eventually went direct — built Anduril, became one of the most visible defense founders alive. But he lost years he didn't need to lose.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical — specific to this framework)

**Don't:** Hire a paid spokesperson with polish to "represent" you. A generic paid spokesperson has zero credibility. Everyone knows they're being paid to say the words. They're putting the fries in the bag of the email, and it's not landing.
**Why:** Conviction is the load-bearing element. A messenger whose opinion literally doesn't count — because they're paid to hold it — subtracts signal.

**Don't:** Treat going direct as a launch-day exercise. "We'll do a Twitter push for the launch" is not a Rostra.
**Why:** The Rostra is infrastructure. Its value is the audience you've built when you actually need them — during a crisis, a launch, a fundraise, a hiring push. Building it during the crisis is too late.

**Don't:** Use "I'm not charismatic" or "I'm an introvert" as an exit. Conviction beats charisma. Cult leaders have terrible pitches — rambling, long, unsophisticated. But absolute conviction is magnetic.
**Why:** The bar isn't "be smooth." The bar is "be real about something you actually believe." No hired spokesperson can fake that.

**Don't:** Go direct without a plan, then delete posts when legal panics. Every retracted post teaches your audience you can be pressured into silence.
**Why:** Direct means you own the voice. If you outsource the editing to your general counsel after the fact, you didn't go direct. You went indirect through a more chaotic process.
