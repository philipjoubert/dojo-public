# Positioning Activation

## When to Use
- You finished a positioning exercise, produced a canvas, and now the team is asking "what do we do with it?"
- Sales is pitching something different from what the positioning document says. Marketing's homepage tells a third story. Product's roadmap is heading in a fourth direction.
- You're about to roll out repositioning and need to avoid the classic failure where the pretty deck lives in a Google Drive folder nobody opens.
- Three-plus months after a positioning workshop you're auditing whether it actually changed anything.

## Fails When
- **Positioning is weak.** Activation is downstream of positioning. If the components aren't sharp — loose alternatives, vague value, fuzzy target — no amount of activation will rescue it. Garbage in, garbage out, at scale. Fix the positioning, then activate.
- **You treat this like a marketing launch.** Activation isn't "a campaign to announce the new positioning." It's a cross-functional rollout across sales, product, pricing, hiring, and marketing. A campaign is one output of activation, not the activation itself.
- **Leadership won't enforce the pitch.** Reps revert to demo-first within two weeks if leadership doesn't make the new pitch non-negotiable. Handwaving "they'll come around" is how activation dies quietly.

## Core Concept

Positioning is an *input*, not an output. It's the foundation, not the house. If the positioning exercise produces a beautiful canvas and the team stops there, nothing changes in the business. The canvas has to get translated into every customer-facing function before it counts.

**The translation problem.** Teams finish a positioning workshop and expect the positioning to magically appear on sales calls, in campaigns, in the product roadmap. It doesn't. Each function has to deliberately translate positioning into its own artifacts. Marketing doesn't automatically write messaging from the canvas. Sales doesn't automatically pitch from the canvas. Product doesn't automatically prioritize from the canvas. Somebody has to do the translation, or it doesn't happen.

**Where activation breaks.** In my experience working with dozens of companies that did the exercise but didn't stick the landing, the failure modes cluster:

- **Sales never agreed with the positioning and quietly rejected it.** They weren't in the room when it was built, so they don't buy in. The slides are pretty, but sales modifies them to the point of losing the positioning, or ignores them and reverts to the old pitch.
- **Marketing built "fluffy nonsense" instead of a pitch sales can use.** The sales decks I've seen produced by marketing agencies are gorgeous and useless — no room for discovery, no objection handling, no path to a next step.
- **Teams believe every pitch has to be fully customized** and refuse to build a consistent first-call story. In early sales interactions, consistency trumps customization — you can't customize until you've done discovery, and discovery happens in the first call.
- **Teams build multiple pitches for multiple personas.** One pitch, aimed at the champion. Other personas get objection handling inside the champion's flow, not their own pitch.
- **Positioning that lives only in marketing.** Sales tells a different story than the website. Product doesn't prove the positioning claims. Support experience doesn't match the brand promise. The positioning never became the company's positioning — it stayed a marketing artifact.

**The activation is across five functions, minimum.**

1. **Sales.** First-call pitch structure, built jointly by marketing and sales, tested on real prospects by the best AE, then rolled out with training and recertification. Without this, nothing else matters.
2. **Marketing.** Homepage, campaign themes, content strategy, buyer's guides, point-of-view content — all derived from the pitch structure, not written in isolation. Modifying a sales pitch into marketing is easier than the reverse.
3. **Product.** The roadmap has to prove the positioning. If you're positioning as "the easiest to use," the product had better be easy to use. Features that don't support the differentiated value shouldn't be highlighted.
4. **Hiring.** New hires in sales, marketing, product, and CS need to be onboarded into the positioning — the alternatives, the differentiated capabilities, the value themes, the best-fit accounts, the market category, and the narrative.
5. **Pricing.** Price signals positioning. A premium positioning with accessible pricing confuses buyers. The pricing model (per-seat? per-use? tiered? flat?) also signals what category you're in.

**The real test.** Sitting across from a qualified prospect, telling the story — does the prospect get excited and want to buy something? That's the test. Not "did we produce a canvas," not "did marketing launch a campaign." The activation is working if sales cycles shorten, win rates climb, best-fit customers find you, and the sales team confidently delivers a consistent pitch. It's failing if prospects say "could you back up and explain that again," if reps each tell a different story, and if product features don't match the marketing claims.

## How to Apply

1. **Don't activate until positioning is sharp.** If the canvas still has handwaving in any component, activation will amplify the weakness. Fix positioning first, activate second.

2. **Build the sales pitch before you build anything else.** The pitch is the first and most important activation artifact. Use the Sales Pitch Structure (Insight → Alternatives → Perfect World → Intro → Value → Proof → Objections → Ask). Build it jointly with marketing and sales — not marketing alone, and definitely not an outside agency.

3. **Test before you train.** Take the best AE. Train just that rep on the new pitch (marketing and sales both involved). Test it on real new prospects. Debrief after every call — what worked, what didn't. Iterate. The pass/fail: the best AE says "I like this better than the old pitch and I want to use it on every call." Only then do you roll out.

4. **Use the tested rep to enable the team.** Peer credibility beats marketing presentations. The rep who tested it can say "I've used this, here are my deals, it works." Record them doing the pitch. Use that video as the training asset.

5. **Translate pitch into marketing, not the other way around.** Once the pitch is validated, adapt it into homepage copy, campaign themes, buyer's guides, explainer videos, point-of-view content, case studies. Working backward from the pitch keeps the messaging consistent. Starting from marketing and then asking sales to use it is how you get "fluffy nonsense."

6. **Align the roadmap.** Bring the product team the positioning canvas and ask: "Does this roadmap prove these claims? Where does it diverge?" Features that don't support the differentiated value shouldn't be highlighted in launches. Product decisions should address the current competitive landscape, not theoretical future competitors.

7. **Update hiring and onboarding.** Every new hire in a customer-facing function needs a positioning onboarding — the alternatives, differentiated capabilities, value themes, best-fit accounts, market category, and narrative. Job descriptions, interview questions, and the first-week curriculum all need to reflect it.

8. **Schedule recertification.** Pitches drift. Six months in, the setup gets skipped, the demo creeps earlier, the differentiated value slide grows extra features. Re-test, re-certify, re-record. Otherwise by month twelve you're back to demo-first.

## Examples

**Situation:** A company finished a positioning workshop three months ago and nothing has changed in sales calls.
**Application:** Two failure modes to check. First, was sales in the room when the positioning was built? If not, they didn't buy in and they're quietly ignoring it. Go back and re-do the exercise with sales leadership and a couple of bag-carrying reps. Second, was the sales pitch ever built? A positioning canvas isn't a pitch. Someone has to translate positioning into the 8-step pitch structure, and that someone has to be marketing *and* sales working together.
**Result:** With sales involved and a pitch structure actually built, the positioning starts showing up on calls. Reps use it because they helped build it and because the pitch works on real prospects.

**Situation:** Marketing has rolled out beautiful new homepage messaging and sales is pitching something different.
**Application:** Classic activation break. The homepage was written in isolation without reference to the sales pitch. Sales doesn't use it because it doesn't help them win calls. Flip the order: build the pitch first (with sales in the room), validate it on real prospects, then adapt the validated pitch into homepage copy.
**Result:** Homepage and pitch tell the same story. When inbound leads arrive, they're primed for what the first-call pitch will deliver. Conversion from visit-to-call and call-to-opportunity both climb.

**Situation:** A team asks "how do we measure whether activation is working?"
**Application:** Specific signs activation is landing: sales cycles shorten; win rates improve; prospects understand what you do on the first call (not the third); best-fit customers start finding you rather than bad-fit; sales reps deliver the pitch consistently across the team; marketing campaigns feel specific rather than generic. Specific signs it isn't: prospects say "could you back up and explain that again"; prospects think you're something you're not; prospects say "I get it, but why would I pay for that"; reps each tell a different story; marketing materials gather dust.
**Result:** The team gets a concrete scoreboard. Instead of arguing about whether the positioning is working, they can look at specific signals and intervene where activation is breaking.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Finish the positioning exercise and call it done.
**Why:** The canvas is an input. Nothing in the business changes until you translate it into sales pitch, marketing artifacts, product roadmap, pricing, and hiring. Teams that stop at the canvas get zero return on the positioning investment.

**Don't:** Let a marketing agency build the sales pitch.
**Why:** Agencies don't understand sales discovery, objection handling, or how deals actually move. You get gorgeous, fluffy slides that sales won't use. The pitch has to be built by people who have pitched real customers.

**Don't:** Roll out the new pitch by emailing a deck to sales.
**Why:** Reps default to what they know. A deck without training, without peer-validated proof that it works, and without leadership enforcement will be ignored inside two weeks. Activation is a process, not a file attachment.

**Don't:** Build a different pitch for every persona.
**Why:** The champion is the one who gets you on the shortlist. One pitch, aimed at the champion, with objection handling for the other personas baked in. Multiple persona-specific pitches means inconsistent positioning across the buying committee and confused prospects.

**Don't:** Skip the six-month recertification.
**Why:** Pitches drift. Reps improvise. The setup gets shorter, the demo creeps earlier, value slides accumulate features. Without recertification, the activation you did in Q1 is gone by Q4, and you'll be surprised when win rates slide back.
