# Positioning vs. Strategy vs. Vision vs. Messaging

## When to Use
- The team keeps using the four words interchangeably and it's causing constant miscommunication.
- The sales pitch reads like a VC pitch — full of future promises and macro trends instead of present-tense value.
- The homepage or sales deck is pitching where the product will be in 5 years, and customers are saying "cool, but what do you actually do today?"
- Leadership is making decisions and it's unclear which layer they're operating at — are we changing vision, strategy, positioning, or just messaging?

## Fails When
- **The company has no real vision at all.** If you haven't decided where you want the company to go, you can't position against a journey you haven't mapped. Get the vision in place first, then this framework helps.
- **You try to fix a vision problem by tightening positioning.** Positioning won't fix a wrong vision any more than messaging fixes a wrong positioning. Diagnose which layer is actually broken before you try to fix it.

## Core Concept

Four concepts get mashed together constantly, and sorting them out makes most confused strategy conversations instantly clearer. Here's the clean version:

**Vision is the destination.** Where do you want the company to be in the long term — 5, 10, 15 years out? "Become the leading CRM for enterprises." "Give small businesses the financial infrastructure of a large company." Vision is aspirational, long-range, and doesn't change often. It's what investors buy into. It's what recruits get excited about.

**Strategy is the road.** The sequence of plays you'll run to get from today to the vision. Strategy describes stages — where you'll play first, where you'll play next, what you'll do in what order. "Start by selling CRM to investment banks. Then expand to retail banks. Then insurance. Then attack the broad enterprise market." Strategy is how you actually get from today to the vision. It changes occasionally, as you learn from the market.

**Positioning is where you are right now on the road.** How you're winning business *today* in the market *as it exists today*, against the alternatives *as they exist today*. Positioning is firmly rooted in the present. It's the foundation of your current sales pitch, the thing that makes today's deals close. Positioning changes as you move through the stages of the strategy — the positioning you need in stage one is not the positioning you need in stage three. "CRM for investment banks" is the stage-one positioning. "Enterprise CRM" is the final-stage positioning.

**Messaging is how you express the positioning in words.** It's the output, not the input. Your tagline, your homepage copy, your pitch language, your ad headlines — all messaging. Messaging has to serve the positioning underneath it; when messaging drifts from positioning, you get those incoherent homepages where nothing quite lines up.

The common mistake: people collapse these four layers into one and it causes everything from bad sales pitches to unclear product roadmaps. Let's take the most destructive version of the confusion.

**The biggest single mistake: treating your sales pitch like your VC pitch.**

An investor is buying the future. They want to hear about macro trends that will disrupt the market, why you're uniquely positioned to ride those trends, and how you'll become a dominant leader of a massive market in five or ten years. Future, future, future.

A buyer is solving a problem today with money they have today. They want to know why they should pick you over the alternatives *right now*. "Your product will be amazing in three years" gives them exactly zero reasons to buy today — in fact, it gives them a reason to wait. Research shows about 40% of B2B purchase attempts end in "no decision," often because the buying team can't agree on an approach. If your sales pitch is selling the future, you're actively giving buyers reasons to delay.

Investor pitch: your vision, the macro trends that favor it, your path to leadership. Sales pitch: your current differentiated value, the alternatives you beat today, why a specific buyer should pick you now. These should be different decks.

Similarly: your strategy might say "eventually we'll sell to all enterprises" — great for a board meeting, useless for a sales call with a mid-market insurance company *this quarter*. For that deal, you need positioning that makes your value obvious to *mid-market insurance*, not positioning that makes your value obvious to "all enterprises." The positioning evolves with strategy; it's not static.

## How to Apply

1. **When someone says "positioning," check which layer they actually mean.** Half the time they mean messaging (they want a new tagline). Sometimes they mean strategy (they want to change target markets). Occasionally they mean vision (they want to change what the company is trying to become). Don't assume; clarify.

2. **Separate your investor deck from your sales deck.** They should share some DNA — company name, product name, what the product does at a high level. Past that, they diverge. The investor deck leans forward; the sales deck lives in the present. If your sales reps are using the investor deck, you're losing deals to "no decision."

3. **Update positioning as strategy advances.** Moving from stage one to stage two of your strategy almost always means a positioning adjustment. The alternatives change. The target expands or shifts. The value proposition sharpens. Expect positioning to evolve every time strategy moves.

4. **Keep messaging downstream of positioning.** Every messaging asset — homepage, tagline, ad copy, email subject lines, sales decks — should be traceable back to the positioning components. If someone can't tell you which component of positioning a piece of messaging comes from, that messaging is off-brief.

5. **Check whether the question is actually a positioning question.** "We have a bad quarter, should we fix our positioning?" Not necessarily. A bad quarter might be a sales execution problem, a product problem, a pricing problem, or a market problem. Positioning is one possible root cause, not the default.

6. **Use different stories for different audiences.** Investor story = vision + strategy. Sales story = positioning. Recruiting story = vision + culture. Customer case study = specific outcome achieved with the product. Don't try to compress all stories into one. They serve different purposes.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder's homepage opens with "The future of work is here" and pitches a 5-year vision.
**Application:** That's investor pitch content on a customer-facing asset. The homepage should be present-tense: what does the product do today, for whom, against which alternatives, with what differentiated value. The vision belongs in the "about" page or the investor deck, not on the top of the homepage. Cut the future-tense language on the homepage. Rewrite around present-tense differentiated value.
**Result:** Conversion improves. Buyers who used to bounce because "this doesn't look like something that exists yet" now recognize what they're looking at.

**Situation:** A CEO says "we need new positioning" and what they actually want is a new tagline.
**Application:** A tagline is messaging. If the positioning underneath is sound, you need a messaging exercise, not a positioning exercise. If the positioning is weak, no tagline will save you — you need positioning work first. Diagnose which one is actually broken before picking a tool.
**Result:** If the positioning is sound, the team does a messaging sprint and the tagline gets sharper in a week. If the positioning is weak, a tagline exercise would have been lipstick on a pig; the team does positioning work first, and the tagline that emerges afterward is genuinely differentiated.

**Situation:** A company selling CRM to investment banks wants to go broader into financial services.
**Application:** Check: is this a strategy change (we're advancing to the next stage of the long-term plan), a positioning change (our current target needs to expand), or a vision change (we're rethinking what the company is trying to be)? Usually it's strategy: we're moving from stage one (investment banks) to stage two (financial services broadly). That strategy shift triggers a positioning update: new alternatives to consider, new target-customer profile, possibly a new category framing.
**Result:** The team doesn't just "rebrand" — they do the positioning work that matches the new stage. Sales pitch, messaging, and target-account list all get updated in a coordinated way.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use the same deck for VC pitches and customer pitches.
**Why:** They're selling different things to different audiences. A VC pitch that works with customers will make you sound like you're selling a promise instead of a product. A customer pitch that works with VCs will make you sound small and unambitious. Use two decks.

**Don't:** Pitch the future to customers in the hopes of looking visionary.
**Why:** Customers hate that. They want to know what they're buying today. Future pitching gives them a reason to wait until the future arrives. Save the vision for the investor conversation.

**Don't:** Rewrite positioning every time someone suggests a new tagline.
**Why:** Messaging and positioning are different layers. A tagline exercise doesn't require redoing positioning. Don't burn two days of leadership time on a five-word change.

**Don't:** Let "vision" and "positioning" blur in internal conversations.
**Why:** Teams that conflate the two end up with positioning that's trying to sell the future and strategy conversations that collapse into feature debates. Keep the layers clean. When you're talking about where you want to be in 10 years, it's vision. When you're talking about the sales pitch, it's positioning. Police the vocabulary in meetings — it prevents a lot of downstream confusion.

**Don't:** Assume positioning is static.
**Why:** Positioning evolves with every stage of the strategy. If you haven't revisited positioning in a year, the odds are good it's at least partly stale. Check it at least annually and whenever something big shifts — a new round, a new competitor on shortlists, a big customer win that opened a new segment, a new senior exec.
