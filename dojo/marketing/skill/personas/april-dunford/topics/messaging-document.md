# The Messaging Document

## When to Use
- Positioning is done, sharp, and validated. You need an artifact that translates positioning into approved copy that marketing and sales can pull from without re-writing it every time.
- The marketing team is burning hours rewriting the same boilerplate for every campaign, email, and landing page.
- Sales collateral, homepage copy, deck copy, and campaign copy have drifted — each channel is saying something slightly (or more than slightly) different.
- You're onboarding a new copywriter, agency, or marketer who needs to write on-brand from week one.
- AI tools need grounded context to generate consistent copy — a messaging document is increasingly useful for that.

## Fails When
- **Positioning isn't done.** A messaging document assumes a validated positioning canvas underneath. Without that, what you'll get is five stakeholders assembling committee copy that describes what everyone wishes were true. That's brochure copy, not messaging.
- **The company sells multiple products.** This template assumes a single-product company where company positioning equals product positioning. Multi-product positioning has its own mechanics — see chapter 68 of the second edition of *Obviously Awesome*.
- **The team treats it as a done-once document.** Positioning evolves. Proof points accumulate. Customers change. Trends layer on and off. A messaging document that isn't updated becomes stale messaging with the word "approved" stamped on it, which is worse than no document at all.

## Core Concept

Every time you create a marketing campaign or a new piece of sales collateral, you need copy that describes what you do and the value you deliver. Companies without a messaging document run into four predictable problems:

1. They waste time re-creating messages from scratch for every tactic.
2. Messages shift — sometimes a little, sometimes a lot — resulting in inconsistent and confusing messaging across channels.
3. New employees don't understand the positioning that gives context to the messaging, so the drift accelerates.
4. Executives waste time reviewing and approving copy for every new piece because there's no approved boilerplate for the team to pull from. This slows everyone down.

The messaging document solves all four. It becomes:

- The consistent record of the current approved company positioning that provides context for all marketing and sales content creation.
- A set of approved messages and content that can be used as the foundation for any marketing and sales materials.

The document is not positioning. Positioning sits inside the document as Section One, for context. Everything else in the document has to align with that positioning.

**The eight sections:**

1. **Positioning (for context).** The positioning canvas: market category, competitive alternatives, distinct capabilities, differentiated value themes, best-fit account characteristics. Note: the value themes captured here are *not* the messaging. They're positioning inputs. The approved message boilerplate is in Section Two.

2. **Boilerplate messaging.** Approved copy at four lengths: the one-liner (one or two sentences — what you are and the biggest benefit, bonus points for hinting at who it's for); the elevator pitch (one paragraph — what you are, top one or two value points, some indication of target market, bonus points for proof); the 100-word description (one to two paragraphs — top 2-3 value points with proof and target indication); and the 500-word description (four to five paragraphs — room for 3-4 value points, proof, general traction points, and target).

3. **Value points boilerplate.** For each of 1-3 value themes: the target segment (specific, identifiable characteristics), the champion persona, the competitive alternatives, the shortcomings of current solutions, the value theme itself, the supporting features that enable the value, and the proof you can deliver it.

4. **Competitive messaging.** A table of competitor claims and your approved responses. In general, avoid direct competitor references publicly, but teams need internal material they can pull from in sales calls. Approved response wording only — so sales reps aren't inventing it on the fly.

5. **Approved customer assets.** Per customer: link to video assets (long, short, one-slide versions), link to case studies, approved quotes (captured separately so they can be reused), and approved visual assets.

6. **Brag points.** Awards, usage stats, independent reviews, named customers, brag-worthy numbers. For each: type, date, details, and approved wording. Keeps the team from re-inventing how to phrase the same achievement for every campaign.

7. **Spokesperson bios and links.** For each spokesperson: name, title, headshots (print and web), short bio (2-3 sentences), medium (100 words), long (200 words), and approved talk descriptions.

8. **Links to approved visual assets.** Screenshots, diagrams, illustrations, logos. For each: description, link to the approved version, notes on when it can or cannot be used.

**Don't be a slave to the template.** Good templates jump-start a project. Edit, expand, or reduce to fit the situation. The template is a starting point, not a straitjacket.

## How to Apply

1. **Complete positioning first.** The canvas populates Section One and provides the context for everything downstream. If the canvas has weak spots — fuzzy value, loose best-fit, ghost alternatives — fix them before writing boilerplate.

2. **Don't use the value themes in Section One as Section Two copy.** This is the most common mistake. Positioning value themes are strategic statements — "improve profitability through dynamic pricing." Section Two copy is the *messaging* of that value — written for a buyer to read, with proof, persona, and hint of market. Different jobs. Write them separately.

3. **Build Section Two lengths in order: short to long.** The one-liner is the hardest. Write it first. Then expand to the elevator pitch, then 100 words, then 500. Each longer version accretes proof and target language onto the core of the shorter one. Writing the 500-word first and trying to compress down almost always produces a fluffy short version.

4. **Section Three is the workhorse.** One block per value theme. Everyone writing copy pulls from here. Supporting features and proof points live with the value they support — don't scatter them into separate lists. Marketers writing a campaign on value theme #1 find everything they need in one place.

5. **For competitive messaging (Section Four), write the response, not the claim.** You can't approve the competitor's claim. You can approve your response. Response wording should be honest, specific, and resistant to being quoted back at you out of context.

6. **Capture customer assets as they get approved, not in a batch.** Every time a case study, video, or quote clears legal and customer approval, add it. Sections Five, Six, and Eight decay fast if treated as one-time populate-and-forget lists.

7. **Review every six months, or on any positioning change.** Stale messaging with the "approved" stamp on it is worse than no document. Schedule the review; treat it like a dependency update.

8. **Consider the format for AI use.** If your team uses AI tools to generate copy, think about whether the document should be one file, multiple files with metadata, or a single doc with a separate contextual "instruction" sheet. Best practices here are changing fast — pick the format that matches how your team actually uses AI, and revisit.

## Examples

**Situation:** A company with solid positioning has three marketing channels — homepage, sales decks, outbound email — and each tells a slightly different story about the product.
**Application:** Build Sections One through Three. The positioning canvas in Section One makes the underlying story shared. Section Two's four boilerplate lengths give homepage, sales deck opening, outbound intro line, and long-form collateral a common source. Section Three's value boilerplate populates every campaign. Within a quarter, the three channels are saying variations of the same thing instead of three different things.
**Result:** Messaging consistency without sacrificing channel-appropriate length. Copywriters work faster because they're not re-inventing the wheel. Executives stop reviewing every piece because there's approved boilerplate to pull from.

**Situation:** A founder asks for a messaging document to speed up campaign work, but positioning hasn't been done.
**Application:** Refuse the order. A messaging document without positioning is committee-written brochure copy dressed up as a messaging framework. You'd be approving language for a story the team hasn't actually chosen yet. Do the positioning first; then the messaging document has something real to carry.
**Result:** Founder grumbles. Three weeks later, positioning is sharp, the messaging document writes itself in a day, and the speed-up the founder wanted shows up where it actually matters — in campaign work.

**Situation:** A team builds a messaging document, then never updates it. Eighteen months in, the value themes have shifted, two major new customer stories have closed, and the document still reflects last year's state.
**Application:** Stale messaging with "approved" on it is actively harmful — new hires reference it, agencies pull from it, AI tools ground on it. Schedule a quarterly review. Assign ownership (usually product marketing). Update Sections One through Three when positioning moves; keep Sections Five, Six, and Eight current as assets land.
**Result:** The document stays useful instead of becoming a buried artifact. Teams actually check it before writing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build a messaging document as your first artifact after a positioning workshop.
**Why:** The first artifact should be the sales pitch — the pitch is how you test whether the positioning actually works in front of a real prospect. Only once the pitch has been validated does the messaging document have proven language to codify. Build pitch first, document second.

**Don't:** Let the Section One positioning value themes become the Section Two elevator pitch.
**Why:** They sound similar but do different jobs. Positioning themes are strategic labels. Messaging copy is buyer-facing prose with proof and context. Copy-pasting one into the other produces marketing language that feels abstract and unanchored.

**Don't:** Write the 500-word description first.
**Why:** You'll end up with a fluffy 500 words that won't compress cleanly into a one-liner. Write short first. Each longer version is the previous version plus proof plus target specificity. That compounds correctly; the reverse doesn't.

**Don't:** Treat the document as "done."
**Why:** Positioning, customers, proof, and trends evolve. A messaging document that isn't maintained drifts into being a record of what was true last year, which is worse than no document.

**Don't:** Use this template for a multi-product company as-is.
**Why:** The template assumes single-product company positioning. Multi-product positioning has different mechanics — portfolio-level, product-level, and segment-led decisions all stack differently. Start from the multi-product positioning chapter of *Obviously Awesome* (2nd ed., ch. 68) and adapt the document shape to match.
