# When to Reposition (and When Not To)

## When to Use
- The business just experienced a significant event — an acquisition, a new funding round, a senior exec hire, or the opposite: a bad quarter, a string of painful losses, a surprise competitor entering the shortlist, a regulatory change.
- The operational symptoms of positioning drift are showing up: long sales cycles, low close rates, buyers who can't articulate what you do, rising price pressure, customer churn driven by misunderstood value.
- You're heading into an annual sales kick-off (typically January) and want your 2026 story to be tighter than your 2025 story.
- You're about to launch new products or enter new markets. Those shifts almost always require positioning updates.

## Fails When
- **The real problem is operational, not positioning.** If sales execution is broken, onboarding is bad, pricing is uncompetitive, or the product has reliability issues — positioning won't save you. Fix the actual problem.
- **Leadership bandwidth is thin.** Positioning work requires senior attention and follow-through. If the CEO is in fire-fighting mode or the team can't commit to rewriting downstream artifacts after the exercise, don't start — the output won't stick.
- **Somebody wants to "rebrand" for political reasons.** A new CMO wanting to put their stamp on the company is not a business reason to reposition. Disentangle the political ask from the business question.

## Core Concept

Positioning isn't static. Your product evolves, your market evolves, your competitors evolve, and the segment of buyers who care most about your value shifts over time. Great positioning has a shelf life. The question isn't "should we ever revisit it?" — yes, obviously. The question is "when, and what triggers it?"

There are triggers that clearly say *yes, reposition now*, triggers that say *revisit and adjust*, and symptoms that look like positioning problems but are actually something else wearing a positioning mask. Sorting these apart is the work.

**Clear triggers to reposition:**

- **Major business events, positive or negative.** Acquisitions, funding rounds, a new CEO, major exec hires, M&A activity, a pivot. These are natural inflection points where the exec team is already thinking hard about the business, and positioning work fits that headspace. It's also the moment when you have political permission to reopen assumptions that might have been closed for years.

- **A new, serious competitor starts appearing on shortlists.** If prospects are now comparing you to someone they weren't comparing you to six months ago, your competitive alternatives have shifted — which means unique attributes, differentiated value, and possibly market category all need re-examination.

- **A product shift or new launch.** If you've added significant new capability or launched a second product, your positioning has to account for that. Single-product positioning does not automatically extend to multi-product positioning.

- **A target-market shift.** You've been selling mostly mid-market and now you're pushing upmarket, or vice versa. The positioning that wins mid-market deals is rarely the positioning that wins enterprise deals. Different alternatives, different values, different buyer roles, often different category framing.

- **Category shifts.** Your category itself is evolving — new sub-categories emerging, leaders losing ground, buyer expectations changing. If the frame you were inside is becoming a less useful frame, you need to decide whether to stay in it or move to a better one.

- **Regulatory or macro shifts.** New regulations, big economic swings, structural industry changes. These alter buyer priorities, which alters which value points matter, which alters positioning.

**Operational symptoms that suggest positioning drift:**

- New prospects can't articulate what you do, even though existing customers love you.
- Sales cycles are getting longer and close rates dropping.
- You're losing deals late, after serious evaluation.
- You're under price pressure — buyers treating you as interchangeable with alternatives.
- Customer churn shortly after purchase, driven by misunderstood value.
- Sales team keeps getting pulled into bad-fit deals and burning cycles on them.

**When NOT to reposition:**

- **A single bad quarter with no structural cause.** Sometimes sales has a bad quarter because pipeline coverage was thin or a few key reps left. That's not positioning — that's sales ops.
- **When sales execution is broken.** If reps aren't hitting quota because reps aren't running the process correctly, new positioning won't fix it. Fix the execution.
- **When the product is genuinely undifferentiated.** Positioning can't rescue a product that delivers nothing the alternatives don't. That's a product problem. Go build something different before spending leadership time on positioning.
- **When the problem is pricing, packaging, or go-to-market motion.** These look like positioning problems but aren't. Diagnose the root before running the exercise.
- **When nobody is ready to change anything.** If you run the exercise and nobody will rewrite the sales deck, update the homepage, or change the target-account list, the exercise is theater.

A specific timing point I've learned the hard way: if you want your January sales kick-off to have new, tighter messaging, start the positioning work in September or October. Not December. You cannot get cross-functional alignment on new positioning in a single rushed week, and you cannot retrofit a whole sales deck onto new positioning in a holiday-shortened December. Start early or accept that next year's pitch will be a warmed-over version of this year's.

## How to Apply

1. **Run the diagnostic before you run the exercise.** Before assuming positioning is broken, ask: is this a positioning problem, a sales execution problem, a product problem, a pricing problem, or a go-to-market motion problem? Talk to sales, customer success, and a few recent lost deals. Look at the pattern of what's breaking down. Match the tool to the problem.

2. **Identify your trigger event clearly.** If you're doing this because a big event just happened (acquisition, new funding, new exec), great — name it. If you're doing it because operational symptoms are showing up, name those too. Be specific. "We're doing this because the sales cycle has grown from 60 to 110 days over the past year and deals are dying late with 'not sure this is the right fit'" is useful. "We feel our positioning is stale" is not.

3. **Assess leadership bandwidth.** Positioning work requires the CEO or business leader to show up, make decisions, and commit to rewriting the sales pitch and homepage at the end. If that commitment isn't there, don't start. Wait until it is.

4. **Set the scope.** Is this a full positioning re-work or a targeted adjustment? A new competitor on shortlists might require adjusting the Alternatives and Unique Attributes components without touching Market Category. A big pivot or acquisition might require re-doing the whole canvas. Decide before you start.

5. **Schedule the follow-through at the same time as the exercise.** Book the sales-deck rewrite workshop. Block homepage revision time. Plan the sales kick-off communication. If these don't get on the calendar at the moment the positioning work starts, they won't happen.

6. **Use the exercise to realign the exec team.** Positioning workshops are a rare forcing function that gets the CEO, sales, marketing, product, and customer success in the same room making the same decisions. Even if the positioning changes are modest, the alignment side benefit is usually worth the time.

## Examples

**Situation:** A startup is acquired by a larger company. The acquirer is asking what the positioning should be.
**Application:** Acquisition is a clear trigger. The alternatives have changed (the new parent changes the competitive set). The target might shift (parent-company accounts are now in-scope). The category may need to move (to align with parent's broader portfolio). Run the full exercise with leadership from both teams in the room.
**Result:** The acquired product comes out of the integration with positioning that works inside the parent's portfolio, rather than being a confusing orphan product nobody knows how to sell alongside the rest.

**Situation:** A company has had three bad quarters in a row. CEO wants to "reposition."
**Application:** Before agreeing, diagnose. Talk to sales — what's killing deals? Look at the win/loss data. Talk to churned customers. Is this positioning, or is it product reliability, or is it that a new competitor showed up? Sometimes the answer is genuinely positioning (buyer profile has shifted, value messaging is no longer hitting). Sometimes it's operational. Don't assume.
**Result:** In about half the cases I've seen, the problem is partly positioning and partly something else. You address the real root causes, and the positioning work that happens alongside is scoped appropriately rather than being asked to fix everything.

**Situation:** The CEO says "let's reposition for 2026" but it's already mid-December.
**Application:** Too late to hit January sales kick-off with new positioning and a rewritten sales deck. Be honest about the timeline. Options: do it properly now and aim for Q2, or do a tight messaging refresh for January and a full positioning exercise in Q1 with rollout at the mid-year sales meeting. Both are valid. Rushing it into four weeks produces a weak output and a deck nobody fully believes in.
**Result:** Leadership picks a realistic timeline. The work gets done properly. The team ends up with positioning they actually commit to, instead of a hurried document they quietly abandon by February.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Reposition because a new CMO wants to.
**Why:** New execs often reposition to put their stamp on the company. That's a political motive, not a business one. If the existing positioning is working, leave it alone. If it's broken, diagnose the breakage with evidence — not with "it doesn't feel fresh."

**Don't:** Assume every bad quarter is a positioning problem.
**Why:** Positioning is one of many things that can cause revenue shortfalls. Sales execution, product quality, pricing, market conditions, macro factors — all can cause the same symptoms. Diagnose before you act.

**Don't:** Reposition without committing to change downstream artifacts.
**Why:** Positioning that doesn't flow into a new sales deck, a new homepage, a new target-account list, and a product roadmap review is positioning that lives in a drawer. If leadership isn't willing to rewrite the downstream stuff, don't run the exercise.

**Don't:** Try to reposition in the final weeks before a sales kick-off.
**Why:** You cannot get cross-functional alignment and rewrite all the downstream artifacts in three rushed weeks. You'll produce a watered-down document and a warmed-over deck. Start early (September/October for a January kick-off) or accept the delay.

**Don't:** Reposition in isolation from strategy.
**Why:** If your strategy is changing — new target markets, new stages — the positioning change has to be coordinated with it. Trying to fix positioning without touching the strategy question underneath is treating the symptom, not the disease.
