# Competitive Alternatives

## When to Use
- At the very start of any positioning or repositioning exercise. This is step one. Everything else — unique attributes, value, target, category — depends on this being right.
- When the team is convinced they have no competitors ("we're a new category!"). They do. It might be Excel, it might be a manual process, it might be doing nothing, but there's always an alternative.
- When deals are slipping to "no decision." That's not a loss to no one; that's a loss to the status quo, which is the most dangerous competitor most B2B companies have.
- When the team is obsessed with competitors that never show up on real shortlists, while ignoring what customers are actually weighing them against.

## Fails When
- **You list competitors from your product team's watchlist instead of from real deals.** Your product team tracks horizon threats. Your sales team sees what's actually on shortlists. These are different lists. For positioning, only the shortlist list matters.
- **You refuse to count the status quo.** If your customers' alternative is "keep using the spreadsheet," that's a competitive alternative — often the hardest one to beat. Ignoring it means ignoring the reason most deals die.
- **You list so many alternatives you can't differentiate against any of them.** If everything is an alternative, nothing is. Prioritize by how often it shows up in real buyer deliberation.

## Core Concept

A competitive alternative is what your customer would actually do if your product didn't exist. Not what you consider your competition. Not what your product team benchmarks against. What your target customer would actually pick instead. That distinction is the whole game.

There are two types of alternatives, and you need both on the list.

**Status quo alternatives** — what the customer is doing today, before they buy anything new. For B2B tech, the most common status-quo alternative is some combination of Excel, manual processes, and a general-purpose tool being used for a narrow purpose. "We built a template in Google Sheets." "We hired an intern." "We've been using the reporting feature in our ERP, even though it wasn't built for this." Sales teams often describe losses to status quo as "no decision," but that's dangerous framing. The customer did decide — they decided to stick with what they had. You competed against inertia and inertia won.

**Direct alternatives** — other vendors that actually show up on the buyer's shortlist during a purchase process. Note: *on the buyer's shortlist*. You may know of five competitors. If only two of them appear in real deals, those two are your direct alternatives. The others are ghosts — companies that exist in your strategy deck but not in your customer's consideration set. Ghosts don't count. When and if they start showing up on real shortlists, you'll adjust your positioning. Until then, ignore them.

Here's the key move: customers almost always see the competitive landscape differently than vendors do. You obsess over the two startups in your space who got funding recently. Your customers have never heard of them. Your customers are weighing you against "do nothing" and against the legacy monolith they already own. Positioning against the competitors *you* worry about, rather than the alternatives *customers* actually consider, is a common failure mode. It leads to messaging that fights battles your customers aren't fighting.

A second key move: group the alternatives into *approaches*. You're not competing against companies — you're competing against approaches to the problem. One approach might be "manual labor plus a spreadsheet." Another might be "easy-to-use all-purpose tools (Trello-class)." Another might be "heavy enterprise tools (Jira-class)." Grouping alternatives this way is how you help buyers make sense of the market and how you set up a clean comparison to your differentiated value. Individual company names are messy and political; approaches are clean and teachable.

## How to Apply

1. **Interview your best-fit customers and ask: what would you have done if our product didn't exist?** This is the single most important question in positioning. Their answer will surprise you more often than not. Don't suggest answers. Let them tell you.

2. **Separately, ask your sales team: what do we actually see on shortlists in real deals?** Your sales team knows this better than marketing or product. They've heard the buyer say "we're also looking at X" a thousand times. Pull a win/loss review if you need data. This gives you your direct alternatives.

3. **Include the status quo explicitly, with specifics.** Not just "do nothing." Name the actual current state. "They track project hours in a shared spreadsheet." "They export from the CRM and manipulate it in Excel." "They pay a contractor to generate the report quarterly." Specificity is what lets you later articulate the pain that switching to your product relieves.

4. **Group alternatives into approaches, not company names.** Two or three groups is ideal. Five is a maximum. Give each approach a clean name ("manual + spreadsheet," "all-purpose tool," "enterprise platform"). The groups should reflect how buyers think about their options, not how vendors market themselves.

5. **Rank the groups by frequency.** Which approach shows up most often in real deals? Which shows up occasionally? Which is rare? Weight your positioning work toward the frequent ones. You don't need to position against every edge case.

6. **Delete the ghosts.** If you listed a competitor who never shows up in real deals, delete them. You don't need to position against them. Your product team can keep tracking them as a horizon threat; your positioning doesn't need to carry them.

7. **Revisit quarterly.** Competitors emerge and die. Status quo shifts as the market matures. If a new competitor starts appearing on shortlists, adjust. If an old one stops appearing, drop them.

## Examples

**Situation:** A B2B SaaS founder says "we have no direct competitors, we're a new category."
**Application:** Ask them what their three most recent customers were doing before they bought. Inevitably the answers include "they had a spreadsheet," "they had built something internal," and "they were using a feature buried inside their CRM for this." Those are the three competitive alternatives. You group them as "manual + homegrown + spreadsheet" and that becomes the thing you position against.
**Result:** The founder stops trying to articulate differentiation against hypothetical competitors and starts articulating it against the real alternative: the status quo. The pitch gets sharper because it's answering a real buyer question.

**Situation:** A sales enablement company is losing deals and the team assumes it's to competitors.
**Application:** Pull the win/loss data. Most losses are to "no decision" — buyers stayed on their existing learning management system. That LMS is the real competitive alternative, not the other sales enablement startups. The positioning needs to directly contrast "LMS" with "sales enablement that measures sales outcomes."
**Result:** The team stops spending pitch time on feature comparisons against smaller competitors and spends it explaining why a generic LMS cannot do what a purpose-built sales enablement tool can. Close rates improve on deals where the buyer was leaning toward their existing LMS.

**Situation:** A project management tool for agencies is lost in a sea of PM tools.
**Application:** Don't list every PM tool. Group them into approaches: manual solutions (status quo — Excel plus Word), easy all-purpose tools (Trello-class), enterprise tools (Jira-class). Now the positioning has somewhere to go: we're designed for the middle — agencies that have outgrown spreadsheets but are too small and too margin-conscious for enterprise tooling.
**Result:** The positioning gets a clean shape because the alternatives got a clean shape. Buyers can see where you fit the moment you paint the market for them.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Include every competitor your product team tracks.
**Why:** A competitor who doesn't show up on real shortlists isn't a competitive alternative — it's a ghost. Positioning against ghosts gives buyers new names to go Google, which delays their decision. It also means your positioning is answering a question nobody is asking.

**Don't:** Describe the status quo as "no decision" in your win/loss.
**Why:** "No decision" is a label that lets the team off the hook. The reality is the buyer *did* decide — they decided to keep doing what they were doing. Understanding exactly what they kept doing is how you figure out what you have to beat. "We lost to Excel" is actionable. "No decision" is not.

**Don't:** Start positioning work by listing your features. Always start with alternatives.
**Why:** Features are only "unique" relative to the alternatives. If you start with features, you're unconsciously benchmarking against the competitors you already have in mind — usually the wrong ones. Start with alternatives and the features that matter become obvious.

**Don't:** Let marketing name the alternatives alone.
**Why:** Marketing doesn't live in the shortlist conversation. Sales does. If you do this exercise without sales in the room, you'll get aspirational alternatives ("we compete with [leader]") instead of real ones ("we usually come in against their homegrown Google Docs solution and a one-man consulting firm").

**Don't:** Name competitors by name in the pitch unless you know the buyer is already looking at them.
**Why:** Naming a competitor the buyer hadn't considered gives them a homework assignment. They'll go Google it and your deal slows down. Use categorical language ("enterprise tools like Jira") unless you're dead certain the buyer has them on the shortlist.
