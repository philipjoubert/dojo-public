# The Sales Pitch Structure

## When to Use
- You've got positioning nailed down and now you need a sales pitch that actually reflects it.
- Your current pitch starts with a product demo, a company history slide, or a generic problem statement that every competitor also opens with.
- Sales deals are dying in first call or losing to "no decision" — the pitch isn't helping buyers make a choice.
- You're building or rebuilding a first-call deck and want a battle-tested structure, not a "vision narrative" or "hero's journey" framework.

## Fails When
- **Your positioning is weak.** The pitch structure is only as good as the inputs. If your alternatives, differentiated value, target customer, and category aren't clear, the pitch will still be mush. Garbage in, garbage out.
- **The sales team isn't trained on it.** Reps default to what they know. If you roll out a new deck but don't train on the flow, reps will revert to demo-first inside a week.
- **The rep skips the setup and jumps to the follow-through.** The setup (Insight, Alternatives, Perfect World) is the thing that makes the follow-through work. Skip it and you're back to the product walkthrough that wasn't winning deals before.

## Core Concept

A good sales pitch doesn't begin with a product demo. It doesn't begin with a problem statement every competitor could make. It doesn't begin with a hero's journey or a vision slide. It begins with *the market insight that makes your differentiated value important to a specific customer*. Everything else flows from there.

The structure has two phases: **the setup** and **the follow-through**. The setup is about the market. The follow-through is about your solution. Don't invert them. Don't shortcut them.

**The Setup** — three steps:

1. **Insight.** Open with your unique point of view on the market. The problem inside the problem. What do prospects need to understand in order for your differentiated value to matter? This is not a generic problem statement. Generic problems are problems every competitor can claim to solve. Your insight is the specific angle on the problem that only your product is built to address. One or two minutes. One slide if you're using slides.

2. **Alternatives.** Walk the prospect through the options in the market — grouped into approaches, not individual company names. Manual / status quo. Easy-to-use all-purpose tools. Enterprise heavy tools. Whatever the approaches are in your market. Outline pros and cons of each for a customer who looks like the prospect. This step is conversational — you're doing discovery while you're teaching. You learn what they're doing today. They learn how to think about the market.

3. **The Perfect World.** Summarize: if you were designing a perfect solution for a customer like this one, based on the alternatives we just discussed, what would it look like? List the key characteristics of that ideal. Then the magic turn: "right?" If the prospect agrees, they've just agreed that their purchase criteria look like your differentiated value. The deal is yours to lose. If they disagree, they've disqualified themselves — which is a good outcome too, because now you're not wasting the rest of the meeting.

**The Follow-Through** — five steps:

4. **The Introduction.** Introduce the product using your market category. "We're X for Y." This is where you use the category you picked during positioning. Short. Move on.

5. **Differentiated Value.** The meat. This is where most of your time goes in a first call. You're showing — often via demo — the value that only your product delivers, and the features that enable that value. Critical move: the demo is *not* a product walkthrough. It's a demonstration of the specific differentiated value the customer just agreed they wanted. You skip features that are table-stakes. You deep-dive on the features that prove the unique value.

6. **Proof.** Case studies, analyst quotes, customer testimonials, hard deployment data. You can't expect the buyer to believe your value claims on your say-so — their procurement team certainly won't. Proof is not optional.

7. **Objections (optional).** If there are common objections you know will kill the deal if they surface later — security, integration complexity, pricing concerns — handle them here, proactively. Not every pitch needs this step. Sometimes it's better to handle objections in a follow-up. Your call.

8. **The Ask.** A clear, specific next step. Not "we'll follow up." A meeting. A pilot. A technical deep-dive. Something with a date on the calendar.

The genius of this structure — and why it's different from "problem/solution" pitches or "hero's journey" pitches — is that it starts by positioning your product relative to other alternatives in the market, from the first minute. You're not selling your product in a vacuum. You're teaching the buyer how to think about the market in a way that naturally points at your differentiated value.

## How to Apply

1. **Start from your positioning.** Pull out your five components. Every step of the pitch maps to a positioning component. Insight comes from differentiated value + best-fit customer. Alternatives maps directly to competitive alternatives. Perfect World maps to differentiated value. Introduction uses market category. Differentiated value in the pitch maps to unique attributes + differentiated value in positioning. You do not build this pitch from scratch — you translate positioning into pitch.

2. **Write the Insight first.** This is the hardest step and the one most pitches get wrong. Start with your differentiated value and reverse-engineer: what does a buyer need to know about the market for this value to matter? That's the insight. It should be something only you would say — if a competitor could say the same thing, it's not specific enough.

3. **Write Alternatives as a conversation, not a pitch.** Reps will do discovery here. Keep the slide (if any) sparse — a table with three columns (approach, pros, cons). The slide is a conversation guide, not a script. You want the rep asking "how are you handling this today?" and listening.

4. **Land Perfect World with a confirming question.** "In a perfect world, a tool like this would do A, B, and C. Right?" That "right?" is the pivot point. Yes: the deal is qualified and teed up. No: the prospect is disqualified (which is fine — better to find out now).

5. **Reshape the demo to lead with differentiated value.** Every demo I see in the field starts with the homepage, walks through the menu, shows the admin settings, and eventually gets to the thing that matters. Flip it. Start the demo with the thing that matters. Skip the features that are table-stakes. Double-click on the features that prove your unique value.

6. **Time-budget the setup.** In a 30-to-60 minute first call, the setup (Insight + Alternatives + Perfect World) should take 5-10 minutes. Tight. The follow-through gets the rest of the time. If the setup is taking 20 minutes, it's turned into a lecture. Cut it.

7. **Train the sales team.** Don't just hand them a deck. Walk through the flow. Roleplay first calls. Make sure reps understand *why* each step is there, not just what the slides say. Otherwise they'll revert to demo-first by the second week.

## Examples

**Situation:** A sales enablement company has strong positioning but first-call close rates are flat.
**Application:** Build the pitch. Insight: sales enablement only matters if it moves sales metrics — time-to-first-deal, time-to-quota. A company realizes the value of onboarding only when it can measure it. Alternatives: shared drive (cheap, untrackable), CMS (organized content, can't track learning), LMS (tracks learning, can't tie to sales outcomes). Perfect World: a tool that does what an LMS does plus connects directly to sales metrics. Introduction: "We're the only sales enablement platform built on Salesforce." Differentiated Value demo: show how the platform correlates training events to sales pipeline movement. Proof: named customer case study with specific ramp-time reduction numbers.
**Result:** Every first call now teaches the buyer the framework that makes the unique value matter. The pitch stops being a feature tour and becomes a market-shaping conversation.

**Situation:** A startup's sales team opens first calls with "let me walk you through our platform."
**Application:** That's a product walkthrough, not a pitch. There's no setup. The buyer has no context for why any of the features matter. Restructure: before the demo, spend 5 minutes on Insight + Alternatives + Perfect World. Only then does the demo happen, and it happens with the buyer already oriented toward the differentiated value.
**Result:** Reps report that buyers now nod along during the demo instead of politely waiting for it to end. Close rates climb. Deals that would have stalled at "we need to think about it" now progress because the setup already made the case.

**Situation:** A rep says "I don't have time for the setup, the meetings are only 30 minutes."
**Application:** The setup is the reason the meeting converts. Skipping it to "save time" means you're going to burn the whole 30 minutes on a product demo the buyer has no framework for, which is why your meetings aren't converting. Tighten the setup — one slide per step, two minutes each — but don't skip it. Six minutes of setup is a better 30-minute meeting than zero minutes of setup.
**Result:** Rep tries it reluctantly, reports back that "it actually goes faster now because the buyer stops asking random feature questions and starts engaging with the value."

**Worked Example — SnoutDesk.** For a full worked example of this structure flowing across a real (fictional) pitch deck, see `personas/april-dunford/content/sources/snoutdesk-sales-pitch-example.md`. SnoutDesk is a made-up Business Management Software for mid-to-large pet services companies, used to demonstrate how a positioning canvas translates into a first-call narrative across 15 slides. Key things to study in the example: the Alternatives slide is a three-column table with honest pros and cons for each approach (don't trash the competition); the Perfect World slide is explicitly optional and often evaporates into the Alternatives conversation; Introduction anchors on the market category phrase ("Business Management Software for Pet Services Businesses"); Value is split into 1A/1B sub-slides when a single theme has two strong supporting branches; Proof is case-study-specific to the exact promises in the Value slides (not a generic logo wall); Objections is skipped when there are no unspoken objections worth handling proactively; the Ask is a concrete next meeting, not "let us know what you think."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with a company history slide. "We were founded in 2019 by two ex-Google engineers..."
**Why:** Nobody cares. The buyer is trying to solve a problem. Every second you spend on company history is a second not spent teaching them how to think about your market. Kill the slide.

**Don't:** Lead with a generic problem statement.
**Why:** Generic problems attract generic solutions. "Teams struggle with alignment" could be solved by fifty competitors. Your insight has to be *specific enough that it points at your product*, not at the category.

**Don't:** Use a vision narrative in a sales pitch.
**Why:** Vision narratives are for investors. Buyers want to solve a problem today with budget they have today. Pitching them on a vision five years out just gives them a reason to wait. Save the vision for the VC deck.

**Don't:** Name every competitor by name in the Alternatives step.
**Why:** Naming competitors the buyer hadn't heard of gives them homework. They'll go research those competitors and the deal slows down. Use approach-level language ("enterprise tools like Jira") unless you know for certain they're already on the shortlist.

**Don't:** Skip the "right?" confirmation in Perfect World.
**Why:** That confirmation is the moment the buyer internally commits to your value as their purchase criteria. Without it, you've explained a perfect world but haven't checked that the buyer agrees it's perfect for them. If they don't agree, better to know now than after a six-week evaluation.

**Don't:** Let the demo be "here's our product."
**Why:** A context-free product walkthrough teaches the buyer nothing about why this product is different. The demo has to be organized around the differentiated value points you established in the setup. Every screen you show is proving a value claim. If it's not, skip that screen.
