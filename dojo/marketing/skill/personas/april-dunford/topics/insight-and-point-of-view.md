# Insight and Point of View

## When to Use
- You're writing the first two minutes of a sales pitch, first-call conversation, or keynote.
- The opening of your pitch feels interchangeable with every competitor's opening.
- You know your differentiated value but haven't articulated what buyers need to *understand about the market* for that value to matter.
- You're developing thought leadership — articles, podcast appearances, conference talks — and need a unique market angle rather than a generic trend take.

## Fails When
- **There's no differentiated value behind the insight.** The insight has to point at your unique value. If there's no unique value to point at, no amount of cleverness in the insight will save you. Fix the positioning first.
- **The insight is a platitude a competitor could claim.** "AI is changing everything." "Buyers expect more from their tools." "The old way isn't working." These are trend observations, not insights. Every competitor could open with them. Cut.
- **The insight is too complex.** If it takes five slides to explain, the buyer will lose the thread. Insight should land in 30 seconds. One frame. One idea.

## Core Concept

The opening of a sales pitch — the first thing out of a sales rep's mouth, the first slide in the deck — is the most valuable real estate in the entire sales process. Most companies waste it on company history, a generic problem statement, or worst of all, a product demo. The right thing to put there is your unique market insight.

An insight is your point of view on the market — the problem *inside* the problem. It's the angle only you would take, because only your product is built around it. A good insight immediately establishes three things:

1. **You're credible.** You know the space. You've seen the patterns. You're not a generalist.
2. **You've framed the conversation.** The buyer is now thinking about the problem *your way* — which is the frame in which your differentiated value is obvious.
3. **You've pointed at your value without pitching it.** You haven't talked about your product yet. But everything you just said makes it clear that the kind of solution that fits this frame is... the kind of solution you sell.

Here's the test: if a competitor could open with your insight word-for-word, it isn't an insight. It's a platitude. If the problem you're stating is a problem every solution in your category claims to solve, you haven't differentiated yet. "How do we train sales reps effectively?" — every sales enablement tool solves that. Not an insight. "Sales enablement only creates value when it's measured against sales outcomes like time-to-first-deal and time-to-quota" — now that points at a specific approach. A competitor without measurement capability cannot open with that line. That's an insight.

The way to find your insight is to reverse-engineer from your differentiated value. Ask: what does a buyer need to understand about the market for my unique value to matter? What's the angle on the problem that makes my specific capabilities the obvious answer?

Sometimes the insight was the reason you built the product — the founding insight that said "the existing category solves the visible problem but misses the real one." Other companies only articulate their insight after years of selling, when they finally see the pattern. Either is fine. Both produce the same artifact: a single frame on the market that makes your product the natural next step.

A crucial practical point: insight isn't supposed to be complex. It's not a long research presentation. It's one idea, one frame, delivered in about a minute. Sometimes it's a simple statement of fact. That's OK. The goal isn't to blow the buyer's mind — the goal is to orient them. Get them nodding. Move on to Alternatives, where you can flesh out the picture of the market.

## How to Apply

1. **Start from your differentiated value.** What is the one thing only you deliver? Write it down.

2. **Ask: what does the buyer need to understand for this value to matter?** Not "what's a problem they have?" — too generic. Specifically: what framing of their situation makes *my unique value* the obvious answer? That's the insight.

3. **Stress-test against competitors.** Could any direct alternative or status-quo alternative open with the same insight? If yes, sharpen it until the answer is no. The insight should be something a buyer would hear and think "that's a specific point of view I haven't heard stated that way before."

4. **Check it against your best-fit customer.** Will a best-fit buyer hear your insight and immediately nod, "yes, that's how we think about it too"? If not, it's either not landing or you're aiming at the wrong segment. A great insight makes your best-fit buyer feel seen.

5. **Write it tight.** Two or three sentences. One slide if you're using slides. If it takes five slides and ten minutes to explain, it's not insight, it's a whitepaper. Cut until it lands in under 60 seconds.

6. **Back it with evidence only if needed.** Some insights need one data point for credibility ("X% of companies struggle with this"). Most don't. Don't pad the setup with research for its own sake.

7. **Use it consistently — everywhere.** The insight isn't just the opening of the sales pitch. It's the core of your thought leadership, the message in your keynote, the hook in your content, the frame in your homepage copy. One company, one point of view, everywhere.

## Examples

**Situation:** A sales enablement startup's pitch opens with "companies need to train their sales reps."
**Application:** That's a category-wide problem every competitor solves. Not an insight. Reverse-engineer from their differentiated value — they're built on Salesforce and can tie training activity to sales outcomes. So their insight is: "Sales enablement only pays off when you can measure its impact on sales metrics. Most tools track completion of training, not whether training moved the needle." That's something only a company with sales-data integration would say first.
**Result:** The pitch opens with a frame that positions LMS-class tools as insufficient *before* the rep ever shows the product. By the time they get to the demo, the buyer is already looking for "can this thing measure impact?" — which is exactly where the demo is built to shine.

**Situation:** An API development platform's pitch starts with a product walkthrough.
**Application:** No insight at all — straight to product. Reverse-engineer from value: they unify what used to be a pile of disconnected API tools. Insight: "APIs have become critical infrastructure, but most companies treat them as an afterthought and manage them with siloed tools — which makes quality, governance, and consistency impossible at scale." A buyer who hears that and nods is already pre-sold on the category of solution this platform delivers.
**Result:** The pitch opens with a market frame that makes the platform's unified approach feel like the obvious answer to a problem the buyer now sees more clearly.

**Situation:** A project management tool for agencies opens with "teams struggle with collaboration."
**Application:** That's a platitude. Every PM tool claims to solve it. Reverse-engineer from value: they do multi-project resource planning for thin-margin agencies. Insight: "Custom-dev agencies run on thin margins. The difference between a profitable project and a loss-making one is almost always resource allocation across multiple simultaneous projects — not task tracking within a single project." A buyer running a 20-person agency hears that and feels seen.
**Result:** The opening orients the buyer toward a specific operational reality that makes multi-project resource planning — not general PM features — the value they're going to care about.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with a trend observation. "AI is changing everything." "Remote work is the new normal."
**Why:** Every competitor opens with the same trend observations. You've just demonstrated that you don't have a distinctive point of view. Start with a frame *only you would take*, not the frame everyone in the category is taking.

**Don't:** Use your insight as a data dump.
**Why:** An insight isn't a research presentation. Two or three sentences. One frame. If you have supporting data, use one data point and move on. The goal is orientation, not education.

**Don't:** Make the insight long or clever.
**Why:** Sometimes the insight seems obvious, almost like a statement of fact. That's fine. Your goal is to get the buyer nodding and to frame the rest of the conversation. An obvious, clear insight that lands is better than a clever, complex insight that leaves the buyer confused.

**Don't:** Skip the insight because "the buyer already knows the market."
**Why:** Buyers don't. Even in mature markets, most buyers have done one or two purchase cycles ever. You've done hundreds. Your insight is teaching them a way to think about their situation that they haven't heard before. That's valuable to them, even — especially — when they think they know the category.

**Don't:** Have a different insight for every customer segment.
**Why:** Your insight is your company's point of view on the market. One company, one insight. You can tailor examples and evidence for different segments, but the underlying frame stays constant. Companies with multiple insights usually have unclear positioning underneath.

**Don't:** Try to differentiate at the *problem* level instead of at the *insight* level.
**Why:** Problems aren't unique. Dozens of companies solve the same problem. Insights are unique — they're your particular angle on the problem that points at your particular solution. Build your differentiation at the insight layer, not the problem layer.
