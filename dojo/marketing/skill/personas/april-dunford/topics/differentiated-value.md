# Differentiated Value

## When to Use
- The team has the features documented but not the value. This is the single most common gap in B2B positioning.
- Sales decks open with a product tour. The first real value statement doesn't show up until slide twelve.
- The messaging reads "we're fast, easy, and reliable" — language indistinguishable from every competitor's homepage.
- The team can tell you what their product *has* but not what a customer *gets*.
- You're writing a sales pitch and need the meat of the differentiated-value section.

## Fails When
- **Value is stated without proof.** "Increase productivity 30%" without a case study, third-party data, or hard number from a deployment is noise. Buyers won't believe it and their procurement team definitely won't.
- **Value is something every competitor also claims.** If your "differentiated value" is something any competitor could copy into their deck, it isn't differentiated. It's a category expectation.
- **Value is actually an objection handle.** "We have SOC 2." "We integrate with your existing stack." These aren't value. These are reasons you aren't disqualified. Mixing them into your value story buries the real differentiation under noise.

## Core Concept

Differentiated value is the value your product delivers that customers *can only get from you*. Not value your product delivers, full stop. Value only you deliver. That distinction is the difference between a pitch that wins and a pitch that gets a polite "thanks, we'll get back to you."

The path to differentiated value runs: **features → benefits → value → who cares**. Every step matters, and teams skip steps all the time.

**Feature** is something your product has or does. "15-megapixel camera." "Patented indexing algorithm." "Built on Salesforce." Features are facts about the product.

**Benefit** is what the feature enables. "Sharper photos." "Faster query response." "Access to Salesforce data." Benefits are mechanical — one level of "so what?" applied to the feature.

**Value** is what the benefit enables in the customer's *business* or *life*. "Photos that still look sharp when printed at poster size." "Answering customer questions in the same phone call instead of calling them back." "Measuring the revenue impact of sales training programs." Value ties the benefit to a goal the customer actually cares about.

The "so what?" test is the tool. Take every feature and ask "so what?" Keep asking until you hit a business outcome the customer cares about. You are not done until you've hit a goal that a CFO, a CEO, or the person writing the check can articulate as important. Anything short of that is a benefit, not value.

Then the last step: **who cares?** Not every customer cares about every value. A feature that enables "reduces invoice errors" is enormous value to a company sending 5,000 invoices a month and irrelevant to a company sending five. Value is always relative to a segment. If you claim universal value, you have fuzzy value, which is the same thing as weak value.

And the most important gate: **"differentiated"** value. Differentiated against the competitive alternatives. If the alternative you're being compared to also delivers the value, it's not a differentiator — it's a table-stake. If every competitor also claims to deliver it, it's not a differentiator — it's a platitude. You have to be able to say "no other alternative delivers this specific combination of value" to a best-fit customer. If you can't, go back to the alternatives list and the attributes list, because something's off.

One more thing: value and objection handling are not the same, and confusing them is a common failure mode. **Value** is the reason the buyer picks *you over the alternatives*. **Objection handling** is the reason they don't get disqualified from consideration. "We have enterprise-grade security" is objection handling — nobody is choosing you because you have SOC 2, but if you don't, you get cut. Don't lead with objection handles. Lead with value. Use objection handles to keep the deal alive after value has been established.

## How to Apply

1. **Start from unique attributes, not from a generic "what's our value" brainstorm.** You've already done the alternatives-and-attributes work. Walk down the attribute list systematically.

2. **For each unique attribute, apply the "so what?" test repeatedly.** First "so what?" usually gets you to a mechanical benefit. Second "so what?" usually gets you to an operational outcome. Third or fourth "so what?" gets you to the business goal the buyer cares about. Keep going until the answer reaches something a CEO would nod at.

3. **Cluster value into themes.** You should not end up with forty value bullets. Group them. Aim for one to four clusters. One is common. A single, razor-sharp value theme is much stronger than four fuzzy ones. Customers can remember one thing. They cannot remember seven.

4. **Gate each value theme through the differentiation test.** For every theme, ask: can any of our competitive alternatives also claim this? If the answer is yes for all of them, it's not differentiated — it's a category expectation. Either cut it or reframe it in terms where the differentiation holds.

5. **Gate each value theme through the "who cares?" test.** Which segment cares about this specifically, and why? If you can't identify a specific segment whose situation makes this value matter a lot, the value is too generic. Make it more specific.

6. **Attach proof to every claim.** Customer quotes. Third-party validation. Hard deployment data. Analyst validation. If you're claiming "3x faster," there's a customer who measured it. If there isn't, drop the number and reframe the claim qualitatively — or go get the data.

7. **Separate value from objection handling explicitly.** Make a second list of things buyers ask about that aren't reasons they pick you — security, compliance, integration, pricing model, service levels. Those go in a separate part of the pitch. They don't belong in the value section.

## Examples

**Situation:** A sales enablement startup claims its value is "sales training that drives results."
**Application:** Walk the chain. Feature: built on Salesforce. Benefit: access to Salesforce sales data inside the enablement platform. Value first pass: "trackable training." So what? Can measure whether training moves metrics like time-to-first-deal and time-to-quota. So what? Sales enablement leaders can prove the ROI of their programs and iterate to improve sales outcomes. So what? Companies onboarding new reps can get those reps productive weeks faster, which maps directly to revenue. *That* is the value. "Sales enablement that connects training to revenue outcomes" — and no other alternative (shared drive, CMS, LMS) can claim it.
**Result:** The value statement stops being a platitude and becomes a concrete, defensible claim tied to a revenue metric the buyer will actually fight to protect.

**Situation:** A team says their differentiators are "fast, easy, and reliable."
**Application:** That's three platitudes, not three differentiators. Every competitor says the same thing. Force the team to reframe each in terms of unique value. "Fast" — fast at *what* specifically, compared to *which alternatives*, with *what proof*? If you're fast to deploy compared to enterprise tools, the real value is "live in 30 days instead of 18 months, with an internal team rather than a consulting engagement." That's a differentiator. "Fast" is not.
**Result:** The team kills two of the three platitudes and sharpens the third into a specific, defensible value claim with proof. The pitch immediately stops sounding generic.

**Situation:** A team's "value" list includes "we have SOC 2 compliance, we integrate with Salesforce, our customer support is 24/7, and we drive a 3x improvement in time-to-revenue for customer-facing teams."
**Application:** The first three are objection handles. Every enterprise buyer will ask about them, but none of them is why a customer picks this vendor over another. The fourth is real value. The team needs to structure the pitch around the fourth and use the first three only when an objection specifically comes up or in a late-stage IT review.
**Result:** The pitch foregrounds the one thing that actually differentiates and stops wasting top-of-deck airtime on table-stakes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write value in the language of marketing platitudes ("unlock your potential," "supercharge your workflow," "revolutionize your business").
**Why:** These phrases are indistinguishable from a thousand other companies' homepages. They don't teach the buyer anything. Use concrete, specific outcome language. "Reduces invoice errors by 80% for companies sending over 2,000 invoices a month" beats "unlock invoicing excellence" every time.

**Don't:** Claim value without proof.
**Why:** An unbacked claim is marketing fluff. A buyer's procurement team will absolutely not let them buy based on an unbacked claim. Proof isn't optional — it's the thing that makes the value real. If you don't have proof, you either aren't ready to make the claim, or you need to go get a customer to validate it.

**Don't:** Mix value and objection handling in the same section of your pitch.
**Why:** They serve different purposes. Value is why they pick you. Objection handling is why they don't cut you. Buyers process the two differently, and blending them makes both weaker. Separate them explicitly.

**Don't:** Claim "universal value" that applies to all customers.
**Why:** Value that's valuable to everyone is usually weak-to-medium value to anyone specifically. Value that's acute to a particular segment is the value that wins deals. Narrower, sharper value beats broader, fuzzier value every time.

**Don't:** Let one executive say "our product has no differentiation" and accept it without running the exercise.
**Why:** In my experience, when a team insists they have no differentiation, it's almost always wrong. The product is differentiated — the team just hasn't articulated it, or parts of the team are blind to what customers actually value. Before accepting the "no differentiation" claim, do the work. Interview the happy customers. Run the feature → value chain. The differentiation is almost always there; it's just buried.
