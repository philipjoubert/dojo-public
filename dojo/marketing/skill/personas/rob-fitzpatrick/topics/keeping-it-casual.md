# Keeping It Casual

## When to Use
- Whenever you're in early discovery and the goal is to *learn* (not sell, not demo).
- At any industry event, meetup, conference, party, or coffee shop where your potential customers happen to be.
- When you've been struggling to get meetings — there's a good chance you're trying to formalise something that should be a chat.
- When you have a serendipitous encounter (the "dream customer next to you in line") and you'd otherwise let it slip past because "it's not a meeting."

## Fails When
- **You need to sell or demo.** Once you've got a product and you're pushing for advancement, casual is the wrong mode. Block 30+ minutes, run a real meeting, ask for the commitment. Casual is the early-discovery superpower; sales is structured.
- **The contact is gated.** A C-level exec at a Fortune 500 isn't going to "grab a coffee." The casual frame relies on social adjacency — when you don't have it, you need a properly framed ask (Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask) and a calendar block.
- **You haven't worked out what you want to learn.** Casual doesn't mean unprepared. Walking into a chat with no big questions just means you'll have a pleasant chat that taught you nothing.

## Core Concept

The Meeting Anti-Pattern is the tendency to relegate every opportunity for customer conversation into a calendar block. *"Should we set up a 30-minute call?"* / *"Let me grab some time with you next week."* / *"Hi, I'd like to interview you for our research."* All of these turn a chat into a Process, and a Process is exactly what you don't want, because it tells the other person: shields up, this is going to be tedious, and you'll probably try to sell me something at the end.

Imagine: you're in a crowded cafe and your dream date sits down next to you. You slam down an espresso to psych yourself up and fumble through some monologue about how they seem like a really nice person and would they want to grab a coffee sometime — like, a different coffee, at a different place? It's a silly situation. The whole purpose of a date is to talk and see if you get along. You're already on a date. You just messed it up by formalising it instead of having the conversation.

Same trick for early customer conversations. Strip the pomp and circumstance and reduce it from a meeting to a chat. If you do it right, *they won't even know you were learning about a business idea.* They'll just remember it as an interesting conversation. You'll walk away with as much value as a formal meeting and a tenth of the calendar overhead.

The early-stage answer to "where do customer learning conversations come from?" isn't a calendar tool. It's: *go where your customers already are, and have interesting chats.* I once played with a product idea on a Friday, figured out the big questions over the weekend, and went to an industry event on Monday. A handful of office managers were there. Without any of them realising we'd "had a meeting," I'd learned the big problem was really about debt collection rather than efficiency. I got there by being interested and asking things like *"X seems really annoying — how do you deal with it?"* / *"Is Y as bad as it sounds?"* over a beer.

The casual frame also creates the conditions where people will *actually tell you the truth*. In a formal meeting, shields are up — they're wondering when the pitch starts, whether you'll waste their time, whether what they say will be used in negotiation. In a chat, shields are down — they're just enjoying telling you about their day. That's where the real information is.

*Rule of thumb: Learning about a customer and their problems works better as a quick and casual chat than a long, formal meeting.*

## How to Apply

1. **Audit your "meeting requests" — are any of them really chats?** If you're trying to schedule a 30-minute Zoom to "ask a few questions about how you handle X," consider whether you could instead bump into them at the next industry meetup and just... ask. The meeting overhead is often more than the conversation requires.

2. **Show up where your customers physically are.** Industry meetups, conferences, hobbyist events, coworking spaces, niche message boards. The whole reason these people exist in groups is that the topic is on their mind. Walk in, ask a question, listen.

3. **Open with curiosity about *them*, not about your idea.** *"How did you end up getting this gig?"* / *"That sounds annoying — how are you handling it?"* / *"Where did the new cookbook on your shelf come from?"* You don't need a "reason for the conversation." The reason is that you're an interested human and the topic is interesting.

4. **Don't say you're starting a business.** That's a frame trigger. As soon as it's on the table, the other person starts wondering what you'll ask of them, and they start pulling their punches. If they ask what you do, you can be vague: *"I'm thinking about getting into this space, just trying to understand how it works."* Or even just answer with another question.

5. **Ten minutes is plenty.** Early conversations are very fast. It takes 5 minutes to learn whether a problem exists and is important. 10–15 minutes covers most of the early-stage learning you need from one person. Don't try to extract an hour from a chat — diminishing returns set in fast and you'll burn the relationship.

6. **Take notes after, not during.** Notes during a casual chat blow your cover. Have the conversation, then immediately retreat to a corner — bathroom stall, your car, a quiet wall — and write down what they said. Verbatim quotes where you can. Symbols + signals if you've got a system. (See `running-the-process.md` for the note-taking system.)

7. **Have your big question ready for serendipity.** You'll bump into people. The dream customer will sit next to you in a coffee shop and you'll have 90 seconds. Know your one most important question cold. *"How do you handle [the thing]?"* / *"What's the worst part of [the workflow]?"* You don't need a script — you need to be able to ask one good question without thinking.

8. **Switch out of casual the moment you have a product to validate.** Once you're in advancement / commitment territory, casual stops working — you need calendar slots, structured asks, and clear next steps. Casual is for "is this a real problem and do they care?" Structured is for "will you commit to using / paying for / piloting this?"

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder building tools for professional speakers bumps into one at a conference.

**Application:** Don't try to set up a meeting. Don't introduce the idea. Just ask: *"Hey, I'm curious — how did you end up getting this gig?"*

**Result:** Now you're having an interesting conversation about her career. She tells you how the booking worked, what the agent did or didn't do, what she wished was easier. As a side bonus, she'll remember you as the nice guy who was super interested in her career — far more likely to take a follow-up call later than if you'd asked for a meeting upfront. You walked into the conference with no leads and walked out with one strong relationship and a head full of customer insight.

---

**Situation:** A founder considering a tool to make office managers more efficient.

**Application:** He plays with the idea on Friday, figures out the big questions over the weekend, and goes to an industry event on Monday. Doesn't introduce his idea to anyone. Just asks: *"X seems really annoying — how do you deal with it?"* / *"Is Y as bad as it seems?"* / *"You guys did a great job with Z — where did you get that from?"*

**Result:** Without any of the office managers realising they'd "had a meeting," he learns that the real problem isn't efficiency — it's debt collection. None of them would have surfaced that in a formal interview about "office manager efficiency." It came out because the conversation was casual enough that they weren't editing themselves to fit his frame.

---

**Situation:** A founder is talking to me at a cafe about his coffee-bean-story product. He says he's been pavement-pounding for two weeks and getting turned away from cafes.

**Application:** I cut in: *"Who have you talked to so far?"* "Nobody — I don't know the owners and the staff won't help me." *"Have you talked to this cafe?"* "No, I don't know them." A waitress walks by. I flag her down: *"Can I speak to the owner?"* "Umm." *"Don't worry, it's nothing bad. This coffee is amazing and I wanted to ask him about the story behind the beans."* The owner wasn't around but the manager came over and gave us the owner's contact details for Tuesday.

**Result:** Two weeks of cold-pitching cafe owners had produced zero conversations. Five minutes of casual asking ("the coffee is amazing — what's the story?") produced a chat with the manager and a meeting with the owner. The mode change unblocked the entire customer development effort.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Open with *"Thanks for agreeing to this interview."*
**Why:** "Interview" is the kiss of death. It tells the other person: I have a script, I'm going to march through it, and you'll be politely bored for 30 minutes. They reply in interview mode — short, polished, unrevealing. The fix is not to call it an interview. *"Quick chat"* / *"can I pick your brain"* / *"got a weird question"* are all softer frames that produce more honest answers.

**Don't:** Use phrases like *"on a scale of 1 to 5..."* or *"could you rank these..."*
**Why:** Numerical ranking questions feel rigorous. They aren't. They produce confident-sounding answers that mean nothing — people will rank "convenience" above "cost" without ever having joined a gym. The casual equivalent is to ask about specific events: *"Talk me through the last time you tried that"* gets you real data. Asking them to rank hypothetical features gets you a lie.

**Don't:** Treat the casual frame as an excuse for being unprepared.
**Why:** Casual *style*, prepared *content*. You should still know your three big questions cold. You should still know who you're trying to reach and what you're trying to learn. The casualness is in the social register, not in the discipline. A casual conversation with no plan is just chitchat.

**Don't:** Try to keep it casual on a video call.
**Why:** Calls have an agenda by default. The other person joined the call to "have a meeting" — they're watching the clock and waiting for the point. The casual frame depends on physical context: walking together, waiting in line, sharing a beer, leaning against a wall at a meetup. Online, you have to manufacture casual space (see `remote-mom-test.md`) — which is harder, and works less well, but is sometimes necessary.

**Don't:** Skip the chat because *"I haven't prepared properly yet."*
**Why:** Founders use "I'm not ready" as an excuse to avoid the awkwardness. You'll never be ready. The first ten chats will feel weird. They'll get less weird. Show up with one good question and improvise the rest. Most of what you'll learn happens regardless of whether you have the perfect interview guide.
