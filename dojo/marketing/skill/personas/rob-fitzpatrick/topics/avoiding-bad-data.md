# Avoiding Bad Data

## When to Use
- After any customer conversation, when you're reviewing notes and trying to figure out what was real.
- When you catch yourself or a teammate saying "everyone loves it" or "we're getting a lot of positive feedback."
- When meetings end with warm vibes and no clear next step.
- When you keep accumulating feature requests and your roadmap is becoming a Frankenstein of "this one customer said..."

## Fails When
- **You over-correct and disregard everything.** Some compliments are just sign-offs ("nice meeting you, good luck"). Some "that's interesting" really is interesting. The point isn't to dismiss kind words — it's to refuse to count them as evidence. Distinguishing data from noise isn't the same as treating everything as noise.
- **You apply this without commitment-checking.** Filtering bad data is half the job. The other half is asking for something real (time, reputation, money). If you only filter and never ask for commitment, you'll just end up with cleaner-looking but equally hollow notes.

## Core Concept

They say that to bankrupt a fool, give him information. Practically every founder I've watched run customer conversations — myself included — has been giving themselves bad information. Bad data gives you false negatives (thinking the idea is dead when it's not) and, much more dangerously, false positives (convincing yourself you're right when you're not). A drunk who's been told he's sober is more dangerous than one who knows he's drunk. False positives are the same.

There are three flavours of bad data, and they show up in basically every conversation, regardless of how cleanly you ran the questions:

- **Compliments.** "That's so cool, I love it." "Great idea." "You're really onto something."
- **Fluff.** Generics ("I usually...", "I always...", "I never..."), hypothetical futures ("I would...", "I might..."), and feel-good promises ("I would definitely buy that").
- **Ideas.** Feature requests, suggestions, "have you thought about adding..."

None of these are facts. None of them are commitments. All three feel like signal because they involve enthusiastic noises and engaged body language. All three will, if you treat them as data, walk you off a cliff.

The good news is each one has a fix. Compliments get **deflected**. Fluff gets **anchored** (back to specific events in the past). Ideas get **dug into** (what's the motivation behind the request?). Once you've practiced the moves a few times, they become automatic.

*Rule of thumb: Compliments are the fool's gold of customer learning: shiny, distracting, and entirely worthless.*

## How to Apply

1. **Spot compliments and deflect them.** When you hear "that's really cool, I love it," don't say "thanks!" and move on — that's how you accidentally collect them. Apologise for slipping into pitch mode (whether you did or not, it's a useful framing) and pivot back to their life: *"Whoops — sorry, didn't mean to start pitching. You guys seem to have a handle on this — do you mind if I ask how you're dealing with this stuff at the moment?"* The compliment is now behind you. The conversation is back on facts.

2. **Spot fluff and anchor it.** When you hear "I always..." or "I usually..." or "I would definitely..." treat the answer as worth zero. Then ask the anchoring question: *"When's the last time that happened?"* Or: *"Can you talk me through that?"* Or: *"What did you do about it?"* The generic claim is replaced with a real specific event, which is data you can trust.

3. **Spot ideas and dig.** When someone says "you should add X" or "the killer feature would be Y," don't write it on your todo list. Ask why. *"What would that let you do?"* / *"How are you coping without it now?"* / *"How would that fit into your day?"* Nine times out of ten the request masks a deeper motivation that has a much simpler solution. The MTV story below is the canonical example.

4. **Watch the symptoms.** If you catch yourself or your team saying any of the following back at the office, you're collecting bad data:
   - "That meeting went really well."
   - "We're getting a lot of positive feedback."
   - "Everybody I've talked to loves the idea."
   - "He said he'd definitely buy it."
   None of these contain a fact. Each one is a vibe wearing data clothes.

5. **Don't fish for compliments and don't expose ego.** "I'm thinking of starting a business — do you think it'll work?" / "Here's the secret project I quit my job for — what do you think?" Both invite people to protect your feelings. Keep the idea and your ego out until you're ready to ask for a commitment.

6. **Cut yourself off when you slip into pitch mode.** Being pitchy is the dark side of seeking approval. *"No no, you don't get it..."* / *"Yes but it also does this!"* You will catch yourself doing it. Apologise, deflect, get back to their life: *"Sorry — got excited there. Can we go back to what you were saying about..."*

7. **Talk less.** When they say "so it's similar to..." or "I like it but..." it's tempting to interrupt and "fix" their understanding. Don't. They were about to give you a privileged glimpse into how they're processing what you do. The more you're talking, the worse you're doing.

## Examples

**Situation:** You're pitching and the prospect says, *"That's cool, love it."*

**Application:** Read this as a warning sign, not a compliment. Deflect: *"Whoops, sorry I slipped into pitching. Listen — you guys seem to be handling this well — how are you doing it at the moment?"*

**Result:** They drop the polite-compliment-mode and answer the actual question. *"What? Oh, well, sure. We've got a couple people who manage the process just to make sure we're all in sync, and then we use Excel and a lot of emails to keep it all moving."* Now you're talking real workflow. Two full-time employees plus Excel-and-email is a much louder signal than "love it." That's gold dust — you didn't know the workaround was that expensive.

---

**Situation:** Someone tells you, *"I'm an Inbox 0 zealot — it's totally changed my life."*

**Application:** Generic claim. Anchor it. *"Haha — I'm an Inbox 0 failure. What's your inbox at right now?"* → "About ten that have come in this morning." → *"When's the last time it totally fell apart for you?"* → "Three weeks ago — I was travelling and the hotel internet was down. Took me ten days to recover."

**Result:** The generic claim ("I'm always on top of email") got refined into the real picture: *"Except when I'm not, in which case it's a nightmare to recover from."* That's the actual product opportunity. The fluff disguised it; anchoring revealed it.

---

**Situation (the MTV story):** At my first company, MTV told me they needed analytics dashboards for their campaigns. I built a beautiful one. They never used it — kept calling me every Friday asking me to email a CSV. I built CSV export. They asked for PDFs. I built PDF export. They still called me every Friday.

**Application (the question I should have asked early):** Eventually they asked if I could add their logo to the reports, and I — finally, exasperated — asked: *"Why do you want this feature? What do branded reports get you that unbranded ones don't?"* They replied: *"Oh — nobody even reads these. Our clients just like to get something emailed to them every Friday and we think they'd be happier if it was a bit fancier."*

**Result:** They had asked for analytics. I'd assumed they wanted to *understand their data*. They'd actually wanted *a way to keep their own clients happy*. If I'd dug into the original request — "you want analytics, why?" — I would have built something a tenth as complicated and an intern could have run it. Three months wasted because I obeyed the feature request instead of understanding the motivation behind it.

*Rule of thumb: Ideas and feature requests should be understood, but not obeyed.*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Add "sync to Excel" (or whatever the request is) to your roadmap because a customer asked for it.
**Why:** Feature requests are the customer doing your job for you — they're suggesting a *solution* to a problem they own. They're almost always wrong about the solution. They might not even be right about the problem. Your job is to dig until you find the underlying motivation, then design the right solution. That's why people pay you.

**Don't:** Treat "I would definitely buy that" as a pre-order.
**Why:** This is the single most expensive lie in customer development. The first startup I worked at lost ten million bucks confusing this phrase for a commitment. People are wildly optimistic about what they'll do in the future. They are always more excited, more willing, and more "definitely" in the imagined future than they are once it arrives. The fix is to convert it into a current commitment — letter of intent, deposit, pre-order, intro to their boss, calendar slot for next week. If they can't (or won't) do any of those, the "yes" was just polite noise.

**Don't:** Take silence after a strong emotional signal as "I got the answer, moving on."
**Why:** I once watched a founder ask someone about a problem and hear back: *"Yeah, that's pretty much the worst part of my day."* He wrote it down and moved to the next question. *What?* It's the worst part of his day and you're not going to find out why? Any strong emotion — anger, embarrassment, excitement — is worth digging into. *"Tell me more about that."* / *"What makes it so bad?"* / *"Why haven't you been able to fix it already?"* The detail behind the emotion is where the product opportunity is.

**Don't:** Tell yourself "they were just being polite" and dismiss enthusiasm as fluff to feel rigorous.
**Why:** The point of filtering bad data isn't to disbelieve everything. It's to ask: *what currency did this cost them?* Compliments cost nothing. So they prove nothing. But if the same person who said "I love it" then introduces you to their boss, agrees to a paid trial, or buys a prototype — that's not fluff anymore. The currency is what you're filtering on. Don't dismiss the enthusiasm; require the commitment.

**Don't:** Skip the deflect because the meeting "is going well anyway."
**Why:** This is how meetings end with vibes-only outcomes — you, in the moment, decided that since they're being nice, you don't need to push. Then you walk out with no commitment, no concrete next step, and you're back in zombie-lead territory. Every compliment is a chance to redirect to facts. Take it.
