# The Mom Test

## When to Use
- Any time you're about to ask someone — friend, customer, investor, mentor, your actual mom — what they think of your idea.
- When you've been told "go talk to customers" and have no idea how to do that without it turning into a pitch.
- When you've already had a bunch of customer chats and they all "went great" but somehow you don't know any more than you did before.
- Before the meeting, not after. The whole point of the Mom Test is upstream — it shapes the questions you walk in with, not the conclusions you walk out with.

## Fails When
- **You apply the rules but ignore the spirit.** People rattle off "what was the last time you did X" like a survey question and wonder why the answers are wooden. The Mom Test is a way of paying attention, not a checklist.
- **You're already in pitch mode and trying to retroactively "do customer development."** If your ego is on the table, no question phrasing is going to save you. Cut yourself off, apologise, and reset.
- **The risk in your business is in the product, not the market.** Video games, social apps, anything where the question is "can we build something good enough" — conversations help around the edges (do they have a budget? can we reach them?) but they can't tell you if the thing is fun.

## Core Concept

People say you shouldn't ask your mom whether your business is a good idea. That's technically true, but it misses the point. You shouldn't ask *anyone* whether your business is a good idea. At least not in those words. Your mom will lie to you the most (just 'cuz she loves you), but it's a bad question and invites *everyone* to lie to you at least a little. It's not anyone else's responsibility to show us the truth. It's our responsibility to find it. We do that by asking good questions.

The Mom Test is three rules for crafting questions that even your mom can't lie to you about:

1. **Talk about their life instead of your idea.**
2. **Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future.**
3. **Talk less and listen more.**

That's it. It's deceptively simple, and the reason it works is that it strips out everything that invites lying. Opinions are worthless. Hypotheticals about the future are over-optimistic lies. Compliments are fool's gold. The only data that survives the lie-detector test is *facts about what the person actually does and has done*. Their life. The stuff already happening, with or without you.

If you just avoid mentioning your idea, you automatically start asking better questions. That single discipline — *don't say what you're building* — is the largest single improvement you can make to your customer conversations. When you do it right, they won't even know you have an idea.

A useful mental model: customer conversations are bad by default. You don't accidentally have a great learning chat. Every default move — explain your idea, ask if they like it, ask if they'd pay — is engineered to give you false positives. The Mom Test is the work of overriding those defaults.

*Rule of thumb: Customer conversations are bad by default. It's your job to fix them.*

## How to Apply

1. **Decide what you actually want to learn before the meeting.** Not "what do they think of my idea." Something like: do they actually have this problem, what are they doing about it now, how much does the workaround cost them, and is there a budget. If you can't write down what you want to learn, don't have the meeting.

2. **Strip your idea out of every question.** Read each question and ask: would this question still make sense if I had no idea at all? If the question only makes sense because you've got a specific product in mind, it's biased. Rewrite it so it's about *their* life.

3. **Anchor everything to specific events in the past.** "How do you usually deal with X" gets you a fictional version of the person they wish they were. "Talk me through the last time X happened" gets you the real them. Always swap the first for the second.

4. **Shut up.** Once you've asked a good question, your job is done for thirty seconds. Don't fill silences. Don't help them along. Don't correct their understanding of your idea, because you're not supposed to have shared it. Let them talk. The longer they talk, the more you learn.

5. **Cut yourself off when you slip into pitch mode.** You will. Everyone does. The recovery line is: *"Whoops — sorry, I just slipped into pitch mode. Listen, you guys seem to be doing well in this space — do you mind if I ask how you're handling this stuff at the moment?"* Apologise, deflect, get back to their life.

6. **Treat the silence after a "useful" answer as more important than the answer itself.** When someone says something interesting, the temptation is to ask the next question on your list. Don't. Ask "why?" or "tell me more about that" or "when's the last time that came up?" The good stuff is two layers down.

7. **End by asking who else you should talk to.** Not "is there anything else I should ask?" though that's also a fine crutch question. The intro ask is the real test of whether the meeting was worth anything. Nobody intros you to people they think you'll waste time with.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder is building digital cookbooks for the iPad. He decides to run his idea by his mom.

**Application (failing the test):**
> Son: "Mom, would you ever buy a cookbook app for your iPad? It's only $40 — cheaper than those hardcovers on your shelf. You can share recipes with friends, and there's a shopping list..."
> Mom: "Oh honey, that sounds amazing. And $40 *is* a good deal. Will it have pictures?"
> Son: *I have completely misinterpreted this conversation as validation.*

**Application (passing the test):** Don't mention the idea at all. Ask about her iPad use:
> Son: "How's the new iPad treating you?" → "What's the last thing you did on it?" → "Did you use an app for that?" → "Where did you find out about the other apps you use?" → "I saw a couple new cookbooks on the shelf — where did those come from?" → "What's the last cookbook you bought for yourself?"

**Result:** From the second conversation you learn (a) old people don't search the App Store — they wait for recommendations from a Sunday paper section; (b) they don't actually need another generic recipe collection; (c) the cookbooks they own are mostly gifts they haven't opened. Three concrete facts about her life. The first conversation gave him a fistful of false positives. The second gave him three things that should change his plan. Mom couldn't lie because the topic was never the idea — it was her.

---

**Situation:** A founder hears someone in a coffee line complain: *"Which idiot decided I should carry around a thousand cafe loyalty cards?"* He's building a mobile loyalty app.

**Application (failing):** "Hey, I'm building a mobile app to help stores give discounts to loyal customers — would you use something like that?" Answer: "Heck yes! I would definitely use that."

**Application (passing):** "It's crazy, right? Have you ever tried any of those loyalty apps for your phone?" Answer: "Oh yeah, I downloaded a couple. You need a different one for every chain. I don't want a hundred apps clogging my phone any more than I want a bunch of cards in my wallet."

**Result:** The first version produced a dazzling false positive. The second version revealed (a) the person is actively searching, which is useful, but (b) the constraint is critical mass at the chains they use, which the founder hadn't been thinking about. Nobody lied; the question framing didn't invite them to.

---

**Situation:** A founder is talking to construction companies about a supplier-management tool.

**Application:** Instead of asking "would you buy a supplier-management tool that did X?", he asks them to walk him through how they currently manage suppliers — what they love and hate about the way it works, what other tools they tried before settling on this one, where they're losing money with the current setup, whether there's a budget for better.

**Result:** He walks out with a real picture of the workflow, the budget ballpark, and the alternatives. He decides for himself whether it's a good idea. *Opinions are worthless. The point of the chat was the facts.*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Lead with "I'm working on an idea — can I run it by you?"
**Why:** The minute they know you have an idea you care about, the social contract switches from honest conversation to don't-hurt-his-feelings mode. They'll pull punches. They'll volunteer compliments. They'll ask "supportive" questions. Even people who *want* to give you brutal honesty will soften it. Keep the idea off the table for as long as humanly possible — ideally the entire conversation.

**Don't:** Ask "would you ever..." or "do you usually..." or "could you see yourself..."
**Why:** These invite generics and hypotheticals — fluff. Generics are who the person *wishes* they were. Hypotheticals are over-optimistic lies. Both feel concrete and aren't. If you find yourself asking one, treat the answer as worthless and immediately follow with: "When's the last time that actually happened?"

**Don't:** Treat the questions in the book (or any question list) as a script.
**Why:** You're having a conversation. Scripts produce stilted exchanges that telegraph "I am running an interview process" — which is the exact thing the Mom Test is trying to avoid. Memorise the *principles* (talk about their life, get specific, listen more) and then chat like a human who's curious. The questions emerge.

**Don't:** Conclude that a meeting "went well" because they were nice and said positive things.
**Why:** That's the warning sign, not the success metric. There's no such thing as a meeting that just "went well" — every meeting either succeeds (concrete fact, commitment, or rejection) or fails (compliments, stalls, "let me know when it launches"). If you're not sure which it was, it failed.

**Don't:** Use the Mom Test to confirm an idea you've already decided to build.
**Why:** Then you're not learning, you're shopping for permission. The Mom Test is engineered to surface contradictory information. If you set the conversation up to avoid contradiction, the rules will technically pass and the answers will technically be facts, but you'll cherry-pick the ones that support your existing belief and ignore the rest. The honest version is to walk in expecting to be surprised, and to actually update when you are.
