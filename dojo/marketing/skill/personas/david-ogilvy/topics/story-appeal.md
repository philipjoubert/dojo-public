# Story Appeal

## When to Use
- Choosing the lead image for a print advertisement. Poster. Out-of-home. Opening frame of a television commercial.
- The headline is written but the reader has to stop on the page before the headline has a chance to work. Five times more people look at the picture than read the body copy; the picture has to earn the stop.
- The product is a parity product and the differentiator is the aura around it — Hathaway's class, Rolls-Royce's silence, Schweppes's Englishness.
- Evaluating a beautifully shot but lifeless ad: the photograph is artistic, it wins awards, and it sells nothing. Story appeal is usually missing.

## Fails When
- **The arresting element has nothing to do with the product.** A naked woman on a kitchen stove is an eye-stopper and irrelevant — and my first advertisement, and a mistake. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The reader remembers the stunt, forgets the brand.
- **The category genuinely demands the product itself.** If you are advertising a fire extinguisher, open with the fire. — *Confessions* Don't manufacture drama around a product that already has its own drama.
- **The "story" is a visual pun.** Artsy trick photography, surreal composites, wink-and-nudge symbolism — these are the camera-club prize winners Gallup showed do not sell. — *Confessions*
- **The illustration fights the headline instead of telegraphing it.** Photo and headline should point at the same promise; if the picture sells one thing and the line sells another, both are wasted.

## Core Concept

"The kind of photographs which work hardest are those which arouse the reader's curiosity. He glances at the photograph and says to himself, 'What goes on here?' Then he reads your copy to find out. Harold Rudolph called this magic element 'Story Appeal,' and demonstrated that the more of it you inject into your photographs, the more people look at your advertisements." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

This is the single most useful finding in the research literature of advertising, and it is almost seventy years old. Harold Rudolph, Research Director at Stirling Getchell's agency, published *Attention and Interest Factors in Advertising* in 1947 — a careful measurement of what makes readers look at a magazine advertisement. One of his observations was that photographs with an element of "story appeal" were far above average in attracting attention. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* That finding put the eye patch on the Hathaway man, the beard on Commander Whitehead, and the Rolls-Royce on the page of every *New Yorker* from 1958 to 1962.

The reader's first thought on seeing the photograph is a silent question: *What's going on here?* The picture must answer that question — and the answer must be the product. If the question is "what's going on here?" and the answer is "some model's face, cropped strangely, against a sunset," the reader turns the page. If the question is "what's going on here?" and the answer is "a man conducting the New York Philharmonic while wearing an eye patch and a Hathaway shirt," the reader reads the copy.

Gallup has shown that the kind of photographs which win prizes at camera clubs — sensitive, subtle, beautifully composed — don't sell. — *Confessions* What sells: a single arresting detail that makes the reader stop and ask what's going on. A before-and-after photograph, which Gallup found did not fail to increase sales in a single case out of seventy campaigns studied. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A photograph with a person in it (people draw attention more than things). A photograph, not a drawing, because photographs are more believable, better remembered, pull more coupons, and sell more merchandise.

"What you show is more important than what you say." — *How to Create Advertising That Sells* The subject of your illustration is all-important. If you don't have a remarkable idea for it, not even a great photographer can save you. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* This is the part the camera-club tradition gets wrong: technique is downstream of idea. Irving Penn cannot save you if the brief is empty.

And when Ogilvy wrote 18 ways to inject story appeal into the Hathaway shirt, the eighteenth was the one. On the way to the studio, I ducked into a drugstore and bought an eye patch for $1.50. Exactly why it turned out to be so successful, I shall never know. — *Confessions* That is worth dwelling on. The craft is not a matter of reasoning the right picture into existence on the first attempt. It is generating eighteen candidate ideas and recognizing the one the reader will stop on.

The supporting rules are unromantic. Keep illustrations simple, with the focus of interest on one person. Crowd scenes don't pull. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Don't show human faces enlarged bigger than life; they repel. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Historical subjects bore most readers. Photographs beat drawings. Color beats black-and-white — twice as memorable, for a 50 per cent cost premium. Babies draw women. Men look at ads with men in them; women look at ads with women in them. Always put a caption under the picture — four times as many people read captions as read the body copy — and the caption should include the brand name and the promise. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## How to Apply

1. **Before the photographer is hired, answer one question: what makes the reader say *What's going on here?*** If you cannot answer it in one sentence, go back to the brief. Not even Irving Penn can save a blank.

2. **Generate candidates in volume.** For Hathaway I wrote 18 different ways to inject story appeal. The eye patch was the eighteenth. You will not land the right element on the first attempt; do the eighteen.

3. **Make sure the arresting element is the product or relates directly to it.** The eye patch belongs to the Hathaway man — the man wearing the Hathaway shirt. The bearded Commander Whitehead is the man from Schweppes. The naked woman on the cooking stove was irrelevant to the product, and therefore the wrong stunt. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

4. **Choose a photograph, not an illustration, unless printing conditions demand otherwise.** Photographs are more believable; they are better remembered; in direct response they pull more coupons; in retail they sell more merchandise. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

5. **Use a person.** People attract more attention than things. If the category demands no people, a before-and-after photograph is the reliable alternative: Gallup could not find a single before-and-after campaign out of seventy that did not increase sales. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

6. **Keep it simple. One person, one focus.** Crowd scenes don't pull. No enlarged faces. No historical subjects unless you are selling whisky.

7. **Place the headline below the picture, the caption below the headline, the copy below the caption.** That follows the reader's natural scanning order — top to bottom. Headlines below the illustration are read by 10 per cent more people than headlines above. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

8. **Always put a caption under the photograph.** More people read captions than read body copy, so the caption is a second headline. Put the brand name and the promise in it.

9. **Test, if you can.** If you can't decide between two pictures, split-run them in a newspaper. That is how KLM settled the question of aircraft vs destination photographs; the destination pictures pulled twice as many coupons, and KLM ads were illustrated with destinations from then on. — *Confessions*

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway, "The Man in the Hathaway Shirt" (1951).
**Application:** The archetype. Model Baron Wrangell photographed wearing a Hathaway shirt — and an eye patch. The eye patch has no functional relation to the shirt; that is precisely what makes it work. The reader sees it and asks *What's going on here?* and reads the copy to find out. In the years that followed, the Hathaway man was photographed conducting the New York Philharmonic, playing the oboe, copying a Goya at the Metropolitan Museum, driving a tractor, fencing, sailing, buying a Renoir — each execution a new situation around the same aristocratic prop. — *Confessions* "The eye patch conveyed an aristocratic aura and story appeal of the Hathaway man." — Ogilvy, *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Hathaway, 116 years old and unknown, became a household name. The campaign ran for 21 years. This is the canonical demonstration of Rudolph's research applied to a live brief. The $1.50 eye patch from a drugstore built a brand.

**Ad / Situation:** Schweppes, Commander Edward Whitehead (1953–1970s).
**Application:** The client's own bearded face, used as the symbol of his own product. The beard is the arresting element; the Commander himself is the story. The reader asks *Who is this man?* — he is the man who brings you Schweppes — and the brand is welded to a personality the reader recognizes across the eighteen-year run of the campaign. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Whitehead became a talk-show guest. Schweppes established itself in the US on a peppercorn budget. Demonstrates that story appeal can be generated by casting a real person — particularly a real person connected to the product — rather than by a prop. A single memorable face works for the same reason a single memorable detail does: it answers the reader's silent question with the brand.

**Ad / Situation:** Puerto Rico tourism — Elliott Erwitt's photograph for the Casals Festival.
**Application:** Instead of photographing Pablo Casals playing his cello, Erwitt photographed an empty room, with the great man's cello leaning against a chair. Why is the room empty? Where is Casals? Those are the questions the reader asks. The answer is in the copy — and the answer is "come to Puerto Rico for the Casals Festival." — *Confessions*
**Result:** During the campaign's first six years, tourist expenditures in Puerto Rico rose from $19 million to $53 million a year. Demonstrates that story appeal does not always require a person in frame. Sometimes the absence of a person, placed carefully, creates a bigger question than the presence of one.

**Ad / Situation:** Zippo, "The amazing story of a Zippo that worked after being taken from the belly of a fish" (1961).
**Application:** The arresting image — a lighter, a fish, a guarantee made vivid — answers the reader's silent question before the headline repeats it. The story in the photograph is the story in the headline is the story in the body copy. Everything points at one durable claim: *it still works.*
**Result:** Every morning, old and battered Zippos arrived at the factory in the mail; they were returned the same day, repaired, free of charge. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Singled out in *Ogilvy on Advertising* as a model of storytelling in advertising. Demonstrates that the most vivid guarantee is a single specific incident made into a picture.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Choose a photograph because it is beautiful, artistic, or sensitive.
**Why:** The photographs that win prizes at camera clubs — sensitive, subtle, beautifully composed — don't sell. — *Confessions* Beauty is a feature of the picture; story appeal is a function of the reader's curiosity. You can have one without the other, and the one that sells is the second.

**Don't:** Use trick photography, surreal composites, or visual puns.
**Why:** The camera-club aesthetic and the visual-pun school both optimize for the creative director's pleasure, not the reader's stop. Abstract art does not telegraph what is for sale fast enough for an advertisement. — *Confessions* The reader is turning the page; you have a second or two to stop the hand.

**Don't:** Default to the talking head for television, or the cropped face for print.
**Why:** Don't show enlarged close-ups of the human face; they repel readers. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* And a talking head on television is not a commercial — it is a speech. If Dr. Gallup is right that the viewer forgets anything you say but don't also show, then a talking head is mostly forgotten words. Run your commercial with the sound off; if it doesn't tell without sound, it is useless. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Use a crowd scene or a historical subject.
**Why:** Crowd scenes don't pull. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Historical subjects bore the majority of readers (whisky is the one exception). The reader needs one person and one focus; any more than that and the picture becomes a group photo, which is to say a thing nobody stops for.

**Don't:** Duplicate the picture in the caption.
**Why:** Captions are second headlines. More people read them than read body copy. If the caption merely describes the picture, you have wasted a headline's worth of space. The caption should include the brand name and the promise — a mini-advertisement in itself. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

**Don't:** Default to a drawing when a photograph is possible.
**Why:** Photographs are more believable, better remembered, draw more readers, and pull more coupons. Direct-response advertisers and department stores both know this from split-run tests. — *Confessions* The exception is poor reproduction conditions (some newspapers), where a line drawing can actually give a more lifelike impression. Outside that edge case, photograph.

**Don't:** Put the headline above the illustration.
**Why:** Readers scan top to bottom. Their eye lands first on the photograph; if the headline is above the photograph, they have to jump back up and then jump down past the image to the copy — which, in the words of a Cockney editor of the *Daily Mirror*, is "not bloody likely." Headlines below the illustration are read by 10 per cent more people. On a 20-million reader magazine, that is two million more readers — not to be sneezed at. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
