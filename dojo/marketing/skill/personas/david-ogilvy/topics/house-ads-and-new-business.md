# House Ads and New Business

## When to Use
- Building an agency, a studio, or any services firm whose deliverable is judgement and craft rather than a packaged product.
- Planning a lead-generation programme where the work itself must be the evidence — a services firm cannot claim what it is unwilling to demonstrate.
- Weighing whether to enter a competitive shoot-out or to stay on the bench; whether to accept a pitch invitation or to decline it.
- Training a new-business function that is drifting into chasing every RFP that arrives in the mail.

## Fails When
- **The house ad is written to impress other copywriters.** "Left alone, copywriters write house ads to impress other copywriters, and art directors make layouts to impress other art directors. But trendy layouts and fancy copy don't impress prospective clients who have come up through finance, production or sales." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
- **The agency's best brains are being pulled off client work onto new-business theatre.** Chasing twenty pitches produces worse client work and worse new work. "The best way to get new accounts is to create for our *present* clients the kind of advertising that will attract *prospective* clients." — *Principles of Management*
- **The firm promises a standard in house ads that its present work does not meet.** Clients read your house ads. If you boast about brilliance, they will ask why their own work doesn't have any.
- **The house programme is run as one-shot rather than as a campaign.** "Don't start it unless you mean to do it *consistently.* Young & Rubicam advertised in every issue of *Fortune* for 40 years." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## Core Concept

"Direct mail was my first love and secret weapon in the avalanche of new business acquisitions which made Ogilvy & Mather an instant success." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

When I founded the agency in 1948 with $6,000 and no clients, I had no track record to point to. I had two things: the method I had learned at Gallup and from the mail-order fraternity, and the conviction that a services firm should sell itself the same way it would sell a can of soup. So I drew up a list of the five clients I wanted most — General Foods, Bristol-Myers, Campbell Soup, Lever Brothers, Shell. An act of mad presumption; all five eventually became clients. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* The way I got them was by sending frequent progress reports to six hundred people in every walk of life, and by practising on the agency the discipline I wanted to sell to them. When I solicited part of the Seagram account, Sam Bronfman played back to me the last two paragraphs of a sixteen-page speech I had sent him; he hired us. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* If an agency is not willing to use its own method on itself, it should not be paid to use it on anyone else. "Physician, heal thyself." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The "How to…" series was the public form of this discipline. Each ad — *How to create advertising that sells, How to launch new products, How to advertise travel, How to create industrial advertising that sells, How to create financial advertising that sells, How to make successful television commercials, How to run an advertising agency, How direct response advertising can increase your sales and profits* — was built the same way I would build an ad for a client. A direct promise in the headline. Long copy, often a numbered list of rules assembled from research. Set in the editorial style with serif type, captions, cross-heads, coupons to write in for reprints. The ads not only *promised* useful information, they *provided* it. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The argument the ad made was not "we are creative"; the argument was "here are forty pages of what we know, and if you read this ad you will have taken home a slice of it for free." Unique, because no other agency could run such ads — they did not have the knowledge. I note in passing that it took me 22 years to get my first house ads approved by my partners. Patience is part of the game. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

The discipline extends to how you pitch. Never enter a contest with more than four other agencies — the time bleeds the firm. Withdraw if the prospective client releases the names of the suitors to the press; it is unwise to risk being publicly defeated. Never accept an account so big that you cannot afford to lose it; that is how you start taking bad advice from clients because you are frightened. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* I refused the Edsel pitch on exactly those grounds: "Your account would represent one-half of our total billing. This would make it difficult for us to sustain our independence of counsel." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Had we won, we would have gone down with Edsel. In new business the most important decisions are the pitches you decline.

Refuse clients whose ethos is incompatible with yours, even when the revenue is indispensable. I refused Revson of Revlon and Rosenstiel of Schenley. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* We turn down an average of fifty-nine less-desirable accounts every year. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* This is not purity for purity's sake; it is the arithmetic of a firm whose only asset is its judgement. Every account you take colours the firm's taste, and every account you refuse is another signal to your own people about what the firm stands for.

## How to Apply

1. **Use your own method on yourself.** If you sell direct response, run a direct-response house campaign. If you sell long copy, write long-copy house ads. The self-demonstration is the argument. "How direct response advertising can increase your sales and profits" was a long-copy print ad for O&M's Direct Response office — a direct-response ad selling direct response. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

2. **Decide the one thing the house campaign will promise.** "Make up your mind which you want – before you start writing house ads." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Reputation for creativity, reputation for marketing skill, new-business leads in a hurry — all three are legitimate, none of them are compatible. Choose.

3. **Write house ads for businessmen, not for copywriters.** Your reader is a CEO who came up through finance, production or sales. He does not care about your layout award. He cares whether you will move his share of market.

4. **Deliver real information.** "My ads not only *promised* useful information, they *provided* it." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A house ad that promises expertise and then hides behind rhetoric is worse than no ad. Every "How to…" ad was a working manual for the topic.

5. **Be consistent over years, not campaigns.** Young & Rubicam advertised in every issue of *Fortune* for 40 years. O&M ran the "How to…" series through the 1970s. A house programme that runs once and stops is worse than one that never started.

6. **Pick your five targets in writing.** I picked General Foods, Bristol-Myers, Campbell Soup, Lever Brothers, Shell. All five joined eventually. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Named targets concentrate the mind; "more big accounts" does not.

7. **Decline pitches that multiply the agency's time faster than its revenue.** Contests with more than four rivals. Publicly-announced shortlists. Associations. Accounts whose advertising must clear five levels of approval. These are sinks. Refuse them early; the refusal is cheaper than the loss.

8. **Do not resign in pique, and never hire a new-business department that steals your best talent.** "If you resign accounts every time you feel like doing so, you will empty your portfolio every year." — *Principles of Management* And — "No first-class man will take the job" of running new business, so staffing it with your stars is theatre.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** "How to create advertising that sells" (1972) — flagship of the "How to…" series.
**Application:** A long-copy agency ad whose body was a numbered list of the rules Ogilvy had compiled from research. The headline is an unadorned promise. The format followed the same editorial layout O&M used for client work — serif type, captions, cross-heads. The ad IS the method. Prospective clients who read it took home a slice of O&M's expertise for free; the implicit offer was, "if you liked the ad, engage us to do this for your brand."
**Result:** People requested reprints for a decade after it ran. Considered by many the best ad Ogilvy ever wrote. Demonstrates the principle that the services firm's best new-business ad is the one that demonstrates rather than claims.

**Ad / Situation:** "How direct response advertising can increase your sales and profits" (1974).
**Application:** A long-copy print ad announcing the opening of O&M's Direct Response office. The ad practised on itself what it was selling — a measurable, promise-led, long-copy advertisement for the direct-response service. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
**Result:** Served both as lead-generation and as proof that the agency's brand-minded clients should take direct response seriously. Demonstrates the "self-demonstration as argument" move in its purest form.

**Ad / Situation:** "Every secret but one is in this book" — the Confessions advertisement (1964).
**Application:** A book-as-lead-gen ad. The curiosity-gap headline performs a near-complete disclosure with one hook left dangling — which secret is it? The implicit answer (which clients Ogilvy had) was inside the book. Ran alongside O&M's house campaign and did the same work: convert readers into prospects who thought they already knew how the agency would handle their account.
**Result:** The book Ogilvy wrote to attract new clients, condition the market for a public offering, and make himself known in the business world achieved all three. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Ad / Situation:** Sending frequent progress reports to six hundred people during the early years.
**Application:** Direct mail at its most personal — unsolicited updates sent to a hand-picked list of influencers, drawing on Ogilvy's own principle that direct mail was the agency's secret weapon. Sam Bronfman quoted back to Ogilvy the last two paragraphs of a sixteen-page speech Ogilvy had sent him, and hired the agency. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
**Result:** One of the channels through which the new agency accumulated its first real clients. Demonstrates that new business is won by sustained, specific, personal communication — not by pitch theatre.

**Ad / Situation:** Refusing the Edsel pitch.
**Application:** "Your account would represent one-half of our total billing. This would make it difficult for us to sustain our independence of counsel." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Ogilvy wrote to Ford declining the invitation to compete.
**Result:** Edsel failed; the agency that won the account suffered. Demonstrates that the best new-business decision is often the one to decline. An account you cannot afford to lose is an account that will soon own you.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Run house ads to impress the creative community.
**Why:** "Trendy layouts and fancy copy don't impress prospective clients who have come up through finance, production or sales." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Your prospect is not at the award show; he is at his desk reading *Fortune*.

**Don't:** Enter competitive shoot-outs with more than four other agencies.
**Why:** "The ritual of competitive courtship requires a series of long meetings. A hot agency finds itself on the shopping list of almost every prospective client, and it is all too easy for its top man to fritter away his time in this way. We have other fish to fry — the fish of our present clients." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Don't:** Agree to pitch when the prospect releases the shortlist to the press.
**Why:** "I like to succeed in public, but to fail in secret." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Publicly-announced shortlists hand the prospect the power to humiliate you for his own publicity.

**Don't:** Take an account so big you cannot afford to lose it.
**Why:** "The day you do that, you commit yourself to living with fear. Frightened agencies lose the courage to give candid advice; once you lose that you become a lackey." — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Don't:** Staff a new-business department with your first-rate people.
**Why:** "No first-class man will take the job; no second-class man can do it effectively." — *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* The best new-business department is a client-service department whose present work is so good that prospects ask to join the queue.

**Don't:** Take a client whose ethos you cannot stomach because the billings would be indispensable.
**Why:** "Avoid clients whose ethos is incompatible with yours. I refused Charles Revson of Revlon and Lew Rosenstiel of Schenley." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The revenue you need most is the revenue that will cost you the firm.

**Don't:** Run the house campaign for six months and stop.
**Why:** "Don't start it unless you mean to do it *consistently.*" — *Ogilvy on Advertising* A half-year of house ads is a half-year of wasted media. Young & Rubicam ran theirs for forty years in *Fortune*. House advertising compounds or it fails.
