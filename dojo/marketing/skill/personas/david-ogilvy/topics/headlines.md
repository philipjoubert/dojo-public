# Headlines

## When to Use
- Writing the first words the reader will ever see. On the page, on the screen, on the envelope.
- Choosing between sixteen, twenty, forty candidate headlines for one advertisement. You should be writing that many.
- Evaluating a draft where the headline is doing nothing — no brand, no promise, no news, no flag.
- The media schedule is locked, the space is paid for, and you have one shot at making the reader stop.

## Fails When
- **The headline is being used to "create mood" instead of sell.** Mood is what you get when the copywriter has nothing to promise. Go back to the product.
- **The cleverness is judged in the agency, not the shopping aisle.** A headline that makes the copywriter laugh and the reader shrug has failed.
- **The "headline" is really a tagline or a theme line.** Those belong at the bottom of the ad, not the top. A headline flags down a prospect today; a tagline glues the brand together across years. Do not confuse them.
- **The brand is not in the headline and the product is not obvious from the picture.** 80 per cent of readers never read the body copy. If the headline doesn't identify the product, those 80 per cent will never know what you were advertising.

## Core Concept

"On the average, five times as many people read the headlines as read the body copy. It follows that unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90 per cent of your money." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

Five to one. That is the ratio that runs this entire craft. Before you worry about layout, before you argue about body copy, before you negotiate the media plan, the top line of your advertisement is doing five times the work of everything beneath it. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* Treat it accordingly.

The headline has one job: sell the product. Not win an award. Not delight the client. Not display the copywriter's wit. Sell. The best headlines are promises — a whiter wash, more miles per gallon, freedom from pimples, fewer cavities. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The second-best headlines are news. Ads with news are recalled by 22 per cent more people than ads without. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* If you are lucky enough to have some news to tell, do not bury it in your body copy, which nine out of ten people will not read. State it loud and clear in your headline. And do not scorn tried-and-true words like *amazing, introducing, now, suddenly.* — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The third-best headlines offer helpful information — "HOW TO WIN FRIENDS AND INFLUENCE PEOPLE." These beat cleverness every time because they announce a benefit to the reader on first glance.

The anatomy is unromantic. A headline should (a) name the product, (b) promise a benefit, and (c) preferably contain news. It should flag down the specific reader you want — if your product is for asthmatics, the word *asthma* in the headline pulls them out of the traffic. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* It should include the brand name, because 80 per cent of readers who scan only the headline will otherwise never know what you are advertising. It should be plain enough that a tired reader, scanning 350 other headlines on the same page, can telegraph its meaning without stopping to decipher it. — *Confessions*

What disqualifies a headline: puns, double meanings, literary allusions, and other obscurities. In the average newspaper your headline competes with 350 others. Readers travel fast through this jungle. Your headline should telegraph what you want to say. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Blind headlines — the kind that mean nothing unless you read the body copy underneath — are about 20 per cent below average in recall. Negative headlines mislead the skimmer: if you write OUR SALT CONTAINS NO ARSENIC, many readers will miss the negative and go away believing your salt contains arsenic. — *Confessions* Reverse type (white on black) is harder to read. A period at the end of a headline is a full stop — that is literally what it is called — and it stops the reader dead in his tracks. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Headlines above the illustration are read by 10 per cent fewer people than headlines below. And the silliest thing of all is to run an advertisement without a headline at all — "a headless wonder." — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

Write at least sixteen headlines for a single advertisement. I never write fewer. — *Confessions* John Caples of BBDO demonstrated that a change of headline can make a difference of nineteen and a half times in the sales produced by the same advertisement. Nineteen-and-a-half to one. When one number in an ad can swing sales by that factor, that is the number that deserves your attention first.

## How to Apply

1. **Know the product cold before you write a word.** For Rolls-Royce I spent three weeks reading about the car. The electric-clock line fell out of a British motor magazine that described the observed interior silence. Without the reading, no headline.

2. **Write at least sixteen candidates.** Twenty is better. Keep going past the point where you think you are finished; the twelfth is usually better than the first, and the eighteenth is sometimes the one — the Hathaway eye patch was idea number eighteen. — *Confessions*

3. **Run each candidate through the anatomy check.**
   - Does it name the product or brand?
   - Does it promise a benefit, deliver news, or offer helpful information?
   - Does it flag down the specific reader you want?
   - Can a tired reader telegraph its meaning in one pass?
4. **Apply the disqualifiers.** If the candidate is a pun, a double-meaning, a literary allusion, a blind headline, a negative claim, or a "headless wonder," throw it out. These are not judgement calls; they are Caples's test results.

5. **Choose news over cleverness.** If the product is genuinely new or improved, announce it. Ads with news are recalled by 22 per cent more people. The power words — *NEW, FREE, NOW, SUDDENLY, ANNOUNCING, INTRODUCING, AMAZING, REMARKABLE, REVOLUTIONARY* — have earned their cliché status by working for a hundred years. — *Confessions*

6. **Be specific.** When research reported that the average shopper thought Sears Roebuck made a profit of 37 per cent on sales, I headlined an advertisement *Sears makes a profit of 5 per cent.* This specific was more persuasive than saying "less than you might suppose." — *Ogilvy on Advertising* Numerals beat generalities. Sixty miles an hour, not "high speed." $13,550, not "expensive."

7. **Include the brand name.** If the headline is "Ellerton Jette owns 279 Hathaway Shirts," the 80 per cent who only read headlines still learn what Hathaway is.

8. **Test the pull-through.** Read the headline, then the first line of body copy. Does the body copy deliver on the promise the headline made? If not, fix one of them.

9. **Set it below the illustration, in lowercase, no full stop.** Ten per cent more readership, free.

## Examples

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (1958).
**Application:** The archetype. Eighteen words; benefit (silence) proved by an observable consequence, not claimed as an adjective; brand in the headline; news angle (the "new" Rolls-Royce); specific numeral (60 mph). "When I advertised Rolls-Royce, I gave the facts — no hot air, no adjectives, no gracious living." — Ogilvy (quoted in *Ogilvy on Advertising*) The headline does not say "quietest car"; it gives you the quietness as an experience the reader can picture before reading a further word.
**Result:** Rolls-Royce sales rose 50% in 1958 against 1957. "The best headline I ever wrote contained eighteen words." — *Confessions* The line has been copied so often it is almost a genre.

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway, "The Man in the Hathaway Shirt" (1951).
**Application:** Simplicity that looks like nothing until you see what it refuses to do. No adjective, no promise, no news — just a definite article and a man. The line works because the eye patch in the photograph does the curiosity work. This is a rare case where the headline's minimalism is the correct move: the story-appeal element in the picture is what sells, and an adjectival headline would only compete with it.
**Result:** A 116-year-old shirt company with a $30,000 budget became a household name. Ran for twenty-one years. Demonstrates that "include the brand in the headline" is a more flexible rule than it sounds — you can satisfy it with six words, if the six are the right six.

**Ad / Situation:** Rolls-Royce, "Should every corporation buy its president a Rolls-Royce?" (1960).
**Application:** The question-headline variant, aimed at corporate buyers rather than individuals. The question forces the reader to answer it by reading on. The body copy then makes the unexpected business case — the car as a place to "accommodate a whole board of directors in club-like comfort and quiet."
**Result:** A single re-angled headline opened a new audience for the same body copy. Demonstrates how one campaign's material can be pivoted to a second buyer through the headline alone — no new research, no new shoot.

**Ad / Situation:** Hathaway, "Ellerton Jette owns 279 Hathaway Shirts" (1952).
**Application:** Specific-number headline. The non-round numeral (279, not "hundreds") is credible and creates a curiosity gap — who is Ellerton Jette, and why does he own so many shirts? The brand is in the headline. The answer — he founded the company — is held back for the body copy, which enforces the read-through.
**Result:** A clean demonstration that specific numerals outperform rounded claims; a variant of story-appeal delivered through a number instead of a photograph.

**Ad / Situation:** Dove, "Darling, I'm having the most extraordinary experience… I'm head over heels in Dove" (1957).
**Application:** Emotional-word headline. A psychologist flashed hundreds of words on a screen with an electric gadget measuring emotional reaction. *Darling* came out top. So I used it in the headline. — *Ogilvy on Advertising* The brand is in the headline. The promise is implied by the rapture of the speaker.
**Result:** Dove grew into the #1 cleansing brand in the world. Demonstrates that "include the brand and make a promise" does not require the plain declarative form — it can be satisfied by a voice that makes the reader want to know what she is experiencing.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Write a pun, a double-meaning, or a "clever" headline that requires a second pass to decode.
**Why:** In the average newspaper your headline competes with 350 others. Readers travel fast through this jungle. They don't stop to decipher. They turn the page. — *Confessions* The *Times Literary Supplement* was right to call the whimsical tradition "a kind of middle-class private joke, apparently designed to amuse the advertiser and his client." Amen.

**Don't:** Use negative headlines ("Our salt has no arsenic").
**Why:** A skimming reader will miss the *no* and remember that you have arsenic in your salt. If you must counter a negative, do it in body copy after you have already named the benefit on top.

**Don't:** Leave the brand out of the headline.
**Why:** 80 per cent of the people who read your headline will never read your body copy. If the brand is not in the headline, those people will never know what product you were advertising. You have paid for their eyes and told them somebody else's name.

**Don't:** Run a "headless wonder" — an ad with no headline at all.
**Why:** You have just thrown away 80 cents out of your dollar and replaced it with a mood. "The wickedest of all sins is to run an advertisement without a headline." — *Confessions*

**Don't:** Let the headline try to close the whole sale.
**Why:** A headline that tries to do the job of the body copy does neither well. The headline stops the reader and states the promise; the body copy proves the promise. Confuse the jobs and you wind up with a ten-word summary that sells nothing and a hundred words of body copy that has nothing left to say.

**Don't:** Set the headline in all capitals above the illustration in 72-point reverse type.
**Why:** Every part of that sentence is a separate loss: capitals retard reading (no ascenders or descenders to recognize words by), headlines above the illustration lose 10 per cent of readers, 72-point is laid out for a bulletin board and is unreadable at the 20-inch distance the magazine will actually be held at, and reverse type has been known to be harder to read for fifty years. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
