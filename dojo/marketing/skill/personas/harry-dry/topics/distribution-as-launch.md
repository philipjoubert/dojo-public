# Treat Content as Product Launches

## When to Use
- Any piece of content you actually care about. Brilliant articles die in obscurity every day because their authors published and prayed.
- Planning a launch (product, major article, thread, newsletter issue). The launch plan should be drafted before the content is finished.
- Diagnosing underperformance on good content. Nine times out of ten it's not the writing — it's the distribution.

## Fails When
- **The content is bad.** Distribution amplifies what's there. If what's there is wallpaper, the amplification is amplified wallpaper. Fix the content first.
- **Niche audiences where individual distribution beats mass distribution.** For some B2B products the right "launch" is five personalised DMs to the five buyers who matter. Launching broadly with the same piece is worse.
- **Over-engineering the launch for small pieces.** A 300-word blog post doesn't need a launch plan. The plan is for content you meaningfully care about seeing travel.

## Core Concept

Most people publish and pray. Smart people launch.

Here's how most content dies. You spend two weeks writing something genuinely good. Hit publish. Tweet a link. Wait. Crickets. So you assume the content wasn't good enough. You blame the writing. Wrong. The content was fine. Your distribution was lazy. Brilliant articles die in obscurity while mediocre ones go viral. The difference wasn't quality. It was effort after the publish button got clicked.

Treat every piece of content like a product launch. This isn't optional work. This is half the work.

**Write the title first.** Before writing anything, write the title. Not a working title. The actual title. The hook that makes someone stop scrolling. If the title doesn't excite you, the content won't excite anyone else. The title is the product. The content is the delivery. Most people do it backwards — write the article, slap a title on at the end. Like building a restaurant and naming it after whatever's left in the fridge. Title first. Always.

**Platform-specific packaging.** The same content needs different packaging for different audiences. What works on Twitter doesn't work on Reddit. What works on LinkedIn makes Hacker News cringe. Same article, different wrapping. One article about landing pages:
- For blog: "How to Write a Landing Page That Converts"
- For Twitter: "Your landing page has 3 seconds. Here's what to do with them."
- For Reddit: "I analysed 50 landing pages. Here's what actually works."
- For LinkedIn: "I doubled my conversion rate by changing 6 words."

Same content. Four different angles. This isn't dishonesty. It's translation. You're speaking the language of each platform.

**Zero-click content.** People don't click links. They scroll past them. I fell into this trap for years. I'd post a link on Twitter saying "New article — check it out!" Nobody clicked. Don't redirect people. Wow them on the platform they're already using. Amanda Natividad calls it "zero-click content." Give the value right there. No click required. On Twitter, write the thread that delivers the entire lesson in-feed. On Instagram, create carousels. On Reddit, write long posts that give everything away. Same interview reposted: one as a link, one as a zero-click thread with the key insights right there. The zero-click version: 700x more reach. Same content. Different packaging. The CTA comes at the end. After you've already delivered. "If you enjoyed this thread, I write a newsletter about this stuff every week." No hard sell. Just: here's where you get more.

**Customer delight as marketing.** Chewy, the pet food company. Ryan Cohen and Michael Day dropped out of college to start it. Investors said no. Amazon was already there. Five years later, Chewy overtook Amazon as the biggest online pet food retailer in America. They weren't cheaper. Didn't sell special pet food. The difference was customer service. Every customer gets a handwritten card. They employ 100 artists whose sole job is painting customers' pets. The portraits get mailed to unsuspecting customers. If they hear about a pet passing away, they send flowers and a condolence note. That's not customer service. That's marketing. Every portrait posted on social media. Every tearful thank-you shared online. Customers doing the selling. For free. Works because pet food is recurring, lifetime value is high, and people love sharing pet content. Delight your customers when LTV is high and they're likely to broadcast the delight.

**Ask for specific shares.** Generic share buttons are useless. "If you enjoyed this, retweet this thread" works better than "share this article." Why? You've done the work for them. You've chosen which tweet. All they do is click. Even better: write the tweet for them. Pre-written sharing is 10x more effective than hoping someone figures out what to say. Most people don't share because they don't know what to say. Give them the words.

**The distribution checklist.** Before publishing anything substantial:
- Who are five people who would genuinely find this useful? Message them directly.
- Where are three communities where this would be welcome? Not spam — genuinely welcome.
- What's the Twitter angle? The LinkedIn angle? The Reddit angle?
- Is there a pre-written tweet I can include?

Takes 30 minutes. For content that took 30 hours to create. Skipping distribution is like training for a marathon and sleeping through the race.

**Exit-intent popups.** When someone moves their cursor to leave, hit them with a signup form. Feels annoying. Feels desperate. 50% of my email signups come from exit-intent popups. Half the people who became subscribers were caught on the way out. Don't apologise for capturing attention you've earned.

**The compound effect.** Distribution compounds. First launch, you get 10x more eyeballs than publishing and praying. Second launch, some of those eyeballs remember you. They share. They subscribe. They become your distribution network. By the tenth piece, you have an audience waiting for your work. Each launch builds the infrastructure for the next one. The creator who publishes 100 pieces and promotes each one will crush the creator who publishes 200 pieces and promotes none. Distribution is the multiplier.

**Why publishing isn't enough.** There's a romantic notion that good work finds its audience. That cream rises. Rubbish. Good work that nobody sees might as well not exist. You need both — quality AND distribution. But given the choice between perfect content with lazy distribution and good content with excellent distribution? The second wins. The internet is noisy. Algorithms decide what surfaces. Attention is limited. Don't just drop your work and hope. Fight for it.

## How to Apply

1. **Write the title before you write anything.** If the title doesn't make you want to click, the content won't either. Draft five titles; pick the strongest. Use the header patterns (headers.md) — four-layer for promise-heavy content, misdirection for surprising takes, two-sentence for clarity.

2. **Plan the launch before you finish the content.** Who are five specific people you'll DM it to? Which three platforms are you packaging it for? What's the zero-click version for each?

3. **Package platform by platform.** For each, write a native version. Twitter: a thread where each tweet stands alone. Reddit: a long post that doesn't link out. LinkedIn: a polished version with a specific business angle. Hacker News: a title that signals depth without claiming it. Each needs its own headline.

4. **Deliver value in-feed before asking for the click.** Zero-click is the default. The full-value post is on the platform; the click is a bonus for people who want more. This flips the old "read the article to get the value" model.

5. **Include a pre-written share.** "If you enjoyed this, RT this tweet" is 10x more effective than "share this." Pick the tweet you want shared and make it trivially easy to share.

6. **DM five people directly.** Not a bulk email. Five individual notes to five specific humans. "Hey, I wrote this about X. You were on my mind because you mentioned Y. Curious what you think." People you've never DM'd before. The high-leverage introduction.

7. **Add an exit-intent popup.** Specific offer + pain-framed outcome. "Stop killing your plants. Free 10-lesson course" beats "Sign up for updates."

8. **For products, delight early customers disproportionately.** Chewy's handwritten cards. Notion's quick founder replies. Amy's customer-named support team. The delight shows up on social; the social is the marketing.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder writes a 2,000-word article on landing pages, posts a tweet saying "new article, check it out" with a link. Gets 12 views.
**Application:** Relaunch from the distribution plan. Zero-click Twitter thread delivering the key lessons in-feed (15 tweets). LinkedIn version angled at marketers with a specific business ROI framing. Reddit post in r/marketing with no link, full post — the article becomes the comment reply if someone asks for more. DM to five specific people whose work informed the article. Exit-intent popup on the article page offering a "Landing Page Checklist PDF" for email signup.
**Result:** Same content. From 12 views to tens of thousands across platforms. The article gets read by the people who want the deep version, and the threads/posts reach the people who'd never have clicked.

**Situation:** A SaaS launches a feature. Blog post, press release, nothing else.
**Application:** Rebuild the launch. Pre-launch: three "behind the scenes" threads from the founder showing the build. Launch day: zero-click product announcement on LinkedIn with before/after screenshots. Customer spotlight from three beta users (with their permission) who post their own experiences. Reddit AMA or HN post with the founder responding to comments. Five DMs to journalists who've covered the company before. Exit-intent popup on the feature page offering a "first week free" trial.
**Result:** The launch becomes an event rather than an announcement. Press release alone was a forgotten file; the orchestrated launch creates the conversation.

**Situation:** A newsletter has 400 subscribers after six months of publishing weekly.
**Application:** Distribution flywheel. Each issue gets a Twitter thread version and a LinkedIn post version. Every post ends with a specific share ask: "If this helped, RT this tweet → [link]." Exit-intent popup on the signup page with a specific lead magnet. Every fifth issue, DM five people the full issue with a personal note. Referral link at the bottom: "Know someone who'd love this? Forward them this email."
**Result:** The newsletter's distribution machinery compounds. 400 → 4,000 in the same content cadence the subscriber was doing before, because each issue now launches rather than publishes.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Post a link and call it distribution.
**Why:** "New article — check it out!" is the dying move. People don't click. The zero-click version is 100x more effective. The link is the follow-up; the in-feed content is the lead.

**Don't:** Write once, post to five platforms unchanged.
**Why:** Copy-pasting a LinkedIn-style post onto Twitter, or a Twitter thread onto Reddit, performs worse than writing native versions. Each platform has its own grammar. Write five versions, not one version dumped five times.

**Don't:** Save distribution for "after launch."
**Why:** Post-launch distribution is reactive. Pre-planned distribution (title first, packaging drafted before publish, DMs lined up) compounds from moment one. "I'll worry about distribution after I publish" is how good content dies.

**Don't:** Be scared of exit-intent popups.
**Why:** Yes, they're slightly annoying. They also account for half the newsletter's email signups. "Desperate-looking" is a brand anxiety; the conversion is real. Don't apologise for capturing attention you've earned.

**Don't:** Rely on quality alone.
**Why:** "The content will speak for itself" is optimistic folklore. The internet is too noisy. Good content with great distribution beats great content with no distribution every single time. Pick both.
