# Curation as Creation

## When to Use
- When a founder or writer asks how to build a point of view over time. The answer is almost never "be more creative." It's "collect more, credit openly, explain clearly."
- When someone is stuck staring at a blank page. Usually the block is a symptom of not having collected enough inputs.
- When teaching someone how to develop a distinctive voice. Taste is built through collection, not through sitting alone and thinking harder.

## Fails When
- **Used as an excuse to avoid creating.** Collection is input. Without synthesis and application, it's hoarding screenshots. The point is to build a lens, not a library.
- **Curation without credit.** Passing off others' frameworks as your own corrodes the trust the personal brand depends on. The whole model breaks without attribution.
- **Over-reliance on collection.** If you're citing five people in every piece, readers start to wonder what's yours. The synthesis has to land as yours even when the sources are credited.

## Core Concept

None of my ideas are original. I'm just always looking for the next thing to learn from.

People ask where my ideas come from. Everywhere but my head. I didn't invent "Can I visualise it?" Lisa Cron said it. She wrote a book about story structure. I took the principle and applied it to copy. I didn't invent "Never write an ad a competitor can sign." Jim Dury said it. Legendary ad man. I just kept banging on about it until people associated it with me. I didn't come up with the doorstep test. Claude Hopkins wrote about it in 1926. A hundred years ago.

What I do is collect. Combine. Explain. My taste plus existing patterns equals my contribution. That's the whole game.

**Your taste is your filter.** The act of choosing is creative. A thousand ads exist. You notice three. Those three say something about what you value. Your filter is your voice. When I started collecting marketing examples, I wasn't trying to build a style. I was just saving things I liked. VW ads from the 60s. Apple product pages. Basecamp's manifestos. But if you looked at that collection, you'd see patterns. Short sentences. Concrete words. Conflict built into every claim. That's how taste works. You discover what you value by paying attention to what you save.

**Copy the thinking, not the words.** Here's where most people go wrong. They copy the wrong thing. Someone reads corporate copy. "Seamless solutions." "Cutting-edge technology." "World-class platforms." They think: this is how professional marketing sounds. So they write the same way. They're copying the output of lazy thinking. Not the thinking itself.

The thing to copy is the process. When you see "1,000 songs in your pocket," copy the approach: translate the feature into what's literally true for the person holding it. When you see "The dating app designed to be deleted," copy the approach: find what competitors can't claim because of their business model. When you see New Balance's line about supermodels and Ohio dads, copy the approach: find the surprising range of your customer base. The surface is the last 10%. The substance is the first 90%.

**The reading list.** The sources I keep coming back to:
- David Ogilvy. "We don't choose the whiskey, we choose the image." Marketing is about what the product says about the buyer.
- Luke Sullivan. "Draw a line down the middle of the page. Write any two conflicts." Problem on one side, solution on the other.
- George Orwell. The 1946 essay. "Pre-fabricated words tacked together like sections of a pre-fabricated hen house." He was describing political language. Could have been describing marketing copy.
- Lisa Cron. "If I can't see it, it's not there yet." She writes about fiction. The principle translates perfectly.
- Eugene Schwartz. "All writing is just a search for juxtaposition." We only know the hot water because of the cold water.
- Claude Hopkins. The doorstep test. 1926.
- Jim Dury. "Never write an ad a competitor can sign."

When someone asks where my ideas come from, I point at this list. These people did the thinking. I did the explaining.

**Make it yours.** Synthesis is the final step. You've collected patterns. Studied the greats. Identified the principles worth learning from. Now combine them in ways nobody else has. That combination is your contribution. Every principle I teach comes from somewhere else. The three tests. The doorstep method. The juxtaposition framework. None of it is original. But the combination is mine. The specific examples I choose. The way I explain things. The British voice. The short paragraphs. When I started Marketing Examples, all the other marketing sites were faceless. Corporate. Indistinguishable. So I had personality. That personality came from everything I'd collected. Ogilvy's directness. Orwell's hatred of abstraction. Basecamp's willingness to pick a fight. I didn't copy their words. I absorbed their approach. Then I explained things in my own voice, which had been shaped by theirs.

**The swipe file method.** Start a swipe file. Digital or physical. Save everything that catches your eye. Ads. Headlines. Landing pages. Tweets. If it caught your attention, save it. Sort later. Once a month, review. Look for patterns. What do the good ones have in common? Create categories: "Short sentences." "Concrete images." "Juxtaposition." Now you're building a library of principles, not just a folder of screenshots. When you need to write, open the swipe file. Find similar examples. Copy the structure. Copy the principle. Write in your own voice. Collect widely. Analyse deeply. Apply specifically.

**Credit your sources.** When I use someone else's idea, I say so. "Jim Dury said: never write an ad a competitor can sign." "Lisa Cron puts it best: if I can't see it, it's not there yet." This isn't just being nice. It's being accurate. My authority comes from having read widely, synthesised carefully, and explained clearly. When I credit sources, I'm showing my work. These principles come from advertising legends who spent decades figuring this stuff out. The "guru" model is broken — the guy who pretends he figured out everything through pure genius. Who claims revolutionary new frameworks that are really just repackaged old ideas. I'm not that guy. I'm a collector. A synthesiser. An explainer. That's enough.

**The museum of bad marketing.** Also keep a separate folder. Everything that made you wince. "Delivering excellence." "Trusted partner." "Innovative solutions." "Taking you to the next level" — about thirty times. By collecting bad examples, you develop an immune system. When you're tempted to reach for an easy phrase, you open the folder. Fifty other companies reached for the same phrase. They became forgettable. Eventually "cutting-edge solutions" makes you physically uncomfortable. That's when the collection is working.

**The teachers list.** Write down the five people who've most influenced how you think. Not people you've met. People whose work has shaped you. For me: David Ogilvy for clarity above cleverness. George Orwell for hatred of abstract language. Jason Fried for picking fights and having opinions. Luke Sullivan for the conflict framework. Seth Godin for the importance of being remarkable. What do they have in common? That's your taste. That's the through-line that should run through all your work. When you're stuck, go back to your teachers. Ask: what would they cut? Nobody creates in a vacuum. Be honest about where your ideas come from. Then make them yours through combination.

## How to Apply

1. **Start a swipe file today.** Digital folder, Notion database, physical notebook — whichever you'll actually keep up. Save every piece of copy, every ad, every landing page, every tweet, every headline that stops you.

2. **Also keep a museum of bad marketing.** Every piece of corporate wallpaper that makes you wince. You'll need it as an immune system.

3. **Review the files monthly.** Look for patterns. Three commonalities the best examples share. Three mistakes the bad ones share. Write the patterns down. These are your emerging principles.

4. **Copy the thinking, not the words.** When you see a great line, reverse-engineer it. What approach produced this? What assumption is it inverting? What cold tap is making the hot tap land? Note the approach, not the sentence.

5. **Pick your teachers.** Five people whose work shaped you. Read or re-read one each week. Don't just admire — articulate what they taught you. The articulation is the absorption.

6. **Synthesise into your voice.** Take three patterns you've collected, combine them with your own perspective, explain the combination in your words. Your voice emerges from the synthesis.

7. **Credit openly.** "Jim Dury wrote: never write an ad a competitor can sign." Not "I always say." Credit builds authority; borrowing without credit erodes it.

8. **Publish the synthesis.** Don't just collect. The output that builds a personal brand is you-explaining-synthesised-principles-with-credits. Not you-claiming-original-frameworks. The first scales. The second breaks at the first critic.

## Examples

**Situation:** A founder wants to build a thought-leadership voice but feels they "have nothing original to say."
**Application:** Flip the frame. Nobody has anything fully original to say. Start the swipe file. Pick five teachers. Collect for three months. Look for patterns. Synthesise. Publish posts explaining the synthesis, crediting every source. Within six months, the synthesis will have a distinctive shape — theirs.
**Result:** The "original voice" that was blocking them was a false gate. Synthesis-plus-credit is a legitimate, durable way to build authority — and matches how every serious writer actually operates.

**Situation:** A marketer reads Ogilvy's "Confessions of an Advertising Man" and tries to mimic his 1960s voice in a modern SaaS landing page.
**Application:** Wrong lesson. The voice copy is the last 10%. What's the first 90%? Ogilvy's approach: research before reaching for a pen, find the specific detail hiding in the product, write falsifiable claims (Rolls-Royce electric clock), resist decoration. That's the approach. Apply the approach to the modern SaaS in your own voice. Don't write "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise in this SaaS is the Slack notification" — that's copying the surface. Do write "After watching 30 support tickets, we found the median response comes in 4 hours" — that's copying the approach.
**Result:** The attempt to mimic Ogilvy's voice would have produced a pastiche. Copying the approach produces modern copy with ancient discipline.

**Situation:** A copywriter claims to have "invented" a three-test framework and sells a course on it.
**Application:** The tests come from Ogilvy (falsify), Lisa Cron (visualise), Jim Dury (nobody else can say it). The right move isn't to claim invention. It's to synthesise and credit: "I borrowed these three tests from Ogilvy, Lisa Cron, and Jim Dury — here's how I stack them together, and here's what I found when I ran them against 200 landing pages." That's honest authority. Claiming invention is a short path to a public correction.
**Result:** Credited synthesis is durable. Claimed invention is brittle. Personal brands break on the first discoverable lie.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Hoard without synthesising.
**Why:** A 5,000-item swipe file nobody's reviewed is a hoard, not a filter. Monthly review forces the synthesis step — patterns emerge when you look at the collection as a collection. Without review, it's just storage.

**Don't:** Copy the surface of great copy.
**Why:** Copying "1,000 songs in your pocket" as "1,000 X in your pocket" for your product produces derivative wallpaper. The approach is what transfers: translate a spec into the lived experience. "1,000 Y in your Z" is the surface; the approach is the lesson.

**Don't:** Pretend synthesis is invention.
**Why:** The "guru" model is short-term powerful and long-term fragile. Every audience eventually discovers the sources. Credit upfront — "this comes from X, here's what I added" — builds trust. Invention claims collapse when the sources are found.

**Don't:** Collect only within your field.
**Why:** Harry's frameworks borrow from fiction writing (Lisa Cron), political essays (Orwell), direct-response advertising (Ogilvy, Hopkins), and product thinking (Basecamp). Cross-domain collection is where the non-obvious patterns come from. In-field collection produces in-field clichés.

**Don't:** Wait for original ideas before publishing.
**Why:** The wait is infinite. Synthesis-plus-credit is a legitimate creative output from day one. Publish your synthesis, credit openly, keep collecting. The voice emerges over time. Demanding originality before you start is the block that prevents most people from starting.
