# Pricing and Offers

## When to Use
- Any pricing page decision: single price vs tiers, which tier to push, how to anchor the price against alternatives.
- A product priced too low because the format anchors it there (ebooks, courses, templates).
- Designing a free offer, lead magnet, viral hook, or trial that's supposed to drive signups.
- Reviewing pricing that's leaving money on the table.

## Fails When
- **Truly elastic products priced for the masses.** Peter Thiel's Zero to One sells at $10 because the masses are price-sensitive. Raising the price to $30 loses more revenue than it captures. Don't mistake an inelastic product strategy for a universal law.
- **Sky-Bet-style offers applied to the wrong products.** A million-pound prize works for a betting app where customers already watch football. Trying to retrofit the same hook to a B2B SaaS is a category mismatch.
- **Tier design that doesn't have a real product difference between tiers.** Hierarchy and naming can nudge choice, but if the middle tier and top tier are basically the same, customers notice and trust erodes.

## Core Concept

The average cost to acquire a betting customer is about $200. Sky Bet does it for $2.60. Not because they spend less on ads. Because they wrote a better offer. How you frame the price is as important as the price itself. Sometimes more important.

**Price elasticity: the maths nobody does.** Peter Thiel spends six months writing everything he knows about business. His book sells for $10 on Amazon. Adam Wathan and Steve Schoger spend six months writing everything they know about design. Their book sells for $79 on their own website. Both prices make perfect sense. Thiel sells to the masses. The masses are price sensitive. Raise the price to $30 and they'll grab a cheaper alternative. Adam and Steve sell to hardcore fans. People who've followed the Twitter tips, watched the screencasts, read the Medium posts. In their minds, there's no substitute. When demand is inelastic, you raise your price. An 8x price increase (from $10 to $79) led to just a 2x drop in copies sold. That's 4x more revenue. The question isn't "what can I charge?" It's "how price-sensitive is my buyer?" Rough test: if you double your price, do you lose more than half your customers? If not, you're undercharging. Charging 2x means the same revenue with half the customers. Half the customers means half the support tickets, half the complaints, and better customers overall.

**Escaping the format anchor.** You can't sell an ebook for $79. People are anchored to what they pay on Amazon. $9.99. Maybe $14.99. Adam Wathan: "The format commands the price. No matter what the content is." So Adam and Steve escaped the format. Set up their own website. Added video tutorials, an icon pack, colour palettes, font recommendations, a component library. Now it wasn't an ebook. It was an exhaustive design package. Same content at the core. Different context. Different price ceiling. Refactoring UI grossed over $1.35 million. Most from the $149 higher tier. If your product looks like something cheap, repackage it until it doesn't. A $60 ebook becomes a $149 complete package becomes a $249 design system. Same knowledge. Completely different frame. The format is the ceiling. Change the format, change the ceiling. Online courses escape book pricing. Memberships escape course pricing. Done-for-you services escape everything. Pick the container that lets your value breathe.

**Tier design that steers.** Adam and Steve offered two tiers: $79 and $149. They wanted people to buy the $149. Here's how they nudged them.
- **Hierarchy.** The higher tier takes up twice the screen space. Larger font. Primary button colour. The lower tier button feels like a consolation prize.
- **Disproportionate discounts.** The higher tier was reduced by $100 (40% off). The lower tier by just $20 (25% off). If you want people to buy the bigger package, make the bigger discount impossible to ignore.
- **Naming.** They called the higher tier "The Complete Package." That label implies if you choose anything less, you're missing out. They could have called it "The Bonus Package." Same content. Completely different psychology.
Result: 78% of buyers chose the higher tier. Three subtle tweaks. No discounting. No pressure. Just better framing.

**Price anchoring in copy.** People don't evaluate prices in a vacuum. They compare. Your job is to choose what they compare to. Basecamp charges $99 a month. Instead of justifying that number, they show a comparison table. Slack: $8/user/month for 50 users = $400. Asana: $11/user/month for 50 users = $550. Basecamp: $99. Flat. Done. The alternatives anchor the price. Basecamp looks cheap because everything around it is expensive. You can also anchor against everyday spending. "For less than your morning coffee." "For the price of one client lunch." Pick an anchor your buyer already spends on without thinking. Then put your price next to it. The best price anchors don't feel like marketing. They feel like common sense.

**The Sky Bet Super 6 offer.** Every bookmaker in the UK runs the same offer. "Bet 10 quid, get a 20 quid free bet." Cool. Whatever. No differentiation. Sky Bet built something different: "Predict six correct scores. Win one million pounds." Free to enter. Takes 20 seconds. Why it worked:
- **Low friction.** No money down. No complicated signup. Just pick six scores.
- **Fits into my life.** "I'm watching the games anyway."
- **Fits into my social life.** "All my mates play."
- **Huge prize.** A million pounds. Talk trigger.
- **Seems doable.** "Guess six scores? Can't be that hard." (Actually about as likely as winning the lottery.)

Fifteen years. 5 million downloads. $13 million paid out. Sky Bet valuation up 600% since launch — now $3.5 billion. Napkin maths: $13 million ÷ 5 million downloads = $2.60 per customer acquired. The offer checklist:
1. Is it free to try?
2. Does it fit into their existing routine?
3. Can they share it with friends?
4. Is the upside huge?
5. Does it seem doable?

Three out of five = you've got something. All five = Super 6.

**The price you can charge.** Refactoring UI could charge $149 because Adam and Steve had given away two years of free tips, screencasts, and articles before they ever asked for money. The price you can charge equals the value you've already created. Not the value you promise. The value they've already received. That's the difference between a hard sell and a natural one. Build the trust first. The pricing follows.

## How to Apply

1. **Diagnose elasticity.** Who's your buyer? Price-sensitive mass market or inelastic fan? Rough test: if you double the price, do you lose more than half your customers? If not, you're underpriced.

2. **Check the format anchor.** What format is your product presented in (ebook, video, SaaS, membership, done-for-you)? What's the typical price ceiling for that format? If you're at the ceiling and customers still buy happily, repackage into a higher-ceiling format.

3. **Design tiers to steer.** If you want the $149 tier chosen:
   - Give it twice the screen space.
   - Use the primary button colour.
   - Apply a larger headline discount.
   - Name it "The Complete Package," not "The Bonus Package."

4. **Anchor the price.** Most expensive alternative first (Basecamp's competitor table). Or anchor against a familiar expense the buyer spends on daily (coffee, lunch, taxi). Price comparison charts work for B2B; daily-spending anchors work for consumer.

5. **For free/viral offers, run the five-point checklist.** Free to try? Fits existing routine? Shareable? Huge upside? Seems doable? Aim for 3+. Adjust the offer until you hit as many as possible.

6. **Build trust before asking for price.** If the product's priced at a premium, the customer needs to have already received value. Give away two years of tips. Ship a lead magnet. Publish publicly. Then charge confidently when the moment comes.

7. **Handle the cancellation objection upfront.** For subscriptions, the Blinkist move — tell the customer exactly when they'll be charged and how to cancel. Raised trial signups 23% and cut support complaints 55%. The thing you're scared to say is often the thing that converts.

## Examples

**Situation:** A designer sells a 120-page design ebook on Gumroad for $19.
**Application:** Format anchor. Ebooks cap at ~$30. The content is worth much more. Repackage: combine the ebook with 12 hours of video lessons, a component library (Figma + Tailwind), a colour palette toolkit, and a founder's office-hours session. Now it's a "Design System Course," not an ebook. Price at $149. Offer a $299 "Pro" tier with 1:1 critique sessions — more screen space, bigger discount, "Complete Package" naming.
**Result:** Same knowledge. Different format, different price ceiling, steered tier design. Revenue up multiples with the same audience.

**Situation:** A B2B SaaS charges £29/month with a single tier.
**Application:** Check elasticity by talking to five happy customers. If they say "I'd pay much more — we save twenty hours a week on this" the product is underpriced. Add a Pro tier at £99 with the two features customers ask for most (better reporting, team seats). Keep the £29 tier but redesign the pricing page so the £99 tier takes up twice the space, gets the primary CTA, and is called "Business." Add a comparison table with the two nearest competitors priced at £200–£500.
**Result:** Pricing anchoring (competitors at £200+) plus tier steering (Business tier prominent) plus format expansion (real team-seat product) moves ARPU substantially. The £29 tier remains for price-sensitive buyers.

**Situation:** A fintech wants a viral consumer offer with low CAC.
**Application:** Run the Sky Bet checklist against drafted offers.
- Offer A: "Refer a friend, get £20." Free to try (sort of, not to recipient). Fits routine (mildly). Shareable (yes). Upside (small). Doable (yes). 3/5.
- Offer B: "Top up by £10, get £100 in stock to trade." Free (no). Fits routine (maybe). Shareable (yes). Upside (medium). Doable (yes). 3/5.
- Offer C: "Predict the FTSE's close on Friday. Closest to the mark wins £10,000. Free to enter." Free (yes). Fits routine (news anyway). Shareable (yes). Upside (huge). Doable (yes — just guess). 5/5.
Pick C.
**Result:** The Sky Bet move — turning a mundane-sounding offer into a talk-trigger free game — slashes CAC versus any standard referral promo.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Assume the market's price is the right price.
**Why:** Format anchors lock most sellers into category norms that aren't set by economics — they're set by historical defaults. Adam Wathan's $79 ebook (repackaged) is more profitable than the same content at $19 because he left the format. Check whether you're in a local pricing maximum or the real one.

**Don't:** Design three tiers when two will do.
**Why:** Three-tier pricing ("Basic, Pro, Enterprise") is a cliché partly because it gives the middle tier a psychological advantage (the "anchor") — but for simple products, three tiers just creates choice paralysis. Two tiers with a clear hierarchy often outperform three.

**Don't:** Use a competitor anchor that's dishonest.
**Why:** Comparing your £99/month to an enterprise competitor's £5,000 tier (which the buyer would never actually buy) breaks on inspection. Use the tier the buyer would genuinely consider — even if that makes you look less cheap. Dishonest anchors corrode trust when the customer notices.

**Don't:** Add a Sky-Bet-style viral hook to a product where the buying behaviour doesn't fit.
**Why:** Sky Bet works because football fans are watching anyway. "Predict the weather and win £10,000" for a CRM is random noise to the customer. The hook must match real behaviour.

**Don't:** Price before trust exists.
**Why:** Refactoring UI could charge $149 because Adam and Steve had given away two years of free content. A new product with no distribution can't charge the same number — not because the content is worse, but because the trust hasn't compounded. Give first. Charge second.
