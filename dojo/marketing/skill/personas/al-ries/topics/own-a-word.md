# Own a Word

## When to Use
- When a brand has established category presence but lacks a single ownable concept.
- When a product team is debating brand positioning and everyone has a different answer for "what we stand for."
- When a company has a long list of attributes, values, or differentiators that add up to noise.
- When a competitor with an inferior product is consistently winning because they have a clearer message.
- When a company is spending heavily on brand advertising and not getting the recognition returns they expect.

## Fails When
- **The word is already claimed.** "Two companies cannot own the same word in the prospect's mind." *(22 Laws, Law 6)* Volvo owns "safety." No amount of safety advertising from Mercedes or GM will plant that word in their name. If the word you want is already filed under a competitor, find another word.
- **The word is too generic.** You cannot own "quality," "service," "innovation," or "reliability." Every company claims these. "Does any company proclaim itself as the 'unquality' corporation? No, everybody stands for quality. As a result, nobody does." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Words with no opposing position cannot be owned.
- **The word isn't true.** Owning a word means being able to sustain it in every product, service, and customer experience. Domino's owned "home delivery in thirty minutes, guaranteed" — until safety incidents made it impossible to maintain the guarantee. When they dropped it, they lost the word.

## Core Concept

The Law of Focus — developed by Ries in *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* (with Jack Trout) and elaborated at length in his solo book *Focus* — states something that sounds simple until you try to live by it: "The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a word in the prospect's mind." *(22 Laws, Law 5)*

Not a sentence. Not a positioning statement. Not a list of values. A word. "A company can become incredibly successful if it can find a way to own a word in the mind of the prospect. Not a complicated word. Not an invented one. The simple words are best, words taken right out of the dictionary." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* This is the ultimate act of positioning compression. Everything a brand believes about itself, everything it wants customers to believe, every message it has ever communicated — all of it must be reducible to one word if it's going to survive the filtering mechanisms of the human mind.

The examples are instructive because they are so starkly simple. From *The 22 Immutable Laws*: "Crest … cavities. Mercedes … engineering. BMW … driving. Volvo … safety. Domino's … home delivery. Pepsi-Cola … youth. Nordstrom … service." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Federal Express earned its word through ruthless focus: "Federal Express was able to put the word overnight into the minds of its prospects because it sacrificed its product line and focused on overnight package delivery only." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The word is not a slogan. It's a consequence of strategic commitment. The word doesn't create the position — the business decisions that narrow the focus to one thing create the position, and the word is the compressed label for what the mind stores.

The power of word ownership goes deeper than recall. When a brand owns a word, the word does ongoing brand work without any advertising spend. The mind telescopes the brand name and the category word together. "Instead of saying 'Please give me a Kleenex tissue,' people will say 'Please give me a kleenex,' even when the tissue box in front of them says 'Scott.'" *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* Kleenex owns the word "tissue" in the mind. "Please xerox this proposal and fedex it to Los Angeles." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* When brand names become verbs, the ownership is complete. "Owning a word in the mind is a leader's fundamental strength, more valuable than its offices, its factories, its warehouses, its distribution systems. You can always replace a physical facility that burns down, but you can't easily replace somebody else's word in the prospect's mind." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)*

The halo effect is real and should be factored in. When a word is owned cleanly, it does not limit the brand to that attribute alone — it radiates outward. "If you strongly establish one benefit, the prospect is likely to give you a lot of other benefits, too. A 'thicker' spaghetti sauce implies quality, nourishing ingredients, value, and so on. A 'safer' car implies better design and engineering." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Volvo's ownership of "safety" does not prevent buyers from concluding that Volvo must also be well-engineered, reliable, and sensibly designed. The word anchors the cluster. Trying to communicate all those attributes directly produces noise; owning the one word that implies them all produces a position.

The discipline required to get to one word is the hardest part. Most organizations resist it violently because owning one word feels like giving up everything else. "The essence of marketing is narrowing the focus. You become stronger when you reduce the scope of your operations. You can't stand for something if you chase after everything." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The resistance to narrowing — from sales teams who want to serve every segment, from product teams who want to claim every feature, from executives who want to appeal to every investor narrative — is the primary force that keeps brands from ever owning anything.

## How to Apply

1. **Start with a word association test on the competitive set.** Ask a room of prospects: "When I say [competitor], what word comes to mind?" Do this for all major players in the category. Note which words are already occupied and which are genuinely available. The map of claimed words is the map of where you cannot go and where you can.

2. **Look for the benefit-oriented, simple, available word.** "The most effective words are simple and benefit oriented. No matter how complicated the product, no matter how complicated the needs of the market, it's always better to focus on one word or benefit rather than two or three or four." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The word should be: a real dictionary word, benefit-oriented rather than feature-oriented, not already owned by a competitor, and capable of being true of your product over the long term.

3. **Examine what the business must do to sustain the word.** A word is not a communication decision. It is a business decision. Federal Express didn't own "overnight" because they ran clever ads about overnight delivery. They owned it because they restructured their entire business model — hub-and-spoke logistics, dedicated fleet, package tracking — around the guarantee. Before claiming a word, ask: what does the product, operations, and delivery have to look like to make this word true in every customer interaction?

4. **Subtract until the word is clean.** "The essence of marketing is narrowing the focus." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* If you are trying to own "overnight" and also offer five-day economy shipping, you are diluting the word. BMW owned "driving" until it launched large luxury sedans — "how can a living room on wheels be the ultimate driving machine?" *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Every product decision, service extension, and marketing message should be filtered through: does this support or dilute the word?

5. **Spend the word consistently over years.** Words are planted slowly and deeply. A word that has been consistently reinforced for five years is far more powerful than a word announced in a single campaign. "More than anything else, successful positioning requires consistency. You must keep at it year after year." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* Change the word only when the original word has become genuinely unavailable — not because you're bored with it.

## Examples

**Situation:** Volvo is a small Swedish automaker with 1 percent of the global car market competing against giants like Mercedes-Benz and BMW. Direct product-feature competition is impossible at their scale.

**Application:** Volvo builds its strategy around a single word: safety. This is not just a messaging decision. Starting with its 1959 introduction of the world's first three-point lap-and-shoulder automotive seat belt, Volvo invests continuously in safety engineering — airbags, safety-cage construction, collapsible steering columns, daytime running lights. The word and the product decisions are inseparable. "Ask any car owner what Volvo stands for and you'll usually get the reply 'safety.' This is not an accident of history, but a deliberate strategy of Volvo." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* The Volvo Saved My Life Club turns customers into living proof of the word.

**Result:** Despite 1 percent global market share, "Volvo has become the largest selling imported European luxury car in America. Volvo has consistently outsold both BMW and Mercedes-Benz. In the past decade, for example, Volvo sold 880,000 cars in the United States versus 750,000 for Mercedes and also 750,000 for BMW." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* One word, sustained, beats a broader narrative from larger competitors.

---

**Situation:** Prego enters the spaghetti sauce category, which Ragu dominates. Rather than compete on taste, heritage, or "quality," they look for an available word.

**Application:** "Prego went against leader Ragu in the spaghetti sauce market and captured a 27 percent share with an idea borrowed from Heinz. Prego's word is thicker." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Thicker implies quality, richness, and better value without the expense of proving any of it. The halo effect does the work. Ragu doesn't own "thick." The word is available. Prego stakes a claim.

**Result:** 27 percent market share for a challenger brand in a leader-dominated category. Not through outspending Ragu but through owning an available, benefit-oriented word that Ragu had left on the table.

---

**Situation:** BMW wants to grow revenue and decides to broaden its product line into large luxury sedans to compete with Mercedes in the high-end segment. Their existing word is "driving."

**Application:** The large sedans — the 700-series — are the opposite of what "driving" implies. "How can a living room on wheels be the ultimate driving machine? Not only can you not feel the road, but you'll also crush all the pylons in your driving commercials." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The expansion dilutes the word. The mind cannot reconcile "the ultimate driving machine" with a car designed for rear-seat passengers to be comfortable in.

**Result:** "Things started downhill for BMW." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The recovery came when BMW reintroduced small performance-oriented cars and emphasized "driving" again. "The company has regained its focus." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* The word was there waiting. It just needed the business decisions to support it again.

---

**Situation:** A startup offers a contact management and scheduling tool aimed at salespeople. The product does "everything" — mailing lists, schedules, follow-up letters, and more. The founder wants to position it as a comprehensive sales productivity platform.

**Application:** Ries and Jack Trout advised the company to narrow. "After much discussion, we arrived at the concept of 'contact' software." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* The word "contact" — not CRM, not sales productivity, not pipeline management — is simple, benefit-oriented, and unclaimed. The company is renamed Contact Software International. Advertising runs only in airline publications, where road salespeople are most likely to see it.

**Result:** "Eight years after the decision to focus the company and the product as 'contact software,' Patrick Sullivan sold the company to Symantec for $47 million. Today Act has about 850,000 users and 70 percent of the market." *(Focus, Finding Your Word)* One word. One channel. Dominant category leadership.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to own a word that a competitor already owns.
**Why:** "When a competitor owns a word or position in the prospect's mind, it is futile to attempt to own the same word." *(22 Laws, Law 6)* "Volvo owns safety. Many other automobile companies, including Mercedes-Benz and General Motors, have tried to run marketing campaigns based on safety. Yet no one except Volvo has succeeded in getting into the prospect's mind with a safety message." *(22 Laws, Law 6)* You do not dislodge a word that is already filed. The attempt reinforces the competitor's position by making the category more important while spending your own budget.

**Don't:** Try to own a quality word.
**Why:** "You can't narrow the focus with quality or any other idea that doesn't have proponents for the opposite point of view." *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Quality, service, reliability, innovation — no competitor will take the "anti-quality" or "anti-innovation" position. Words without a real opposite can't be owned. They are ambient noise. The words that work are the ones where genuine trade-offs exist: safe vs. fast, driving vs. comfortable, overnight vs. standard delivery.

**Don't:** Change the word when it's working.
**Why:** The value of a word accumulates over time. Every year of consistent reinforcement compounds its power. "Although we've been touting that words stick in the mind, nothing lasts forever" — but they last far longer than most marketing organizations are patient enough to let them. *(22 Laws, Law 5)* Boredom inside the company is not a reason to change the word. The word should be changed only when the business has genuinely moved, the category has collapsed, or the word has become impossible to sustain. Not before.

**Don't:** Let the word drift by expanding the product line.
**Why:** Every product that contradicts the word weakens it. Federal Express walked away from "overnight" when it tried to take "worldwide" from DHL. "Federal Express has walked away from overnight and is in the middle of trying to take worldwide away from DHL." *(22 Laws, Law 6)* The result: FedEx neither owns "overnight" anymore nor can it own "worldwide" from DHL, which already holds it. A word abandoned doesn't wait for you — it becomes available for others or simply dies.
