# The Law of Sacrifice

## When to Use
- When a brand is trying to compete with a leader and has nothing differentiated to stand on.
- When the instinct is to add products, markets, or messages to drive growth.
- When a company keeps changing strategy every budget cycle without compounding success.
- When you need to explain why the narrowest competitor in a category consistently beats the broadest one.

## Fails When
- **The market is genuinely too thin** — A sacrifice that narrows focus to a market segment too small to sustain the business is not discipline, it's failure. Sacrifice has to be strategic, not suicidal.
- **The company hasn't found its focus yet** — You can only sacrifice productively once you know what you are sacrificing for. A startup that doesn't yet have a product/market fit should not sacrifice prematurely; it needs to find the focus first.

## Core Concept

"The law of sacrifice is the opposite of the law of line extension. If you want to be successful today, you should give something up." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* There are exactly three dimensions of sacrifice: product line, target market, and constant change. In each dimension, the counterintuitive move is to give up more, not less.

On product line: "Where is it written that the more you have to sell, the more you sell? The full line is a luxury for a loser." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* Federal Express built a business around one idea — small packages overnight — by sacrificing everything else that Emery Air Freight was doing. "The power of the sacrifice for Federal Express was in being able to put the word overnight in the mind of the prospect." Emery did everything; FedEx did one thing. FedEx won. Duracell built the leading battery brand by sacrificing everything except alkaline. Eveready had heavy-duty, alkaline, and everything else. Duracell had one thing: "long-lasting." Duracell became the leader.

On target market: "Where is it written that you have to appeal to everybody?" *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* In the early 1960s, Pepsi-Cola was losing to Coke by more than five to one. The solution that closed the gap: radical sacrifice of market. "In the early sixties Pepsi-Cola finally developed a strategy based on the concept of sacrifice. The company sacrificed everything except the teenage market." Within one generation, Pepsi closed to within 10 percent of Coke in total U.S. cola sales. Philip Morris took Marlboro — a female cigarette — and sacrificed the female market entirely, narrowing the target to men. Then narrowed further to a man's man, the cowboy. "Today, Marlboro is the largest-selling cigarette in the world." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* The critical insight: "The target is not the market. That is, the apparent target of your marketing is not the same as the people who will actually buy your product." Marlboro targets cowboys; its market is everyone. Pepsi targeted teenagers; its market was everyone who wanted to feel young.

On constant change: Strategy is not a quarterly event. "Where is it written that you have to change your strategy every year at budget review time? If you try to follow the twists and turns of the market, you are bound to wind up off the road." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* People Express had a brilliant, narrow position — no-frills airline, no-frills cities, no-frills prices — and abandoned it for expansion, 747s, European routes, first-class sections. It ended in bankruptcy. White Castle has never changed its position in sixty years and sells more than $1 million per unit per year, outperforming many full-service chains. Consistency is the compound interest of brand-building.

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the one thing the brand could own.** Ask: if you had to pick a single word or concept for the brand to own in the mind — one that no competitor currently owns — what would it be? That is what you sacrifice toward. Federal Express sacrificed to own "overnight." Duracell sacrificed to own "long-lasting." Foot Locker sacrificed to own "athletic shoes." The Limited sacrificed to own "upscale clothing for working women."

2. **Reduce the product line, not expand it.** "Take Kraft, for example. Everybody thinks Kraft is a strong brand name. In jellies and jams, Kraft has 9 percent of the market. But Smucker's has 35 percent. Kraft means everything, but with a name like Smucker's, it has to be jelly or jam because that's all Smucker's makes. In mayonnaise, Kraft has 18 percent of the market. But Hellmann's has 42 percent." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* The specialist beats the generalist every time there is a focused alternative.

3. **Pick a target, not a market.** Narrow the target deliberately. The 50-year-old who wants to feel young will drink Pepsi. The non-cowboy who wants to feel rugged will smoke Marlboro. You do not lose the market by narrowing the target — you gain it by making the brand mean something specific.

4. **Freeze the strategy.** "The best way to maintain a consistent position is not to change it in the first place." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* Sacrifice includes sacrificing the temptation to reinvent. Toys "R" Us went bankrupt as Interstate Department Stores, looked at the books, found toys was the one profitable segment, focused on toys, changed the name, and built a $5.5 billion business doing 20 percent of the retail toy market. The sacrifice happened once. The discipline was never changing it.

5. **Name the specialists winning in your category.** Before the client objects that sacrifice means giving up revenue, map the retail landscape in their category. "In the retail field generally, the big successes are the specialists: The Limited, The Gap, Benetton, Victoria's Secret, Foot Locker, Banana Republic." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* Where are the department stores that tried to sell everything? In bankruptcy court.

## Examples

**Situation:** Emery Air Freight offered everything — small packages, large packages, overnight, delayed, any size, any timeline.
**Application:** Federal Express concentrated exclusively on small packages overnight.
**Result:** "Today Federal Express is a much bigger company than Emery." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* Then FedEx repeated the mistake by buying Tiger International's Flying Tiger cargo line and losing $1.1 billion in international operations in 21 months — sacrifice works until you stop sacrificing.

**Situation:** Eveready was the long-time leader in batteries but sold heavy-duty, alkaline, and standard batteries all under the same name.
**Application:** P.R. Mallory introduced Duracell as a line of alkaline batteries only, with a better name, and the single message "lasts twice as long."
**Result:** "Duracell had already become the leader in the battery market." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* Eveready was forced to rename its alkaline battery the Energizer — too late. The sacrifice of the full line gave Duracell the only position that mattered.

**Situation:** Interstate Department Stores had filed for bankruptcy selling general merchandise.
**Application:** Management looked at the books, found toys was the only profitable segment, sold everything else, changed the name to Toys "R" Us.
**Result:** "Today Toys 'R' Us does 20 percent of the retail toy business in the country. Very profitably, too. In its last fiscal year, Toys 'R' Us made $326 million on sales of $5.5 billion." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Sacrifice the target market and then immediately widen the net when early results look good.
**Why:** Pepsi closed a five-to-one gap with Coke by owning teenagers. Then it succumbed. "According to Advertising Age, 'Pepsi-Cola Co. has outgrown the Pepsi generation. In a major marketing shift, flagship Pepsi will be pitched as the soft drink for the masses.' 'Gotta have it' is Pepsi's new theme." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* This is the sacrifice law in reverse — giving back the very thing that created the power. Pepsi's ads began featuring Yogi Berra and Regis Philbin. The narrowing stopped. So did the gains.

**Don't:** Confuse sacrifice with poverty.
**Why:** A focused company is not a small company. Wrigley sacrifices everything except chewing gum and does $1.6 billion in sales, with net profit margins that doubled from 7 to 14 percent in a decade. *(Focus, Building a Multistep Focus)* WD-40 sells one product and earns 17 percent net margins — three times the Fortune 500 average. Sacrifice creates depth of market share, not reduction of it. The focused brand becomes the category in the mind. When you are the category, every new customer in that space is your customer first.

**Don't:** Treat "the full line" as a competitive advantage.
**Why:** It sounds like more is better. It is, operationally, more for the salesforce to pitch. But the full line is a blur in the mind. "The full line is a luxury for a loser." *(22 Laws, Ch. 13)* The competitor with one sharp thing always beats the competitor with ten blurry things — because the one sharp thing is what people remember when they have a problem to solve.
