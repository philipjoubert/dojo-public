# The Law of Focus: Narrowing to Laser Power

## When to Use
- When a brand needs to know what it should stand for.
- When competing against specialists who own clear positions in the mind.
- When a company describes itself in three sentences and every sentence is a different business.
- When the question is: "What is our brand?"

## Fails When
- **The chosen word is a platitude** — "Quality," "innovation," "customer-centric," "solutions." Every company claims these. No one owns them. "This is great stuff inside the corporation... But outside the corporation, the message falls apart. Does any company proclaim itself as the 'unquality' corporation? No, everybody stands for quality. As a result, nobody does." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)*
- **Focus is aimed at the wrong level** — Owning "computers" when you are IBM means owning everything and therefore nothing. The narrower the word, the more powerful the position. "IBM owns computer" is not focus — it is the entire category. Owning a piece of a category is focus.

## Core Concept

"The sun is a powerful source of energy. Every hour the sun washes the earth with billions of kilowatts of energy. Yet with a hat and some sunscreen you can bathe in the light of the sun for hours at a time with few ill effects. A laser is a weak source of energy. A laser takes a few watts of energy and focuses them in a coherent stream of light. But with a laser you can drill a hole in a diamond or wipe out a cancer. When you focus a company, you create the same effect. You create a powerful, laserlike ability to dominate a market." *(Focus, Introduction)* The unfocused company is the sun — vast, diffuse, survivable. The focused company is the laser — small, precise, penetrating.

The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a word in the prospect's mind. Not a complicated word. Not an invented one. "The simple words are best, words taken right out of the dictionary." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* "This is the law of focus. You 'burn' your way into the mind by narrowing the focus to a single word or concept. It's the ultimate marketing sacrifice." Federal Express owns "overnight." Volvo owns "safety." Domino's owns "home delivery." BMW owns "driving." Heinz owns "ketchup" and then went further, owning "slowest ketchup in the West" — the thickness attribute. "Owning the word slow helps Heinz maintain a 50 percent market share." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)*

The connection between narrowing and power is causal, not correlative. "The essence of marketing is narrowing the focus. You become stronger when you reduce the scope of your operations. You can't stand for something if you chase after everything." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* More is less. Less is more. "If you want to be successful today, you have to narrow the focus in order to build a position in the prospect's mind." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* IBM had $65 billion in revenues in 1991 and lost $2.8 billion. When IBM was focused on mainframe computers, it made a ton of money. When it spread across every segment of computing, it bled. "What does IBM stand for? It used to stand for 'mainframe computers.' Today it stands for everything, which means it stands for nothing." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)*

Specialization is the mechanism. "Power lies with the specialist, not the generalist." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* In medicine, you trust the cardiologist over the general practitioner for a heart problem. Not because the GP is incompetent, but because "everybody knows a specialist knows more about his or her specialty than a general practitioner does. Whether it's true or not doesn't really matter. The perception is the reality." *(Focus, The Quality Axiom)* The same logic operates in every business category. The focused competitor creates the perception of depth, expertise, and commitment that the generalist can never match — because the generalist, by definition, can't be as committed to any single thing.

## How to Apply

1. **Find the word the brand could own.** What is not currently owned by any competitor? What word or concept sits unclaimed, credibly available to this brand? "The most effective words are simple and benefit oriented." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* Prego went against Ragu in spaghetti sauce and took 27 percent market share with a single borrowed idea: "thicker." One word. Unambiguous. Available. Credible given the product.

2. **Test the word against the category leader.** "Also, there's the halo effect. If you strongly establish one benefit, the prospect is likely to give you a lot of other benefits, too. A 'thicker' spaghetti sauce implies quality, nourishing ingredients, value, and so on. A 'safer' car implies better design and engineering." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* The narrow word radiates outward. You don't have to claim everything — you just have to own one thing that pulls the rest in.

3. **Kill the alternatives.** Narrowing means choosing. Once you have chosen the word, you are implicitly giving up every other claim. This is psychologically difficult. Most management teams want to add one more word, just to cover the objection in the meeting. Resist. "You can't narrow the focus with quality or any other idea that doesn't have proponents for the opposite point of view." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* The word must be one that your competitor could plausibly disagree with. That tension is what makes it real.

4. **Build operational reality around the word.** BMW's "driving" word was authentic because BMW built small, light, responsive cars. Then the company decided to build large 7-series sedans to chase Mercedes. "How can a living room on wheels be the ultimate driving machine? Not only can you not feel the road, but you'll also crush all the pylons in your driving commercials." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* The word fails when operations contradict it. Narrow the operations to match the word, or change the word to match the operations you're willing to build.

5. **Protect the word from internal dilution.** "Once you have your word, you have to go out of your way to protect it in the marketplace." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* Every successful brand faces the internal pressure to extend. Legal wants to trademark. Sales wants a broader line. Management wants to cover more segments. The CEO's job is to say no to all of it — because every addition to the product line is a subtraction from the word.

## Examples

**Situation:** Prego entered the spaghetti sauce category against dominant leader Ragu.
**Application:** Instead of claiming to be better overall, Prego focused on a single borrowed attribute: "thicker."
**Result:** Prego captured 27 percent market share — a powerful position in a mature category — from a single narrowly focused word. *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)*

**Situation:** Lotus Development Corporation owned "spreadsheet" as it was being commoditized by Microsoft.
**Application:** Instead of trying to compete across the software board, Lotus focused its corporate resources on a new, unclaimed concept: "groupware." Networked software for business teams. First in the category.
**Result:** "Unlike Microsoft, Lotus now has a corporate focus. It won't happen overnight, but Lotus could develop a powerful long-term position in the software field. What 'overnight' did for Federal Express and 'safety' did for Volvo, 'groupware' could do for Lotus." *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)*

**Situation:** Main Street Muffins was a $100,000 retail muffin business that added a wholesale frozen-batter operation at the same time. Both businesses suffered. Employee morale fell. Bankruptcy loomed.
**Application:** The founders chose the wholesale batter business — one-third of total sales at the time — and sold the retail stores.
**Result:** "Within three months the wholesale business became profitable and has remained so ever since. Since 1990, Main Street's sales have increased an average of 100 percent a year. Currently the company does $10 million worth of muffin business a year." *(Focus, The Unfocusing of Corporate America)* Confucius: "Man who chases two rabbits catches neither."

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Use "quality," "service," or "innovation" as the word.
**Why:** These are table stakes. "Does any company proclaim itself as the 'unquality' corporation?" *(22 Laws, Ch. 5)* If no competitor is willing to own the opposite position, the word is a claim, not a position. GM: "Putting quality on the road." Ford: "Quality is Job 1." Chrysler: "We just want to be the best." Three claims to the same undifferentiated word. None of them owns anything.

**Don't:** Confuse the company vision with the prospect's word.
**Why:** Executives want their word to encompass the corporate ambition — something big, inspiring, future-facing. "The conventional view, strategy is a tent. You stake out your tent big enough so it can hold everything you might possibly want to get into. IBM has erected an enormous computer tent. This is a recipe for disaster." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* The word must live in the prospect's mind, not in the strategy deck. What would a stranger say this company stands for, without being coached?

**Don't:** Narrow focus once and then widen it as success arrives.
**Why:** Success is the primary cause of unfocusing. "When a company becomes incredibly successful, it invariably sows the seeds for its future problems." *(22 Laws, Ch. 12)* The pattern is universal: IBM mainframes, Microsoft operating systems, Coca-Cola cola, Sears hard goods. Each of these was once a brilliantly focused brand. Each became unfocused at the moment of peak success, when growth pressures and opportunity hunger combined to push management toward extension. The discipline of focus is hardest to maintain when it seems least necessary.
