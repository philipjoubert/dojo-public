# The Law of Expansion: Why Narrow Brands Win

## When to Use
- When a company wants to grow its brand by adding more products, more variants, or more categories.
- When a manager argues that more SKUs or line extensions will capture more shelf space and therefore more sales.
- When a brand that used to be clearly associated with one thing now tries to be many things to many people.
- When a company confuses brand-building with revenue-chasing and starts milking what it built.
- When a category leader is slowly losing its grip and no one can explain why.

## Fails When
- **The brand never had a position to begin with.** You cannot narrow your way to clarity if you were never clear in the first place. The Law of Expansion is a preservation and strengthening tool, not a positioning tool from scratch.
- **The market is genuinely too small.** Some niches are financially unviable. Focus is right directionally, but a brand needs a category large enough to sustain a business.
- **Competitors are also narrow and equally focused.** Narrowing alone does not win; you must narrow to something the competitor does not own.

## Core Concept

"The power of a brand is inversely proportional to its scope." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)*

That single sentence contradicts everything most marketing departments want to believe. The conventional marketing instinct says: more is more. More products, more variants, more SKUs, more categories — that's how you grow. Ries's argument is precisely the opposite. Every time you put your brand name on something new, you dilute what the name means. And when a name loses its meaning, it loses its power to move product.

The proof is everywhere. "Think Chevrolet. What immediately comes to mind? Having trouble? It's understandable. Chevrolet is a large, small, cheap, expensive car . . . or truck." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* Chevrolet used to be the bestselling automobile brand in America. Then it expanded into ten separate car models. Ford expanded into eight. That gap — two fewer models — is one of the reasons Ford overtook Chevrolet. The brand that tried to be slightly less everything is the one that survived better. Neither is a success story, though; both Ford and Chevrolet are, in Ries's words, "slowly heading for the scrap heap." The danger of expansion is that it feels like progress right up until it isn't.

American Express ran the same experiment with credit cards. In 1988, AmEx had a handful of cards and 27 percent of the market. Then it launched a blizzard of new cards: Senior, Student, Membership Miles, Optima, Optima Rewards Plus Gold, Delta SkyMiles Optima, Optima True Grace, Optima Golf, Purchasing, Corporate Executive. The CEO announced the goal was to issue twelve to fifteen new cards per year. American Express's market share today: 18 percent. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* Levi Strauss did the same with jeans — expanded into twenty-seven different cuts — and watched its denim market share fall from 31 to 19 percent. Procter & Gamble did it with Crest: when they had 36 percent of the toothpaste market, Crest had thirty-eight SKUs. When asked whether that was too many or too few, Ries asked the marketing manager: "How many teeth do you have in your mouth?" Answer: thirty-two. "No toothpaste should have more stock-keeping units than teeth in one's mouth." Today Crest has over fifty SKUs and has lost its market leadership to Colgate. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)*

The counterintuitive insight is that short-term sales gains from expansion are real. You can absolutely move more units in the quarter by widening your line. What you're doing, though, is borrowing against the future value of the brand. "While milking may bring in easy money in the short term, in the long term it wears down the brand until it no longer stands for anything." *(22 Laws of Branding, Introduction)* The discipline Ries demands is choosing to build brand equity now rather than extract sales today — because the brand you build is the one that will move product without needing expensive advertising campaigns to explain what you are.

The positive case is even more clarifying than the examples of failure. "What did Howard Schultz do? In an incredible burst of business creativity, he opened a coffee shop that specialized in, of all things, coffee." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 2)* Every coffee shop in America already sold coffee — along with pancakes, muffins, hamburgers, and pie. Schultz's genius was not product innovation; it was focus. Starbucks is worth billions not because it invented espresso but because it narrowed its identity to coffee and then dominated that space in the mind. Fred DeLuca narrowed to one type of sandwich, called the chain Subway, and built it into a major global fast-food empire by getting very good at one thing. Toys "R" Us threw out the furniture and kept the toys — and went on to sell 20 percent of all toys in the United States. Charles Lazarus "contracted rather than expanded" and built the model for every category killer that followed: Home Depot, The Gap, Victoria's Secret, PetsMart, Foot Locker. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 2)*

The five-step pattern of every category killer is worth knowing cold:
1. Narrow the focus. "A powerful branding program always starts by contracting the category, not expanding it." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 2)*
2. Stock in depth.
3. Buy cheap.
4. Sell cheap.
5. Dominate the category.

When you dominate a category, you become extraordinarily powerful. Microsoft has 95 percent of the worldwide market for desktop computer operating systems. Intel has 80 percent of the worldwide market for microprocessors. Coca-Cola has 70 percent of the worldwide market for cola. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 2)* None of them got there by expanding their brand names indiscriminately. They got there by owning one thing so completely that the category and the brand became synonymous.

## How to Apply

1. **Audit what your brand currently means in the prospect's mind.** Not what you want it to mean. Not what your positioning document says. Ask outsiders what word or phrase they associate with your brand name. If the answers are scattered or contradictory, you have an expansion problem.

2. **Count your SKUs, variants, and sub-categories.** Every item that carries your brand name is a claim on what the name means. Ask, for each one: does this reinforce the core brand word, or does it dilute it? Products that dilute are candidates for removal, renaming, or spinning off under a separate brand.

3. **Resist the sales argument.** When someone in your organization says, "But if we add this variant, we'll capture that additional segment," ask: at what cost to brand clarity? The teeter-totter metaphor is useful here: "There are thousands of tiny teeter-totters in the consumer's mind. And like their real-life counterparts, both sides can't be up at the same time." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* When you add a model or variant, one side goes down.

4. **Find the one word your brand can own.** Volvo owns "safety." BMW owns "driving." FedEx owns "overnight." What does your brand own? That word is your constraint and your asset. Everything you add to the product line should pass this test: does this reinforce the word, or does it work against it?

5. **Separate brand strategy from sales strategy.** If you need revenue this quarter, find ways to grow the market for your existing focused offering — not ways to stretch the brand name onto new offerings. FedEx expanded the market for overnight delivery by making overnight feel prestigious and important. It did not expand by slapping the FedEx name on two-day ground delivery. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 5)*

6. **If you must expand into adjacent categories, use a new brand name.** Procter & Gamble understood this: it did not launch "Tide Dishwashing Liquid." It launched Dawn. The corporate umbrella is P&G; the brand is kept narrow and powerful. "The easiest way to destroy a brand is to put it on everything." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 10)*

## Examples

**Situation:** A SaaS company has one core product (project management) and is being pressured to "leverage the brand" into CRM, HR software, and data analytics.
**Application:** Each proposed extension makes sense in isolation — the company has existing customers who use all these tools, and there's a real cross-sell opportunity. But "brand leverage" is the language of expansion, and expansion kills the word the brand owns. If the brand owns "project management" in the prospect's mind, adding CRM does not strengthen that word; it softens it. The right move: keep the core brand focused on project management and consider whether CRM belongs under a separate brand or is better left to specialists. Grow the project management market (new verticals, new geographies, deeper features) rather than grow the brand into new categories.
**Result:** The brand retains its clarity. When someone thinks "project management software," they think of you first. That's worth more than a diluted presence across five categories.

**Situation:** A specialty food brand started as "premium hot sauce" and has since extended into salad dressings, marinades, rubs, and now a line of kitchen knives because the CEO thinks it's all "flavor-forward cooking."
**Application:** The core brand word — whatever it is, probably "heat" or "bold flavor" or the brand name itself associated with hot sauce — is being pulled in six directions. The kitchen knives are the clearest violation, but the others are also problematic. The question is not "does this product fit our lifestyle?" but "does putting our brand name on this product strengthen or weaken the word we own in the prospect's mind?" For each extension, trace back to the original word. Hot sauce reinforces it. Knives contradict it. Salad dressing complicates it.
**Result:** Without correction, the brand gradually becomes "a food company that makes various things" — indistinguishable from hundreds of others. With correction, it becomes the hot sauce brand, full stop, and commands premium pricing and strong loyalty in a focused category.

**Situation:** An established B2B consulting firm is considering launching a certification program, a software tool, and a recruiting service — all under the same brand name that currently means "operational efficiency consulting."
**Application:** "The power of a brand is inversely proportional to its scope." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* The consulting firm's brand is trusted because it means one specific thing. A certification program can reinforce that expertise; a software tool and a recruiting service start pulling the name in new directions. Each new offering raises a question in the prospect's mind: wait, what exactly is this firm? The firm should consider separate brands for the software and recruiting services, maintaining the consulting brand as the focused, prestigious anchor.
**Result:** Each business can be built properly for its own market without eroding the consulting brand.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Justify line extension with "masterbrand" or "megabrand" logic.
**Why:** "Many companies try to justify line extension by invoking the masterbrand, superbrand, or megabrand concept." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* The logic goes: we have a strong parent brand, so putting it on sub-brands creates a halo. In practice, people do not think in megabrand architecture. They assign one brand name to one product. "The Lumina owner will say, 'I drive a Chevrolet.' The Corvette owner will say, 'I drive a Vette.'" *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* The megabrand concept is a management convenience, not a mental reality.

**Don't:** Mistake sales growth from expansion as proof the brand is healthy.
**Why:** "Marketers often confuse the power of a brand with the sales generated by that brand. But sales are not just a function of a brand's power. Sales are also a function of the strength or weakness of a brand's competition." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* If your competition is weak, you can expand and still grow. You've demonstrated the weakness of the competition, not the health of your brand. The day a focused competitor arrives — and does to you what FedEx did to Emery Air Freight — the damage from years of expansion becomes visible all at once.

**Don't:** Add absurd brand stretches because a product sells.
**Why:** Ries lists real examples: Harley-Davidson wine coolers, Heinz all-natural cleaning vinegar, Fruit of the Loom laundry detergent, Kleenex Super Dry baby diapers. *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* Each of these sold some units. Each of them also did something worse: they made the original brand name slightly less meaningful. You cannot run these experiments forever without accumulating brand damage.

**Don't:** Copy the expansion behavior of successful companies.
**Why:** "Most people search for success in all the wrong places. They try to find out what rich and successful companies are currently doing and then try to copy them." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 2)* You observe Starbucks expanding into ice cream and bottled drinks. You conclude that expansion is what successful companies do. But Starbucks became worth $8.7 billion before it started expanding — by narrowing its focus to coffee when every other coffee shop was selling everything. Copy the early Starbucks, not the late Starbucks.

**Don't:** Confuse the line extension with the brand.
**Why:** Once line extension becomes habitual, the extensions start defining the brand rather than the other way around. At that point, you no longer have a brand — you have a product catalog with a common label. "When you put your brand name on everything, that name loses its power." *(22 Laws of Branding, Law 1)* A powerful brand is a specific promise. A product catalog is just an inventory.
