# The Mind Ladder

## When to Use
- When someone asks why the number two brand can't just outspend the leader and win.
- When strategy is being built on the assumption that product quality alone determines market position.
- When launching into a category where competitors already have established positions.
- When a company keeps running campaigns that fail to move the needle — often because they are marketing as if their competitors don't exist.
- When a brand needs to understand what position is realistically available to them before deciding what to say.

## Fails When
- **You're creating a category.** If no ladder exists, your job is to build one — you're establishing the first rung, not climbing an existing one. Law of Category applies here; ladder thinking is a next step.
- **You're already the undisputed leader.** Leaders don't climb ladders; they defend them. The ladder framework is most useful for brands that are not first.
- **The ladder is unstable or immature.** In very early or rapidly shifting markets, the rankings haven't hardened yet — timing matters more than ladder position.

## Core Concept

Ries and Trout — writing together in *Positioning* — introduced the mind ladder as the core explanatory model for how memory and competitive preference actually work. The logic starts with a simple observation about the human mind under information overload.

"In our overcommunicated society, the human mind is a totally inadequate container. According to Harvard psychologist Dr. George A. Miller, the average human mind cannot deal with more than seven units at a time. Which is why seven is a popular number for lists that have to be remembered. Seven-digit phone numbers, the Seven Wonders of the World, seven-card stud, Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* Ask someone to name every brand they remember in a product category and "rarely will anyone name more than seven. And that's for a high-interest category. For low-interest products, the average consumer can usually name no more than one or two brands." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)*

The mind's response to this overload is structural. It doesn't record brands as a flat list. It ranks them. "To cope with the product explosion, people have learned to rank products and brands in the mind. Perhaps this can best be visualized by imagining a series of ladders in the mind. On each step is a brand name. And each different ladder represents a different product category." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* Rent-a-cars: Hertz on top, Avis on the second rung, National on the third. Colas: Coke, Pepsi, and then a long drop to everyone else. Each category has its own ladder. And the rules for competing depend entirely on which rung you are on — or whether you are on the ladder at all.

The ladder has a second property that is as important as its structure: it is highly resistant to rearrangement. "A competitor that wants to increase its share of the business must either dislodge the brand above — a task that is usually impossible — or somehow relate its brand to the other company's position." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* Trying to dislodge the brand above you through direct assault is almost never worth the investment. The more effective move is to acknowledge your rung and build a strategy that works from it. "The mind is selective. Prospects use their ladders in deciding which information to accept and which information to reject. In general, a mind accepts only new data that is consistent with its product ladder in that category. Everything else is ignored." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* This is why brands that claim positions they don't own are simply disbelieved — the prospect's mental filing system rejects inconsistent information at the door.

The relationship between rungs also follows a predictable mathematical pattern: "There's a relationship between market share and your position on the ladder in the prospect's mind. You tend to have twice the market share of the brand below you and half the market share of the brand above you." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* This ratio holds with remarkable consistency across categories. It isn't coincidence. It is a structural consequence of how the ladder works.

## How to Apply

1. **Establish your current rung honestly.** Before building any strategy, determine your actual position in the prospect's mind through research, not wishful thinking. Ask prospects to name brands in your category. Note what word or attribute they associate with each. Where does your brand fall? Is it on the ladder at all? "Before starting any marketing program, ask yourself: Where are we on the ladder in the prospect's mind? On the top rung? On the second rung? Or maybe we're not on the ladder at all." *(22 Laws, Law 7)*

2. **Accept the rung you are on as your strategic starting point.** This is the hardest step for companies. Nobody wants to accept that they are No. 2, No. 3, or "not on the ladder." But it is the only honest starting point. Avis lost money for thirteen consecutive years pretending they belonged at the top. The moment they accepted their rung — "Avis is only No. 2" — they started making money. The strategy that works for your rung is different from the strategy that works for the rung above.

3. **Choose the right strategy for your rung.** The top-rung brand should reinforce its ownership of the category word and defend against attacks. The second-rung brand should acknowledge the leader and position as the credible alternative — not as the winner. A third-rung brand must often find a way to reframe the category or identify a sub-ladder it can own. Off the ladder entirely? Forget climbing — find an adjacent ladder or build a new one.

4. **Relate your brand to the brands already in the mind.** You cannot position in a vacuum. "The mind has no room for what's new and different unless it's related to the old." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* 7-Up worked as "the uncola" because it connected explicitly to the cola ladder the prospect already had. Avis worked because it connected to Hertz. DEC worked as "the minicomputer" because it connected to IBM's mainframe. Use the existing mental architecture as your on-ramp.

5. **Watch category-size dynamics.** Sometimes the more valuable insight is that your ladder is too small. "Sometimes your own ladder, or category, is too small. It might be better to be a small fish in a big pond than to be a big fish in a small pond. In other words, it's sometimes better to be No. 3 on a big ladder than No. 1 on a small ladder." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* 7-Up's leadership of the lemon-lime ladder was less valuable than being the "uncola" on the cola ladder — a far bigger category.

## Examples

**Situation:** A car rental company has tried for years to win customers from Hertz by positioning itself as offering superior service. Despite product investments and advertising spend, market share stays flat.

**Application:** The problem is not execution. The problem is that the company is marketing against the mental structure of the category. On the rent-a-car ladder, Hertz occupies rung one. Any claim of superiority bounces off. The prospect looks at the ad and asks: How can they have the finest service when they're not even on top of my ladder? The only move that works is to acknowledge the ladder honestly and use it. "Avis is only No. 2 in rent-a-cars. So why go with us? We try harder." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* This acknowledges the rung, attaches the brand to the top rung by naming it, and proposes a credible reason to prefer the second.

**Result:** Alignment with mental reality. The prospect's ladder-checking system no longer rejects the message because the message is consistent with what the ladder already says. Sales follow.

---

**Situation:** A new analytics software product is entering a market where Salesforce dominates the CRM-adjacent space. The product team wants to market around all the features that beat Salesforce's analytics module.

**Application:** Salesforce is on top of the CRM ladder in the prospect's mind. Attacking from directly below — claiming superior analytics while the prospect files the product under "CRM competitor" — means fighting uphill against an entrenched position. Better question: Is there a separate ladder that this product can lead? If the product targets revenue operations teams rather than sales managers, or if it focuses on pipeline analytics rather than general CRM, there may be a sub-ladder — or a new ladder — available. "Some ladders have many steps. Others have few, if any." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* A new ladder with your brand on top rung one is worth more than third place on someone else's ladder.

**Result:** Category creation rather than head-on competition. The product wins the ladder it builds rather than losing the ladder it can't climb.

---

**Situation:** A beer brand launches with a campaign claiming it is the best-tasting beer in America. Taste tests support the claim.

**Application:** The prospect doesn't care about the taste tests. The ladder for domestic beer is Budweiser, Miller, Coors. The prospect has no reason to file a newcomer near the top based on a quality claim — the mind rejects information inconsistent with existing ladder structure. "The mind accepts only new data that is consistent with its product ladder in that category. Everything else is ignored." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* The relevant question is: what position on what ladder is available? Miller Lite succeeded not by claiming to taste best, but by creating a new ladder — light beer — and owning rung one.

**Result:** A best-taste claim against an established ladder is expensive noise. A new rung on a new ladder is a business.

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Build strategy based on your desired position rather than your current one.
**Why:** "Your marketing strategy should depend on how soon you got into the mind and consequently which rung of the ladder you occupy." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* A strategy built for a No. 1 brand applied to a No. 3 brand will fail. When Avis ran ads saying "Avis is going to be No. 1," the prospect said no — and went to Hertz. "No, they're not, said the prospect. They're not on the top rung of my ladder. And to make the point, many picked up the phone and called Hertz." *(22 Laws, Law 7)* Claiming the position you want, rather than acknowledging the position you have, actively damages you.

**Don't:** Assume you can unseat the brand above you with better advertising.
**Why:** Once a brand occupies a rung, "moving up the ladder in the mind can be extremely difficult if the brands above have a strong foothold and no leverage or positioning strategy is applied." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* Direct assault is almost always a losing strategy. The energy required to dislodge a well-lodged brand is nearly always better spent finding a different ladder to lead.

**Don't:** Ignore the ladder when introducing a new product.
**Why:** "An advertiser who wants to introduce a new product category must carry in a new ladder. This, too, is difficult, especially if the new category is not positioned against the old one." *(Positioning, Ch. 4)* New products fail most often not because they are bad, but because they are introduced without any relationship to existing mental structure. The horseless carriage succeeded partly because the name itself positioned it against the existing mental category of horse-drawn transportation. Give the prospect the connection they need to file your product correctly.

**Don't:** Communicate more than the mind can hold for your rung.
**Why:** The seven-unit limit is a ceiling for the entire category. For most categories it's far lower — often two or three rungs. If you are not on rung one, your entire brand presence in the prospect's mind is one slot. Trying to fill that slot with five messages produces no message at all. A brand below the leader must be even more ruthlessly simple than the leader: one word, one rung, one meaning.
