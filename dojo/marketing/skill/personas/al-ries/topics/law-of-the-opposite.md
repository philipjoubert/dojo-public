# The Law of the Opposite

## When to Use
- When your company occupies or is building toward a No. 2 position in its category.
- When the leader has an identifiable, authentic essence — an attribute or positioning that is so central to its strength that it creates an unavoidable shadow on the other side.
- When research shows that a significant portion of the market wants what the leader is *not* — younger vs. older, new vs. established, small vs. large, nimble vs. institutional.
- When you are writing brand strategy for a challenger and the instinct in the room is to list all the ways you're "better than" the leader.

## Fails When
- **You stop attacking.** Burger King's history is a case study in what happens when a No. 2 goes timid. "Have it your way" attacked McDonald's mass manufacturing. "Broiling, not frying" attacked McDonald's cooking method. "The Whopper beats the Big Mac" attacked the product directly. All three worked. Then Burger King got timid: "Herb the nerd," "The best food for fast times," programs to attract little kids — McDonald's territory. "Burger King's sales per unit declined and have never returned to the level they were when it was on the attack." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* A No. 2 that stops pointing at No. 1 has no identity.
- **You emulate rather than oppose.** "Too many potential No. 2 brands try to emulate the leader. This usually is an error." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* In 1961, Johnson & Johnson introduced Micrin mouthwash with a "scientific" positioning — which put it on Listerine's terrain. Micrin briefly became No. 2. Then Procter & Gamble introduced Scope as the opposite of Listerine: good-tasting vs. medicinal. Scope became No. 2. By 1978, Micrin's share had fallen to 1 percent and Johnson & Johnson withdrew it. Scientific No. 2 cannot survive when the leader is also scientific. Opposite No. 2 can.
- **The negative you hang on the leader isn't authentic.** "There has to be a ring of truth about the negative if it is to be effective." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* The Scope gambit worked because one whiff of Listerine confirms the medicine-breath claim instantly. Royal Doulton's positioning of Lenox as "the china of Pomona, New Jersey" worked because Pomona, New Jersey genuinely sounds like a place that doesn't make fine bone china. If the negative requires the prospect to take your word for it, it won't work.

## Core Concept

The Law of the Opposite, Law 9 in Ries and Trout's *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing*, is the strategic principle that governs No. 2 positioning. The law is compact: if you're shooting for second place, your strategy is determined by the leader. Not by your own products. Not by your own strengths. By the leader. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

The mechanism works because every market contains two fundamentally different types of buyers. There are those who want the leader — they want the established brand, the reliable choice, the original. And there are those who specifically do not want the leader — they want the alternative, the upstart, the challenger, the "not that." A competent No. 2 brand does not try to appeal to the first group; the leader will always win that contest. The No. 2 must claim the second group entirely. "In other words, by positioning yourself against the leader, you take business away from all the other alternatives to No. 1. If old people drink Coke and young people drink Pepsi, there's nobody left to drink Royal Crown cola." *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

Pepsi-Cola is the textbook application. Coca-Cola is genuinely old — a 100-year-old formula locked in a safe in Atlanta, known by only seven people in recorded history. That heritage is Coke's most powerful asset. Pepsi's strategists saw the shadow cast by that asset: if Coke is the drink of history, permanence, and established America, there is an open position for the drink of youth, change, and the future. The Pepsi Generation did not claim to be better than Coke. It claimed to be what Coke, by its very essence, could never be: new. Coke cannot become the choice of the next generation without abandoning what makes it Coca-Cola. The strength of the leader is inseparable from the weakness the No. 2 exploits. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

The operational requirement is to "discover the essence of the leader and then present the prospect with the opposite." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* This is more demanding than it sounds. The opposite must be genuinely opposite — not merely different, but the photographic negative of what the leader stands for. The parallel is wrestling: you use the opponent's weight against him. You don't fight his strength with your strength; you let his momentum carry him past you. Scope did not fight Listerine's germ-killing claims head-on. Scope labeled Listerine's core product virtue — its medicinal intensity — as the consumer's problem: "medicine breath." Then it repositioned itself as the solution: good taste and germ-fighting together. The attack turned Listerine's deepest strength (the formulation that actually kills germs is the one that also smells like a hospital) into the reason to buy Scope instead.

Stolichnaya vodka deployed the same logic against Smirnoff, Samovar, and Wolfschmidt. These American-made vodkas had Russian-sounding names because Russian origin is part of vodka's identity. Stolichnaya's campaign simply pointed out where these brands were actually made: "Hartford, Connecticut. Schenley, Pennsylvania. Lawrenceburg, Indiana." Then: "Stolichnaya comes from Leningrad, Russia." The implication was not that Smirnoff was a bad product but that it was an inauthentic one — and authenticity was the category's highest value. Stolichnaya did not manufacture a negative. It revealed one that was already there. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

## How to Apply

1. **Identify the leader's authentic essence — not a random weakness, but the deepest thing the leader stands for.** Coke stands for heritage. Hertz stands for size. Listerine stands for medicinal intensity. IBM stands for institutional credibility. What is the single most central truth about the leader? That is the thing whose shadow you will occupy.

2. **Find the shadow.** Heritage's shadow is rigidity or age. Size's shadow is impersonal service. Medicinal intensity's shadow is bad taste. Institutional credibility's shadow is bureaucratic slowness. The shadow is not manufactured. It is a logical consequence of the strength. "In strength there is weakness. Wherever the leader is strong, there is an opportunity for a would-be No. 2 to turn the tables." *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

3. **Build your position as the pure opposite.** Not "better at the same thing." Not "all the same advantages plus one more." The opposite. Pepsi is not older Coke — it is explicitly younger. Scope is not stronger Listerine — it is explicitly the alternative to medicine taste. Avis is not a bigger Hertz — it is explicitly smaller, and it makes the smaller counter line into the reason to choose it.

4. **Articulate the negative first, then twist the sword.** "The law of the opposite is a two-edged sword. It requires honing in on a weakness that your prospect will quickly acknowledge. Then quickly twist the sword." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* First, make the prospect feel the leader's liability. Then, position your brand as the relief. Scope: one whiff of Listerine confirms medicine breath. *Then*: Scope is the good-tasting mouthwash that kills germs. The sequence matters. Assertion without prior acknowledgment is just a claim. Acknowledgment followed by assertion is a story the prospect tells herself.

5. **Never stop attacking.** The opposite position is not a static tagline — it is a strategic stance that must be actively maintained. Every time Burger King ran a campaign that didn't position against McDonald's, it lost ground. The No. 2 position lives only as long as the No. 2 keeps pointing at No. 1.

## Examples

**Situation:** Pepsi-Cola competes against Coca-Cola, which owns 60 percent of the cola market and has done so for decades.
**Application:** Discover the essence of Coke: it is old, established, timeless, the heritage product. The opposite: Pepsi is new, youthful, forward-looking — "the choice of a new generation." Coke cannot rebrand itself as youthful without abandoning the heritage that is its greatest strength.
**Result:** Over 22 years, Coke's share drops from roughly 60 percent to 45 percent; Pepsi's rises to 40 percent. Royal Crown, which positioned itself as neither old nor new, falls from 6 percent to 3 percent. Positioning against the leader absorbed all the "anti-Coke" buying motivation, leaving nothing for anyone else. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

**Situation:** Scope enters the mouthwash market behind dominant Listerine.
**Application:** Listerine's strength is its harsh, medicinal formula that genuinely kills germs. That formula also produces an unmistakably medicinal smell and taste. Scope labels this "medicine breath" — a weakness the prospect immediately recognizes. Then: "Scope is the good-tasting mouthwash that kills germs." The positioning does not claim better germ-killing than Listerine. It claims the same germ-killing plus the thing Listerine's formula structurally prevents: palatability.
**Result:** Scope becomes the No. 2 mouthwash. Micrin — which tried to compete with Listerine on scientific/medical positioning rather than opposing it — fell from No. 2 to 1 percent share and was withdrawn from the market. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

**Situation:** Stolichnaya competes against American-made vodkas with Russian names — Smirnoff (Hartford, CT), Samovar (Schenley, PA), Wolfschmidt (Lawrenceburg, IN).
**Application:** Vodka's cultural authority comes from Russian origin. American-made vodkas leverage Russian names while being manufactured domestically. Stolichnaya's campaign simply states the facts of origin: "Hartford, Connecticut. Schenley, Pennsylvania. Lawrenceburg, Indiana." Then: "Stolichnaya comes from Leningrad, Russia." The implication — fake Russian vs. real Russian — needed no further argument.
**Result:** Stolichnaya establishes a durable premium position built on a negative that was already true. The consumer does all the logical work once the facts are presented. *(22 Laws, Law 9)*

## Anti-Patterns (tactical)

**Don't:** Try to be better than the leader at what the leader is already best at.
**Why:** "No matter how strong a No. 2 company is in a certain category or attribute, it cannot win if this is also where the leader is strong." *(Marketing Warfare, Ch. 8)* Micrin tried to out-science Listerine. It failed because the prospect already credited Listerine with scientific authority. You cannot outflank a mental position by attacking the strongest wall.

**Don't:** Go timid when the attacks are working.
**Why:** Burger King's "Have it your way" campaign directly attacked McDonald's and produced the company's best sales results. The moment Burger King shifted to generic messaging — "The best food for fast times," "We do it the way you do it" — it lost altitude it never recovered. The No. 2 position exists in relation to No. 1. Remove the reference point and you have no position.

**Don't:** Hang a negative on the leader that isn't immediately confirmable.
**Why:** The prospect must be able to confirm the negative from their own experience, immediately. "One whiff of Listerine and you know that your mouth would smell like a hospital." *(22 Laws, Law 9)* The negative has to land before the prospect even evaluates the positive claim. If it requires argument, citation, or proof, it won't work as a positioning device. Use negatives that are self-evident, not negatives that require the prospect to trust you.
